    Probabilistic circuits (PCs) represent a probability distribution as a directed acyclic computation graph whose leaves are simple distributions and whose internal nodes combine them by weighted sums and products. Their central design principle is unusual in modern generative modeling: make exact queries tractable by construction, then spend representational capacity inside that tractable language.

    This volume develops that principle from first evaluation to current deep and hybrid circuits. The canonical framework follows Choi, Vergari, and Van den Broeck's unified treatment; modern systems and research claims are linked to primary papers.

    Graphical Inference and Message Passing derives how elimination and junction-tree computations can be retained as arithmetic circuits. This volume begins where that bridge ends: it treats a constrained circuit as the model representation and audits its query guarantees property by property.

    Evidence convention

    A tractability statement always names the query, structural properties, leaf assumptions, and complexity measure. Established marks algebraic results and mature algorithms; peer-reviewed research marks archival work; preprint marks non-archival research. The page metadata records the verification date. Benchmark claims remain tied to the reported circuit class, data, parameter count, query, and hardware.

    Read continuously for the full argument. The numbered chapter pages are projections of the same source and provide stopping points without duplicating content.
    Beginner route: Chapters 1–3.
    Practitioner route: Chapters 1–5 plus the deployment checklist.
    Research route: all chapters, with special attention to which query requires which structural property.

    1. From Arithmetic Expression to Probability Model¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand finite mixtures, factorized distributions, DAGs, and probability masses/densities with respect to a declared base measure. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. evaluate a sum/product circuit bottom-up and compute every node's scope;
    2. state sufficient conditions under which the root is normalized;
    3. interpret sum choices as discrete latent variables without expanding the DAG into a mixture tree;
    4. distinguish circuit evaluation, probabilistic validity, and query tractability; and
    5. construct and normalize a complete finite-state example.

    Consider variables \(X=(X_1,\ldots,X_D)\). A circuit is a DAG. Every node \(n\) has a scope \(\operatorname{sc}(n)\): the variables on which its output depends.

    • A leaf computes a tractable univariate or small multivariate mass/density, such as a Bernoulli, categorical, Gaussian, or histogram.
    • A product node multiplies child values.
    • A sum node computes a nonnegative weighted sum of child values.

    For input \(x\), write

    \[ v_n(x)= \begin{cases} p_n(x_{\operatorname{sc}(n)}) & n\text{ is a leaf},\\ \prod_{c\in\operatorname{ch}(n)}v_c(x) & n\text{ is a product},\\ \sum_{c\in\operatorname{ch}(n)}w_{nc}v_c(x) & n\text{ is a sum}. \end{cases} \]

    When leaves are normalized, sum weights are nonnegative and normalized, and the graph satisfies the required scope constraints, the root computes a normalized distribution. Products express independence factorizations; sums express mixtures. Sharing subcircuits lets the DAG reuse distributions rather than expand into an exponentially large mixture tree.

    More precisely, a standard sufficient construction is smooth and decomposable: children of each sum have the same scope, and children of each product have disjoint scopes. If every leaf integrates to one over its scope and each sum's outgoing weights sum to one, induction over the DAG proves every node integrates to one over its scope. Without those conditions, a positive arithmetic circuit may still define a nonnegative function, but its partition function need not be one or tractable.

    The reference measure matters. Bernoulli and categorical leaves use counting measure; Gaussian leaves use Lebesgue measure. Mixing a mass and a density over the same variable without an explicit mixed measure is not a well-defined shortcut. Likewise, a missing-value sentinel is an observed category only if the model says so; otherwise missingness is a query operation that marginalizes an unobserved variable.

    Three different uses of “tractable”¤

    Keep three complexity claims separate:

    1. Evaluation tractability: given a circuit and a complete assignment, one bottom-up pass evaluates its arithmetic function in \(O(|E|)\) scalar node operations plus leaf cost.
    2. Query tractability: a particular marginal, maximization, expectation, or circuit operation has a polynomial algorithm under stated properties.
    3. Representation tractability: the target distribution admits a compact circuit satisfying those properties.

    The first can hold while the second fails, and both can hold while the third requires exponential circuit size. Learning adds a fourth question: whether data and optimization can discover the compact representation when one exists.

    A conceptual evaluator is short:

    for node in topological_order_from_leaves:
    if leaf(node): value[node] = leaf_value(node, observation_or_query)
    if product(node): value[node] = product(value[child] for child in children(node))
    if sum(node): value[node] = sum(weight[node, child] * value[child]
                                     for child in children(node))
return value[root]

    Changing leaf_value from point evaluation to a leaf marginal turns the same graph into a marginal algorithm only when the structural proof in Chapter 2 applies.

    A Running Four-Variable Circuit¤

    Let \(X_1,X_2,X_3,X_4\) be binary. Build two alternative decompositions of the same full scope:

    \[ C_A(X)=C_{12}^{A}(X_1,X_2)C_{34}^{A}(X_3,X_4), \]
    \[ C_B(X)=C_{12}^{B}(X_1,X_2)C_{34}^{B}(X_3,X_4), \]

    and mix them:

    \[ C(X)=\lambda C_A(X)+(1-\lambda)C_B(X). \]

    Inside either product branch, the two variable groups are independent. After mixing over the unobserved branch, they need not be marginally independent. This is the same “mixture of factorizations” mechanism that makes mixture models more expressive than any component.

    Introduce a latent branch variable \(H\in\{A,B\}\). Then

    \[ p(x)=\sum_h p(h)\,p(x_{1:2}\mid h)p(x_{3:4}\mid h). \]

    Nested sum nodes introduce several latent choices. Expanding all choices produces a mixture whose number of components can grow exponentially with depth, while shared subcircuits keep the representation and evaluation graph compact. This latent-variable view is useful for EM, but it does not imply every combination of local choices is reachable: DAG sharing and deterministic support constraints can couple which traces represent valid assignments.

    Circuits, SPNs, and Compiled Models¤

    The terminology overlaps several traditions:

    • sum-product networks emphasize learned deep sum/product DAGs;
    • arithmetic circuits may be compiled from Bayesian networks or logical theories;
    • probabilistic sentential decision diagrams combine logical and probabilistic structure;
    • Einsum Networks organize regular circuit computations into dense tensor operations.

    Probabilistic circuit is the umbrella representation. The graph is not merely a neural architecture: its structural constraints determine which probabilistic queries remain exact.

    A positive sum/product network is not automatically tractable for every query

    Evaluating one complete assignment can be easy while marginal, MAP, entropy, or circuit-combination queries remain hard. State the circuit properties and query together.

    Worked example: normalize and evaluate a two-variable circuit¤

    Let \(X,Y\in\{0,1\}\) and

    \[ C(x,y)=0.6\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.8)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.7) +0.4\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.2)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.1). \]

    Each product node is decomposable because one child has scope \(\{X\}\) and the other \(\{Y\}\). The root sum is smooth because both products have scope \(\{X,Y\}\). Therefore

    \[ \sum_{x,y}C(x,y)=0.6(1)(1)+0.4(1)(1)=1. \]

    For \((x,y)=(1,1)\), the child products are \(0.8\cdot0.7=0.56\) and \(0.2\cdot0.1=0.02\), so \(C(1,1)=0.344\). The arithmetic evaluation costs time linear in the circuit edges. That statement concerns this represented function; finding a compact circuit from data is a separate learning problem.

    Counterexample: normalized parts, invalid product

    Let two normalized Bernoulli leaves both have scope \(\{X\}\) and multiply them. The result is \(p_1(x)p_2(x)\), whose sum over \(x\) is generally not one. Normalized children do not make a product normalized when scopes overlap.

    Independent practice and assessment map: probabilistic-circuit semantics¤

    Work from scopes, leaf measures, and local normalization rather than from the graph's appearance. For every claimed probability or complexity result, show the induction or finite enumeration that makes it true.

    P1 — Build and falsify a finite probabilistic circuit¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: every nonnegative sum/product DAG is normalized; normalized leaves and normalized sum weights suffice even when product scopes overlap; linear-time point evaluation implies linear-time arbitrary marginalization; a smooth sum is a mixture over one common scope; DAG sharing preserves the represented function while reducing repeated computation; and an unobserved missing-value sentinel should always be assigned probability mass. For every false statement, name the missing structural, measure, or query assumption.
    2. P1.2 — Scope and normalization ledger. Let \(C(x,y)=0.3\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.9)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.2)+0.7\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.4)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.8)\). Draw the leaf, product, and sum DAG; label every scope; evaluate all four assignments; verify their masses sum to one; and compute the root value at \((1,0)\) in two independent ways. Give one overlapping-scope edit that keeps point evaluation easy but destroys the standard normalization proof.
    3. P1.3 — Measure and missingness contract. Design a two-variable circuit for a nonnegative count and a positive continuous measurement. Declare counting and Lebesgue reference measures, leaf supports, units, and any support transform. Compare treating a missingness flag as an observed Bernoulli variable with marginalizing an unobserved measurement. Show why mixing a point mass and density on the same coordinate without a mixed reference measure is not a valid shortcut.
    4. P1.4 — Latent traces and sharing. Interpret the root of P1.2 as a latent branch \(H\). Then construct \(0.4\,S(X,Y)p_A(Z)+0.6\,S(X,Y)p_B(Z)\) with a shared normalized subcircuit \(S\). Expand it as a mixture tree and compare nodes, edges, values, and latent traces with the shared DAG. Generalize the trace count for \(L\) nested binary sums and identify which combinations can disappear under deterministic support constraints.
    5. P1.5 — Separate three tractability claims. Give one circuit that is easy to evaluate but not yet proved normalized, one normalized smooth/decomposable circuit with expensive multivariate leaf marginals, and one compact-query circuit class whose representation of a chosen target requires a large compiled graph. For each, state input size, query, leaf oracle, and complexity measure; reject any conclusion that moves from evaluation to validity, query tractability, representation size, or learnability without a proof.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Transfer P1.2 to either zero-inflated event counts with exposure or circular sensor angles. Redefine support, base/reference measure, leaves, scopes, normalized sums, and required queries. Provide a finite or quadrature normalization oracle and explain which Bernoulli-circuit arguments survive unchanged and which must be replaced.

    Chapter 1 Synthesis¤

    A PC composes tractable leaf distributions with mixture and factorization operations. Normalization follows from explicit local conditions and an induction over scopes—not from drawing a sum/product DAG. The next chapter asks which additional properties preserve particular queries.

    Retrieval practice: circuit semantics
    1. What does a sum node mean probabilistically when it is normalized and smooth?
    2. Why must a normalized product's child scopes be disjoint in the standard construction?
    3. Does linear-time evaluation prove the circuit is a normalized distribution?
    4. What does DAG sharing save compared with expanding every latent trace?

    Solutions. (1) A mixture over child distributions on the same variables. (2) Disjoint scopes let the integral factor into the product of child integrals without integrating the same variable twice. (3) No. Any arithmetic DAG can be evaluated; validity requires parameter, leaf, scope, and measure conditions. (4) It reuses identical subcomputations/subdistributions and can avoid an exponentially larger mixture tree.

    Bridge. Semantics tells us what function the graph computes. Chapter 2 turns structural properties into a query-by-query guarantee map. Continue continuously or open Chapter 2.

    2. Structural Properties and Their Guarantees¤

    The four most important properties answer different questions. None should be silently substituted for another.

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the node and scope semantics from Chapter 1. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. audit smoothness, decomposability, determinism, and structured decomposability independently;
    2. prove the marginal-evaluation recursion for smooth decomposable circuits;
    3. explain why max-product is exact for appropriate deterministic circuits but not overlapping mixtures;
    4. distinguish ordinary, marginal, and query-specific determinism; and
    5. identify when operations between two circuits require compatible vtrees or can cause size blow-up.

    Smoothness¤

    A sum node is smooth when all children have the same scope:

    \[ \operatorname{sc}(c_i)=\operatorname{sc}(c_j) \quad\text{for all children }c_i,c_j. \]

    Then the sum mixes distributions over the same variables. Missing variables can sometimes be repaired by multiplying a branch by a normalized unit distribution, but the repair changes circuit size and must preserve other properties.

    Decomposability¤

    A product node is decomposable when its child scopes are pairwise disjoint:

    \[ \operatorname{sc}(c_i)\cap\operatorname{sc}(c_j)=\varnothing \quad(i\ne j). \]

    This makes marginalization distribute through products without double-counting a variable. Smoothness plus decomposability supports linear-time evaluation of marginals in the circuit size, assuming tractable leaf marginals.

    Determinism¤

    A sum node is deterministic when at most one child has nonzero value for any complete assignment. Its mixture components have disjoint support. Determinism turns summation over alternatives into mutually exclusive case analysis and enables additional queries, including tractable MPE and query-specific MAP transformations for appropriate circuit classes. It does not make arbitrary marginal MAP tractable.

    Determinism is a representational restriction. Ordinary soft mixtures are nondeterministic because several components can explain the same observation. Determinism may improve query tractability while requiring a larger circuit to represent a smooth overlapping density.

    Determinism can also be query-relative. A circuit may become deterministic after projecting onto a subset of variables, or be deterministic with respect to the variables maximized in a marginal-MAP query. This is often called marginal or \(Q\)-determinism. The qualifier matters: exclusivity over complete assignments does not automatically survive marginalizing variables that distinguished the branches.

    Structured Decomposability¤

    Decomposable circuits may split scopes differently at different product nodes. A structured-decomposable circuit conforms to a shared hierarchical variable decomposition, commonly represented by a vtree. Compatible structure makes operations between circuits—products, expectations, logical compositions, and some causal queries—tractable because their partitions align.

    Strudel is a primary example of learning structured-decomposable circuits. Restructuring Tractable Probabilistic Circuits studies transforming a structured PC to conform to a target vtree, illustrating that circuit compatibility is an algorithmic problem rather than superficial graph formatting.

    A vtree is a binary tree whose leaves are variables. Each internal vtree node specifies a left/right split of a scope. A circuit respects the vtree when product decompositions follow those splits recursively. This restricts which context-specific factorizations can coexist compactly, but makes separately built circuits align for operations such as products and expectations.

    Sentential decision diagrams and probabilistic SDDs add logical decision structure, typically combining structured decomposability with determinism. Their tractability comes from the compiled logical/circuit properties, not from the name “decision diagram.” A vtree is therefore both an inductive bias and an interface contract.

    Property-to-Query Map¤

    Table PC.1. Property-to-Query Map
    Query or operation Typical sufficient requirements Caveat
    Complete-assignment likelihood tractable leaves and arithmetic evaluation normalization still requires valid parameters/structure
    Marginal probability smooth + decomposable leaf marginals must be tractable
    Conditional probability tractable joint marginals and nonzero evidence ratio can be numerically unstable for rare evidence
    Exact sampling normalized smooth/decomposable generative circuit requires posterior branch probabilities under evidence for conditional sampling
    MPE determinism plus compatible max replacement in the circuit class marginal MAP is generally harder
    Entropy/cross-entropy/KL between circuits additional compatibility, often structured decomposability not guaranteed for arbitrary pairs of PCs
    Multiply circuits compatible decompositions or a compilation procedure product may blow up in size

    These are sufficient-condition summaries, not universal biconditionals. A particular non-smooth circuit may still answer a particular query cheaply; the structural properties give family-wide guarantees.

    Why the marginal recursion works¤

    Let \(M_n(E=e)\) denote the integral of node \(n\) over variables in its scope that are not observed. At a leaf, compute the declared leaf marginal. At a smooth sum,

    \[ M_n(e)=\sum_c w_{nc}M_c(e), \]

    because every child represents the same variables. At a decomposable product,

    \[ M_n(e)=\prod_c M_c(e), \]

    because disjoint child scopes allow the integral to factor. Applying these identities in topological order gives time linear in the represented circuit edges plus leaf-query costs. The proof does not say the circuit is small; a polynomial-time algorithm in an exponentially large compiled circuit is not a practical victory.

    Semirings: Reusing the Graph for New Queries¤

    Sum-product evaluation occurs over the ordinary \((+,\times)\) semiring. Replacing operations can compute related quantities when the circuit properties justify the transformation:

    • max-product for most-probable explanations;
    • expectation semirings for moments or sufficient statistics;
    • Boolean operations for logical support queries;
    • automatic differentiation for all node/edge sensitivities in one upward/downward pass.

    The circuit is therefore both a distribution and a compiled dynamic program.

    Semiring replacement is not a license to swap operators blindly. Max-product gives exact MPE in a suitable deterministic, decomposable circuit because only one sum child contributes to any complete assignment. In a nondeterministic mixture, two branches can support the same assignment; replacing their sum by max discards probability mass and can change the maximizing assignment.

    Worked example: audit properties before choosing an algorithm¤

    Consider

    \[ C(X,Y)=\tfrac12[I(X=0)p_0(Y)+I(X=1)p_1(Y)] +\tfrac12[I(X=0)q_0(Y)+I(X=1)q_1(Y)]. \]

    Each bracket can be a deterministic sum over \(X\), and each indicator–\(Y\) product is decomposable. The root sum is generally nondeterministic because both large children support \(X=0\) and \(X=1\). Thus the circuit can be smooth and decomposable while failing determinism at one node. Marginals remain tractable; blindly replacing every sum with max is not justified.

    Now force the first root child to support only \(X=0\) and the second only \(X=1\). The root becomes deterministic with respect to \(X\), enabling max over \(X\) after summing \(Y\) when the remaining structure satisfies the marginal-MAP conditions. The graph shape barely changed, but the query guarantee did.

    Counterexample: compatible sizes are not free

    Two structured-decomposable circuits can each be compact under different vtrees. Multiplying them may require restructuring one or both, and the resulting compatible circuit can grow substantially. "Both inputs are tractable" does not imply every binary operation is linear in their original sizes.

    Independent practice and assessment map: circuit properties¤

    Treat every property as a local, executable contract and every tractability claim as query-specific. A property name without its node set, variable set, and leaf assumptions is not a completed answer.

    P1 — Derive a property-to-query guarantee¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each implication is valid: decomposable implies deterministic; smooth plus decomposable makes any leaf marginal cheap; complete-assignment determinism always survives marginalization; max-product is exact in every normalized PC; two compact structured PCs can always be multiplied with linear output size; and a vtree is only a visualization choice. For each failure, give the smallest counterexample or the missing qualifier.
    2. P1.2 — Node-by-node property audit. Analyze \(C=0.5\,[I(X=0)p_0(Y)+I(X=1)p_1(Y)]r(Z)+0.5\,[I(X=0)q_0(Y)+I(X=1)q_1(Y)]s(Z)\). Compute every scope and audit smoothness, decomposability, and determinism at each internal node. Explain why the inner sums may be deterministic while the root is generally nondeterministic, and list the marginals and maximization queries justified by exactly those results.
    3. P1.3 — Prove and break the marginal recursion. For P1.2, derive the upward marginal for evidence on \(Y\) while \(X\) and \(Z\) are unobserved, first algebraically and then by complete finite enumeration for chosen Bernoulli leaves. Modify one product so two children contain \(Y\) and show the precise equality in the factorized-integral proof that fails.
    4. P1.4 — Determinism and the max/sum boundary. Construct a two-level circuit in which every complete assignment activates one child at each sum, and verify MPE by max-product and enumeration. Then hide a variable that distinguishes the branches and test whether determinism survives for the remaining maximized variables. If it does not, exhibit a marginal-MAP disagreement and state the query-specific determinism needed to repair the argument.
    5. P1.5 — Vtree compatibility ledger. Draw vtrees \(((A,B),(C,D))\) and \(((A,C),(B,D))\) for two structured-decomposable circuits. Identify which product partitions align, what restructuring or compilation would be required before a circuit product or expectation, and which size quantity must be reported.     3. Exact Inference by Upward and Downward Passes¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes a smooth, decomposable normalized circuit unless a stronger property is stated. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. compute evidence, marginals, and conditionals with upward messages;
    2. sample unconditionally and conditionally using posterior sum-edge probabilities;
    3. derive downward sensitivities and normalized responsibilities;
    4. implement stable log-domain evaluation without confusing log-sum-exp with a new circuit semantics; and
    5. distinguish MPE from marginal MAP and recognize zero-evidence failure cases.

    Marginalization as Leaf Substitution¤

    For discrete indicator leaves, a complete assignment selects indicator values. To marginalize an unobserved variable, set all of its indicators to one. More generally, replace a leaf by its integral over unobserved coordinates. Smoothness and decomposability ensure the root then equals the desired marginal.

    For evidence \(e\) on observed variables \(E\), compute \(C(e)=p(E=e)\). A conditional follows from

    \[ p(Q=q\mid E=e)=\frac{C(q,e)}{C(e)}. \]

    This is exact arithmetic inference, not amortized imputation. Numerical underflow remains possible, so log-space evaluation or scaled messages are important for deep circuits.

    In log space, product nodes add child log-values and sum nodes use

    \[ \log v_n=\operatorname{logsumexp}_c(\log w_{nc}+\log v_c). \]

    Zero-valued branches map to \(-\infty\). Conditional probabilities should be formed by subtracting log evidence and exponentiating only when needed. If \(C(e)=0\), the model conditional is undefined; clipping the denominator silently invents a different distribution.

    For a mixed query, each leaf needs an explicit interface:

    • point mass or density for observed coordinates;
    • integral over omitted coordinates;
    • conditional sampler for unobserved coordinates given any observed coordinates in the same multivariate leaf; and
    • optionally a maximizer and maximum value for MPE.

    Univariate normalized leaves make the marginal interface trivial: an unobserved leaf returns one. Multivariate leaves can be more expressive, but their internal marginal and conditional problems become part of total query cost.

    Worked Example: Marginal and Conditional Query¤

    Let a circuit over binary \(X,Y\) be

    \[ p(x,y)=0.6\,p_A(x)p_A(y)+0.4\,p_B(x)p_B(y), \]

    with \(p_A(X=1)=0.8\), \(p_A(Y=1)=0.7\), \(p_B(X=1)=0.2\), and \(p_B(Y=1)=0.1\).

    The joint event is

    \[ p(X=1,Y=1)=0.6(0.8)(0.7)+0.4(0.2)(0.1)=0.344. \]

    Marginalizing \(X\) means each branch's \(X\) factor integrates to one:

    \[ p(Y=1)=0.6(0.7)+0.4(0.1)=0.46. \]

    Therefore

    \[ p(X=1\mid Y=1)=\frac{0.344}{0.46}\approx0.748. \]

    Although \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent within each product component, observing \(Y\) updates the posterior mixture weight and therefore changes the distribution of \(X\).

    The posterior component weight for branch A is \(0.6(0.7)/0.46\approx0.913\). Conditional sampling first draws the branch from \((0.913,0.087)\), then samples \(X\) from that branch's Bernoulli. This produces the same \(p(X\mid Y=1)\) as the ratio calculation; it is not ancestral sampling from the original weights \((0.6,0.4)\).

    Sampling¤

    At a product node, sample every child because scopes are disjoint. At a sum node, sample one child according to its mixture weight for unconditional sampling. Under evidence, weights become posterior branch probabilities proportional to

    \[ w_{nc}\,v_c(e). \]

    Then recursively sample unobserved leaves conditioned on evidence. This yields exact conditional samples when the needed evaluations and leaf conditionals are exact.

    A robust conditional sampler performs one evidence pass, caches every child evidence value, then traverses top-down:

    values = upward_evidence_pass(e)
visit(root)
  product: visit every child
  sum: draw child c with probability weight[c] * values[c] / values[parent]
  leaf: draw missing coordinates from the leaf conditional

    The quotient assumes values[parent] > 0. For a DAG, one sampled trace visits the chosen descendants; cached upward values can be reused across samples, while random choices must remain independent unless coupled sampling is intended.

    Derivatives and Responsibilities¤

    Let the root value be \(C(x)\). A downward pass computes \(\partial C/\partial v_n\) for every node. For an edge from sum node \(n\) to child \(c\), its posterior responsibility is proportional to

    \[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial v_n}\,w_{nc}v_c(x). \]

    Automatic differentiation implements this calculation, but the probabilistic interpretation depends on the normalized circuit semantics. Gradients are not evidence of a valid posterior if the graph violates those semantics.

    For a normalized complete-data circuit, divide by the root value to obtain the expected use or flow of that edge under the posterior trace:

    \[ \gamma_{nc}(x)=\frac{1}{C(x)} \frac{\partial C(x)}{\partial v_n}\,w_{nc}v_c(x). \]

    Shared DAG nodes can receive flow from several parents, which is why the downward derivative is essential; a local softmax over one sum node is insufficient when the probability of reaching that node also depends on upstream choices.

    Marginal MAP Is a Warning Case¤

    MPE maximizes all unobserved variables. Marginal MAP maximizes some variables while summing out others:

    \[ \arg\max_y\sum_z p(y,z,e). \]

    Max and sum do not generally commute, so replacing every sum by max is wrong. Specialized transformations can solve cases on suitable PCs; see Choi and Friedman's exact marginal-MAP transformations. The lesson generalizes: “exact inference” is never one undifferentiated capability.

    For a concrete counterexample, let \(p(a,0)=0.30\), \(p(a,1)=0.30\), \(p(b,0)=0.39\), and \(p(b,1)=0.01\). MPE chooses \((b,0)\) because \(0.39\) is the largest complete-assignment mass. Marginal MAP over the first variable chooses \(a\) because \(p(a)=0.60>p(b)=0.40\). Maximizing before summing returns the wrong query.

    Beyond marginals: expectations and circuit operations¤

    Moments can be computed by expectation semirings or by combining the distribution circuit with a compatible function circuit. Entropy, cross-entropy, KL, products, quotients, and powers require operation-specific closure and compatibility conditions. The compositional atlas supplies algorithms and hardness boundaries. It is incorrect to infer that because \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are each tractable, \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)\) is automatically tractable.

    4. Parameter and Structure Learning¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand mixture-model EM, maximum likelihood, softmax parameterization, and validation-based model selection. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive sum-edge EM updates from posterior circuit flows;
    2. compare EM, gradient, and minibatch updates without calling any of them universally stable;
    3. separate parameter count from effective circuit capacity and structure;
    4. explain recursive, random/regular, pruning/growing, and distillation-based structure learning; and
    5. audit distributed or tensorized scaling claims independently from probabilistic tractability.

    Parameter Learning with Fixed Structure¤

    For normalized sum weights, maximum likelihood can use projected/softmax gradient optimization or EM. In EM, latent branch choices receive posterior responsibilities in the E-step; the M-step normalizes expected edge counts:

    \[ w_{nc}^{\mathrm{new}} =\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\gamma_{i,nc}} {\sum_{c'}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\gamma_{i,nc'}}. \]

    Leaf parameters update from responsibility-weighted sufficient statistics. Dirichlet or other priors add pseudo-counts and regularization. Deterministic circuits can make branch assignments hard; nondeterministic circuits usually require soft responsibilities.

    The update follows from maximizing the expected complete-trace log likelihood. If \(N_{nc}=\sum_i\gamma_{i,nc}\) is the expected count for edge \((n,c)\), the constrained problem

    \[ \max_{w_{n\cdot}}\sum_c N_{nc}\log w_{nc} \quad\text{subject to}\quad w_{nc}\ge0,\ \sum_c w_{nc}=1 \]

    has solution \(w_{nc}=N_{nc}/\sum_{c'}N_{nc'}\). With a Dirichlet prior, pseudo-count conventions must be stated. If a sum node receives zero total flow, the maximum-likelihood update is undefined; retaining old weights, smoothing, pruning, or reinitializing are different algorithmic choices.

    Gradient learning often parameterizes \(w_{nc}=\operatorname{softmax}(a_n)_c\). It optimizes the observed-data log likelihood directly and composes naturally with neural leaves, but minibatch gradients can have high variance and redundant latent states can create flat or symmetric directions. EM's monotonic full-batch likelihood property under exact E/M steps does not transfer automatically to generalized, stochastic, truncated, or regularized EM.

    Identifiability, redundancy, and regularization¤

    Permuting latent sum children leaves the represented distribution unchanged. Duplicate children create continua of equivalent weights. Very narrow continuous leaves can produce pathological likelihood behavior, and unused subcircuits receive almost no posterior flow. These are model/optimization facts, not inference errors.

    Regularization choices target different failures:

    • Dirichlet pseudo-counts discourage exactly zero mixture weights;
    • entropy penalties can encourage or discourage diffuse routing;
    • leaf priors or variance floors control degenerate local densities;
    • pruning removes persistently low-flow edges;
    • diversity or distillation terms may break redundant latent symmetry.

    Each changes the learned objective. Report it rather than describing the result simply as maximum likelihood.

    A fixed-structure full-batch EM iteration is:

    zero expected edge counts and leaf sufficient statistics
for observation x:
    run upward values and downward flows
    accumulate normalized posterior edge flows
    accumulate responsibility-weighted leaf statistics
normalize outgoing expected counts at every sum node
fit each leaf from its weighted sufficient statistics

    The exact messages can be parallelized over observations, but the global normalization and parameter synchronization still have systems costs.

    Peer-reviewed research: Liu, Shao, and Van den Broeck (AISTATS 2026 Oral) reinterpret full-batch PC EM as optimizing a first-order approximation regularized by a KL penalty on the distributional update. Their anemone minibatch method adaptively scales each parameter update by its contribution to batch likelihood, addressing the paper's diagnosis that ordinary minibatch EM/gradient updates can overfit the current minibatch. The reported language, image, and DNA improvements concern fixed circuit families and optimizer comparisons; they do not solve structure learning, initialization, or every hybrid continuous-latent circuit.

    Structure Learning¤

    Structure determines both expressivity and available queries. Common strategies include:

    • recursively split data into clusters for sum nodes and variables into approximately independent groups for product nodes;
    • start from a random or region-graph structure and optimize parameters;
    • grow, prune, or locally restructure a circuit under a validation objective;
    • compile from a graphical model, logical theory, or vtree;
    • distill latent assignments or representations from a less tractable teacher.

    LearnSPN-style recursive learning is efficient but greedy. SoftLearn replaces hard routing during structure induction with soft clustering and analyzes the relation between learning and inference semantics. Sparse PCs via pruning and growing show that nominally large dense structures can waste capacity and that learned sparsity matters.

    These strategies optimize different objects. Recursive partitioning makes irreversible local decisions. A fixed random region graph delegates structure to a prior and spends learning on parameters. Pruning/growing edits a superstructure. Distillation injects teacher-chosen latent organization. Comparing them requires matching parameter/edge budgets, leaf families, preprocessing, optimization steps, validation searches, and query requirements.

    Regular Structures and Einsum Networks¤

    Einsum Networks organize large numbers of circuit operations into dense tensor contractions. The probabilistic semantics still come from sum/product scopes; einsum is an efficient implementation strategy, not a new density family. Regular region graphs improve accelerator utilization but impose a structural prior on how variables interact.

    Peer-reviewed research: Scaling Probabilistic Circuits via Data Partitioning introduces federated circuits for horizontal, vertical, and hybrid data partitions. Its UAI 2025 experiments address distributed learning and classification/density tasks. They do not imply that arbitrary advanced queries are communication-free, that privacy follows without a threat model, or that the learned global circuit has the same size as a centrally optimized alternative.

    Worked example: a small EM update¤

    Return to the two-component circuit from Chapter 3. For observation \((X=1,Y=1)\), component likelihoods are \(0.56\) and \(0.02\). The posterior responsibility of component A is

    \[ \gamma_A= \frac{0.6(0.56)}{0.6(0.56)+0.4(0.02)} =\frac{0.336}{0.344}\approx0.977. \]

    One point should not drive a reliable update, but it shows the mechanism: exact upward evaluation supplies soft latent counts; normalized counts update mixture weights; weighted leaf statistics update distributions.

    The Expressivity–Tractability Trade-off¤

    Circuit size is the price of exact queries. A distribution may have a compact neural sampler but require a very large circuit under determinism or a fixed vtree. Conversely, a circuit can compactly reuse repeated context-specific independence that a flat mixture expands exponentially.

    Claims that one circuit class “subsumes” another refer to representational size under specified constraints, not automatically easier training or better finite-data generalization. Query guarantees must be carried through the translation.

    The size issue survives approximation. Peer-reviewed research: Leland and Choi, NeurIPS 2025 study approximation under \(f\)-divergences, prove hardness for broad tractable-marginal model classes, and establish an exponential approximation-size separation between decomposable circuits and deterministic decomposable circuits. This does not say practical deterministic PCs are always large; it shows that allowing small approximation error does not erase worst-case expressivity costs.

    Preprint research: Zhao et al., 2026 compare autoregressive PCs with Transformer language models. They identify probability-space convex-combination and fixed-routing bottlenecks, propose logit-space parameterization, and analyze vtree-aligned separation rank. As a May 2026 preprint, these results are an important hypothesis/theory checkpoint, not established evidence that one parameterization closes the language-modeling gap across scales.

    Neural Leaves and Conditional Circuits¤

    Leaves may be flexible tractable distributions whose parameters come from neural networks. Conditional PCs allow weights or leaf parameters to depend on context \(c\), producing \(p(x\mid c)\). For each fixed context, parameters must remain normalized and the circuit structure must preserve the query guarantees. Conditional SPNs combine conditional density estimation with circuit structure.

    A neural feature extractor before the circuit changes the modeled variable if it is non-invertible. Exact likelihood in feature space is not exact likelihood of raw observations. If the encoder is invertible, its Jacobian must be included; if it is stochastic, the result becomes a latent-variable model with an inference problem.

    Peer-reviewed research: Autoencoding Probabilistic Circuits, accepted by TMLR in 2025, jointly model data and probabilistic embeddings, infer embeddings with circuit queries, and attach a neural decoder using differentiable sampling. The paper reports reconstruction and missing-data robustness for its protocols. The tractable PC encoder does not make every neural-decoder likelihood or raw-space posterior query exact; the complete hybrid must be decomposed into its circuit and neural claims.

    Flow and Circuit Hybrids¤

    Probabilistic Flow Circuits augment circuit components with normalizing flows. Decomposability remains necessary to keep the combined model tractable. A flow can make leaf or component densities flexible, while the circuit supplies mixtures, factorizations, and exact high-level queries.

    This is hybrid composition, not an algebraic equivalence between PCs and flows. The flow handles a change of variables; the circuit handles reuse of sum/product structure.

    If a flow leaf covers scope \(S\), its change-of-variables density must be evaluated and marginalized only for queries the leaf supports. A full-scope flow leaf can give a tractable complete density yet destroy cheap arbitrary-subset marginals. "Every leaf has a likelihood" is weaker than "every leaf answers the marginal interface required by the parent circuit."

    Distillation from Deep Generative Models¤

    A teacher VAE, flow, or other deep model can suggest latent assignments or synthetic data for circuit learning. Liu et al. use Transformer-derived latent assignments and report substantial likelihood improvements over PCs trained without that supervision. Distillation does not transfer every teacher behavior or imply teacher dominance/reversal: conditioning, calibration, rare modes, and queries absent from the distillation loss require independent validation.

    Structured and Relational Domains¤

    Circuits extend beyond fixed image/tabular tensors. Sum-Product-Set Networks combine set semantics with circuits for tree-structured graphs. Logical circuits represent constraints exactly; tensor-network views expose connections to low-rank decompositions. Each extension must define variable scope and normalization for variable-size objects.

    The Sum-Product-Set work and Probabilistic Graph Circuits are preprint/workshop research as verified on 2026-07-12. Permutation invariance, graph validity, variable size, and tractable queries are separate requirements. Padding a graph into a fixed tensor can recover a conventional PC only if masks, node identity, and normalization semantics are explicit.

    The Random Graphs and Relational Generators volume places probabilistic graph circuits beside classical random-graph laws and other neural compositions, and separates exact circuit queries from graph-isomorphism and variable-cardinality requirements.

    Connections to Other Families¤

    • Mixtures/VAEs: sum nodes are discrete latent choices; exact circuit inference replaces a learned approximate encoder when structure permits.
    • Autoregressive models: both can have exact likelihood, but AR models normalize local conditionals while PCs compile reusable mixture/factorization structure.
    • Flows: both can evaluate exact densities under constraints; flows use invertible geometry, PCs use algebraic structure.
    • EBMs: a normalized PC can be written as energy \(E=-\log p\), but generic EBM composition does not preserve circuit tractability.
    • GFlowNets: a circuit sampling trace is a constructive process, but ordinary circuit MLE and GFlowNet reward-proportional training are different objectives.
    • Tensor-network and Born models: fixing a contraction order produces a sum-product circuit, while squared amplitudes produce squared circuits with different marginalization costs. The complete tensor/Born volume distinguishes circuit scopes and structural properties from MPS bond rank, gauges, and variable order.
    • Probabilistic grammars: an inside chart for one observation is an equivalent finite sum-product circuit under the chosen semiring; the recursive grammar is the reusable family-level specification, and cycles or unbounded length can prevent a finite tractable compilation. See Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations.

    Logical, tensor-network, and causal views¤

    Indicator leaves and deterministic sums make a PC resemble a weighted logical case decomposition. Compiling a Boolean theory into a deterministic structured circuit can make weighted model counting and constrained probabilistic queries efficient in the compiled size. The compilation step may be expensive, and an inconsistent theory gives zero mass rather than a usable conditional.

    Products and sums also resemble tensor contractions and low-rank factorizations. Einsum Networks exploit this computational view, but tensor rank, circuit width, vtree, and probabilistic normalization are different notions. A fast contraction order does not by itself supply normalized leaves or query-compatible scopes.

    Tensor-Network and Born Probability Models develops the other side of this relationship: nonnegative and amplitude-squared probability semantics, MPS canonical forms, exact conditional contractions, and the structural assumptions under which tensor factorizations and circuits support the same queries.

    Causal use requires another semantics layer. A joint observational PC can answer \(p(y\mid x)\); it does not identify \(p(y\mid\operatorname{do}(x))\). Causal circuits must encode or compose mechanisms and retain the structural properties required by the target interventional/counterfactual query. The relationship is hybrid system composition, not “tractable conditional equals causal effect.”

    Worked example: a flow leaf that preserves likelihood but loses a query¤

    Suppose a root mixture has two children, each a two-dimensional normalizing flow over \((X,Y)\). Complete density evaluation is tractable:

    \[ p(x,y)=\sum_{h=1}^2 w_h p_h(x,y). \]

    But the circuit query \(p(Y=y)=\sum_h w_h\int p_h(x,y)dx\) is tractable only if each flow component supplies that marginal. A generic coupled two-dimensional flow does not. Replacing independent univariate leaves with a full-scope flow increased local expressivity while breaking the leaf marginal interface on which Chapter 3 relied.

    One repair is to use decomposable flow leaves over separate scopes; another is a special flow family with tractable required marginals; a third is approximate integration, which must be labeled approximate. The parent sum/product graph alone cannot restore a query the leaf cannot answer.

    Counterexample: exact embeddings are not exact reconstructions

    A circuit may compute \(p(z\mid x_{\mathrm{obs}})\) exactly while a neural decoder maps \(z\) to a reconstruction. That output can be useful, but unless the decoder defines and supports the required normalized likelihood/inference operations, reconstruction is not an exact sample from a tractable raw-data conditional.

    Chapter 5 Synthesis¤

    Deep and hybrid PCs increase local expressivity while circuit structure protects only the queries implemented by every component. Worst-case approximation lower bounds explain why extra tractability can require extra size. Every hybrid must say which variable has exact density, which constraints survive, and which costs move into leaves, encoders, decoders, or compilation.

    6. Evaluation, Deployment, and Research Directions¤

    Capstone objectives¤

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. select a PC from a declared query workload;
    2. benchmark inference as a compiled system;
    3. distinguish exact arithmetic from calibration, robustness, causality, privacy, and correctness of the learned distribution;
    4. trace every research claim to its evidence maturity; and
    5. design an end-to-end study that tests both model quality and tractability.

    When a Probabilistic Circuit Is a Strong Choice¤

    Prefer a PC when the application repeatedly needs exact missing-data marginals, arbitrary evidence queries, conditionals, logical constraints, a tractable tabular density whose calibration is separately validated, or expectations that match a compatible circuit structure. They are especially compelling when query latency matters more than unconstrained perceptual sample quality.

    Do not choose a PC solely because “exact inference” sounds safer. A small misspecified circuit answers the wrong model exactly. High-dimensional raw perception may require large circuits, hybrid features, or a different family.

    Exactness also does not imply calibrated uncertainty or OOD robustness. Probabilistic Circuits That Know What They Don't Know demonstrates overconfidence and distribution-shift failures in standard PCs, then studies tractable uncertainty estimation. The UAI 2023 result is evidence that uncertainty must be evaluated, not a guarantee that its proposed estimator repairs every shift.

    Production Checklist¤

    1. Validate nonnegative normalized sum weights after serialization and quantization.
    2. Test smoothness, decomposability, determinism, and vtree conformity as executable contracts.
    3. Evaluate in log space or with scaling for deep circuits.
    4. Guard conditional queries when evidence probability is zero or extremely small.
    5. Version preprocessing, leaf support, variable ordering, and missing-value conventions.
    6. Benchmark the actual query mix; likelihood evaluation and conditional sampling have different costs.
    7. Trace hybrid feature transformations so “exact” is attached to the correct variable.

    Benchmark the compiled artifact, not the family name¤

    Circuit latency depends on more than node count. Record edges, active edges under determinism/evidence, leaf cost, tensor shapes, sparsity layout, batch size, dtype, compilation time, peak memory, device, and whether values are in probability or log space. Dense tensorization can execute nominally unused edges; sparse kernels can lose accelerator efficiency; a smaller irregular circuit can be slower than a larger regular one.

    Amortized compilation may be appropriate for repeated queries but misleading for a one-off query. Report cold and warm paths when both matter.

    A query-first selection procedure¤

    1. Write the required queries and approximation tolerance.
    2. Choose the weakest structural properties that guarantee them.
    3. Select leaf interfaces and state-space semantics.
    4. estimate or measure circuit size under those restrictions;
    5. compare against AR, flow, VAE, graphical-model, or approximate-inference baselines on the same workload; and
    6. reject the circuit route if compilation/learning size overwhelms its query advantage.

    This avoids demanding determinism or structured compatibility “just in case,” which can pay an expressivity cost without serving a real query.

    Research Directions¤

    Research directions include structure restructuring, data partitioning at scale, soft structure induction, minibatch EM with distribution-aware regularization, flow/circuit hybrids, circuit distillation, causal compositions, and circuits over structured objects. Exactness claims remain property-specific. Scaling a model while preserving smoothness/decomposability is not the same as making MAP, arbitrary circuit products, or causal effects tractable.

    The primary-source audit covers peer-reviewed work on soft learning, restructuring, data-partition scaling, and anemone parameter learning. Search results without clear archival acceptance remain excluded or explicitly labeled; the page metadata records the audit date.

    Compositional probabilistic and causal inference demonstrates how marginal determinism and structural formulations expand causal queries. Such results do not make observational PCs causal by default; the causal graph/mechanisms and identifiability assumptions remain necessary.

    Cumulative worked study: clinical records with arbitrary missingness¤

    Suppose a hospital wants a joint model of demographics, laboratory values, diagnoses, and outcomes for imputation and cohort queries.

    1. Semantics: declare categorical/counting and continuous/Lebesgue leaves, support transforms, units, and whether missingness indicators are modeled variables or query masks.
    2. Query workload: list joint likelihood, \(p(\text{outcome}\mid e)\), arbitrary lab marginals, conditional sampling, and cohort expectations. Do not request MAP, entropy, or causal effects without checking their extra properties.
    3. Structure: choose smooth/decomposable structure; require determinism only if its query benefit justifies size; choose a shared vtree when circuit products or policy/utility expectations are required.
    4. Learning: split patients rather than rows to avoid leakage; compare EM/gradient/anemone under fixed structure; nest structure and hyperparameter selection inside validation; record zero-flow handling.
    5. Evaluation: report held-out NLL in declared units, calibration, mask-pattern performance, multiple-imputation coverage, subgroup error, OOD shifts, circuit/leaf size, and query latency distributions.
    6. Safety boundary: observational conditionals are not treatment effects. Privacy needs an explicit mechanism/threat model. Exact inference cannot compensate for selection bias, unmeasured confounding, or invalid missingness assumptions.

    This study demonstrates the book's central rule: first declare variables and queries, then choose properties and leaves, then learn and benchmark the complete compiled artifact.

    Final Synthesis¤

    The density–latent–energy relationship guide compares circuit normalization and exact marginalization with AR factorization, invertible flows, and monolithic energy networks.

    Probabilistic circuits trade unrestricted architecture for an algebra in which selected queries compile to exact dynamic programs. Leaves model local distributions, sums mix alternatives, products factor disjoint scopes, and graph sharing reuses computation. Smoothness, decomposability, determinism, and structured decomposability are separate tools with separate guarantees. Modern neural, flow, relational, and distilled circuits expand local capacity, but the central research discipline remains unchanged: name the query, state the structural properties, and measure the circuit size required to keep it tractable.

    2. Why is complexity linear in circuit size not the same as tractability in the original problem description?
    3. Which property justifies ordinary marginal recursion, and which additional property can justify max-product MPE?
    4. Derive a normalized sum-edge responsibility from an upward value and downward derivative.
    5. Give two ways a hybrid can retain complete density but lose arbitrary marginals.

    Solutions. (1) Leaves integrate to one; a decomposable product's integral factors into child integrals; a smooth normalized sum integrates to the weighted sum of ones. (2) compilation or structural restrictions may produce an exponentially large circuit. (3) Smoothness plus decomposability supports marginals; suitable determinism plus leaf maximization supports MPE. (4) Divide \((\partial C/\partial v_n)w_{nc}v_c\) by the root value \(C(x)\). (5) A non-invertible neural encoder changes the modeled variable; a full-scope flow leaf may not expose subset marginals. (6) Calibration, causal validity, privacy, robustness, fairness, data representativeness, numerical safety, or that the learned distribution is correct.
