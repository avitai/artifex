    6. Evaluation, Applications, and Current Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–5 and the general protocol in Evaluating Generative Models. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Build a layered evaluation matrix: separate marginal, copula-validity, dependence, conditional, observed-joint, and compute claims.
    2. O2 — Diagnose marginal and residual validity: combine effect sizes, multiplicity-aware marginal checks, Rosenblatt uniformity, and independence tests.
    3. O3 — Evaluate tails and conditional queries: choose finite-threshold, limiting, calibration, and uncertainty diagnostics from the scientific target.
    4. O4 — Design controlled comparisons: align data, marginal transforms, observation handling, search budget, sampler, compute, and metric implementations.
    5. O5 — Recombine layers for decisions: propagate marginal and dependence uncertainty to observed-scale risks, interventions, or downstream losses without assuming shift invariance.
    6. O6 — Transfer a research protocol: bound a modern copula claim by publication status, reproducibility, effective sample size, application scope, and failure evidence.

    Evaluation has two axes before it has one score¤

    A fitted copula system contains at least a marginal model and a dependence model. Evaluate them separately and jointly.

    Table COP.5. Evaluation has two axes before it has one score
    Layer Question Examples
    Marginals on data scale Does each \(F_j\) fit its coordinate? held-out log score, PIT histogram, calibration, tail quantiles
    Copula validity Are generated \(U_j\) individually uniform? per-coordinate ECDF, KS/CvM tests, boundary mass, calibration error
    Dependence Does the joint rank structure fit? held-out copula log score, Kendall/Spearman matrices, tail events, energy/MMD tests
    Conditional law Are conditional distributions calibrated? Rosenblatt residuals, conditional coverage, interval scores
    Observed joint law Does composition answer the application query? full joint log score, scenario probabilities, downstream decision loss
    Computation What queries are feasible at what cost? fit time, density latency, conditional-query cost, MCMC ESS, sample throughput

    If a sample metric is computed directly on \(U\), marginal nonuniformity and dependence error are mixed. That can be useful as a system test but should not be mislabeled as a pure dependence score.

    Marginal and Rosenblatt diagnostics¤

    For generated copula samples, inspect each marginal ECDF against the identity and report uncertainty across coordinates. Hundreds of uncorrected marginal tests will produce false positives; use multiplicity-aware summaries and effect sizes. Conversely, averaging across coordinates can hide a few catastrophic marginals.

    For models with conditional CDFs, Rosenblatt residuals should be both uniform and mutually independent. Useful checks include:

    • univariate uniformity and boundary occupancy;
    • autocorrelation or serial tests when rows are time-indexed;
    • pairwise and multivariate independence tests;
    • repeated coordinate orders; and
    • calibration conditional on relevant covariates or regimes.

    Ranks computed from the same evaluation set are not known oracle PITs. Calibration envelopes should reproduce the estimation procedure.

    Dependence and tail evaluation¤

    No finite summary identifies an arbitrary high-dimensional copula. Use a panel tied to the scientific query:

    • held-out copula log likelihood when a normalized density exists;
    • pairwise and conditional rank-dependence matrices;
    • lower-, upper-, and mixed-corner exceedance probabilities at several finite thresholds;
    • multivariate return regions or stable-tail summaries for extreme-value applications;
    • two-sample tests on the copula scale;
    • conditional coverage and proper scores; and
    • application losses such as portfolio shortfall, flood coincidence, reliability failure, or multivariate forecast value.

    Tail coefficients are limits estimated from few effective observations. Report thresholds, estimators, confidence intervals, and sensitivity curves instead of a single unqualified number.

    Controlled model comparison¤

    A fair comparison holds constant or discloses:

    • train/validation/test rows and any temporal grouping;
    • marginal estimators and pseudo-observation convention;
    • tie, missingness, censoring, and dequantization treatment;
    • whether test marginals were refit or leaked;
    • conditional covariates and observation regimes;
    • parameter counts, structure search budget, and restarts;
    • sampling algorithm, NFE, MCMC burn-in, ESS, and wall-clock hardware;
    • density availability and numerical boundary clipping; and
    • metric implementation and uncertainty across data/model seeds.

    Comparing a vine using empirical marginals with a neural model using oracle marginals is not a pure comparison of dependence estimators. Neither is comparing direct IID samples with a short unverified MCMC chain.

    Complete worked protocol: multivariate environmental risk¤

    Suppose \(X\) contains river level, rainfall, soil moisture, and wind at aligned times.

    1. Split by time blocks, not random rows, to avoid leaking seasonal episodes.
    2. Fit conditional marginals using season and location, then inspect PIT calibration and extreme quantiles on held-out blocks.
    3. Fit candidate copulas—for example Gaussian, \(t\), a truncated vine, and a marginal-preserving neural model—using identical pseudo-observations.
    4. Validate copula margins for every sampler. Reject claims of copula validity if coordinate ECDFs materially deviate from uniformity.
    5. Evaluate dependence using held-out copula log score where available, rank matrices, multiple joint-exceedance thresholds, and Rosenblatt residuals.
    6. Evaluate the decision query: estimate the probability that river level and rainfall exceed safety thresholds while wind impedes response. Use bootstrap intervals that refit marginals and copulas by time block.
    7. Stress the invariance assumption by season and climate regime. If dependence changes, a fixed copula with replaced marginals is not supported.

    The best central log score and the best rare-event decision estimate can belong to different models. The protocol makes that disagreement visible rather than forcing one universal ranking.

    Independent practice and assessment map: copula evaluation¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. A Gaussian copula and a \(t_4\) copula share \(\rho=0.5\) and therefore the same Gaussian-family rank-concordance mapping. Predict whether matching the pairwise Kendall matrix establishes matching upper-tail risk, then support the answer with their tail coefficients.
    2. P1.2 — Quantify multiplicity. Type: derive · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. If 100 truly uniform generated marginals are each tested at level \(0.05\), compute the expected number of false rejections and the Bonferroni per-test level for familywise level \(0.05\). State why neither calculation replaces effect sizes.
    3. P1.3 — Audit a rare-event estimate. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. With 1,000 independent test rows and true joint-exceedance probability \(0.01\), compute the expected exceedance count and the standard error of the empirical probability. Explain the consequence for fine model rankings.
    4. P1.4 — Refuse a universal ranking. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Model A has better coordinatewise uniformity but worse copula-scale two-sample error than Model B. State what can be concluded without a target query and design two follow-up comparisons that isolate the disagreement.
    5. P1.5 — Normalize sampler evidence. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 8 min. An MCMC copula sampler returns 10,000 post-burn-in draws with integrated autocorrelation time 20. Compute its approximate ESS and state what must accompany raw samples-per-second when comparing it with direct IID sampling.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Write a preregistered evaluation protocol for seasonal compound-flood risk comparing Gaussian, Student-\(t\), vine, and neural copulas. Include splits, marginals, tails, conditional diagnostics, uncertainty, compute, shift, and evidence-maturity boundaries.
    Feedback contract: copula evaluation¤
    Table COP.22. Feedback contract: copula evaluation — table 1
    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 Matching central/rank summaries are separated from the zero-versus-positive asymptotic tail coefficients.
    P1.2 Both expected false positives and adjusted level are correct, with multiplicity kept distinct from practical calibration error.

    Table COP.23. Feedback contract: copula evaluation — table 2

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. Matching \(\rho\) or Kendall matrices does not establish matching tail risk. The nondegenerate Gaussian copula has \(\lambda_U=0\), whereas the \(t_4\) copula at \(\rho=0.5\) has \(\lambda_U\approx0.253\). Check. Finite-threshold risks also require direct evaluation; the limiting coefficients alone do not rank every operational threshold.
    2. P1.2. The expected false-rejection count is \(100(0.05)=5\). The Bonferroni level is \(0.05/100=0.0005\) per coordinate. Check. Adjustment controls a testing error criterion; coordinatewise deviations and confidence bands still need effect sizes because a tiny detectable error may be harmless while an underpowered large error may be important.
    3. P1.3. The expected count is \(1000(0.01)=10\). The standard error is \(\sqrt{0.01(0.99)/1000}\approx0.00315\), almost one third of the target probability. Check. Small score differences at this threshold are not credible without more tail information, structured estimators, or intervals that expose the uncertainty.
    4. P1.4. Without a target query there is no universal winner: A better satisfies the marginal contract, while B better matches the measured joint sample geometry. First re-uniformize both generated samples with a common transparent procedure and compare dependence diagnostics; then compose both dependence models with identical validated marginals and compare observed-scale target scores. Rubric. Full credit reports any correction as part of the system rather than retroactively calling the original sampler valid.
    5. P1.5. \(\operatorname{ESS}\approx10000/20=500\). Compare effective samples per second, not raw draws per second, and report burn-in, initialization, multiple-chain diagnostics, autocorrelation estimates, and target accuracy. Check. A direct sampler's IID claim also needs verification when it includes learned calibration or rejection steps.
    6. P1.6. A defensible protocol preregisters a compound threshold or proper decision score; uses blocked train/validation/test years and seasonal/location covariates; fits identical conditional marginals and pseudo-observation conventions; compares central, finite-tail, Rosenblatt, and observed-risk diagnostics; refits every stage in a blocked bootstrap; matches search budget, seeds, hardware, and sampler accounting; reports marginal validity and ESS/NFE separately; tests season and climate-regime shift; and labels conference results, independent reproduction, and application transfer distinctly. Rubric. Full credit makes it impossible to improve a “copula” score through test-marginal leakage or unmatched oracle marginals.

    The ICLR 2026 work reports a classification-diffusion copula for density estimation and a reflection construction for sampling, with explicit marginal-uniformity diagnostics in its experiments. It is evidence that probability paths can be designed around the copula constraint. It is not evidence that marginal validity can be checked once and then assumed for every learned solver, dataset, or discretization.

    Open research questions¤

    1. How can marginal preservation be exact under finite neural approximation and finite-step solvers?
    2. Which non-simplified conditional structures scale beyond moderate-dimensional vines?
    3. How should uncertainty from learned marginals, copula structure selection, and neural training be propagated jointly?
    4. Which diagnostics retain power for rare multivariate tails without imposing the tested family?
    5. How should copulas be defined and identified for mixed, censored, functional, graph-valued, or manifold-valued coordinates?
    6. When is dependence stable enough across environments that marginal replacement is scientifically defensible?

    Chapter 6 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Passing all coordinatewise uniformity tests does not validate dependence.
    • Matching a Kendall-tau matrix does not identify the joint copula or its tails.
    • A lower Wasserstein distance can reward marginal correction rather than better dependence.
    • Synthetic-data utility does not establish privacy; privacy needs a formal mechanism and accounting.
    • Holding a copula fixed across a shift is an empirical and scientific assumption, not a consequence of Sklar's theorem.
    • An accepted current paper is peer-reviewed research, not established doctrine.
    Retrieval questions
    1. Why should marginal validity and dependence fit be reported separately?
    2. What two properties should Rosenblatt residuals have?
    3. Why must tail thresholds and uncertainty be reported?
    4. What makes a comparison between a direct sampler and MCMC incomplete?
    5. What does a successful marginal-preserving diffusion construction establish—and what does it not establish?

    Solutions. (1) Either can fail while the other succeeds, and many joint metrics mix the two errors. (2) Uniform marginals and mutual independence, with any serial/covariate structure also checked. (3) Tail coefficients are limits inferred from few effective observations, so estimates depend strongly on threshold and sampling variability. (4) MCMC needs transition count, burn-in, convergence, autocorrelation, and ESS, whereas direct sampling has a different cost contract. (5) It establishes validity for the stated process under its assumptions; it does not make arbitrary diffusions, learned approximations, or solvers copulas.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Copula evaluation begins by separating marginal calibration from dependence fit and ends by recombining them for the actual observed-scale decision. Conditional residuals, tail queries, computation, and shift tests are part of the model contract. Current neural work broadens valid constructions, but it does not relax Sklar's uniqueness clauses, discrete likelihoods, or marginal-preservation requirements.
