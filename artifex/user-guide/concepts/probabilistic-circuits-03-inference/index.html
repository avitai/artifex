    3. Exact Inference by Upward and Downward Passes¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes a smooth, decomposable normalized circuit unless a stronger property is stated. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. compute evidence, marginals, and conditionals with upward messages;
    2. sample unconditionally and conditionally using posterior sum-edge probabilities;
    3. derive downward sensitivities and normalized responsibilities;
    4. implement stable log-domain evaluation without confusing log-sum-exp with a new circuit semantics; and
    5. distinguish MPE from marginal MAP and recognize zero-evidence failure cases.

    Marginalization as Leaf Substitution¤

    For discrete indicator leaves, a complete assignment selects indicator values. To marginalize an unobserved variable, set all of its indicators to one. More generally, replace a leaf by its integral over unobserved coordinates. Smoothness and decomposability ensure the root then equals the desired marginal.

    For evidence \(e\) on observed variables \(E\), compute \(C(e)=p(E=e)\). A conditional follows from

    \[ p(Q=q\mid E=e)=\frac{C(q,e)}{C(e)}. \]

    This is exact arithmetic inference, not amortized imputation. Numerical underflow remains possible, so log-space evaluation or scaled messages are important for deep circuits.

    In log space, product nodes add child log-values and sum nodes use

    \[ \log v_n=\operatorname{logsumexp}_c(\log w_{nc}+\log v_c). \]

    Zero-valued branches map to \(-\infty\). Conditional probabilities should be formed by subtracting log evidence and exponentiating only when needed. If \(C(e)=0\), the model conditional is undefined; clipping the denominator silently invents a different distribution.

    For a mixed query, each leaf needs an explicit interface:

    • point mass or density for observed coordinates;
    • integral over omitted coordinates;
    • conditional sampler for unobserved coordinates given any observed coordinates in the same multivariate leaf; and
    • optionally a maximizer and maximum value for MPE.

    Univariate normalized leaves make the marginal interface trivial: an unobserved leaf returns one. Multivariate leaves can be more expressive, but their internal marginal and conditional problems become part of total query cost.

    Worked Example: Marginal and Conditional Query¤

    Let a circuit over binary \(X,Y\) be

    \[ p(x,y)=0.6\,p_A(x)p_A(y)+0.4\,p_B(x)p_B(y), \]

    with \(p_A(X=1)=0.8\), \(p_A(Y=1)=0.7\), \(p_B(X=1)=0.2\), and \(p_B(Y=1)=0.1\).

    The joint event is

    \[ p(X=1,Y=1)=0.6(0.8)(0.7)+0.4(0.2)(0.1)=0.344. \]

    Marginalizing \(X\) means each branch's \(X\) factor integrates to one:

    \[ p(Y=1)=0.6(0.7)+0.4(0.1)=0.46. \]

    Therefore

    \[ p(X=1\mid Y=1)=\frac{0.344}{0.46}\approx0.748. \]

    Although \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent within each product component, observing \(Y\) updates the posterior mixture weight and therefore changes the distribution of \(X\).

    The posterior component weight for branch A is \(0.6(0.7)/0.46\approx0.913\). Conditional sampling first draws the branch from \((0.913,0.087)\), then samples \(X\) from that branch's Bernoulli. This produces the same \(p(X\mid Y=1)\) as the ratio calculation; it is not ancestral sampling from the original weights \((0.6,0.4)\).

    Sampling¤

    At a product node, sample every child because scopes are disjoint. At a sum node, sample one child according to its mixture weight for unconditional sampling. Under evidence, weights become posterior branch probabilities proportional to

    \[ w_{nc}\,v_c(e). \]

    Then recursively sample unobserved leaves conditioned on evidence. This yields exact conditional samples when the needed evaluations and leaf conditionals are exact.

    A robust conditional sampler performs one evidence pass, caches every child evidence value, then traverses top-down:

    values = upward_evidence_pass(e)
visit(root)
  product: visit every child
  sum: draw child c with probability weight[c] * values[c] / values[parent]
  leaf: draw missing coordinates from the leaf conditional

    The quotient assumes values[parent] > 0. For a DAG, one sampled trace visits the chosen descendants; cached upward values can be reused across samples, while random choices must remain independent unless coupled sampling is intended.

    Derivatives and Responsibilities¤

    Let the root value be \(C(x)\). A downward pass computes \(\partial C/\partial v_n\) for every node. For an edge from sum node \(n\) to child \(c\), its posterior responsibility is proportional to

    \[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial v_n}\,w_{nc}v_c(x). \]

    Automatic differentiation implements this calculation, but the probabilistic interpretation depends on the normalized circuit semantics. Gradients are not evidence of a valid posterior if the graph violates those semantics.

    For a normalized complete-data circuit, divide by the root value to obtain the expected use or flow of that edge under the posterior trace:

    \[ \gamma_{nc}(x)=\frac{1}{C(x)} \frac{\partial C(x)}{\partial v_n}\,w_{nc}v_c(x). \]

    Shared DAG nodes can receive flow from several parents, which is why the downward derivative is essential; a local softmax over one sum node is insufficient when the probability of reaching that node also depends on upstream choices.

    Marginal MAP Is a Warning Case¤

    MPE maximizes all unobserved variables. Marginal MAP maximizes some variables while summing out others:

    \[ \arg\max_y\sum_z p(y,z,e). \]

    Max and sum do not generally commute, so replacing every sum by max is wrong. Specialized transformations can solve cases on suitable PCs; see Choi and Friedman's exact marginal-MAP transformations. The lesson generalizes: “exact inference” is never one undifferentiated capability.

    For a concrete counterexample, let \(p(a,0)=0.30\), \(p(a,1)=0.30\), \(p(b,0)=0.39\), and \(p(b,1)=0.01\). MPE chooses \((b,0)\) because \(0.39\) is the largest complete-assignment mass. Marginal MAP over the first variable chooses \(a\) because \(p(a)=0.60>p(b)=0.40\). Maximizing before summing returns the wrong query.

    Beyond marginals: expectations and circuit operations¤

    Moments can be computed by expectation semirings or by combining the distribution circuit with a compatible function circuit. Entropy, cross-entropy, KL, products, quotients, and powers require operation-specific closure and compatibility conditions. The compositional atlas supplies algorithms and hardness boundaries. It is incorrect to infer that because \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are each tractable, \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)\) is automatically tractable.

    Independent practice and assessment map: exact circuit inference¤

    Use a finite enumeration as an oracle for every optimized circuit pass. Report whether the requested result is a point value, marginal, conditional, sample, MPE assignment, marginal-MAP assignment, derivative, or expectation.

    P1 — Reconcile upward, downward, and sampling calculations¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each procedure is valid: set every unobserved normalized univariate leaf to one; sample a sum child from prior weights after observing evidence; clip a zero evidence denominator and call the result exact; replace all sums by maxima for marginal MAP; interpret an automatic-differentiation edge gradient as a posterior responsibility in any arithmetic DAG; and compute KL from two individually tractable circuits without checking compatibility. Name the structural, normalization, or numerical condition behind every answer.
    2. P1.2 — Upward evidence oracle. Let \(p(x,y)=0.25\,p_A(x)p_A(y)+0.75\,p_B(x)p_B(y)\) with \(p_A(X=1)=0.9\), \(p_A(Y=1)=0.3\), \(p_B(X=1)=0.2\), and \(p_B(Y=1)=0.8\). Compute all four joint masses, \(p(X=1)\), \(p(X=1,Y=1)\), and \(p(Y=1\mid X=1)\) by enumeration and by leaf substitution. Verify normalization and retain exact fractions or sufficient precision to expose a reduction error.
    3. P1.3 — Conditional-sampling trace. For evidence \(X=1\) in P1.2, compute the posterior branch probabilities, then derive the distribution used to sample \(Y\). Simulate or enumerate enough conditional draws to compare with the analytic conditional. Explain why drawing the branch from \((0.25,0.75)\) would be ancestral sampling from the wrong conditional and specify which cached upward values are reusable across draws.
    4. P1.4 — Downward responsibility check. Derive \(\partial C/\partial v_n\) and the normalized edge flows for the root mixture in P1.2 at \((X,Y)=(1,1)\). Extend the graph so one subcircuit has two parents, compute its total downward sensitivity as the sum of both paths, and compare with automatic differentiation. Show why a local child softmax alone omits the probability of reaching the shared node.
    5. P1.5 — Numerical and query failure suite. Implement or specify tests for log-sum-exp at extreme log weights, exactly zero evidence, rare nonzero evidence, and a marginal-MAP table whose MPE projection is wrong. For each case, state the expected log value, assignment, error type, and permitted behavior; distinguish an explicit smoothing model change from denominator clipping.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Transfer the inference interface to a multivariate Gaussian leaf with one observed and one missing coordinate, or to a categorical sequence leaf with a constrained suffix. Define point evaluation, subset marginal, conditional sampler, and maximizer costs. Identify which parent-circuit queries remain exact and which become approximate or unavailable because of the new leaf.

    Chapter 3 Synthesis¤

    The circuit evaluates evidence upward, propagates sensitivities downward, and can reuse these computations for marginals, posterior branch weights, samples, and sufficient statistics. Exactness remains conditional on the query and compiled structure; numerical stability and zero-probability evidence remain runtime concerns. Chapter 4 asks how the graph and parameters are learned.

    Retrieval practice: exact circuit inference
    1. What replaces an unobserved normalized univariate leaf during marginalization?
    2. How does evidence change a sum node's sampling probabilities?
    3. Why does the downward pass matter in a DAG with shared subcircuits?
    4. Why is marginal MAP harder than MPE?
    5. What should code do when \(C(e)=0\)?

    Solutions. (1) Its integral, usually one. (2) Reweight each edge by its prior weight times the child evidence value, then normalize. (3) It aggregates the probability or sensitivity of reaching a shared node through all parents. (4) Maximization and summation do not commute, so local max replacements can optimize a different expression. (5) Report that the model conditional is undefined or use an explicitly documented smoothing/model change; do not hide it with denominator clipping.

    Bridge. Upward/downward messages expose latent sufficient statistics. Chapter 4 turns them into gradient, EM, and structure-learning procedures while keeping optimization claims distinct from inference guarantees. Continue continuously or open Chapter 4.
