    A statistical generator answers questions about patterns it was trained to represent. A causal generative model additionally specifies how those patterns arise from mechanisms, how the distribution changes when a mechanism is intervened on, and—under stronger assumptions—what would have happened to the same unit under an unrealized intervention.

    That extra semantics does not come from choosing a VAE, GAN, flow, diffusion model, or Transformer. Those are parameterizations, objectives, or samplers. Causal meaning comes from a structural model plus assumptions and data that connect its variables and mechanisms to interventions. This book develops that distinction from finite structural causal models to deep counterfactual generators, causal representation learning, mechanism shift, causal discovery, and the current research record.

    Book-length routes into the subject¤

    No single causal-inference tradition supplies every layer needed by a causal generator. Use these routes as complements, chosen by the query you need to understand:

    • Accessible empirical route: Cunningham's Causal Inference: The Mixtape connects graphs and potential outcomes to concrete designs and code. It is a practitioner bridge, not a substitute for structural identification theory.
    • Potential-outcome and study-design route: Imbens and Rubin's Causal Inference for Statistics, Social, and Biomedical Sciences develops assignment mechanisms, randomized experiments, observational designs, matching, propensity scores, and instrumental variables.
    • Longitudinal and policy route: Hernán and Robins's freely available Causal Inference: What If progresses from causal effects without models through estimation and then complex longitudinal data, including time-varying treatments and g-methods.
    • Structural and graphical route: Pearl's Causality is the advanced reference for structural models, interventions, counterfactuals, graphical criteria, and do-calculus.
    • Machine-learning route: Peters, Janzing, and Schölkopf's open-access Elements of Causal Inference is a concise bridge from statistics and machine learning to structural models and discovery, with exercises and code.
    • Discovery route: Spirtes, Glymour, and Scheines's Causation, Prediction, and Search develops equivalence, manipulation, constraint-based search, and latent- variable discovery in depth.

    These books disagree in notation and emphasis. Translate each result into this volume's contract before transferring it: causal object, target population, query, graph or structural class, available regimes, identification argument, estimator, and uncertainty claim.

    The governing rule

    A good fit to \(p(x)\), a disentangled-looking latent space, or a plausible edited sample does not by itself identify \(p(y\mid do(x))\) or an individual counterfactual. Every causal query must state the graph, structural assumptions, available regimes, and identification argument.

    1. Structural Causal Models and Three Kinds of Query¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Joint/conditional probability, DAGs, and latent-variable models.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Specify structural semantics: name endogenous variables, exogenous variables, mechanisms, graph, and exogenous dependence for an SCM.
    2. O2 — Separate four query operations: distinguish observation, intervention, abduction, and unit counterfactual prediction in one model.
    3. O3 — Compute the finite running model: reproduce its observational, interventional, abductive, and counterfactual probabilities exactly.
    4. O4 — Connect SCMs and potential outcomes: use consistency while keeping marginal potential-outcome laws distinct from their cross-world coupling.
    5. O5 — Audit adjustment and support: derive truncated factorization or back-door adjustment only when its graphical and positivity conditions hold.
    6. O6 — Transfer beyond acyclic IID units: state how cycles, time, treatment versions, interference, or measurement change the causal query.

    Variables, mechanisms, and exogenous uncertainty¤

    A structural causal model (SCM) contains endogenous variables \(X_1,\ldots,X_d\), exogenous variables \(U_1,\ldots,U_d\), structural assignments, and an exogenous distribution:

    \[ X_i=f_i(X_{\operatorname{pa}(i)},U_i), \qquad U\sim p_U. \]

    The parents \(X_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}\) are direct causes according to the model. In an acyclic Markovian SCM, each endogenous variable has its own mutually independent exogenous disturbance and the observational distribution factorizes as

    \[ p(x_1,\ldots,x_d)=\prod_{i=1}^{d}p(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}). \]

    The converse is false: writing a density as a DAG factorization does not prove that its arrows are causal. The same observational distribution can often be represented by several directed graphs, while hidden common causes can make an observed DAG inadequate.

    Correlated exogenous variables encode latent confounding in a semi-Markovian representation and generally replace the simple observed-DAG factorization with a latent-variable marginal. “Causal sufficiency,” “independent errors,” “acyclicity,” and “no selection bias” are related but distinct assumptions and should be declared separately.

    Observation, intervention, and counterfactual¤

    The three queries use related but different probability objects.

    1. Observational: \(p(Y\mid X=x)\). What tends to co-occur with \(X=x\)?
    2. Interventional: \(p(Y\mid do(X=x))\). What distribution follows when the mechanism assigning \(X\) is replaced by \(X:=x\)?
    3. Counterfactual: \(p(Y_{x'}\mid X=x,Y=y)\). For the same unit whose evidence was observed, what would \(Y\) have been under \(X:=x'\)?

    The same structural program answers all four operations below; changing the operation changes which part of the program is restricted or replaced.

    An atomic intervention produces a mutilated SCM:

    \[ X:=x, \]

    and removes incoming arrows into \(X\). For an acyclic causally sufficient graph, the truncated factorization is

    \[ p(x_1,\ldots,x_d\mid do(X_k=a)) =\mathbb 1[x_k=a]\prod_{i\ne k}p(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}). \]

    This is a change to the data-generating process, not ordinary conditioning on the event \(X_k=a\).

    The intervention distribution can also be obtained by simulating the modified equations: sample exogenous variables from their original law, set the intervened variable, and propagate descendants in causal order. Non-descendants remain governed by their original mechanisms. This operational semantics is what a modular deep generator must reproduce.

    Running finite SCM: four queries, one data-generating program¤

    We will use one finite SCM throughout Chapters 1 and 2 so that the query—not a change of example—accounts for every change in the answer. Let \(C\) be an observed baseline variable, \(T\) a binary treatment, and \(Y\) a binary outcome. Let the mutually independent exogenous variables be

    \[ \begin{aligned} U_C&\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}\!\left(\tfrac12\right),\\ U_T&\sim\operatorname{Uniform}\{0,1,2,3\},\\ U_Y&\sim\operatorname{Uniform}\{0,1,\ldots,7\}. \end{aligned} \]

    The structural assignments are

    \[ \begin{aligned} C &= U_C,\\ T &= \mathbb 1[U_T < 1+2C],\\ Y &= \mathbb 1[U_Y < 1+4T+2C]. \end{aligned} \]

    Thus \(C\) affects both treatment and outcome, treatment is more common when \(C=1\), and treatment raises the outcome threshold in either baseline stratum. The graph is \(C\to T\), \(C\to Y\), and \(T\to Y\). This is a fully specified causal model: its equations define not only a joint distribution but also what is retained and what is replaced under an intervention.

    The four parent configurations have exact probabilities:

    Table CAUSAL.2. Running finite SCM: four queries, one data-generating program
    Baseline \(C\) Treatment \(T\) Joint probability \(P(C,T)\) Outcome probability \(P(Y=1\mid C,T)\) Joint outcome probability \(P(C,T,Y=1)\)
    0 0 \(3/8\) \(1/8\) \(3/64\)
    0 1 \(1/8\) \(5/8\) \(5/64\)
    1 0 \(1/8\) \(3/8\) \(3/64\)
    1 1 \(3/8\) \(7/8\) \(21/64\)

    Observation: select through the existing treatment mechanism¤

    Because \(P(T=1)=P(T=0)=1/2\), conditioning the table gives

    \[ P(Y=1\mid T=1)=\frac{26/64}{1/2}=\frac{13}{16}, \qquad P(Y=1\mid T=0)=\frac{6/64}{1/2}=\frac{3}{16}. \]

    The associational difference is \(5/8\). This comparison mixes the treatment response with the fact that the treated group contains more units with \(C=1\), whose outcome threshold is already higher.

    Intervention: replace the treatment mechanism¤

    Under \(do(T=1)\), the assignment for \(T\) is replaced, while \(C\) and \(U_Y\) retain their population laws. Standardizing the two outcome mechanisms over the original \(P(C)\) gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} P(Y=1\mid do(T=1)) &=\tfrac12\!\left(\tfrac58\right)+\tfrac12\!\left(\tfrac78\right) =\tfrac34,\\ P(Y=1\mid do(T=0)) &=\tfrac12\!\left(\tfrac18\right)+\tfrac12\!\left(\tfrac38\right) =\tfrac14. \end{aligned} \]

    The average treatment effect is therefore \(1/2\), not the observational difference \(5/8\). In particular,

    \[ P(Y=1\mid T=1)=\frac{13}{16} \ne P(Y=1\mid do(T=1))=\frac{12}{16}. \]

    A conditional generator trained for \(P(Y\mid T=1)\) reproduces the selected treated population. It does not, merely by accepting \(T=1\) as context, simulate the population after replacement of the treatment mechanism. Under the stated graph, an observational conditional model can contribute to the adjustment formula by estimating \(P(Y\mid T,C)\); causal identification and standardization over \(P(C)\) are additional steps.

    Abduction: update the exogenous state from factual evidence¤

    Now consider a unit observed with

    \[ E=\{C=1,T=0,Y=0\}. \]

    The equations imply \(U_C=1\), \(U_T=3\), and

    \[ U_Y\mid E\sim\operatorname{Uniform}\{3,4,5,6,7\}. \]

    Abduction does not yet change the world. It computes a posterior over the unit-specific exogenous state compatible with the factual record.

    Counterfactual: act and predict with the abducted state¤

    For the same unit under \(T:=1\), retain the abducted \(U_Y\) and evaluate the modified outcome equation:

    \[ Y_1=\mathbb 1[U_Y<7]. \]

    Four of the five posterior noise values, \(3,4,5,6\), produce success. The threshold SCM therefore answers

    \[ P(Y_1=1\mid C=1,T=0,Y=0)=\frac45. \]

    This differs from the stratum-level intervention probability \(P(Y=1\mid do(T=1),C=1)=7/8\) because the factual failure has already updated the unit's outcome noise. The value \(4/5\) is a valid answer conditional on this SCM's shared-noise coupling. It is not identified by the observational or interventional marginals alone; Chapter 2 constructs another SCM that agrees on those marginals and gives a different unit answer.

    Table CAUSAL.3. Counterfactual: act and predict with the abducted state
    Operation Object in the running SCM What changes?
    observe \(P(Y\mid T=1)\) beliefs are restricted to units selected by the existing \(T\) mechanism
    intervene \(P(Y\mid do(T=1))\) the \(T\) assignment is replaced; population exogenous variables retain their original law
    abduct \(P(U\mid C=1,T=0,Y=0)\) beliefs about factual unit noise change; no mechanism changes
    counterfactual \(P(Y_1\mid C=1,T=0,Y=0)\) the \(T\) mechanism changes while the abducted unit noise is retained
    Running-model retrieval
    1. Why does \(13/16\) differ from \(3/4\)?
    2. Why is \(U_Y\) not resampled before computing the unit counterfactual?
    3. Which part of the calculation comes from causal identification rather than conditional-density estimation?

    Solutions. (1) Conditioning on \(T=1\) changes the distribution of the common cause \(C\); intervention retains the population distribution \(P(C)\). (2) Resampling would describe another unit from the intervened population, not the same factual unit. (3) The graph and causal assumptions justify replacing the interventional law by the standardized functional \(\sum_c P(Y\mid T,C=c)P(C=c)\); a conditional estimator only estimates terms in that functional.

    Worked confounding example¤

    Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) be a common cause of treatment \(X\) and outcome \(Y\):

    \[ X=Z, \qquad Y=X\oplus Z, \]

    where \(\oplus\) is exclusive OR. Observationally, \(X=Z\) always, so \(Y=0\) always:

    \[ p(Y=1\mid X=0)=p(Y=1\mid X=1)=0. \]

    Under \(do(X=1)\), however, \(Z\) keeps its original distribution and

    \[ Y=1\oplus Z, \]

    so \(p(Y=1\mid do(X=1))=1/2\). Conditioning selected units through the old \(X=Z\) mechanism; intervention broke that mechanism.

    This deterministic example deliberately violates positivity: within a fixed \(Z=z\), only \(X=z\) is observed. Therefore it demonstrates that conditioning and intervention differ, but the interventional effect is not recoverable from these observational data by ordinary back-door adjustment. A model that outputs \(1/2\) uses the stipulated SCM, not identification from \(p(X,Y,Z)\) alone.

    When an observed set \(Z\) satisfies an appropriate back-door criterion, the interventional effect is identified by

    \[ p(y\mid do(x))=\sum_z p(y\mid x,z)p(z). \]

    This adjustment is not universally valid. Descendants of treatment, colliders, incomplete confounder sets, measurement error, and selection can invalidate it.

    Adjustment additionally requires overlap/positivity for the treatment levels within relevant confounder strata. When positivity is weak rather than exactly absent, finite estimators can have extreme variance even though the population functional is formally identified.

    Potential Outcomes and the Consistency Bridge¤

    SCM interventions induce potential outcomes \(Y_x\). Under consistency, the observed outcome equals the potential outcome under the treatment actually received:

    \[ X=x\quad\Longrightarrow\quad Y=Y_x. \]

    Randomized treatment identifies the marginal laws of \(Y_0\) and \(Y_1\) and hence the average treatment effect

    \[ \operatorname{ATE}=\mathbb E[Y_1]-\mathbb E[Y_0]. \]

    It does not generally identify the joint coupling \(p(Y_0,Y_1)\) because one unit reveals at most one outcome. Quantities such as the variance of \(Y_1-Y_0\), probability of individual benefit, or a unit's missing outcome require additional structural, monotonicity, rank-preservation, or parametric assumptions. A counterfactual generator always chooses such a coupling, whether or not it admits doing so.

    SUTVA is not independent exogenous noise. The stable unit treatment value assumption combines a well-defined treatment or consistency condition with no interference between units under the stated exposure mapping. Mutual independence of SCM disturbances is instead a Markovian structural assumption; it concerns latent confounding within the model. Either condition can hold without the other. When treatments have hidden versions or one unit's treatment changes another unit's outcome, potential outcomes must be indexed by the relevant version or joint exposure rather than repaired by assuming independent noise. See Rubin's original formulation and the multiple-treatment-versions analysis.

    Counterfactuals by abduction, action, prediction¤

    Counterfactual inference preserves unit-specific exogenous information:

    1. Abduction: infer \(p(u\mid x_{\mathrm{obs}})\) from factual evidence.
    2. Action: replace the intervened structural equations.
    3. Prediction: propagate the same sampled \(u\) through the modified model.

    If a learned structural map is invertible, abduction may recover \(u\) directly. Otherwise it requires a posterior, often approximated variationally or by MCMC. Resampling fresh exogenous noise after intervention gives a population interventional sample, not the counterfactual outcome for the same unit.

    A minimal numerical counterfactual¤

    Consider

    \[ X=U_X,\qquad Y=2X+U_Y, \]

    with independent zero-mean noises. For a unit observed at \((X,Y)=(3,7)\), abduction gives \(U_Y=1\). Under the counterfactual action \(X:=5\), prediction with the same noise yields

    \[ Y_{5}=2(5)+1=11. \]

    A new interventional draw would instead sample a new \(U_Y\) and would not answer the individual question.

    This numerical answer assumes the coefficient 2 and additive-noise mechanism are known and correctly specified. The factual point alone cannot identify that structural equation. Counterfactual computation conditional on an SCM and counterfactual identification from data are separate steps.

    Cycles, time, and interference¤

    Feedback systems may use equilibrium SCMs, dynamic SCMs, or stochastic differential equations rather than an acyclic one-shot graph. With interference, one unit's outcome depends on other units' treatments, so ordinary unit-level potential-outcome notation is incomplete. The model must state timing, equilibrium selection, and the intervention target. “Causal” does not license silently applying acyclic formulas to every dynamical system.

    Chapter 1 retrieval Questions¤

    1. What is physically changed by \(do(X=x)\)?
    2. Why does the XOR example not justify back-door estimation from its observational sample?
    3. What does an RCT identify about \((Y_0,Y_1)\), and what remains missing?
    Complete solutions
    1. The structural mechanism assigning \(X\) is replaced; the exogenous law and other mechanisms remain unless the intervention definition says otherwise.
    2. Treatment is deterministic within each confounder stratum, so positivity fails and the needed off-support conditionals are not observed.
    3. It identifies the two marginal potential-outcome laws under randomization, but not their within-unit joint coupling.

    Independent practice and assessment map: causal semantics¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. A causal answer is complete only when its assumptions and probability object are visible.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    An SCM couples mechanisms with exogenous uncertainty. Observation conditions within the existing system, intervention changes a mechanism, and counterfactual inference changes a mechanism while retaining factual unit information. Chapter 2 asks when any of these hidden structures are recoverable from data.

    If a generator exactly learns the observational joint, has it learned the causal model? No. Observationally equivalent SCMs may agree on \(p(x)\) and disagree on interventions or counterfactuals.

    Estimation begins only after identification¤

    A causal quantity is identifiable when all causal models compatible with the stated assumptions and observed distribution agree on that quantity. An estimator can converge perfectly and still estimate the wrong target if identification fails. More neural capacity cannot repair missing causal information.

    Identification is query-specific. A graph may be only partially oriented while one average effect is still identified; conversely, knowing the DAG may not identify a unit counterfactual when latent response coupling is unspecified. Distinguish:

    • graph/Markov-equivalence identification;
    • interventional-distribution identification;
    • counterfactual-functional identification;
    • latent representation identification up to a named transformation group;
    • finite-sample estimation and computation.

    Identification-before-estimation decision sequence¤

    The order of work is a correctness condition, not editorial preference:

    An estimand is the mathematical target; an identification formula rewrites it using available distributions; an estimator is a finite-sample algorithm for that formula. If step 4 fails, a larger generator or lower optimization loss estimates an assumption-chosen completion, not the missing causal information. If step 6 fails, a population formula may exist while the available data provide no stable support for it.

    From adjustment to graphical identification¤

    Back-door adjustment is one identification pattern, not the definition of identification. With latent confounding, no observed set may block every back-door path, yet a causal effect can still be identified through other observed mechanisms. In the classical front-door graph, an observed mediator \(M\) intercepts every directed path from \(X\) to \(Y\); there is no unblocked back-door path from \(X\) to \(M\); and \(X\) blocks every back-door path from \(M\) to \(Y\). Under consistency, positivity, and those graphical conditions,

    \[ p(y\mid do(x)) =\sum_m p(m\mid x) \sum_{x'} p(y\mid m,x')p(x'). \]

    The inner sum estimates the causal effect of changing \(M\) after blocking its back-door paths with \(X\); the outer sum transports that response through the mediator distribution induced by \(X\). Conditioning on \(M\) alone would generally open or retain bias and is not the formula above.

    For general acyclic latent-variable models, observed margins can be represented by acyclic directed mixed graphs (ADMGs). The complete identification algorithms for the causal hierarchy determine when an interventional or counterfactual query can be reduced to available lower-level distributions and return a graphical obstruction when it cannot. Do-calculus is complete for the corresponding nonparametric effect-identification problem; this does not say that every query is identifiable, that the returned functional has good finite-sample support, or that high-dimensional estimation is easy.

    An expert workflow therefore has three possible outputs before model fitting: an observed-data functional, a sharp or assumption-indexed bound, or a non-identification certificate. A neural generator is an estimator or assumption-chosen completion downstream of that output.

    Return to the finite SCM: one identified effect, one unidentified coupling¤

    Return to the running finite SCM. Its graph has observed back-door set \(C\), and every \(P(T=t\mid C=c)\) is either \(1/4\) or \(3/4\), so positivity holds. Define \(m_t(c)=P(Y=1\mid T=t,C=c)\) and \(p_t^{\mathrm{do}}=P(Y=1\mid do(T=t))\). Under causal sufficiency, consistency, and the declared graph,

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_t^{\mathrm{do}} &=\sum_{c=0}^{1}m_t(c)P(C=c). \end{aligned} \]

    The intervention probabilities \(p_0^{\mathrm{do}}=1/4\) and \(p_1^{\mathrm{do}}=3/4\), and hence the ATE \(1/2\), are identified from the observational joint. Estimation begins only after this equality and its assumptions have been recorded.

    The unit counterfactual uses more information. Within \(C=1\), both the observational and interventional laws determine only

    \[ \begin{aligned} P(Y_0=1\mid C=1)&=\frac38,\\ P(Y_1=1\mid C=1)&=\frac78. \end{aligned} \]

    They do not determine how \(Y_0\) and \(Y_1\) are paired within a unit. Two response-type couplings illustrate the ambiguity:

    Sensitivity as a response-coupling calculation¤

    Let

    \[ q_{11}=P(Y_0=1,Y_1=1\mid C=1). \]

    The Fréchet bounds from the two known marginals are

    \[ \max\!\left(0,\frac38+\frac78-1\right) \le q_{11}\le \min\!\left(\frac38,\frac78\right), \qquad \frac28\le q_{11}\le\frac38. \]

    Consequently,

    \[ P(Y_1=1\mid Y_0=0,C=1) =\frac{7/8-q_{11}}{5/8} \in\left[\frac45,1\right]. \]

    Reporting this sharp finite-model range is more informative than silently selecting one neural coupling. Peer-reviewed research. Partial counterfactual identification generalizes this principle by optimizing bounds over canonical finite SCMs for discrete observed domains. Positive identification results add explicit structure: exogenous isomorphism studies bijective and triangular-monotone SCM classes, while dynamic optimal transport gives a flow-based construction under its monotonicity, rank-preservation, and regularity conditions. Preprint. A non-monotone triangular-SCM result proposes a broader assumption set based on mechanism-wise invertibility and context-independent inverse transport. These are class-specific routes that reduce the compatible coupling set; none erases the identification step for an unrestricted generator.

    Latent variables make the problem harder¤

    Suppose high-dimensional observations are generated from latent causal variables:

    \[ Z_i=f_i(Z_{\operatorname{pa}(i)},U_i), \qquad X=g(Z,\epsilon). \]

    Causal representation learning seeks a representation \(h(X)\) aligned, up to an explicitly characterized ambiguity, with the causal variables \(Z\). Unsupervised disentanglement from a single IID observational distribution is generally non-identifiable: many invertible transformations of \(Z\) induce the same \(p(X)\).

    Even recovering latents up to component-wise invertible transformations may be sufficient for some graph or intervention queries and insufficient for others. A theorem should state its equivalence class—permutation, component-wise transformation, scaling, affine map, or a broader graph-preserving transformation—and the curriculum should evaluate only invariant claims.

    The useful research question is therefore not “did the model discover the true factors?” but:

    • what equivalence class is identifiable;
    • from which observations, interventions, views, time structure, or environments;
    • under which assumptions on \(g\), the mechanisms, noise, sparsity, or interventions;
    • and which downstream causal queries remain invariant within that class.

    Auxiliary information and nonlinear ICA¤

    Identifiable VAE / nonlinear ICA results show how auxiliary variables can restore identifiability under conditional exponential-family and mixing assumptions. Schematically,

    \[ p(z\mid e)=\prod_i p(z_i\mid e), \qquad x=g(z), \]

    where environment or auxiliary variable \(e\) changes latent distributions in a sufficiently rich way. The recovery guarantee is not “a VAE always identifies causes”; it is a theorem tied to the assumed conditional family, variation in \(e\), invertibility, and model specification.

    Multiple distributions and environments can similarly expose mechanism changes. General-environment work studies recovery beyond neatly labeled single-node interventions, while multiple-distribution CRL formalizes broader environment structure. These are assumption-dependent identifiability results, not universal recipes.

    General Identifiability and Achievability for CRL proves recovery in a nonparametric latent causal/mixing setting using specified hard intervention coverage, including two uncoupled interventions per node in its main result. Multi-node intervention work replaces the single-target convention with coverage/diversity and linear-mixing assumptions. Grouping-based CRL obtains an observational result by assuming observed variables are organized into groups related to latent causal variables. “No intervention” in the last result does not mean “no structural information”: grouping is the additional information.

    Interventional pairs and sparse changes¤

    Pairs before and after an action provide a particularly informative signal. If an intervention changes only a sparse subset of causal variables while leaving the scene and other mechanisms fixed, the difference can reveal intervention targets and invariant content. Causal delta embeddings represent interventions through sparse, scene-invariant latent differences. This is peer-reviewed research evidence: the reported out-of-distribution gains do not establish that arbitrary image differences correspond to causal actions.

    Worked non-identifiability example¤

    Let \(Z=(Z_1,Z_2)\) have a continuous density and observations be \(X=Z\). Define an invertible mixing

    \[ \tilde Z_1=Z_1, \qquad \tilde Z_2=Z_2+Z_1^2. \]

    A decoder using \(\tilde Z\) can reconstruct exactly as well because \(Z_2=\tilde Z_2-\tilde Z_1^2\). Reconstruction, likelihood, and sample quality alone cannot select between the coordinate systems. If the true intervention modifies \(Z_2\) while holding \(Z_1\) fixed, an intervention described as “change only the second coordinate” has different meaning in the two systems. Intervention or environment information is needed to privilege a causal coordinate system.

    The example also shows why sparsity is coordinate-dependent: an intervention sparse in \(Z\) can become dense after a generic invertible mixing. Penalizing sparse latent changes introduces a preference for a coordinate system; whether that preference identifies causal variables depends on a theorem and intervention coverage.

    Disentanglement is not causality¤

    Independent or visually interpretable coordinates may be statistically useful without being causal variables. Conversely, causal variables can be dependent because mechanisms connect them. Marginal independence penalties can destroy causal dependence rather than reveal it. A causal representation must be evaluated under interventions, mechanism shifts, or identified causal queries—not only with latent traversal images.

    Measurement and abstraction¤

    Real causal variables are often not uniquely defined at pixel level. Temperature, cell type, intent, and object identity are abstractions with measurement error and domain conventions. A high-level causal model should approximately commute with low-level interventions: performing a permitted low-level intervention and then abstracting should agree with intervening in the high-level model. Accepted ICLR 2026 nonlinear causal-reduction work studies interventional consistency for policy explanations; its guarantees apply to the modeled reduction class, not all learned abstractions.

    If \(\tau\) maps low-level states to high-level variables and \(\omega\) maps allowed low-level interventions to high-level interventions, a causal abstraction seeks a commuting relation of the form

    \[ \tau_\#P_L^{\,i}\approx P_H^{\,\omega(i)}. \]

    The approximation metric, intervention set, and level of aggregation matter. Matching observational abstractions alone does not validate this relation.

    Identification checklist¤

    Before fitting a causal generator, record:

    1. the exact query—association, average intervention, conditional effect, mediation, or unit counterfactual;
    2. observed and latent variables, temporal order, and possible interference;
    3. the graph or model class and its equivalence class;
    4. available regimes—IID observation, environments, known/unknown interventions, temporal data, or paired counterfactual-like observations;
    5. assumptions—causal sufficiency, positivity, consistency, faithfulness, noise form, invertibility, sparsity, and selection;
    6. a graphical or algebraic identification argument;
    7. sensitivity analysis for assumptions that cannot be tested from the observed data.

    3. Deep Structural Generators: VAEs, Flows, GANs, and Diffusion¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate semantics from parameterization: identify which claims come from the SCM and which come from a VAE, flow, GAN, diffusion, AR, EBM, or GFlowNet component.
    2. O2 — Match mechanisms to computational contracts: compare likelihood, sampling, posterior inference, invertibility, and abductive reuse.
    3. O3 — Execute modular interventions: replace one mechanism, regenerate descendants, and preserve the correct non-descendant and exogenous state.
    4. O4 — Distinguish population and unit generation: decide when to sample fresh noise and when a factual posterior must be retained.
    5. O5 — Diagnose neural pseudocausality: reject claims based only on prompting, latent editing, attention masks, realism, or observational fit.
    6. O6 — Design hybrid causal generators: combine families across mechanism boundaries while keeping identification and approximation errors explicit.

    Separate causal semantics from neural machinery¤

    A deep SCM replaces some structural assignments or noise models with neural components. For a known DAG,

    \[ X_i=f_{\theta_i}(X_{\operatorname{pa}(i)},U_i), \qquad U_i\sim p_i. \]

    The components can be conditional flows, decoder networks, autoregressive conditionals, implicit generators, or diffusion samplers. The graph and intervention rules supply causal semantics; the component family supplies expressivity and computation.

    With observed causally sufficient variables and known topological order, maximum likelihood can train the modular factorization

    \[ \log p_\theta(x)=\sum_i\log p_{\theta_i}(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}). \]

    This estimates observational mechanisms under invariance/modularity assumptions. It does not test that the supplied parent sets are causal, and highly flexible conditionals can fit several observationally equivalent graphs.

    The causal and neural layers should therefore remain separate in diagrams, code, and evaluation. A graph edge declares a structural parent relation; the box implementing its child mechanism declares a computational contract.

    This is a hybrid-composition connection, not an equivalence claim. Changing the mechanism family can change approximation error, likelihood access, sampling cost, and abduction. It leaves unresolved whether the graph, intervention, latent variables, or unit coupling are identified.

    Causal VAEs¤

    A causal VAE commonly combines an encoder \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\), a structured latent prior or neural SCM \(p_\theta(z)\), and a decoder \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\):

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) \ge \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z)). \]

    CausalVAE is an influential example using a neural structural causal layer and concept supervision. Its architecture is not a universal identifiability theorem. Posterior collapse, decoder misspecification, latent-coordinate ambiguity, and imperfect concept labels remain possible.

    For counterfactuals, the encoder must support abduction of unit-specific exogenous or latent noise. Encoding an image, changing a labeled latent, and decoding can be a useful edit, but it is a counterfactual only if the learned representation, structural intervention, and noise retention are causally justified.

    The VAE posterior introduces two uncertainty layers: uncertainty about the unit's latent/exogenous state and uncertainty about learned parameters/structure. Sampling a new \(z\) after intervention estimates a population regime; sampling from \(q(z\mid x)\) and reusing the causally appropriate components estimates a model-dependent counterfactual distribution. A point encoder can hide this distinction.

    Conditional flows and deep SCMs¤

    An invertible conditional mechanism

    \[ X_i=f_{\theta_i}(X_{\operatorname{pa}(i)},U_i) \]

    can provide exact abduction:

    \[ U_i=f_{\theta_i}^{-1}(X_{\operatorname{pa}(i)},X_i). \]

    This makes flows attractive for deep structural causal models. Exact invertibility of the neural map does not make the causal graph correct, and maximum likelihood can still choose observationally equivalent mechanisms. Discrete variables, non-invertible measurement, hidden confounding, and support mismatch require additional inference machinery.

    Exact abduction requires that \(u_i\mapsto f_i(pa_i,u_i)\) is invertible for fixed parents, with compatible dimensions/support and a correctly fitted mechanism. Causal Autoregressive Flows proves identifiability for specified affine/additive autoregressive-flow causal families and order settings. It does not mean every autoregressive flow ordering is a causal order.

    Adversarial and implicit mechanisms¤

    GAN-like components can model complex mechanism conditionals without tractable likelihoods. They support sampling under an assumed intervention but make abduction harder: many noise values may produce similar observations, and no encoder is supplied automatically. Cycle consistency or an auxiliary encoder does not prove recovery of the true exogenous state.

    Adversarial losses can also match interventional datasets across regimes. This is a shared estimator/objective pattern, not an algebraic equivalence between GANs and causal models. The discriminator distinguishes distributions; it does not determine whether the regime labels correspond to valid interventions.

    A causal GAN can condition on graph-consistent parent variables or match several regime distributions. Without an abductive encoder, individual counterfactuals remain set-valued or require posterior inference over noise. Cycle consistency can select one coupling among many observationally valid couplings without identifying the true unit-level one.

    Diffusion mechanisms¤

    A conditional diffusion model may parameterize

    \[ p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}) \]

    or decode high-dimensional observations from causal latents. Under a specified SCM, replacing a parent mechanism and sampling descendants can approximate an intervention. However, classifier-free guidance, text prompting, or image editing are not automatically \(do\)-operations. Prompts usually condition a learned joint or steer a sampler; they need not sever incoming causal edges.

    Counterfactual diffusion additionally needs a coupling between factual and counterfactual randomness. Reusing an inversion trajectory is an algorithmic coupling. Calling it unit-level exogenous noise requires an SCM justification and stability analysis because inversion can be non-unique and solver-dependent.

    Diffusion Causal Models study counterfactual estimation under a known causal structure using diffusion/score machinery. Their image realism and minimality results are evidence for that construction and benchmark, not identification of counterfactual images from unrestricted observational data.

    Autoregressive Models, EBMs, and GFlowNets¤

    “Causal attention” in sequence models usually means a left-to-right information mask, not an SCM. An autoregressive factorization can follow a known causal order, but any joint density admits orderings that are predictive without being causal.

    An EBM can define an unnormalized mechanism or posterior over structures, while a GFlowNet can amortize sampling from such a reward. These components preserve uncertainty/diversity only relative to their energy or Bayesian score. Neither turns a misspecified graph score into causal truth.

    Worked modular generator¤

    Suppose the causal graph is \(A\to M\to Y\) with \(A\to Y\). Let

    \[ A=U_A, \quad M=f_M(A,U_M), \quad Y=f_Y(A,M,U_Y). \]

    Fit \(f_M\) with a conditional flow and \(f_Y\) with a conditional diffusion model. To sample \(p(Y\mid do(A=a))\):

    1. set \(A:=a\);
    2. sample \(U_M\) and generate \(M=f_M(a,U_M)\);
    3. sample from the diffusion mechanism for \(Y\) conditioned on \((a,M)\).

    To estimate a natural direct or indirect effect, additional cross-world identification assumptions are required; simply holding the generated mediator fixed is not automatically a valid mediation estimand. To produce an individual counterfactual, infer the factual \(U_M,U_Y\) and reuse them after changing \(A\).

    Hybrid systems and modularity¤

    Different mechanisms can use different generator families. This is hybrid composition, one of the curriculum's six relationship labels. A tractable flow for tabular variables, a categorical autoregressive model for events, and a diffusion decoder for images can coexist in one SCM. The scientific interface is the variable/mechanism boundary, not architectural uniformity.

    4. Mechanism Shift, Counterfactual Generation, and Causal World Models¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–3, domain shift, and sequential decision processes.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Specify intervention type: distinguish atomic, soft, stochastic, mechanism, and history-dependent policy interventions.
    2. O2 — Separate population shift from unit coupling: explain why complete interventional marginals do not determine individual counterfactuals.
    3. O3 — Execute abduction–action–prediction: retain factual exogenous state and regenerate exactly the descendants of the changed mechanism.
    4. O4 — Audit causal edits and world models: test composition, effectiveness, descendant response, non-descendant invariance, support, and planner exploitation.
    5. O5 — Distinguish related advanced queries: keep mediation, transport, longitudinal policies, and counterfactual fairness tied to their own identification assumptions.
    6. O6 — Design safe shift studies: expose uncertainty, population scope, off-policy support, and high-stakes governance limits.

    Independent causal mechanisms¤

    The independent-causal-mechanisms principle treats the generative process as modules whose changes can be localized. If environment \(e\) changes only mechanism \(k\),

    \[ p^{(e)}(x)=p^{(e)}(x_k\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(k)}) \prod_{i\ne k}p(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}). \]

    This is stronger than ordinary domain adaptation. A global covariance shift need not correspond to one changed causal module; multiple mechanisms, selection, measurement, or the population itself may change.

    Mechanism labels enable targeted adaptation: retain invariant modules, relearn the changed one, and recompute descendants. If the graph or changed mechanism is wrong, modular adaptation can be worse than an unstructured predictor.

    Independent causal mechanisms is a principle/inductive bias, not a theorem that mechanisms are statistically independent parameters in every real system. Shared physical constraints, population changes, or measurement pipelines can move several observed conditionals together.

    Soft, stochastic, and policy interventions¤

    An atomic intervention sets \(X:=x\). A soft intervention changes the conditional mechanism without fixing a value:

    \[ p(x\mid \operatorname{pa}(x)) \longrightarrow \tilde p(x\mid \operatorname{pa}(x)). \]

    Policy interventions assign actions as a function of history. Stochastic interventions replace a mechanism by a distribution. Transporting effects across environments requires stating which mechanisms and populations are invariant.

    Dynamic policies may depend only on information available before action. Conditioning a policy on future observations defines an impossible intervention. For continuous treatments, shift or modified-treatment policies can have better overlap than atomic values, but their identification still depends on the graph, selection, and support.

    Interventional Marginals Do Not Fix Counterfactual Coupling¤

    Suppose a randomized study identifies

    \[ P(Y_0=1)=P(Y_1=1)=\tfrac12. \]

    One SCM coupling can make \(Y_1=Y_0\) for every unit; another can make \(Y_1=1-Y_0\). Both have identical factual and interventional marginals. In the first, no unit changes; in the second, every unit changes. Thus even perfect regime-specific generative models do not identify individual treatment effects unless the cross-world coupling is constrained.

    Counterfactual identifiability of bijective causal models gives positive results for specified bijective mechanism classes and causal structures, including some hidden-confounding settings. The conclusion is class-specific: bijectivity and structural assumptions supply information absent from arbitrary interventional marginals.

    Counterfactual image or trajectory generation¤

    A defensible counterfactual pipeline contains four auditable objects:

    1. a factual measurement model;
    2. an abducted posterior over unit-specific exogenous state;
    3. an explicit intervention on a causal variable/mechanism;
    4. a decoder that propagates the changed latent causes while retaining non-descendant information.

    The output should satisfy factual reconstruction when no action is applied, respect known invariances, change descendants appropriately, and preserve variables outside the intervention's causal scope. Perceptual realism checks only whether the sample lies near an image manifold; it does not verify the causal change.

    The factual and counterfactual worlds share abducted unit state. The population intervention branch does not.

    Counterfactual composition checks that applying the factual action reconstructs the factual outcome; effectiveness checks that the target mechanism took the requested value; minimality is meaningful only relative to the graph and metric. A minimal pixel edit can be causally wrong, while a valid intervention can require large descendant changes.

    Worked mechanism-shift example¤

    Let demand \(D\), price \(P\), and sales \(S\) obey

    \[ D=U_D, \qquad P=f_P(D,U_P), \qquad S=f_S(D,P,U_S). \]

    Historical data reflects a pricing policy \(f_P\). A new pricing policy is a mechanism intervention \(f_P\to\tilde f_P\), not conditioning the old data on prices that happened to equal the new values. To simulate the policy:

    \[ D\sim p_D, \quad P=\tilde f_P(D,U_P), \quad S=f_S(D,P,U_S). \]

    A regression \(p(S\mid P)\) may be biased because demand affects both price and sales. A generator that reproduces historical \((P,S)\) pairs can inherit the same bias.

    For an individual store-day, abduction additionally conditions on the observed demand, pricing disturbance, and sales disturbance. Reusing those disturbances under \(\tilde f_P\) answers the model's unit counterfactual; resampling them produces the new-policy population. These are both useful but different simulation products.

    Temporal models and actions¤

    A causal world model distinguishes state, observation, action, and exogenous disturbance:

    \[ S_{t+1}=f(S_t,A_t,U_{t+1}), \qquad O_t=g(S_t,V_t). \]

    Planning queries the transition system under a policy intervention on \(A_t\). A predictive video model trained on passive trajectories may conflate agent actions, hidden policies, camera motion, and environmental dynamics. Good next-frame prediction does not ensure correct response to novel actions.

    Long-horizon generation compounds epistemic model error and aleatoric uncertainty. Planning can exploit model errors, so evaluation needs held-out interventions, uncertainty calibration, and policy-aware stress tests—not only rendered realism.

    Off-policy data also reflect a behavior policy that controls which state-action regions are observed. A generative transition model cannot validate a novel policy where coverage is absent merely by producing plausible rollouts. Conservative planning, uncertainty sets, online intervention, or a trusted simulator may be needed.

    Mediation, transport, and longitudinal policy boundaries¤

    These advanced queries all modify or compare mechanisms, but they are not interchangeable:

    • Mediation decomposes effects through a mediator. Natural direct and indirect effects use cross-world counterfactuals such as \(Y_{a,M_{a'}}\) and require assumptions beyond identifying \(p(Y\mid do(A=a))\). Interventional analogues avoid some cross-world objects but answer different questions.
    • Transport moves a causal answer across populations or environments. It requires a selection/population model and declarations of which mechanisms, baseline distributions, and measurement processes change. Reweighting alone cannot repair an unmeasured change in the outcome mechanism.
    • Longitudinal policy evaluation replaces a sequence of treatment mechanisms with a rule using only past information. Identification by the longitudinal g-formula or related g-methods requires sequential exchangeability, consistency, positivity at every history, and correct treatment/confounder timing. A sequence generator trained under one behavior policy does not create missing action support.

    Hernán and Robins's longitudinal route develops these g-methods in depth. This volume's narrower obligation is to keep the estimand upstream of the neural trajectory model and to report when a generated scenario is only model-based.

    Counterfactual fairness and high-stakes use¤

    Counterfactual fairness asks whether a decision would remain appropriately invariant under a modeled intervention on a protected attribute for the same unit. The answer depends on contested choices: which descendants carry unfair influence, how the protected attribute is represented, and whether the SCM is credible. A photorealistic counterfactual of a patient, applicant, or person is not evidence that the unobserved alternative is medically or socially valid.

    In healthcare and science, counterfactual generators should expose uncertainty and sensitivity to plausible graphs/mechanisms. They should not fabricate certainty for unidentifiable individual outcomes. Prospective or interventional validation is much stronger than agreement with another observational predictor.

    Failure modes¤

    • Conditioning–intervention confusion: treating filtered observational samples as manipulated outcomes.
    • Fresh-noise counterfactuals: generating a new plausible person instead of the same unit under a changed action.
    • Leakage: the decoder recovers the intervention target from supposedly invariant latents.
    • Off-support action: the intervention enters a region with no data or violated positivity.
    • Descendant freezing: preserving variables that should change after intervention.
    • Non-descendant drift: altering background attributes unrelated to the intervention.
    • Measurement reification: treating a proxy or annotation as the causal construct itself.
    • World-model exploitation: a planner finds unrealistic high-reward trajectories caused by model error.

    The target of discovery¤

    Causal discovery estimates a graph, an equivalence class, or a posterior over structures from observational and/or interventional data. It is not identical to effect estimation: one may identify an effect without identifying every edge, or learn a graph class while a desired counterfactual remains unidentified.

    It is also narrower than generic dependency-structure learning. A Gaussian precision graph or observational Bayesian network can encode conditional independences without assigning intervention semantics to an edge. The classical structure-learning chapter develops that observational problem and the boundary between dependency, random-graph, computation-graph, and causal targets.

    Constraint-based methods use conditional independences under Markov and faithfulness assumptions. Score-based methods optimize a penalized likelihood or Bayesian score. Functional causal models exploit restrictions such as additive noise or non-Gaussianity. Continuous differentiable methods relax acyclicity for optimization, but a smooth penalty does not remove statistical ambiguity.

    For a weighted adjacency matrix \(W\), NOTEARS-style methods use the smooth characterization

    \[ h(W)=\operatorname{tr}(e^{W\odot W})-d=0. \]

    This enforces acyclicity at an exact feasible solution; finite penalties/tolerances can leave cycles, thresholding can change the graph, and optimization can stop locally. Most importantly, acyclicity is a computational constraint, not evidence that the selected direction is statistically identifiable.

    Equivalence objects and algorithm families¤

    Under causal sufficiency, acyclicity, Markovness, faithfulness, and oracle conditional-independence information, PC-type search targets a CPDAG: directed edges are compelled within the observational Markov equivalence class and undirected edges remain reversible. With latent confounding or selection, FCI- type methods target a PAG whose endpoint marks summarize ancestral relations shared across a maximal ancestral graph equivalence class. A PAG is not a DAG with decorative edgeheads; it represents a different observed-variable uncertainty object.

    Score-equivalent search can target the same observational class under its score and model assumptions; functional/noise asymmetries can orient more edges only inside the restricted family. Known intervention targets define an interventional equivalence class, which can be smaller than the observational class without necessarily becoming a singleton. Unknown targets add another latent assignment problem.

    The discovery path in Causation, Prediction, and Search develops the conditional-independence and latent-variable foundations. The machine-learning path in Elements of Causal Inference connects those targets to restricted functional causal models. The different outputs should not be compared as though all methods promised one true DAG from the same information.

    Bayesian graph posterior¤

    For data \(D\) and graph \(G\),

    \[ p(G\mid D)\propto p(D\mid G)p(G). \]

    The marginal likelihood integrates mechanism parameters:

    \[ p(D\mid G)=\int p(D\mid \theta,G)p(\theta\mid G)d\theta. \]

    Returning one maximum-scoring graph discards structural uncertainty. Bayesian model averaging can propagate graph uncertainty into an effect:

    \[ p(y\mid do(x),D) =\sum_G p(y\mid do(x),G,D)p(G\mid D), \]

    provided the effect is defined and identified under the included graphs.

    The expression also suppresses parameter uncertainty:

    \[ p(y\mid do(x),D) =\sum_G\int p(y\mid do(x),\theta,G) p(\theta\mid G,D)p(G\mid D)d\theta. \]

    If some plausible graphs do not identify the query from available data, silently dropping them or plugging in an arbitrary mechanism understates uncertainty. Report partial identification, bounds, or assumption-conditional answers.

    DAG-GFlowNet¤

    DAG-GFlowNet constructs graphs edge by edge and trains a GFlowNet so terminal DAG probability is proportional to an unnormalized Bayesian score. This can amortize diverse posterior sampling rather than repeatedly running a separate MCMC chain.

    The construction requires a state representation, legal acyclic actions, a terminal reward, and balance training. It samples according to the supplied reward only to the degree optimization and exploration succeed. A posterior over graphs is conditional on the model class, prior, likelihood/score, and data; it is not proof that the high-probability arrows are causal truths.

    The terminal reward usually contains a Bayesian graph score, not prospective intervention utility. Path multiplicity must not distort graph mass, and graph-posterior calibration should be checked on enumerated small systems. Diversity of sampled DAGs is valuable only when their probabilities approximate the declared posterior.

    Worked three-node equivalence class¤

    Suppose data imply \(X\not\perp Z\) and \(X\perp Z\mid Y\). With no interventions or temporal knowledge, the chains

    \[ X\to Y\to Z, \qquad X\leftarrow Y\leftarrow Z, \qquad X\leftarrow Y\to Z \]

    share the same skeleton and no unshielded collider, so they are Markov equivalent. A discovery algorithm should not orient all edges confidently from these independences alone. An intervention on \(Y\), an ordering constraint, or a justified functional/noise assumption can add information.

    This class has the same skeleton and v-structures. An intervention on the middle node distinguishes fork-like from chain responses, but exactly which orientations become identifiable depends on intervention targets and whether interventions are perfect, soft, or unknown. Interventional data define their own equivalence classes rather than automatically revealing every arrow.

    Latent confounders and selection¤

    Unobserved common causes are represented by richer graph objects such as ancestral graphs and partial ancestral graphs. DiCoLa gives a recursive decomposition framework for constraint-based discovery with latent variables, with soundness/completeness inherited under its assumptions from the base discovery method. It is a computational framework, not a claim that latent confounding becomes harmless.

    Distributional equivalence in linear non-Gaussian latent-variable cyclic models illustrates the importance of characterizing what is recoverable before designing an algorithm. Its peer-reviewed conclusions are tied to linear non-Gaussian parametric structure.

    Unknown interventions and test-time adaptation¤

    Interventional data can orient edges, but real datasets may not label intervention targets and may differ from training tasks. Accepted ICML 2026 TICL combines test-time training with joint causal inference for structure and intervention-target detection. Its identifiability and empirical results belong to the stated interventional setting; they do not license target-free causal discovery for arbitrary uncontrolled domain shifts.

    Active Intervention Design¤

    When interventions can be chosen, discovery and generation form a sequential design problem. A policy may maximize expected information gain about graphs, reduce uncertainty in a target effect, or prioritize safety/expense. These objectives differ: orienting every edge may be wasteful if only one intervention response matters.

    A learned generator can simulate candidate experiments only under its current model posterior. If that posterior omits the true mechanism, simulated information gain can be overconfident. Evaluation should include intervention cost, posterior calibration, cumulative regret/information, and robustness to model misspecification.

    Diffusions and continuous-time causal structure¤

    Time order and stochastic dynamics can add structure, but dependence in time is not automatically causality. Stationary-diffusion causal modeling studies how properties of stationary stochastic differential equations can support causal modeling. Sampling trajectories from a fitted SDE and discovering an instantaneous causal graph are distinct tasks; discretization, latent processes, feedback, and observation frequency matter.

    Discovery-to-generation pipeline¤

    A careful workflow is:

    1. define variables and candidate intervention semantics;
    2. discover a graph/equivalence class under explicit assumptions;
    3. perform stability and sensitivity analyses;
    4. fit modular mechanisms for each candidate structure;
    5. validate on held-out interventions where available;
    6. average or report a range of causal queries across plausible structures;
    7. refuse an individual counterfactual when structural/exogenous uncertainty makes it unsupported.

    Jointly optimizing a graph and high-capacity generator can overfit: flexible mechanisms may make many graphs score similarly, while optimization relaxation can favor convenient rather than causal orientations.

    Synthetic, semi-synthetic, and real evidence¤

    Fully synthetic SCMs provide known graphs, exogenous variables, interventions, and counterfactuals but risk favoring the simulator assumptions. Semi-synthetic benchmarks preserve real covariates while imposing a known outcome mechanism; they test estimation under more realistic inputs but not whether the imposed SCM describes nature. Real randomized interventions provide stronger effect evidence but rarely reveal complete individual counterfactual ground truth.

    No single rung dominates every validity question. Exact enumeration is strongest for implementation truth, randomized interventions for supported population effects, and prospective deployment for operational transport and harm. A claim should stop at the highest rung that directly tests that query while retaining failures from every earlier rung.

    CausalTriplet tests intervention-related representation changes in synthetic and real image pairs. Interchange intervention training tests whether internal representations support specified causal abstractions. These benchmarks evaluate particular notions of intervention consistency; success should not be generalized to all causal semantics.

    Benchmark ground truth can itself encode the researcher's causal assumptions. A simulator may prove implementation correctness relative to its SCM while rewarding an algorithm that shares its functional/noise family. Real paired images can show before/after association but need timing, intervention-target, and selection documentation before serving as causal supervision.

    Metrics and diagnostics¤

    • Graph: structural Hamming distance, structural intervention distance, precision/recall of adjacencies/orientations, equivalence-aware scoring, and posterior calibration.
    • Effects: bias, RMSE, interval coverage, calibration, and error stratified by propensity/overlap.
    • Counterfactuals: factual reconstruction, composition/reversibility where justified, descendant/non-descendant change tests, diversity conditional on abducted uncertainty, and sensitivity across SCMs.
    • Representations: intervention target identification, sparse-change localization, OOD mechanism generalization, and recovery modulo the proven ambiguity.
    • Generative quality: distributional metrics within each regime, not pooled across regimes; memorization and privacy audits remain mandatory.
    • Compute: parameter and active-parameter counts, training FLOPs, inference NFE, abduction cost, graph-search cost, memory, wall clock, and hardware.

    For graph posteriors, include calibration or coverage rather than only SHD of one selected graph.     Cumulative Worked Study: A Treatment-Response Generator¤

    Suppose the goal is to generate outcome trajectories under alternative treatment policies from longitudinal health records.

    1. Query: choose population response under a dynamic policy, not an undefined “what-if image.”
    2. Variables: define baseline state, time-varying confounders, treatment, outcome, censoring, measurement processes, and possible interference.
    3. Identification: derive a longitudinal g-formula or another valid functional; state sequential exchangeability, consistency, positivity, and selection assumptions.
    4. Mechanisms: use an autoregressive/flow model for tabular transitions and a stochastic-process model for trajectories; reserve a high-dimensional decoder for measurements rather than confusing pixels with causal state.
    5. Training: fit only mechanisms justified invariant across regimes, preserve treatment timestamps, and model missingness/censoring where needed.
    6. Population simulation: replace the treatment policy and sample disturbances to estimate regime outcomes.
    7. Unit counterfactual: perform only if the exogenous coupling/abduction is identified or explicitly assumption-conditional; otherwise report population effects or bounds.
    8. Evaluation: use held-out trials/interventions where available, overlap-stratified error, calibration, negative controls, sensitivity to hidden confounding, and prospective validation.
    9. Safety: do not convert a model-dependent counterfactual into clinical advice; audit subgroup harms, privacy, and off-support policies.

    This design keeps the causal estimand upstream of architecture selection. A photorealistic trajectory with the wrong treatment assignment semantics is a failed causal model.

    Research Evidence¤

    The reviewed research does not overturn the foundations. They sharpen three active fronts:

    • Intervention representations: causal delta embeddings, ICLR 2026, learn sparse scene-invariant action differences—Peer-reviewed research.
    • Equivalence with hidden structure: latent-variable cyclic equivalence, ICLR 2026, characterizes a linear non-Gaussian setting—Peer-reviewed research.
    • Interventional and latent-confounder discovery: TICL and DiCoLa, ICML 2026, address unknown targets/test-time shift and scalable latent-variable discovery respectively—Peer-reviewed research.

    Two results sharpen evaluation boundaries. Selection-bias identification, ICML 2026, gives necessary/sufficient conditions for an ATE in its selected-population setting; it does not make arbitrary biased generative data transportable. Task-tailored proper scoring, ICML 2026, adapts probabilistic training curvature to downstream causal estimation such as IPW; it improves an estimator objective without replacing identification or positivity.

    These peer-reviewed results have protocol- and assumption-specific boundaries. Submitted, withdrawn, desk-rejected, and workshop-only papers are not promoted to peer-reviewed evidence. The research evidence ledger records the audit and should be refreshed at release time.

    Safety and governance¤

    Causal language can make generated explanations sound authoritative. In medicine, policy, hiring, credit, and science:

    • distinguish model counterfactuals from known alternate outcomes;
    • disclose non-identifiability and sensitivity to graphs/priors;
    • avoid actionable recommendations outside validated intervention support;
    • audit privacy, provenance, subgroup error, and feedback effects;
    • involve domain experts in variable and intervention definitions;
    • retain a path from each claim to data, assumptions, code, and evidence status.

    Licensing and consent matter especially for paired intervention data, longitudinal records, and generated human counterfactuals. A causal objective does not waive data rights.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Construct two SCMs with the same observational joint but different \(p(Y\mid do(X))\); identify the missing information.
    2. Implement abduction–action–prediction for a linear Gaussian SCM and compare it with fresh-noise intervention samples.
    3. Fit mechanisms under two Markov-equivalent graphs, then find an intervention on which their predictions differ.
    4. Design an evaluation where an image editor achieves perfect realism but fails descendant preservation.
    5. For a graph posterior, propagate structural uncertainty into an average treatment effect and report interval decomposition.
    6. Reproduce one peer-reviewed result and separate what follows from its theorem, benchmark protocol, and your own interpretation.
    7. Construct two response-type couplings with identical randomized interventional marginals but different probabilities of individual benefit.
    8. Audit a counterfactual image model by descendant, non-descendant, factual-composition, and graph-misspecification tests.
    Exercise 1 solution

    Let \(X\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(Y=X+N\) with independent \(N\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\). This \(X\to Y\) SCM induces a correlated Gaussian. The same joint admits a reverse regression SCM \(Y\to X\) with independent Gaussian residual. Both fit the observational joint exactly. Under \(do(X=x)\), the first model shifts \(Y\)'s mean to \(x\); in the reverse model, intervening on child \(X\) leaves \(Y\) marginally unchanged. Observational Gaussian data alone do not orient the edge.

    Exercise 2 solution

    Infer the factual residuals \(u=x-Bx_{\operatorname{pa}}\), replace the selected equation, and solve descendants with the same residuals. Fresh residuals estimate a population intervention; reused residuals estimate the SCM's unit counterfactual.

    Exercises 7–8 solution sketches
    1. Couple \(Y_0,Y_1\) identically in one SCM and anti-correlate them in another while preserving both marginals; the probability of benefit changes.
    2. Verify no-action reconstruction, target effectiveness, appropriate descendant changes, non-descendant invariance, and sensitivity across plausible graphs/abduction procedures.

    2. Why can two models match every observed and interventional marginal yet disagree on an individual counterfactual?

    Final synthesis¤

    Causal generative modeling is a semantics and identification layer that can be implemented with many generator families. Its power comes from modular mechanisms and explicit interventions; its danger comes from presenting observational patterns or appealing edits as causal answers. Research directions include representations of interventions, equivalence characterization, structure uncertainty, continuous-time systems, and robust discovery; every result remains bounded by assumptions and evidence.
