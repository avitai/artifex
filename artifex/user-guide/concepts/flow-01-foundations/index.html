    • Flows 2 — Discrete-Time Architectures

    This chapter establishes map direction, the change-of-variables theorem, Jacobian signs, support, topology, likelihood, and the distinction between classical normalizing flows and flow matching. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    Early neural normalizing-flow constructions include NICE and the variational-inference formulation of Rezende & Mohamed (2015). They define distributions using invertible transformations with tractable Jacobian determinants and provide an exact symbolic expression for the model density; reported data likelihood may still include dequantization bounds, preprocessing Jacobians, finite precision, or numerical integration. Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023) trains a time-dependent vector field without requiring the same structured-Jacobian architecture. Its likelihood, when requested, is obtained through continuous-time dynamics and divergence computation rather than the cheap finite composition used by coupling flows.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be comfortable with densities, conditional probability, multivariable derivatives, determinants, and the distinction between a random variable and one of its coordinates. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. derive change of variables in both map directions without changing the Jacobian sign;
    2. explain which assumptions make a transformation a diffeomorphism almost everywhere;
    3. distinguish an exact model-density expression from a discrete-data likelihood or a floating-point computation;
    4. diagnose support and topology mismatches; and
    5. calculate a complete scalar and two-dimensional flow likelihood.

    Consequences of map structure¤

    Classical flows couple density evaluation and sampling through the same bijection. The consequences depend on the map architecture:

    • Evaluate the model density with an exact change-of-variables formula when the inverse and Jacobian determinant are tractable
    • Generate samples through fast inverse transformations
    • Encode and decode latents directly without an amortised inverse; arbitrary marginal, conditional, or posterior queries can remain intractable
    • Train by maximum likelihood without an adversarial game; optimisation stability remains architecture-, preprocessing-, and precision-dependent

    Two flavours of flow

    The flow-matching family trades the structured-Jacobian requirement for architectural freedom: a DiT, U-Net, or another network regresses a time-dependent vector field. Likelihood evaluation then requires solving an ODE and integrating or estimating its divergence, so it is numerical rather than the cheap exact formula of a coupling flow. This document covers both the discrete-step classical-flow architectures and the continuous-time / flow-matching family.

    Evidence and family boundaries¤

    The volume covers flow matching, rectified flow, MeanFlow, discrete flow matching, and Riemannian or equivariant variants. Rectified-flow objectives are used in systems such as SD3 and FLUX.1; MeanFlow reports one-evaluation ImageNet generation under its class-conditional protocol (Geng et al., NeurIPS 2025); and discrete flow-matching papers evaluate language and structured-state generation. These are method- and protocol-specific results, not a family-wide ordering. Their assumptions and costs are developed in Conditional and Average-Velocity Flow Objectives.

    Constructive interpretation: transport and volume change¤

    For a classical flow \(x=f(z)\), generation and density evaluation use opposite directions of the same map:

    1. Choose a base law. Select \(p_Z\) with tractable sampling and density evaluation on a declared support.

    2. Generate through the forward map. Draw \(z\sim p_Z\) and compute \(x=f(z)\). A composition of invertible layers preserves a one-to-one correspondence within its modeled support.

    3. Evaluate through the inverse map. Compute \(z=f^{-1}(x)\) and use the Jacobian determinant to account for local volume expansion or contraction.

    4. Audit representation and numerics. Dequantization, preprocessing, map direction, determinant evaluation, support, topology, conditioning, and floating-point error remain part of the likelihood claim.

    Flow matching retains the transport view but learns a velocity field rather than a finite bijective composition. Generation then solves an ODE or related dynamics, and density evaluation—when required—adds numerical divergence integration.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Change of Variables Formula¤

    The change of variables formula serves as the cornerstone of all normalizing flow architectures. Given a random variable \(\mathbf{z}\) with known density \(p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z})\) and an invertible transformation \(\mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z})\), the density of \(\mathbf{x}\) becomes:

    \[ p_{\mathcal{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = p_{\mathcal{Z}}(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) \left| \det \frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \right| \]

    Or equivalently in log space:

    \[ \log p_{\mathcal{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}) + \log \left| \det \frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \right| \]

    where \(\mathbf{z} = f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})\).

    Geometric Intuition

    The Jacobian determinant \(\left| \det \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}} \right|\) quantifies the relative change in volume of an infinitesimal neighborhood under transformation \(f\). When the transformation expands a region (\(|\det J| > 1\)), the probability density must decrease proportionally to conserve total probability mass. Conversely, contraction (\(|\det J| < 1\)) concentrates probability, increasing density.

    For \(D\)-dimensional vectors, the Jacobian matrix \(J_f(\mathbf{z})\) is the \(D \times D\) matrix of partial derivatives \([\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial z_j}]\). Computing a general determinant requires \(O(D^3)\) operations, which becomes intractable for high-dimensional data like 256×256 RGB images with \(D = 196{,}608\) dimensions.

    The entire field of normalizing flows revolves around designing transformations with structured Jacobians—triangular, diagonal, or block-structured matrices where determinants reduce to \(O(D)\) computations.

    Composing Multiple Transformations¤

    A single invertible transformation typically provides limited modeling capacity. The power of flows emerges through composition: stacking \(K\) transformations:

    \[ \mathbf{x} = f_K \circ f_{K-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{z}) \]

    The log-likelihood decomposes additively:

    \[ \log p_{\mathcal{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}_0) - \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log \left| \det \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial \mathbf{z}_{k-1}} \right| \]

    where \(\mathbf{z}_0 = \mathbf{z}\) and \(\mathbf{z}_k = f_k(\mathbf{z}_{k-1})\) for \(k=1,\ldots,K\).

    Additive Structure in Log-Space

    The chain rule for Jacobians states \(\det J_{f_2 \circ f_1}(\mathbf{u}) = \det J_{f_2}(f_1(\mathbf{u})) \cdot \det J_{f_1}(\mathbf{u})\), so log-determinants simply add: \(\log|\det J_{\text{total}}| = \sum_k \log|\det J_k|\). This ensures numerical stability and makes total computational cost \(O(KD)\) when each layer has \(O(D)\) Jacobian computation.

    Three Requirements for Flow Layers¤

    For a transformation \(f\) to be a valid flow layer, it must satisfy:

    1. Invertibility: \(f\) must be bijective (one-to-one and onto)
    2. Efficient Jacobian: \(\log \left| \det \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}} \right|\) must be tractable to compute
    3. Efficient Inverse: \(f^{-1}\) must be computable efficiently (for sampling)

    Different flow architectures make different trade-offs among these requirements.

    The list is intentionally computational as well as mathematical. A bijection with an intractable inverse is a valid transformation theorem but a poor sampler. A bijection with a dense \(D\times D\) Jacobian may define a model but defeats the purpose of a scalable normalizing-flow layer. Conversely, flow matching does not require each neural-network evaluation to be invertible: invertibility, when it holds, belongs to the integrated ODE flow under regularity and uniqueness assumptions.

    Support, topology, and almost-everywhere qualifications¤

    Suppose the base density is positive everywhere on connected \(\mathbb R^D\) and \(f\) is a continuous bijection with a continuous inverse. Then the image support is connected. Such a map can assign very little mass to a bridge between two modes, but it cannot turn the support into two truly disconnected components. The same homeomorphism logic prevents an ordinary fixed-dimensional flow from exactly mapping Euclidean space onto a lower-dimensional manifold.

    This does not mean flows cannot model multimodal data. It means the approximation may retain low-density connections, become ill-conditioned near a topological obstruction, or require a changed construction: mixtures, surjective or injective flows, augmented dimensions, manifold-valued maps, stochastic dynamics, or discrete state-space generators. Density formulas also hold only where the needed derivatives and inverse exist; modern layers are often piecewise differentiable, so the correct statement is "almost everywhere" with respect to the reference measure.

    What changes when the transformation is not bijective?¤

    The ordinary change-of-variables formula applies to a dimension-preserving bijection. Three common extensions change the statistical contract rather than merely relaxing an implementation detail.

    Surjective transformations. If several latent states map to the same observation, an observation density must aggregate all preimages. In a finite example, let

    \[ p_Z(1)=0.2,\qquad p_Z(2)=0.3,\qquad p_Z(3)=0.5, \]

    and let \(f(1)=f(2)=a\), \(f(3)=b\). Then \(p_X(a)=0.5\) and \(p_X(b)=0.5\). Choosing one convenient inverse for \(a\) would return either \(0.2\) or \(0.3\) and is wrong. In continuous models, summation becomes integration over a fibre or an auxiliary reverse distribution. SurVAE Flows organize bijective, surjective, and stochastic transformations in one modular framework; particular surjections can retain exact likelihood terms, whereas others yield a variational bound.

    Injective transformations. If \(f:\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^D\) with \(d<D\), the image distribution is generally singular with respect to \(D\)-dimensional Lebesgue measure. When \(f\) is a regular injective chart, its density with respect to \(d\)-dimensional volume on the image uses the metric factor

    \[ p_{\mathcal M}(f(z)) =\frac{p_Z(z)}{\sqrt{\det\!\left(J_f(z)^T J_f(z)\right)}}. \]

    This is not an ordinary ambient-space likelihood. Chart coverage, self-intersection, observation noise, and manifold dimension must be declared.

    Augmented transformations. A model can append an auxiliary \(U\) and learn a bijection for the joint \((X,U)\). Exact joint density \(p(x,u)\) does not by itself give exact observed density because

    \[ p(x)=\int p(x,u)\,du. \]

    The integral may be analytic, estimated, or variationally bounded. Augmentation can ease topology and optimization while adding auxiliary dimension, inference, and compute.

    These constructions fill genuine architecture and representation gaps, but they do not turn every non-bijective decoder into a classical normalizing flow. The conditioning and constraints volume separately explains feasible maps, projection, and conditional-law preservation.

    Retrieval practice: topology-changing flow components
    1. Why is one inverse branch insufficient for a surjection?
    2. With respect to which measure is an injective manifold density defined?
    3. Does exact \(p(x,u)\) imply tractable exact \(p(x)\)?

    Solutions. (1) Probability from every preimage contributes to the observation. (2) The appropriate lower-dimensional volume measure on the image, not ordinary ambient Lebesgue measure. (3) No; the auxiliary must still be marginalized.

    Base Distribution¤

    Efficient sampling usually starts from a simple reference law \(p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z})\):

    Standard Gaussian (most common):

    \[ p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{z}; \mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{D/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{z}\|^2\right) \]

    Uniform (less common):

    \[ p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{U}(\mathbf{z}; -a, a) = \frac{1}{(2a)^D} \mathbb{1}_{[-a,a]^D}(\mathbf{z}) \]

    The base is part of the model, not a harmless placeholder. A bounded base passed through a continuous map has bounded image if the map extends continuously to the compact closure; a full-support Gaussian avoids that particular restriction but still inherits the topology constraints above. A learned or mixture base can improve fit while changing sampling, density evaluation, and identifiability.

    Worked example: one affine map, both Jacobian directions¤

    Let \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(X=f(Z)=2Z+3\). For the observation \(x=5\), the inverse is \(z=(5-3)/2=1\) and \(\lvert dz/dx\rvert=1/2\). Therefore

    \[ \log p_X(5)=\log p_Z(1)-\log 2 =-\frac12-\frac12\log(2\pi)-\log 2. \]

    Using the forward Jacobian gives the identical answer only if its sign is reversed:

    \[ \log p_X(f(z))=\log p_Z(z)-\log\lvert df/dz\rvert. \]

    In two dimensions, \(f(z)=Az+b\) gives \(\log p_X(x)=\log p_Z(A^{-1}(x-b))-\log|\det A|\). If \(A=\operatorname{diag}(2,1/2)\), the transformation changes shape but preserves volume because \(|\det A|=1\); individual scale changes do not imply a nonzero total log-determinant.

    Counterexample: invertible does not mean universally expressive

    No homeomorphism from connected \(\mathbb R\) has an image equal to \((-2,-1)\cup(1,2)\). A one-dimensional continuous normalizing flow can approximate a two-component target by leaving a very low-density bridge, but it cannot exactly reproduce that disconnected support while retaining the stated assumptions.

    Independent practice and assessment map: flow foundations¤

    Declare the probability space, map direction, support, reference measure, and numerical representation before using the change-of-variables formula. Show the Jacobian direction and sign behind every density equality.

    P1 — Reconstruct a normalized flow without hiding its assumptions¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: an invertible neural network has a tractable model density; a zero log-determinant means the transformation is the identity; a bijection from a full-support Gaussian can exactly produce disconnected support; exact \(p(x,u)\) makes \(p(x)\) tractable; direct inversion computes any partial-observation posterior; and an exact continuous density is an exact uint8 probability mass. For every false statement, name the missing map, measure, marginalization, topology, or data-semantics condition.
    2. P1.2 — Direction and composition ledger. Let \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\), \(f_1(z)=3z-2\), and \(f_2(u)=\exp(u)\). For the observation \(x=e^1\), recover every intermediate state and compute \(\log p_X(x)\) once with forward Jacobians and once with the inverse-map Jacobian. Identify the support of \(X\) and explain why reversing only the determinant sign without reversing the evaluated states is invalid.
    3. P1.3 — Two-dimensional likelihood and oracle. For \(X=AZ+b\) with \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,I_2)\), \(A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\0&1/2\end{bmatrix}\), and \(b=(1,-1)^\top\), compute the density at the image of \(z=(1,2)^\top\). Derive the forward and inverse Jacobians, verify the determinant product, and design a finite-difference or automatic-differentiation oracle that would catch a batched sign or reduction bug.
    4. P1.4 — Change the transformation contract. For (a) a finite surjection with three preimages, (b) the injective map \(f(t)=(t,t^2)\), and © an augmented bijection over \((X,U)\), state the measure with respect to which a density exists and derive the sum, metric factor, or marginal integral that replaces ordinary bijective change of variables. Give one incorrect single-inverse calculation for each and show exactly where probability is lost.
    5. P1.5 — Exactness and support audit. Design two targets with the same low-order moments but different support topology. Determine which can be represented exactly by a continuous Euclidean diffeomorphism from a full-support Gaussian and which needs a mixture, augmentation, non-bijective map, manifold construction, or discrete generator. State the differentiability, inverse-differentiability, and nonzero-Jacobian conditions required almost everywhere, including how piecewise-smooth boundaries are treated. Separate mathematical model-density exactness from dequantization, preprocessing, floating-point inversion, and ODE-integration error in an evidence table.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Rebuild the construction for either a circular angle with periodic reference measure or a finite molecular graph. Declare the support, base/reference measure, map or Markov kernel, normalization rule, training query, sampling algorithm, and one tractable normalization test. State which Euclidean determinant identities survive and which require a manifold-volume or discrete-state replacement.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A classical flow is a normalized density construction because a base law, a map direction, and a volume correction are specified together. Exactness attaches to that mathematical construction. Data discretisation, preprocessing, finite precision, and numerical ODE integration are additional layers whose errors must be reported rather than hidden behind the word exact.

    Retrieval practice: flow foundations
    1. If \(x=f(z)\), which Jacobian sign accompanies the forward map in \(\log p_X(f(z))\)?
    2. Why can a standard Euclidean flow represent two modes but not exactly disconnected support?
    3. Does direct inversion compute an arbitrary posterior \(p(z_1\mid x_2)\)?
    4. What changes if an image is stored as uint8 rather than observed from a continuous density?

    Solutions. (1) Subtract \(\log|\det J_f(z)|\); equivalently add the inverse-map log-determinant. (2) A continuous bijection preserves connectedness, although it can create a low-density bridge. (3) No. It recovers the complete latent paired with a complete observation under the deterministic bijection; partial or noisy observations define a different inference problem. (4) The probability of a discrete symbol is a mass, not a pointwise continuous density, so one needs a discrete construction or a dequantisation bound with all scale Jacobians included.
