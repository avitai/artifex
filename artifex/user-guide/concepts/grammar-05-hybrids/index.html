    5. Neural, Constrained, and Constructive Hybrids¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes VAEs, autoregressive decoding, discrete diffusion, and GFlowNet basics. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Attribute hybrid components: separate grammar support, neural scores, latent inference, the sampler, search, execution, and verification.
    2. O2 — Derive local constrained decoding: normalize the base next-token law on viable actions and compute the resulting sequence probability.
    3. O3 — Compare global conditioning: use valid-completion mass to explain when a local mask does or does not preserve the desired conditional law.
    4. O4 — Generalize beyond prefixes: state the extra feasibility problem for arbitrary-order generation, tokenization, graph rules, or attributes.
    5. O5 — Connect charts and constructive flows: distinguish a compiled semiring circuit, recursive grammar, GFlowNet state graph, and terminal law.
    6. O6 — Design and transfer a hybrid: specify support, target distribution, multiplicity correction, failure policy, and attribution evidence.

    Grammar-aware latent-variable models¤

    A grammar VAE encodes an observation or parse tree into \(z\) and decodes a rule sequence while masking productions that cannot expand the current nonterminal. The grammar provides syntactic support; the VAE provides a learned latent prior and amortized posterior. Grammar VAE demonstrated this construction for expressions and molecules.

    Attribute-guided decoders can propagate inherited and synthesized information while constructing a tree. Syntax-Directed VAE uses this idea to enforce a declared collection of syntax-directed constraints. Such constraints are stronger than CFG validity but remain bounded by the attributes and checks actually encoded.

    Hypergraph grammars can preserve graph-specific invariants during construction. Molecular Hypergraph Grammar reports method-specific molecular validity and optimization results. Foundation Molecular Grammar, ICML 2025, uses multimodal foundation models in grammar induction. Neither result means an arbitrary learned graph grammar captures all chemical feasibility or synthesis constraints.

    Autoregressive yield models plus formal constraints¤

    Let a base autoregressive model assign \(p_\theta(v\mid x_{<i})\). A completion checker returns the next tokens \(V_L(x_{<i})\) that keep at least one path to the formal language \(L\). A common constrained decoder samples from

    \[ q(v\mid x_{<i})= \frac{p_\theta(v\mid x_{<i})\mathbf1\{v\in V_L(x_{<i})\}} {\sum_{v'\in V_L(x_{<i})}p_\theta(v'\mid x_{<i})}. \]

    This guarantees syntactic support when tokenization, parser state, end-of-string handling, and numerical implementation are correct. In general it does not equal the globally conditioned law

    \[ p_\theta(x\mid x\in L) =\frac{p_\theta(x)\mathbf1\{x\in L\}} {P_\theta(X\in L)}. \]

    Global conditioning weights each prefix by its total valid completion mass. Local renormalization ignores that mass unless the procedure computes an exact lookahead or equivalent Doob transform.

    Complete worked example: local masks change valid-string ratios¤

    Suppose a two-token base model has

    \[ P(X_1=a)=P(X_1=b)=0.5, \]

    with

    \[ P(a\mid a)=0.9, \quad P(b\mid a)=0.1, \quad P(a\mid b)=P(b\mid b)=0.5. \]

    Thus

    Table GRAM.5. Complete worked example: local masks change valid-string ratios
    string base probability
    aa 0.45
    ab 0.05
    ba 0.25
    bb 0.25

    Constrain generation to \(L=\{\texttt{aa},\texttt{ba},\texttt{bb}\}\). Both first tokens remain viable. After prefix a, the local mask forces the second token to a. The locally constrained law is therefore

    \[ q(\texttt{aa})=0.5, \qquad q(\texttt{ba})=q(\texttt{bb})=0.25. \]

    The base model conditioned globally on validity instead gives

    \[ p(\texttt{aa}\mid L)=\frac{0.45}{0.95}, \qquad p(\texttt{ba}\mid L)=p(\texttt{bb}\mid L)=\frac{0.25}{0.95}. \]

    Both laws generate only valid strings, but they are different distributions. Grammar-constrained decoding and GRAMMAR-LLM provide particular algorithms and task evidence; their support guarantees should not be misreported as preservation of every base-model probability ratio.

    Constraints for non-left-to-right generation¤

    Masked diffusion generates tokens in arbitrary orders, so a prefix parser is insufficient. The 2026 ICLR work on constrained decoding of diffusion LLMs with context-free grammars formulates an additive-infilling feasibility problem and reduces it to intersection with a regular language. It reports near-perfect syntactic correctness on named code and structured-extraction tasks with its stated solver. This is a Peer-reviewed method result, not a proof that every diffusion sampler can enforce arbitrary semantic constraints cheaply or preserve its unconstrained conditional law.

    Grammar charts and probabilistic circuits¤

    An inside chart for a fixed input is a sum-product circuit. Reusing low-rank rule tensors also resembles circuit factorization. The correspondence is exact for the compiled computation under a named semiring. It does not make every probabilistic circuit recursively generative or every grammar query tractable: cycles, unbounded length, and grammar form still determine compilation size.

    Grammars and GFlowNets¤

    A partial derivation can be a GFlowNet state; applying a production is an action; a complete tree or program is terminal. With terminal reward \(R(t)\), a correctly trained GFlowNet targets

    \[ P_T(t)=\frac{R(t)}{\sum_{t'}R(t')}. \]

    If several action orders reach the same tree or several trees execute to the same object, trajectory multiplicity must be handled by flow conservation and the chosen terminal identity. Assigning reward to a program and then reporting an object distribution again requires summing over equivalent programs.

    A canonical leftmost expansion turns each ordered tree into one construction trace and yields an autoregressive rule policy. Allowing any expandable node creates multiple traces to the same tree. These are different state graphs even if their terminal support is identical.

    Program priors, neural proposals, and verifiers¤

    Neural-guided program search commonly composes:

    1. a grammar or DSL defining candidate programs;
    2. a prior or learned production policy;
    3. a recognition proposal conditioned on the task;
    4. an executor or verifier;
    5. a search algorithm and resource budget; and
    6. an optional library-learning step.

    Only the first two define the unconditioned program law. A verifier used for rejection, reward, or posterior likelihood induces three different systems. PHOG conditions code productions on learned higher-order AST context; it is not a context-free PCFG despite retaining grammar-based construction.

    Independent practice and assessment map: neural and constrained hybrids¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: distributional distortion. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. In the three-valid-string example, predict whether local viability masking raises or lowers the probability of aa relative to exact global conditioning. Compute both laws for all three valid strings.
    2. P1.2 — Recover the conditional first step. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. The base first-token probabilities are \((0.5,0.5)\). Valid-completion probabilities after a and b are \(0.9\) and \(1\). Use completion-mass weighting to compute the exact globally conditioned first-token distribution.
    3. P1.3 — Audit tokenizer alignment. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A character grammar accepts a quoted field only after seeing a closing quote, but the tokenizer contains tokens a, ab, and b". Explain why checking only whether each token's first character is currently legal can admit an invalid continuation. State the state update an exact checker needs.
    4. P1.4 — Count constructive multiplicity. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Program \(P\) can be reached by two action orders and program \(Q\) by one. If a naïve terminal sampler is uniform over the three trajectories, compute the induced program probabilities. Then state what a canonical one-trace construction with equal terminal rewards would target.
    5. P1.5 — Attribute a grammar VAE. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For a VAE whose decoder masks invalid productions, assign responsibility for syntactic support, latent prior, amortized posterior, rule probabilities, reconstruction objective, and prior-sample validity. Name one claim that still requires empirical evidence.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design an arbitrary-order masked-diffusion generator for typed JSON. Specify the grammar/token interface, infilling-feasibility oracle, target conditional law, correction or admitted distortion, semantic/type checks, failure accounting, and compute comparison.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. Local masking gives \((q(\texttt{aa}),q(\texttt{ba}),q(\texttt{bb}))=(0.5,0.25,0.25)\). Global conditioning gives \((0.45,0.25,0.25)/0.95=(9/19,5/19,5/19)\approx(0.4737,0.2632,0.2632)\). Local masking raises aa mass because it ignores that prefix a has less valid completion mass. Check. Both laws assign zero to invalid ab; support agreement did not imply probability agreement.
    2. P1.2. The unnormalized conditional first-step weights are \((0.5)(0.9)=0.45\) and \((0.5)(1)=0.50\). Dividing by \(0.95\) gives \((9/19,10/19)\approx(0.4737,0.5263)\). Check. The second component aggregates the two valid b continuations.
    3. P1.3. First-character checking can accept token b" because b is legal even when consuming the trailing quote closes a field at an impossible parser position, or reject/accept ab without respecting the lexer boundary it crosses. An exact checker feeds the whole token character sequence through the current lexer/parser product state and retains the token only if the resulting state has at least one valid completion. Check. End-of-string handling is part of that transition.
    4. P1.4. Uniform trajectory probability is \(1/3\), so pushforward gives \(P(P)=2/3\) and \(P(Q)=1/3\). With one canonical trace per program and equal terminal rewards, the target is \((1/2,1/2)\). Check. Flow conservation can also correct multiple paths without canonicalization, but the terminal identity and backward policy must be declared.
    5. P1.5. The grammar and mask determine declared syntactic support; the latent prior determines \(p(z)\); the encoder determines \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\); neural decoder scores normalized over allowed productions determine rule choices; and the ELBO or reconstruction term trains these components. Prior-sample validity concerns \(z\sim p(z)\) followed by decoding, not only reconstructions from \(q_\phi\). Semantic correctness, useful property coverage, latent calibration, and robustness remain empirical. Check. A perfect parser mask cannot prove those later properties.
    6. P1.6. One valid design uses a tokenizer-aware product of JSON grammar, string-escape lexer, schema/type state, and partial-position mask. For each proposed infill it solves whether the left and right fragments admit a completion. If it can compute exact completion mass it applies the conditional transform; otherwise it labels local renormalization as a different valid-support law and measures distortion. It separately executes schema and semantic validators, retains invalid, unresolved, and timed-out attempts in denominators, and compares solver calls, latency, memory, and accepted samples at matched task success. Rubric. Full credit covers all seven requested layers and does not turn syntactic feasibility into semantic correctness.

    Chapter 5 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Support versus distribution: two constraint algorithms can guarantee the same language while assigning different probabilities within it.
    • Tokenizer mismatch: character-level grammar validity does not follow from naive token masks when tokens cross lexical boundaries.
    • Reward versus likelihood: a GFlowNet reward need not be an observation likelihood or a normalized program prior.
    • Hybrid attribution: improved validity may come from the grammar, while quality within valid support comes from the neural model and search budget.
    Retrieval question: when does local masking preserve a conditional law?

    Only when the renormalization accounts for each candidate prefix's total probability of valid completion, or an equivalent exact conditional transform. Merely retaining tokens with at least one valid completion generally changes valid-string ratios.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Grammars provide support, recursive structure, or a derivation state space; neural models provide scores and representations; samplers determine the actual law produced under constraints. Chapter 6 evaluates these layers separately and places current results behind protocol-specific evidence boundaries.
