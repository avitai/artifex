    6. Evaluation, Safety, and Research Directions¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–5 and the curriculum modules on evaluation, evidence, reproducibility, privacy, and compute.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Match evidence to the causal claim: distinguish observational, interventional, counterfactual, representation, discovery, invariance, and world-model evidence.
    2. O2 — Build a triangulated evidence ladder: combine exact synthetic, semi-synthetic, randomized/quasi-experimental, and prospective evidence without confusing simulator truth with real-world validity.
    3. O3 — Decompose uncertainty and failure: separate graph, parameter, finite-sample, approximation, support, and external-validity uncertainty.
    4. O4 — Use fixed-denominator reporting: make invalid, failed, abstained, and unsafe cases visible rather than scoring only successful outputs.
    5. O5 — Audit research claims and maturity: connect every conclusion to assumptions, protocol, evidence status, and independent reproduction.
    6. O6 — Design a safe end-to-end study: specify query, identification, mechanisms, validation, subgroup risk, privacy, governance, and refusal.

    Evaluate the query, not merely the samples¤

    Causal generators require ordinary generative evaluation—likelihood or bounds where appropriate, sample fidelity, diversity, calibration, memorization, and compute—plus causal evaluation matched to the intended query.

    Table CAUSAL.23. Evaluate the query, not merely the samples
    Claim Required evidence Insufficient proxy
    Observational generation held-out density/sample/conditional tests training reconstruction alone
    Interventional prediction held-out randomized or justified quasi-experimental regimes conditioning on treatment in observational data
    Counterfactual prediction factual consistency, known structural tests, semi-synthetic truth, or repeated-world proxy with assumptions realistic edited images
    Causal representation recovery up to the theorem's equivalence class and intervention/OOD tests disentanglement score alone
    Structure discovery SHD/SID or equivalence-aware metrics, calibration, interventions, and sensitivity one graph visualization
    Mechanism invariance held-out mechanism shifts and localization tests average IID accuracy
    Causal world model action interventions, long-horizon calibration, planner robustness, and constraint violations video realism alone

    Synthetic, semi-synthetic, and real evidence¤

    Fully synthetic SCMs provide known graphs, exogenous variables, interventions, and counterfactuals but risk favoring the simulator assumptions. Semi-synthetic benchmarks preserve real covariates while imposing a known outcome mechanism; they test estimation under more realistic inputs but not whether the imposed SCM describes nature. Real randomized interventions provide stronger effect evidence but rarely reveal complete individual counterfactual ground truth.

    No single rung dominates every validity question. Exact enumeration is strongest for implementation truth, randomized interventions for supported population effects, and prospective deployment for operational transport and harm. A claim should stop at the highest rung that directly tests that query while retaining failures from every earlier rung.

    CausalTriplet tests intervention-related representation changes in synthetic and real image pairs. Interchange intervention training tests whether internal representations support specified causal abstractions. These benchmarks evaluate particular notions of intervention consistency; success should not be generalized to all causal semantics.

    Benchmark ground truth can itself encode the researcher's causal assumptions. A simulator may prove implementation correctness relative to its SCM while rewarding an algorithm that shares its functional/noise family. Real paired images can show before/after association but need timing, intervention-target, and selection documentation before serving as causal supervision.

    Metrics and diagnostics¤

    • Graph: structural Hamming distance, structural intervention distance, precision/recall of adjacencies/orientations, equivalence-aware scoring, and posterior calibration.
    • Effects: bias, RMSE, interval coverage, calibration, and error stratified by propensity/overlap.
    • Counterfactuals: factual reconstruction, composition/reversibility where justified, descendant/non-descendant change tests, diversity conditional on abducted uncertainty, and sensitivity across SCMs.
    • Representations: intervention target identification, sparse-change localization, OOD mechanism generalization, and recovery modulo the proven ambiguity.
    • Generative quality: distributional metrics within each regime, not pooled across regimes; memorization and privacy audits remain mandatory.
    • Compute: parameter and active-parameter counts, training FLOPs, inference NFE, abduction cost, graph-search cost, memory, wall clock, and hardware.

    For graph posteriors, include calibration or coverage rather than only SHD of one selected graph. For effects, separate uncertainty conditional on a graph, across graphs, from finite samples, and from neural approximation. For individual counterfactual images, no-reference perceptual metrics are supplementary—not substitutes for identification.

    A complete reporting template¤

    Every experiment should state:

    1. causal variables, graph assumptions, hidden-confounder policy, and intervention semantics;
    2. target estimand and identification formula;
    3. datasets, regimes, splits, intervention targets, selection, and overlap;
    4. generator family, inference method, structural constraints, and abduction procedure;
    5. baselines with matched data and causal information—not only matched architecture size;
    6. metrics, uncertainty intervals, multiple seeds, and sensitivity analyses;
    7. failures under graph misspecification, latent confounding, measurement error, and off-support interventions;
    8. evidence maturity and independent reproduction status.

    Cumulative Worked Study: A Treatment-Response Generator¤

    Suppose the goal is to generate outcome trajectories under alternative treatment policies from longitudinal health records.

    1. Query: choose population response under a dynamic policy, not an undefined “what-if image.”
    2. Variables: define baseline state, time-varying confounders, treatment, outcome, censoring, measurement processes, and possible interference.
    3. Identification: derive a longitudinal g-formula or another valid functional; state sequential exchangeability, consistency, positivity, and selection assumptions.
    4. Mechanisms: use an autoregressive/flow model for tabular transitions and a stochastic-process model for trajectories; reserve a high-dimensional decoder for measurements rather than confusing pixels with causal state.
    5. Training: fit only mechanisms justified invariant across regimes, preserve treatment timestamps, and model missingness/censoring where needed.
    6. Population simulation: replace the treatment policy and sample disturbances to estimate regime outcomes.
    7. Unit counterfactual: perform only if the exogenous coupling/abduction is identified or explicitly assumption-conditional; otherwise report population effects or bounds.
    8. Evaluation: use held-out trials/interventions where available, overlap-stratified error, calibration, negative controls, sensitivity to hidden confounding, and prospective validation.
    9. Safety: do not convert a model-dependent counterfactual into clinical advice; audit subgroup harms, privacy, and off-support policies.

    This design keeps the causal estimand upstream of architecture selection. A photorealistic trajectory with the wrong treatment assignment semantics is a failed causal model.

    Independent practice and assessment map: evaluation and evidence¤

    Attempt the tasks before opening their hints. Use the original cohort or attempt count as the denominator unless the question explicitly asks for a conditional rate; causal evaluation must not erase invalid or failed cases.

    1. P1.1 — Predict a fixed-denominator evaluation funnel. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. A benchmark has 200 requested causal queries. Of these, 180 parse to a valid declared query, 150 execute, and 120 satisfy the success criterion. Compute query-validity rate, execution conditional on validity, success conditional on execution, and end-to-end success. Explain which number belongs in the headline.
    2. P1.2 — Audit graph-posterior coverage. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 9 min. Across 100 exactly enumerated SCM datasets, nominal 90% posterior credible sets contain the true equivalence object in 72 cases. Report empirical coverage, the calibration gap, and why the SHD of the posterior mode cannot replace this test.
    3. P1.3 — Match claims to evidence rungs. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For each claim—implementation-correct abduction, population effect in a supported trial, individual real-world counterfactual, and safe transport to a new hospital—name the minimum direct evidence and the residual uncertainty. State which claim may still require refusal.
    4. P1.4 — Decompose uncertainty in an intervention response. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 13 min. A paper reports one interval obtained from decoder samples under one selected graph. Redesign the report so graph/equivalence, mechanism parameter, finite-sample, neural approximation, overlap, measurement, and external-validity uncertainty are not collapsed.
    5. P1.5 — Audit a current research claim. Type: research · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. Choose one peer-reviewed method from the Research Evidence section. Produce a claim card containing venue/version, exact task and data regime, theorem assumptions, empirical protocol, baselines, uncertainty, compute, failure boundary, and independent-reproduction status. Identify one conclusion the paper supports and one it does not.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer into an end-to-end causal-generator protocol. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design a preregistered study for the treatment-response generator above. Include query and population, identification formula, temporal/support checks, evidence ladder, fixed denominators, uncertainty decomposition, subgroup and privacy audits, prospective stop rules, and the conditions for abstention or deployment.

    Feedback contract: evaluation and evidence¤

    Table CAUSAL.24. Feedback contract: evaluation and evidence — table 1
    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 All four denominators are explicit and end-to-end success remains \(120/200\).
    P1.2 Nominal and empirical coverage are compared on exact truth; mode accuracy is not substituted for posterior calibration.
    P1.3 Each evidence rung is tied to a particular query and remaining real-world uncertainty.
    P1.4 Epistemic, data, implementation, support, measurement, and transport failures remain separately inspectable.
    P1.5 The supported claim is no broader than the method's assumptions, task, protocol, and evidence maturity.
    P1.6 Architecture is downstream of an identified query, and safety/refusal conditions are declared before results.

    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup
    P1.1 Draw nested sets from requested to successful. Use \(180/200\), \(150/180\), \(120/150\), and \(120/200\); do not headline \(120/120\).

    Outcome-to-task alignment: evaluation and evidence¤

    Table CAUSAL.26. Outcome-to-task alignment: evaluation and evidence
    Learning objective Independently assessed by
    O1 — Match evidence to the causal claim P1.2, P1.3
    O2 — Build a triangulated evidence ladder P1.3, P1.6
    O3 — Decompose uncertainty and failure P1.2, P1.4

    O5 — Audit research claims and maturity P1.5
    O6 — Design a safe end-to-end study P1.6
    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. Query validity is \(180/200=0.90\). Execution conditional on validity is \(150/180=5/6\approx0.8333\). Success conditional on execution is \(120/150=0.80\). End-to-end success is \(120/200=0.60\), which belongs in the headline alongside the stagewise failure counts. Check. Conditional rates diagnose stages; they do not replace the original denominator.
    2. P1.2. Empirical coverage is \(72/100=0.72\), which is \(0.18\) (18 percentage points) below the nominal \(0.90\). The posterior is under-covering in this experiment. SHD of the mode asks whether one selected graph is structurally close; it cannot reveal whether the posterior set assigns calibrated mass or includes truth at its advertised frequency. Check. Report binomial uncertainty across repeated datasets as well as the point gap.
    3. P1.3. Exact finite SCM enumeration can directly verify abduction/action/prediction code relative to its declared simulator, but not realism. Held-out randomized regimes can directly test a supported population effect, retaining sampling, adherence, selection, and transport uncertainty. An individual real-world counterfactual needs an unobserved cross-world coupling and is normally assumption-conditional even with both regime marginals. Safe transfer to a new hospital requires target-population support, prospective validation, monitoring, and domain governance; it cannot be inferred from source-site accuracy. Refuse the individual counterfactual or deployment claim when identification, support, or safety criteria fail. Rubric. Higher evidence maturity does not erase query-specific non-identification.
    4. P1.4. First report query identification under every plausible graph/equivalence object, including unidentified mass or bounds. Within each identified model, propagate mechanism-parameter and finite-sample uncertainty; separately measure approximation error against exact small cases and multiple optimization seeds. Stratify by overlap, missingness, and measurement quality, then perform transport sensitivity across target populations. Present the components and failure rates before any aggregate interval. Check. Decoder diversity under one graph measures only a narrow conditional component.
    5. P1.5. A complete card cites the exact archival version and status, names variables/regimes and the estimand, transcribes theorem assumptions, distinguishes synthetic from real protocols, checks information-matched baselines and uncertainty, records compute and known failures, and labels reproduction as absent, partial, or independent. A supported conclusion must stay inside that card; for example, improved target detection in the evaluated unknown-intervention setting does not establish unrestricted causal discovery under arbitrary shifts. Rubric. Venue status alone is not external validity.
    6. P1.6. Preregister the target population, dynamic policy contrast, longitudinal g-formula, sequential exchangeability/consistency/positivity assumptions, time ordering, missingness, and censoring. Validate code on enumerated SCMs, stress controlled violations, test supported policies on held-out interventions, and require prospective domain validation. Keep all enrolled/requested cases in denominators; decompose graph, parameter, sampling, approximation, overlap, measurement, and transport uncertainty; audit subgroup error, privacy, and harmful feedback. Stop for support violations, interval/calibration failure, subgroup harm, privacy breach, or distribution shift; deploy only within validated policy support with monitoring and human governance. Check. A better decoder cannot relax the identification or stop rules.

    Research Evidence¤

    The reviewed research does not overturn the foundations. They sharpen three active fronts:

    • Intervention representations: causal delta embeddings, ICLR 2026, learn sparse scene-invariant action differences—Peer-reviewed research.
    • Equivalence with hidden structure: latent-variable cyclic equivalence, ICLR 2026, characterizes a linear non-Gaussian setting—Peer-reviewed research.
    • Interventional and latent-confounder discovery: TICL and DiCoLa, ICML 2026, address unknown targets/test-time shift and scalable latent-variable discovery respectively—Peer-reviewed research.

    Two results sharpen evaluation boundaries. Selection-bias identification, ICML 2026, gives necessary/sufficient conditions for an ATE in its selected-population setting; it does not make arbitrary biased generative data transportable. Task-tailored proper scoring, ICML 2026, adapts probabilistic training curvature to downstream causal estimation such as IPW; it improves an estimator objective without replacing identification or positivity.

    These peer-reviewed results have protocol- and assumption-specific boundaries. Submitted, withdrawn, desk-rejected, and workshop-only papers are not promoted to peer-reviewed evidence. The research evidence ledger records the audit and should be refreshed at release time.

    Safety and governance¤

    Causal language can make generated explanations sound authoritative. In medicine, policy, hiring, credit, and science:

    • distinguish model counterfactuals from known alternate outcomes;
    • disclose non-identifiability and sensitivity to graphs/priors;
    • avoid actionable recommendations outside validated intervention support;
    • audit privacy, provenance, subgroup error, and feedback effects;
    • involve domain experts in variable and intervention definitions;
    • retain a path from each claim to data, assumptions, code, and evidence status.

    Licensing and consent matter especially for paired intervention data, longitudinal records, and generated human counterfactuals. A causal objective does not waive data rights.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Construct two SCMs with the same observational joint but different \(p(Y\mid do(X))\); identify the missing information.
    2. Implement abduction–action–prediction for a linear Gaussian SCM and compare it with fresh-noise intervention samples.
    3. Fit mechanisms under two Markov-equivalent graphs, then find an intervention on which their predictions differ.
    4. Design an evaluation where an image editor achieves perfect realism but fails descendant preservation.
    5. For a graph posterior, propagate structural uncertainty into an average treatment effect and report interval decomposition.
    6. Reproduce one peer-reviewed result and separate what follows from its theorem, benchmark protocol, and your own interpretation.
    7. Construct two response-type couplings with identical randomized interventional marginals but different probabilities of individual benefit.
    8. Audit a counterfactual image model by descendant, non-descendant, factual-composition, and graph-misspecification tests.
    Exercise 1 solution

    Let \(X\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(Y=X+N\) with independent \(N\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\). This \(X\to Y\) SCM induces a correlated Gaussian. The same joint admits a reverse regression SCM \(Y\to X\) with independent Gaussian residual. Both fit the observational joint exactly. Under \(do(X=x)\), the first model shifts \(Y\)'s mean to \(x\); in the reverse model, intervening on child \(X\) leaves \(Y\) marginally unchanged. Observational Gaussian data alone do not orient the edge.

    Exercise 2 solution

    Infer the factual residuals \(u=x-Bx_{\operatorname{pa}}\), replace the selected equation, and solve descendants with the same residuals. Fresh residuals estimate a population intervention; reused residuals estimate the SCM's unit counterfactual.

    Exercises 7–8 solution sketches
    1. Couple \(Y_0,Y_1\) identically in one SCM and anti-correlate them in another while preserving both marginals; the probability of benefit changes.
    2. Verify no-action reconstruction, target effectiveness, appropriate descendant changes, non-descendant invariance, and sensitivity across plausible graphs/abduction procedures.

    Primary Reading and Source-Tracing Checklist¤

    Start with primary SCM/identification references, then Deep Structural Causal Models, Causal Autoregressive Flows, Diffusion Causal Models, Bijective Causal Models, and the CRL/discovery sources cited in Chapters 2 and 5.

    For each claim record: estimand; graph/model class; observed and hidden variables; regimes/intervention targets; identification assumptions and equivalence class; estimator/generator and abduction coupling; data/overlap/selection; metric and uncertainty decomposition; compute; paper version/evidence status; and independent reproduction. If no identification argument exists, label the output a model-based scenario or edit rather than a recovered causal counterfactual.

    Cumulative retrieval: causal generators
    1. What operation distinguishes observation from intervention in an SCM?
    2. Why can two models match every observed and interventional marginal yet disagree on an individual counterfactual?
    3. What does changing a VAE to a flow alter, and what causal question does it leave unresolved?
    4. Which evidence ladder is appropriate for a claimed counterfactual generator?

    Solutions. (1) Intervention replaces a structural mechanism rather than conditioning through the existing one. (2) Counterfactuals require a cross-world coupling through shared exogenous variables that marginals need not identify. (3) It changes parameterization, likelihood/inference, and computational properties, but not identification of the causal graph or exogenous coupling. (4) Begin with exactly known SCMs, add semi-synthetic mechanisms with controlled violations, then use real observational/interventional evidence plus sensitivity analysis; claims must stop at the strongest identified and validated query.

    Final synthesis¤

    Causal generative modeling is a semantics and identification layer that can be implemented with many generator families. Its power comes from modular mechanisms and explicit interventions; its danger comes from presenting observational patterns or appealing edits as causal answers. Research directions include representations of interventions, equivalence characterization, structure uncertainty, continuous-time systems, and robust discovery; every result remains bounded by assumptions and evidence.
