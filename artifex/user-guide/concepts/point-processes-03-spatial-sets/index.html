    3. Spatial Processes, Interaction, and Random Finite Sets¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1, multivariate probability, and positive-semidefinite matrices.

    Numbered learning outcomes. After completing the chapter, you can:

    1. O1 — Separate first and higher order. Explain why intensity fixes expected local counts but not clustering, repulsion, empty space, or the complete law.
    2. O2 — Compare spatial constructions. Distinguish Poisson, Cox, cluster, Gibbs, and determinantal processes by the source of their dependence.
    3. O3 — Compute a finite DPP. Normalize an \(L\)-ensemble, derive its marginal kernel, and verify inclusion probabilities.
    4. O4 — Interpret local interaction. Use a Papangelou density ratio without confusing it with temporal predictable intensity or full likelihood.
    5. O5 — Preserve finite-set semantics. Relate point processes and RFSs while retaining cardinality, multiplicity, labels, and collision conventions.
    6. O6 — Transfer configuration structure. Choose a spatial or set-valued family from the target query and diagnose which summary statistics can distinguish it.

    First-order intensity does not determine interaction¤

    For a spatial point process, the intensity measure is

    \[ \nu(A)=\mathbb E[N(A)]. \]

    If \(\nu\) has a density with respect to \(\mu\), then \(\nu(A)=\int_A\rho(x)\,\mu(dx)\). The function \(\rho\) answers an expected-count question. It does not by itself say whether points cluster, repel, or occur independently. Second-order product densities, pair-correlation functions, empty-space probabilities, and the full configuration law provide different information.

    The distinction is easiest to see at a fixed constant intensity:

    • a Poisson process places points independently;
    • a Cox process can cluster them through a shared random intensity;
    • a Gibbs or determinantal process can inhibit close configurations;
    • a cluster process can create parent–offspring groups.

    All four may have the same first-order expected density after parameters are matched.

    Same intensity, different geometry¤

    The top node is deliberately shared: an intensity map can be perfect while the model's burstiness, gaps, or repulsion is wrong. The lower diagnostics are projections of the law, so several complementary checks are needed.

    Poisson, Cox, and cluster constructions¤

    A Poisson point process with locally finite intensity measure \(\nu\) is defined by

    \[ N(A)\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\nu(A)) \]

    for measurable \(A\), with independent counts on disjoint regions. On a bounded window with intensity density \(\rho\), its Janossy densities are

    \[ j_n(x_{1:n}) =\exp[-\nu(W)]\prod_{i=1}^n\rho(x_i). \]

    A Cox process first draws a random locally finite intensity measure \(\Xi\), then draws a Poisson process conditional on \(\Xi\). A log-Gaussian Cox process sets \(\rho(x)=\exp Z(x)\) for a Gaussian random field \(Z\), following the construction studied by Møller, Syversveen, and Waagepetersen (1998). Conditional independence given \(Z\) becomes marginal dependence after \(Z\) is integrated out.

    A Poisson cluster process first samples parent points and then a random offspring configuration around each parent. The observed process can omit the parents. A Hawkes process has a related immigration–branching construction under its nonnegative linear assumptions, but temporal causality in the construction and spatial cluster geometry should not be conflated.

    Gibbs processes and Papangelou conditional intensity¤

    On a bounded window, a Gibbs point process often has a density of the form

    \[ f(X)=\frac{1}{Z(\theta)}\exp[-U_\theta(X)] \]

    with respect to a declared Poisson reference process. The energy \(U_\theta\) can penalize close pairs, enforce hard cores, or reward selected structures. The partition function \(Z(\theta)\) generally depends on the parameters and can be intractable.

    For a hereditary density, the Papangelou conditional intensity can be written informally as the density ratio

    \[ \lambda_{\mathrm P}(u\mid X) =\frac{f(X\cup\{u\})}{f(X)}, \]

    whenever the denominator is positive and the reference conventions are fixed. It describes the relative propensity to insert a point at \(u\) given the rest of the configuration. It is not the same object as the temporal predictable intensity, although both are called conditional intensities. Spatial pseudolikelihood methods exploit \(\lambda_{\mathrm P}\) because the global normalizer cancels in this ratio.

    Determinantal point processes¤

    A determinantal point process (DPP) models diversity or repulsion through determinants. On a finite ground set \(\mathcal Y=\{1,\ldots,M\}\), an \(L\)-ensemble uses a positive-semidefinite matrix \(L\) and assigns

    \[ \Pr(X=Y)=\frac{\det(L_Y)}{\det(I+L)}, \qquad Y\subseteq\mathcal Y, \]

    where \(L_Y\) is the principal submatrix indexed by \(Y\) and \(\det(L_\varnothing)=1\). The marginal kernel is

    \[ K=L(I+L)^{-1}, \]

    and inclusion probabilities satisfy \(\Pr(A\subseteq X)=\det(K_A)\). Positive semidefiniteness alone does not make every arbitrary continuous kernel a valid trace-class DPP on an unbounded domain; existence conditions depend on the state space and operator.

    Complete worked example: all subsets of a two-item DPP¤

    Let

    \[ L=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Then \(\det(I+L)=\det\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]=8\). The four subset masses are

    Table POINT.3. Complete worked example: all subsets of a two-item DPP
    Set \(Y\) \(\det(L_Y)\) \(\Pr(X=Y)\)
    \(\varnothing\) \(1\) \(1/8\)
    \(\{1\}\) \(2\) \(2/8\)
    \(\{2\}\) \(2\) \(2/8\)
    \(\{1,2\}\) \(3\) \(3/8\)

    They sum to one. Moreover,

    \[ K=L(I+L)^{-1} =\frac18\begin{bmatrix}5&1\\1&5\end{bmatrix}, \]

    so each item is included with probability \(5/8\), while both occur with probability \(\det(K)=3/8\). The off-diagonal similarity lowers the determinant of the pair relative to the product of the diagonal qualities; “repulsion” here is a property of the subset law, not a force acting during one mandatory sampler.

    Random finite sets¤

    A random finite set (RFS) is a random variable whose value is a finite subset of a state space. On finite simple configurations, point-process and RFS descriptions can represent the same laws. The vocabulary differs because finite-set statistics emphasizes multi-object state estimation, set integrals, cardinality distributions, and recursive Bayes filtering.

    Important finite-set families include:

    • Bernoulli RFS: empty with probability \(1-r\); otherwise a singleton with spatial density \(p(x)\);
    • multi-Bernoulli RFS: a union of independent Bernoulli components, with care about collisions and labels;
    • Poisson RFS: Poisson cardinality and iid locations conditional on cardinality;
    • iid-cluster RFS: an arbitrary cardinality law and iid conditional locations;
    • labeled RFS: each element includes a unique label, making identity and trajectory queries explicit.

    For a Bernoulli RFS,

    \[ F(\varnothing)=1-r, \qquad F(\{x\})=r\,p(x), \qquad F(X)=0\ \text{for }|X|>1 \]

    under the set-integral convention of Chapter 1. Normalization follows from \((1-r)+r\int p(x)\,d\mu(x)=1\).

    Point clouds, sets, and multisets¤

    Permutation invariance is necessary for an unlabeled set law but not sufficient to specify one. Three choices remain:

    1. Cardinality: fixed, conditioned, or generated;
    2. Coincidence: forbidden, allowed as multiplicity, or represented by marks;
    3. Identity: unlabeled, labeled, or quotient by a symmetry group.

    A model trained on padded point clouds can represent a point process if it supplies a coherent mask/count law and does not let padding symbols leak arbitrary ordering. A labeled tracking state is not permutation invariant in the same way as an unlabeled spatial pattern; label permutations and object identities require their own convention.

    Independent practice and assessment map: spatial processes and finite sets¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. For \(L=\left[\begin{smallmatrix}1&1/2\\1/2&1\end{smallmatrix}\right]\), predict whether the two-item subset has more or less mass than it would if the off-diagonal entries were zero. Then compute the normalizer and all four subset probabilities.
    2. P1.2 — Derive DPP marginals. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For the same \(L\), compute \(K=L(I+L)^{-1}\), each singleton inclusion probability, and the joint inclusion probability. Verify the latter both from \(\det(K)\) and from the subset table.
    3. P1.3 — Prove intensity is insufficient. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. On ground set \(\{a,b\}\), compare independent Bernoulli\((1/2)\) inclusion with a law that chooses exactly one item uniformly. Show that both have inclusion intensity \(1/2\) at each item, then compute a pair or empty-set event that separates them.
    4. P1.4 — Diagnose an RFS collision. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Two independent Bernoulli components each exist with probability \(1/2\) and, when present, both occupy the deterministic location \(0\). Compare the cardinality law of their ordinary-set union with that of the sum of their counting measures. State two principled repairs.
    5. P1.5 — Compute a Papangelou ratio. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. On the finite chain \(a-b-c\), let a Gibbs mass be proportional to \(\exp[-\theta C(X)]\), where \(C(X)\) counts occupied adjacent pairs and \(\theta=\log2\). Given \(X=\{a\}\), compute the unnormalized insertion ratios for adding \(b\) and for adding \(c\).
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. A molecular generator emits an unknown number of atoms in a binding pocket. Propose one Poisson or Cox baseline and one repulsive alternative, then specify count, first-order, pair, empty-space, mark, and symmetry diagnostics that could distinguish them.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The off-diagonal similarity reduces the pair determinant from \(1\) to \(3/4\). Since \(\det(I+L)=15/4\), the probabilities for \(\varnothing\), \(\{1\}\), \(\{2\}\), and \(\{1,2\}\) are respectively \(4/15\), \(4/15\), \(4/15\), and \(3/15\). Check. They sum to \(15/15\) and the pair has less mass than the \(4/16\) it would receive for \(L=I\).
    2. P1.2. Direct multiplication gives \(K=\left[\begin{smallmatrix}7/15&2/15\\2/15&7/15\end{smallmatrix}\right]\). Thus each item is included with probability \(7/15\), and \(\det(K)=(49-4)/225=1/5=3/15\). The table also gives \(\Pr(\{1,2\}\subseteq X)=\Pr(X=\{1,2\})=3/15\). Check. For item one, \(4/15+3/15=7/15\), agreeing with \(K_{11}\).
    3. P1.3. Under independent inclusion, every subset has mass \(1/4\); under the exactly-one law, the two singleton sets have mass \(1/2\). Both give \(\Pr(a\in X)=\Pr(b\in X)=1/2\). But pair inclusion is \(1/4\) versus \(0\), and empty-set mass is \(1/4\) versus \(0\). Check. The first-order vectors match even though both a second-order and a void event differ.
    4. P1.4. The ordinary-set union has cardinality zero with probability \(1/4\) and one with probability \(3/4\); simultaneous existence still yields \(\{0\}\). The counting-measure sum has counts zero, one, and two with probabilities \(1/4\), \(1/2\), and \(1/4\). Use unique labels or continuous/non-atomic component locations so collisions occur with probability zero; alternatively retain a multiset/counting measure. Check. A repair must preserve the event that both components exist.
    5. P1.5. For \(X=\{a\}\), adding \(b\) changes \(C\) from zero to one, so the ratio is \(e^{-\theta}=1/2\). Adding \(c\) leaves \(C=0\), so the ratio is \(1\). Check. The unknown partition function cancels from both density ratios.
    6. P1.6. A baseline may draw a Poisson count and conditionally iid atom locations/types from pocket covariates; a Cox version adds a latent spatial field. A repulsive DPP or hard-core Gibbs model can penalize implausible proximity. Compare count and empty-pocket probabilities, intensity maps, pair correlation or distance distributions, atom-type calibration, joint geometry, and permutation plus rotation/translation behavior under matched pockets. Rubric. Full credit requires two concrete families and at least one diagnostic for each requested law component.

    Chapter 3 misconception checks¤

    Does a DPP always generate a fixed-size diverse subset?

    No. An ordinary \(L\)-ensemble has random cardinality. A \(k\)-DPP conditions on \(|X|=k\). Conditioning changes the law and the normalizer.

    Is every random finite set a simple point process?

    With appropriate measurability, an RFS corresponds to a finite simple point process because a mathematical set cannot retain duplicate elements. A non-simple point process with multiplicities needs a counting-measure or marked representation rather than an ordinary set.

    Chapter 3 retrieval questions¤

    1. Why can two spatial processes share \(\rho(x)\) but have different laws?
    2. What does a Papangelou intensity compare?
    3. What are the four subset probabilities in the two-item DPP example?
    Complete solutions
    1. First-order intensity fixes expected local counts but not higher-order dependence, empty-space probabilities, clustering, or repulsion.
    2. Under a fixed reference convention it compares the density of \(X\cup\{u\}\) with that of \(X\), describing the relative propensity to insert \(u\).
    3. \(1/8,2/8,2/8,3/8\) for \(\varnothing,\{1\},\{2\},\{1,2\}\), respectively.

    Chapter 3 synthesis and bridge¤

    Spatial and finite-set models require more than an intensity surface. Cox and cluster constructions induce attraction; Gibbs and DPP constructions express interactions or repulsion; RFS models make uncertain object count and identity explicit. Chapter 4 asks how these laws are learned when compensator integrals, latent intensities, missing events, or partition functions obstruct direct maximum likelihood.
