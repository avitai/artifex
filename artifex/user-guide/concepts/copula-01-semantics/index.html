    1. The Copula as a Generative Object¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes univariate and multivariate CDFs, densities, generalized inverses, and conditional probability. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Validate the copula object: check cube support, CDF volume, boundary values, and uniform one-dimensional marginals.
    2. O2 — Apply Sklar's theorem precisely: separate existence from whole-cube uniqueness for continuous, discrete, and mixed marginals.
    3. O3 — Derive the density factorization: identify the copula density as a joint-to-product density ratio under the required regularity.
    4. O4 — Execute both directions of the construction: transform observations to the copula scale and copula samples back through generalized quantiles.
    5. O5 — Diagnose invariance claims: distinguish marginal replacement, monotone reparameterization, dependence stability, and causal intervention.
    6. O6 — Transfer the semantic contract: specify a valid copula model for a new continuous, discrete, censored, or mixed observation system.

    Definition before parameterization¤

    A \(d\)-dimensional copula is a CDF \(C:[0,1]^d\to[0,1]\) whose one-dimensional marginals are uniform. Equivalently, if \(U\sim C\), then

    \[ U_j\sim \operatorname{Uniform}(0,1), \qquad j=1,\ldots,d. \]

    As a CDF, \(C\) must be grounded, \(d\)-increasing, and have the correct boundary values. In two dimensions these requirements include

    \[ C(u,0)=C(0,v)=0, \qquad C(u,1)=u, \qquad C(1,v)=v, \]

    and nonnegative rectangle volume

    \[ \Delta C =C(u_2,v_2)-C(u_1,v_2)-C(u_2,v_1)+C(u_1,v_1)\ge0 \]

    whenever \(u_1\le u_2\) and \(v_1\le v_2\). A smooth candidate additionally needs a nonnegative mixed derivative \(c(u,v)=\partial^2 C/(\partial u\,\partial v)\) and uniform marginal integrals. Merely mapping points into the cube proves none of these facts.

    Sklar's theorem, with its uniqueness clause¤

    For every joint CDF \(F\) with marginals \(F_1,\ldots,F_d\), there exists a copula \(C\) such that

    \[ \boxed{F(x_1,\ldots,x_d) =C\!\left(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d)\right).} \]

    If all marginals are continuous, \(C\) is unique on the whole cube. In general it is uniquely determined only on

    \[ \operatorname{Ran}(F_1)\times\cdots\times\operatorname{Ran}(F_d), \]

    and can have multiple extensions elsewhere. Conversely, combining any copula with univariate CDFs through the displayed equation produces a valid joint CDF with those marginals.

    This is an algebraic decomposition of a probability law. It does not say that estimating the pieces separately is always statistically efficient, that the pieces are independent parameters, or that dependence is causally invariant when marginals change.

    Density factorization¤

    Suppose \(F\) and all \(F_j\) are absolutely continuous with densities \(f\) and \(f_j\), and the copula has density \(c\). Repeated differentiation gives

    \[ \boxed{ f(x_1,\ldots,x_d) =c\!\left(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d)\right) \prod_{j=1}^{d}f_j(x_j). } \]

    Thus \(c\) is the density ratio between the joint law and the product of its marginals:

    \[ c(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d)) =\frac{f(x_1,\ldots,x_d)}{\prod_j f_j(x_j)}. \]

    The equation explains why copula likelihood, mutual information, and density-ratio estimation meet. It also explains why a poor marginal fit contaminates apparent dependence: the inputs to \(c\) and the denominator are both wrong.

    The generative program¤

    Given a copula \(C\) and marginal CDFs \(F_j\):

    1. sample \(U\sim C\);
    2. set \(X_j=F_j^{-1}(U_j)\) for every coordinate.

    Then \(X\) has joint CDF \(F(x)=C(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d))\). The generalized inverse handles atoms; it need not be a smooth bijection.

    Replacing \(F_j\) while holding \(C\) fixed defines a new joint distribution. It is a modeling intervention on the distribution, not automatically a causal intervention on a data-generating mechanism.

    Probability-integral transforms¤

    If \(F_j\) is continuous and \(X_j\sim F_j\), then \(U_j=F_j(X_j)\) is uniform. If \(F_j\) has jumps, \(F_j(X_j)\) is generally not uniform. A randomized distributional transform restores uniformity:

    \[ \boxed{ U_j=F_j(X_j^-)+V_j\bigl(F_j(X_j)-F_j(X_j^-)\bigr), \qquad V_j\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1). } \]

    Conditioned on an atom \(X_j=x\), the transform spreads its mass uniformly across the interval \((F_j(x^-),F_j(x)]\). The auxiliary randomization is part of the construction. Different treatments of it can imply different latent copula stories while producing the same observed discrete law.

    Complete worked example: a valid dependence density¤

    The bivariate Farlie–Gumbel–Morgenstern family is

    \[ C_\theta(u,v)=uv\left[1+\theta(1-u)(1-v)\right], \qquad -1\le\theta\le1. \]

    Its density is

    \[ c_\theta(u,v)=1+\theta(1-2u)(1-2v). \]

    For \(\theta\in[-1,1]\), the product \((1-2u)(1-2v)\) lies in \([-1,1]\), so \(c_\theta\ge0\). Moreover,

    \[ \int_0^1 c_\theta(u,v)\,dv =1+\theta(1-2u)\int_0^1(1-2v)\,dv=1, \]

    and symmetrically for the other coordinate. Therefore both marginals are uniform. At \(\theta=0\), \(c=1\) and the coordinates are independent. Even at \(|\theta|=1\), however, this family supports only modest rank dependence; a valid copula family need not cover every dependence pattern.

    Now choose \(X_1=\Phi^{-1}(U_1)\) and \(X_2=-\log(1-U_2)\). The resulting marginals are standard normal and unit exponential, while their dependence remains \(C_\theta\). This example separates marginal shape from dependence without claiming that the fitted \(\theta\) is invariant across environments.

    Independent practice and assessment map: copula semantics¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 6 min. Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and set \(U_1=U_2=Z^2\). Predict whether \((U_1,U_2)\) is a copula sample, then compute \(\Pr(U_1\le1/4)\).
    2. P1.2 — Compute a copula rectangle. Type: derive · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For the FGM copula with \(\theta=1\), compute the probability of \((U_1,U_2)\in(0,1/2]\times(0,1/2]\) and compare it with independence. Explain what this one calculation does not prove.
    3. P1.3 — Locate discrete identifiability. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Let \(X,Y\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and \(\Pr(X=1,Y=1)=0.35\). State the interior copula value identified by the table and whether \(C(0.6,0.6)\) is identified.
    4. P1.4 — Evaluate the density factorization. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Use the FGM model with arbitrary valid \(\theta\), a standard-normal first marginal, and a unit-exponential second marginal. Compute the joint density at \(x_1=0\) and \(x_2=\log2\), and explain why the answer does not imply independence.
    5. P1.5 — Verify a randomized PIT. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/4)\), write the conditional distributional transform for \(X=0\) and \(X=1\), then prove by interval masses that the unconditional transform is uniform.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Specify a copula contract for a zero-inflated laboratory measurement paired with a continuous biomarker. Include the observation law, latent randomization, uniqueness boundary, sampling direction, target query, and a dependence-stability claim that would require empirical evidence.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The pair lies in the cube, but \(\Pr(U_1\le1/4)=\Pr(Z\le1/2)=1/2\), whereas a uniform marginal would give \(1/4\). Therefore neither coordinate is uniform and the pair is not a copula sample. Check. Perfect equality of coordinates is allowed for a copula; the failure is the \(Z^2\) marginal.
    2. P1.2. Groundedness makes the rectangle mass \(C_1(1/2,1/2)=\tfrac14[1+(1/2)(1/2)]=5/16=0.3125\). Independence gives \(1/4\). The excess \(1/16\) reflects positive lower-left association. Check. One nonnegative rectangle and correct value at one point do not establish all boundary, marginal, and two-increasing conditions.
    3. P1.3. The table identifies \(C(1/2,1/2)=\Pr(X=0,Y=0)=0.35\); symmetry of the half-Bernoulli table also makes the \((1,1)\) cell \(0.35\). The value \(C(0.6,0.6)\) lies off the product of the marginal ranges and is not identified without choosing an extension. Check. The observed PMF is identified even though the whole-cube latent copula is not.
    4. P1.4. At \(x_1=0\) and \(x_2=\log2\), \(u=v=1/2\), so \(c_\theta(u,v)=1\). Hence \(f(0,\log2)=\phi(0)e^{-\log2}=1/(2\sqrt{2\pi})\). Check. A dependent density may equal its product-marginal density at isolated points; independence requires \(c=1\) almost everywhere.
    5. P1.5. Since \(F(0^-)=0\), \(F(0)=3/4\), \(F(1^-)=3/4\), and \(F(1)=1\), the transform is \(U=(3/4)V\) when \(X=0\) and \(U=3/4+(1/4)V\) when \(X=1\). The first conditional density is \(4/3\) on an interval selected with mass \(3/4\), and the second is \(4\) on an interval selected with mass \(1/4\); both unconditional products equal density one on their intervals. Check. The intervals partition \((0,1]\), so \(U\) is uniform.
    6. P1.6. One valid answer models a point mass at zero plus a positive continuous laboratory tail, a continuous biomarker marginal, and a latent copula scale obtained with explicit independent distributional-transform variables for the atom. The observed likelihood uses the corresponding rectangle/derivative contribution; generation samples \(U\sim C\) and applies generalized quantiles. The off-grid copula extension is not identified by the zero-inflated observations. A query might be the probability of a zero laboratory value jointly with an extreme biomarker. Holding \(C\) fixed across treatment groups or assay changes is an empirical stability hypothesis, not Sklar's theorem. Rubric. Full credit covers all six requested layers and does not call marginal replacement causal.

    Counterexamples and misconception checks¤

    • Cube-valued is not copula-valued. If \(U_1=U_2=Z^2\) for \(Z\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), both coordinates lie in \([0,1]\) but have CDF \(\sqrt u\), not the uniform CDF.
    • Zero correlation is not independence. A copula can encode nonlinear or tail dependence while Pearson correlation is zero.
    • A discrete copula is not globally identified. Bernoulli marginals constrain \(C\) only at four range-grid points; many continuous-cube extensions agree there.
    • Marginal replacement is not automatically causal. Holding a copula fixed under a policy or mechanism change is an extra invariance assumption.
    Retrieval questions
    1. Which part of Sklar's theorem changes when a marginal has atoms?
    2. Why does \(F(X)\) fail to be uniform for a Bernoulli variable?
    3. What two properties, beyond cube support, must a smooth neural density satisfy to be a copula density?
    4. Which operation turns a copula sample into an observation with declared marginals?

    Solutions. (1) The copula remains existent but is unique only on the product of marginal ranges, not necessarily on the whole cube. (2) Its CDF jumps, so \(F(X)\) takes only the jump endpoints. (3) Nonnegativity and unit marginal integrals, together with normalization implied by them. (4) Coordinatewise generalized inverse CDFs.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A copula is a probability distribution with uniform marginals, not a synonym for dependence score or a generic bounded generator. Sklar's theorem separates a joint CDF into marginals and a copula, uniquely on the whole cube only for continuous marginals. The density factorization and quantile sampling program make the separation computational; the discontinuous case makes its identifiability boundary unavoidable.

    Why Chapter 2 follows. Once the object is fixed, we can compare families by the dependence patterns, symmetries, and tail events they can represent.
