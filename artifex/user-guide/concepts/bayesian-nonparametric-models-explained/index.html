    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A Bayesian nonparametric model places a prior on an object whose dimension is not fixed in advance by one finite parameter vector. Gaussian processes do this for functions. This volume studies a different branch: random measures, exchangeable partitions, and feature or trait allocations. These objects support mixture models with an unbounded collection of components, latent representations whose number of active features can grow with the observations, and related-group models that share atoms without fixing a common finite catalog.

    The word nonparametric does not mean parameter-free, automatically flexible, or immune to misspecification. The prior has parameters, the likelihood has assumptions, a finite dataset activates finite structure, and an algorithm uses finite computation. The central discipline of this volume is to keep those four facts visible at the same time.

    Curriculum position

    Read Classical Generative Models first for finite mixtures and Latent Variables and Inference for the model–inference distinction. Gaussian and Neural Processes remain in Stochastic-Process and Function-Space Generators; point processes and random graphs have their own generated-object semantics in the Extended Generative Model Landscape.

    1. Random Measures: A Distribution over Distributions¤

    Chapter 1 definition and scope¤

    A random measure is a random element whose realization assigns a measure to measurable sets. A random probability measure \(G\) additionally satisfies \(G(\Theta)=1\) almost surely. Drawing \(G\) and then drawing \(\theta_i\mid G\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim G\) defines a joint law over a potentially unbounded collection of repeated or novel latent values.

    This is a probability-level model family. A neural network that accepts variable-sized inputs, a finite mixture with a large \(K\), or an adaptive data structure is not a Bayesian nonparametric model merely because its effective behavior grows with data. Conversely, an implementation may represent only finitely many atoms at one iteration while still targeting an infinite-dimensional posterior.

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes measurable events, conditional probability, the beta and Dirichlet distributions, and Bayesian conjugacy. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Define a Dirichlet process through its compatible finite-partition laws.
    2. O2 — Derive posterior and predictive probabilities for observed atoms.
    3. O3 — Connect finite-partition, predictive-urn, and stick-breaking views without confusing their random objects.
    4. O4 — Explain how projective consistency specifies an infinite-dimensional law from finite restrictions.
    5. O5 — Distinguish a completely random measure, its normalization, and the resulting loss of independent increments.
    6. O6 — Diagnose whether a finite representation is occupied structure, adaptive computation, or fixed truncation.

    The four common views answer different questions; the arrows below are mathematical relationships, not a recipe that makes the objects identical.

    The Dirichlet-process contract¤

    Let \(H\) be a probability measure on \((\Theta,\mathcal B)\) and \(\alpha>0\). The statement

    \[ G\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H) \]

    means that for every finite measurable partition \((A_1,\ldots,A_m)\) of \(\Theta\),

    \[ \bigl(G(A_1),\ldots,G(A_m)\bigr) \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet} \bigl(\alpha H(A_1),\ldots,\alpha H(A_m)\bigr). \]

    This finite-partition property is the definition introduced by Ferguson (1973). It gives, for any measurable \(A\),

    \[ \mathbb E[G(A)]=H(A), \qquad \operatorname{Var}[G(A)] =\frac{H(A)(1-H(A))}{\alpha+1}. \]

    Thus \(H\) is the prior mean distribution and \(\alpha\) controls concentration around it. Increasing \(\alpha\) does not add a fixed number of components. It decreases prior variability of set masses and changes the induced partition growth jointly with sample size.

    Conjugacy and the posterior random measure¤

    If the latent values themselves are observed,

    \[ \theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n\mid G\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim G, \]

    then the posterior remains a Dirichlet process:

    \[ G\mid\theta_{1:n} \sim\operatorname{DP}\!\left( \alpha+n, \frac{\alpha H+\sum_{i=1}^n\delta_{\theta_i}}{\alpha+n} \right). \]

    Integrating \(G\) out gives the Blackwell–MacQueen predictive distribution

    \[ \theta_{n+1}\mid\theta_{1:n} \sim \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+n}H +\frac{1}{\alpha+n}\sum_{i=1}^n\delta_{\theta_i}. \]

    If \(H\) is diffuse, the base-measure draw is almost surely new while the empirical atoms repeat old values. The sequence is exchangeable even though the predictive expression is written sequentially. The order is a factorization of a symmetric joint law, not a temporal mechanism.

    Worked finite event update¤

    Let \(A\subseteq\Theta\), \(H(A)=2/5\), and \(\alpha=4\). Before observing latent values,

    \[ G(A)\sim\operatorname{Beta}\!\left(\frac85,\frac{12}{5}\right), \qquad \mathbb E[G(A)]=\frac25. \]

    Suppose three observed atoms include two in \(A\) and one in \(A^c\). Conjugacy gives

    \[ G(A)\mid\theta_{1:3} \sim\operatorname{Beta}\!\left(\frac{18}{5},\frac{17}{5}\right), \]

    so the next latent draw lies in \(A\) with probability

    \[ \Pr(\theta_4\in A\mid\theta_{1:3}) =\frac{18/5}{7}=\frac{18}{35}. \]

    The predictive-mixture calculation agrees:

    \[ \frac{4}{7}\frac25+\frac{3}{7}\frac23 =\frac{8}{35}+\frac{10}{35} =\frac{18}{35}. \]

    This is an update for directly observed atoms. In a mixture model the \(\theta_i\) are latent, so their posterior assignments and component parameters must also be inferred; DP conjugacy does not make the mixture posterior closed form.

    Stick breaking: atoms and size-biased weights¤

    Sethuraman's constructive representation draws

    \[ v_k\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim\operatorname{Beta}(1,\alpha), \qquad \pi_k=v_k\prod_{j<k}(1-v_j), \qquad \phi_k\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim H, \]

    and sets

    \[ G=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\pi_k\delta_{\phi_k}. \]

    The weights sum to one almost surely under the usual conditions. Their displayed order is size-biased, not sorted from largest to smallest. In particular,

    \[ \mathbb E[\pi_k] =\frac{1}{1+\alpha} \left(\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha}\right)^{k-1}, \qquad \mathbb E\!\left[\prod_{j=1}^{K}(1-v_j)\right] =\left(\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha}\right)^K. \]

    The second expression is a prior expected residual mass, not a deterministic truncation-error bound and not a posterior guarantee.

    A diffuse base does not make a DP draw continuous

    Even when \(H\) has no atoms, a draw \(G\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H)\) is almost surely discrete. A Dirichlet-process mixture density can be continuous because a kernel \(p(x\mid\theta)\) smooths those random atoms. The random mixing measure and the induced observation density are different objects.

    Completely random measures and normalization¤

    A completely random measure (CRM) \(\mu\) assigns independent random masses to disjoint measurable sets. It often has an atomic representation

    \[ \mu=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}J_k\delta_{\phi_k}, \]

    with jumps governed by a Poisson random measure and a Lévy intensity. A gamma process is a CRM. When \(0<\mu(\Theta)<\infty\) almost surely, normalization produces

    \[ G=\frac{\mu}{\mu(\Theta)}. \]

    Normalizing a gamma process yields a Dirichlet process with corresponding base measure. The normalized masses are no longer independent across disjoint sets because they share the random denominator. Other normalized CRMs generate alternatives such as normalized generalized-gamma models. The Lévy measure, normalization, and observation likelihood must all be declared; “uses a CRM” does not determine a unique generative model.

    Three finite objects that must not be confused¤

    Table BNP.3. Three finite objects that must not be confused
    Finite object Why it is finite What it says about the model
    occupied atoms in \(n\) observations at most \(n\) distinct values can appear the next observation can still instantiate a new atom
    instantiated atoms in one slice/MCMC step an auxiliary variable makes only finitely many atoms relevant to that transition the transition may still target the untruncated posterior
    fixed truncation at \(T\) atoms the algorithm or model discards/aggregates the remaining stick an approximation error or changed finite model must be reported

    Infinite objects are specified through compatible finite restrictions¤

    An infinite-dimensional prior is usable because every finite query has a well-defined law, and those laws agree when a query is reordered, restricted, or coarsened. The required operation depends on the random object:

    • Random function \(f\). A finite restriction is the vector \(f(x_1),\ldots,f(x_n)\). Permuting query points must permute coordinates, and marginalizing points must recover the smaller law.
    • Random probability measure \(G\). A finite restriction is its mass vector on a measurable partition. Merging partition cells must add their masses and recover the law declared directly on the coarser partition.
    • Random partition or feature allocation. A finite restriction contains the first \(n\) items or rows. Deleting later items must give the law declared directly for those retained.

    These are model-level coherence conditions. They are not guarantees that a finite truncation, separately trained network, or approximate posterior has preserved the infinite law.

    For a concrete Dirichlet-process check, let a three-cell partition satisfy

    \[ \bigl(H(A_1),H(A_2),H(A_3)\bigr) =\left(\frac12,\frac13,\frac16\right) \]

    and set \(\alpha=6\). The DP definition gives

    \[ \bigl(G(A_1),G(A_2),G(A_3)\bigr) \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,2,1). \]

    Merge \(A_2\) and \(A_3\). Dirichlet aggregation gives

    \[ \bigl(G(A_1),G(A_2\cup A_3)\bigr) \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,3). \]

    This is exactly the law obtained by applying the DP definition directly to the coarser partition, because \(H(A_2\cup A_3)=1/2\). The mean mass of \(A_1\) remains \(1/2\) in both restrictions. One cannot choose unrelated Dirichlet parameters at different partition resolutions and still claim that they arise from one random measure.

    The analogous coordinate-restriction rule for random functions is developed in Stochastic-Process and Function-Space Generators.

    Connections and misconception checks¤

    • A DP is a latent-variable prior that can be paired with many likelihoods; it is not itself a Gaussian mixture.
    • A stochastic process and a random measure are both infinite-dimensional random objects, but one supplies compatible indexed values while the other assigns random mass to sets.
    • A probabilistic program may express a DP model and inference algorithm, but the programming language does not define the DP semantics.
    • A large finite neural mixture is not distributionally equivalent to a DP mixture merely because both can activate different experts.

    Independent practice and assessment map: random measures¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Keep \(H(A)=2/5\) and \(alpha=4\) from the worked example, but observe one atom in \(A\) and four in \(A^c\). Predict whether the next-event probability is above or below the prior mean \(2/5\), then derive it from both the beta posterior and the Blackwell–MacQueen rule.
    2. P1.2 — Finite-partition derivation. For \(alpha=6\) and base masses \((1/2,1/3,1/6)\), write the three-cell Dirichlet law, merge the final two cells, and prove that the result equals the law defined directly on the coarser partition.
    3. P1.3 — View ledger. For the DP, identify what is random and what has been integrated out in the finite-partition, predictive-urn, and stick-breaking views. Explain why size-biased stick order is not a ranked frequency list.
    4. P1.4 — Truncation audit. With \(alpha=3\), compute the expected residual after \(K=4\) ordinary sticks. Give one reason this number is neither a deterministic residual bound nor a posterior predictive error bound.
    5. P1.5 — CRM normalization. Suppose two disjoint-set masses are independent before normalization. Show algebraically why dividing both by their shared total generally couples them, and name the independence property that is lost.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Specify the proof obligations for replacing the gamma CRM by another CRM and calling its normalization a random probability measure: state the Lévy intensity, total-mass condition, normalization, finite restriction or predictive law, likelihood, posterior query, and finite inference representation.

    1. The posterior is \(G(A)\mid\theta_{1:5}\sim\operatorname{Beta}(8/5+1,12/5+4)=\operatorname{Beta}(13/5,32/5)\), so the next-event probability is \((13/5)/9=13/45<2/5\). The predictive calculation is \((4/9)(2/5)+(5/9)(1/5)=8/45+5/45=13/45\).
    2. The fine law is \(\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,2,1)\). Dirichlet aggregation gives \((G(A_1),G(A_2)+G(A_3))\sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,3)\), exactly the direct DP law because \(6H(A_1)=3\) and \(6H(A_2\cup A_3)=3\).
    3. The finite-partition view samples set masses of \(G\); the predictive view has integrated out \(G\) and samples the next atom conditional on previous atoms; stick breaking samples an explicit atomic \(G\) from weights and locations. Stick order records discovery under size-biased sampling and need not be decreasing, so it is not the ranked-mass representation.
    4. The expectation is \((3/4)^4=81/256\approx0.3164\). A realized residual is random, and the expectation is prior rather than posterior/query-specific; a small or large tail mass can have a disproportionate effect on a selected likelihood functional.
    5. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent positive masses and \(T\) includes \(X+Y\), then \(X/T+Y/T\le1\) and increasing one normalized share constrains the others. Their common random denominator induces dependence. The CRM's independent-increments property over disjoint sets is lost.
    6. A valid transfer gives the CRM's Lévy intensity and conditions ensuring \(0<\mu(\Theta)<\infty\) almost surely, defines \(G=\mu/\mu(\Theta)\), establishes compatible finite laws or a predictive construction, declares \(p(x\mid\theta)\) and the posterior functional, and says whether computation integrates, adaptively instantiates, or truncates atoms. The name of the CRM alone supplies none of those obligations.
    Retrieval questions
    1. What finite-dimensional statement defines a Dirichlet process?
    2. Why can a diffuse \(H\) coexist with repeated \(\theta_i\) values?
    3. Is a stick-breaking truncation the same operation as marginalizing unused atoms?
    4. Which independence property is lost when a CRM is normalized?
    5. What must agree when one finite partition of \(\Theta\) is coarsened?
    Complete solutions
    1. Every finite measurable partition receives Dirichlet-distributed random masses with parameters \(\alpha H(A_j)\).
    2. A DP draw is discrete almost surely; conditional IID draws from that discrete \(G\) repeat its atoms even though atom locations came from diffuse \(H\).
    3. No. Marginalization preserves the infinite model; fixed truncation approximates it or defines a finite alternative.
    4. Independent increments over disjoint sets: normalized masses share the total-mass denominator.
    5. The law obtained by adding masses of merged cells must equal the law defined directly on the coarser partition. For a DP this follows from Dirichlet aggregation.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A random probability measure is the model object; compatible finite partitions, posterior conjugacy, predictive reinforcement, and stick breaking are views of the same Dirichlet process. Finite occupied structure is not fixed truncation, and none of these constructions makes an arbitrary mixture posterior exact. Chapter 2 composes the random measure with a likelihood and derives the resulting partition law.

    2. Exchangeable Partitions and Random-Measure Mixtures¤

    Chapter 2 definition and scope¤

    A partition places every item in exactly one unlabeled block. A random-measure mixture generates such a partition by letting observations share atom-valued component parameters. The partition prior, component likelihood, posterior partition, observation density, and scientific interpretation of a cluster are separate layers.

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and finite mixture models. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive the DP exchangeable partition probability function and account for partition multiplicities.
    2. O2 — Separate the random measure, sample partition, observation likelihood, and posterior scientific claim.
    3. O3 — Compute prior occupied-cluster growth and likelihood-weighted posterior reassignment.
    4. O4 — Compare Dirichlet- and Pitman–Yor-process predictive rules and their growth implications.
    5. O5 — Use label-invariant posterior summaries and decision losses.
    6. O6 — Distinguish density estimation, observed-sample partitioning, and finite component-count recovery.

    Two equivalent marginalizations of the same model expose different structure. The bottom-row warning is the main interpretive boundary: a mixture component is part of a density representation before it is a claimed population.

    From random measure to mixture density¤

    A Dirichlet-process mixture (DPM) is

    \[ G\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H), \qquad \theta_i\mid G\sim G, \qquad x_i\mid\theta_i\sim f(x_i\mid\theta_i). \]

    Using the stick representation,

    \[ p(x\mid G)=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\pi_k f(x\mid\phi_k). \]

    The almost-surely discrete \(G\) supplies tied component parameters; the kernel \(f\) determines the observation distribution around each atom. Changing \(f\) can change smoothness, tail behavior, component overlap, identifiability, and posterior cluster count without changing the DP prior.

    The partition law¤

    Assume \(H\) is diffuse. Integrating out \(G\) gives the predictive allocation rule

    \[ \Pr(c_{n+1}=k\mid\Pi_n)=\frac{n_k}{\alpha+n}, \qquad \Pr(c_{n+1}=\text{new}\mid\Pi_n)=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+n}, \]

    where \(n_k\) is the size of occupied block \(k\). Multiplying these sequential factors yields the exchangeable partition probability function (EPPF)

    \[ \Pr(\Pi_n=\{B_1,\ldots,B_K\}) =\frac{\alpha^K\Gamma(\alpha)}{\Gamma(\alpha+n)} \prod_{k=1}^{K}\Gamma(|B_k|). \]

    The right-hand side depends only on block sizes, not arrival order or arbitrary cluster labels. It assigns probability to a particular unlabeled partition. Summing over all partitions with the same size profile introduces combinatorial multiplicities.

    Worked partition distribution for three items¤

    Set \(\alpha=1\). For \(n=3\):

    • the one-block partition \(\{123\}\) has probability \(1/3\);
    • each of \(\{12|3\}\), \(\{13|2\}\), and \(\{23|1\}\) has probability \(1/6\), hence the size profile \((2,1)\) has total probability \(1/2\);
    • the all-singleton partition \(\{1|2|3\}\) has probability \(1/6\).

    These probabilities sum to one. The expected number of occupied blocks is therefore

    \[ \mathbb E[K_3] =1\cdot\frac13+2\cdot\frac12+3\cdot\frac16 =\frac{11}{6}. \]

    More generally,

    \[ \mathbb E[K_n] =\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+i-1}. \]

    For fixed \(\alpha\), this grows logarithmically. The value depends on both \(n\) and \(\alpha\); there is no dataset-independent conversion from concentration to “the number of clusters.”

    Posterior assignment includes the likelihood¤

    The predictive rule above is a prior over partitions before seeing the next \(x\). In a conjugate mixture, a collapsed reassignment for item \(i\) has weights

    \[ \Pr(c_i=k\mid c_{-i},x_{1:n}) \propto \begin{cases} n_{-i,k}\,m_k(x_i), & k\text{ occupied},\\[3pt] \alpha\,m_0(x_i), & k\text{ new}, \end{cases} \]

    where \(m_k\) is the posterior predictive under existing block \(k\) and \(m_0\) is the base-prior predictive. The count term and data-fit term play different roles.

    Worked beta–Bernoulli reassignment¤

    Let component success probabilities have prior \(\phi\sim\operatorname{Beta}(1,1)\). One occupied block contains two successes. Consider assigning a new failure \(x=0\) with \(\alpha=1\).

    The existing-block failure predictive is \(1/4\), so its unnormalized weight is

    \[ 2\times\frac14=\frac12. \]

    The new-block failure predictive is \(1/2\), so its unnormalized weight is

    \[ 1\times\frac12=\frac12. \]

    The posterior assignment probabilities are equal. The partition prior alone would favor joining the existing block by \(2/3\) to \(1/3\); the observed failure supplies exactly enough contrary likelihood evidence to balance the weights. “Rich get richer” is therefore an incomplete description of posterior clustering.

    Label switching and the inferential object¤

    Mixture labels are arbitrary. Relabeling every component leaves the likelihood unchanged, and a posterior sampler may move between symmetric labelings. Label-specific posterior means can therefore be meaningless. Useful label-invariant summaries include:

    • the posterior co-clustering matrix \(S_{ij}=\Pr(c_i=c_j\mid x)\);
    • a representative partition minimizing posterior expected variation of information or another declared loss;
    • posterior distributions of functionals such as density, cluster size profile, or predictive probability;
    • application-defined summaries attached to identifiable component features.

    An adjusted Rand index is appropriate in a simulation with known labels, not as a universal unsupervised score. A visually stable partition is not evidence that latent groups are scientifically real.

    Pitman–Yor and other partition growth laws¤

    A Pitman–Yor process introduces discount \(0\le d<1\) and strength \(\vartheta>-d\):

    \[ \Pr(c_{n+1}=k\mid\Pi_n) =\frac{n_k-d}{\vartheta+n}, \qquad \Pr(c_{n+1}=\text{new}\mid\Pi_n) =\frac{\vartheta+dK_n}{\vartheta+n}. \]

    The DP is the case \(d=0\) with \(\vartheta=\alpha\). For \(0<d<1\), the occupied-block count typically grows on the order of \(n^d\) under the prior rather than \(\log n\), and the induced frequencies have different tail behavior. This is a model choice, not a free robustness improvement. The discount changes small-cluster prevalence and unseen-species prediction and must be checked against the intended query.

    Normalized generalized-gamma and broader Poisson–Kingman models provide other random-mass and partition laws. Their predictive probabilities may require auxiliary variables or special functions. Sharing the phrase “infinite mixture” does not make their partition distributions or posterior algorithms equivalent.

    Density estimation is not component-count recovery¤

    Three targets are commonly collapsed:

    1. the observation density \(p(x)\);
    2. the partition of the observed sample;
    3. a finite number of data-generating populations or mixture components.

    Under stated regularity and support assumptions, Dirichlet mixtures of normals can have consistent density posteriors (Ghosal, Ghosh, and Ramamoorthi, 1999). Yet Miller and Harrison (2014) show that broad Dirichlet- and Pitman–Yor-process mixtures are inconsistent for the number of components when data come from many ordinary finite mixtures. Both statements can be true because they concern different functionals.

    If the scientific estimand is a finite component count, use a model that places a prior on that count and check its identifiability and consistency assumptions. A mixture of finite mixtures places \(K\sim p_K\) before finite mixture weights and can retain restaurant-like computation without asserting that a DPM occupied-block count estimates \(K\).

    Failure case: a narrow kernel manufactures clusters

    A DPM with a likelihood kernel much narrower than the true within-population distribution may use several nearby components to approximate one broad density feature. Better held-out density can then coexist with an increasing occupied-cluster count. Interpreting every occupied component as a distinct cell type, speaker, disease subtype, or physical regime changes a density approximation into an unsupported ontology.

    Connections to adjacent families¤

    • A finite mixture and a DPM share latent-mixture grammar; the mixing-measure prior and partition growth law differ.
    • A VAE can use a random-measure prior, but an amortized encoder does not turn every VAE into a BNP model.
    • An autoregressive clustering network may parameterize a posterior over partitions; it is an inference composition unless its generated partition law is declared as the model.
    • A random graph may use a BNP partition for communities, while node-count, graph-exchangeability, and edge-law semantics still belong to the graph family.

    3. Feature Allocations, Traits, and Completely Random Measures¤

    Chapter 3 definition and scope¤

    A feature allocation assigns each item a finite subset of an unbounded feature collection. Unlike a partition, one item may possess several features and two features may occur together. A trait allocation generalizes binary possession to nonnegative counts or other marks. The prior on allocations does not specify how those features generate observations; a likelihood is still required.

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes binary latent-factor models and Chapter 1's random-measure distinction. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Distinguish partition, feature-allocation, and trait-allocation row semantics.
    2. O2 — Derive IBP predictive counts and the probability of an unlabeled finite feature allocation.
    3. O3 — Connect beta-process atoms to Bernoulli-process observations without treating inclusion weights as mixture weights.
    4. O4 — Prove local finiteness from the weighted Lévy-integrability condition.
    5. O5 — Diagnose column relabeling, duplicate histories, decoder nonidentifiability, and observation-model failure.
    6. O6 — Transfer the CRM grammar from binary features to count-valued traits or dependent feature measures.

    From one label to many features¤

    For a partition matrix, every row contains exactly one 1. For a feature matrix \(Z=(z_{ik})\), a row may contain any finite number:

    \[ z_{ik}=1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{item }i\text{ uses latent feature }k. \]

    Columns are unlabeled: permuting them does not change the feature allocation. Repeated columns require multiplicities in the probability law because two distinct latent features can have the same observed usage pattern.

    The Indian buffet process is an exchangeable distribution over equivalence classes of binary matrices with finitely many rows and an unbounded number of columns. In its one-parameter sequential construction:

    1. item 1 selects \(K_1\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\alpha)\) features;
    2. item \(i\ge2\) selects each previously used feature \(k\) with probability \(m_k/i\), where \(m_k\) is its previous usage count;
    3. item \(i\) selects \(K_i^{+}\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\alpha/i)\) new features.

    The construction is sequential, but the induced row law is exchangeable after columns are treated as unlabeled.

    Expected local and global complexity¤

    The expected number of features used by any row is \(\alpha\). The expected total number of distinct active features after \(N\) rows is

    \[ \mathbb E[K_N]=\alpha H_N =\alpha\sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac1i. \]

    These are prior expectations. Posterior feature counts also depend on the likelihood, feature effects, noise, and hyperparameter inference. “The IBP discovers dimensionality” is meaningful only relative to that complete model and posterior query.

    Worked two-row allocation¤

    Let \(\alpha=2\) and condition on the first item having instantiated exactly two features. For item 2:

    • each existing feature is selected independently with probability \(1/2\);
    • the number of new features is \(\operatorname{Poisson}(1)\).

    The probability that item 2 selects the first existing feature, rejects the second, and creates exactly one new feature is

    \[ \frac12\cdot\frac12\cdot e^{-1} =\frac{1}{4e}. \]

    Its conditional expected number of selected existing features is \(1\), and its expected number of new features is also \(1\). Unconditionally, every row has expected feature count \(\alpha=2\), while the expected number of distinct features after two rows is

    \[ 2H_2=3. \]

    Per-row sparsity and global dictionary growth are different quantities.

    The exchangeable probability of an unlabeled feature allocation¤

    The sequential buffet description hides two combinatorial facts: feature columns are unlabeled, and several features may have the same binary usage pattern. For an \(N\)-row binary matrix with \(K_+\) nonzero columns, let \(m_k\) be the number of ones in column \(k\). Index the \(2^N-1\) possible nonzero column histories by \(h\), and let \(K_h\) be the multiplicity of history \(h\). The one-parameter IBP assigns the equivalence class \([Z]\) the probability below. This is an exchangeable feature probability function (EFPF): it depends on feature multiplicities through a symmetric function. EFPF models form an important subclass, but exchangeability alone does not force every feature allocation to admit an EFPF. The representation boundary is characterized by Broderick, Pitman, and Jordan.

    Define the history-multiplicity and column-mass factors

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal D_N(Z) &=\prod_{h=1}^{2^N-1}K_h!,\\ \mathcal M_N(Z) &=\prod_{k=1}^{K_+} \frac{(N-m_k)!(m_k-1)!}{N!}. \end{aligned} \]

    Then the one-parameter IBP probability is

    \[ \Pr([Z]) =\alpha^{K_+}e^{-\alpha H_N} \frac{\mathcal M_N(Z)}{\mathcal D_N(Z)}. \]

    The multiplicity denominator prevents repeated identical columns from being counted as distinct labelings. The expression depends on row-exchangeable statistics, so the arrival order used to derive it does not become temporal semantics.

    For a finite check, take \(\alpha=2\), \(N=2\), and the unlabeled feature matrix

    \[ Z= \begin{bmatrix} 1&1&0\\ 1&0&1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Its three column histories are distinct and have counts \(m=(2,1,1)\). Since \(H_2=3/2\), the displayed probability is

    \[ \Pr([Z]) =2^3e^{-3}\left(\frac12\right)^3 =e^{-3}. \]

    The sequential calculation agrees. The first item creates exactly two features with probability \(2e^{-2}\). The second selects exactly one of them with probability \(1/2\) and creates exactly one new feature with probability \(e^{-1}\). Their product is again \(e^{-3}\). If the two initial features had been treated as fixed labels, the factor of two in “select exactly one” would be lost.

    The beta–Bernoulli process construction¤

    A beta process draw is an unnormalized atomic measure

    \[ B=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\omega_k\delta_{\phi_k}, \qquad 0<\omega_k<1. \]

    Conditional on \(B\), each item draws a Bernoulli process

    \[ \begin{aligned} Z_i &=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}z_{ik}\delta_{\phi_k},\\ z_{ik}\mid B &\overset{\mathrm{ind}}\sim \operatorname{Bernoulli}(\omega_k). \end{aligned} \]

    Thibaux and Jordan (2007) identify the beta process as the de Finetti mixing measure underlying the IBP, in the same structural sense that a DP underlies its exchangeable partition sequence. The analogy has a limit: DP atom masses sum to one and select one component per draw, whereas beta-process atom weights are inclusion probabilities and an item can select many atoms.

    For a diffuse finite base measure \(B_0\) and concentration \(c\), one common beta-process Lévy intensity is

    \[ \nu(d\omega,d\phi) =c\,\omega^{-1}(1-\omega)^{c-1} \,d\omega\,B_0(d\phi). \]

    Parameterizations vary, so \(c\), \(B_0(\Theta)\), and any discount parameters must be stated rather than inferred from the name “beta process.” Under the convention above, \(\mathbb E[B]=B_0\) and \(B_0(\Theta)\) controls the expected feature count per Bernoulli-process draw.

    Why infinitely many atoms can yield a finite row¤

    The local-finiteness claim follows from Poisson thinning. If the global atoms form a Poisson random measure with intensity \(\nu(d\omega,d\phi)\) and a Bernoulli-process draw retains atom \((\omega,\phi)\) with probability \(\omega\), then its active atoms form a thinned Poisson random measure. Hence

    \[ \begin{aligned} K_i &\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\Lambda),\\ \Lambda &=\int_{\Theta}\int_0^1 \omega\,\nu(d\omega,d\phi). \end{aligned} \]

    provided \(\Lambda<\infty\). The global measure may nevertheless have infinitely many atoms when \(\int\nu(d\omega,d\phi)=\infty\). For the beta-process intensity above,

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Lambda &=B_0(\Theta) \int_0^1 c(1-\omega)^{c-1}\,d\omega\\ &=B_0(\Theta). \end{aligned} \]

    Under the one-parameter IBP convention \(B_0(\Theta)=\alpha\), this recovers \(K_i\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\alpha)\) and \(\mathbb E[K_i]=\alpha\). Thus “infinite dictionary, finite row” is a consequence of a weighted Lévy-integrability condition, not of truncating the model.

    A likelihood turns features into a data model¤

    One linear-Gaussian latent-feature model is

    \[ x_i=z_i A+\varepsilon_i, \qquad \varepsilon_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I), \]

    where row \(z_i\) selects feature-effect rows of \(A\). Other likelihoods can use noisy-OR composition, neural decoders, count superposition, or temporal dynamics. The IBP supplies \(p(Z)\); it does not choose \(p(x\mid Z,A)\).

    This separation exposes failure modes:

    • column permutation is always nonidentifiable;
    • duplicate or nearly collinear feature effects can yield similar likelihoods;
    • a flexible decoder may ignore \(Z\) or encode several observations with one uninterpretable feature;
    • correlated true causes conflict with the conditional-independence assumptions of the simplest beta–Bernoulli construction;
    • missing-not-at-random features require an observation/selection model.

    From binary features to count-valued traits¤

    CRMs provide a reusable grammar. Let

    \[ \mu=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}J_k\delta_{\phi_k} \]

    and draw item-specific counts \(x_{ik}\sim h(\,cdot\,;J_k)\). Choices include gamma–Poisson and beta–negative-binomial constructions. Each row still activates finitely many traits under appropriate Lévy-integrability conditions, while counts allow repeated or graded association.

    The observation can then be generated from the random trait measure

    \[ X_i=\sum_k x_{ik}\delta_{\phi_k}. \]

    This is not the same as a normalized mixture: total mass, number of active traits, and trait counts all carry information. Normalizing \(X_i\) can erase a scientifically relevant exposure or abundance variable.

    Temporal and grouped feature measures¤

    Hierarchical beta processes share a feature dictionary across groups while allowing group-specific inclusion probabilities. Dependent beta processes let probabilities evolve with time or covariates. The model must specify whether features persist, appear and disappear, or change only their effects. A dynamic prior over inclusion probabilities is not equivalent to independently applying an IBP at each time.

    The Wright–Fisher IBP, for example, constructs time-varying beta-process weights through Wright–Fisher diffusions. Its stationarity and transition assumptions are part of that model; “dynamic IBP” is not one universal process.

    4. Hierarchies, Dependence, and Neural Composition¤

    Chapter 4 definition and scope¤

    Hierarchical random measures share atoms and statistical strength across related groups. Dependent random measures let masses or atoms vary with a covariate, time, or known graph. Neural components can parameterize likelihoods or posterior algorithms inside either construction. These are three distinct compositions: hierarchy, stochastic dependence, and function approximation.

    Why independent group priors fail to share atoms¤

    Suppose group \(j\) has a random mixing measure \(G_j\). If

    \[ G_j\overset{\mathrm{ind}}\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H) \]

    with diffuse \(H\), each \(G_j\) is discrete, but two independent draws almost surely place their atoms at different locations. The groups can have similar prior distributions without sharing exact mixture components.

    The hierarchical Dirichlet process instead draws

    \[ G_0\sim\operatorname{DP}(\gamma,H), \qquad G_j\mid G_0\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,G_0), \]

    followed by

    \[ \theta_{ji}\mid G_j\sim G_j, \qquad x_{ji}\mid\theta_{ji}\sim f(x_{ji}\mid\theta_{ji}). \]

    Because \(G_0\) is discrete almost surely, every \(G_j\) draws its atoms from the same countable global catalog. Group weights differ, while atom locations are shared.

An arrow specifies a conditional distribution, not a causal effect. The visual roles follow the plate-notation key introduced in Classical Generative Models.

    The Chinese restaurant franchise view¤

    Integrating group measures yields a nested partition representation. Items sit at group-specific tables; tables select global dishes; tables in different groups may serve the same dish. The auxiliary table variables make the sharing structure computationally convenient, but they are not necessarily scientific entities.

    The HDP is useful for grouped mixtures and shared topic catalogs. It does not by itself impose temporal persistence. An HDP hidden Markov model combines the shared-state measure with transition distributions; “sticky” variants add self-transition bias. The transition law and random-measure hierarchy are separate model decisions.

    Worked shared-atom update¤

    Condition on a two-atom global measure

    \[ G_0=0.6\delta_a+0.4\delta_b \]

    and let a group have \(G_j\sim\operatorname{DP}(4,G_0)\). If that group has observed three assignments to \(a\) and one to \(b\), its posterior weights are

    \[ (G_j(a),G_j(b))\mid\theta_{j,1:4} \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(2.4+3,1.6+1). \]

    The next group-specific latent assignment chooses \(a\) with probability

    \[ \frac{5.4}{5.4+2.6}=\frac{27}{40}=0.675. \]

    This calculation conditions on \(G_0\). A full HDP posterior also updates the uncertain global masses and atoms using evidence from every group.

    Hierarchical and dependent are not synonyms¤

    A hierarchy expresses conditional sharing through parent random measures. A dependent random measure is a collection \((G_s)_{s\in\mathcal S}\) whose values are statistically coupled across an index such as time, space, treatment, or known graph. Desirable properties depend on the application:

    • each marginal \(G_s\) may or may not be a DP;
    • atoms may be shared, move, appear, or disappear with \(s\);
    • weights may be smooth, Markovian, graph-dependent, or only partially exchangeable;
    • the construction must remain a valid jointly measurable collection of random measures.

    Calling \(s\) a covariate does not make the dependence causal. It models observational variation in distributions unless interventions and structural mechanisms are added separately.

    Broader normalized random-measure families¤

    Normalizing different CRMs changes the atom-frequency and partition law. The Dirichlet process is the normalized-gamma case; normalized inverse-Gaussian and normalized generalized-gamma processes provide other predictive behavior. Pitman–Yor and broader Poisson–Kingman constructions connect random ranked masses, exchangeable partitions, and species-sampling models.

    The choice matters when rare atoms, unseen species, vocabulary growth, or heavy frequency tails are part of the query. It also changes posterior computation. A closed-form CRP update cannot be assumed for every normalized CRM, and an auxiliary variable introduced for tractability becomes part of the inference algorithm rather than the random-measure definition.

    Invariance of a deterministic set encoder means reordering inputs does not change its output. Exchangeability is a property of a normalized joint probability law across every finite sample size.     5. Posterior Computation Without Infinity Theater¤

    Chapter 5 definition and scope¤

    Posterior computation answers a finite query about an infinite-dimensional model. An algorithm can integrate out the random measure, instantiate a finite conditional subset, truncate the representation, optimize a variational family, propagate particles, or amortize repeated inference. Each strategy has a different exactness, bias, convergence, and compute contract.

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes MCMC, variational inference, importance sampling, and Chapters 1–4. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Name the posterior target and query before choosing an algorithm.
    2. O2 — Derive collapsed DPM assignment weights, including predictive likelihoods.
    3. O3 — Classify blocked, slice, SMC, variational, and amortized representations by what they integrate, instantiate, or approximate.
    4. O4 — Distinguish finite work per iteration, fixed model truncation, and achieved asymptotic exactness.
    5. O5 — Decompose truncation, variational, Monte Carlo, optimization, amortization, and deployment-shift errors.
    6. O6 — Design label-invariant, query-specific computational diagnostics and budget-matched comparisons.

    Name the posterior target first¤

    For a DPM, the target may include

    \[ p(G,\theta_{1:n},c_{1:n},\eta\mid x_{1:n}), \]

    where \(\eta\) contains concentration, base-measure, and likelihood hyperparameters. Possible queries include the posterior predictive density, a co-clustering probability, the number of occupied blocks, a functional of \(G\), or a component-specific parameter. An inference method that approximates one marginal well may be poor for another.

    Direct DP conjugacy applies when \(\theta_i\) are observed. In a DPM they are latent and coupled through \(x_i\), so the conjugate formula is a building block rather than the finished posterior.

    Collapsed Gibbs sampling¤

    The family-independent rules for full-conditionals, scan order, blocking, collapsing, and auxiliary variables are developed in Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics. Here the target is specifically a random partition and its component parameters.

    When component parameters can be integrated analytically, remove item \(i\) from its block, compute

    \[ w_k=n_{-i,k}m_k(x_i), \qquad w_{\mathrm{new}}=\alpha m_0(x_i), \]

    normalize, and sample a new assignment. One sweep is schematically:

    for i in a randomized order:
    remove item i and delete an empty block
    compute log n[-i,k] + log posterior_predictive(x[i], block k)
    compute log alpha + log base_predictive(x[i])
    normalize with log-sum-exp and sample the assignment
    update only the selected block's sufficient statistics
update alpha and other hyperparameters from their declared conditional or kernel

    Collapsing can reduce posterior dependence and storage, but it does not guarantee rapid mixing. Moving one item at a time can struggle to split a merged block or merge two separated blocks. Split–merge proposals, tempering, or better initialization address transition geometry, not the model definition.

    For nonconjugate likelihoods, auxiliary component proposals or explicit component parameters are needed. Neal's algorithms illustrate several valid DPM samplers; their transition kernels and computational costs differ even though they target the same posterior under their assumptions.

    Blocked stick-breaking and fixed truncation¤

    A blocked sampler instantiates weights and atoms explicitly. A finite truncation might set \(v_T=1\) or aggregate all remaining mass into a terminal component. This enables vectorized assignments but changes the representation.

    For a DP stick, the prior expected residual after \(T\) ordinary breaks is

    \[ \mathbb E[R_T] =\mathbb E\!\left[\prod_{k=1}^{T}(1-v_k)\right] =\left(\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha}\right)^T. \]

    With \(\alpha=1\) and \(T=5\), this is \(1/32\). It is not a bound on posterior predictive error: data can favor atoms beyond the truncation, and a small mass can matter greatly for a rare-event query.

    A truncation report should state \(T\), the terminal-stick convention, prior and posterior residual diagnostics, sensitivity to larger \(T\), and compute changes. Calling \(T\) “large enough” without a query-relative check hides approximation bias.

    Slice and retrospective methods¤

    Slice augmentation introduces variables that make only finitely many weights relevant at one transition. Walker (2007) developed this idea for DP mixtures; retrospective methods instantiate additional sticks only when required. General CRM slice samplers extend the construction to feature and trait models (Zhu and Bouchard-Côté, 2020).

    An exact slice transition means no fixed truncation of the target model. It does not mean:

    • independent posterior draws;
    • convergence after one transition;
    • immunity to numerical or coding error;
    • bounded runtime independent of the state;
    • exact likelihood or hyperparameter integration.

    The chain still requires stationarity and mixing diagnostics.

    Sequential Monte Carlo over partitions¤

    SMC can add observations sequentially, propose an assignment or feature set, update importance weights using the predictive likelihood, and resample. It naturally represents uncertainty over many partitions and supplies normalizer estimates under a valid Feynman–Kac construction. Poor proposals cause weight degeneracy; resampling controls weight concentration but reduces ancestry diversity.

    Report particle count, proposal, resampling rule and threshold, rejuvenation kernels, log-normalizer estimator, effective sample size over time, and repeated-run variability. A large final number of distinct particles does not prove that important early partition choices survived.

    Variational inference¤

    Blei and Jordan (2006) use a truncated stick-breaking variational family for DPMs. Coordinate or stochastic optimization replaces sampling with an ELBO. This can scale well and provide deterministic deployment latency, but introduces several restrictions:

    1. the representational truncation;
    2. the chosen factorization of \(q\);
    3. local optima of the finite optimization;
    4. stochastic-gradient and numerical error.

    An ELBO comparison is meaningful only under the same model and variational conventions. A higher bound does not by itself mean better cluster-count calibration, mixing-measure uncertainty, or downstream decisions.

    Adaptive truncation can grow a variational representation when diagnostics indicate active boundary mass. It remains an approximate-posterior algorithm unless an exact limiting argument and achieved limit are established.

    Amortized inference¤

    An amortized network maps an entire dataset to partition or feature posterior parameters/samples. It pays a training cost to reduce repeated inference cost. The error decomposition includes:

    • target-model posterior approximation;
    • function-class approximation;
    • amortization/optimization error;
    • shift between simulated training problems and deployed datasets;
    • finite-sample Monte Carlo error if the network samples.

    Neural Clustering Processes provide one peer-reviewed construction, with inference organized to respect partition symmetries. A complementary 2024 result, a metalearned neural circuit for nonparametric Bayesian inference, learns a reusable sequential inference procedure from simulated BNP tasks with an open set of classes. Both are evidence that amortization can accelerate repeated inference over a declared training distribution—not that a learned circuit is an exact BNP posterior or calibrated after simulator, model, or dataset shift. They should be compared with conventional samplers on the same model and query, including training simulations in the compute budget.

    Diagnostics that respect the latent object¤

    Use multiple chains, initializations, particle runs, or optimization seeds. Monitor:

    • held-out posterior predictive density and replicated data summaries;
    • \(K_n\), largest block sizes, entropy, and tail mass as distributions, not only means;
    • posterior co-clustering matrices and split/merge frequencies;

    Failure case: finite allocation does not certify finite approximation

    Seeing eight occupied components in every saved sample cannot show that a truncation at ten is harmless. The posterior may place important mass on alternative partitions requiring more atoms, while a sampler trapped near one mode never visits them. Increase the truncation, use different initial partitions, inspect boundary mass, and compare predictive and label-invariant functionals.

    Prior predictive checking¤

    Before fitting, sample complete datasets from the prior. Inspect:

    • \(K_n\) and block-size profiles over the intended sample-size range;
    • per-row and total active features;
    • component scales, overlap, and tail behavior under \(H\) and the likelihood;
    • group-sharing, temporal persistence, and covariate smoothness;
    • replicated observables, not only latent allocations.

    For a DP, a gamma hyperprior on \(\alpha\) creates a distribution over growth curves rather than one curve. It does not remove sensitivity; it moves the choice to hyperprior shape/rate and posterior identifiability. Report those choices and prior-posterior movement.

    Posterior predictive checking¤

    Draw model parameters/allocations from the posterior and replicate observations under the full likelihood. Compare statistics relevant to the task: density tails, multimodality, within-block dispersion, unseen-type rate, feature co-occurrence, group differences, or transition durations. Posterior predictive agreement is a model check, not a proof that the latent partition is true; flexible mixtures can reproduce observables through several latent decompositions.

    Held-out log predictive density is a proper distributional score when computed accurately. It should state whether the value is conditional on a fitted truncation, estimated by Monte Carlo, averaged over posterior draws, or evaluated through a variational approximation. Training likelihood and ELBO are not substitutes.

    Partition and feature decisions¤

    A posterior on partitions is not one partition. If an action needs a point estimate, declare a loss. Variation of information, Binder-type pairwise losses, or application-specific costs lead to different summaries. Report credible uncertainty in co-clustering or block membership rather than only a colored heat map.

    For features, match columns using a permutation-invariant assignment in simulations. In real data, test whether feature-based predictions replicate across cohorts or align with measurements not used to fit the model. Semantic names inferred from the same observations are interpretations, not independent validation.

    Computational calibration¤

    Simulation-based calibration can test an inference implementation by drawing parameters and data from the model, fitting repeatedly, and checking rank/uniformity diagnostics for identifiable functionals. For partitions, use label-invariant summaries. Passing simulation calibration under the assumed model does not establish model adequacy for real data; failing it reveals an algorithm, implementation, or finite-budget problem.

    • chains/particles/seeds and warmup;
    • effective sample size or repeated-run variation;
    • truncation and residual-mass checks;
    • likelihood and gradient evaluations;
    • total training/inference wall-clock, hardware, and peak memory;
    • amortized training simulations and reuse count;

    Cumulative worked study: subpopulations across two laboratories¤

    Suppose two laboratories measure the same biological assay, and the goals are to predict new measurements and learn whether some latent response patterns are shared.

    1. Declare the object and likelihood¤

    Use an HDP mixture only if shared component parameters are scientifically meaningful. Specify \(G_0\), laboratory measures \(G_1,G_2\), and an observation likelihood that includes batch-specific measurement noise. Do not let a shared atom silently mean “the same causal cell type.”

    2. Inspect the prior¤

    Simulate the number and sizes of global/local components at each laboratory's actual sample size. Check how often one laboratory receives components absent from the other and whether the likelihood produces realistic within-component variation.

    3. Fit with two independent algorithms or budgets¤

    For example, compare a collapsed/auxiliary MCMC scheme with a substantially enlarged variational truncation. Agreement in held-out prediction with disagreement in partitions is scientifically informative: the density query may be stable while latent interpretation is not.

    4. Diagnose computation¤

    Run overdispersed chains, inspect label-invariant co-clustering, global atom usage, \(\alpha,\gamma\), residual/truncation mass, and ESS per wall-clock. Hold out complete groups as well as random observations to test transfer.

    5. Match evaluation to the claims¤

    • For prediction: new-observation and new-laboratory proper scores.
    • For sharing: posterior probabilities that measurements co-cluster across laboratories, with sensitivity to batch likelihoods.
    • For biological identity: external markers or interventions not used in fitting.
    • For component count: do not use the HDP occupied count as an estimator of a finite truth without an explicit count model.

    6. State the failure boundary¤

    If a batch shift is absorbed by new mixture components, the model may predict well while inventing laboratory-specific “subtypes.” If the likelihood underestimates heavy tails, it may split one population. If aggressive sharing is imposed, rare genuine group-specific structure may disappear. These are model and query failures, not merely sampler bugs.

    Open research questions¤

    1. Query-specific consistency under misspecification. Which density, partition, rare-type, and decision functionals remain calibrated when the component likelihood is wrong?
    2. Scalable posterior uncertainty. Can amortized, variational, SMC, and distributed methods retain calibrated tail and partition uncertainty at modern data scale?
    3. Dependent-measure validity and interpretation. How should flexible covariate-dependent measures trade marginal semantics, atom sharing, smoothness, and identifiable change?
    4. Neural/random-measure composition. Which neural likelihoods preserve useful random-measure inductive bias rather than making allocations arbitrary explanations of a universal decoder?
    5. Robust rare-event and unseen-type prediction. How should power-law, sampling-bias, censoring, and privacy constraints enter species and trait models?
    6. Reproducible latent-structure evaluation. Which benchmark designs separate predictive fit, algorithmic calibration, latent recovery, and scientific validity?

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. The result supports the narrower claim that the implementation can be calibrated for \(K_n\) when data truly come from the tested simulator and budgets. It does not establish real-data model adequacy; tail underprediction is direct evidence of a model, preprocessing, inference-at-deployment, or shift failure that must be localized. Thus “both” is unjustified, while “neither” throws away the simulator-scoped computational evidence.
    2. Density estimation targets the posterior predictive and uses held-out proper scores against matched finite/flexible density baselines; training likelihood is invalid. Unseen-category prediction targets discovery/frequency functionals and uses held-out new-type calibration against species-sampling or finite-count baselines; posterior mean \(K_n\) alone is invalid. Scientific feature discovery targets \(p(Z,A\mid x)\) up to column equivalence and needs permutation-invariant recovery in simulation plus external measurements/interventions in reality, against finite sparse-factor baselines; naming a visualized decoder feature is invalid evidence.
    3. Co-clustering reports pairwise posterior uncertainty, the variation-of-information action is one loss-optimal summary of that posterior, and external labels are measurements defined outside the fitted allocation. Report the matrix with uncertainty, the declared loss and chosen partition, and agreement/disagreement with independently justified labels; do not retroactively define components by those labels or call the action the posterior itself.
    4. Repeatedly draw \(G\), latent structure, and observations from the declared prior/likelihood, refit with the full algorithm, and rank the true \(G(A)\) among posterior draws. For partitions use a label-invariant scalar such as number of co-clustered pairs or \(K_n\) under conditions where it is identifiable, and check rank uniformity across sample sizes, hyperparameters, truncations, and budgets. Passing under this simulator cannot prove the likelihood or latent semantics fit real data.
    5. For sketches, the source defines Poisson–Kingman/trait random-measure priors and posterior frequency/cardinality recovery from declared sketch summaries. The replication should implement one published model and sketch protocol, reproduce its posterior query and uncertainty on the stated synthetic or benchmark setting, and compare with the declared baseline. It must not claim lossless recovery for arbitrary sketches, streams, priors, or misspecified populations.
    6. Preregister realistic prior simulations for component counts, sharing, noise, and tails; hold out both observations and an entire laboratory; compare overdispersed MCMC with a substantially enlarged variational budget; and record predictive scores, co-clustering uncertainty, residual mass, convergence, hardware, wall-clock, and seeds. Test batch-shift and no-sharing alternatives, use markers/interventions withheld from fitting for identity claims, and revise or withdraw subtype language when prediction is stable but partitions, external evidence, or batch sensitivity are not.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Bayesian nonparametric random-measure models make distributions and latent structure uncertain without fixing one finite catalog. Their value depends on a complete likelihood, a query-matched prior, a calibrated posterior algorithm, and evaluation that does not turn density components into scientific facts. The family remains current through hierarchical, dependent, compressed-data, and neural compositions, while its classical consistency and identifiability warnings remain fully active.

    Final synthesis¤

    The family can be read as one generative grammar:

    1. draw a random measure or allocation law;
    2. instantiate a finite subset through observations;
    3. generate data through an explicit likelihood;
    4. condition on data using a declared finite algorithm;
    5. evaluate the posterior functional that matches the real question.

    Dirichlet processes, Pitman–Yor processes, normalized CRMs, beta processes, IBPs, HDPs, and dependent random measures change different parts of this grammar. Neural networks can extend likelihoods and inference, but they do not erase the need to state the random object, exchangeability/dependence assumptions, approximation, or evidence boundary.
