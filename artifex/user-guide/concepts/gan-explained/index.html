    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A generative adversarial network defines an implicit generator \(x=G_\theta(z)\) and trains it through a learned comparison function, usually called a discriminator or critic. The generator distribution, comparison objective, finite two-player optimization algorithm, and deployed sampler are distinct objects; the guarantees of an ideal population game do not automatically describe a trained neural system.

    • Adversarial Objective

      Couple a generator with a learned density-ratio, divergence, or integral-probability-metric witness

    • Implicit Distribution

      Define samples by transforming latent randomness without requiring tractable pointwise likelihood evaluation

    • Fidelity–Coverage Trade-offs

      Measure both perceptual fidelity and support coverage under a declared data, architecture, and optimization protocol

    • Two-Player Optimization

      Analyze generator and discriminator updates as a coupled dynamical system rather than one scalar minimization problem

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    The original logistic GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014) learns a generator and binary discriminator from real and generated samples. Other adversarial generators replace the logistic witness with an \(f\)-divergence estimator, Wasserstein critic, projection discriminator, or task-specific comparison. Adversarial objectives also train few-step distilled diffusion samplers (Sauer et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024); those systems remain hybrid compositions rather than pure GANs. Architecture, benchmark, and latency comparisons appear later with their data, conditioning, guidance, evaluation, and network-evaluation protocols.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be comfortable with expectations, binary cross-entropy, probability densities or probability masses, KL and Jensen–Shannon divergence, and gradient-based optimization. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. define an implicit generator as a pushforward probability law and determine when an ordinary output density need not exist;
    2. write the minimax and non-saturating objectives without conflating them;
    3. derive the optimal logistic discriminator under arbitrary real/fake class priors;
    4. distinguish proper class-probability scoring, variational \(f\)-divergence estimation, IPMs, and moment matching;
    5. reduce the ideal minimax value to Jensen–Shannon divergence;
    6. explain why this population result does not prove convergence of a finite neural game; and
    7. diagnose why 50% discriminator accuracy is not sufficient evidence of distribution matching.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    From a deterministic map to a probability law¤

    Let \(Z\sim P_Z\) on a latent space \(\mathcal Z\) and let \(G_\theta:\mathcal Z\to\mathcal X\) be measurable. The generated random object is

    \[ X=G_\theta(Z), \]

    and its law is the pushforward

    \[ P_\theta(A) = P_Z\!\left(G_\theta^{-1}(A)\right), \qquad A\subseteq\mathcal X\ \text{measurable}. \]

    This definition needs neither an inverse map nor a density. It says how probability assigned to latent events becomes probability assigned to generated events. The phrase implicit distribution means that sampling and differentiation through the construction may be available even when evaluating a normalized probability density or mass \(p_\theta(x)\) is not.

    The dimensions and regularity of \(G_\theta\) determine which stronger statements are possible:

    Table GAN.2. From a deterministic map to a probability law
    Construction Probability object Density consequence
    \(m=d\) and \(G_\theta:\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^d\) is a differentiable bijection pushforward law plus an inverse the change-of-variables formula gives an ordinary density where its assumptions hold; this is the normalizing-flow case
    \(m<d\) and \(G_\theta:\mathbb R^m\to\mathbb R^d\) is locally Lipschitz law concentrated on an at-most-\(m\)-dimensional image the law is generally singular with respect to \(d\)-dimensional Lebesgue measure, so an ambient density need not exist
    \(m>d\) or \(G_\theta\) is many-to-one probability from all latent preimages a density may exist under rank and integrability conditions, but the one-inverse Jacobian formula is unavailable
    \(Y=G_\theta(Z)+\varepsilon\) with declared observation noise convolution or observation-model law suitable full-dimensional noise can produce an ambient density, while changing the model and its queries

    Finite geometric example. Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Uniform}[-1,1]\) and \(G(z)=(z,z^2)\). Every sample lies on a parabola in \(\mathbb R^2\). Any two-dimensional region that intersects the curve can receive probability, but the curve itself has two-dimensional area zero. The generated law is therefore perfectly well defined and directly sampleable while having no ordinary density with respect to planar area. Adding independent Gaussian observation noise produces a full-dimensional density around the curve; it does not retroactively make the noiseless law nonsingular.

    Discrete output is not the definition of explicitness

    A categorical or token-valued generator is explicit only if it supplies tractable normalized probability masses for the requested outputs. A policy-gradient text GAN may use an explicit autoregressive policy; a hard quantizer after an implicit continuous generator need not expose tractable masses. Conversely, a continuous normalizing flow is explicit. State-space type and density access are separate taxonomy axes.

    What must a sample-based comparison accomplish?¤

    Suppose real observations follow \(P\) and generated observations follow \(Q_\theta\), but only samples are available. A useful comparison objective must answer three separate questions:

    1. Identification: under what witness class does its population optimum distinguish \(P=Q_\theta\) from \(P\ne Q_\theta\)?
    2. Estimation: can finite samples estimate the objective or its gradient with controlled bias and variance?
    3. Optimization: does the implemented parameterization, regularization, and update rule expose a useful finite gradient?

    The third property does not follow from the first two. A discrepancy can identify equality in population yet be poorly estimated, saturated, or optimized by a cycling game. Conversely, a restricted neural witness can provide a useful local gradient while no longer equal to the named population divergence.

    Table GAN.3. What must a sample-based comparison accomplish?
    Comparison route Population witness Quantity exposed to training Alternating learned witness required?
    Proper class-probability scoring posterior real-class probability, hence prior-corrected density ratio classification risk or a linked divergence at the unrestricted optimum usually
    Variational \(f\)-divergence measurable critic in a convex-conjugate representation a lower bound when the critic family is restricted usually
    Integral probability metric test function from a declared class \(\mathcal F\) difference of expectations; the class defines the metric or pseudometric for learned Wasserstein or learned-kernel critics, but not always
    Fixed feature moments a declared vector \(s(x)\) squared difference between empirical moments no
    Fixed-kernel MMD unit ball of an RKHS kernel two-sample statistic no

    The routes can yield related finite games, but their probability semantics are not interchangeable. Objectives and Statistical Distances develops the common machinery; this chapter follows the consequences for adversarial generators.

    The Adversarial Game¤

    GANs operate through a minimax game between a generator \(G\) that creates synthetic data and a discriminator \(D\) that distinguishes real from fake samples. The generator learns to transform random noise \(z\) into realistic outputs \(G(z)\), while the discriminator estimates the probability that samples originated from real data.

    The mathematical foundation rests on the minimax objective. In the logistic formulation below, \(D(x)\in(0,1)\) is the posterior probability assigned to the real class under an equal mixture of real and generated examples:

    \[ \min_G \max_D V(D, G) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{data}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))] \]

    Where:

    • \(G\) is the generator that maps latent vectors \(z\) to data space
    • \(D\) is the discriminator that outputs probability of input being real
    • \(p_{data}\) is the true data distribution
    • \(p_z\) is the prior distribution over latent variables (typically Gaussian)
    • \(V(D,G)\) measures how well the discriminator distinguishes real from fake

    The discriminator maximizes this objective by improving its logarithmic class-probability score. The generator minimizes a term computed through that fitted discriminator. Only the population-optimal discriminator has the Bayes posterior interpretation derived below; a finite neural output is an uncalibrated estimate unless calibration is separately established.

    Mathematical Foundation: Theoretical Analysis¤

    Optimal Discriminator:

    For a fixed generator \(G\), the optimal discriminator takes the form:

    \[ D^*_G(x) = \frac{p_{data}(x)}{p_{data}(x) + p_g(x)} \]

    where \(p_g\) represents the generator's distribution with respect to a common dominating measure. This result emerges by maximizing pointwise: \(a\log y+b\log(1-y)\) has maximizer \(a/(a+b)\) when \(a+b>0\). With unequal class priors, the numerator and denominator acquire the corresponding prior weights, so the familiar formula is not prior-free.

    Interpretation

    The optimal discriminator is the Bayes classifier for the declared real/fake mixture. A neural discriminator trained for finitely many updates is an estimator of this object, not the object itself.

    Global Optimum:

    After optimizing over all measurable discriminators, the generator-side value has its global minimum when \(p_g=p_{data}\) almost everywhere, where it reaches:

    \[ C(G) = -\log(4) \]

    By substituting the optimal discriminator into the objective:

    \[ C(G) = -\log(4) + 2 \cdot \text{JSD}(p_{data} \| p_g) \]

    where \(\text{JSD}\) denotes Jensen–Shannon divergence. Thus the ideal minimax game with an optimal unrestricted discriminator has a JS-divergence interpretation. Alternating stochastic gradients with a restricted, non-optimal discriminator do not literally minimize this reduced scalar objective at every step.

    Proper scoring rules and density-ratio recovery¤

    Introduce a class label \(Y\in\{1,0\}\) for real and generated observations, with \(P(Y=1)=\pi\). The Bayes posterior is

    \[ D_\pi^*(x) = P(Y=1\mid X=x) = \frac{\pi p(x)}{\pi p(x)+(1-\pi)q_\theta(x)}. \]

    Where the ratio is defined,

    \[ \frac{p(x)}{q_\theta(x)} = \frac{1-\pi}{\pi} \frac{D_\pi^*(x)}{1-D_\pi^*(x)}. \]

    Under the common equal real/fake class prior, \(\pi=1/2\), the prior-odds factor is one and the expression reduces to \(D^*(x)/(1-D^*(x))\).

    A strictly proper binary scoring rule makes the true class posterior the unique population optimum over unrestricted probability reports. Logarithmic loss and the Brier score are examples. A neural classifier can miss that optimum because of finite data, restricted capacity, regularization, or optimization. Moreover, not every useful adversarial classification loss is a proper probability score: the ordinary hinge loss is a margin surrogate and its raw optimum should not be read as a calibrated posterior without an additional link and assumptions. Probability estimation, classification decisions, and generator gradients are three different contracts (Reid and Williamson, 2010).

    For the two-point example below, change the real-class prior from \(1/2\) to \(\pi=3/4\). Then

    \[ D_\pi^*(a)=\frac{(3/4)(3/4)}{(3/4)(3/4)+(1/4)(1/4)}=\frac9{10}, \qquad D_\pi^*(b)=\frac{(3/4)(1/4)}{(3/4)(1/4)+(1/4)(3/4)}=\frac12. \]

    The raw odds are \(9\) and \(1\), but multiplying by \((1-\pi)/\pi=1/3\) recovers the density ratios \(3\) and \(1/3\). A class-balanced minibatch is therefore part of the familiar uncorrected-odds formula, not an irrelevant implementation detail.

    Variational \(f\)-divergence witnesses¤

    For a convex \(f\) with \(f(1)=0\), an \(f\)-divergence has the convex-conjugate representation

    \[ D_f(P\|Q) = \sup_T \left\{ \mathbb E_P[T(X)] - \mathbb E_Q[f^*(T(X))] \right\}, \]

    under the required measure-theoretic and integrability conditions. The supremum is over the admissible measurable functions that make the identity valid. Restricting \(T\) to a neural family \(\mathcal T_\phi\) gives

    \[ \sup_{T\in\mathcal T_\phi} \left\{ \mathbb E_P[T(X)] - \mathbb E_Q[f^*(T(X))] \right\} \le D_f(P\|Q). \]

    This is the basis of \(f\)-GAN (Nowozin, Cseke, and Tomioka, 2016). The restricted critic supplies a lower bound for fixed \(Q\). Minimizing that bound with respect to the generator does not guarantee that the true divergence decreases: the bound can loosen while the named divergence stays fixed or grows. Critic approximation and generator optimization must therefore remain visible.

    IPMs, Wasserstein critics, MMD, and moments¤

    An integral probability metric (IPM) is defined by a witness class:

    \[ d_{\mathcal F}(P,Q) = \sup_{f\in\mathcal F} \left( \mathbb E_P[f(X)]-\mathbb E_Q[f(X)] \right). \]

    The unit ball of 1-Lipschitz functions yields the Wasserstein-1 dual under its domain and finite-first-moment assumptions. The unit ball of a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space yields MMD. A characteristic kernel makes zero population MMD identify equality, while a non-characteristic kernel or restricted neural feature map may miss differences. Fixed-kernel MMD and fixed finite moments can train a generator directly; learning the feature map to maximize the discrepancy introduces another game.

    Finite moment matching makes the identification issue especially concrete:

    \[ L_s(\theta) = \left\| \mathbb E_P[s(X)]-\mathbb E_{Q_\theta}[s(X)] \right\|_2^2. \]

    If \(s(x)=x\), matching the mean cannot distinguish distributions with different variances. Enlarging or learning \(s\) can improve discrimination but changes estimation, regularization, and optimization. GMMN uses MMD for direct implicit-generator training (Li, Swersky, and Zemel, 2015); it is neighboring sample-comparison training, not evidence that every implicit generator must contain a discriminator.

    Support mismatch changes both theory and implementation

    Ratio-based divergences can become infinite or locally uninformative when singular supports do not overlap, while Wasserstein distance can remain sensitive to how far supports are separated. A smooth restricted classifier can nevertheless supply gradients between separated empirical supports. That gradient belongs to the restricted learned game; it does not prove that the underlying \(f\)-divergence is smooth or decreasing. Instance noise changes the compared distributions by smoothing them, and annealing the noise defines a sequence of games rather than a free numerical trick.

    Four Objects, Not One “GAN Loss”¤

    The density-ratio and JS results are often compressed into the sentence “a GAN minimizes JS divergence.” That sentence erases the order of operations. Keep these four objects distinct:

    Table GAN.4. Four Objects, Not One “GAN Loss”
    Object What is held fixed or optimized Mathematical role
    Bayes discriminator \(D_q^*\) fix the current model law \(q\); optimize over all admissible classifiers recovers a prior-corrected density ratio where the ratio is defined
    reduced population value \(C(q)=V(D_q^*,q)\) substitute the optimized discriminator into the original minimax value equals \(-\log4+2D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p_{data}\|q)\) under the equal-prior convention used here
    implemented generator loss hold the current finite discriminator fixed while updating \(G_\theta\) may use the minimax term, non-saturating loss, feature matching, or another declared surrogate
    alternating training trajectory update finite networks from minibatches with optimizer state and a chosen update schedule follows a coupled dynamical system; it need not trace gradient descent on \(C(q_\theta)\)

    The finite classifier-to-ratio example computes all four distinctions. In particular, the minimax and non-saturating generator losses have different frozen-discriminator derivatives even when both point toward distribution matching in that example. The density-ratio result characterizes the auxiliary population optimum; it does not make every generator surrogate a divergence derivative.

    Worked example: a two-point game¤

    Let the sample space be \(\{a,b\}\), with \(p_{data}=(3/4,1/4)\) and \(p_g=(1/4,3/4)\). The pointwise optimal discriminator is

    \[ D^*(a)=\frac{3/4}{3/4+1/4}=\frac34, \qquad D^*(b)=\frac{1/4}{1/4+3/4}=\frac14. \]

    The mixture \(m=(p_{data}+p_g)/2=(1/2,1/2)\), so

    \[ \operatorname{JSD}(p_{data}\|p_g) =\frac12\left[\frac34\log\frac32+\frac14\log\frac12\right] +\frac12\left[\frac14\log\frac12+\frac34\log\frac32\right] =\frac34\log\frac32+\frac14\log\frac12. \]

    Substitution into \(C(G)=-\log4+2\operatorname{JSD}\) gives the same value as summing \(p_{data}(x)\log D^*(x)+p_g(x)\log(1-D^*(x))\) over \(a,b\). If the generator moves to \((3/4,1/4)\), JSD becomes zero and \(D^*(a)=D^*(b)=1/2\). This finite example exposes every assumption hidden by the continuous notation.

    Training Dynamics¤

    The training alternates between two steps:

    1. Discriminator Update (maximize \(V\))

    Train \(D\) to maximize the probability of correctly classifying real and fake samples:

    \[ \max_D \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{data}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))] \]

    2. Generator Update (minimize \(V\))

    Train \(G\) to maximize the probability of discriminator being wrong:

    \[ \min_G \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))] \]

    In practice, we often use the non-saturating generator loss for better gradients (Goodfellow et al., 2014, §3):

    \[ \max_G \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log D(G(z))] \]

    Why Non-Saturating Loss?

    With a sigmoid discriminator, the saturating generator objective can have a weak parameter gradient when generated samples are confidently rejected. The non-saturating alternative supplies a stronger early gradient and has the same ideal fixed point, but it is not the generator half of the original minimax value and should not be described as literal JS minimization during alternating training. Support mismatch, discriminator approximation, parameterization, and game dynamics all matter.

    Nash Equilibrium¤

    The ideal population game has a Nash equilibrium at distribution matching where:

    1. The generator produces samples indistinguishable from real data: \(p_g = p_{data}\)
    2. The discriminator outputs \(\frac{1}{2}\) everywhere (cannot tell real from fake)
    3. Neither network can improve by changing its strategy alone

    Equilibrium vs. Reality

    This statement characterizes an ideal equilibrium; it is not a convergence theorem for simultaneous or alternating neural-network optimization. Finite games can cycle, diverge, approach a local stationary point, or be limited by discriminator and generator capacity. “Local Nash equilibrium,” “stationary point,” and “low FID checkpoint” are different claims.

    Counterexample: 50% accuracy without a good generator¤

    Suppose the real data contain cats and dogs equally, the generator emits only cats, and the discriminator is an untrained constant \(D(x)=1/2\). Its balanced accuracy is 50%, yet the generator has dropped the dog mode. The same accuracy can therefore mean ideal matching, an underfit critic, or a threshold that hides score calibration. Always pair game losses with held-out critic behavior, samples, precision/recall or coverage measures, and training dynamics.

    Generator Network¤

    The generator transforms random noise into realistic samples:

    Key Design Choices:

    • Input: A declared latent distribution such as \(z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\). Latent dimension is a capacity and geometry choice to validate, not a universal 64–512 rule.
    • Architecture Options:
    • MLP for simple data (MNIST, toy datasets)
    • Transposed convolutions (DCGAN) for images
    • ResNet blocks for complex images
    • Normalization: Batch normalization was central to DCGAN, while modern ResNet, StyleGAN, and transformer generators use different normalization and modulation schemes.
    • Activation: The output parameterization must match the data transform and observation domain. Tanh is convenient for images scaled to \([-1,1]\); it is not mandatory.
    • Output: Same shape as target data

    DCGAN Guidelines

    These are the successful historical recipe from Radford et al., not laws of GAN design:

    • Replace pooling with strided convolutions
    • Use batch normalization in both networks (except output/input layers)
    • Remove fully connected hidden layers for deeper architectures
    • Use ReLU in generator (except output), LeakyReLU in discriminator
    • Use Tanh activation in generator output

    Discriminator or critic network¤

    The network produces a score whose semantics come from the declared objective. Logistic class-probability training may view a sigmoid-transformed score as a real-class probability; hinge and Wasserstein objectives do not:

    Key Design Choices:

    • Input: Real or generated samples
    • Architecture: Convolutional, residual, transformer, graph, sequence, or task-specific backbones; receptive field and invariances define which differences the finite witness can detect
    • Normalization and control: Batch normalization is historical in DCGAN, while spectral normalization, gradient penalties, or no normalization appear in other objectives; batch-coupled layers are incompatible with some per-sample penalties
    • Activation: LeakyReLU with \(\alpha=0.2\) is a DCGAN setting, not a family requirement
    • Output: A sigmoid probability for probability-based logistic cross-entropy, or an unconstrained scalar logit/critic value for logits-based logistic, hinge, least-squares, Wasserstein, and many relativistic objectives.

    Common Pitfalls

    • Pooling, strided convolutions, and resize-convolution blocks are alternatives with different aliasing and inductive biases.
    • Batch-normalization placement depends on the objective and architecture; per-example gradient penalties are especially sensitive to batch-coupled critics.
    • Match the generator output to preprocessing: tanh for a bounded \([-1,1]\) convention, sigmoid for \([0,1]\) when appropriate, or an unconstrained output for continuous data.
    • Balance estimator quality and game dynamics. A capable critic is necessary for a useful discrepancy, but too many or poorly regularized critic updates can create uninformative generator gradients.

    The Learned Comparison Signal¤

    Alternating witness and generator updates¤

    At a generator iterate \(\theta_k\), discriminator training estimates a witness that separates samples from \(p_{\mathrm{data}}\) and \(p_{\theta_k}\). The generator update then changes \(\theta\) in the direction favored by that fitted witness. The next discriminator is trained against the changed generator distribution, so the objective surface itself evolves during optimization. In the ideal equal-prior logistic population analysis, an optimal discriminator equals \(1/2\) almost everywhere on \(\{x:p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)+p_{\theta_k}(x)>0\}\) exactly when the two laws agree there. Outside that union of supports the objective does not determine its value. Finite 50% classification accuracy is not equivalent to the population condition: a weak discriminator, calibration error, finite batches, or unobserved support gaps can give the same statistic.

    What information reaches the generator¤

    In the basic unconditional adversarial generator update, the generator consumes \(z\) rather than a paired real target. Its gradient nevertheless depends on real data because the discriminator was fitted using real examples. Conditional, reconstruction-augmented, feature-matching, and distillation systems can feed real or teacher targets into additional generator losses. This distinction is more precise than saying that the generator “never sees” real data.

    • VAEs: Optimize a likelihood bound containing an expected log likelihood and a posterior-to-prior KL; “reconstruction loss” is shorthand for the negative conditional log likelihood
    • Autoregressive models: Directly maximize likelihood of training data
    • GANs: Implicitly minimize distribution divergence through adversarial dynamics

    An adversarial critic can penalize unrealistic high-frequency statistics that a fixed-variance pixel likelihood may average. It does not guarantee sharpness, realism, or mode coverage, and richer likelihood models need not be blurry.

    Common Misconceptions¤

    Training Myths

    • Myth: Losses should decrease monotonically
      • Reality: Player losses need not be monotone because each player changes the other's objective. Oscillation can occur in a useful run, but it is not evidence of health by itself.
    • Myth: Generator "wins" when discriminator accuracy approaches 50%
      • Reality: It is ambiguous. It can occur at the ideal population equilibrium, with a weak or under-trained discriminator, or because finite-batch accuracy hides calibration and support errors. Inspect held-out scores, samples, diversity, and game dynamics.
    • Myth: Lower loss always means better samples
      • Reality: A loss value is interpretable only with its sign, reduction, opponent state, regularization terms, and held-out protocol. Pair it with distributional, conditional, memorization, and visual diagnostics.
    • Myth: Every oscillation proves training has failed
      • Reality: A two-player game can oscillate even when each loss is locally improving, but instability is not inevitable. Architecture, regularisation, update rules, and the local game Jacobian determine the dynamics.
    • Myth: Mode collapse indicates insufficient training
      • Reality: More updates can preserve, worsen, or move a collapse. Diagnose latent use, data and conditioning pipelines, critic generalization, coverage, and game dynamics before choosing an intervention.

    The Discriminator's Role¤

    The discriminator's role extends beyond simply classifying real versus fake:

    • Provides training signal to the generator
    • Implicitly defines the loss function through its architecture and training
    • Learns useful feature representations exploitable for downstream tasks
    • Can induce a task-specific feature discrepancy over the distributions and augmentations on which it is trained; this is not a guaranteed perceptual metric outside that protocol

    In the ideal WGAN dual (Arjovsky et al., 2017), the supremum over all admissible 1-Lipschitz functions equals Wasserstein-1 distance. A finite, imperfectly constrained critic supplies one witness value—a lower bound on that supremum—rather than an automatically calibrated distance estimate. This changes the witness class and losses, but does not by itself determine the finite training dynamics.

    Worked example: audit the last layer before training¤

    Consider 32×32 RGB images preprocessed to \([-1,1]\) and a logistic non-saturating loss implemented with logits. A coherent pair is:

    1. \(G:\mathbb R^{128}\rightarrow\mathbb R^{3\times32\times32}\) with a final tanh;
    2. \(D:\mathbb R^{3\times32\times32}\rightarrow\mathbb R\) with no final sigmoid; and
    3. a numerically stable softplus or binary-cross-entropy-with-logits implementation that applies the logistic transformation inside the loss.

    Adding a sigmoid to \(D\) and then passing its output to a “with logits” loss applies the sigmoid semantics twice. Removing tanh from \(G\) while leaving real images bounded can make range an easy shortcut. Replacing the logistic loss with WGAN requires interpreting the scalar as a critic value, enforcing a suitable Lipschitz-control strategy, and changing both losses—not merely deleting the sigmoid.

    Mode Collapse¤

    Mode collapse or mode dropping occurs when the generator assigns too little or no probability to important regions of the target distribution. Severe collapse can make outputs nearly identical, but partial collapse can retain substantial within-mode variety while omitting whole subpopulations.

    Possible symptoms:

    • Many latent inputs produce nearly identical outputs
    • Generated samples cluster into too few semantic or geometric regions
    • Missing modes in the data distribution

    Interventions to test:

    • Minibatch discrimination (Salimans et al., 2016): let the discriminator see batch-wise feature similarities, so it can directly punish "everything looks the same".
    • Unrolled GANs (Metz et al., 2017): unroll \(k\) discriminator update steps when computing the generator gradient, so \(G\) optimises against a "forward-looking" \(D\) rather than the current step.
    • Experience replay / past-sample buffers (Shrivastava et al., 2017): mix in older fakes so \(D\) doesn't forget previously-defeated modes.
    • PacGAN (Lin et al., 2018): give \(D\) packs of \(m\) samples instead of singletons. The paper proves, under its mode-collapse and hypothesis-testing formalisation, that packing penalises collapsed generator distributions more strongly; it does not guarantee that finite neural GAN training will recover every data mode.
    • Top-k generator updates (Sinha et al., 2020): in each generator step, only back-propagate through the top-k most-realistic generated samples in the batch.
    • Mixtures or ensembles: allocate capacity across generators or combine independently trained models, then measure aggregate coverage. Merely selecting one member does not guarantee recovery of missing modes.

    Vanishing Gradients¤

    When the discriminator becomes too confident, it provides vanishing gradients to the generator.

    Problem stated at the right variable. Let \(s=D_{\mathrm{logit}}(G_\theta(z))\) and let the reported probability be \(\sigma(s)\). For the minimax generator term,

    \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial s}\log(1-\sigma(s))=-\sigma(s), \]

    which approaches zero when a generated sample is confidently rejected. By contrast, the non-saturating loss has

    \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial s}\big[-\log\sigma(s)\big]=-(1-\sigma(s)), \]

    which approaches \(-1\) in the same limit. Saying merely that \(\partial\log(1-D)/\partial D\) vanishes would be false; saturation arises after composing the loss with the sigmoid score and generator.

    Solutions:

    1. Non-saturating loss (Goodfellow et al., 2014): use \(\max_G \mathbb{E}[\log D(G(z))]\) instead.
    2. Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017): optimize a Kantorovich–Rubinstein dual objective over an approximately Lipschitz critic. Lipschitz control bounds sensitivity; it does not guarantee that the finite critic supplies a useful nonzero gradient everywhere.
    3. Least Squares GAN (Mao et al., 2017): replace the cross-entropy classifier with the squared-error objective \(\mathbb{E}[(D(G(z)) - 1)^2]\).
    4. Relativistic objectives (Jolicoeur-Martineau, 2018): score real samples relative to generated samples. R3GAN later combines a particular relativistic paired loss with zero-centered regularization and proves a local convergence result under its stated assumptions; that theorem does not transfer automatically to every relativistic loss.

    Training Instability¤

    GAN optimization adds coupled-estimation problems beyond ordinary fixed-target supervised learning:

    The Optimizer Geometry and Coupled Learning Dynamics chapter derives the bilinear rotation example, separates Fisher from empirical-Fisher curvature, and states the local conditions under which common GAN regularizers have convergence evidence.

    Gradients and Geometry in Generative Modeling then separates simultaneous, alternating, extragradient, unrolled, implicit, natural, and transport-pullback derivatives—and explains why positive-definite preconditioning alone does not remove game rotation.

    • No per-latent target in the basic unconditional game—the critic supplies a learned training signal, while evaluation still requires independent definitions of fidelity, coverage, conditioning, and harm
    • Loss landscape continuously shifts as both networks update
    • Stationary points, cycles, and local game behavior depend on the parameterization and update rule; a pure-strategy equilibrium need not exist in a restricted nonconvex parameter game even though mixed-strategy existence and the ideal population equilibrium are separate results
    • Small hyperparameter changes can cause dramatic differences in convergence
    • Two competing objectives must be balanced rather than minimizing a single loss

    Worked dynamical example: one game, three trajectories¤

    Consider the scalar zero-sum game

    \[ \min_\theta\max_\phi V(\theta,\phi), \qquad V(\theta,\phi)=\theta\phi. \]

    Its simultaneous gradient vector field is \((-\phi,+\theta)\). In continuous time,

    \[ \dot\theta=-\phi, \qquad \dot\phi=\theta, \qquad \frac{d}{dt}(\theta^2+\phi^2)=0. \]

    The state circles the equilibrium instead of approaching it. Simultaneous Euler updates with step size \(\eta\) give

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}\theta_{k+1}\\\phi_{k+1}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1&-\eta\\\eta&1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\theta_k\\\phi_k\end{bmatrix}, \qquad \theta_{k+1}^2+\phi_{k+1}^2 =(1+\eta^2)(\theta_k^2+\phi_k^2). \tag{GAN.1}\label{eq:gan-bilinear-simultaneous-radius} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:gan-bilinear-simultaneous-radius}\) makes the discretization failure explicit: every nonzero state spirals outward for every \(\eta>0\). If the discriminator instead uses the just-updated generator value,

    \[ \theta_{k+1}=\theta_k-\eta\phi_k, \qquad \phi_{k+1}=\phi_k+\eta\theta_{k+1}, \]

    the update matrix has determinant one and trace \(2-\eta^2\). For \(0<\eta<2\), its eigenvalues lie on the unit circle: the iterates are bounded under the corresponding invariant quadratic form but still need not converge. Thus the population equilibrium, continuous vector field, simultaneous discretization, and alternating discretization are four different claims.

    The game Jacobian at the equilibrium is

    \[ J= \begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}, \]

    with eigenvalues \(\pm i\). Purely imaginary directions expose rotation; negative real parts would be needed for local asymptotic stability of the continuous system. Neural GAN Jacobians add self-curvature, cross-player blocks, optimizer state, stochastic noise, and regularizers, but the two-dimensional calculation explains why lowering each player's instantaneous loss is not a convergence proof.

    Discrete outputs require an estimator choice¤

    For a differentiable continuous generator, write \(h_\phi(x)=\ell(D_\phi(x))\). The latent-noise construction then gives the pathwise derivative

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L(\theta) &= \mathbb E_{Z\sim P_Z}[h_\phi(G_\theta(Z))],\\ \nabla_\theta \mathcal L(\theta) &= \mathbb E_{Z\sim P_Z} [\nabla_\theta h_\phi(G_\theta(Z))]. \end{aligned} \]

    when exchanging differentiation and expectation is justified. A hard categorical sample or argmax breaks that differentiable path. Three common responses answer different mathematical problems:

    Table GAN.5. Discrete outputs require an estimator choice
    Route Generator object Gradient and boundary
    Score-function estimator explicit normalized policy \(q_\theta(x)\) unbiased under standard support and integrability conditions, but often high variance
    Continuous relaxation differentiable soft state used by the critic low-variance pathwise gradient for a relaxed game; bias appears when the deployed hard state differs
    Straight-through estimator hard forward state, surrogate backward derivative generally biased; useful behavior is empirical rather than a derivative identity

    For an explicit discrete policy and reward \(R_\phi(x)\) supplied by a fixed critic,

    \[ \nabla_\theta\mathbb E_{X\sim q_\theta}[R_\phi(X)] = \mathbb E_{X\sim q_\theta}\!\left[ R_\phi(X)\nabla_\theta\log q_\theta(X) \right]. \]

    This estimator needs tractable policy log probabilities even though the adversarial objective does not need a data likelihood. A baseline independent of the sampled action can reduce variance without changing the expectation.

    Finite check. Let \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(p)\) with \(p=\sigma(\theta)\) and rewards \(R(1)=r_1\), \(R(0)=r_0\). Then

    \[ J(\theta)=pr_1+(1-p)r_0, \qquad \frac{dJ}{d\theta}=p(1-p)(r_1-r_0). \]

    Because \(\partial_\theta\log q_\theta(X)=X-p\),

    \[ \mathbb E[R(X)(X-p)] =pr_1(1-p)+(1-p)r_0(-p) =p(1-p)(r_1-r_0), \]

    which recovers the exact derivative. The equality does not remove Monte Carlo variance, long-sequence credit assignment, critic drift, or exposure to prefixes omitted by pretraining. Discrete state space, explicit policy probability, and stable adversarial training remain separate properties.

    Stability Techniques

    Architectural:

    • Batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) — except discriminator input / generator output, per the DCGAN guidelines.
    • Spectral normalization (Miyato et al., 2018) — normalize linear operators by an estimated largest singular value. Per-layer bounds compose into a network-level upper bound only after accounting for activations, residual branches, convolution-operator estimation, and other paths; it is not an automatic exact 1-Lipschitz certificate for an arbitrary discriminator.
    • Self-attention for long-range dependencies (SAGAN, Zhang et al., 2019).

    Algorithmic:

    • Two-Time-scale Update Rule (TTUR) (Heusel et al., 2017): update the players on separated stochastic-approximation time scales. The paper proves convergence to a stationary local Nash equilibrium under assumptions on step-size sequences, noise, dynamics, and boundedness; “always use a larger discriminator learning rate” is a practical interpretation, not the theorem itself.
    • Gradient penalties — WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al., 2017), R1/R2 zero-centred GP (Mescheder et al., 2018). In the setting analyzed by Mescheder et al., the one-centred WGAN-GP construction does not generally guarantee local convergence, whereas the zero-centred penalties yield local convergence under the paper's regularity, equilibrium, and update assumptions. This is not a theorem for every architecture and optimizer.
    • Label smoothing (Salimans et al., 2016) — one-sided on real labels only.

    Hyperparameter:

    • Tune learning rates jointly with batch size, loss scale, regularization frequency, and architecture; \(10^{-4}\)-scale values are historical starting points, not a safe universal interval.
    • Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with \(\beta_1=0.5,\beta_2=0.999\) is the DCGAN setting; later recipes commonly use different moments.
    • Treat the number of critic updates per generator update as part of the method. Five critic steps is the WGAN-GP paper's baseline protocol, not a property of all Wasserstein or spectral-normalized GANs.

    GAN Variants¤

    DCGAN: Deep Convolutional GAN¤

    DCGAN (Radford, Metz & Chintala, 2016) established the foundation for stable GAN training using convolutional architectures.

    Architecture Guidelines:

    1. Replace pooling layers with strided convolutions
    2. Use batch normalization in both G and D
    3. Remove fully connected hidden layers
    4. Use ReLU in G (except output), LeakyReLU in D
    5. Use Tanh in G output

    Historical impact: DCGAN supplied a reproducible convolutional baseline and representation-learning evidence for the datasets and architectures studied. Later successful GANs retain some ideas and reject others.

    WGAN: Wasserstein GAN¤

    WGAN (Arjovsky, Chintala & Bottou, 2017) replaces the logistic classification game with a neural approximation to the Kantorovich–Rubinstein dual of Wasserstein-1 distance. The population distance has useful continuity properties under support mismatch; a finite WGAN remains an approximation whose behavior depends on critic capacity, constraint enforcement, and optimization.

    Wasserstein Distance:

    \[ W(p_r, p_g) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(p_r, p_g)} \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \gamma}[\|x - y\|] \]

    Practical Objective (with weight clipping):

    \[ \min_G \max_{D \in \mathcal{D}} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_r}[D(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[D(G(z))] \]

    where the mathematical supremum is over 1-Lipschitz functions. The original algorithm clipped every critic parameter to a bounded interval as a crude proxy; weight clipping neither characterizes nor certifies the full 1-Lipschitz function class.

    Motivations and observed behavior:

    • The critic objective can correlate with sample quality when the critic is trained sufficiently close to its constrained optimum; its raw scale also depends on constraint enforcement
    • The paper observed improved training behavior relative to its baselines, not universal stability
    • Smoother, more informative gradients under support mismatch; mode collapse can still occur because the critic, generator, optimization dynamics, and finite data remain imperfect
    • Works with diverse architectures

    WGAN Terminology

    The discriminator in WGAN is called a critic because it emits an unconstrained real score rather than a class probability. Only the optimized supremum over the admissible function class equals Wasserstein-1 distance. A finite critic value is one witness value and therefore a lower bound, not automatically the distance itself.

    WGAN-GP: Gradient Penalty¤

    WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al., 2017) replaces the original WGAN parameter clipping with a sampled gradient-norm penalty and reports improved behavior for its tested architectures and datasets.

    Key idea and boundary: Under an optimal-transport coupling and regularity assumptions, an optimal Kantorovich potential has unit directional slope along transport segments. WGAN-GP samples straight interpolants between independently drawn real and generated examples and encourages unit gradient norm there. This is a practical surrogate, not a theorem that every optimal critic has norm one almost everywhere under both endpoint distributions.

    Gradient Penalty:

    \[ \lambda \cdot \mathbb{E}_{\hat{x} \sim p_{\hat{x}}}[(\|\nabla_{\hat{x}} D(\hat{x})\|_2 - 1)^2] \]

    where \(\hat{x} = \epsilon x + (1-\epsilon)\tilde{x}\) with \(\epsilon \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\), sampling along straight lines between real and generated data.

    Implementation Details:

    • The paper used \(\lambda=10\) in its main experiments; retune it when architecture, data scale, units, or regularization frequency changes
    • Remove batch normalization from critic (interferes with gradient penalty)
    • The paper commonly used five critic iterations per generator update; this is an experimental setting to report and ablate

    Paper result — Established, protocol-bounded: Gulrajani et al. reported an Inception Score of \(7.86\pm0.07\) for their residual WGAN-GP model on CIFAR-10 and demonstrated training of deep residual critics. Compare only after matching architecture, sample count, preprocessing, and metric implementation; the number is not a family guarantee.

    Conditional GAN (cGAN)¤

    Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) extend the adversarial framework to enable controlled generation by conditioning both networks on additional information \(y\) (class labels, text, or images).

    Objective:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{cGAN}(G, D) = \mathbb{E}_{x,y \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D(x, y)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z, y}[\log(1 - D(G(z, y), y))] \]

    Applications:

    • Class-conditional generation: Generate specific digit classes in MNIST
    • Attribute manipulation: Change hair color, age, expression in face images
    • Text-to-image: Generate images matching text descriptions

    Pix2Pix: Image-to-Image Translation¤

    Pix2Pix (Isola, Zhu, Zhou & Efros, 2017) learns paired image translation with both adversarial and reconstruction losses.

    Complete Objective:

    \[ G^* = \arg\min_G \max_D \mathcal{L}_{cGAN}(G, D) + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{L1}(G) \]

    where:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{L1}(G) = \mathbb{E}_{x,y,z}[\|y - G(x, z)\|_1] \]

    and the original Pix2Pix experiments used \(\lambda=100\) to balance the terms. Its numerical meaning depends on loss reductions and data scaling.

    Architecture:

    • Generator: U-Net with skip connections that expose high-resolution input features to corresponding decoder stages; they reduce an information bottleneck but do not guarantee preservation
    • Discriminator: PatchGAN (classifies \(N \times N\) patches as real/fake)

    Applications: Edges→photos, labels→scenes, day→night, black-and-white→color

    CycleGAN: Unpaired Translation¤

    CycleGAN (Zhu, Park, Isola & Efros, 2017) enables translation between unpaired image domains using cycle consistency.

    Cycle Consistency Loss:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{cyc}(G, F) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_X}[\|F(G(x)) - x\|_1] + \mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_Y}[\|G(F(y)) - y\|_1] \]

    Total Objective:

    \[ \mathcal{L}(G, F, D_X, D_Y) = \mathcal{L}_{GAN}(G, D_Y, X, Y) + \mathcal{L}_{GAN}(F, D_X, Y, X) + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{cyc}(G, F) \]

    Unpaired marginals do not identify the intended joint correspondence. If both domains are uniform on \(\{0,1\}\), the identity maps \(G(x)=x\), \(F(y)=y\) and the swapped maps \(G(x)=1-x\), \(F(y)=1-y\) both match the marginals and achieve exact cycle consistency. Only task semantics say which correspondence is correct. In image systems, a cycle loss can likewise preserve information through imperceptible or task-irrelevant channels rather than recover the desired causal relation. Paired evaluation, semantic constraints, and application-specific failure tests are therefore required.

    Applications: Horse↔zebra, photo↔painting, summer↔winter, without paired training data

    StyleGAN: Style-Based Generator¤

    StyleGAN (Karras, Laine & Aila, 2019) introduces a style-based generator and evaluates improved control and latent geometry under its face-generation protocol.

    Key Innovations:

    1. Mapping Network: Maps \(z \in \mathcal{Z}\) to an intermediate latent space \(w \in \mathcal{W}\).
    2. Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN) (Huang & Belongie, 2017): injects style at each resolution.
    3. Stochastic Variation: per-pixel noise injection for fine-grained details.
    4. Progressive-growing training inherited from PGGAN (Karras et al., 2018): the original StyleGAN experiments used a generator and discriminator grown from low to high resolution; later StyleGAN versions did not make this a family requirement.

    W-Space Benefits:

    The original StyleGAN paper reports more favorable perceptual-path-length and linear-separability measurements in \(\mathcal W\) than in its input \(\mathcal Z\) under the evaluated models:

    • Perceptual Path Length (PPL) was lower in \(\mathcal W\) than in \(\mathcal Z\) for the paper's reported configurations. PPL measures expected perceptual change along sampled latent interpolation segments; it is not a proof of a globally smooth data manifold.
    • The paper's labeled attributes were more linearly separable under its metric and probe protocol.
    • Manipulation transfer remains an empirical property to test across samples, directions, edit strengths, and subgroups.

    The mapping network pushes the simple input distribution in \(\mathcal Z\) to a learned distribution in \(\mathcal W\). StyleGAN's perceptual-path-length and linear-separability experiments show that this can produce a more useful geometry for the evaluated generator. They do not identify a unique “data factor density,” guarantee disentanglement, or prove that coordinates correspond to independent semantic causes.

    The StyleGAN family:

    • StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020) — addresses characteristic artefacts associated with the original normalization design using weight modulation/demodulation and introduces path-length regularization. It reported FID 2.84 on FFHQ 1024² under the paper's dataset and evaluation protocol.
    • StyleGAN3 (Karras et al., 2021) — alias-free generator that reduces coordinate leakage and "texture sticking" by treating intermediate signals as continuous bandlimited functions. The paper evaluates a translation-equivariant configuration (StyleGAN3-T) and a more restrictive rotation-and-translation-equivariant configuration (StyleGAN3-R); the distinction and measured equivariance errors matter when reproducing the claim.
    • StyleGAN-XL (Sauer, Schwarz & Geiger, 2022) — scales style-based generation to diverse ImageNet classes with projected discriminators, classifier guidance, and class conditioning. Its comparisons were competitive with selected diffusion baselines under reported ImageNet protocols; “matches diffusion” is not meaningful without naming resolution, guidance, metric, and baseline.

    Worked example: select a stabilizer from evidence¤

    Assume training has good held-out precision but low recall, the discriminator separates train real from held-out real, and generator gradients remain finite. This is evidence for critic overfitting plus coverage failure—not saturation. A controlled study might:

    1. freeze architecture, optimizer, data order, and evaluation samples;
    2. add differentiable augmentation or reduce critic capacity to address the train/held-out gap;
    3. separately test an anti-collapse intervention such as packing or minibatch features;
    4. report precision/recall, FID/KID uncertainty, critic generalization, and wall-clock; and
    5. test the interaction only after estimating each main effect.

    Switching to WGAN-GP, changing five hyperparameters, and declaring success from a single FID cannot identify the cause.

    Diagnose the failed object before choosing an intervention. Each answer must separate population objective, finite critic estimate, generator gradient, coupled update dynamics, and evaluation protocol.

    Use the bilinear game \(V(\theta,\phi)=\theta\phi\) as a numerical oracle, then transfer its distinctions to a generator whose outputs may be continuous or categorical.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict which observations distinguish mode dropping, critic overfitting, sigmoid saturation, and rotational dynamics when a run has low discriminator loss, oscillating player losses, finite generator gradients, and low aggregate FID. State which additional measurement can falsify each diagnosis.
    2. P1.2 — Three trajectory calculations. Starting from \((\theta_0,\phi_0)=(1,0)\) with \(\eta=1/2\), compute four steps of continuous-time rotation at matching sample times, simultaneous Euler, and the stated alternating update. Verify the simultaneous radius multiplier, derive the alternating matrix trace and determinant, and distinguish boundedness from convergence.
    3. P1.3 — Stabilizer contract table. For spectral normalization, WGAN-GP, R1/R2, TTUR, packing, and differentiable augmentation, state the controlled object, sampling location, effective units, computational cost, and theorem or empirical boundary. Mark why none alone guarantees mode coverage or global convergence.
    4. P1.4 — Objective and estimator audit. Compare logistic minimax/non-saturating, hinge, least-squares, Wasserstein, and relativistic generator/critic objectives at the level of raw scores and derivatives. For a Bernoulli generator, derive the exact score-function gradient, then contrast its target, bias, and variance with a continuous relaxation and straight-through estimator.
    5. P1.5 — Controlled intervention. Design a factorial or staged study for low held-out recall plus a widening train-versus-held-out critic gap. Freeze data order, architecture, compute, and metric samples; specify baselines, main effects, interactions, uncertainty, stopping rules, and observations that would reject critic regularization or an anti-collapse method.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Apply the study to variable-length categorical protein sequences. Define the normalized generator policy or relaxation, masking and length law, critic inputs, exposure/credit-assignment tests, sequence-level coverage measures, and evidence required before promoting an image-GAN stabilizer to this domain.

    GAN stabilizers encode different hypotheses: Lipschitz sensitivity, local game convergence, critic overfitting, missing diversity, or gradient-estimator variance. Their assumptions, computational cost, and estimator semantics should remain visible. Even a scalar bilinear game separates equilibrium, continuous dynamics, simultaneous discretization, and alternating discretization.

    Latent Space Properties: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    GAN latent spaces are often tested for the following desirable properties; none follows from adversarial training alone:

    • Local output continuity for small latent perturbations
    • Plausible interpolation along a declared path and latent distribution
    • Sample fidelity and coverage under the actual sampling or truncation rule

    Semantic Vector Arithmetic¤

    Some trained generators and chosen latent parameterizations exhibit approximately linear semantic edits:

    \[ z_{smiling\_woman} \approx z_{woman} + (z_{smiling\_man} - z_{man}) \]

    These are empirical constructions, not vector identities. Their validity must be tested for edit success, attribute leakage, identity preservation, realism, and subgroup consistency. Candidate examples include:

    • Face with glasses = face + glasses vector
    • Older face = face + aging vector
    • Different expression = face + expression vector

    Latent Space Structure¤

    Latent structure influences controllability, but none of the following follows from the GAN objective alone:

    • discovered directions may correlate with labeled attributes such as age, expression, or lighting;
    • selected paths may change outputs smoothly;
    • approximately separated controls may exist over a bounded domain.

    Discovering Interpretable Directions

    Methods for finding semantic directions:

    • Linear probes (Shen et al., 2020 — InterFaceGAN): train binary classifiers on \(w\) for each attribute; the classifier normal is the attribute direction.
    • GANSpace (Härkönen et al., 2020): unsupervised PCA of intermediate feature activations; the top components recover interpretable axes.
    • SeFa (Shen & Zhou, 2021): closed-form factorisation of the generator's first weight matrix recovers semantic directions without training.
    • Supervised methods: Use attribute labels to find control vectors via paired-sample regression (StyleGAN-NADA, StyleCLIP).

    GAN Inversion and Inference¤

    Begin with the requested object¤

    Let a trained generator define

    \[ Z\sim p_Z,\qquad X=G_\theta(Z),\qquad Y\mid X\sim p_{\mathrm{obs}}(y\mid X). \]

    \(X\) is the generated object and \(Y\) is what a measurement system reveals. For an ordinary image inversion task, \(Y\) may simply be the target image and the observation map may be the identity. The word inversion is nevertheless used for several different queries:

    Table GAN.6. Begin with the requested object
    Query Desired output Success means What it does not establish
    Latent recovery The original \(z_*\), or its declared equivalence class, when \(x=G_\theta(z_*)\) A synthetic ground-truth code is recovered A non-injective generator need not reveal which preimage produced \(x\)
    Reconstruction or projection One code \(\hat z\) or expanded generator state whose output resembles \(y\) \(G_\theta(\hat z)\) is close under declared distortions The code need not be the original latent, typical under \(p_Z\), editable, or unique
    Posterior inference The conditional law \(P_\theta(dz\mid y)\) or expectations under it Compatible latents receive calibrated relative mass under a declared observation model A point optimizer or deterministic encoder is not a posterior distribution
    Editing representation A code and edit operation that change intended attributes while preserving protected content Edit success, locality, realism, and identity/content retention hold over a declared domain The lowest-reconstruction-error code need not be the most editable

    When \(p_{\mathrm{obs}}(y\mid G_\theta(z))\) is evaluable, the latent posterior has the familiar unnormalized form

    \[ p_\theta(z\mid y) \propto p_Z(z)\,p_{\mathrm{obs}}\!\left(y\mid G_\theta(z)\right). \]

    This does not require an observation-space density for the noiseless pushforward \(G_{\theta\#}p_Z\). It does require an observation model. Without one, a reconstruction loss specifies an optimization criterion, not a probabilistic posterior. For a noiseless deterministic generator, a regular conditional law over preimages may exist even when it has no ordinary Lebesgue density.

    Worked counterexample: perfect reconstruction without latent recovery¤

    Let \(Z\) be uniform on \(\{-1,0,+1\}\) and let

    \[ G(z)=z^2. \]

    Then \(P(X=0)=1/3\) and \(P(X=1)=2/3\). Observing \(X=1\) does not identify the generating latent:

    \[ P(Z=-1\mid X=1)=P(Z=+1\mid X=1)=\frac12. \]

    A deterministic encoder can choose \(E(0)=0\) and \(E(1)=+1\). It reconstructs every generated observation exactly because \(G(E(X))=X\). Yet it fails to recover samples generated from \(Z=-1\), so

    \[ P\!\left(E(G(Z))=Z\right)=\frac23, \]

    which is the best any deterministic encoder can do for this example. Returning \(+1\) for \(X=1\) also discards half of the posterior mass. Reconstruction, recovery, and posterior inference are therefore different even before neural approximation or optimization error enters.

    Now present the same generator with the real-valued target \(y=1/2\). No latent has \(G(z)=1/2\). A squared-error optimizer can return \(0\) or either of \(\pm1\), all with distortion \(1/4\). That is a projection onto the generator range, not evidence that the target came from the generator.

    Injectivity is an assumption, not an effect of inversion

    A deterministic encoder and generator can be mutual inverses almost surely only when their maps and probability laws are compatible with that property. Positive-mass collisions such as \(G(-1)=G(+1)\) rule it out. A theorem proved for an ideal deterministic bidirectional GAN at exact joint matching does not make an arbitrary pretrained generator injective.

    Per-example optimization¤

    A common post-hoc inversion holds the generator fixed and solves

    \[ \hat z = \arg\min_z \left[ \lambda_{\mathrm{data}}d_{\mathrm{data}}\!\left(y,G_\theta(z)\right) +\lambda_{\mathrm{feat}}d_{\mathrm{feat}}\!\left(y,G_\theta(z)\right) +\lambda_{\mathrm{prior}}R(z) \right]. \]

    Pixel, perceptual-feature, identity, segmentation, or frequency losses answer different similarity questions. The optimizer is a MAP procedure only if the complete objective is the negative log of a declared likelihood times prior, up to constants, and the relevant optimum is found. Calling an arbitrary perceptual objective “posterior inference” silently invents a likelihood and uncertainty model.

    A defensible optimization protocol uses several prior draws or encoder initializations, records every objective component, retains distinct near-optimal solutions, and reports steps, restarts, wall-clock, and stopping rules. Selecting only the best-looking restart hides ambiguity and compute.

    for restart k = 1, ..., K:
    initialize z_k from the declared prior or encoder
    optimize the declared data, feature, and prior terms
    retain z_k, every loss component, and the stopping diagnostic
cluster near-optimal outputs and report more than one when they differ materially

    Image2StyleGAN is an influential optimization-based StyleGAN embedding study. Its results are evidence for that architecture, latent parameterization, loss, and data protocol—not a universal inverse for GANs.

    Amortized, joint, and hybrid inference¤

    An amortized encoder learns \(E_\phi(y)\), or parameters of \(q_\phi(z\mid y)\), across a training distribution. It makes repeated inversion fast but introduces representation, generalization, and amortization errors. A hybrid method initializes with an encoder and refines the code, generator features, or selected weights for each target. Extra degrees of freedom can lower distortion while moving away from the states on which latent edits were learned.

    BiGAN (Donahue, Krähenbühl, and Darrell, 2017) and ALI (Dumoulin et al., 2017) instead learn generation and inference jointly. Their discriminator compares the two joint laws

    \[ q_\phi(x,z)=p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x), \qquad p_\theta(x,z)=p_Z(z)p_\theta(x\mid z). \]

    At ideal population optimum with a rich enough discriminator and successful optimization, equality of these joints implies matching marginals and \(q_\phi(z\mid x)=p_\theta(z\mid x)\) almost everywhere on the matched model/data law. This is a population-optimum correspondence, not an algebraic property of every trained network. It also learns a coupling between the data and prior; marginals alone do not choose a unique semantic coupling. ALICE analyzes this non-identifiability and adds conditional-entropy or cycle-style constraints to select more reconstructive couplings.

    A post-hoc encoder for a pretrained GAN and a jointly trained BiGAN answer different engineering questions. Adding an encoder after training does not retroactively give the original GAN likelihood semantics, while retraining a joint model can change both the generator and its learned representation.

    Reconstruction, realism, and editability pull in different directions¤

    Style-based generators expose several candidate inversion spaces: the input prior space, mapped style spaces, per-layer extended codes, intermediate features, residuals, and sometimes tuned generator parameters. Increasing the state dimension usually enlarges the set of reconstructible details, but an edit learned on ordinary prior samples need not remain meaningful in those enlarged regions.

    e4e makes the distortion–perception–editability distinction explicit. Later work continues to test the same boundary with constrained latents, feature states, spatial refinement, and frequency-aware residuals—for example Katsumata et al. (WACV 2024), StyleFeatureEditor (CVPR 2024), and WINE (WACV 2025). These are architecture- and protocol-specific system results. They do not establish that one latent space or loss universally optimizes all three properties.

    Observation uncertainty How do noise, missing pixels, or a forward operator change compatibility? An explicit \(p_{\mathrm{obs}}(y\mid G(z))\) and sensitivity to its assumptions

    1. Can \(G(E(x))=x\) prove that \(E(x)\) recovered the latent that generated \(x\)?
    2. When is a reconstruction optimizer a MAP estimator?
    3. Does exact BiGAN joint matching identify a unique semantic coupling from the marginals alone?
    4. Why can a more expressive inversion space improve reconstruction but harm editing?
    5. Do frequencies from ordinary random restarts estimate posterior mode probabilities?

    Solutions. (1) No. A non-injective generator can have several preimages, as the finite square-map example proves. (2) Only when the complete objective is the negative log of a declared likelihood and prior, up to constants, and the relevant optimum is found. (3) No. It makes the learned encoder and generator joints agree, but several couplings can share the same marginals; additional structure selects among them.     Evaluation Metrics¤

    Inception Score (IS)¤

    Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016) measures quality and diversity using a pre-trained classifier.

    \[ \text{IS} = \exp\left(\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_g}[D_{KL}(p(y|x) \| p(y))]\right) \]
    • High IS: Generated images are clear (low entropy \(p(y|x)\)) and diverse (high entropy \(p(y)\))
    • Limitations: Doesn't compare to real data; can be gamed

    Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)¤

    FID (Heusel et al., 2017) measures similarity between real and generated distributions in InceptionV3 feature space.

    \[ \text{FID} = \|m_r - m_g\|_2^2 + \operatorname{Tr}\!\left(C_r+C_g-2(C_r^{1/2}C_gC_r^{1/2})^{1/2}\right) \]

    where \(m_r, m_g\) are feature means and \(C_r, C_g\) are covariance matrices.

    • Lower is better: population FID is zero when the fitted feature-space Gaussians have identical means and covariances. Distinct non-Gaussian feature distributions—and certainly distinct image distributions—can share those moments, so FID zero does not by itself prove identical distributions.
    • Unlike IS, it uses a real reference set, but remains feature-, preprocessing-, sample-size-, and implementation-dependent.
    • Widely reported, not sufficient alone: use the same feature extractor and code path, disclose sample counts, and pair it with coverage, memorization, and task-specific measures.

    Precision and Recall¤

    Sajjadi et al. (2018) and Kynkäänniemi et al. (2019) extend FID into separate quality vs. coverage metrics:

    Precision: An estimator-dependent approximation to how much generated feature mass lies near the reference manifold (a fidelity proxy).

    Recall: An estimator-dependent approximation to how much reference feature mass is covered by generated samples (a coverage proxy).

    Trade-off: A particular GAN checkpoint can improve a fidelity proxy while losing coverage—for example through truncation or collapse—but this must be measured rather than assigned to the family universally.

    Human Evaluation¤

    Human evaluation measures judgments that automated metrics may miss, but it is not an assumption-free gold standard. Results depend on prompts, annotator population, instructions, interface, presentation order, quality control, and statistical model:

    • Perceptual quality: Do samples look realistic?
    • Diversity: Is there sufficient variety?
    • Artifacts: Are there visible distortions or patterns?

    Metric Limitations

    • FID: Measures overall distribution quality but doesn't separate precision from recall.
    • IS: Assesses the generated distribution without comparing to real data.
    • PR curves: Separate quality from diversity but require more computation.
    • KID (Bińkowski et al., 2018): unbiased kernel-based alternative to FID, more reliable on small sample sets.
    • CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024): replaces InceptionV3 features with CLIP features and the Fréchet distance with MMD. The paper reports higher correlation with human judgments on its evaluated model and dataset collection; this is protocol evidence, not a universal metric ordering.

    Use multiple complementary measures plus visual inspection.

    One defensible development workflow is:

    1. Start simple: DCGAN on MNIST to verify implementation
    2. Scale up: Move to CIFAR-10, CelebA for complexity
    3. Add stability: Implement WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, R1 GP as needed
    4. Monitor carefully: Track both metrics (FID, IS) and visual quality
    5. Save checkpoints: Training is unstable—save frequently
    6. Iterate: Experiment with architectural variations once baseline works
    7. Transfer learning: Use pretrained models when possible

    Historical starting points, not universal ranges¤

    DCGAN used Adam with learning rate \(2\times10^{-4}\) and \((\beta_1,\beta_2)=(0.5,0.999)\); WGAN used RMSProp plus weight clipping; WGAN-GP used Adam and commonly five critic updates; StyleGAN and modern ResNet recipes use still other settings. Reproduce a named baseline first, then tune player learning rates, optimizer moments, update ratio, regularization strength/frequency, effective batch size, and augmentation jointly. Larger batches can improve some estimators and batch statistics but can also change compute, regularization, and optimization; “larger is better” is not a law.

    Stability Techniques¤

    R1 Gradient Penalty (Mescheder, Geiger & Nowozin, 2018):

    \[ R_1 = \frac{\gamma}{2} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_r}[\|\nabla_x D(x)\|^2] \]
    • Applied only to real samples. R2 is the analogous zero-centred penalty on generated samples. Either supports the local convergence result for the analyzed games under the paper's assumptions; neither is sufficient for arbitrary neural architectures and optimizers.
    • \(\gamma\) and its effective strength depend on data units, loss reduction, resolution, batch size, and lazy frequency. Report the full convention rather than transporting the number 10 blindly.
    • Lazy regularisation (Karras et al., 2020 — StyleGAN2): evaluate the penalty only on scheduled minibatches and rescale its contribution according to the declared interval. This amortizes the extra gradient computation; the actual time and memory saving depends on the implementation and is not fixed at one half.
    • R1/R2 are zero-centred gradient penalties. Mescheder et al. prove local convergence for the analyzed regularized games under explicit assumptions and give counterexamples for finite-update WGAN/WGAN-GP. Neither result is an architecture-independent promise.

    Spectral Normalization (Miyato et al., 2018):

    Normalise weight matrices by their spectral norm (largest singular value):

    \[ W_{SN} = \frac{W}{\sigma(W)} \]
    • Controls each normalized linear operator; a whole-network Lipschitz bound requires accounting for every layer and branch.
    • The common approximate implementation uses power iteration; its cost and accuracy depend on the operator and update scheme.
    • Works well with the standard non-saturating GAN loss; the basis of SAGAN and BigGAN.

    Label Smoothing (Salimans et al., 2016):

    Replace binary labels with smoothed versions:

    • Real labels: \(0.9\) instead of \(1.0\).
    • Fake labels: \(0.0\) (no smoothing).

    One-sided smoothing was proposed to reduce overconfident real-class predictions. It changes the density-ratio target, and its effect is objective- and data-dependent; evaluate calibration, gradients, fidelity, and coverage rather than assuming it prevents instability.

    Research landscape:

    • Diffusion and flow models with iterative samplers lead many high-quality image/video protocols, but step count, guidance, decoder, and model size vary.
    • Pure GANs remain attractive for some low-latency applications, interactive systems, and latent editing; dominance depends on quality, model size, decoder cost, batching, and hardware.
    • Adversarially distilled diffusion/flow models (for example ADD and DMD2) combine teacher or score signals with adversarial learning to reach few-step operating points. Other few-step methods omit an external discriminator, so “fast sampler” and “GAN” are not synonyms.

    Complementary Use Cases:

    • Consider a GAN when: a validated one-pass generator meets the domain's fidelity, coverage, latency, memory, and editing requirements, or a domain-specific adversarial architecture has unusually strong evidence.
    • Consider diffusion or flow models when: their conditioning, coverage, likelihood/path semantics, or available pretrained ecosystem wins under the target quality–latency budget.
    • Consider adversarial distillation when a pretrained diffusion or flow model must be moved to a measured few-evaluation operating point and the added teacher, discriminator, data, and stability costs are acceptable. Compare against non-adversarial students under the same quality and systems budget.

    On the speed advantage

    A direct GAN often uses one top-level generator call, but that call can contain a cascade, renderer, codec, or sequential subroutine. A distilled student can also use one or a few top-level transitions. Outer steps, branch-equivalent network evaluations, launched calls, auxiliary calls, and end-to-end latency are separate quantities. Compare measured wall-clock, memory, and achieved quality at the same operating point rather than assuming a universal family-level speed multiplier.

    Worked example: FID can miss a distributional error¤

    In one feature dimension, let the real feature distribution be \(r=(-1,+1)\) with equal probability and the generated distribution be \(g=(-\sqrt2,0,+\sqrt2)\) with probabilities \((1/4,1/2,1/4)\). Both have mean zero and variance one, so the population Gaussian-moment FID is zero. The distributions are nevertheless different: the generator puts half its mass at a feature value the real distribution never takes. KID or a suitable two-sample test can detect higher-order differences, while a precision/recall estimator can expose support geometry. The example is deliberately simple: it proves that FID is not an identity test.

    Progressive Growing¤

    Karras et al. (2018) — PGGAN train GANs by gradually increasing resolution:

    1. Start at 4×4
    2. Stabilize training
    3. Add layers for 8×8
    4. Fade in new layers smoothly
    5. Repeat to target resolution

    Paper-era evidence: the authors reported improved high-resolution training behavior and sample metrics relative to their baselines. Later StyleGAN variants returned to end-to-end fixed-resolution training, so progressive growing is a historical technique rather than a required modern component.

    Self-Attention¤

    SAGAN (Zhang et al., 2019) adds self-attention to capture long-range dependencies:

    \[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \]

    SAGAN reported improved ImageNet metrics and qualitative long-range structure relative to its convolutional baselines. BigGAN subsequently combined self-attention with large-scale class-conditional residual training; “ancestor” denotes architectural lineage, not a causal ablation by itself.

    BigGAN¤

    BigGAN (Brock, Donahue & Simonyan, 2019) scales GANs to ImageNet with:

    • Larger batch sizes (2048)
    • Larger architectures (more channels)
    • Truncated-normal latent sampling at inference (the truncation trick — trade diversity for fidelity)
    • Orthogonal regularisation on weights
    • Class-conditional batch normalisation

    Established, protocol-bounded result: BigGAN reported IS \(166.3\) and FID \(9.6\) at ImageNet 128×128 for one reported truncation setting, while BigGAN-deep reported other resolution/truncation operating points. Truncation explicitly trades diversity for fidelity, so the score must be paired with its sampling setting and evaluation code.

    3D-Aware Generation¤

    EG3D (Chan et al., 2022) generates 3D-consistent faces from 2D images:

    • Tri-plane hybrid 3D representation for efficient volumetric rendering.
    • Differentiable neural rendering from multi-view.
    • Adversarial training on 2D image collections only.
    • Learns a 3D-aware representation from 2D image supervision and camera assumptions, without ground-truth 3D shapes; that does not guarantee physically correct hidden geometry.

    Successors include GET3D (Gao et al., 2022) and GeNVS (Chan et al., 2023).

    Applications: 3D face synthesis, novel-view synthesis, relighting.

    Adversarial Training: Pure GANs and Diffusion Distillation¤

    Adversarial generative modelling includes two distinct but composable lines of work: (a) developing pure-GAN architectures, regularizers, and convergence analyses, and (b) using adversarial objectives with pretrained diffusion or flow models to distill them into 1–4-step samplers. The shared discriminator machinery does not make the resulting probabilistic models or training pipelines equivalent.

    Diffusion–GAN Hybrids (Architecturally)¤

    Beyond distillation, several architectures interleave diffusion and GANs:

    • DDGAN (Xiao et al., 2022) parameterises each reverse step with a GAN.
    • Wavelet Diffusion-GAN (Phung et al., 2024) operates in the wavelet domain for cheaper high-resolution training.
    • Flow2GAN (2025) hybridises flow matching with adversarial training for few-step audio generation.

    The throughline is narrower: adversarial objectives remain useful both in pure implicit generators and as one component of some diffusion/flow post-training systems. Consistency, score, transport, regression, and the discriminator-free APEX construction demonstrate that few-step generation does not require an external discriminator.

    Open Research Directions¤

    Open Research Problems¤

    • Scaling pure GANs: GigaGAN studies billion-parameter text-to-image adversarial training, while R3GAN studies a simpler modern baseline at different scales. Whether either recipe remains favorable on broader prompt distributions and matched compute is open.
    • One-step video: APT and AAD-1 report promising one-step protocols, but long-horizon coherence, motion diversity, prompt alignment, memory, and independent reproduction remain open.
    • Convergence theory beyond local: R3GAN gives provable local convergence, but global convergence under realistic data + architectures is still open.
    • Pretrained-feature discriminators: ADD and related methods reuse frozen or pretrained vision features, while DMD2 uses a separate real/fake discriminator alongside score estimators. Which feature sources, update rules, and data regimes transfer reliably remains open.
    • Adaptive stability mechanisms: Learning or controlling update schedules, regularization, and critic difficulty without hiding new state variables or eliminating the need for held-out diagnostics.

    Research Directions Across System Types¤

    Adversarial distillation as one fast-sampler route:

    • ADD, DMD2, and Diffusion2GAN establish the discriminator as one available few-step post-training ingredient. Many production diffusion deployments and non-adversarial distillers do not use one.
    • Adversarial signals are being studied with diffusion, flow, autoregressive video, and consistency-style students; which combinations remain useful at scale is empirical.

    Retrieval-Augmented and Multimodal GANs:

    • Combine generators with retrieval systems for few-shot adaptation.
    • Cross-modal (text-image-audio-video) generation with shared latent spaces.

    Efficient Fine-Tuning:

    • Parameter-efficient adapters are being explored across modern generators; StyleGAN-NADA is an earlier CLIP-guided domain-adaptation method, not itself evidence that LoRA is intrinsic to GAN fine-tuning.
    • Few-shot domain adaptation from foundation models.

    Quality-Aware Training:

    • DINOv2 / SigLIP / CLIP features as discriminator backbones (ADD).
    • Wavelet-driven discriminators (Wavelet Diffusion-GAN).
    • Learned perceptual losses tuned to specific generators.

    Hybrid Approaches¤

    Hybrid systems are an important branch, not a foregone universal endpoint:

    • GAN + Diffusion — one family of fast-inference post-training methods (ADD, DMD2, APT), alongside non-adversarial alternatives.
    • GAN + VAE — VAE-GAN style perceptual codecs (the SD-VAE training recipe).
    • GAN + Transformers / DiTs — adversarial post-training can operate on transformer backbones; production fitness still requires system-specific quality, safety, latency, and cost evidence.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    Capstone objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. specify a GAN experiment from probability model through deployment;
    2. state which claims are population identities, local theorems, empirical results, or interpretations; and
    3. decide whether adversarial training is the primary model, an auxiliary objective, or unnecessary for the target system.

    GANs established adversarial density-ratio and critic learning as a major route to implicit generation. They produced influential high-fidelity image systems and latent-editing tools, while exposing difficult questions about games, coverage, evaluation, and generalization. Their role includes both pure generators and adversarial components in larger systems.

    Core Principles:

    • Minimax game between generator and discriminator drives learning
    • Ideal population equilibrium includes distribution matching and a half-valued logistic discriminator; finite neural training is not guaranteed to reach it
    • Implicit density modeling avoids explicit likelihood computation
    • Learned discrepancy can reward perceptual and high-frequency statistics, but neither sharpness nor coverage is guaranteed
    • Inversion is query-dependent: latent recovery, reconstruction, posterior inference, and editing representations require different outputs and tests

    Key Variants:

    • DCGAN: stable convolutional architecture (the foundational recipe).
    • WGAN / WGAN-GP: Wasserstein dual training and gradient-penalty control; instructive and useful in some domains, while many image GAN recipes instead use logistic or hinge losses with spectral control and/or R1.
    • Pix2Pix / CycleGAN: image translation with / without paired data.
    • StyleGAN → StyleGAN2 → StyleGAN3 → StyleGAN-XL: style-based architecture, measured latent controls, signal-processing treatment of aliasing, and scaling to diverse ImageNet classes.
    • R3GAN (NeurIPS 2024): a peer-reviewed modern baseline combining a regularized relativistic paired loss, local convergence analysis, and a simplified residual backbone.
    • GigaGAN, StyleGAN-T: large-scale text-to-image GANs.
    • Diffusion-GAN, DDGAN, ADD, DMD2, APT: distinct diffusion–GAN or adversarial post-training constructions; public evidence does not justify a universal claim about production dominance.

    Training Challenges:

    • Mode collapse, vanishing gradients, training instability
    • Require explicit game, generalization, and coverage diagnostics
    • Stabilizers address different hypotheses and should be ablated rather than stacked automatically

    Best Practices:

    • Reproduce a named, domain-appropriate baseline on a small controlled dataset
    • Match output semantics, objective, normalization, regularizer, and data scale
    • Monitor both quantitative metrics and visual quality
    • Report player time scales, update ratio, regularization strength/frequency, and optimizer state
    • Save checkpoints frequently

    When to Use GANs:

    • A measured one-pass generator satisfies the required quality–coverage–latency point
    • A validated latent-editing or domain-specific adversarial ecosystem is a decisive advantage
    • The application benefits from a learned distributional critic or adversarial auxiliary loss
    • A strong domain result—such as selected 3D-aware synthesis protocols—transfers under matched assumptions

    System comparison:

    • Diffusion models lead in raw quality and diversity for offline generation.
    • Pure GANs such as R3GAN, GigaGAN, and StyleGAN-XL remain competitive on selected protocols; there is no protocol-independent “GAN versus diffusion” quality or cost ordering.
    • Adversarial distillation (ADD, DMD2, APT) has erased the clean GAN-vs-diffusion divide — discriminators are a major component of several fast diffusion samplers, alongside non-adversarial distillation families.
    • Many production systems are hybrid (codec + diffusion / flow prior + optional adversarial post-training).

    Cumulative research exercise: build an auditable conditional generator¤

    Design a conditional generator for a long-tailed 64×64 scientific-image dataset where rare classes matter and the serving target is 20 ms per sample on a specified accelerator. Your proposal must include:

    1. the target conditional distribution and sampling semantics;
    2. generator and critic inputs/outputs, including class conditioning and raw-logit or critic semantics;
    3. one primary objective and one justified regularizer, with equations and units;
    4. an update protocol and the diagnostics that would cause you to change it;
    5. fidelity, per-class coverage, calibration/conditional correctness, memorization, latency, memory, and uncertainty measures;
    6. matched VAE, diffusion/flow, and autoregressive baselines; and
    7. an evidence ledger separating established identities, paper results, your hypotheses, and unverified research hypotheses.
    Worked solution: one defensible design

    Use a class-conditional residual generator and projection discriminator with a raw-logit non-saturating loss. Start with R1 on real inputs because the hypothesis is local critic regularity, not Wasserstein-distance estimation. Sample classes using a declared training reweighting scheme but evaluate against both the natural and balanced class distributions. Track per-class precision/recall or density/coverage, KID confidence intervals, nearest-neighbor/memorization tests, held-out discriminator scores, generator gradient norms, and conditional accuracy from an independently trained classifier whose own errors are disclosed. Benchmark measured end-to-end latency—including preprocessing and any decoder—at fixed batch size and precision. Compare against baselines at both equal wall-clock training budget and equal served quality. If rare-class recall fails while the critic generalizes, test a class-balancing or packing intervention before changing divergence families. If the critic overfits, test augmentation or capacity control. Do not infer physical correctness from FID.

    Final misconception check¤

    • The optimal-discriminator theorem is not a training-convergence theorem.
    • A 50% discriminator accuracy and a low generator loss are both ambiguous.
    • Spectral normalization controls operators but does not automatically certify an arbitrary whole critic as exactly 1-Lipschitz.
    • Adversarial fidelity does not guarantee coverage, non-memorization, causal correctness, or calibrated uncertainty.
    • Exact reconstruction does not prove latent recovery, posterior calibration, or editability.
    • A diffusion or flow student trained with an adversarial term is a hybrid system, not evidence that the original pretrained family “is a GAN.”
    • One-step sampling is not a hardware-independent latency measurement.
    Retrieval practice: cumulative GAN synthesis
    1. Under which assumptions does the original minimax GAN reduce to JS divergence?
    2. Which relationship label connects an adversarially post-trained diffusion student to GANs?
    3. What distinguishes R3GAN's theorem from the claim that relativistic GANs always converge?
    4. Which measurements would reveal a high-fidelity generator that drops rare modes?

    Solutions. (1) Optimize an unrestricted logistic discriminator for an equal real/fake mixture, use densities or masses with respect to a common measure, and evaluate the resulting population value. (2) Hybrid system composition; an objective correspondence may also describe a particular gradient derivation, but it does not make all components algebraically equivalent. (3) R3GAN analyzes a specific regularized relativistic loss locally under stated assumptions; it is not a global theorem for every relativistic objective, architecture, and optimizer. (4) Per-class recall/coverage, class-frequency comparison, stratified samples, and uncertainty-aware estimates—not aggregate FID alone.
