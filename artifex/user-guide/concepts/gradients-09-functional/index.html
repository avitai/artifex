    9. State Scores, Functional Gradients, and Distributional Flows¤

    Chapter 9 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Scores, continuity equations, Langevin dynamics, and optimal transport.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Convert differentials to gradients: explain why a first variation needs a declared metric or inner product.
    2. O2 — Derive Wasserstein flow: move from a functional to its continuity equation and distinguish the exact PDE from a finite JKO surrogate.
    3. O3 — Connect free energy and sampling: derive Langevin's Fokker--Planck equation while retaining discretization and score-estimation error.
    4. O4 — Separate state and parameter derivatives: type score matching, guidance, and training updates in their correct vector spaces.
    5. O5 — Interpret transport estimators and particles: distinguish pathwise velocity fields, SVGD, and distributional gradient flows.
    6. O6 — Transfer to structured states: specify tangent projection, manifold measure, retraction/integrator, and the separate law geometry.

    A functional derivative needs a geometry¤

    Let \(\mathcal F[\rho]\) be a functional of a density \(\rho\). Its first variation \(\delta\mathcal F/\delta\rho\) is defined through

    \[ \left.\frac{d}{d\epsilon} \mathcal F[\rho+\epsilon\chi] \right|_{\epsilon=0} =\int \frac{\delta\mathcal F}{\delta\rho}(x)\chi(x)\,dx \]

    for admissible mass-preserving perturbations \(\chi\). The first variation is analogous to a differential, not yet a unique gradient vector. Converting it to a gradient requires an inner product or metric on density perturbations. \(L^2\), Fisher--Rao, Wasserstein, and kernelized transport geometries therefore produce different flows from the same functional.

    Wasserstein gradient flow¤

    Under the formal \(W_2\) geometry, the gradient flow of \(\mathcal F\) satisfies

    \[ \partial_t\rho_t =\nabla\!\cdot\!\left( \rho_t\nabla \frac{\delta\mathcal F}{\delta\rho}(\rho_t) \right). \]

    The minimizing-movement or JKO discretization replaces this PDE by successive proximal problems,

    \[ \rho_{k+1} \in\arg\min_\rho \left\{ \mathcal F[\rho] +\frac{1}{2\tau}W_2^2(\rho,\rho_k) \right\}. \]

    Each numerical implementation must approximate a density, coupling, transport map, or dual potential. Solving a neural JKO surrogate for finite inner steps is not the same finite algorithm as integrating the exact gradient-flow PDE. Variational and semi-dual neural constructions give useful scalable examples (Fan et al.; Choi et al.).

    Worked free-energy flow becomes Langevin dynamics¤

    Let the target density be \(\pi(x)\propto e^{-V(x)}\) and define

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal F[\rho] &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\rho\|\pi)\\ &=\int \rho(x)\log\rho(x)\,dx\\ &\quad+\int V(x)\rho(x)\,dx +\text{constant}. \end{aligned} \]

    Its first variation is

    \[ \frac{\delta\mathcal F}{\delta\rho} =\log\rho+1+V. \]

    Substitution into the \(W_2\) flow gives

    \[ \partial_t\rho =\Delta\rho+\nabla\!\cdot(\rho\nabla V). \]

    This is the Fokker--Planck equation for

    \[ \begin{aligned} dX_t &=-\nabla V(X_t)\,dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t\\ &=\nabla\log\pi(X_t)\,dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t. \end{aligned} \]

    Thus Langevin sampling has a Wasserstein gradient-flow interpretation under the stated smoothness and boundary conditions. Euler--Maruyama with finite step size, an approximate score, and minibatch noise has additional discretization and estimation error; the interpretation does not erase them.

    State scores, energy forces, and guidance¤

    The state score

    \[ s_t(x)=\nabla_x\log p_t(x) \]

    is a vector field on sample space. Score matching trains parameters so a neural field approximates it; the optimizer still uses a parameter gradient of the score-matching loss. Hyvärinen's score-matching identity removes the unknown data score under smooth-support and boundary assumptions. Denoising score matching changes the target to a corrupted marginal at each noise level.

    Conditional guidance uses the identity

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_t(x\mid y) =\nabla_x\log p_t(x) +\nabla_x\log p_t(y\mid x), \]

    when the conditional quantities and derivatives are well defined. Classifier guidance estimates the second state gradient with a noise-conditional classifier (Dhariwal and Nichol). Scaling or replacing that term changes the sampled vector field; it need not remain the exact score of the original conditional law. Guidance gradients are sampling-time state derivatives, not training gradients of the base generator.

    Pathwise gradients are transport velocities¤

    For a differentiable scalar-parameter density family \(q_\theta\), or for one declared directional derivative of a vector parameter, a velocity field \(v_\theta(z)\) that satisfies

    \[ \partial_\theta q_\theta(z) +\nabla_z\!\cdot\! \left(q_\theta(z)v_\theta(z)\right)=0 \]

    transports probability mass as \(\theta\) changes. Under boundary and integrability conditions,

    \[ \partial_\theta \mathbb E_{q_\theta}[f_\theta(Z)] =\mathbb E_{q_\theta} \left[ \nabla_z f_\theta(Z)^\top v_\theta(Z) +\partial_\theta f_\theta(Z) \right]. \]

    Multiple velocity fields can satisfy the continuity equation and yield unbiased pathwise estimators with different variance. Explicit reparameterization supplies one field; implicit differentiation can supply fields for Gamma, Beta, Dirichlet, and von Mises families (Figurnov, Mohamed, and Mnih). The transport-equation view also supports alternative low-variance fields (Jankowiak and Obermeyer).

    This is an estimator-level transport correspondence. It does not make a VAE encoder update a Wasserstein gradient flow of the generated data distribution.

    Stein variational gradients move an interacting particle system¤

    SVGD considers infinitesimal maps \(T(x)=x+\epsilon\phi(x)\) and chooses a vector field from a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space to decrease \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(T_\sharp q\|p)\). Under its assumptions, the optimal direction has the form

    \[ \begin{aligned} \phi^*(\cdot) =\mathbb E_{X\sim q}\bigl[ &k(X,\cdot)\nabla_X\log p(X)\\ &+\nabla_X k(X,\cdot) \bigr]. \end{aligned} \]

    The first term follows the target score; the second repels particles through the kernel. The finite algorithm replaces \(q\) by an empirical particle measure and the expectation by a particle average (Liu and Wang). Its particles are interacting and generally are not independent exact samples from \(p\) after a finite number of updates. Kernel choice and particle count define the approximation.

    Sample-manifold gradients¤

    If states lie on a Riemannian manifold \((\mathcal X,g)\), the state gradient is defined by

    \[ g_x(\operatorname{grad}_g f(x),v)=df_x[v] \]

    for all tangent \(v\). In an embedded manifold, projecting an ambient Euclidean gradient can be correct only with the appropriate induced metric and tangent projection. Retractions, exponential maps, chart transitions, volume measures, and numerical integrators then affect the finite sampler.

    This is geometry of generated states. Fisher--Rao geometry is geometry of probability laws. Riemannian score models can use both, but the two metric tensors act on different spaces.

    Independent practice and assessment map: functional and state-space flows¤

    Begin with a differential, name the metric that turns it into a vector, and only then discretize. Tasks that use particles or manifolds retain both their population object and finite approximation.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: geometry-dependent motion. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. For one functional \(\mathcal F[\rho]\), explain why its \(L^2\), Fisher--Rao, and Wasserstein gradients need not produce the same density evolution even though the first variation is unchanged.
    2. P1.2 — Separate JKO from a PDE step. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. Write the Wasserstein gradient-flow PDE and one JKO step. List three approximation choices that make a finite neural JKO solver different from direct PDE integration.
    3. P1.3 — Derive free-energy Langevin flow. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 13 min. Starting from \(\mathcal F[\rho]=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\rho\|\pi)\) with \(\pi\propto e^{-V}\), derive the first variation, continuity equation, and SDE. State two finite-algorithm error sources.
    4. P1.4 — Verify a pathwise transport velocity. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. For \(q_\theta=\mathcal N(\theta,1)\), show that \(v_\theta(z)=1\) satisfies the one-dimensional continuity equation. For \(f(z)=z^2\), verify the transport estimator equals \(d\mathbb E[f]/d\theta\).
    5. P1.5 — Compute a two-particle SVGD field. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Let target \(p=\mathcal N(0,1)\), particles \(x_1=-1,x_2=1\), and \(k(x,y)=\exp[-(x-y)^2/2]\). Compute the SVGD direction at each particle and separate attraction from repulsion.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to the sphere. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. For samples constrained to the unit sphere, turn an ambient vector \(a(x)\) into a tangent update, give a first-order retraction, and separately name a law-level geometry if the goal is to move a distribution over the sphere.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. \(\delta\mathcal F/\delta\rho\) specifies how the scalar changes against an admissible perturbation; it is not yet a velocity. The \(L^2\) Riesz map produces pointwise density change, Fisher--Rao geometry produces mass-preserving reaction-like motion under its constraint, and Wasserstein geometry produces a velocity and continuity equation transporting mass. Check. Equal stationary points need not imply equal trajectories or finite algorithms.
    2. P1.2. The PDE is \(\partial_t\rho=\nabla\cdot(\rho\nabla\delta\mathcal F/\delta\rho)\); JKO sets \(\rho_{k+1}\in\arg\min_\rho\{\mathcal F[\rho]+W_2^2(\rho,\rho_k)/(2\tau)\}\). A finite neural solver chooses a restricted density or map class, an approximate primal/dual/regularized OT computation, Monte Carlo samples, and a finite inner optimizer. Direct PDE integration instead chooses spatial/state representation and a time integrator. Rubric. At least three named choices and their errors are separated.
    3. P1.3. \(\mathcal F=\int\rho\log\rho+\int V\rho+\text{const}\), so \(\delta\mathcal F/\delta\rho=\log\rho+1+V\). The Wasserstein flow is \(\partial_t\rho=\Delta\rho+\nabla\cdot(\rho\nabla V)\), the Fokker--Planck equation for \(dX_t=-\nabla V(X_t)dt+\sqrt2dW_t\). Finite step size, approximate scores/forces, boundary handling, and Monte Carlo error remain. Check. The SDE correspondence does not make Euler--Maruyama exact.
    4. P1.4. For \(q(z)\propto\exp[-(z-\theta)^2/2]\), \(\partial_\theta q=(z-\theta)q\) and \(\partial_z(qv)=\partial_zq=-(z-\theta)q\), so their sum is zero. \(\mathbb E[Z^2]=\theta^2+1\) has derivative \(2\theta\); the transport identity gives \(\mathbb E[2Z\cdot1]=2\theta\). Check. This is a parameter-sensitivity estimator, not a claim about Wasserstein training of another law.
    5. P1.5. Let \(c=e^{-2}\). At \(x_1=-1\), the self term is attraction \(1\) and zero repulsion; the cross attraction is \(-c\) and cross repulsion \(-2c\), so \(\phi(x_1)=(1-3c)/2\approx0.297\). Symmetry gives \(\phi(x_2)=-(1-3c)/2\approx-0.297\). The score terms pull toward zero while kernel-gradient terms push the particles apart. Check. Removing repulsion would give two independent smoothed score steps, not SVGD.
    6. P1.6. The tangent field is \(v=(I-xx^\top)a=a-(x^\top a)x\), satisfying \(x^\top v=0\). A first-order retraction is \(R_x(\epsilon v)=(x+\epsilon v)/\lVert x+\epsilon v\rVert\). To move a law on the sphere, separately choose a geometry such as Wasserstein distance built from geodesic cost, Fisher--Rao on a dominated spherical density family, or a kernelized Stein construction with tangent kernels. Rubric. Projecting individual vectors does not choose a distribution metric.

    Chapter 9 synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Functional derivatives become gradients only after a geometry is chosen. Wasserstein flows move densities, state scores move samples, pathwise velocities estimate parameter sensitivities, Stein fields move interacting particles, and manifold gradients remain tangent to structured state spaces. Their equations connect, but their finite algorithms and target objects remain distinct.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why is a first variation not yet a unique gradient?
    2. What functional has Langevin's Fokker--Planck equation as its \(W_2\) gradient flow?
    3. What are the two terms in the SVGD field?
    4. Why does a pathwise transport velocity not make VAE training a Wasserstein gradient flow of data laws?

    Solutions. (1) A metric or inner product is needed to identify a differential with a vector. (2) \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\rho\|\pi)\). (3) A target-score attraction term and a kernel-gradient repulsion term. (4) The velocity estimates sensitivity of an expectation under the variational law; the population objective, moving law, and geometry differ from data-law Wasserstein descent.
