    Learning path

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Geometric and Equivariant Generative Modeling

    Diffusion and flow models learn infinitesimal motion and pay for numerical integration at inference. Few-step methods instead learn finite transitions, endpoint predictions, average velocities, or flow maps. The speed gain is real, but it changes the learning problem: a long jump must preserve a multimodal distribution that many small local updates could resolve gradually.

    This volume connects consistency models, flow-map matching, shortcut and MeanFlow models, Transition Matching, continuous autoregression, and teacher-based distillation. It distinguishes a solver step, a model call, an internal transition sample, and an end-to-end map.

    The motivating question is not merely “how can sampling use fewer steps?” It is: which finite object replaces repeated local evolution, how is that object identified, and what does the deployed program actually preserve? The answer can be an endpoint predictor, a deterministic two-time map, an interval-average velocity, a stochastic transition kernel, or a student trained to imitate one aspect of a teacher. These objects can share an architecture and still answer different probabilistic queries.

    Use six ledgers throughout the volume:

    1. the dynamics ledger records the reference vector field, diffusion, generator, or teacher process;
    2. the finite-object ledger records an endpoint map, two-time map, transition kernel, conditional moment, or output distribution;
    3. the supervision ledger records data, paired trajectories, teacher evaluations, bootstrapped targets, adversarial features, and auxiliary scores;
    4. the algorithm ledger records the exact sequence of outer transitions, internal samples, network stages, guidance branches, and codec calls;
    5. the error ledger separates reference-dynamics, representation, estimation, composition, discretization, and systems error; and
    6. the evidence ledger limits conclusions to the state space, time pairs, conditions, compute regime, and metrics actually tested.

    The central anti-shortcut

    “One step” is a cost label, not a mathematical object and not a quality guarantee. A one-call endpoint network, a one-stage Runge--Kutta update, a stochastic transition with an internal flow head, and a complete noise-to-data program can have different laws, costs, and failure modes.

    Book-length research route¤

    Few-step modeling is too new for one book to provide both settled foundations and current method coverage. The route below was checked against proceedings, journal, or author-hosted primary records. Read it as a sequence of questions, not as a leaderboard:

    • Start with a numerical-analysis treatment such as Hairer, Nørsett, and Wanner's Solving Ordinary Differential Equations I to understand local truncation error, stability, and why changing a solver is different from learning a new finite map.
    • Progressive Distillation for Fast Sampling of Diffusion Models establishes the repeated two-teacher-steps-to-one-student-step construction. Consistency Models establishes endpoint consistency with both distillation and standalone training routes.
    • Consistency Trajectory Models and the TMLR paper Flow Map Matching with Stochastic Interpolants broaden endpoint prediction toward arbitrary two-time traversal. They do not make an approximate learned map an exact semigroup.
    • One Step Diffusion via Shortcut Models learns step-size-conditioned recursive shortcuts, whereas Mean Flows for One-step Generative Modeling learns interval-average velocity through a differential identity. Their shared goal does not make their losses interchangeable.
    • Transition Matching makes each outer transition an expressive stochastic generative problem and connects refinement with continuous autoregressive ordering. Its internal head must remain in the compute and probability ledgers.
    • One-step Diffusion with Distribution Matching Distillation and Learning Few-Step Diffusion Models by Trajectory Distribution Matching distinguish output-distribution matching from paired trajectory imitation. Chapter 5 adds finite KL and error-propagation examples so “distribution matching” does not become an unqualified preservation claim.

    Later papers can improve a benchmark while changing guidance, representation, data access, or training compute. Carry the six ledgers across every comparison before attributing the improvement to “fewer steps.”

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to distinguish an infinitesimal velocity, finite flow map, transition kernel, internal model sample, and end-to-end generator; derive composition consistency; explain why long transitions can be multimodal; account for teacher and distillation cost; and compare few-step systems using total NFE, wall-clock, memory, and matched quality.

    1. From Infinitesimal Dynamics to Finite Transitions¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes elementary ODEs, Markov kernels, and iterative generative sampling. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Type the learned object: distinguish an infinitesimal field, exact propagator, deterministic flow map, stochastic kernel, and finite program.
    2. O2 — Apply composition laws: compose deterministic maps and Markov kernels with one explicit time convention.
    3. O3 — Falsify a coarse solver claim: compare exact and Euler updates for stability, sign, and global error.
    4. O4 — Diagnose hidden branching: construct a conditional transition that a deterministic conditional mean cannot represent.
    5. O5 — Audit the compute unit: separate outer steps, network stages, guidance branches, internal-head calls, and end-to-end latency.
    6. O6 — Allocate error: distinguish reference-dynamics, finite-object, estimation, composition, numerical, and systems error in a new method.

    Let an ODE have flow map

    \[ \Phi_{t\to s}(x_t)=x_s, \qquad \frac{dx_t}{dt}=v_t(x_t). \]

    For \(r\le s\le t\), exact maps satisfy

    \[ \Phi_{t\to r} =\Phi_{s\to r}\circ\Phi_{t\to s}, \qquad \Phi_{t\to t}=I. \]

    Under uniqueness they are invertible with \(\Phi_{s\to t}=\Phi_{t\to s}^{-1}\). A network that approximates a finite map need not satisfy identity, composition, or invertibility without explicit construction or training.

    For a Markov process, a transition kernel replaces the deterministic map:

    \[ K_{t\to r}(x_t,dx_r). \]

    Chapman–Kolmogorov gives

    \[ K_{t\to r}=K_{s\to r}K_{t\to s}. \]

    Deterministic maps are Dirac kernels. Stochastic kernels can represent multiple possible destinations from one current state, which matters when a coarse transition hides unresolved latent choices.

    For time-inhomogeneous dynamics, write the propagator as \(\Phi_{s,t}\) with source time first:

    \[ x_t=\Phi_{s,t}(x_s),\qquad \Phi_{s,t}=\Phi_{u,t}\circ\Phi_{s,u},\qquad s\leq u\leq t. \]

    The corresponding kernel convention is

    \[ (K_{s,u}K_{u,t})(x,A) =\int K_{u,t}(y,A)K_{s,u}(x,dy) =K_{s,t}(x,A). \]

    This book sometimes generates from a high noise time back toward data time. In that direction the indices decrease, but the rule is unchanged: the rightmost map acts first. Writing source and destination on every finite object prevents a large class of sign and composition errors.

    Five distinct finite objects¤

    Table FEW.3. Five distinct finite objects
    Object Typical notation Deterministic? What it answers
    exact ODE flow \(\Phi_{s,t}(x)\) yes under uniqueness where this state moves under the declared field
    Markov transition \(K_{s,t}(x,dy)\) not necessarily the conditional law at a later time
    endpoint predictor \(f(x_t,t)\) usually a clean endpoint estimate from this state
    learned two-time map \(F_\theta(x_s,s,t)\) usually an approximate jump between requested times
    inference program \(\widehat K^{(m)}_{0,1}\) possibly the law produced after all \(m\) deployed operations

    Equality of endpoints for one initial distribution does not identify the transition kernel. Equality of transition kernels on all states and time pairs is stronger; equality of a finite implementation additionally fixes random number generation, schedules, precision, and auxiliary operations.

    Why Euler one-step collapses¤

    Euler applied over a large interval uses

    \[ x_0\approx x_1-v_1(x_1). \]

    The local velocity is not generally the integrated displacement. If several conditional paths cross at \(x_1\), a regression velocity is their average. A single large Euler jump can move toward a conditional mean rather than follow any valid trajectory.

    There are two independent failures here. Numerical failure occurs when a local field is evaluated over an interval outside the solver's accuracy or stability region. Representation failure occurs when the desired coarse conditional law is not a Dirac mass but the model outputs only one vector.

    Worked stability counterexample: valid flow, invalid coarse step¤

    For the stable scalar ODE \(\dot x=-4x\) over one unit of time,

    \[ \Phi_{0,1}(x)=e^{-4}x\approx0.0183x, \qquad x_1^{\mathrm{Euler}}=(1-4)x=-3x. \]

    The exact solution preserves sign and contracts magnitude. One Euler step reverses sign and triples magnitude. The learned vector field can be exact while the one-step solver is catastrophically wrong. Four Euler steps with \(h=1/4\) collapse immediately to zero; eight steps give \((1/2)^8x=0.00390625x\). More steps do not create monotone accuracy unless the method and step size lie in a useful regime.

    Worked branching counterexample: the conditional mean is not a sample¤

    Let a hidden variable \(Z\in\{-1,+1\}\) be equally likely. Suppose a coarse state forgets \(Z\) and is always \(X_s=0\), while the endpoint is \(X_t=Z\). Then

    \[ K_{s,t}(0,\cdot)=\tfrac12\delta_{-1}+\tfrac12\delta_{+1}, \qquad \mathbb E[X_t\mid X_s=0]=0. \]

    A squared-error deterministic predictor returns zero, a point outside the support of the correct conditional endpoint law. Initial-noise diversity does not repair this conditional query because the current state has already erased the branch. A stochastic head, retained latent state, or finer transition is needed.

    Four meanings of “one step”¤

    1. One solver step: possibly several network stages, as in Runge–Kutta.
    2. One NFE: one evaluation of the principal neural network.
    3. One outer transition: may contain an internal stochastic head or repeated substeps.
    4. One direct map: one network maps base noise to an output.

    Guidance may require conditional and unconditional evaluations; self-conditioning, rejection, or decoding adds cost. Every speed claim must state NFE, wall time, batch, precision, hardware, and auxiliary models.

    An NFE is itself ambiguous unless the counted network is named. A Heun step can use two evaluations of one backbone; classifier-free guidance can use two logical branches fused into one launch; a transition head can perform several cheap internal evaluations after one expensive backbone call. Report both logical work and realized execution.

    Four acceleration axes and one systems overlay¤

    A smaller step count can result from four different interventions:

    1. Numerical solver or schedule: retain a learned local field but change where and how it is evaluated.
    2. Learned finite transition: replace repeated local evaluations with a trained endpoint map, average velocity, student, or stochastic kernel.
    3. State representation: generate in a compressed or structured state and pay for a codec, renderer, or cascade.
    4. Training target or path: change the field, parameterization, weighting, interpolation, or endpoint objective that is learned.

    Architecture, kernels, caching, quantisation, batching, and hardware are a systems overlay that changes the cost of each evaluation. These axes can be combined, but they answer different questions. A solver comparison can hold the checkpoint fixed; a distilled map, latent generator, or newly trained path usually cannot.

    For outer transition \(i\), let \(m_i\) be its backbone stages, \(b_{ij}\) its logical guidance branches, and \(\ell_{ij}\) its launched calls after branch fusion. Then

    \[ E_{\mathrm{branch}}=\sum_{i,j}b_{ij}, \qquad L_{\mathrm{backbone}}=\sum_{i,j}\ell_{ij}. \]

    Neither count includes an internal transition head, codec, cascade, reward model, rejection loop, or reranker unless those are reported separately. A fair record therefore includes outer transitions, branch-equivalent evaluations, actual launches, head and auxiliary calls, tensor shapes, peak memory, end-to-end latency, and—when the model changed—training or distillation cost.

    The diffusion sampling chapter gives an explicit counterexample: a hypothetical eight-step system fuses two guidance branches into eight launches and finishes in 116 ms, while a four-step, two-stage system executes the branches serially in sixteen launches and finishes in 180 ms. Both perform sixteen branch-equivalent evaluations. The example does not rank their quality; it proves that the reported outer-step count alone does not order latency.

    Error ledger for a finite generator¤

    For a metric or discrepancy \(d\), a useful decomposition is conceptual rather than universally additive:

    \[ E_{\mathrm{total}} \leadsto E_{\mathrm{reference}} +E_{\mathrm{representation}} +E_{\mathrm{estimation}} +E_{\mathrm{composition}} +E_{\mathrm{numerical}} +E_{\mathrm{systems}}. \]
    • \(E_{\mathrm{reference}}\) asks whether the chosen path or teacher ends at the intended distribution.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{representation}}\) asks whether the model class can express the required finite map or kernel.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{estimation}}\) covers finite data, stochastic optimization, and imperfect auxiliary targets.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{composition}}\) measures how learned local errors propagate across repeated transitions.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{numerical}}\) covers a solver or internal sampler.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{systems}}\) covers finite precision, codecs, guidance plumbing, rejection, and implementation drift.

    Do not add unlike quantities numerically without a theorem. The ledger is a causal debugging partition: change one layer at a time and use exact small systems to isolate it.

    Worked linear system¤

    For \(dx/dt=ax\), the exact map over interval \(h\) is \(e^{ah}x\). Euler gives \((1+ah)x\). The local error is \(O(h^2)\) and compounds over steps. Learning \(e^{ah}\) directly can be exact for this system, but the learned map must cover every \(h\) and state encountered.

    Retrieval practice: generators and finite transitions
    1. How are a generator \(L_t\) and a finite transition \(P_{s,t}\) related?
    2. Why does a large Euler step generally fail to equal the exact one-step map?
    3. Name four distinct claims that may be hidden by the phrase “one step.”
    4. Which error term remains even if the learned finite map is exact but implemented approximately?

    Solutions. (1) The transition is the time-ordered evolution induced by integrating or exponentiating the generator, not the generator itself. (2) Euler retains only a local first-order approximation and accumulates curvature and stability error over a large interval. (3) One numerical solver step, one learned transition, one network evaluation, or one visible system call. (4) Implementation or numerical realization error, including precision, decoding, and system overhead.

    Misconception checks: finite transitions¤

    • “An exact field plus one evaluation gives an exact endpoint.” Only special fields and integrators have that property.
    • “A deterministic global map cannot generate a multimodal marginal.” It can, because its initial noise varies; the limitation concerns a multimodal conditional law after information has been discarded.
    • “A Markov kernel is a matrix.” A matrix is the finite-state case; kernels also act on continuous, structured, and mixed state spaces.
    • “Fewer outer transitions means less compute.” Internal heads, guidance, model size, codecs, and hardware can reverse the ordering.

    Independent practice and assessment map: finite transitions¤

    Attempt the prediction before calculating. Label every answer as a dynamics, finite-object, program, or deployed-system claim.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: coarse Euler stability. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For \(\dot x=-4x\), predict the sign of one Euler update with \(h=1\) and of the exact update, then compute their multipliers.
    2. P1.2 — Compose an affine flow. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For \(\dot x=ax+b\), derive \(\Phi_{s,t}\) for \(a\ne0\) and verify \(\Phi_{s,t}=\Phi_{u,t}\circ\Phi_{s,u}\).
    3. P1.3 — Diagnose a collapsed branch. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For the hidden-sign example, compute the squared-error-optimal deterministic endpoint and its total-variation distance from the true conditional law.
    4. P1.4 — Reconcile four compute counts. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A sampler uses four outer transitions, two Runge--Kutta stages per transition, two guidance branches fused per stage, and one decoder. Report outer transitions, branch-equivalent backbone evaluations, launched backbone calls, and auxiliary calls.
    5. P1.5 — Allocate a failed experiment. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 15 min. A student matches analytic training targets but degrades only after repeated fp16 composition. Assign the strongest supported error categories and propose two isolation tests.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to discrete generation. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. For a masked-token model, define the finite conditional kernel, the meaning of an outer step and an NFE, and one example in which parallel unmasking discards dependence that sequential refinement could retain.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. Euler multiplies by \(1-4=-3\), so it reverses sign. The exact flow multiplies by \(e^{-4}\approx0.0183156\), so it preserves sign and contracts. Check. The failure is caused by the finite step; the vector field itself is exact and stable.
    2. P1.2. Solving the shifted system gives \(\Phi_{s,t}(x)=e^{a(t-s)}x+\frac ba(e^{a(t-s)}-1)\). Substituting \(\Phi_{s,u}\) into \(\Phi_{u,t}\) makes the exponential factors multiply and the two constant terms combine to \(\frac ba(e^{a(t-s)}-1)\). At \(s=t\) the map is identity. Check. With \(b=0\) it reduces to the linear example.
    3. P1.3. Squared loss returns the conditional mean zero. The model law is \(\delta_0\) and the target is \((\delta_{-1}+\delta_{+1})/2\). Taking \(A=\{-1,+1\}\) yields \(|1-0|=1\), the maximum possible TV distance. Check. Zero regression loss relative to the conditional mean is not distribution matching.
    4. P1.4. There are four outer transitions. Two stages and two logical branches give \(4\cdot2\cdot2=16\) branch-equivalent evaluations. Fusion launches one batch per stage, so there are \(4\cdot2=8\) backbone launches, plus one auxiliary decoder call. Check.     2. Consistency Models and Flow-Map Matching¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1's finite-transition distinction and basic distillation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate endpoint and two-time targets: distinguish endpoint invariance, arbitrary-time traversal, score access, and stochastic sampling.
    2. O2 — State anchoring conditions: combine boundary, data, and identity constraints so trivial constant solutions are excluded.
    3. O3 — Audit composition: compute direct-versus-composed residuals and classify what small sampled residuals do and do not establish.
    4. O4 — Bound propagated map error: use a Lipschitz recursion to explain why a locally accurate map can fail after repeated composition.
    5. O5 — Classify supervision: distinguish teacher distillation, self-distillation, and standalone/direct consistency training by target provenance rather than method name.
    6. O6 — Transfer the map contract: design identity, composition, inversion, off-grid, and endpoint tests for a new state space.

    Consistency Models learn a function \(f_\theta(x_t,t)\) that maps points on the same probability-flow trajectory to a common endpoint, with a boundary condition near data time. Ideally,

    \[ f_\theta(x_t,t)=f_\theta(x_s,s) \]

    whenever \(x_s=\Phi_{t\to s}(x_t)\). Consistency distillation obtains adjacent trajectory pairs from a pretrained diffusion/ODE teacher; consistency training constructs a teacher through self/EMA targets.

    Endpoint consistency permits one-step generation. Multistep procedures may alternate endpoint prediction and controlled noise injection, but they no longer simply evaluate the original deterministic flow map at more points.

    Consistency alone is underidentified. Every constant function satisfies \(f(x_t,t)=f(x_s,s)\), including one that maps all inputs to the same image. A usable construction therefore combines trajectory consistency with a boundary parameterization or loss such as \(f(x_\epsilon,\epsilon)\approx x_\epsilon\), and with training pairs whose endpoint distribution is anchored to data. The three statements answer different questions:

    When a multistep consistency sampler predicts an endpoint, adds fresh noise, and predicts again, its law is a composition of a deterministic predictor and a corruption kernel. It can improve sample quality, but it is not “a more accurate numerical integration” of the same deterministic map unless that equivalence is separately derived.

    Two-time flow maps¤

    Flow Map Matching, TMLR 2025, learns

    \[ F_\theta(x_t,t,s)\approx\Phi_{t\to s}(x_t) \]

    for arbitrary pairs \((t,s)\). This retains the endpoint model as \(s=0\) while supporting an a-posteriori step-count choice. Its mathematical framework relates consistency models, consistency trajectory models, progressive distillation, and neural-operator views.

    Useful constraints include:

    \[ F(x,t,t)=x, \]
    \[ F(F(x,t,s),s,r)=F(x,t,r), \]

    and, for invertible ODE maps,

    \[ F(F(x,t,s),s,t)=x. \]

    Matching one constraint on sampled states does not guarantee the others off distribution.

    The identity and composition laws can be built into a parameterization only in special cases. Usually they are learned or regularized on sampled states and times. Report the sampling distribution over \((x,s,t,r)\): excellent residuals on nearby teacher states do not establish long-jump composition from generated off-trajectory states.

    Consistency Trajectory Models learn arbitrary traversal along a probability-flow ODE and expose a score-like quantity; Flow Map Matching provides a broader stochastic-interpolant framework for two-time maps. These are useful connections, not synonyms. Compare their parameterized object, training identity, path construction, and sampling program before transferring a theorem.

    Teacher, self-distillation, and direct training¤

    • Teacher distillation: accurate trajectory pairs but costly preprocessing and inherited teacher bias.
    • Self-distillation: avoids a fixed teacher but creates moving-target stability problems.
    • Direct map objectives: avoid rollouts where identities permit, but may require Jacobian-vector products, inverse networks, or nested model calls.

    “Direct” and “teacher-free” are not interchangeable. A loss can avoid explicit teacher trajectories while using a pretrained score, or can bootstrap from an EMA copy whose targets depend on earlier model states. Conversely, standalone consistency training may use analytically corrupted data and an estimated score without a pretrained generative teacher. Record every target-producing network and whether gradients pass through it.

    The deployed map is deterministic unless it receives new noise. It can still generate a multimodal marginal because the initial noise varies, but a coarse conditional transition from a fixed intermediate state cannot branch without extra randomness.

    Worked composition residual¤

    Define

    \[ R=\|F(F(x,t,s),s,r)-F(x,t,r)\|. \]

    A model can have small endpoint error for \(r=0\) yet large \(R\) at intermediate times. It may be an effective one-step generator but an unreliable editable trajectory model. Evaluation must match the claimed query.

    Worked error propagation bound¤

    Let exact maps satisfy \(\Phi_{k+1}\circ\Phi_k\), and learned maps satisfy the uniform one-step bounds

    \[ \|F_k(x)-\Phi_k(x)\|\leq\varepsilon_k. \]

    If \(\Phi_{k+1}\) is \(L_{k+1}\)-Lipschitz, then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \|F_{k+1}(F_k(x))-\Phi_{k+1}(\Phi_k(x))\| &\leq \varepsilon_{k+1} +L_{k+1}\varepsilon_k. \end{aligned} \]

    For \(m\) maps with a common bound \(\varepsilon\) and \(L=1\), the worst-case bound is \(m\varepsilon\); for \(L>1\) it is \(\varepsilon( L^m-1)/(L-1)\). This is an upper bound, not a prediction that errors always align. It explains why direct long-jump and repeated-short-jump tests are complementary.

    Worked affine composition residual¤

    Suppose a true half-step is \(\Phi(x)=2x+1\), so the true full-step map is \(\Phi(\Phi(x))=4x+3\). Let a learned half-step be \(F_h(x)=2x+1.1\) and a learned full-step be \(F_{2h}(x)=4x+2.9\). At \(x=1\),

    \[ F_h(F_h(1))=7.3, \qquad F_{2h}(1)=6.9, \qquad R=0.4. \]

    Their errors relative to the exact full-step value seven are \(0.3\) and \(0.1\). The direct map is more endpoint-accurate here even though the two learned maps are inconsistent with each other. A composition residual diagnoses algebraic coherence; it is not an endpoint-quality metric.

    3. Shortcut Models, Average Velocity, and MeanFlow¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes velocity-based transport and Chapter 2's map composition view. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate velocity objects: distinguish instantaneous velocity, endpoint displacement, interval-average velocity, and a learned shortcut.
    2. O2 — Derive the MeanFlow identity: differentiate a finite displacement along a trajectory with correct total derivatives and time orientation.
    3. O3 — Verify shortcut recursion: derive the relation between one long update and two short updates for a step-conditioned predictor.
    4. O4 — Audit training compute: identify Jacobian--vector products, stop-gradient targets, bootstrap networks, and hidden model evaluations.
    5. O5 — Diagnose guidance and interval shift: test whether deployed guidance scales, path curvature, and time intervals match training.
    6. O6 — Transfer average motion: construct a finite average-velocity oracle for a nonlinear, conditional, or constrained system.

    For an exact ODE trajectory and \(r<t\), define average velocity

    \[ u(x_t,r,t)=\frac{x_t-x_r}{t-r} =\frac{1}{t-r}\int_r^t v(x_\tau,\tau)d\tau. \]

    Then

    \[ x_r=x_t-(t-r)u(x_t,r,t). \]

    Differentiating along the trajectory gives

    \[ u=v(x_t,t)-(t-r)D_tu, \]

    where \(D_tu\) is the total derivative, including state motion. This identity is the basis of MeanFlow, a NeurIPS 2025 oral, which trains average rather than instantaneous velocity and can learn one-step generation from scratch.

    The total derivative is not merely \(\partial_tu\):

    \[ D_tu(x_t,r,t) =\partial_tu(x_t,r,t) +v(x_t,t)^\top\nabla_xu(x_t,r,t). \]

    For vector-valued \(u\), the second term is a Jacobian--vector product. Dropping it changes the identity whenever the predicted average varies with state. Likewise, differentiating while holding the observed tensor \(x_t\) fixed answers a different question from differentiating along the trajectory. Autodiff code must make that distinction visible.

    The identity can be read in two directions. Given exact \(v\), it constrains \(u\). Given a parameterized \(u_\theta\), it yields an induced instantaneous target involving \(u_\theta\) and its derivative. The original MeanFlow objective and later reformulations make different optimization choices around this self-dependence. The CVPR 2026 paper Improved Mean Flows explicitly reformulates the objective and treats guidance as conditioning. That is evidence that training-objective and guidance design matter; it is not a retroactive guarantee for every average-velocity implementation.

    The derivative target typically uses automatic differentiation/Jacobian-vector products and stop-gradient choices. “One NFE at inference” does not mean the training objective costs one ordinary forward pass.

    Worked nonlinear average velocity¤

    For \(\dot x=x^2\) with \(x(0)=x_0\) and \(t<1/x_0\),

    \[ x_t=\frac{x_0}{1-tx_0}. \]

    The average velocity over \([0,t]\) is

    \[ u(x_t,0,t)=\frac{x_t-x_0}{t}=x_0x_t. \]

    It differs from both endpoint velocities: \(v(x_0)=x_0^2\) and \(v(x_t)=x_t^2\). For \(x_0=1\) and \(t=1/2\), the start, average, and end velocities are \(1\), \(2\), and \(4\). Yet the finite update \(x_t-tu=2-(1/2)2=1\) returns the exact start. The example also exposes an interval boundary: the flow blows up at \(t=1/x_0\), so no globally valid long shortcut exists across that singularity.

    Shortcut models¤

    Shortcut Models, ICLR 2025, condition a flow model on a desired step size and enforce recursive self-consistency: a long shortcut should agree with two shorter shortcuts. This supports variable NFE in one model.

    For a forward update written

    \[ S_d(x,t)=x+d\,s_\theta(x,t,d), \]

    agreement between one \(2d\) shortcut and two \(d\) shortcuts requires

    \[ s_\theta(x,t,2d) =\frac12\left[ s_\theta(x,t,d) +s_\theta(S_d(x,t),t+d,d) \right]. \]

    This is a finite recursion. A stop-gradient or EMA copy is commonly used in the bootstrapped target, so specify which side receives gradients. Grounding at small \(d\) links the recursion to the data/path objective; recursion alone again admits coherent but wrong families.

    MeanFlow uses a differential average-velocity identity; shortcut training uses a finite interval-doubling relation. They share a flow-map goal but are not the same loss.

    For a constant field \(v(x,t)=c\), both targets reduce to \(c\) at every interval. For curved dynamics, they constrain different quantities: MeanFlow relates an average to a local total derivative, while shortcut recursion compares nested finite predictions. Agreement on the constant-field oracle is therefore a minimum implementation check, not evidence of general equivalence.

    Directness versus distillation¤

    MeanFlow is self-contained: it does not require a pretrained teacher by definition. A MeanFlow-based student can still be used for distillation, but that is a hybrid application. Direct training can avoid teacher inference cost while taking on derivative estimation and optimization sensitivity.

    Guidance¤

    Classifier-free guidance is nonlinear at the level of finite maps. Combining conditional and unconditional instantaneous velocities and then integrating need not equal combining separately learned average velocities. Guidance formulation, scale, training dropout, and NFE must be reported.

    If the deployed instantaneous field is

    \[ v_w=v_{\mathrm{uncond}} +w(v_{\mathrm{cond}}-v_{\mathrm{uncond}}), \]

    then its trajectory \(x_t^{(w)}\) depends on \(w\). The average velocity integrates \(v_w\) along that guided trajectory. In general it is not \(u_{\mathrm{uncond}}+w(u_{\mathrm{cond}}-u_{\mathrm{uncond}})\) because the three averages are evaluated on different state paths. Equality holds for special shared or affine trajectories, not by linearity of the displayed field alone. Test unseen \(w\) values and count whether conditional/unconditional branches are separate or fused.

    Failure cases¤

    • Large intervals combine heterogeneous path segments and increase regression difficulty.
    • One-step maps can lose fine modes even when FID is good.
    • Derivative targets can be noisy or memory intensive.
    • Training on a narrow interval distribution can make arbitrary-NFE sampling unreliable.
    • A direct map may produce good endpoints while failing interpolation, inversion, or editing.

    4. Transition Matching and Continuous Autoregression¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional generation, transition kernels, and exposure bias in autoregressive systems. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Specify a transition model: declare the outer state, context, conditional kernel, supervision process, and internal sampler.
    2. O2 — Separate deterministic and stochastic transitions: identify when a Dirac map cannot represent the required coarse conditional law.
    3. O3 — Audit continuous autoregression: factorize blocks while keeping causal generation order distinct from causal inference.
    4. O4 — Bound kernel error: propagate uniform total-variation errors through a finite chain and state the conditioning assumptions.
    5. O5 — Diagnose exposure and history shift: separate teacher-forced, partial-history, full-history, and free-running distributions.
    6. O6 — Audit nested compute: count backbone, head, internal-solver, codec, and end-to-end costs for a new transition architecture.

    Transition Matching, NeurIPS 2025, models a finite sequence of continuous-state transitions rather than an infinitesimal ODE/SDE. An outer backbone represents the current state and schedule; an internal generative head samples an expressive transition kernel.

    Schematically,

    \[ X_{k+1}\sim K_{\theta,k}(\cdot\mid X_k,C_k). \]

    The transition can be stochastic and can use supervision paths that are not continuous. Flow/diffusion-like refinement and continuous autoregressive generation become choices of state, context, and transition order.

    A complete specification must say whether \(X_k\) is the current canvas, only the new block, a latent plus mask, or an augmented history. If the head samples from

    \[ X_{k+1}=G_{\theta,k}(X_k,C_k,Z_k), \qquad Z_k\sim p_Z, \]

    then \(G\) induces \(K\) by pushing forward \(p_Z\). The map \(G\) and kernel \(K\) are not interchangeable: different latent parameterizations can induce the same kernel, and fixing \(Z_k\) turns a stochastic transition into one coupled sample, not its distribution.

    The supervision process can be discontinuous or non-Markov in the visible state. A learned transition is Markov only relative to the state supplied to it. Full history can be made Markov by augmenting the state with that history, at the cost of a growing context and a different transition space.

    Difference Transition Matching¤

    Difference Transition Matching learns coarse refinements between states. Unlike one Euler step, its kernel can model residual uncertainty after conditioning on the current state. More expressive transitions can reduce outer steps, but the head has its own NFE, sampling randomness, and compute.

    The official NeurIPS record describes DTM, partially causal ARTM, and fully causal FHTM under controlled architecture/data settings. Treat the reported quality as evidence for those implementations and protocols. “TM generalizes flow matching” is an architectural/probabilistic containment statement: a deterministic or narrow head can recover special cases. It is not a theorem that every trained TM dominates a flow model in likelihood, latency, or quality.

    Continuous autoregression¤

    A continuous autoregressive model factorizes generation by blocks or positions but models each conditional continuous value with a generator or transition distribution:

    \[ p(x)=\prod_{i=1}^mp(x_{B_i}\mid x_{B_{<i}}). \]

    The outer factorization is causally ordered; each conditional may be flow-, diffusion-, or transition-based. It should not be confused with causal inference—the word causal here refers to the attention/generation order.

    Transition Matching studies partially causal ARTM and full-history FHTM variants. Full history exposes all earlier generated states; partial variants trade context and parallelism. Exact likelihood is not automatic merely because the outer order is autoregressive: the continuous conditional kernel may remain implicit.

    Likewise, “fully causal” refers to the information available to each generated block or state. It does not establish an intervention semantics. The factor \(p(x_{B_i}\mid x_{B_{<i}})\) is observational unless the model and data include a structural causal interpretation.

    Worked block model¤

    Generate an image latent in four blocks. A pure block AR model samples each \(p(x_{B_i}\mid x_{B_{<i}})\) once. A transition model may maintain a full canvas and update the next block through several internal stochastic refinements. Outer steps equal four; total neural calls can exceed four. Quality comparisons must separate block count, head calls, and backbone calls.

    If each of four outer steps uses one expensive backbone evaluation and three cheap head evaluations, the system uses four backbone calls and twelve head calls, not “four NFEs” without qualification. If a codec runs once at the end, there are seventeen named model calls. FLOPs and latency still depend on tensor shapes, caching, branch fusion, and hardware.

    Exposure and error propagation¤

    Teacher-forced continuous conditionals see clean histories during training, while generation supplies model histories. Full-history architectures can exploit richer context but also propagate earlier errors. Scheduled sampling does not generally provide a consistent fix and can bias the learned conditional objective.

    Kernel error propagation in total variation¤

    Let \(P_i\) be reference kernels and \(Q_i\) learned kernels. If

    \[ \sup_x\operatorname{TV}(P_i(x,\cdot),Q_i(x,\cdot))\leq\varepsilon_i, \]

    then Markov-kernel contraction and a telescoping replacement give

    \[ \operatorname{TV}(\mu P_1\cdots P_m, \mu Q_1\cdots Q_m) \leq\sum_{i=1}^m\varepsilon_i. \]

    The uniform supremum matters: an average error measured only under clean teacher prefixes does not control states reached by \(Q_1\cdots Q_{i-1}\). Tighter bounds can exploit contraction/mixing, while adversarial history shift can make the simple sum loose or unattainable from available measurements.

    Worked binary two-block propagation¤

    The reference emits \(X_1\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and sets \(X_2=X_1\). A learned model emits \(Q(X_1=1)=0.6\) and then copies its generated \(X_1\) correctly. The conditional second kernel has zero error for every prefix, yet the final joint law differs:

    \[ P(0,0)=P(1,1)=0.5, \qquad Q(0,0)=0.4,\quad Q(1,1)=0.6. \]

    Their joint TV distance is \(0.1\), exactly the first-step error. Now suppose the second learned kernel flips its input with probability \(0.05\). Its uniform conditional error is \(0.05\), so the two-step bound is \(0.15\). Direct calculation gives masses \((0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1)=(0.38,0.02,0.03,0.57)\) and TV \(0.12\). The bound is valid but not necessarily tight because some errors redistribute rather than align.

    Misconception checks: transition models¤

    • “A stochastic head is free because the backbone is reused.” Its internal calls, memory traffic, and random sampling still contribute to cost.
    • “Autoregressive factorization gives tractable likelihood.” Each continuous conditional must itself have a tractable normalized density.
    • “Full history is non-Markov.” It is non-Markov only relative to a state that omits history; augmentation restores Markov form.
    • “Causal generation order discovers causal effects.” Ordering is not an intervention model.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Transition Matching expands finite steps into learned stochastic kernels and connects global refinement with continuous autoregressive orders. Its expressivity makes “step count” especially ambiguous. Chapter 5 classifies methods that compress a pretrained teacher rather than learn long transitions from scratch.

    Progressive Distillation trains a student step to match two teacher steps, repeatedly halving the schedule. It preserves teacher trajectories locally but requires multiple stages and can accumulate approximation error.

    Worked linear progressive target¤

    Suppose two deterministic teacher half-steps multiply a scalar by \(0.8\) and \(0.75\). The exact paired full-step target is

    \[ x_2=(0.75)(0.8)x_0=0.6x_0. \]

    A student multiplier \(0.61\) has one-stage paired error \(0.01|x_0|\). If a later distillation stage composes two copies of this student to make its next target, the inherited target multiplier is \(0.61^2=0.3721\), whereas four original teacher half-steps give \(0.6^2=0.36\). The inherited discrepancy is \(0.0121|x_0|\) before the new student's own fitting error. Progressive distillation can work extremely well; the example shows why every stage must be evaluated against the original teacher, not only its immediate predecessor.

    Consistency distillation¤

    Consistency distillation teaches states on one teacher trajectory to share an endpoint prediction. It can reach one or few steps without repeating a halving curriculum. The result inherits the teacher path and solver used to construct targets.

    Two teacher solvers can discretize the same nominal probability-flow ODE into different finite training pairs. If the student is trained on those pairs, its finite target includes solver choice and tolerance. Report the solver, schedule, guidance, precision, and whether targets are cached or generated online.

    Latent adversarial diffusion distillation¤

    Latent Adversarial Diffusion Distillation (LADD) replaces ADD's fixed pretrained DINOv2 discriminator with generative features from a pretrained latent diffusion model. The resulting adversarial distillation method supports few-step, high-resolution synthesis while avoiding that specific fixed-discriminator dependency. It still adds a discriminator-mediated objective to the teacher signal, so its feature choice, optimization behavior, and distribution coverage must be evaluated separately rather than inferred from perceptual quality alone.

    The archival publication is the SIGGRAPH Asia 2024 paper identified by DOI 10.1145/3680528.3687625. The arXiv record remains useful for open access, but should not be mislabeled as a CVPR paper. “Avoiding a fixed DINOv2 discriminator” also does not mean “no discriminator” or “no pretrained feature network.”

    Distribution Matching Distillation¤

    DMD updates a one-step generator using score differences between teacher/data-related and student distributions, with an additional regression component. DMD-style methods match distributions rather than individual teacher trajectories. Their learned auxiliary score, mode coverage, and data access are central qualifiers.

    For generator \(x=G_\theta(z)\), the state-space score difference becomes a parameter update only through the generator Jacobian. Schematically,

    \[ \nabla_\theta\mathcal D \propto \mathbb E_{z,t,\epsilon} \left[J_\theta G_\theta(z)^\top w(t)\bigl(s_{\mathrm{fake}}(x_t,t)-s_{\mathrm{target}}(x_t,t)\bigr)\right]. \]

    The fake score is itself estimated from student samples and changes during training. Stability, stale-target effects, weighting, and auxiliary-model quality therefore belong to the result.

    Score differences become parameter updates only after they are pulled through the student Jacobian and assigned noise-time weights. The score-distillation gradient treatment explains why a stopped-gradient pseudo-loss can install that update without proving that its displayed scalar is a global divergence.

    Worked KL caution: direction matters only with restrictions¤

    For a two-mode target \(p=(1/2,1/2)\) and candidate \(q=(0.99,0.01)\),

    \[ \operatorname{KL}(q\|p)\approx0.637, \qquad \operatorname{KL}(p\|q)\approx1.614. \]

    The forward direction penalizes the nearly missing second mode more strongly in this example. But if \(q\) can represent every distribution and optimization is exact, both KL directions have the same unique optimum \(q=p\). Saying “reverse KL is mode-seeking” describes behavior under restrictions, approximation, and optimization—not a different unconstrained optimum. With an exact zero where \(p>0\), forward KL is infinite; reverse KL can remain finite because it averages under \(q\).

    Modern methods combine objectives to address different failures. For example, the ICCV 2025 paper Trajectory Distribution Matching matches student and teacher distributions at intermediate trajectory times, while Adversarial Distribution Matching adds adversarial distributional machinery. These accepted results expand the design space; their text-to-image/video protocols do not establish a universal ordering over consistency or trajectory methods.

    Inverse and real-data distillation¤

    RealUID, ICLR 2026 oral, gives a universal inverse-distillation construction for matching models with real-data supervision and no GAN discriminator. Its theoretical coverage and empirical protocols do not imply that all teacher families can be compressed losslessly.

    Here “universal” belongs to the paper's declared construction and assumptions, not to every inverse problem or architecture. Audit observation operators, posterior identifiability, access to paired or unpaired real data, teacher queries, and the metric used to compare inverse outputs.

    Gumbel Distillation for Parallel Text Generation (Preprint, 2026) uses a Gumbel-noise coupling to define training targets from an autoregressive teacher for parallel students. Its reported language-model results are evidence for that teacher–student construction, not a general proof of distribution preservation. In particular, shared Gumbel latent randomness, exact configuration-level Gumbel-Max, local perturb-and-MAP, and a relaxed argmax are different objects; the randomized-optimization chapter derives the distinction.

    What distillation can and cannot preserve¤

    A student may match:

    • teacher endpoints for coupled noise;
    • teacher marginal distribution;
    • selected intermediate maps;
    • perceptual features or rewards;
    • or a combination.

    These targets are different. Low FID does not prove paired trajectory agreement; low paired error does not prove diversity; preference gains do not prove density matching.

    2. O2 — Construct matched baselines: hold data, representation, guidance, evaluation, and compute controls fixed where the research question permits.
    3. O3 — Build a complete compute ledger: report outer transitions, component calls, FLOPs, memory, latency, energy, and lifecycle training cost.
    4. O4 — Quantify uncertainty: separate sampling, prompt/data, training-seed, and hardware-run variation with suitable intervals and paired comparisons.
    5. O5 — Diagnose failure: combine exact finite oracles, composition tests, coverage, calibration, conditional fidelity, and systems profiling.
    6. O6 — Write a bounded conclusion: state the strongest supported result, important non-results, scope, artifacts, and reproduction conditions.

    Compute-matched evaluation¤

    Report:

    1. outer transitions, backbone calls, head calls, total NFE, and guidance evaluations;
    2. decoder/tokenizer cost, rejection or reranking, batch size, precision, compiler, and hardware;
    3. training from scratch versus teacher distillation, teacher generation cost, and total training FLOPs;
    4. dataset, resolution, conditioning, guidance scale, sample count, and metric implementation;
    5. endpoint quality, precision/recall or coverage, memorization, and paired/map error when claimed;
    6. quality across 1, 2, 4, and higher NFE rather than only the best operating point;
    7. composition, identity, inversion, and off-grid interval residuals for flow-map claims.

    For component \(c\), record calls \(n_c\), per-call floating-point work \(f_c\), measured energy \(e_c\), and latency contribution under the actual execution graph. Then report, without pretending the quantities are interchangeable,

    \[ F_{\mathrm{inference}}=\sum_cn_cf_c, \qquad E_{\mathrm{inference}}=\sum_cn_ce_c, \qquad T_{\mathrm{end}}\ne\sum_cn_ct_c \]

    in general because calls can overlap, fuse, synchronize, transfer data, or wait on other components. Lifecycle accounting additionally includes teacher training if performed for the study, teacher-target generation, student training, hyperparameter sweeps, failed runs, and the expected deployment volume. Amortization assumptions must be explicit rather than used to declare one method globally “efficient.”

    Statistical unit and uncertainty¤

    Thousands of generated samples from one trained checkpoint do not constitute thousands of independent training replicates. Distinguish:

    • sampling uncertainty: finite generated/reference samples for a fixed checkpoint;
    • prompt or condition uncertainty: which evaluation conditions were drawn;
    • training uncertainty: initialization, data order, and optimization seed;
    • systems uncertainty: warm-up, compiler, clock, contention, and repeated hardware runs.

    Use paired comparisons when methods share prompts or latent seeds, bootstrap at the correct sampling unit, and report variation across independent training runs when claiming method stability. If only one checkpoint exists, say so; a narrow sample-level interval is not a training-stability interval.

    Worked exact comparison: accuracy, composition, and cost¤

    Use \(\dot x=-2x\), \(x_0=1\), and horizon one. The exact endpoint is \(e^{-2}\approx0.135335\). Compare four declared programs:

    Program Endpoint Absolute error Principal calls
    exact analytic map \(0.135335\) \(0\) 0 neural
    one Euler step \(-1\) \(1.135335\) 1 field
    two Euler half-steps \(0\) \(0.135335\) 2 field
    learned direct multiplier \(0.14\) \(0.14\) \(0.004665\) 1 map
    learned half multiplier \(0.38\), composed twice \(0.1444\) \(0.009065\) 2 maps

    The direct learned map is more accurate than its composed half-map in this example, while the analytic map is exact without a network. No universal method ranking follows: the learned multipliers were stipulated, not trained, and latency depends on the field/map architectures. The oracle nevertheless catches sign errors, verifies composition arithmetic, and forces the study to name the counted component.

    Now suppose measured warm latency for the one-call direct map is \(6\) ms, two half-map calls take \(9\) ms because of cache reuse, and one/two field programs take \(4\)/\(7\) ms. Accuracy-versus-call and accuracy-versus-latency order the programs differently. Report both; neither substitutes for distributional quality on a real generative task.

    Reproduction and limitation card¤

    Every few-step result should ship or record:

    1. source revision, environment/containers, weights, licenses, and data split;
    2. target-generation code, teacher revision, solver, schedule, guidance, and cache provenance;
    3. student architecture, objective weights, time/interval sampler, JVP and stop-gradient choices, optimizer, precision, and independent seeds;
    4. exact inference pseudocode with outer transitions and every component call;
    5. metric implementations, reference statistics, prompts/conditions, sample counts, confidence intervals, and failed/excluded runs;
    6. hardware, warm-up, synchronization, batching, compilation, memory, latency, throughput, and energy protocol;
    7. known unsupported intervals, guidance scales, modalities, resolutions, editing/inverse queries, and failure cases.

    An artifact can reproduce a number without validating the scientific attribution. Conversely, a mathematically correct identity can be implemented incorrectly. Preserve source, semantic, numerical, statistical, and systems evidence as separate layers.

    Exercise 1 solution

    For \(dx/dt=ax\), \(F(x,t,s)=e^{a(s-t)}x\). Identity follows at \(s=t\); composition follows because \(e^{a(r-s)}e^{a(s-t)}=e^{a(r-t)}\). Any learned residual measures approximation, not a failure of the exact flow.

    1. Why should quality-versus-NFE and quality-versus-latency both be reported?
    2. Which baseline separates architectural improvement from additional teacher compute?
    3. What diagnostic distinguishes mode loss from ordinary sampling variance?

    Misconception checks: evidence and deployment¤

    • “More generated samples establish training stability.” They narrow sampling error for one checkpoint, not variation across trained models.
    • “Matched NFE is matched compute.” Architecture, internal heads, guidance, shapes, and hardware remain uncontrolled.
    • “A better FID at one budget dominates the frontier.” Quality is multi-objective and budgets form a curve.
    • “Reproducible means scientifically valid.” Repeating an implementation does not repair a confounded comparison or overbroad claim.

    Final synthesis¤

    Few-step generation learns finite-time behavior instead of repeatedly evaluating infinitesimal dynamics. Consistency, flow-map, MeanFlow, Transition-Matching, and distillation methods differ in whether they target endpoints, deterministic two-time maps, average velocities, stochastic kernels, or teacher distributions. Their fairest common currency is not the word “step,” but complete compute plus the exact behavior trained to be preserved.
