    1. Random Measures: A Distribution over Distributions¤

    Chapter 1 definition and scope¤

    A random measure is a random element whose realization assigns a measure to measurable sets. A random probability measure \(G\) additionally satisfies \(G(\Theta)=1\) almost surely. Drawing \(G\) and then drawing \(\theta_i\mid G\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim G\) defines a joint law over a potentially unbounded collection of repeated or novel latent values.

    This is a probability-level model family. A neural network that accepts variable-sized inputs, a finite mixture with a large \(K\), or an adaptive data structure is not a Bayesian nonparametric model merely because its effective behavior grows with data. Conversely, an implementation may represent only finitely many atoms at one iteration while still targeting an infinite-dimensional posterior.

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes measurable events, conditional probability, the beta and Dirichlet distributions, and Bayesian conjugacy. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Define a Dirichlet process through its compatible finite-partition laws.
    2. O2 — Derive posterior and predictive probabilities for observed atoms.
    3. O3 — Connect finite-partition, predictive-urn, and stick-breaking views without confusing their random objects.
    4. O4 — Explain how projective consistency specifies an infinite-dimensional law from finite restrictions.
    5. O5 — Distinguish a completely random measure, its normalization, and the resulting loss of independent increments.
    6. O6 — Diagnose whether a finite representation is occupied structure, adaptive computation, or fixed truncation.

    The four common views answer different questions; the arrows below are mathematical relationships, not a recipe that makes the objects identical.

    The Dirichlet-process contract¤

    Let \(H\) be a probability measure on \((\Theta,\mathcal B)\) and \(\alpha>0\). The statement

    \[ G\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H) \]

    means that for every finite measurable partition \((A_1,\ldots,A_m)\) of \(\Theta\),

    \[ \bigl(G(A_1),\ldots,G(A_m)\bigr) \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet} \bigl(\alpha H(A_1),\ldots,\alpha H(A_m)\bigr). \]

    This finite-partition property is the definition introduced by Ferguson (1973). It gives, for any measurable \(A\),

    \[ \mathbb E[G(A)]=H(A), \qquad \operatorname{Var}[G(A)] =\frac{H(A)(1-H(A))}{\alpha+1}. \]

    Thus \(H\) is the prior mean distribution and \(\alpha\) controls concentration around it. Increasing \(\alpha\) does not add a fixed number of components. It decreases prior variability of set masses and changes the induced partition growth jointly with sample size.

    Conjugacy and the posterior random measure¤

    If the latent values themselves are observed,

    \[ \theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n\mid G\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim G, \]

    then the posterior remains a Dirichlet process:

    \[ G\mid\theta_{1:n} \sim\operatorname{DP}\!\left( \alpha+n, \frac{\alpha H+\sum_{i=1}^n\delta_{\theta_i}}{\alpha+n} \right). \]

    Integrating \(G\) out gives the Blackwell–MacQueen predictive distribution

    \[ \theta_{n+1}\mid\theta_{1:n} \sim \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+n}H +\frac{1}{\alpha+n}\sum_{i=1}^n\delta_{\theta_i}. \]

    If \(H\) is diffuse, the base-measure draw is almost surely new while the empirical atoms repeat old values. The sequence is exchangeable even though the predictive expression is written sequentially. The order is a factorization of a symmetric joint law, not a temporal mechanism.

    Worked finite event update¤

    Let \(A\subseteq\Theta\), \(H(A)=2/5\), and \(\alpha=4\). Before observing latent values,

    \[ G(A)\sim\operatorname{Beta}\!\left(\frac85,\frac{12}{5}\right), \qquad \mathbb E[G(A)]=\frac25. \]

    Suppose three observed atoms include two in \(A\) and one in \(A^c\). Conjugacy gives

    \[ G(A)\mid\theta_{1:3} \sim\operatorname{Beta}\!\left(\frac{18}{5},\frac{17}{5}\right), \]

    so the next latent draw lies in \(A\) with probability

    \[ \Pr(\theta_4\in A\mid\theta_{1:3}) =\frac{18/5}{7}=\frac{18}{35}. \]

    The predictive-mixture calculation agrees:

    \[ \frac{4}{7}\frac25+\frac{3}{7}\frac23 =\frac{8}{35}+\frac{10}{35} =\frac{18}{35}. \]

    This is an update for directly observed atoms. In a mixture model the \(\theta_i\) are latent, so their posterior assignments and component parameters must also be inferred; DP conjugacy does not make the mixture posterior closed form.

    Stick breaking: atoms and size-biased weights¤

    Sethuraman's constructive representation draws

    \[ v_k\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim\operatorname{Beta}(1,\alpha), \qquad \pi_k=v_k\prod_{j<k}(1-v_j), \qquad \phi_k\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim H, \]

    and sets

    \[ G=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\pi_k\delta_{\phi_k}. \]

    The weights sum to one almost surely under the usual conditions. Their displayed order is size-biased, not sorted from largest to smallest. In particular,

    \[ \mathbb E[\pi_k] =\frac{1}{1+\alpha} \left(\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha}\right)^{k-1}, \qquad \mathbb E\!\left[\prod_{j=1}^{K}(1-v_j)\right] =\left(\frac{\alpha}{1+\alpha}\right)^K. \]

    The second expression is a prior expected residual mass, not a deterministic truncation-error bound and not a posterior guarantee.

    A diffuse base does not make a DP draw continuous

    Even when \(H\) has no atoms, a draw \(G\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H)\) is almost surely discrete. A Dirichlet-process mixture density can be continuous because a kernel \(p(x\mid\theta)\) smooths those random atoms. The random mixing measure and the induced observation density are different objects.

    Completely random measures and normalization¤

    A completely random measure (CRM) \(\mu\) assigns independent random masses to disjoint measurable sets. It often has an atomic representation

    \[ \mu=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}J_k\delta_{\phi_k}, \]

    with jumps governed by a Poisson random measure and a Lévy intensity. A gamma process is a CRM. When \(0<\mu(\Theta)<\infty\) almost surely, normalization produces

    \[ G=\frac{\mu}{\mu(\Theta)}. \]

    Normalizing a gamma process yields a Dirichlet process with corresponding base measure. The normalized masses are no longer independent across disjoint sets because they share the random denominator. Other normalized CRMs generate alternatives such as normalized generalized-gamma models. The Lévy measure, normalization, and observation likelihood must all be declared; “uses a CRM” does not determine a unique generative model.

    Three finite objects that must not be confused¤

    Table BNP.3. Three finite objects that must not be confused
    Finite object Why it is finite What it says about the model
    occupied atoms in \(n\) observations at most \(n\) distinct values can appear the next observation can still instantiate a new atom
    instantiated atoms in one slice/MCMC step an auxiliary variable makes only finitely many atoms relevant to that transition the transition may still target the untruncated posterior
    fixed truncation at \(T\) atoms the algorithm or model discards/aggregates the remaining stick an approximation error or changed finite model must be reported

    Infinite objects are specified through compatible finite restrictions¤

    An infinite-dimensional prior is usable because every finite query has a well-defined law, and those laws agree when a query is reordered, restricted, or coarsened. The required operation depends on the random object:

    • Random function \(f\). A finite restriction is the vector \(f(x_1),\ldots,f(x_n)\). Permuting query points must permute coordinates, and marginalizing points must recover the smaller law.
    • Random probability measure \(G\). A finite restriction is its mass vector on a measurable partition. Merging partition cells must add their masses and recover the law declared directly on the coarser partition.
    • Random partition or feature allocation. A finite restriction contains the first \(n\) items or rows. Deleting later items must give the law declared directly for those retained.

    These are model-level coherence conditions. They are not guarantees that a finite truncation, separately trained network, or approximate posterior has preserved the infinite law.

    For a concrete Dirichlet-process check, let a three-cell partition satisfy

    \[ \bigl(H(A_1),H(A_2),H(A_3)\bigr) =\left(\frac12,\frac13,\frac16\right) \]

    and set \(\alpha=6\). The DP definition gives

    \[ \bigl(G(A_1),G(A_2),G(A_3)\bigr) \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,2,1). \]

    Merge \(A_2\) and \(A_3\). Dirichlet aggregation gives

    \[ \bigl(G(A_1),G(A_2\cup A_3)\bigr) \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,3). \]

    This is exactly the law obtained by applying the DP definition directly to the coarser partition, because \(H(A_2\cup A_3)=1/2\). The mean mass of \(A_1\) remains \(1/2\) in both restrictions. One cannot choose unrelated Dirichlet parameters at different partition resolutions and still claim that they arise from one random measure.

    The analogous coordinate-restriction rule for random functions is developed in Stochastic-Process and Function-Space Generators.

    Connections and misconception checks¤

    • A DP is a latent-variable prior that can be paired with many likelihoods; it is not itself a Gaussian mixture.
    • A stochastic process and a random measure are both infinite-dimensional random objects, but one supplies compatible indexed values while the other assigns random mass to sets.
    • A probabilistic program may express a DP model and inference algorithm, but the programming language does not define the DP semantics.
    • A large finite neural mixture is not distributionally equivalent to a DP mixture merely because both can activate different experts.

    Independent practice and assessment map: random measures¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Keep \(H(A)=2/5\) and \(alpha=4\) from the worked example, but observe one atom in \(A\) and four in \(A^c\). Predict whether the next-event probability is above or below the prior mean \(2/5\), then derive it from both the beta posterior and the Blackwell–MacQueen rule.
    2. P1.2 — Finite-partition derivation. For \(alpha=6\) and base masses \((1/2,1/3,1/6)\), write the three-cell Dirichlet law, merge the final two cells, and prove that the result equals the law defined directly on the coarser partition.
    3. P1.3 — View ledger. For the DP, identify what is random and what has been integrated out in the finite-partition, predictive-urn, and stick-breaking views. Explain why size-biased stick order is not a ranked frequency list.
    4. P1.4 — Truncation audit. With \(alpha=3\), compute the expected residual after \(K=4\) ordinary sticks. Give one reason this number is neither a deterministic residual bound nor a posterior predictive error bound.
    5. P1.5 — CRM normalization. Suppose two disjoint-set masses are independent before normalization. Show algebraically why dividing both by their shared total generally couples them, and name the independence property that is lost.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Specify the proof obligations for replacing the gamma CRM by another CRM and calling its normalization a random probability measure: state the Lévy intensity, total-mass condition, normalization, finite restriction or predictive law, likelihood, posterior query, and finite inference representation.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. The posterior is \(G(A)\mid\theta_{1:5}\sim\operatorname{Beta}(8/5+1,12/5+4)=\operatorname{Beta}(13/5,32/5)\), so the next-event probability is \((13/5)/9=13/45<2/5\). The predictive calculation is \((4/9)(2/5)+(5/9)(1/5)=8/45+5/45=13/45\).
    2. The fine law is \(\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,2,1)\). Dirichlet aggregation gives \((G(A_1),G(A_2)+G(A_3))\sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(3,3)\), exactly the direct DP law because \(6H(A_1)=3\) and \(6H(A_2\cup A_3)=3\).
    3. The finite-partition view samples set masses of \(G\); the predictive view has integrated out \(G\) and samples the next atom conditional on previous atoms; stick breaking samples an explicit atomic \(G\) from weights and locations. Stick order records discovery under size-biased sampling and need not be decreasing, so it is not the ranked-mass representation.
    4. The expectation is \((3/4)^4=81/256\approx0.3164\). A realized residual is random, and the expectation is prior rather than posterior/query-specific; a small or large tail mass can have a disproportionate effect on a selected likelihood functional.
    5. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent positive masses and \(T\) includes \(X+Y\), then \(X/T+Y/T\le1\) and increasing one normalized share constrains the others. Their common random denominator induces dependence. The CRM's independent-increments property over disjoint sets is lost.
    6. A valid transfer gives the CRM's Lévy intensity and conditions ensuring \(0<\mu(\Theta)<\infty\) almost surely, defines \(G=\mu/\mu(\Theta)\), establishes compatible finite laws or a predictive construction, declares \(p(x\mid\theta)\) and the posterior functional, and says whether computation integrates, adaptively instantiates, or truncates atoms. The name of the CRM alone supplies none of those obligations.
    Retrieval questions
    1. What finite-dimensional statement defines a Dirichlet process?
    2. Why can a diffuse \(H\) coexist with repeated \(\theta_i\) values?
    3. Is a stick-breaking truncation the same operation as marginalizing unused atoms?
    4. Which independence property is lost when a CRM is normalized?
    5. What must agree when one finite partition of \(\Theta\) is coarsened?
    Complete solutions
    1. Every finite measurable partition receives Dirichlet-distributed random masses with parameters \(\alpha H(A_j)\).
    2. A DP draw is discrete almost surely; conditional IID draws from that discrete \(G\) repeat its atoms even though atom locations came from diffuse \(H\).
    3. No. Marginalization preserves the infinite model; fixed truncation approximates it or defines a finite alternative.
    4. Independent increments over disjoint sets: normalized masses share the total-mass denominator.
    5. The law obtained by adding masses of merged cells must equal the law defined directly on the coarser partition. For a DP this follows from Dirichlet aggregation.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A random probability measure is the model object; compatible finite partitions, posterior conjugacy, predictive reinforcement, and stick breaking are views of the same Dirichlet process. Finite occupied structure is not fixed truncation, and none of these constructions makes an arbitrary mixture posterior exact. Chapter 2 composes the random measure with a likelihood and derives the resulting partition law.
