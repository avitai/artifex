    Learning path

    Required knowledge: none. This page is a supported entry point.

    Why this map remains one page

    This page is the book-wide orientation and cross-family comparison surface, not a continuous instructional volume. It remains a single, section-linked map so readers can compare families before entering the shorter chapter route. The instructional foundations that follow are divided into chapter views.

    New to generative modeling? Take the first-course route

    Read the short opening of this landscape for orientation, then follow A First Course in Generative Modeling. Its seven chapters assume basic Python but no generative-model background, use one finite example across all six core families, include executable code, and end with a diagnostic that selects your next foundation or family chapter. You do not need to read this entire map first.

    Generative modeling studies mathematical objects and algorithms that construct, simulate, score, complete, or transform structured objects. A probabilistic generative model defines a joint or conditional law; an implicit, energy-based, constructive, or constraint-defined generator may expose a different set of queries. This map classifies each system by the object it defines, the information used to train it, and the operations it supports.

    • Queries and Model Families

      A navigable classification of classical models, VAEs, GANs, diffusion, flows, EBMs, autoregressive models, probabilistic circuits, implicit generators, discrete iterative models, BFNs, GFlowNets, stochastic-process generators, and causal generative semantics

    • Relationship Labels

      Algebraic equivalence, dynamical equivalence, population-optimum correspondence, shared objectives, implementation analogies, and hybrid composition—with the assumptions required by each claim

    • Evidence and Failure Analysis

      Scaling laws, metric failure modes, mechanistic interpretation, representation-alignment hypotheses, and open theoretical problems with explicit evidence boundaries

    • Research Problems

      Falsifiable questions derived from the cross-family map, each scoped by assumptions, available data, compute, and evaluation protocol

    Why This Map Exists¤

    Generative modeling is not one architecture or one sampling algorithm. It studies objects and processes that can construct, simulate, score, complete, or transform data. Many of these objects define probability laws; others expose only a sampler, deterministic construction, feasible set, energy, or reward. A complete specification therefore states its probability semantics when they exist instead of assuming that every generator has a tractable density. Six core-family volumes provide the first neural-model route:

    • Autoregressive — normalized conditional factorization of an ordered object;
    • VAE — latent-variable model trained with approximate posterior inference;
    • GAN — implicit generator trained through an adversarial comparison objective;
    • EBM — normalized or unnormalized law specified through an energy function;
    • Diffusion — probability path with learned reverse-time or transport information; and
    • Flow — invertible change of variables or time-dependent vector-field transport.

    The same map also places classical graphical and latent-variable models, probabilistic circuits, implicit moment-matching generators, discrete iterative models, Bayesian Flow Networks, GFlowNets, distributions over functions, random measures, event processes, relational laws, copulas, constructive generators, causal generative models, and cross-cutting geometric and multimodal constructions. The conditioning, steering, constraints, and selection volume then separates a base family from the mechanisms that condition, guide, restrict, retrieve for, or select from it. Their relationships must state what is preserved, what changes, and which assumptions make a claimed correspondence valid.

    The likelihood and normalizer estimation volume provides the shared inference layer for latent marginals, Bayesian evidence, partition functions, and conditional normalizers. It keeps the probabilistic query separate from importance, annealing, particle, bridge, path, or nested estimators used to approximate it.

    The filtering and smoothing volume provides the sequential-inference layer for state-space generators. It keeps the latent-path law separate from online filtering, retrospective smoothing, forecasting, exact Gaussian closure, nonlinear projection, ensemble or particle representations, and learned inference implementations.

    The Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods volume provides the reusable population-inference layer. It separates growing-path particle filters from fixed-target SMC samplers; current weight concentration from genealogical collapse; particle ESS from MCMC ESS and tempering CESS; and normalized expectations from normalizing-constant estimates. State-space, Bayesian, energy-based, diffusion, autoregressive, and constrained-generation chapters reuse that contract without turning SMC into a model family.

    This volume is therefore both the entry map and the final integration volume. Read its opening first for orientation. If the formal vocabulary is new, take the first-course route; otherwise enter the foundation or family chapter whose prerequisite panel you satisfy. Return to this map for the complete cross-family argument. The routes below are entry points, not abbreviated replacements. Empirical sections—the Platonic Hypothesis, Scaling Laws Across Families, Evaluation, Open Theoretical Problems, and Research Problems—require evidence labels and protocol qualifiers rather than timeless or family-wide interpretation.

    Learning contract¤

    No prior generative-model family is required, but the formal sections assume joint and conditional probability, expectation, gradients, and basic numerical optimization. Readers missing those tools should take the first course before the formal layers; the course introduces each tool just in time and routes remaining gaps to the complete foundation. By the end of the complete guide, you should be able to:

    1. specify a generative system along independent axes—probability semantics, representation, latent/state structure, objective, estimator, architecture, conditioning, and sampler;
    2. place classical, neural, causal, stochastic-process, circuit, implicit, iterative, and constructive families in one extensible taxonomy;
    3. label every claimed cross-family correspondence as algebraic, distributional/dynamical, population-optimum, shared-objective, implementation analogy, or hybrid composition;
    4. work through a finite example showing how two families can represent the same distribution while exposing different queries;
    5. decompose a generative design or world-model system into its reference support, predictive model, target construction, inference engine, and evidence loop;
    6. separate observational conditioning, intervention, exogenous-state abduction, and same-unit counterfactual generation;
    7. reject family-level claims inferred from one architecture, benchmark, sampler, or vendor system; and
    8. design a controlled family comparison with matched data, representation, conditioning, compute, evaluation, and evidence maturity.

    How to Read This Volume¤

    The material has three cumulative reading layers. Each layer assumes the distinctions established by the preceding layer:

    First exposure: read this page only through the route table below, complete the seven-chapter first course, and use its diagnostic. The longer layers here are then a map you can revisit rather than a prerequisite wall.

    A dependency-aware route through six neural core families (after the mathematical foundations):

    1. Autoregressive Models — chain-rule factorization and normalized conditionals provide the most direct example of tractable likelihood by construction.
    2. VAEs — latent-variable marginalization, approximate posterior inference, and the ELBO prepare the variational formulations used again in diffusion.
    3. GANs — implicit distributions, learned density ratios or critics, and two-player optimization prepare the adversarial objectives used in hybrid and distilled systems.
    4. Energy-Based Models — unnormalized densities, scalar energies, scores, and MCMC establish the distinctions needed to compare energy functions, diffusion scores, and flow fields.
    5. Diffusion Models — discrete and continuous probability paths combine variational, score-matching, and numerical-sampling ideas across several state spaces.
    6. Normalizing Flows and Flow Matching — change of variables, continuous transport, and conditional vector-field regression make exact and approximate transport contracts explicit.

    That order is a dependency-aware learning route, not a ranking or a division into primary and secondary families. Probabilistic circuits build on classical graphical models; implicit generators build on kernel and density-ratio objectives; discrete iterative generation builds on autoregressive and diffusion ideas; Bayesian Flow Networks build on Bayesian updating and diffusion; GFlowNets build on flow conservation, rewards, and sequential decisions; stochastic-process generators build on Gaussian processes and latent dynamics; random-measure models, point processes, and random-graph generators make their generated objects explicit; causal generative models add intervention and counterfactual semantics to several base families.

    Begin with the Query, Not the Family¤

    A model family is useful only through the operations it makes possible. “Can this model generate?” is therefore too vague. Generate what, under which evidence or constraints, with what probability semantics, and at what computational cost? The same joint law may admit easy sampling but difficult conditioning, or easy scoring but difficult marginalization. Conversely, two model classes may represent the same law while exposing it through different algorithms.

    Let \(X=(X_O,X_M)\) split an object into an observed part \(X_O\) and a missing part \(X_M\), let \(Z\) denote an optional latent variable, and let \(c\) denote context. The principal questions are:

    Table GM.2. Begin with the Query, Not the Family
    Query or operation Mathematical target when probabilistic What an answer provides What it does not provide automatically
    Unconditional generation \(x\sim p_\theta(x)\) New draws from the model law A density value, likelihood, or controllable sample
    Conditional generation \(x\sim p_\theta(x\mid c)\) A distribution of outputs compatible with context Causal effects of setting \(c\)
    Scoring \(p_\theta(x)\), \(\log p_\theta(x)\), or \(-E_\theta(x)\) Probability mass, density value, or explicitly unnormalized relative score at a specified input Efficient sampling, semantic quality, or OOD reliability
    Marginalization \(p_\theta(x_O)=\int p_\theta(x_O,x_M)\,dx_M\) Probability of a partial observation The most likely or a representative completion
    Distributional completion \(x_M\sim p_\theta(x_M\mid x_O)\) Diverse completions with model uncertainty A single loss-minimizing prediction
    Point completion \(\hat x_M=\arg\min_a\mathbb E[L(a,X_M)\mid x_O]\) One action optimal for a declared loss Samples from the conditional law; for \(0\)–\(1\) loss it returns a mode
    Latent inference \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\) or an approximation \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) Explanations or representations under a latent model Identifiable or semantically unique latent variables
    Design under properties \(p_\theta(x\mid y)\), \(p_\theta(x)R(x)/Z_R\), or constrained construction Candidates satisfying evidence, reward, or feasibility requirements The same target law across these three formulations
    Process simulation \(x_{0:T}\sim p_\theta(x_{0:T}\mid a_{0:T-1})\) Coherent trajectories under initial conditions or actions Interventional or counterfactual validity without causal assumptions
    Representation extraction \(h_\theta(x)\) or a posterior summary Features for retrieval, prediction, control, or analysis A probability query unless \(h_\theta\) belongs to a declared probabilistic model

    Sums replace integrals for discrete variables. Some rows are constructive rather than probabilistic: a grammar may enumerate valid programs, a constraint solver may return feasible objects, and a deterministic decoder may map each seed to one output without exposing a normalized output density. Those are legitimate generative operations, but probability notation must not be attached to them unless a distribution over seeds, choices, or outputs has actually been defined.

    Causal queries: conditioning is not intervention¤

    Conditional generation holds the learned data-generating regime fixed and restricts attention to a context. A causal intervention changes a structural assignment. A unit counterfactual changes that assignment after factual evidence has updated the unit's exogenous state. These operations can all produce samples while answering different questions.

    The causal volume's finite treatment-response SCM makes the separation numerical:

    Table GM.3. Causal queries: conditioning is not intervention
    Operation Target Exact value in the finite SCM Additional contract
    conditional generation \(P(Y=1\mid T=1)\) \(13/16\) reproduce the selected treated population
    intervention \(P(Y=1\mid do(T=1))\) \(3/4\) replace the treatment mechanism and retain the population baseline law
    abduction \(P(U_Y\mid C=1,T=0,Y=0)\) uniform on \(\{3,4,5,6,7\}\) infer factual unit noise under a declared SCM
    counterfactual generation \(P(Y_1=1\mid C=1,T=0,Y=0)\) \(4/5\) in the shared-threshold SCM retain the abducted noise after changing treatment

    The first two values differ because conditioning on treatment changes the distribution of a common cause, whereas intervention does not. The fourth value is even more demanding: another SCM can match the entire observational law and every treatment-regime marginal yet give value \(1\) for the same unit query. Therefore a complete causal-generative workflow asks, in order:

    1. What observational, interventional, or counterfactual quantity is requested?
    2. Which graph, mechanisms, hidden causes, data regimes, and target population are assumed?
    3. Is the quantity identified, partially identified, or unsupported?
    4. Does the available data satisfy consistency, positivity, measurement, and selection requirements?
    5. Only then: which generator, estimator, sampler, and uncertainty procedure computes it?

    Architecture enters at step 5. A diffusion model, flow, VAE, GAN, or autoregressive model can parameterize a conditional mechanism, but none converts a condition into a \(do\)-operation or chooses an identified cross-world coupling by architecture alone. See the identification-before-estimation sequence for the full decision path and finite sensitivity calculation.

    A complete finite example: completion is a distribution¤

    Consider two binary pixels \(X_1,X_2\in\{0,1\}\) with joint mass function

    Table GM.4. A complete finite example: completion is a distribution
    state \((x_1,x_2)\) mass \(p(x_1,x_2)\)
    \((0,0)\) \(0.45\)
    \((0,1)\) \(0.05\)
    \((1,0)\) \(0.10\)
    \((1,1)\) \(0.40\)

    The entries are non-negative and sum to one, so the table defines a normalized model. Suppose the first pixel is observed to be one. Marginalization gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(X_1=1)&=p(1,0)+p(1,1)\\ &=0.10+0.40=0.50. \end{aligned} \]

    The conditional distribution of the missing pixel is therefore

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(X_2=0\mid X_1=1)&=\frac{0.10}{0.50}=0.20,\\ p(X_2=1\mid X_1=1)&=\frac{0.40}{0.50}=0.80. \end{aligned} \]

    A point completion under \(0\)–\(1\) loss fills in \(X_2=1\) every time. A distributional completion samples one with probability \(0.8\) and zero with probability \(0.2\). The point answer is appropriate when one decision is required; it is not a faithful collection of draws from the conditional law. Repeating only the modal answer erases genuine model uncertainty.

    This boundary is not determined by output size or by how many reference answers a dataset provides. A classifier may return a complete non-degenerate law \(p(y\mid x)\) for a scalar label, while an image or sequence system may return a single loss-minimizing output. The probability law, sampler, candidate set, loss, and decision rule are separate interfaces. The finite three-outcome example shows how changing the loss changes the best point action without changing the conditional law.

    The same table also illustrates that representation and query access are separate. An autoregressive factorization can write

    \[ p(x_1,x_2)=p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1), \]

    while an energy representation can write

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(x_1,x_2)&=\frac{\exp[-E(x_1,x_2)]}{Z},\\ E(x_1,x_2)&=-\log p(x_1,x_2)+C. \end{aligned} \]

    On this four-state space, enumeration makes normalization, conditioning, and sampling easy for either representation. In a high-dimensional implementation, locally normalized autoregressive conditionals still give a direct joint likelihood and ancestral sampler, whereas a generic energy parameterization may require an intractable partition function and a mixing-limited Markov chain. This is algebraic equivalence of representations for the finite law, not equivalence of their general-purpose inference costs.

    Sampling access does not imply efficient conditioning¤

    Suppose a joint sampler returns \((X,Y)\) exactly, but the desired condition \(Y=1\) has probability \(\varepsilon\). Rejection sampling—draw from the joint and keep only samples with \(Y=1\)—does produce exact draws from \(p(x\mid Y=1)\). Its expected number of joint draws per accepted sample is

    \[ \mathbb E[N]=\frac{1}{\varepsilon}. \]

    If \(\varepsilon=10^{-9}\), one accepted conditional sample requires one billion joint draws on average. Thus an unconditional sampling oracle establishes that the conditional distribution exists and supplies a correct but potentially unusable algorithm; it does not establish efficient conditioning. The reverse implication also fails in general: evaluating an unnormalized energy does not reveal its partition function or supply an efficient sampler. Query complexity depends on the representation, structural restrictions, and inference algorithm—not merely on the existence of a distribution.

    Probabilistic circuits make this distinction especially explicit: structural properties can make marginals or conditionals efficient, while harder queries such as marginal MAP need stronger restrictions or different algorithms (Choi, Friedman, and Van den Broeck, 2022). An implicit generator such as the original GAN construction provides a direct sampling path without an ordinary tractable output-density query (Goodfellow et al., 2014). These are different query contracts, not better and worse versions of one interface.

    The tractable-query contract¤

    For every model in this book, ask five questions in order:

    1. Definition: Is the requested density, conditional, posterior, intervention, trajectory law, reward-proportional law, or constructive operation actually defined?
    2. Identification: Do the available observations and assumptions determine it? This is indispensable for causal and latent-variable claims.
    3. Exact computation: Does the representation admit an exact algorithm, and under which structural constraints?
    4. Approximation: If exact computation is unavailable, what estimator or sampler is used, and what are its bias, variance, consistency, and convergence assumptions?
    5. Finite resources: At the stated dimension, tolerance, NFE, memory, and wall-clock budget, does the algorithm answer the intended query well enough?

    “Tractable” is never a property of a family name in isolation. It qualifies a particular query, representation class, algorithm, and resource model. The property-based taxonomy now has a purpose: classify the object only after specifying what the reader needs to do with it.

    Retrieval practice: query before family
    1. Why is a conditional mode not a sample from the conditional distribution?
    2. If exact joint samples are available, what determines the cost of rejection-based conditioning?
    3. Can a deterministic generator be a generative object without exposing \(p_\theta(x)\)?
    4. What four qualifiers must accompany the word “tractable”?

    Solutions. (1) A mode repeats one maximizer, whereas sampling preserves all conditional probabilities. (2) The probability of the conditioning event; the expected draw count is its reciprocal. (3) Yes. It can define a constructive map or feasible output mechanism; a density exists only after suitable probability semantics are declared. (4) The query, representation class, algorithm, and resource model.

    Synthesis and bridge. Start model selection with the target operation and its required accuracy and cost. Then classify the state space, probability semantics, objective, estimator, architecture, conditioning mechanism, and sampler. The next section supplies those independent axes.

    A Property-Based Taxonomy¤

    A family name is historical shorthand. A precise model specification answers at least six independent questions:

    1. What object or process is represented, and what are its probability semantics when present? A normalized density, unnormalized energy, conditional distribution, stochastic process, reward-proportional terminal law, deterministic construction, feasible set, or sampling procedure.
    2. Where does uncertainty live? In observed variables, latent variables, parameters, trajectories, constructive actions, or a function space.
    3. What is the state space? Discrete tokens, Euclidean vectors, sequences, sets, graphs, manifolds, mixed modalities, programs, or functions.
    4. What signal trains the model? Likelihood, a variational bound, adversarial density-ratio estimation, moments/kernels, scores, energies, vector fields, transitions, Bayesian updates, or rewards.
    5. How are samples produced? Direct mapping, ancestral factorization, iterative refinement, MCMC, ODE/SDE integration, or constructive policy rollout.
    6. Which queries are tractable? Sampling, likelihood, marginals, conditionals, posterior inference, interventions, counterfactuals, or partition functions.

    Six Relationship Labels¤

    Every cross-family statement in this book uses one of these labels:

    1. Algebraic equivalence: the two expressions are related by exact algebra under the same variables.
    2. Distributional/dynamical equivalence: distributions or marginals coincide under stated regularity and endpoint assumptions.
    3. Population-optimum correspondence: ideal expected objectives share an optimum; finite estimators and networks can differ.
    4. Shared estimator or objective pattern: training code has analogous regression or contrastive form without defining the same model.
    5. Implementation analogy: a useful design metaphor, not a theorem.
    6. Hybrid composition: distinct models are components of one system.

    This vocabulary prevents “uses the same algebra” from silently becoming “is the same model.”

    Causal semantics are an additional layer

    Conditioning on \(C=c\) estimates or samples \(p(x\mid C=c)\). Intervening with \(\operatorname{do}(C=c)\) changes a structural mechanism. They coincide only under assumptions that must be stated. A controllable conditional generator is not automatically a causal model.

    Three Lenses for Looking at Generative Models¤

    A useful exercise before going deeper is to inspect a model through several partially independent lenses. The three below are valuable starting points, not an exhaustive or perfectly orthogonal taxonomy: a single system can combine multiple objectives, representations, and samplers, and some methods do not admit one privileged divergence or corruption process.

    Lens 1 — What Divergence Are We Minimising?¤

    Many generative objectives can be interpreted as minimizing a divergence, discrepancy, likelihood risk, or transport error between a model and the data distribution. The interpretation may hold only after optimizing an auxiliary discriminator, integrating across noise levels, or taking a population limit. It is therefore one design axis—not a universal one-line explanation of empirical behavior:

    Table GM.9. Lens 1 — What Divergence Are We Minimising?

    VAE (exact maximum-likelihood limit) Forward KL \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\|p_\theta)\); practical ELBO training also pays an inference-gap term The model-likelihood target is mode-covering in the ideal limit; amortization, posterior approximation, decoder capacity, and optimization change finite behavior

    Why this matters: objective geometry helps explain why adversarial generators can lose coverage, but no family-level slogan predicts empirical diversity. The original minimax GAN has a JS-divergence support-mismatch problem; the non-saturating loss changes generator gradients without changing the ideal discriminator fixed point; and practical collapse also depends on critic capacity, update balance, regularisation, data, representation, and finite-batch dynamics. Diffusion models can also lose rare modes, memorize, or trade diversity for guidance strength. Coverage must be measured under matched protocols.

    The forward-vs-reverse-KL story is one of the deepest symmetries in the field. Forward KL (\(p_d \| p_\theta\)) is zero-avoiding — assigning low probability to a mode that exists in \(p_d\) is heavily penalised, so MLE-style models (AR, VAE, classical flows) cover all modes but may also assign mass to regions that aren't in \(p_d\) (sometimes called "spreading"). Reverse KL (\(p_\theta \| p_d\)) is zero-forcing — assigning probability to a region without data is heavily penalised, so reverse-KL-like objectives and reward-shaped policies are sharper but may collapse to a subset of modes.

    A Common Mathematical Framework¤

    Many generative models approximate an observational distribution \(p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)\), but the curriculum is broader. Conditional models target \(p(x\mid c)\); stochastic-process models define consistent finite-dimensional marginals; causal models distinguish observational, interventional, and counterfactual laws; GFlowNets target a reward-proportional terminal distribution; and implicit generators may expose only samples. A normalizing integral is central for EBMs and density construction, but it is not the one bottleneck from which every family follows.

    The Fundamental Trilemma¤

    One influential paper framed an empirical generative learning trilemma among sample quality, coverage, and fast sampling for the methods and benchmarks it studied (Xiao et al., 2022). It is a useful historical diagnostic, not a general impossibility theorem saying every family can attain at most two:

    The three branches are independent measurement axes, not stages in a pipeline. A system can improve or regress on any subset, and each claimed movement needs the audit at the end of its branch.

    Systems occupy different empirical trade-off points through distillation, changed representations, stronger training, and specialized samplers. MeanFlow, R3GAN, Equilibrium Matching, and VAR report results that challenge older stereotypes under their respective protocols. None proves or disproves a family-independent trilemma. A scientific comparison must define quality, coverage, memorization, latency, compute, data, and conditioning before asking which trade-off boundary moved.

    Support Geometry Changes the Objective-Selection Problem¤

    The manifold hypothesis proposes that high-dimensional observations concentrate near lower-dimensional structure. Intrinsic dimension is estimator-, scale-, representation-, and dataset-dependent; natural images need not lie on one smooth, noise-free manifold. Treat the hypothesis as a useful geometric lens, not a settled fact that explains most family behavior (Pope et al., 2021).

    A law supported on a curve, surface, graph, or constraint set is still a valid probability law. It may have an intrinsic density with respect to arc length, surface area, or another reference measure while having no density with respect to ambient Lebesgue measure. Trouble begins when the target law, model law, likelihood, or divergence silently assumes incompatible measures or support geometry.

    Three common responses solve different problems:

    Table GM.13. Support Geometry Changes the Objective-Selection Problem
    Response What it changes What it does not guarantee
    Smooth both laws Creates overlapping ambient densities and a resolution scale A finite, low-variance objective at every bandwidth; preservation of hard constraints after sampling
    Use a support-compatible discrepancy Compares probability mass through a ground metric, kernel, or restricted witness class without requiring overlap That a finite critic/kernel estimator equals the population distance or supplies useful gradients
    Learn a representation, then its law Moves generation to an encoded state space and decodes afterward That the encoder preserves all distinctions, reconstruction is exact, or latent matching controls decoded error

    The complete finite-to-continuous counterexample, error decomposition, and assumption table shows why these are not interchangeable. In particular, Gaussian convolution is injective at the population level, Wasserstein claims require a declared metric and moment conditions, MMD claims require kernel conditions, and two-stage recovery requires both codec and latent-model assumptions. “Support-agnostic” is therefore too strong a description for any of them.

    Family implementations inherit these distinctions:

    • Classical flows ordinarily preserve dimension and model a full-dimensional density after preprocessing. Near-singular data geometry can create conditioning and support problems, but equal dimension does not prove wasted capacity or poor images.
    • Deterministic implicit generators define pushforward laws that may be singular when latent dimension is smaller than ambient dimension. Under an ideal original GAN discriminator, separated supports saturate the JS expression; finite smooth discriminators may behave differently and must be analyzed as estimators and gradient fields.
    • Gaussian diffusion smooths singular or separated laws at every positive noise level. This makes scores well-defined under standard conditions, while endpoint recovery still depends on low-noise estimation, the reverse process, and numerical error.
    • Latent diffusion and latent flow matching trade codec distortion for a state space that can be cheaper or easier to model. The learned latent is not guaranteed to equal intrinsic coordinates, and latent fit alone does not certify decoded fidelity.
    • Intrinsic, geometric, and constrained models declare a manifold, graph, symmetry, or constraint measure directly. Their benefit depends on correct structure and tractable intrinsic operations; a misspecified constraint can exclude valid data.
    • EBMs can define ambient or intrinsic energies, but energy parameterization alone does not choose a reference measure or supply a mixing sampler. Specialized constructions can encode equilibria or constraints, with their own assumptions.

    Codec plus diffusion or flow is influential because it reduces generator-side spatial cost while accepting reconstruction loss and codec bias. Better conditioning is an empirical design goal, not guaranteed by compression.

    Key insight from the graphical view: direction alone does not grant tractable likelihood. AR local normalization and flow invertibility do; a VAE's latent marginal is usually intractable. An unconstrained global EBM can represent broad dependencies but incurs normalization and sampling costs. A diffusion reverse chain is directed, while a true marginal score at each time is the gradient of a log-density and can therefore be associated with a time-indexed energy up to an additive constant. That is the precise score–energy relationship used below.

    Adding latent variables introduces marginalization, but it does not always make inference harder: conjugacy, conditional independence, dynamic programming, message passing, and specially structured transitions can make exact inference possible. VAEs use a variational bound for general neural latent models. DDPMs exploit a fixed forward chain and analytic Gaussian conditionals to derive a tractable variational training bound; their exact data marginal is not thereby free. Classical flows use deterministic invertibility so density follows by change of variables rather than latent marginalization.

    The complete graphical-inference volume makes three further distinctions visible.     The Latent-Variable Bridge: Structure Before Architecture¤

    Every latent model begins with a joint law such as

    \[ p_\theta(x,z)=p_\theta(z)p_\theta(x\mid z), \]

    but that notation does not determine how many latents exist, how they are connected, whether the data marginal is tractable, or how posterior inference is performed. Five choices must be named separately:

    1. the state space and semantics of \(z\);
    2. the graph connecting local, global, hierarchical, or temporal latents;
    3. the conditional families and their parameterization;
    4. the query—generation, likelihood, a posterior marginal, filtering, smoothing, or learning;
    5. the exact or approximate algorithm used for that query.

    Three bounded patterns are worth retaining:

    1. Regression form does not imply identical semantics. Noise, score, velocity, conditional expectation, and energy-gradient targets can all use squared error while defining different population objects and samplers. Stability must be measured; it does not follow from “uses MSE.”
    2. Adversarial and contrastive objectives remain base objectives as well as post-training components. Pure GANs, NCE, contrastive EBMs, and adversarial distillation occupy different roles. No chronological rule makes critics only post-training tools.
    3. Likelihood, bounds, and likelihood-free objectives coexist. AR and classical flows can optimize exact model likelihood; VAEs optimize a lower bound unless the posterior approximation is exact; diffusion objectives may be variationally weighted or simplified; implicit models deliberately avoid density evaluation. “MLE” is not one regularizer shared by all of them.

    Cross-Family Correspondences¤

    Family names often obscure relationships among the mathematical objects they use. Some correspondences are exact changes of variables; others preserve marginals or population targets, reuse an estimator pattern, or compose distinct system components. Each relationship below carries an explicit label.

    1. Score-based diffusion as time-indexed EBMs¤

    Algebraic equivalence for an integrable score field. When \(s(x,t)=\nabla_x\log p_t(x)\), define \(E(x,t)=-\log p_t(x)+c(t)\) and obtain \(s=-\nabla_xE\). The probability-flow ODE (Song et al., 2021) uses the marginal score but does not make every learned vector field conservative. → See diffusion-explained.md and ebm-explained.md.

    2. Diffusion and Gaussian-path Flow Matching share an algorithmic core¤

    Distributional/population-target correspondence under a chosen Gaussian path and parameterization. Flow Matching for Generative Modeling and later analyses relate conditional velocity, score, noise, and data targets. Shared marginals or convertible targets do not make finite solvers, weightings, couplings, or stochastic trajectories identical. Product names such as SD3 or FLUX.1 are system labels, not proof of a universal diffusion–flow equivalence. → See flow-explained.md and diffusion-explained.md.

    3. Latent Diffusion = VAE codec + diffusion prior¤

    Hybrid composition. Latent diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) compresses with a learned autoencoder and learns diffusion in the resulting representation. The codec need not be a well-calibrated VAE, and its reconstruction/perceptual objective remains distinct from the latent prior. Public descriptions of closed systems may not identify the exact codec. → See VAE tokenizer section.

    4. Adversarial Distillation = GAN + Diffusion¤

    Hybrid composition and shared adversarial objective. ADD (Sauer et al., 2023) and DMD2 (Yin et al., 2024) combine a pretrained diffusion teacher or score signal with adversarial/distribution-matching components for few-step generation. Exact NFE and quality are protocol-specific; non-adversarial consistency and latent-consistency methods form separate branches. → See GAN explainer.

    5. Energy Matching constrains transport with a scalar energy¤

    Hybrid objective / constrained vector-field parameterization. Energy Matching (Balcerak et al., 2025) uses a scalar potential to connect transport-like and equilibrium objectives under its construction. Equilibrium Matching (Preprint, 2025) reports optimization-defined generation and FID 1.90 on class-conditional ImageNet 256² under its protocol. Gradient optimization toward an equilibrium is not automatically sampling from the normalized Boltzmann law of that energy. → See EBM explainer.

    6. Autoregressive ↔ Energy-Based function-space correspondence¤

    Preprint function-space correspondence under the paper's construction. ARMs are Secretly EBMs relates autoregressive language models, energy functions, and a soft Bellman form. It does not make a generic global EBM's suffix marginals tractable, nor prove that every RLHF/DPO implementation is identical to energy shaping. → See EBM explainer and AR explainer.

    7. Discrete-Time Diffusion as a Hierarchical Latent Model¤

    Algebraic latent-variable/ELBO formulation for discrete-time diffusion. A DDPM reverse chain defines a hierarchical latent-variable model, and its variational bound can be decomposed using the fixed Gaussian forward process (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020). The common simplified denoising loss may drop or change ELBO weights, and continuous-time score models need their own formulation. “Diffusion = VAE” is therefore shorthand for a specific formulation, not family identity.

    8. AR ↔ Diffusion (text and images)¤

    Cross-domain transfer and shared framework—not equivalence. Discrete diffusion language models such as LLaDA (Nie et al., 2025) and the Mercury technical report (Inception, 2025) update several masked positions per iteration; VAR (Tian et al., 2024) applies next-scale autoregression to images. Selected results do not collapse their factorizations or establish a universal speed/quality ordering. Generator Matching gives an operator-level training framework covering several generators under assumptions; finite models and samplers remain distinct.

    The design principle is:

    Start from the query and semantics you need; choose a state space and representation; choose a probabilistic construction or path; choose the target and estimator; then choose a sampler, conditioning mechanism, and post-training procedure. A family name is shorthand for a recurring bundle of these choices, not a separate universe.

    Three recurring conditioning patterns¤

    These mechanisms recur across several families, but none is universal:

    1. Classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) — add an auxiliary conditional log-density gradient to a compatible continuous-state score or energy sampler. Discrete AR control needs a separately derived logit or sequence construction; it is not the same gradient update.
    2. Classifier-free guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) — combine conditional and unconditional predictions. Originally for diffusion, related extrapolation has been adapted to AR visual generation (Sanchez et al., 2024) and flow matching, with CFG-Zero* (Fan et al., 2025) as a flow-oriented variant. The shared pattern is important, but score/noise fields, velocities, and normalized token logits induce different distributions and require target-specific derivations.
    3. Parameter-efficient or side-network conditioning — ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), and adapter families freeze some base parameters and train smaller components. Full fine-tuning, prompting, retrieval, and training from scratch remain important alternatives.

    The cross-family control volume derives the target-law, Doob-transform, hard-constraint, candidate-selection, and retrieval semantics that this orientation table can only summarize.

    CFG: A Cross-Family Technique¤

    CFG started as a diffusion-specific trick but inspired a broader conditional/unconditional extrapolation pattern:

    \[ \hat o(x, c) = (1 + w)\, o_\theta(x, c) - w\, o_\theta(x, \emptyset), \]

    For diffusion noise or score predictions and for flow velocities, linear combination can be interpreted through the corresponding field after applying the correct parameterization conversion. For AR logits, linear extrapolation occurs before softmax and renormalization at every conditional factor; it is not automatically the same density transformation as score guidance. Results from Sanchez et al. (2024) and EditAR (CVPR 2025) show that related algebra is useful in visual AR systems, but usefulness does not prove a single cross-family target distribution.

    In an idealized score setting, the linear combination corresponds pointwise to the score of a tempered density proportional to \(p_t(x\mid c)^{1+w}/p_t(x)^w\) when the component scores are exact. The subtlety is that these time-indexed tilted marginals need not form the forward marginals of one consistent endpoint distribution under the sampler, and learned parameterizations/finite solvers add error. For AR logits, per-prefix renormalization defines an explicit changed autoregressive law. The open questions concern consistency, calibration, quality–diversity trade-offs, and finite-model behavior—not the blanket claim that no distribution can be written.

    The Platonic Representation Hypothesis¤

    The Platonic Representation Hypothesis is a 2024 proposal that representations from different models can become more aligned with scale, task breadth, and shared data structure (Huh et al., 2024). Alignment depends on layer, probe, dataset, metric, and invariances; it is an empirical hypothesis rather than a law that all families converge.

    The authors propose three possible drivers:

    1. Task generality — diverse tasks can reward more broadly useful features.
    2. Model capacity — capacity may permit richer shared structure, while also adding non-identifiability.
    3. Simplicity bias — optimization and architecture may prefer some reusable solutions; the relevant bias is not universal.

    Why This Matters for Generative Modelling¤

    If the hypothesis holds even approximately, the choice of family matters less than the field's history suggests:

    • A DiT and an AR Transformer may exhibit aligned features under a declared comparison, but architecture names alone predict neither metric nor layer alignment.
    • REPA (Yu et al., 2024 — ICLR 2025 Oral) aligns diffusion representations with a pretrained visual encoder and reports large training-efficiency gains under selected quality thresholds. The headline multiplier is protocol-dependent and does not prove a uniquely “right” representation.
    • VFM-tokenizer preprints (2025) explore frozen pretrained visual encoders plus learned decoders. Codec reconstruction, generative suitability, causality, and modality coverage remain empirical.
    • 2025 follow-ups extend the hypothesis to molecular ML (blopig 2026), astronomy (The Platonic Universe, 2025), and neural decoding (Brain–LM Alignment, NeurIPS 2025 UniReps) — evidence that representation alignment is not confined to language and vision.
    • A 2026 critique, Revisiting the Platonic Representation Hypothesis: An Aristotelian View, shows that some global similarity metrics are confounded by model scale. After calibration, the surviving signal is more local: models agree on neighbourhood structure more reliably than on a single universal global geometry.

    The defensible implication is conditional: pretrained representations are useful priors worth testing across generators, while transfer must be audited for task-relevant information, invariances, codec distortion, data leakage, and metric circularity.

    Scaling Laws Across Families¤

    Scaling laws are empirical fits over a specified model family, data distribution, loss, and compute range. Extrapolation across architectures or objectives is not automatic, and many generative families lack controlled scaling studies comparable to language-model work.

    Autoregressive: Chinchilla and Beyond¤

    Hoffmann et al., 2022 fitted compute-optimal parameter/data relations for their dense autoregressive language-model setup. The near-equal scaling exponents are a result of that fit and compute accounting, not a universal rule that every model-size doubling requires exactly twice the tokens.

    Deployment-aware refinements:

    • Beyond Chinchilla-Optimal (2024) — when you expect ≥1B inference requests, train longer than Chinchilla and smaller, paying compute upfront for inference savings.
    • Developer reports of unusually high token-to-parameter ratios are system-specific and require deduplication, token counting, repeated-data exposure, and downstream-quality details before they become scaling evidence.
    • Test-time compute scaling (Snell et al., 2024) adds candidates, tokens, search, or verification as variables. Whether it is more cost-effective than additional training depends on task distribution, amortized request volume, verifier quality, latency, and base model.

    Diffusion: EDM and EDM2¤

    EDM and EDM2 provide controlled diffusion design and scaling experiments. Post-hoc EMA reconstructs several smoothing choices from saved training information; EDM2 reports ImageNet 512² results under its protocol. Neither establishes one compute-optimal law for all diffusion parameterizations, data, or conditioning regimes.

    REPA reports reaching a selected FID threshold with substantially fewer training iterations for SiT-XL under its setup. “17.5×” is a thresholded protocol result, not a new universal compute-optimal curve; pretrained-encoder cost and data must be included in lifecycle comparisons.

    Sparse / Mixture-of-Experts¤

    MoE partially decouples total parameters from active per-token arithmetic but adds routing, imbalance, communication, memory, and kernel concerns. DeepSeek-V3 and Mixtral document particular sparse systems; they do not establish a fixed active-to-dense equivalence ratio or a default flagship architecture. See AR → MoE.

    Evaluation Is Multi-Objective¤

    No single metric captures density fit, fidelity, coverage, conditional correctness, calibration, memorization, latency, safety, and human utility. Metrics are estimators with explicit feature spaces and assumptions, not simply “broken” or “solved.”

    FID Doesn't Match Human Perception¤

    Stein et al., 2023 compare several automatic metrics with human realism judgments and expose ranking and representation sensitivity. Conclusions are bounded by the sampled models, prompts/data, human-study design, and metric implementations. FID remains informative about Gaussianized Inception-feature moments but is not a general perceptual oracle.

    Complementary Metrics and Evaluation Protocols¤

    • CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024) — kernel discrepancy in a chosen embedding space; its paper reports benefits under selected studies.
    • DINOv2-FID (Stein et al., 2023; REPA, 2024) — replace InceptionV3 with DINOv2 features.
    • Human or model-judge preference studies — useful for declared populations and questions, but sensitive to position, style, sampling, judge model, and preference-model bias.
    • Open benchmark catalogues — useful for discovering protocols, but each metric must still be traced to its definition, implementation, and validation evidence.

    The Same Crisis in Language¤

    For language models, perplexity measures average predictive log loss under a tokenizer/data protocol, not instruction following, factuality, coding, or preference. Specialized benchmarks and pairwise preference studies answer different questions and introduce contamination, judge, population, and sampling concerns. See AR evaluation.

    What This Implies for Research¤

    Reported “X beats Y” claims should be bounded by an estimand and protocol:

    1. VAR's ImageNet result is conditional on FID implementation, data, resolution, class conditioning, sample count, and baselines; another metric may or may not change the ranking and must be measured.
    2. Preference ratings complement rather than replace likelihood, task accuracy, calibration, and safety evaluation.
    3. BPD/NLL are proper density scores when likelihood and preprocessing are valid, but they are not the only honest scientific measures; calibration, posterior predictive checks, downstream utility, and simulation-based diagnostics may be essential.

    A major research direction is principled, model-class-agnostic evaluation: protocols that (a) correlate with declared human or task judgments, (b) expose feature-extractor and model-family bias, and © are statistically and computationally efficient.

    Mechanistic Interpretability of Generative Models¤

    Mechanistic interpretability asks which internal computations causally support model behavior. Evidence is strongest for selected Transformer circuits; diffusion, flow, GAN, and multimodal system analysis remains comparatively early.

    Key Findings¤

    • Recurring circuit motifs such as induction-like heads have been identified in selected Transformer models (Olsson et al., 2022). Same architecture does not force the same learned circuit, and observational probes require causal interventions.
    • Diffusion representation studies probe when layout and concepts become decodable during sampling. Decodability at an early step does not prove the final layout is irreversibly determined or validate a Platonic interpretation.
    • Intermediate reasoning representations can sometimes be decoded or causally manipulated, but a probe's success does not establish a faithful hidden scratchpad or equivalence to written chain-of-thought.
    • Open Problems in Mechanistic Interpretability is a 29-author review that organizes open questions around defining features, validating decomposition and circuit methods, scaling analyses, and testing whether interpretability approaches add value relative to black-box baselines in safety-relevant applications.

    Why It Matters for Generative Modelling¤

    Mechanistic methods may help test questions that aggregate metrics cannot answer alone:

    • Which learned discriminator and generator mechanisms predict coverage loss in a trained GAN? The hypothesis must distinguish support mismatch, critic memorization, optimization dynamics, and generator geometry.
    • Why does CFG work? Are guidance scales injecting attention or shifting energies?
    • Why does test-time compute help reasoning models? Is the chain-of-thought constructing circuits at inference time or invoking pre-trained ones?
    • What exactly is the Platonic representation and why does scale converge to it?

    Each question needs an operational hypothesis, intervention, counterfactual control, and falsifiable prediction; an appealing circuit story is not sufficient.

    Reasoning as Generation¤

    Reasoning systems often use generation to externalize candidate steps, but “reasoning” names a task-level capability while “generation” names a distributional process. They overlap without being synonyms.

    Snell et al., 2024, DeepSeek-R1, and vendor reports study additional tokens, candidates, search, or verification at inference. They do not show that no amount of train-time scaling can reach the same capabilities. Closed-system benchmark numbers retain vendor-claim status and must state pass count, selection, tools, and contamination controls.

    Three takeaways from the cross-family lens:

    1. Generation under verification is one reasoning-system pattern. Policy optimization with verifiable rewards is not generally equivalent to fitting a normalized EBM with energy equal to negative reward; the AR–EBM statement is a preprint correspondence with assumptions.
    2. Iterative computation is an implementation analogy. A denoising trajectory and a token reasoning trace both spend sequential compute, but their state, supervision, transition semantics, and correctness criteria differ.
    3. World prediction can support visual reasoning. Producing a plausible counterfactual video is not proof of causal intervention semantics, planning ability, or faithful chain-of-thought. See the next section.

    World Models: Predictive Generators for Decision-Making¤

    World modeling studies predictive models of environment dynamics that can be queried under histories and actions and then used for forecasting, control, planning, or scientific reasoning. A visually plausible generator is not automatically a decision-sufficient world model, and a predictive world model is not automatically causal.

    The Three-Level Taxonomy¤

    The Agentic World Modeling survey proposes:

    • L1 Predictor — one-step local transitions (a common pattern in diffusion video models)
    • L2 Simulator — multi-step action-conditioned rollouts (Sora, Genie, Cosmos)
    • L3 Evolver — autonomous model revision and self-improvement (mostly aspirational)

    Sequential Decoupling vs Joint Coupling¤

    Visual Generation in the New Era (2026) distinguishes two architectural philosophies:

    • Sequential Decoupling — an LMM "planner" emits plan tokens (text), and a video diffusion model "renders" them into pixels. Combines AR's instruction-following with diffusion's per-pixel fidelity.
    • Joint Coupling — causal reasoning and generative power are optimised jointly in a single network. PAN (Physical, Agentic, Nested, 2026) is a representative early example, mixing continuous and discrete representations under a hierarchical generative model.

    What This Adds to the Map¤

    World models illustrate how generative components can participate in prediction and planning, but they do not impose a six-family checklist. A system may use:

    • a fast predictive or generative transition model, which could be AR, latent state-space, diffusion, flow, or another construction;
    • action conditioning and an explicit distinction between observational prediction and causal intervention;
    • a value, reward, energy, or verifier for planning—none of which must be an EBM;
    • a learned or hand-designed representation/codec, not necessarily a VAE;
    • optional adversarial, preference, or reinforcement-learning post-training;
    • uncertainty-aware rollouts and state estimation. Invertibility alone does not provide causal counterfactuals.

    In other words, the historical families provide reusable design vocabularies, but a competent world model need not contain one component from each. Architecture proposals should be labeled as empirical systems or hypotheses, not consequences of a mathematical correspondence.

    Choosing a Family: Query-First Decision Guide¤

    The branches do not expose identical queries. An ELBO is not an exact marginal likelihood, and an invertible-flow density is tied to its declared preprocessing and reference measure. Likewise, “few evaluations” is an accounting claim, not a family identity: the comparison must include every network call, codec, guidance branch, rejection, and post-processing step.

    This is a screening order, not a claim that the query classes are mutually exclusive. If a system must answer several queries, retain every applicable constraint and compare compositions as well as individual families. The guide narrows candidates; it does not select a winner. Next declare the representation, conditioning semantics, data regime, training budget, latency/throughput target, memory, hardware, evidence maturity, and evaluation estimands. Compare complete systems under matched conditions, including codec, guidance, search, distillation, and post-processing costs.

    Composed Generative Systems¤

    Many deployed and research systems are compositions of representations, encoders, generative objectives, samplers, and post-training methods. The diagrams below are design patterns, not reverse-engineered descriptions of every named closed or open product.

    Five components of a generative decision system¤

    Generation becomes design when samples are proposed to satisfy a property, and it becomes model-based decision-making when imagined futures are used to choose actions. In both cases, the complete system has five conceptually separate components. A neural network may implement several components jointly, but joint implementation does not erase their different semantics.

    Table GM.22. Five components of a generative decision system
    Component Scientific or engineering design World-model planning Question that must be answered
    1. Reference generator and support a data distribution, feasible constructor, prior, library, or base generator \(g_0(x\mid c)\) current belief and the trajectory law induced by learned dynamics What can receive nonzero mass or be reached?
    2. Predictive model a property surrogate \(\hat u(x)\), likelihood \(\hat p(y\mid x)\), or higher-fidelity simulator transition, observation, reward, termination, and uncertainty models What outcomes does the model predict?
    3. Target construction conditioning event, acquisition function, utility, constraints, or nonnegative reward \(w(x,c)\) return, goal likelihood, risk measure, constraint, or terminal value Which predicted outcomes should be preferred, and by how much?
    4. Inference engine rejection sampling, guided generation, MCMC, Bayesian optimisation, evolutionary search, diffusion, or a GFlowNet CEM, MPC, MCTS, gradient planning, SMC, or an amortized actor How is the target queried under finite compute?
    5. Evidence and update loop synthesis, assay, simulation, human review, or deployment outcome followed by model revision environment observation, replanning, failure review, and dynamics/policy update Which predictions survive independent or prospective observation?

    The decomposition prevents several category errors. The base generator is not the property model; the property model is not the real property; the reward is not the sampler; the sampler is not the evidence; and successful optimisation of a learned model is not prospective validation. “End to end” describes gradient or software connectivity, not proof that every component is correct.

    A common target construction¤

    When the reference component is a normalized distribution and the preference is a nonnegative measurable function, a useful target is

    \[ p_{\mathrm{tar}}(x\mid c) =\frac{g_0(x\mid c)w(x,c)} {Z(c)}, \qquad Z(c)=\int g_0(x\mid c)w(x,c)\,d\mu(x). \]

    This one expression has several readings:

    • \(w(x,c)=p(y\mid x,c)\) gives a Bayesian posterior over designs after observing \(y\);
    • \(w(x,c)=\exp(\beta u(x,c))\) gives an exponentially tilted or control-as-inference target;
    • \(w(x,c)=\mathbf 1\{x\in\mathcal C(c)\}\) conditions the reference law on a hard feasible set; and
    • a GFlowNet can target the same terminal law by using \(R(x,c)=g_0(x\mid c)w(x,c)\), provided its state graph represents the intended semantic objects and support.

    These are target-level relationships. They do not make Bayesian conditioning, a GFlowNet, an MCMC kernel, a diffusion sampler, and an optimiser the same algorithm. The support boundary is immediate:

    \[ g_0(x\mid c)=0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad p_{\mathrm{tar}}(x\mid c)=0 \]

    for every finite \(w(x,c)\). A hard constructor can ensure validity while excluding an entire useful region; a broad generator can cover that region while wasting mass on invalid objects. Support therefore belongs in the system specification, not only in a sampler benchmark.

    Worked three-candidate tilt: predicted success is not observed success¤

    Suppose a base generator proposes three designs with probabilities \(g_0=(0.6,0.3,0.1)\). A learned property model predicts success probabilities \(\hat p(y=1\mid x)=(0.2,0.6,0.9)\). The last column is hidden during design and is revealed only by prospective evaluation.

    Table GM.23. Worked three-candidate tilt: predicted success is not observed success
    Design base probability \(g_0(x)\) predicted success \(\hat p(y=1\mid x)\) prospective success \(p_{\mathrm{real}}(y=1\mid x)\)
    \(A\) 0.6 0.2 0.2
    \(B\) 0.3 0.6 0.6
    \(C\) 0.1 0.9 0.1

    Using the predicted success as a likelihood factor gives

    \[ \hat Z=0.6(0.2)+0.3(0.6)+0.1(0.9)=0.39 \]

    and hence

    \[ \hat p_{\mathrm{tar}}(A,B,C\mid y=1) =\left(\frac{4}{13},\frac{6}{13},\frac{3}{13}\right). \]

    This derivation is exact for the learned property model. Under that target, predicted success is

    \[ \mathbb E_{\hat p_{\mathrm{tar}}}[\hat p(y=1\mid X)] =\frac{71}{130}\approx0.546. \]

    Prospective success is instead

    \[ \mathbb E_{\hat p_{\mathrm{tar}}}[p_{\mathrm{real}}(y=1\mid X)] =\frac{47}{130}\approx0.362. \]

    The tilt still improves over the base generator's real success rate \(0.31\), but much less than its internal score suggests. Greedy optimisation is worse: it always chooses \(C\), attaining predicted success \(0.9\) and real success \(0.1\). A perfect sampler for \(\hat p_{\mathrm{tar}}\) cannot repair the property model, and a perfect property model on the original data distribution need not remain accurate on designs selected to maximize it.

    After testing, component 5 adds outcomes to the evidence set, audits the selection-conditioned calibration gap, and updates the property model or target. If outcomes are never returned to the system, the workflow is open-loop optimisation, not a closed discovery loop.

    Retrieval practice: separate target, algorithm, and evidence

    1. What changes if reward is \(R(x)=\hat p(y=1\mid x)\) rather than \(g_0(x)\hat p(y=1\mid x)\)?

    2. Does exact reward-proportional sampling establish real-world utility?

    3. Why can ordinary held-out error underestimate the error seen after optimisation?

    4. Which component changes when CEM is replaced by a GFlowNet while the property model and target remain fixed?

    Solutions

    1. The prior/reference factor is removed, so the normalized target changes from a posterior tilt to a distribution proportional only to the predicted score.

    2. No. It establishes fidelity to the declared reward on covered support; reward validity is a separate empirical claim.

    3. Optimisation changes the query distribution and preferentially selects positive prediction errors or unsupported extrapolations.

    4. Component 4, the inference engine. Its approximation, amortization cost, and coverage change; the declared target does not.

    Open Theoretical Problems¤

    Eight problems where the cross-family view exposes deep gaps the field has not yet closed. These are not engineering wishlists; they are conceptual open questions where progress would shift the field.

    1. A principled choice of corruption process¤

    Different constructions use Gaussian or non-Gaussian noising, masking, CTMC jumps, reveal orders, learned couplings, or scale coarsening. Ordinary left-to-right autoregression is a normalized factorization, not literally an erasure process, although a reveal-order construction can represent it that way. A useful theory would specify the model class, state space, computational budget, and estimand under which one path or ordering is preferable.

    2. Finite-compute gaps between related diffusion and flow constructions¤

    Gaussian-path diffusion and flow constructions can share marginals or convertible population targets, yet learned fields, loss weighting, stochasticity, and finite solvers differ. Useful non-asymptotic analyses should bound an explicitly chosen endpoint error as a function of field-estimation error, parameterization, schedule, solver, network class, and dimension rather than starting from the slogan “diffusion = flow matching.”

    3. The partition function for high-dimensional images¤

    Reliable partition-function and likelihood estimation for high-dimensional neural EBMs remains difficult. AIS/RAISE-style estimates depend on intermediate paths, mixing, and computation, and optimization-defined energy generators may not target the normalized Boltzmann law at all. A useful research result would provide controlled bounds, diagnostics, and scaling behavior on modern data—not merely one point estimate.

    4. Why does CFG work?¤

    Idealized CFG fields admit tilted-density interpretations at each noise time, while cross-time consistency, finite-model error, parameterization, solver behavior, and the quality–diversity trade-off remain incompletely characterized. The research question is to connect those idealized fields to the endpoint distribution actually produced by a finite sampler.

    5. The relationship between scaling laws and emergence¤

    Train-time loss scaling and task-specific test-time-compute curves are usually studied separately. A theory predicting the jointly optimal allocation across training, retrieval, candidates, verification, latency, and request volume would be valuable; its importance depends on the deployment question rather than an absolute ranking of open problems.

    6. Sample efficiency on the data manifold¤

    Claims that flow matching needs orders of magnitude fewer training steps than diffusion confound path, weighting, architecture, representation, optimizer, and quality protocol unless controlled. MeanFlow reports one-NFE sampling results under its objective; explaining when average-velocity or transition targets reduce sampling cost without hiding training/teacher cost is a sharper question.

    7. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis: why does it converge?¤

    Huh et al., 2024 gives empirical evidence and arguments. A useful theory would predict alignment after accounting for metric invariances, layer choice, architecture, data overlap, scale confounding, and task. It should also predict non-alignment rather than assuming one Platonic endpoint.

    8. Joint Generation and Reasoning¤

    The connection between iterative state refinement and iterative reasoning is presently an implementation analogy unless a model defines the state, target distribution, transition kernel, verifier, and correctness semantics. A useful theory would make falsifiable predictions across those choices rather than explaining closed systems after the fact.

    Research Problems and Experimental Programs¤

    These research questions follow directly from the cross-family view. Their scope ranges from focused empirical studies to multi-year theoretical programs; tractability depends on the chosen assumptions, data, compute, and evaluation access.

    A. Under-Explored Hybrids¤

    • Planner–renderer interfaces for molecules and proteins. Compare an autoregressive sequence or fragment planner followed by an equivariant diffusion or flow renderer against matched end-to-end baselines. The study must define the intermediate representation, physical constraints, likelihood or reward semantics, compute budget, and whether modularity improves controllability or merely moves error between components.
    • Energy-defined targets for few-step distillation. Compare a time-independent scalar energy, a learned discriminator, and a teacher score as alternative training signals under matched generator architecture and teacher access. The central question is which target law each signal identifies and how approximation error changes coverage, stability, and total teacher compute.
    • Discrete transport objectives for controlled sequences. Discrete flow matching (Gat et al., 2024) defines a base generative training route. A separate research question is how preference or constraint information should modify its path, endpoint law, or post-training objective. This should not be presented as algebraically equivalent to RLHF or DPO without a derivation.

    B. Theoretical Gaps to Fill¤

    • Optimal corruption for a given manifold. Empirical or theoretical work tying intrinsic-dimension estimates of \(p_{\mathrm{data}}\) to optimal noise schedules.
    • Conditional impossibility and lower-bound results for generative trade-offs. State a model class, resource budget, data assumptions, and estimands, then ask which combinations of fidelity, coverage, and sampling cost can or cannot be achieved. The empirical “trilemma” alone is not a theorem to prove.
    • Scaling-to-emergence connector. A predictive theory of which capabilities emerge from train-time vs test-time compute.

    C. Evaluation¤

    • Family-aware metric suites with declared estimands. Study feature-extractor bias, human/task correlation, calibration, memorization, uncertainty, and sample efficiency rather than seeking one family-agnostic scalar. See Evaluation.
    • Likelihood-free density estimation for OOD detection that doesn't fall victim to the higher-likelihood-on-OOD pathology (Nalisnick et al., 2019). This is unexpectedly hard despite a decade of attention.

    D. World Models¤

    • Controlled self-improvement — study models that update from interaction while preserving evaluation integrity, safety constraints, identifiability, and resistance to self-generated-data feedback. Prediction and simulation are not solved.
    • Hybrid planner/renderer representation interfaces — compare discrete communication, shared continuous states, and modular interfaces under controlled task success, causal validity, and sample efficiency. Shared representations are not guaranteed to be better.

    E. Applications Where No Family Yet Dominates¤

    • Long-horizon interactive video and simulation with persistent identity, action-conditioned dynamics, recoverable state, and task-relevant physical consistency over horizons long enough to expose accumulated error.
    • Multi-agent generative simulations (multiple actors with private goals).
    • Generative scientific verification — proposing novel experiments that an automated lab can run, with credit-assignment back through the generative model.
    • Generative embodied models that take continuous actuator commands as part of the conditioning.
    • Privacy-preserving generative models at scale — DP-trained generative models trade off sharply with quality, leaving open whether any family can train on copyrighted or private data with rigorous guarantees.

    F. Mechanistic Interpretability of Diffusion and Flow Models¤

    Mechanistic work on diffusion and flow models is less mature than Transformer-language-model circuit analysis, and methods do not transfer automatically across architectures or time-dependent targets. Productive studies should define a causal unit of analysis, compare probes with interventions and simple baselines, and test whether findings predict behavior across noise times, prompts, samplers, and checkpoints. Candidate questions include how spatial relations emerge, how guidance changes internal computation, and which features remain stable under target-parameterization conversions.

    Common Confusions and Cross-Family Pitfalls¤

    Pedagogical traps that catch most practitioners when they first move between families. Each is a known mistake-pattern worth flagging up-front.

    "Likelihood is the right metric for sample quality"¤

    Reality: likelihood and perceptual quality can move in opposite directions. A flow model can have lower BPD than diffusion on a benchmark and still produce noticeably worse samples. Use the right metric for the right job — see The Evaluation Crisis.

    "Diffusion is fundamentally slow because of MCMC"¤

    Reality: standard reverse-diffusion sampling is not equilibrium MCMC and does not require detailed balance. A probability-flow ODE is deterministic; a reverse-time SDE or DDPM reverse chain is stochastic and Markovian in its augmented time-indexed state; DDIM defines a different, possibly deterministic, generative process. Predictor–corrector samplers may deliberately add MCMC-like corrector steps. Name the kernel and invariant/endpoint law instead of calling every iterative stochastic sampler “MCMC.”

    "GAN inputs are sampled from the same prior as VAE latents"¤

    Reality: both may start from \(z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\), but the variable has different learned semantics. A VAE jointly trains an approximate posterior \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\); a basic GAN specifies only the generative direction. ALI/BiGAN and inversion methods add inference with their own objectives. Neither family guarantees semantic or superior representations.

    "Flows have exact likelihood; that makes them better"¤

    Reality: classical flows have an exact change-of-variables density under declared preprocessing and support assumptions. Equal dimension is a constraint, not proof of wasted capacity. Exact likelihood is useful for density estimation and coding, but high likelihood alone is not a reliable semantic anomaly detector and does not establish sample quality.

    "Score matching = denoising"¤

    Reality: under a declared corruption kernel and regularity conditions, denoising score matching replaces an unknown marginal-score target with a tractable conditional-score target having the same population minimizer. Multi-noise training integrates such objectives across a noise distribution and usually conditions the network on time or noise level. This is a precise estimator correspondence, not the claim that every score objective is one fixed denoising problem.

    "Flow matching is a different thing from diffusion"¤

    Reality: Gaussian-path flow matching and diffusion probability-flow formulations can be written as closely related probability-path algorithms under compatible parameterizations. They can still differ in path/coupling, loss weighting, stochastic versus deterministic dynamics, target parameterization, and finite solver. A generic ODE solver is not “diffusion-style”; the learned dynamics and endpoint law determine the construction.

    "GAN's discriminator is just a classifier"¤

    Reality: the discriminator or critic is an auxiliary variational witness. With an optimal discriminator and the original minimax objective, substituting it back yields a Jensen–Shannon expression; f-GANs use variational bounds for f-divergences; WGAN critics optimize the Kantorovich dual under a Lipschitz constraint. A finite trained critic is neither the divergence itself nor merely an ordinary classifier.

    "AR models can't be trained in parallel"¤

    Reality: feed-forward evaluation across known training positions can be parallel under teacher forcing, although recurrent architectures still contain scan dependencies. Ordinary ancestral sampling is sequential in the chosen units. Multi-token prediction and speculative decoding can reduce target-model serial calls under workload-dependent acceptance and cost; they do not erase the factorization.

    "EBMs are obsolete"¤

    Reality: EBMs remain relevant for unnormalized modeling, composition, structured prediction, scores, and scientific constraints. Score–energy algebra and cited preprints clarify relationships, while partition estimation, mixing, calibration, and deployment remain open limitations. Relevance does not require declaring EBMs the center of the field.

    "FID is the standard metric for image generation"¤

    Reality: FID is historically common and measures first/second moments of Inception features under a Gaussian approximation. Its ranking can disagree with human or task judgments and depends on implementation and sample protocol. CMMD, alternative embeddings, precision/recall, memorization tests, conditional fidelity, and human studies expose different failure modes; none is a universal replacement.

    Research Themes That Organize the Field¤

    Five trends are reshaping the practical and theoretical landscape:

    1. Interpretive synthesis—test-time compute as a scaling axis: reasoning tokens, candidates, search, verifiers, denoising NFE, and solver accuracy expose task-dependent quality/cost trade-offs. They are not one ordered axis across families, and cost-effectiveness requires matched measurement. → See AR explainer.
    2. Interpretive synthesis—hybrid systems: models increasingly combine codecs, sparse or hybrid backbones, retrieval, verification, tools, distillation, and safety layers. This strengthens the need to report components; it does not define one 2026 default stack.
    3. Mathematical correspondences remain productive — Energy Matching, Equilibrium Matching, autoregressive–energy constructions, and Generator Matching (Holderrieth et al., 2024) show that historical family names can hide shared operators, fields, objectives, or dual representations. Treat “master objective” claims cautiously unless they preserve the queries, endpoint laws, conditioning semantics, and finite-compute behavior relevant to the comparison.
    4. Interpretive synthesis—multi-component systems: some deployments combine generation, retrieval, verification, memory, and policy layers; others remain smaller. Keep the base generative model distinct from the agentic or serving system. → See Composed Generative Systems.
    5. Open hypothesis—representation alignment: the Platonic Representation Hypothesis motivates calibrated cross-model comparisons. It does not yet prescribe an optimal research strategy or prove universal convergence.

    Curriculum Synthesis and Retrieval¤

    The cross-family view is not “all families are secretly the same.” It is a discipline for stating which object is shared and which is not. A score may be the gradient of an energy; a velocity may induce the same marginals as a probability-flow ODE; an ELBO may describe a diffusion chain; and a codec may compose with a diffusion prior. None of those statements alone equalizes architectures, finite objectives, samplers, likelihood access, conditioning semantics, or deployment cost.

    Worked family-selection example¤

    Suppose the task is conditional generation of small molecular graphs with exact hard valence constraints, calibrated probabilities for complete graphs, and diverse high-reward candidates.

    1. State space and symmetry: graphs require permutation-aware representation; arbitrary node order must be marginalized, randomized, or justified.
    2. Queries: calibrated graph likelihood and diverse reward-proportional sampling are two different targets.
    3. Candidates: a probabilistic circuit may offer exact queries only under restrictive structure; an AR graph-action model offers tractable trajectory likelihood but may require summing action orders for terminal likelihood; a GFlowNet directly targets diverse reward-proportional terminal objects but does not automatically provide data likelihood; graph diffusion offers order-flexible refinement but likelihood and constraint enforcement depend on its transition design.
    4. Hybrid option: use a constraint-preserving constructive state graph, train a GFlowNet for reward-proportional candidates, and fit a separately calibrated observational model if data likelihood is required. This is hybrid composition, not evidence that GFlowNets and AR likelihood models are equivalent.
    5. Evaluation: report validity, uniqueness, novelty, reward distribution, calibration for whichever probability is claimed, order sensitivity, compute, NFE/action count, and independent scientific verification.

    The correct result may be “no single family satisfies every query.” That is a design conclusion, not a failure of the taxonomy.

    Solutions. (1) Transformer names an architecture, not factorization, objective, density semantics, or sampler. (2) The spatial gradient field; the energy is defined up to a time-dependent additive constant, assuming integrability. (3) No. It is a codec plus latent generative prior; the relation is hybrid composition. (4) Discretization, solver, stochasticity, parameterization, learned error, and schedule can differ. (5) No; those are separate estimands requiring their own protocols. (6) Vendor claim.
