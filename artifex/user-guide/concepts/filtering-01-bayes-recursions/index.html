    1. Bayesian Recursions and Information Sets¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Conditional probability, Bayes' rule, Markov structure, and normalized message passing.

    Learning objectives. Derive the exact predict–correct recursion; distinguish filtering, smoothing, forecasting, and path sampling by their information sets; recover the sequence likelihood from predictive normalizers; and compute a finite filter and smoother without invoking an algorithm name as an accuracy guarantee.

    One law, several questions¤

    At time \(t\), the following distributions answer different questions:

    Table FILTER.1. One law, several questions
    Query Target Information available Typical output
    prediction \(p(z_t\mid y_{1:t-1})\) observations before \(t\) a prior belief for the next correction
    filtering \(p(z_t\mid y_{1:t})\) observations through \(t\) current-state posterior
    fixed-lag smoothing \(p(z_{t-L}\mid y_{1:t})\) a bounded amount of future evidence delayed state posterior
    fixed-interval smoothing \(p(z_t\mid y_{1:T})\) the complete recorded interval retrospective state posterior
    forecasting \(p(y_{t+h}\mid y_{1:t})\) past observations and declared future inputs future-observation law
    path sampling \(p(z_{0:T}\mid y_{1:T})\) complete observations correlated posterior trajectory

    An encoder that reads \(y_{1:T}\) is a smoother even if its output variable is called a filtered state. Conversely, a filter used online cannot use later observations without leakage. Fixed-lag smoothing is a third information contract rather than an informal compromise between the two.

    Deriving the exact Bayesian filter¤

    Suppose the previous filtering law is \(b_{t-1}(z_{t-1})=p(z_{t-1}\mid y_{1:t-1})\). Markov structure gives the one-step predictive law

    \[ b_{t\mid t-1}(z_t) =\int f_t(z_t\mid z_{t-1})b_{t-1}(z_{t-1})\,dz_{t-1}. \]

    The new observation contributes a likelihood factor. Its predictive density is

    \[ \ell_t =p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1}) =\int g_t(y_t\mid z_t)b_{t\mid t-1}(z_t)\,dz_t, \]

    and Bayes' rule gives the normalized correction

    \[ b_t(z_t) =p(z_t\mid y_{1:t}) =\frac{g_t(y_t\mid z_t)b_{t\mid t-1}(z_t)}{\ell_t}. \]

    These are exact probability identities. They are not yet a finite algorithm: the integrals may be unavailable, the returned density may require an unbounded representation, and finite arithmetic may lose normalization or positive semidefiniteness.

    The normalizer is also the incremental evidence. Therefore

    \[ \log p(y_{1:T})=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log \ell_t. \]

    Replacing \(\ell_t\) by an unnormalized score breaks both the posterior update and the likelihood calculation. When an approximation supplies \(\widehat\ell_t\), the sum is an approximate log likelihood unless a separate unbiased-estimation argument applies.

    Deriving the backward smoothing recursion¤

    The Markov graph implies

    \[ p(z_t\mid z_{t+1},y_{1:T}) =p(z_t\mid z_{t+1},y_{1:t}). \]

    Bayes' rule expresses this backward kernel in terms of filtered quantities:

    \[ p(z_t\mid z_{t+1},y_{1:t}) =\frac{f_{t+1}(z_{t+1}\mid z_t)b_t(z_t)} {b_{t+1\mid t}(z_{t+1})}, \]

    on the support where the denominator is positive. Marginalizing the next smoothed state yields

    \[ p(z_t\mid y_{1:T}) =b_t(z_t) \int \frac{f_{t+1}(z_{t+1}\mid z_t) p(z_{t+1}\mid y_{1:T})} {b_{t+1\mid t}(z_{t+1})} \,dz_{t+1}. \]

    This identity explains why smoothing needs stored or recomputed forward information. It also exposes a support requirement that an arbitrary learned backward network need not satisfy.

    Worked finite example: future evidence changes an earlier belief¤

    Let \(z_t\in\{0,1\}\). Initially \(p(z_1=0)=3/5\) and \(p(z_1=1)=2/5\). The state stays unchanged with probability \(4/5\). Observations are binary, with

    \[ p(y_t=1\mid z_t=0)=\frac{1}{10}, \qquad p(y_t=1\mid z_t=1)=\frac{7}{10}. \]

    After observing \(y_1=1\), the two unnormalized masses are \(3/50\) and \(14/50\). Thus

    \[ p(z_1\mid y_1=1) =\left(\frac3{17},\frac{14}{17}\right). \]

    Prediction through the transition gives

    \[ p(z_2\mid y_1=1)=\left(\frac{26}{85},\frac{59}{85}\right). \]

    Now observe \(y_2=0\). The likelihoods are \(9/10\) and \(3/10\), so the filtering law at time two is

    \[ p(z_2\mid y_1=1,y_2=0) =\left(\frac{78}{137},\frac{59}{137}\right). \]

    For smoothing time one, the backward likelihood of \(y_2=0\) is \(39/50\) when \(z_1=0\) and \(21/50\) when \(z_1=1\). Multiplying these values by the time-one filter and renormalizing gives

    \[ p(z_1\mid y_1=1,y_2=0) =\left(\frac{39}{137},\frac{98}{137}\right). \]

    The filtered probability of state one at time one was \(14/17\approx0.824\); after seeing a future zero, its smoothed probability is \(98/137\approx0.715\). Neither result supersedes the other: they condition on different information.

    A point state is not a belief

    Replacing the time-one distribution by its most likely state discards the alternative trajectory before the second observation arrives. Later evidence cannot recover a hypothesis that an approximate representation assigned exactly zero mass.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Filtering is sequential Bayes conditioning, smoothing is posterior inference with later evidence, forecasting propagates without later evidence, and path sampling preserves posterior dependence across time. The predictive normalizer connects online correction to sequence likelihood. Exact identities become algorithms only after a representation and numerical procedure are specified.

    Chapter 1 retrieval practice¤

    1. Why is \(\ell_t\) more than a disposable normalizer?
    2. Which conditional-independence statement permits the backward kernel to ignore \(y_{t+1:T}\) once \(z_{t+1}\) is known?
    3. Does a smaller smoothed variance imply that the online filter was wrong?
    4. What support failure can make the displayed smoothing ratio undefined?
    Complete solutions
    1. It equals \(p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1})\) and its log contributes exactly to the sequence log likelihood.
    2. \(z_t\perp y_{t+1:T}\mid(z_{t+1},y_{1:t})\) under the declared first-order state-space graph.
    3. No. The smoother is allowed to use later observations that were unavailable online.
    4. A next-state value can have positive smoothed mass while the forward predictive law assigns it zero; valid exact models avoid this inconsistency, while approximate supports must be checked.

    The next chapter identifies the principal finite-dimensional closure in which all these integrals reduce to linear algebra.
