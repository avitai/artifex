    Bayesian Learning and Model Assessment¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Classical Generative Models and Graphical Structure

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Inference 1 — Objects, Queries, and Local Approximations
    • Latent Variables and Inference

    A generative model usually contains quantities that are not observed directly: component probabilities, decoder weights, population effects, noise scales, or entire latent structures. Bayesian learning treats uncertainty about such quantities as part of the generative specification. It then asks what the data imply about them, what the fitted model predicts next, and where the model fails.

    This chapter develops those questions as one workflow. It begins with a coin model that can be calculated exactly, expands the same logic to exponential families and hierarchical populations, and then studies evidence, predictive comparison, model averaging, criticism, and incomplete data. The point is not that every modern generator must use a Bayesian neural posterior. The point is that parameter uncertainty, predictive uncertainty, model comparison, and model criticism are different operations, whatever approximation or neural architecture implements them.

    Reading routes

    Beginner: read Sections 1–3, the Beta–Bernoulli example, Section 7, and the retrieval questions.
    Practitioner: also study hierarchical shrinkage, predictive comparison, prior/posterior predictive checks, and missingness.
    Research: read continuously; verify every model-comparison method against its target, asymptotic assumptions, and finite-sample diagnostic.

    Begin with One Model and Five Questions¤

    Suppose a binary observation \(X_i\) has an unknown success probability \(\theta\). A complete Bayesian model places a prior on \(\theta\) and a sampling law on the data:

    \[ \theta\sim p(\theta), \qquad X_i\mid\theta\overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}p(x_i\mid\theta). \]

    One joint law now supports five distinct queries.

    • Learn

      Compute \(p(\theta\mid x_{1:n})\).

    • Predict

      Average \(p(x_{n+1}\mid\theta)\) over posterior uncertainty.

    • Compare

      Evaluate evidence or out-of-sample predictive performance.

    • Criticize

      Ask whether replicated data reproduce relevant observed structure.

    • Complete

      Infer missing values under an explicit observation mechanism.

    The arrows in a generative graph describe the model, not the direction of an inference algorithm. The outer rectangle below is plate notation: the \(X_i\) node and its conditional draw are repeated \(n\) times, while the shared parameter \(\theta\) is drawn once.

    Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be able to manipulate a joint distribution, apply Bayes' rule, integrate or sum out a variable, and interpret a log score. Review Probability and Generative Modeling and Classical Generative Models if those operations are unfamiliar.

    After this chapter you should be able to:

    1. distinguish a parameter, hyperparameter, latent state, estimator, and prediction;
    2. derive a posterior, marginal likelihood, and posterior predictive law from one joint model;
    3. explain why posterior summaries depend on a loss and why flat priors and MAP estimates depend on coordinates;
    4. update conjugate exponential-family hyperparameters without confusing a mean parameter with the expectation of a natural parameter;
    5. distinguish infinite exchangeability from a finite symmetry assumption;
    6. construct a hierarchical model and separate prediction for an existing group from prediction for a new group;
    7. state what empirical Bayes omits and when centered or noncentered parameterizations change computation without changing the model;
    8. compare evidence, Bayes factors, Bayesian model averaging, leave-one-out prediction, WAIC, PSIS, and predictive stacking by the quantity each targets;
    9. design prior and posterior predictive checks without treating either as a proof of model correctness; and
    10. write the observed-data likelihood under MCAR, MAR, or MNAR assumptions and state the conditions required for ignorability.

    1. The Bayesian Generative Object¤

    Let \(D=x_{1:n}\) denote observed data and let \(\theta\) collect unknown model quantities. A Bayesian model begins with the joint law

    \[ p(\theta,D)=p(\theta)p(D\mid\theta). \]

    Bayes' rule produces the posterior

    \[ p(\theta\mid D) =\frac{p(D\mid\theta)p(\theta)}{p(D)}, \qquad p(D)=\int p(D\mid\theta)p(\theta)\,d\theta. \]

    The denominator \(p(D)\) is the marginal likelihood or evidence. It is a probability mass or density for the observed dataset under the complete model, not a posterior probability of the parameter. It normalizes the posterior and also enters model comparison. Its value depends on the data representation, reference measure, sampling design, and prior.

    For a new observation \(\widetilde X\), the posterior predictive law is

    \[ p(\widetilde x\mid D) =\int p(\widetilde x\mid\theta,D) p(\theta\mid D)\,d\theta. \]

    When observations are conditionally IID given \(\theta\), \(p(\widetilde x\mid\theta,D)=p(\widetilde x\mid\theta)\). Time-series, spatial, causal, and adaptive-data models generally retain additional dependence on the observed history or design.

    Parameters and latent variables use the same probability calculus¤

    A distinction between a parameter and a latent variable is often semantic or computational rather than measure-theoretic. A global parameter may affect all records; a local latent state may affect one record; a hyperparameter may govern a population of parameters. Once assigned a distribution, all can be marginalized, conditioned, or predicted. What must remain explicit is their scope and the query being answered.

    A posterior is not yet a decision¤

    An action \(a\) should minimize posterior expected loss,

    \[ a^*(D)=\arg\min_a \int L(a,\theta)p(\theta\mid D)\,d\theta. \]

    For a scalar \(\theta\), squared-error loss selects the posterior mean; absolute-error loss selects a posterior median; a small-neighborhood zero–one loss approaches a posterior mode under regularity. Consequently, "the Bayesian estimate" is incomplete language until the loss, action space, and posterior summary are named. Prediction under log loss selects the full posterior predictive distribution, not a plug-in point estimate.

    Random in the model is not physically random

    A Bayesian distribution over a fixed but unknown quantity records the model's uncertainty. It does not assert that the physical constant is repeatedly redrawn. Conversely, frequentist procedures can be applied to randomized or hierarchical data-generating processes. The useful distinction is the target and calibration claim, not the slogan that one school makes data random and the other makes parameters random.

    2. Worked Example: Beta–Bernoulli Learning¤

    Let \(\alpha,\beta>0\) and

    \[ \theta\sim\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha,\beta). \]

    Conditional on that shared draw,

    \[ X_i\mid\theta\overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim} \operatorname{Bernoulli}(\theta). \]

    For an ordered binary sequence \(x_{1:n}\) containing \(s=\sum_i x_i\) successes and \(f=n-s\) failures,

    \[ p(x_{1:n}\mid\theta)=\theta^s(1-\theta)^f. \]

    Multiplying by the prior gives

    \[ p(\theta\mid x_{1:n}) \propto \theta^{\alpha+s-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta+f-1}, \]

    so

    \[ \theta\mid x_{1:n} \sim\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha+s,\beta+f). \]

    This is conjugacy: posterior and prior belong to the same named family. The data update two sufficient statistics, but conjugacy does not make every larger model or hyperparameter integral analytic.

    A complete numerical update¤

    Take \(\alpha=2\), \(\beta=2\), and observe the ordered sequence

    \[ (1,0,1,1,0), \]

    so \(s=3\) and \(f=2\). Then

    \[ \theta\mid D\sim\operatorname{Beta}(5,4). \]

    The posterior mean and interior mode are

    \[ \mathbb E[\theta\mid D]=\frac{5}{9}. \]

    The attained interior mode is

    \[ \operatorname{mode}(\theta\mid D) =\frac{5-1}{5+4-2} =\frac47. \]

    They differ because they solve different summary problems. The predictive success probability is the posterior mean of the Bernoulli probability:

    \[ P(X_6=1\mid D)=\mathbb E[\theta\mid D]=\frac59. \]

    Ordered sequence evidence is not count evidence¤

    The marginal probability of the particular ordered sequence is

    \[ p(x_{1:n}) =\frac{B(\alpha+s,\beta+f)}{B(\alpha,\beta)}, \]

    where \(B\) is the beta function. The probability of observing only the count \(S=s\), without the ordering, is instead

    For any one such sequence \(x_{1:n}^{(s)}\), the count probability is the sum over its \(\binom ns\) orderings:

    \[ P(S=s)=\binom ns\,p(x_{1:n}^{(s)}). \]

    Substituting the sequence evidence above gives the usual beta–binomial mass.

    The combinatorial factor does not change the posterior for \(\theta\) once \(s\) and \(n\) are known, because it is constant in \(\theta\). It does change the probability of the recorded observation and therefore the evidence. Dataset semantics cannot be discarded merely because they are irrelevant to one parameter update.

    MAP has boundary conditions¤

    For a \(\operatorname{Beta}(a,b)\) posterior, the familiar formula

    \[ \frac{a-1}{a+b-2} \]

    is an attained interior mode only when \(a>1\) and \(b>1\). If \(a<1\) the density diverges toward zero; if \(b<1\) it diverges toward one; if both are below one, both boundaries are modes in the extended-density sense. Equality cases can produce a boundary mode or a flat density. Quoting the interior formula without these conditions can return a negative number, a number above one, or division by zero.

    Pseudo-count language needs a convention¤

    The posterior update adds successes and failures to \((\alpha,\beta)\). That makes \((\alpha,\beta)\) convenient concentration parameters. The prior kernel, however, uses exponents \((\alpha-1,\beta-1)\), and the interior MAP uses those exponent offsets. Thus "the prior has \(\alpha\) successes and \(\beta\) failures" is only a mnemonic, not a convention-free identity.

    Credible sets, confidence procedures, and bootstrap distributions¤

    A \((1-\gamma)\) posterior credible set \(C(D)\) satisfies

    \[ P(\theta\in C(D)\mid D)=1-\gamma \]

    under the fitted model and prior. Equal-tailed, central, shortest, and highest-posterior-density constructions can produce different sets. A credible set need not be one interval: a multimodal posterior can justify disconnected regions. Highest-density sets and MAP points also change under nonlinear reparameterization because density height changes with the Jacobian.

    A frequentist confidence procedure has a different calibration target:

    \[ P_{\theta_0}\!\left(\theta_0\in C(X_{1:n})\right) \ge 1-\gamma \]

    over repeated datasets from each declared \(\theta_0\), at least approximately or asymptotically. This statement does not assign a posterior probability to the fixed realized interval. Bayesian credible coverage and frequentist repeated-sampling coverage can coincide in special models and diverge in others; neither definition should be substituted for the other.

    The nonparametric bootstrap resamples observed units to approximate the sampling distribution of an estimator. Its ordinary IID form assumes that the resampled unit is exchangeable and representative. Sequences, clusters, patients with repeated measurements, spatial fields, or adaptively collected records require a block, cluster, parametric, or design-aware alternative. A bootstrap distribution is not a posterior unless an additional argument establishes a particular correspondence.

    Regular asymptotics are conditional tools¤

    Under differentiability, identifiability, an interior true or pseudo-true parameter, suitable moment and support conditions, and nonsingular information, an MLE can be asymptotically normal. Under correct specification, inverse Fisher information supplies its leading covariance. Under misspecification, the score-variance and expected-Hessian terms need not coincide and a sandwich covariance is required.

    Mixtures, latent neural models, boundary parameters, nonidentified symmetries, changing support, and overparameterized systems frequently violate these regular assumptions. A singular Fisher matrix may reveal a local redundant direction, but a nonsingular local matrix is not a global identifiability proof. Bootstrap and asymptotic intervals should therefore be validated against the actual sampling unit and model geometry rather than attached automatically to every fitted generator.

    Aleatoric and parameter uncertainty depend on the query¤

    For a scalar predictive quantity \(\widetilde Y\), define

    \[ V_{\mathrm{cond}} =\mathbb E_{p(\theta\mid D)} [\operatorname{Var}(\widetilde Y\mid\theta,D)] \]

    and

    \[ V_{\mathrm{param}} =\operatorname{Var}_{p(\theta\mid D)} [\mathbb E(\widetilde Y\mid\theta,D)]. \]

    The law of total variance is then

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(\widetilde Y\mid D) =V_{\mathrm{cond}}+V_{\mathrm{param}}. \]

    The first term is often called conditional or aleatoric uncertainty and the second parameter or epistemic uncertainty. This decomposition is model- and query-dependent, not an intrinsic orthogonal labeling of every error. Latent-state ambiguity, model misspecification, distribution shift, and numerical approximation can enter elsewhere or be absorbed into either term by changing what is included in \(\theta\).

    Bayesian object retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: one Bayesian generative object
    1. In the Beta–Bernoulli example, which expressions are the joint law, evidence, posterior, posterior predictive, and prior predictive?
    2. Why is likelihood not a probability distribution over parameters without further construction?
    3. Which query uses a new observation from an already observed group, and which query concerns a genuinely new group?
    4. What changes when the observation mechanism is included as part of the generative model?

    Solutions. (1) The joint multiplies prior and sampling law; evidence marginalizes the parameter; posterior conditions the joint on data; posterior predictive averages the next-observation law over the posterior; prior predictive averages before seeing data. (2) It is the observed-data density viewed as a function of parameters and need not normalize over them. (3) Posterior predictive for the existing group versus a hierarchical new-group predictive law. (4) Conditioning and missing-data inferences must include selection/missingness rather than treating recorded values as an unbiased sample.

    Bayesian object synthesis¤

    A Bayesian generative analysis begins with one joint law and names the query before selecting an estimator. Prior, likelihood, evidence, posterior, and predictive distributions are related operations on that law, not interchangeable scores. The Beta–Bernoulli example supplies the finite template reused by later hierarchical, comparison, and missing-data chapters.

    3. Priors, Conjugacy, and Exchangeability¤

    Priors and hierarchy learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes the Bayesian object and predictive queries from the first chapter. By the end, you should be able to distinguish proper, improper, weakly informative, and reference priors; derive conjugate exponential-family updates; state what exchangeability does and does not imply; compute partial pooling in the normal–normal model; and distinguish empirical-Bayes fitting, centered/noncentered parameterization, and new-group prediction.

    Priors are part of the sampling model¤

    A prior does more than regularize an optimizer. It defines probability mass over parameter regions, contributes to posterior uncertainty, determines the prior predictive law, and affects evidence. A useful prior audit asks:

    • Is it proper, or is its normalizing constant intentionally unspecified?
    • Does it place mass on scientifically plausible scales and supports?
    • What observations does the induced prior predictive distribution generate?
    • Is it invariant under a stated transformation, or does it change with coordinates?
    • Does it create pathological posterior geometry or conflict with the likelihood?

    A density that is constant in \(\theta\) is generally not constant after a nonlinear reparameterization \(\phi=g(\theta)\), because

    \[ p_\Phi(\phi) =p_\Theta(g^{-1}(\phi)) \left|\frac{d g^{-1}(\phi)}{d\phi}\right|. \]

    For the same reason, MAP is not invariant under nonlinear changes of coordinates: a mode is a property of a density relative to a coordinate and reference measure, not of the underlying probability measure alone.

    Proper, improper, weakly informative, and reference priors¤

    A proper prior integrates to one. An improper prior is a nonnegative kernel with infinite or unspecified total mass. Some improper priors yield a proper posterior for a particular likelihood and dataset, but their arbitrary multiplicative constants usually make ordinary marginal likelihoods and Bayes factors undefined. Posterior propriety must be established rather than assumed.

    "Noninformative" has no coordinate-free universal meaning. Jeffreys' rule,

    \[ p_J(\theta)\propto\sqrt{I(\theta)}, \]

    uses the scalar Fisher information and is invariant under smooth one-to-one reparameterization in the one-parameter setting. In multiparameter problems, joint Jeffreys, independent Jeffreys, and reference-prior constructions can differ. Weakly informative priors instead encode scale and regularity knowledge deliberately; their adequacy should be checked through implied data and sensitivity analyses.

    Multivariate priors should expose scale and dependence¤

    A covariance matrix combines marginal scales and correlations. Writing

    \[ \Sigma=D R D, \qquad D=\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1,\ldots,\sigma_d), \]

    separates positive scales \(\sigma_j\) from a correlation matrix \(R\). This makes it possible to assign domain-calibrated priors to the scales and a distinct dependence prior to \(R\). An inverse-Wishart prior can be conjugate in a multivariate Gaussian model, but its degrees-of-freedom and scale conventions vary across sources, and it couples variance and correlation behavior in ways that may be inappropriate for the application.

    One correlation-matrix family is LKJ:

    \[ p(R\mid\eta)\propto |R|^{\eta-1}, \qquad \eta>0. \]

    At \(\eta=1\) the density is uniform over valid correlation matrices relative to the family’s matrix measure; this does not make the pairwise correlations independent uniforms. Values above one favor matrices nearer the identity, while values below one favor more extreme dependence. If a sampler works with a Cholesky factor of \(R\), the transformed factor density includes the appropriate Jacobian and diagonal powers. It is not obtained by replacing \(R\) with \(L\) in the matrix-density formula.

    For a high-dimensional generator, an unrestricted covariance has \(d(d+1)/2\) free entries and may be both statistically and computationally unreasonable. Diagonal, low-rank-plus-diagonal, sparse-precision, Kronecker, equivariant, or other structured models encode different dependence assumptions; choosing one is part of the generative model, not merely a faster linear-algebra implementation.

    The conjugate exponential-family calculation¤

    Write a regular exponential-family likelihood as

    \[ p(x\mid\eta) =h(x)\exp\!\left\{\eta^\top T(x)-A(\eta)\right\}. \]

    A natural conjugate prior has kernel

    \[ p(\eta\mid\tau,\nu) \propto \exp\!\left\{\eta^\top\tau-\nu A(\eta)\right\}. \]

    For \(n\) conditionally IID observations, multiplication gives the posterior hyperparameters

    \[ \tau' = \tau+\sum_{i=1}^n T(x_i), \qquad \nu'=\nu+n. \]

    The ratio \(\tau/\nu\) belongs to the mean-parameter geometry. Under the properness and boundary conditions used in the Diaconis–Ylvisaker result,

    \[ \mathbb E[\nabla A(\eta)]=\frac{\tau}{\nu}. \]

    It is not generally true that \(\mathbb E[\eta]=\tau/\nu\). Natural parameter \(\eta\) and mean parameter \(\mu=\nabla A(\eta)\) are nonlinear transforms in most families. Confusing them produces incorrect prior means and shrinkage interpretations.

    Bernoulli coordinates expose the distinction

    For Bernoulli data, the natural parameter is the logit \(\eta=\log(\theta/(1-\theta))\), while the mean parameter is \(\mu=\theta\). A Beta prior can have \(\mathbb E[\theta]=\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)\), but \(\mathbb E[\eta]=\psi(\alpha)-\psi(\beta)\), where \(\psi\) is the digamma function. These are not the same coordinate or the same expectation.

    Conjugate analysis is especially useful as an exact reference case, a message passing primitive, and a component of a larger approximate method. It does not imply that a deep likelihood, a coupled graphical model, an unknown hyperparameter, or a requested normalizer remains closed form.

    Exchangeability and mixture representations¤

    A finite sequence \(X_{1:n}\) is exchangeable when its joint law is invariant under every permutation of the indices. Conditional IID sampling from a shared random parameter implies exchangeability after the parameter is integrated out:

    \[ p(x_{1:n}) =\int\prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i\mid\theta)p(d\theta). \]

    The converse requires care. De Finetti-type representations concern an infinitely exchangeable sequence—or a finite law that is extendible to arbitrary length—on an appropriate measurable space. A merely finite exchangeable law need not equal a mixture of IID laws at that fixed length. Finite representation theorems instead give approximation or signed-mixture statements under their own conditions.

    Exchangeability is a symmetry assumption, not a causal conclusion. The mixing measure represents a probability law over component distributions; it does not identify a unique physical mechanism merely from the observed joint law.

    4. Hierarchical Models and Partial Pooling¤

    Suppose observations belong to groups. Estimating every group independently can be noisy, while forcing all groups to share one parameter can erase real variation. A hierarchical model expresses exchangeability at the group level:

    \[ \phi\sim p(\phi), \qquad \theta_j\mid\phi\sim p(\theta_j\mid\phi), \qquad x_{ij}\mid\theta_j\sim p(x_{ij}\mid\theta_j). \]

    Information can now move in two directions. Records update their group state; all group states update the population; the population then regularizes each group. This is partial pooling. It is a consequence of the declared model, not a universal guarantee of improved prediction: nonexchangeable groups, misspecified tails, or an inappropriate population distribution can induce harmful shrinkage.

    Worked normal–normal shrinkage¤

    Let a group mean have prior and observation model

    \[ \theta_j\mid\mu,\tau^2\sim\mathcal N(\mu,\tau^2), \qquad \bar X_j\mid\theta_j\sim \mathcal N\!\left(\theta_j,\frac{\sigma^2}{n_j}\right), \]

    where \(\mu,\tau,sigma\) are temporarily known. The posterior mean is

    \[ \mathbb E[\theta_j\mid\bar x_j] =w_j\bar x_j+(1-w_j)\mu, \qquad w_j=\frac{n_j\tau^2}{n_j\tau^2+\sigma^2}. \]

    If \(\mu=0\), \(\tau=1\), \(\sigma=2\), and \(n_j=4\), then \(w_j=1/2\). A group sample mean of \(3\) is shrunk to \(1.5\). If \(n_j=100\), the weight becomes \(25/26\), so the data dominate. The formula makes the borrowing mechanism visible: it is precision weighting, not an arbitrary penalty.

    Existing-group and new-group prediction differ¤

    For another record in an observed group \(j\), prediction integrates the posterior of its learned state:

    \[ p(\widetilde x_j\mid D) =\iint p(\widetilde x_j\mid\theta_j,\phi) p(\theta_j,\phi\mid D)\,d\theta_j\,d\phi. \]

    For a new exchangeable group, no group-specific posterior exists. One must draw a fresh state from the population:

    \[ p(\widetilde x_{\mathrm{new}}\mid D) =\iint p(\widetilde x_{\mathrm{new}}\mid\widetilde\theta,\phi) p(\widetilde\theta\mid\phi) p(\phi\mid D)\,d\widetilde\theta\,d\phi. \]

    Confusing these targets usually understates uncertainty for a new group.

    Centered and noncentered parameterizations¤

    The centered form directly samples \(\theta_j\sim\mathcal N(\mu,\tau^2)\). An equivalent noncentered form samples

    \[ z_j\sim\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad \theta_j=\mu+\tau z_j. \]

    These are algebraically equivalent model parameterizations when transformed correctly. They can have very different posterior geometry for optimization or MCMC. Noncentering often helps when group data are weak relative to the population prior; centering can be more efficient when each group is strongly informed. Neither dominates universally, and partial or adaptive parameterizations may interpolate between them.

    The sampling foundation's funnel example shows how this algebraic equivalence becomes an HMC step-size problem; this chapter owns the hierarchical modeling and data-strength interpretation.

    Empirical Bayes¤

    Empirical Bayes estimates hyperparameters, often by maximizing the marginal likelihood,

    \[ \widehat\phi =\arg\max_\phi p(D\mid\phi), \qquad p(D\mid\phi)=\int p(D\mid\theta)p(\theta\mid\phi)\,d\theta, \]

    and then conditions on \(\widehat\phi\). This can be scalable and effective, but the plug-in posterior \(p(\theta\mid D,\widehat\phi)\) does not propagate uncertainty about \(\phi\). The omission matters most when few groups determine the hyperparameters, the marginal likelihood is flat or multimodal, or a downstream decision is sensitive to population uncertainty.

    Worked categorical smoothing¤

    For context \(j\), let transition probabilities have prior

    \[ \boldsymbol\theta_j \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(\alpha_0\mathbf m), \qquad \sum_k m_k=1. \]

    If \(N_{jk}\) is the observed count for outcome \(k\) and \(N_j=\sum_k N_{jk}\), then

    \[ P(X_{\mathrm{next}}=k\mid j,D) =\frac{N_{jk}+\alpha_0m_k}{N_j+\alpha_0} =\frac{N_j}{N_j+\alpha_0}\frac{N_{jk}}{N_j} +\frac{\alpha_0}{N_j+\alpha_0}m_k. \]

    The total concentration is \(\alpha_0\), not a context-specific symbol silently substituted into the shrinkage weight. Estimating \(\alpha_0\) or \(\mathbf m\) from all contexts is an empirical-Bayes step; assigning them a posterior is a fully hierarchical step.

    Priors and hierarchy retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: priors and partial pooling
    1. When can an improper prior yield an unusable posterior?
    2. What representation does infinite exchangeability motivate, and what causal claim does it not supply?
    3. Why does partial pooling differ from both complete pooling and independent per-group fitting?
    4. When does a noncentered parameterization often improve computation?

    Solutions. (1) When the likelihood does not provide enough information to make the posterior normalizable. (2) A mixture/latent-variable representation such as de Finetti's for the declared sequence; it does not establish causal independence or a physical latent mechanism. (3) It shares information through a population distribution while retaining group-specific uncertainty. (4) In weak-data or funnel-like regimes where latent effects are tightly coupled to a small group scale; the centered form can be better in strong-data regimes.

    Priors and hierarchy synthesis¤

    Priors are part of the generative and predictive contract. Conjugacy exposes updates analytically, exchangeability motivates shared latent structure, and hierarchies implement adaptive partial pooling. Parameterization changes computation rather than the underlying model, while empirical Bayes changes how hyperparameter uncertainty is represented.

    5. Evidence, Prediction, and Model Comparison¤

    Assessment and missing-data learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes posterior and predictive distributions. By the end, you should be able to compare evidence, Bayes factors, LOO, WAIC, BIC, model averaging, and stacking by estimand and assumptions; design prior and posterior predictive checks; distinguish predictive evaluation from model criticism; and derive when MCAR, MAR, MNAR, and ignorability permit or forbid omission of the observation mechanism.

    Model comparison begins by naming the target. Evidence asks how much prior predictive mass a complete model assigns to the observed data. Predictive criteria ask how well a fitted procedure predicts held-out or future data. These quantities can favor different models because they solve different problems.

    Evidence and Bayes factors¤

    For model index \(m\),

    \[ p(D\mid m) =\int p(D\mid\theta_m,m)p(\theta_m\mid m)\,d\theta_m. \]

    Posterior model probabilities satisfy

    \[ p(m\mid D) =\frac{p(D\mid m)p(m)}{\sum_{m'}p(D\mid m')p(m')}. \]

    For two models, the Bayes factor is

    \[ \mathrm{BF}_{12}=\frac{p(D\mid m_1)}{p(D\mid m_2)}. \]

    Evidence automatically integrates over parameter volume, but this is not a universal anti-overfitting guarantee. Bayes factors can be highly sensitive to prior scale, sampling design, stopping assumptions, and small likelihood changes. Arbitrary constants in improper priors make ordinary cross-model evidence ratios undefined unless a specialized construction resolves them. Threshold tables for Bayes factors are reporting conventions, not universal decision rules.

    The likelihood and normalizer estimation volume develops importance, annealed, bridge, path, particle, and nested estimators. The estimator must not be confused with the evidence it targets.

    Why the harmonic mean is a warning, not a default¤

    The identity

    \[ \frac{1}{p(D)} =\mathbb E_{p(\theta\mid D)} \!\left[\frac{1}{p(D\mid\theta)}\right] \]

    holds when the displayed expectation exists under a proper prior and posterior. It uses posterior draws, not prior draws. The corresponding Monte Carlo estimator is nevertheless notorious: regions with small likelihood can give the reciprocal likelihood heavy or infinite-variance tails. Algebraic unbiasedness of an inverse estimate does not provide useful finite-sample stability after inversion.

    Bayesian model averaging¤

    If the candidate models and their priors are taken literally, the posterior predictive mixture is

    \[ p(\widetilde y\mid D) =\sum_m p(\widetilde y\mid D,m)p(m\mid D). \]

    This is Bayesian model averaging (BMA). It propagates uncertainty over the declared model list, but does not protect against all candidates being misspecified. A generic ensemble may also be a normalized predictive mixture; what distinguishes it from BMA is how component distributions and weights are defined or learned, not whether its weights happen to sum to one.

    Leave-one-out predictive evaluation¤

    For conditionally factorized observations, leave-one-out expected log predictive density is estimated by

    \[ \widehat{\operatorname{elpd}}_{\mathrm{LOO}} =\sum_{i=1}^n \log p(y_i\mid y_{-i}), \]

    where \(y_{-i}\) omits observation \(i\). The factorization unit must match the deployment target: leaving out one token, one image, one patient, or one site answers different questions. Dependence may require blocked, grouped, leave-future-out, or other structured splits.

    Importance sampling can approximate each leave-one-out posterior from draws of the full posterior. Pareto-smoothed importance sampling (PSIS) stabilizes the largest ratios and estimates a tail-shape diagnostic \(\widehat k\). The current finite-sample warning threshold is

    \[ k_{\mathrm{warn}}(S) =\min\!\left(1-\frac{1}{\log_{10}S},\,0.7\right), \]

    where \(S\) is the number of draws used by the importance calculation. It equals \(0.7\) once \(S\ge2155\) (roughly two thousand draws). A large \(\widehat k\) is evidence that the finite approximation may have high bias or poor convergence; it is not a decorative model-fit score. Influential cases may require exact refitting, a more robust model, or a validation design that respects the data dependence.

    WAIC and singular models¤

    For pointwise likelihood terms \(p(y_i\mid\theta)\), one common WAIC convention uses

    \[ \operatorname{lppd} =\sum_i\log\mathbb E_{p(\theta\mid D)} [p(y_i\mid\theta)], \]
    \[ p_{\mathrm{WAIC}} =\sum_i\operatorname{Var}_{p(\theta\mid D)} [\log p(y_i\mid\theta)], \]

    and

    \[ \widehat{\operatorname{elpd}}_{\mathrm{WAIC}} =\operatorname{lppd}-p_{\mathrm{WAIC}}. \]

    The variance is of the pointwise log likelihood across posterior draws, not the variance of the predictive observation. WAIC is asymptotically related to Bayesian leave-one-out prediction under the conditions developed in singular learning theory. Finite-sample diagnostics can still favor PSIS-LOO, and neither method repairs a wrong factorization unit.

    BIC has a narrower contract¤

    The Bayesian information criterion

    \[ \mathrm{BIC}=-2\log p(D\mid\widehat\theta)+d\log n \]

    approximates a log-evidence comparison under regular, identifiable, fixed-dimensional asymptotics and suitable prior mass near an interior optimum. Mixtures, neural networks, hidden-state models, and many latent-variable generators are singular: parameters may be nonidentifiable and Fisher information may be degenerate. Their evidence asymptotics need not have the \(d\log n\) penalty. BIC should therefore not be presented as a generic deep generative-model evidence estimator.

    Predictive stacking¤

    When every candidate is an approximation, predictive stacking chooses simplex weights to optimize held-out predictive performance, for example

    \[ \widehat{\mathbf w} =\arg\max_{\mathbf w\in\Delta^{M-1}} \sum_i \log\!\left( \sum_m w_m p_m(y_i\mid y_{-i}) \right). \]

    This is a predictive optimization criterion, not a posterior probability over models. It can be appropriate in an \(M\)-open setting where no candidate is assumed true. Its result still depends on the validation scheme, score, candidate diversity, and stability of the estimated predictive densities.

    6. Model Criticism: Generate from the Model¤

    Fitting answers, "Which parameter regions agree with the observations under this model?" Criticism asks, "Can this model reproduce the aspects of data that matter?" Generative models make the second question constructive.

    Prior predictive checks¤

    Before fitting, simulate

    \[ \theta^{\mathrm{rep}}\sim p(\theta), \qquad y^{\mathrm{rep}}\sim p(y\mid\theta^{\mathrm{rep}}). \]

    Compare these replications with domain constraints and plausible scales. A prior that places most mass on impossible images, counts, durations, graph sizes, or physical energies is not weak merely because its parameter density looks diffuse. Prior predictive checks expose the induced observation-space assumptions.

    Posterior predictive checks¤

    After observing \(D\), simulate

    \[ \theta^{\mathrm{rep}}\sim p(\theta\mid D), \qquad y^{\mathrm{rep}}\sim p(y\mid\theta^{\mathrm{rep}}), \]

    and compare a discrepancy \(T(y,\theta)\) or statistic \(T(y)\) between replicated and observed data. Useful discrepancies are chosen to expose scientifically important failures: tail mass, group calibration, spatial clustering, sequence length, graph motifs, conservation errors, or conditional coverage.

    A check can reject adequacy for its chosen discrepancy; it cannot prove that the model is correct. Reusing the observations both to fit and to check makes posterior-predictive reference distributions different from classical null distributions. Posterior predictive tail probabilities are generally not uniform under the model and are often conservative. They should be interpreted as diagnostic summaries, not automatically as calibrated frequentist \(p\)-values.

    Worked criticism of the coin model¤

    The Beta–Bernoulli model makes the ordered observations conditionally IID. Suppose the observed sequence has long runs despite a moderate success count. A statistic such as

    \[ T(x_{1:n})=\text{length of the longest constant run} \]

    tests temporal dependence that the success count cannot reveal. Posterior replications preserve uncertainty in \(\theta\) but remain conditionally IID. If their longest runs are consistently much shorter than the observed run, the failure points toward serial dependence, regime switching, or a changing success probability—not merely a different posterior summary for one constant \(\theta\).

    Predictive evaluation and criticism are complementary¤

    A model can predict well under an aggregate score yet fail a safety-critical subgroup discrepancy. It can also reproduce a selected statistic while assigning poor held-out log probability. Report both the scoring target and the diagnostic target. Neither one is a universal scalar measure of realism.

    7. Missing Data and Observation Mechanisms¤

    Missingness is a generative-model problem because the observed dataset is produced by both a data process and an observation process. Let \(Y\) denote the complete data and \(R\) a mask, with \(R_j=1\) when component \(Y_j\) is observed. A selection-model factorization is

    \[ p(y,r\mid\theta,\psi) =p(y\mid\theta)p(r\mid y,\psi). \]

    The observed-data likelihood integrates over unobserved components. Define

    \[ f_\theta(y_{\mathrm{mis}}) =p(y_{\mathrm{obs}},y_{\mathrm{mis}}\mid\theta) \]

    and

    \[ g_\psi(y_{\mathrm{mis}}) =p(r\mid y_{\mathrm{obs}},y_{\mathrm{mis}},\psi). \]

    Then

    \[ p(y_{\mathrm{obs}},r\mid\theta,\psi) =\int f_\theta(u)g_\psi(u)\,du. \]

    MCAR, MAR, and MNAR¤

    The labels describe conditional independence in the missingness mechanism, relative to the variables and observed pattern being modeled.

    • MCAR — missing completely at random

      \(p(r\mid y,\psi)=p(r\mid\psi)\). Missingness does not depend on observed or missing values under the model.

    • MAR — missing at random

      \(p(r\mid y,\psi)=p(r\mid y_{\mathrm{obs}},\psi)\). Dependence on observed values is allowed; dependence on unseen values is excluded after conditioning.

    • MNAR — missing not at random

      Dependence on \(y_{\mathrm{mis}}\) remains. The observation process must be modeled or bounded through additional assumptions.

    MAR does not mean that missingness is independent of the dataset, nor can MAR versus MNAR generally be identified from observed values alone. The distinction depends on untestable or externally informed assumptions about values that were not observed.

    Ignorability requires more than MAR¤

    Under MAR, the missingness term can be taken outside the integral over \(y_{\mathrm{mis}}\). For likelihood or Bayesian inference about \(\theta\), the mechanism is ignorable only with the required parameter distinctness—and, for the Bayesian factorization, an appropriate prior separation such as \(p(\theta,\psi)=p(\theta)p(\psi)\). Shared parameters, informative sampling, nonignorable selection, or a query about the missingness process itself break the simplification.

    Worked binary completion¤

    Let \(Y=(Y_1,Y_2)\) have a joint Bernoulli table and suppose \(Y_2\) is missing while \(Y_1=1\) is observed. Under an ignorable mechanism, completion uses

    \[ q_1(\theta)=P(Y_2=1\mid Y_1=1,\theta). \]

    The completion probability averages that conditional query over parameter uncertainty. Write

    \[ P_1(D)=P(Y_2=1\mid Y_1=1,D). \]

    Then

    \[ P_1(D)=\mathbb E_{p(\theta\mid D_{\mathrm{obs}})}[q_1(\theta)]. \]

    If the probability that \(Y_2\) is hidden depends directly on its unseen value, then the observed mask is itself evidence about \(Y_2\). Omitting \(p(R\mid Y,\psi)\) changes the target posterior, even if the joint model for \(Y\) is otherwise correct.

    Semi-supervised generation¤

    For input \(X\) and label \(Y\), a joint model uses unlabeled inputs through

    \[ p_\theta(x)=\sum_y p_\theta(x,y) \]

    or the corresponding integral. This can improve label prediction when the joint assumptions connect the input structure to the label. It is not an automatic benefit: a flexible discriminative boundary may be easier to learn than a correct model of the entire input distribution, and joint misspecification can make unlabeled data harmful. The evaluation must therefore separate density or generation quality, label prediction, calibration, and the missing-label mechanism.

    Bayesian assessment retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: prediction, criticism, and missingness
    1. Why is the harmonic-mean estimator unsuitable as a default evidence estimator?
    2. What predictive target differs between Bayesian model averaging and stacking?
    3. Can a calibrated posterior predictive check prove that the model is true?
    4. Why is MAR alone insufficient to justify ignoring a missingness mechanism?

    Solutions. (1) It can have infinite or extreme variance and is dominated by poorly sampled low-likelihood posterior regions. (2) Model averaging uses posterior model probabilities under a candidate model set; stacking directly chooses predictive weights, usually by held-out predictive performance. (3) No; checks can expose discrepancies relative to selected statistics but cannot validate every untested implication or identify a unique model. (4) Ignorability also depends on distinct parameters and the inferential/query setup, and the required conditioning variables must actually be observed and modeled.

    Bayesian assessment synthesis¤

    Evidence-based selection, held-out prediction, model averaging, and model criticism answer different questions. Predictive checks generate from the fitted model to reveal mismatches rather than to certify truth. Missingness is another generative mechanism whose omission requires explicit assumptions, not only a label such as MAR.

    8. A Generative Modeling Workflow¤

    Workflow and capstone learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the full Bayesian object, hierarchy, assessment, and missing-data chapters. By the end, you should be able to execute the workflow from sample space and joint law through prior predictive checking, computation, diagnosis, predictive evaluation, sensitivity analysis, and reporting; maintain an error ledger; and route a failed check to the model, inference, data, or decision layer responsible for it.

    The following workflow makes each inferential contract visible.

    1. Declare the generated object and observation process. Name units, support, dependence, missingness, and future prediction target.
    2. Write the joint model. Separate data likelihood, local states, global parameters, population variables, and model index.
    3. Inspect prior implications. Simulate observation-space outcomes and perform sensitivity analysis on consequential scales.
    4. Choose the query. Posterior samples, a decision, evidence, a predictive distribution, or missing-value completion are not interchangeable.
    5. Choose and diagnose computation. Track approximation, optimization, Monte Carlo, mixing, and numerical error separately from model error.
    6. Check the fitted model. Use targeted replicated-data discrepancies and conditional or subgroup diagnostics.
    7. Evaluate the deployment prediction. Use held-out units and scores that match the actual intervention, horizon, group, or modality.
    8. Compare or combine models with a named target. Evidence, LOO, and stacking answer different questions.
    9. Revise assumptions, not only hyperparameters. A failed check may require a new dependence structure, observation model, latent state, or support.
    10. Report sensitivity and unresolved nonidentifiability. Some uncertainty cannot be eliminated by additional computation on the same observations.

    Misconception Checks¤

    A diffuse prior is objective

    Diffuseness depends on coordinates and can imply extreme observation-space behavior. Inspect the prior predictive law and sensitivity of the query.

    Integrating parameters prevents overfitting

    Marginalization propagates uncertainty under a model. Poor priors, misspecification, model search, leakage, or an inappropriate prediction target can still produce bad generalization.

    A hierarchical model always improves every group

    Partial pooling follows an exchangeable population assumption. It can harm nonexchangeable or misspecified groups and must be checked by group-aware prediction.

    Bayes factors, LOO, and posterior predictive checks rank the same thing

    Evidence scores prior predictive probability, LOO estimates a held-out predictive target, and predictive checks diagnose chosen data features. None is a substitute for the others.

    MAR can be verified from the observed table

    MAR constrains dependence on unseen values. Observed data alone generally cannot distinguish it from MNAR without additional assumptions or design information.

    Retrieval Questions¤

    1. Why can a count and an ordered binary sequence produce the same posterior for \(\theta\) but different evidence?
    2. Under what conditions is \((a-1)/(a+b-2)\) a Beta mode?
    3. In the generic conjugate exponential-family prior, what does \(\tau/\nu\) describe—and what does it not generally describe?
    4. Why does finite exchangeability not by itself imply an IID-mixture representation?
    5. How does prediction for an existing group differ from prediction for a new group?
    6. Why can a noncentered parameterization help one hierarchical dataset and hurt another?
    7. What does a large PSIS \(\widehat k\) diagnose?
    8. Why is BIC unreliable as a generic evidence approximation for neural latent-variable models?
    9. Why is a posterior predictive tail probability not automatically a frequentist \(p\)-value?
    10. Which assumptions, in addition to MAR, permit an ignorable Bayesian missingness analysis?
    Complete solutions
    1. The likelihoods differ by a combinatorial factor independent of \(\theta\). It cancels from the posterior normalization but remains part of the probability assigned to the recorded observation.
    2. It is an attained interior mode only when \(a>1\) and \(b>1\); boundary and equality cases require separate treatment.
    3. Under the conjugate-prior regularity and boundary conditions, \(\tau/\nu\) is the expected mean parameter \(\nabla A(\eta)\). It is not generally \(\mathbb E[\eta]\).
    4. De Finetti's exact mixture representation requires an infinitely exchangeable or suitably extendible sequence. A fixed finite symmetric law need not extend.
    5. Existing-group prediction uses the posterior of that group's learned state. New-group prediction draws and integrates a fresh state from the population distribution, usually producing greater uncertainty.
    6. The two forms represent the same model but induce different posterior geometry. Weak group data often favor noncentering; strongly informative group data can favor centering.
    7. It estimates the tail behavior and finite-sample convergence of the importance ratios. Exceeding the sample-size-aware threshold warns that the PSIS approximation may be biased or unstable.
    8. BIC's \(d\log n\) penalty assumes regular, identifiable, fixed-dimensional asymptotics. Neural and latent models are commonly singular or nonidentified.
    9. The observations help construct the posterior used to generate the reference replications. This reuse changes calibration; posterior predictive tail probabilities are generally nonuniform and often conservative.
    10. The data and missingness parameters must be distinct for the target analysis, with an appropriate prior factorization in Bayesian inference. The query must also not require modeling the missingness process itself.

    Synthesis and Next Steps¤

    Bayesian learning starts from a joint generative law and derives posterior, predictive, and evidence queries by conditioning and marginalization. Those operations do not choose a decision loss, validate a model, or guarantee a stable computation. Conjugacy provides exact reference calculations; hierarchies pool information under population assumptions; empirical Bayes trades hyperparameter uncertainty for a plug-in fit; evidence and predictive criteria answer different comparison questions; predictive checks target chosen failures; and missing-data conclusions depend on an explicit observation mechanism.

    Continue to Latent Variables and Inference to study exact and approximate algorithms for the posterior integrals introduced here. Then read Objectives and Statistical Distances to separate a population discrepancy from its estimator and optimizer. Return to this chapter before Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants or Evaluating Generative Models: those volumes assume that evidence, posterior prediction, held-out scoring, and model criticism are already distinct.
