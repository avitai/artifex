    Tensor-Network and Born Probability Models¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Classical Generative Models and Graphical Structure
    • Probabilistic Circuits Explained

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A joint distribution over many discrete variables is a tensor: one axis per variable and one nonnegative entry per configuration. The representation is exact but exponentially large. Tensor-network probability models replace that table by contractions of smaller tensors. The internal bond dimensions control which dependencies can be represented compactly; the contraction graph and variable order control which probability queries remain affordable.

    Born models add a second construction. They represent an amplitude \(\Psi(x)\in\mathbb C\) and define probability from \(|\Psi(x)|^2\). A tensor network may implement this amplitude entirely on a classical computer. “Born” therefore specifies probability semantics, not quantum hardware. Quantum circuits are one possible amplitude parameterization and have a different likelihood, training, and systems contract.

    This volume separates six objects that are often compressed into one phrase:

    1. the probability tensor or density being modeled;
    2. its nonnegative, squared-amplitude, or purified factorization;
    3. the contraction graph, bond dimensions, gauges, and variable order;
    4. the objective and optimizer used to learn the factors;
    5. the marginal, conditional, likelihood, or sampling contraction; and
    6. the classical or quantum system that executes those contractions.

    Evidence posture

    Foundational algebra and exact-query statements are Established under their stated topology and rank assumptions. Architecture and benchmark results are Peer-reviewed research unless a stronger theorem is cited. A tensor-network parameter count, exact small-network contraction, or quantum-inspired vocabulary is not by itself evidence of superior likelihood, sample quality, scaling, or quantum advantage.

    1. Probability Tensors, Factorizations, and Born Semantics¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes finite probability tables and elementary matrix algebra. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. define nonnegative and Born tensor models;
    2. derive their normalizers;
    3. explain internal interference without invoking quantum hardware; and
    4. use a finite table to distinguish independence from a rank-constrained dependency.

    The probability tensor is the generated law¤

    For \(d\) finite variables, the joint probability mass function is an order-\(d\) tensor

    \[ \mathcal P[x_1,\ldots,x_d] =p(X_1=x_1,\ldots,X_d=x_d), \]

    with \(\mathcal P[x]\ge0\) and \(\sum_x\mathcal P[x]=1\). If every variable has \(K\) states, the full table has \(K^d\) entries. A tensor factorization is useful only when the target law has exploitable low-rank or sparse structure in the chosen topology and coordinate system.

    The model family is not “an array passed through einsum.” The free indices must correspond to observed variables, the internal indices and contraction graph must be declared, and the resulting scalar must define a valid probability after the chosen positivity and normalization construction.

    Three probability constructions¤

    Nonnegative contraction¤

    Let every core entry be nonnegative and let their full contraction be \(f_\theta(x)\ge0\). Then

    \[ p_\theta(x)=\frac{f_\theta(x)}{Z_\theta}, \qquad Z_\theta=\sum_x f_\theta(x). \]

    Local stochastic normalization can make \(Z_\theta=1\) in HMM-like parameterizations. Arbitrary nonnegative cores need a global contraction for \(Z_\theta\). Nonnegativity alone does not imply normalization.

    Born contraction¤

    A Born model permits signed or complex cores and defines

    \[ \boxed{ p_\theta(x)=\frac{|\Psi_\theta(x)|^2}{Z_\theta}, \qquad Z_\theta=\sum_x|\Psi_\theta(x)|^2. } \]

    Internal contraction paths can cancel before the magnitude is squared. A global phase \(e^{i\gamma}\Psi\) leaves the distribution unchanged, and even an independent phase \(e^{i\gamma(x)}\) for every completed configuration leaves \(p(x)\) unchanged if the parameterization can realize it. Computational-basis data therefore do not identify amplitude phase in general.

    Locally purified construction¤

    Introduce an auxiliary index \(a\) and marginalize a squared amplitude:

    \[ p_\theta(x) =\frac{1}{Z_\theta} \sum_a |\Phi_\theta(x,a)|^2. \]

    This is a latent-variable model with nonnegative output by construction. It contains a Born model when the auxiliary dimension is one and can offer different rank trade-offs from both nonnegative and pure Born networks. Glasser et al. (2019) prove representation separations among nonnegative MPS, Born models, and locally purified states. The result is incomparability, not a universal winner.

    Complete worked example: a dependent two-bit table¤

    Consider

    \[ \mathcal P= \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 & 0.1\\ 0.1 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Both marginals are \((0.5,0.5)\), but \(P(X_1=X_2)=0.8\), so the variables are not independent. The determinant is \(0.15\ne0\), hence the probability matrix has ordinary matrix rank two; a rank-one product model cannot represent it.

    A nonnegative latent factorization uses \(H\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and conditionally independent noisy copies. Let

    \[ \varepsilon=\frac{1-\sqrt{0.6}}{2}. \]

    Given \(H=h\), set \(X_i=h\) with probability \(1-\varepsilon\) and flip with probability \(\varepsilon\). Then

    \[ P(0,1)=\varepsilon(1-\varepsilon)=0.1, \]

    and each diagonal cell is

    \[ \tfrac12\bigl[(1-\varepsilon)^2+\varepsilon^2\bigr]=0.4. \]

    A Born representation may take the amplitude matrix \(\Psi_{ij}=\sqrt{\mathcal P_{ij}}\). It is already normalized because \(\sum_{ij}|\Psi_{ij}|^2=1\), and it also has rank two. Different semantics do not remove the rank needed by this particular table.

    Internal cancellation is not negative probability¤

    Let

    \[ H=\frac1{\sqrt2} \begin{bmatrix} 1&1\\ 1&-1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Factor a two-site amplitude as \(\Psi=HH=I\). The off-diagonal amplitudes vanish because two internal paths cancel. After normalization, the Born law puts mass \(1/2\) on 00 and 11. Replacing the negative entry by its absolute value gives a dense all-ones product and a uniform law instead. The negative sign is inside an amplitude computation; the final probabilities remain nonnegative.

    Chapter 1 counterexamples and misconception checks¤

    • Small parameter count is not small error. A low-rank network can badly miss a law whose relevant unfolding ranks are large.
    • Born is not quantum execution. Classical MPS contractions implement the same squared-amplitude semantics without qubits or hardware shots.
    • A nonnegative score is not yet a PMF. It must also have finite positive normalizer under the declared support.
    • Phase is not directly learned from basis probabilities. Many amplitudes induce the same observed law.
    • Tensor rank is not one number. CP rank, Tucker ranks, TT ranks, and ranks across different variable orders answer different representation questions.

    Independent practice and assessment map: tensor probability semantics¤

    Keep the probability tensor, factorization, normalizer, phase, rank notion, and execution substrate separate. Verify every validity or compactness claim on a finite law before generalizing it.

    P1 — Construct and falsify tensor probability models¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: nonnegative cores define a normalized PMF; squaring an amplitude removes the need for a normalizer; a low-parameter tensor network has low approximation error; two amplitudes with the same magnitudes are statistically identifiable from basis samples; a Born model requires quantum hardware; and one reported tensor rank determines all relevant factorization costs. For every false statement, name the missing normalization, support, cut, phase, approximation, or systems assumption.
    2. P1.2 — Finite-law ledger. For \(P=\begin{bmatrix}0.3&0.2\\0.1&0.4\end{bmatrix}\), verify normalization, compute both marginals, test independence by determinant and by a cell-product identity, and determine the ordinary matrix rank. Construct the nonnegative score \(f=P\) and Born amplitude \(\Psi_{ij}=\sqrt{P_{ij}}\); calculate both normalizers and explain why the two parameterizations share a law without sharing internal semantics.
    3. P1.3 — Cancellation and phase. Multiply the two Hadamard matrices in the chapter, enumerate the two internal paths contributing to each output amplitude, and normalize the resulting Born law. Replace the negative entry by its magnitude and recompute the law. Then multiply each completed amplitude by a configuration-dependent unit phase and prove that basis probabilities remain unchanged while intermediate contractions may change.
    4. P1.4 — Purification boundary. Build a two-bit locally purified model with a binary auxiliary index by specifying amplitudes \(\Phi(x,a)\), summing \(|\Phi(x,a)|^2\) over \(a\), and normalizing the four visible masses. Show how auxiliary dimension one recovers a Born model, then explain why this containment does not prove that purified, Born, or nonnegative MPS has the smallest rank for every target.
    5. P1.5 — Support and identifiability audit. Give one finite nonnegative contraction with \(Z=0\), one with a finite positive \(Z\) but zero mass on observed data, and two gauge- or phase-related amplitudes with the same PMF. For each, state whether the failure concerns probabilistic validity, likelihood support, parameter identifiability, or numerical representation, and give a finite test that detects it.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Define a tensor probability model for a short protein motif or a discretized physical configuration. Declare variables, alphabet, support, reference measure, order/topology, nonnegative/Born/purified semantics, normalizer, rank cuts, exact queries, and classical or quantum execution. Provide an enumerated small-instance oracle and identify which claims would need new evidence after increasing length or state dimension.

    Retrieval question: what changes when a score becomes a Born amplitude?

    Solution. The model squares a real or complex amplitude before normalization. This guarantees nonnegativity but doubles the contraction used for norms and marginals, permits cancellation among internal amplitude paths, introduces phase and gauge nonidentifiability, and does not specify whether execution is classical or quantum.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Tensor probability models compress a joint table; Born models compress an amplitude whose squared magnitude is the table. Positivity, normalization, rank, phase, and hardware are separate contracts. Chapter 2 turns the abstract factorization into the matrix-product-state chain, where every bond has a precise algebraic and statistical role.

    2. Matrix Product States, Gauges, and Dependency Capacity¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and singular-value decomposition. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. write an open-boundary MPS;
    2. count its parameters;
    3. relate bond dimension to unfolding rank;
    4. explain gauge freedom and canonical forms; and
    5. diagnose why variable ordering can dominate model size.

    The open-boundary MPS¤

    For \(r_0=r_d=1\), an MPS or tensor train represents

    \[ \boxed{ \Psi(x_1,\ldots,x_d) =A_1[x_1]A_2[x_2]\cdots A_d[x_d], } \]

    where \(A_i[x_i]\in\mathbb C^{r_{i-1}\times r_i}\). The matrix product reduces to a scalar. Its parameter count is

    \[ \sum_{i=1}^{d}K_i r_{i-1}r_i, \]

    or \(O(dKR^2)\) under uniform bounds, instead of \(K^d\). This is storage, not a guarantee of approximation quality.

    In a nonnegative MPS, core entries are nonnegative. In a Born MPS, the cores may be real or complex and the probability contraction uses a conjugate copy. An autoregressive MPS instead parameterizes normalized conditionals; it uses tensor contractions inside a different probability factorization.

    Bonds are ranks across declared cuts¤

    Group the first \(i\) variables into a row index and the remainder into a column index. The corresponding unfolding of \(\Psi\) has matrix rank at most \(r_i\). Conversely, successive SVDs construct an exact MPS whose minimal bond ranks are the unfolding ranks for that variable order.

    For a Born model, the Schmidt rank of its normalized amplitude across the cut is at most \(r_i\), so its entanglement entropy is at most \(\log r_i\). This is an amplitude property. For a nonnegative latent-chain interpretation with \(r_i\) latent states, data processing bounds the classical mutual information across that cut by \(\log r_i\). The two statements have different assumptions.

    Rank one means product structure¤

    If every \(r_i=1\), then

    \[ \Psi(x)=\prod_i a_i(x_i). \]

    After nonnegative or Born normalization, the joint law factorizes into independent marginals. Dependence requires a larger bond somewhere. A large bond is capacity, not proof that optimization used the capacity well.

    Gauge freedom and canonical forms¤

    For any invertible matrix \(G\) on an internal bond,

    \[ A_i[x_i]\mapsto A_i[x_i]G, \qquad A_{i+1}[x_{i+1}]\mapsto G^{-1}A_{i+1}[x_{i+1}] \]

    leaves every amplitude unchanged. Raw core parameters are therefore nonidentifiable. Ill-conditioned gauges can make intermediate values overflow or underflow even when the represented distribution is well behaved.

    A left-canonical core satisfies

    \[ \sum_{x_i}A_i[x_i]^\dagger A_i[x_i]=I, \]

    with the orientation adjusted at boundaries. Right-canonical cores obey the mirror condition. A mixed-canonical form puts an orthogonality center at one site or bond. Canonicalization fixes much of the gauge, makes norms local, and stabilizes contractions; it does not change the represented law or remove all residual degeneracy.

    Variable order is part of the model¤

    Complete worked example: variable order changes TT rank¤

    TT ranks refer to contiguous cuts in one order. Suppose four binary variables obey \(X_1=X_3\) and \(X_2=X_4\), with the two shared bits independent and uniform. In order \((1,2,3,4)\), the middle unfolding from \((X_1,X_2)\) to \((X_3,X_4)\) is a scaled \(4\times4\) identity and has rank four. In order \((1,3,2,4)\), each dependent pair is adjacent; after the completed first pair, the middle cut has rank one, and the maximum required rank is only two.

    With many interleaved pairs, the bad-order middle rank grows exponentially in the number of pairs. Permuting columns before fitting can therefore matter more than changing the optimizer.

    Beyond a chain¤

    • CP decomposition sums rank-one products and exposes a global mixture index.
    • Tucker decomposition uses a core coupled to factor matrices and can be expensive when its central core grows.
    • Tree and hierarchical Tucker networks align ranks with hierarchical variable partitions.
    • Tensor rings close the chain, removing distinguished boundaries but complicating gauges and some contractions.
    • PEPS and general loopy networks represent richer spatial structure but may lose tractable exact contraction.

    Topology should follow the intended dependency cuts and queries. A two- dimensional data layout does not automatically justify a two-dimensional loopy tensor network if likelihood and marginalization then become intractable.

    Chapter 2 counterexamples and misconception checks¤

    • Bond dimension is order-dependent. Reporting \(R\) without the variable order and topology makes capacity claims irreproducible.
    • Canonical form is not a unique model explanation. It reduces gauge freedom but does not identify one causal or semantic latent state.
    • Parameter count is not contraction cost. A sparse-looking loopy network can have an expensive contraction tree.
    • Entanglement is an amplitude diagnostic. Physical quantum entanglement requires an actual quantum state and substrate, not merely an MPS-shaped classical model.
    • A ring is not always a better chain. Boundary symmetry trades against more difficult normalization, optimization, and identifiability.

    Independent practice and assessment map: MPS structure and capacity¤

    Treat topology, variable order, cut rank, gauge, storage, and contraction cost as distinct design choices. Use explicit unfoldings and invariant amplitudes rather than interpreting a diagram by appearance.

    P1 — Audit an MPS from cores to dependency cuts¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: doubling every bond doubles parameter count; rank one can represent dependent observations after Born squaring; an invertible gauge changes the generated law; canonical form identifies a unique latent explanation; the same variable permutation preserves every TT rank; a tensor ring always lowers the required rank; and \(O(dKR^2)\) storage implies \(O(dKR^2)\) normalization. For each false claim, name the relevant boundary, cut, gauge, topology, or query distinction.
    2. P1.2 — Core-shape and parameter ledger. For four binary sites with open-boundary ranks \((1,2,3,2,1)\), write every core-slice shape and calculate the 32 raw scalar parameters. Compare that count with the 16-entry full amplitude and identify why gauge redundancy can make the small example use more raw parameters. Then set every bond rank to one, expand the product amplitude, normalize it under Born semantics, and prove that the resulting PMF factorizes.
    3. P1.3 — Ordering and unfolding ranks. For independent uniform bits \(H_1,H_2\) with \(X_1=X_3=H_1\) and \(X_2=X_4=H_2\), form the middle unfolding in orders \((1,2,3,4)\) and \((1,3,2,4)\). Compute the two ranks, identify which dependencies cross each cut, and generalize the maximum bad-order rank to \(m\) interleaved copied pairs. State what this calculation does and does not prove about learned approximation error.
    4. P1.4 — Gauge and canonicalization calculation. Choose adjacent rank-two cores and \(G=\operatorname{diag}(100,0.01)\). Apply \(A_i\mapsto A_iG\) and \(A_{i+1}\mapsto G^{-1}A_{i+1}\), then verify on every local configuration that the contracted amplitude is unchanged while intermediate norms and conditioning change. Specify a QR/SVD canonicalization check and list the residual nonidentifiabilities it cannot remove.
    5. P1.5 — Topology/query decision. Compare a chain, tree, ring, and small loopy grid for the same six variables. For each, declare the dependency cuts it favors, boundary convention, parameter count, exact normalization/marginal strategy, largest intermediate tensor, and whether approximate contraction changes the represented query. Select a topology for repeated missing-data conditionals under a fixed memory budget and give a falsifiable rejection rule.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Design an ordering study for image patches, genomic loci, protein residues, or irregular sensors. Define candidate orders from domain structure without using test outcomes, measure unfolding spectra/effective ranks and held-out likelihood, include contraction cost and restarts, and explain how the validation protocol separates an ordering advantage from optimizer or preprocessing effects.

    Retrieval question: why can a permutation change the required TT rank?

    Solution. TT rank is the matrix rank of an unfolding across a contiguous prefix/suffix cut. A permutation changes which dependencies cross each cut. Interleaving strongly coupled groups can force many joint states through one bond; placing the groups together can close those dependencies before the next cut.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    An MPS is a sequence of low-rank interfaces. Its bonds control dependency capacity only relative to a declared order, while gauges change the numerical representation without changing the law. Chapter 3 uses canonical gauges and left/right environments to compute normalization, marginals, conditionals, and exact sequential samples.

    3. Contraction, Marginalization, Conditioning, and Sampling¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the MPS notation and canonical forms from Chapter 2. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive transfer contractions for a Born norm;
    2. compute prefix and suffix environments;
    3. sample without MCMC;
    4. distinguish amplitude evaluation from probability marginalization; and
    5. state when “exact” ceases to be computationally useful.

    One amplitude versus the whole normalizer¤

    For a fixed configuration, evaluate

    \[ \Psi(x)=A_1[x_1]\cdots A_d[x_d] \]

    in \(O(dR^2)\) dense arithmetic under uniform ranks. A Born likelihood also needs

    \[ Z=\sum_x|\Psi(x)|^2. \]

    Define \(L_0=[1]\) and propagate a density-matrix environment:

    \[ \boxed{ L_i=\sum_{x_i}A_i[x_i]^\dagger L_{i-1}A_i[x_i]. } \]

    Then \(Z=L_d\) is scalar. The dense cost is commonly \(O(dKR^3)\) and working memory \(O(R^2)\), excluding stored cores. A canonical normalized MPS can make \(Z=1\) by construction, but only while the canonical constraints and boundary normalization are maintained.

    A nonnegative chain contracts vector messages rather than a doubled amplitude, often reducing dense normalization to \(O(dKR^2)\). Squared-amplitude and nonnegative models may have similarly sized cores but different query costs.

    Right environments and arbitrary evidence¤

    Mirror the recurrence from the right to obtain \(R_i\). For a candidate value \(a\) at site \(i\), given a fixed prefix, its unnormalized probability is

    \[ w_i(a) =\operatorname{tr}\!\left( A_i[a]^\dagger L_{i-1}A_i[a]R_i \right). \]

    The conditional is \(w_i(a)/\sum_b w_i(b)\). Summing over an unobserved variable uses the transfer operator; fixing an observed value selects one slice. Thus a single contraction engine supports:

    • the partition function by summing every free observed index;
    • a marginal by fixing query variables and summing the rest;
    • missing-data likelihood by fixing only observed variables;
    • conditional probability by dividing a joint evidence contraction by its evidence mass; and
    • posterior sufficient statistics through open-core environments.

    The denominator must be positive. A model that assigns zero probability to the evidence does not define the requested conditional.

    Exact sequential sampling¤

    Precompute right environments. Starting with \(L_0=[1]\):

    1. compute \(w_i(a)\) for every candidate \(a\);
    2. normalize those weights and draw \(x_i\);
    3. update the left environment using the selected slice; and
    4. continue until every variable is assigned.

    This samples from the represented MPS law without a Markov-chain burn-in. The algorithm is autoregressive as a sampler, but the model need not have been trained as an autoregressive factorization. The exact chain rule is recovered from tensor-network marginals at sampling time.

    Caching avoids recomputing a suffix contraction for every prefix. Cost claims must state whether environments are reused across samples, whether cores are batched, and whether the model is nonnegative, Born, or purified.

    Complete worked example: sampling the two-bit law¤

    Return to

    \[ \mathcal P= \begin{bmatrix} 0.4&0.1\\ 0.1&0.4 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    The first marginal is \(P(X_1=0)=0.5\). If the first draw is zero, the conditional weights for \(X_2\) are \((0.4,0.1)\), so

    \[ P(X_2=0\mid X_1=0)=0.8, \qquad P(X_2=1\mid X_1=0)=0.2. \]

    The sequential probability of 00 is \(0.5(0.8)=0.4\), and of 01 is \(0.5(0.2)=0.1\). The same calculation applies after the first draw is one. Sequential contraction has reproduced the full joint table without enumerating all four states during each draw.

    Stable arithmetic¤

    Long products underflow and high-rank contractions overflow. Common controls include:

    • canonicalize cores and keep singular values near a declared orthogonality center;
    • rescale environments and accumulate their logarithmic scale factors;
    • use QR or SVD rather than explicit inverses for gauge changes;
    • clip only with a documented change to the estimator, not as an invisible numerical repair;
    • monitor discarded singular-value mass during truncation; and
    • compare the contracted \(Z\) with the canonical-form expectation.

    Log-sum-exp alone does not solve signed or complex cancellation. Scaling a density-matrix environment preserves its direction while its log scale is tracked separately.

    General contraction graphs¤

    For a tree tensor network, message passing is exact and its cost follows the largest intermediate tensor. For a loopy graph, choosing a contraction order is itself an optimization problem. Complexity is governed by induced widths in the appropriate contraction or line graph, not merely by the number of nodes.

    Exact contraction of general two-dimensional tensor networks can be computationally intractable. Approximate methods truncate bonds, use boundary MPS approximations, Monte Carlo, or variational environments. Once a contraction is approximated, the resulting likelihood, marginal, gradient, and sample law inherit that approximation.

    Chapter 3 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Efficient point evaluation is not efficient normalization. A loopy amplitude may be cheap for one \(x\) but hard to sum over all \(x\).
    • Exact algebra is not low cost. An exact contraction whose intermediate bond grows exponentially is not a scalable query.
    • Sequential sampling is not independent-factor sampling. Each conditional carries all contracted prefix/suffix dependence available within the model.
    • Approximate environments change probabilities. They are not merely a faster implementation of the exact sampler.
    • Canonical normalization can drift. An unconstrained optimizer step may leave the canonical manifold until reorthogonalization.

    4. Learning, Rank Adaptation, and Continuous Variables¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes exact MPS contractions and basic maximum likelihood. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive a Born NLL gradient;
    2. compare global-gradient and DMRG-style sweep optimization;
    3. diagnose zero support and numerical scaling;
    4. select ranks without test leakage; and
    5. construct continuous densities with feature maps and Gram contractions.

    Maximum likelihood and its negative phase¤

    For a Born model and empirical data \(x^{(1:n)}\),

    \[ \mathcal L(\theta) =-\frac1n\sum_{m=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x^{(m)}) =-\frac2n\sum_m\log|\Psi_\theta(x^{(m)})| +\log Z_\theta. \]

    For a real parameter \(\theta_j\) away from amplitude zeros,

    \[ \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\theta_j} =-\frac2n\sum_m \operatorname{Re} \frac{\partial_{\theta_j}\Psi(x^{(m)})}{\Psi(x^{(m)})} +\partial_{\theta_j}\log Z. \]

    The data term raises observed amplitudes; the normalizer term redistributes mass over the whole support. Exact MPS environments compute both. If an observed configuration has \(\Psi=0\), its NLL is infinite and the ratio is singular. Initialization and smoothing must address support, not hide the failure behind an arbitrary finite log floor.

    For a normalized canonical parameterization, \(Z=1\) on the constraint manifold. The normalizer cannot simply be dropped while taking an unconstrained Euclidean gradient that leaves that manifold.

    Global gradient training¤

    Automatic differentiation through stabilized contractions updates every core simultaneously. It integrates readily with minibatches, shared neural feature maps, and modern optimizers. Its main hazards are:

    • products whose scale grows or decays exponentially with sequence length;
    • redundant gauge directions and ill-conditioned cores;
    • dense doubled-network memory for Born contractions;
    • rank fixed before training; and
    • unconstrained steps that violate nonnegativity, stochasticity, isometry, or canonical form.

    Environment rescaling with accumulated log factors, periodic canonicalization, and manifold-aware parameterizations are principled controls. A rejected ICLR 2026 submission on probabilistic tensor-network stabilization remains Preprint evidence; its reported scaling results must not be presented as an accepted benchmark.

    Alternating and DMRG-style sweeps¤

    Sweep algorithms place an orthogonality center at one or two adjacent cores, optimize that local block using environments for the rest, and move the center through the chain. A two-site update can reshape the optimized block and split it by SVD:

    \[ M=U\Sigma V^\dagger. \]

    Retaining singular values above a declared rank or discarded-mass criterion adapts the bond dimension. This can discover where capacity is needed, but the criterion is local and the overall objective remains nonconvex. Sweeps do not guarantee a global likelihood optimum.

    Han et al. (2018) introduced MPS Born-machine learning and direct sampling using quantum-many-body optimization ideas. Historical DMRG terminology describes the optimizer and canonical algebra; it does not make the trained probability model a physical ground state.

    Complete worked example: what rank one cannot learn¤

    For the two-bit target from Chapter 1, the maximum-likelihood independent model matches the uniform marginals, hence assigns \(q_{ij}=1/4\). Its population NLL is

    \[ -\sum_{ij}\mathcal P_{ij}\log\tfrac14=\log4\approx1.38629. \]

    The entropy of the true table is

    \[ H(\mathcal P) =-2(0.4\log0.4)-2(0.1\log0.1) \approx1.19355. \]

    The irreducible rank-one excess is

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathcal P\|q) =\log4-H(\mathcal P) \approx0.19274\ \text{nats}. \]

    A rank-two MPS represents the table exactly, so it can close this approximation gap in the population model. Finite-sample validation is still needed: a larger rank can reduce training NLL while worsening held-out NLL.

    Rank and topology selection¤

    Select order, topology, ranks, regularization, and truncation thresholds on a validation protocol fixed before the test evaluation. Useful diagnostics include:

    • held-out NLL as a function of maximum and effective rank;
    • singular-value spectra at every bond;
    • discarded norm or likelihood change after truncation;
    • contraction FLOPs, peak intermediate memory, and sample latency;
    • sensitivity to variable permutations and random restarts; and
    • calibration of requested marginals and conditionals.

    Rank is a structural hyperparameter, not a post hoc explanation. Searching many orders and reporting only the best test result leaks selection information.

    Continuous variables through feature maps¤

    Let \(\phi_i(x_i)\in\mathbb C^{K_i}\) be a feature map and define

    \[ A_i(x_i)=\sum_{s=1}^{K_i}\phi_{i,s}(x_i)A_i[s], \qquad \Psi(x)=A_1(x_1)\cdots A_d(x_d). \]

    The density is \(p(x)=|\Psi(x)|^2/Z\) with respect to the declared product base measure. Define Gram matrices

    \[ G_i[s,t] =\int \phi_{i,s}(x)\overline{\phi_{i,t}(x)}\,d\mu_i(x). \]

    Normalization and marginalization insert \(G_i\) at integrated sites. If features are orthonormal, \(G_i=I\) and the discrete Born contractions carry over. If not, ignoring the Gram matrix changes the density.

    Tensor-train density estimation uses functional tensor trains for exact partition, marginal, CDF, and sampling queries under its basis and optimization assumptions. Meiburg et al. (2025) develop a continuous-valued Born MPS with feature maps, canonical sampling, and universal approximation results for the stated smooth-density class. Universality permits rank and feature dimension to grow; it is not a fixed-budget performance claim.

    Mixed data and learned features¤

    Discrete sites use one-hot or learned categorical features; continuous sites use basis functions or neural feature maps. Learned features can improve compression but alter the Gram integrals. Exact normalization survives only if those integrals remain tractable or are constrained to a known isometry.

    An encoder followed by a tensor density is a composed latent system. Its likelihood on observed data is not automatically the latent tensor likelihood; the observation map, Jacobian or decoder, and information loss must be declared.

    Chapter 4 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Canonical \(Z=1\) does not license an unconstrained update. The optimization route must preserve or restore the canonical constraint.
    • DMRG is not a global optimizer. Sweeps can stall, depend on order, and prune a bond needed later.
    • Rank growth is not free. It raises contraction time and may overfit.
    • Continuous features define the measure. Basis functions, support, Gram matrices, boundary behavior, and sampling inversion belong in the model.
    • Universal approximation is asymptotic. It does not identify the rank, data, or compute required for one benchmark.

    5. Relationships to Other Generative Model Families¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–4 plus the probability-factorization and tractable-circuit vocabulary developed in the foundations. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. classify tensor relationships as algebraic equivalence, distributional correspondence, implementation analogy, or hybrid composition;
    2. derive the HMM and autoregressive correspondences; and
    3. reject quantum-advantage claims that provide only shared Born notation.

    Tensor notation is a language for multilinear functions, not a guarantee that two models have the same probability law or computational behavior. The safest way to compare families is to separate four questions:

    • What nonnegative or complex-valued function is represented?
    • How is that function normalized into a probability distribution?
    • Which queries can be contracted exactly under the chosen structure?
    • Which algorithm and hardware actually perform those contractions?

    This chapter uses the relationship labels from The Generative Modeling Landscape. An algebraic equivalence is stronger than a shared objective, and a hybrid composition is not evidence that its components are one family.

    Hidden Markov models and weighted automata¤

    For a length-\(d\) hidden Markov model with latent state \(h_i\in\{1,\ldots,R\}\),

    \[ p(x_{1:d}) =\sum_{h_{1:d}} p(h_1)p(x_1\mid h_1) \prod_{i=2}^{d}p(h_i\mid h_{i-1})p(x_i\mid h_i). \]

    Group the initial probabilities, transition probabilities, and emission probabilities into site matrices. The result is a nonnegative MPS contraction. The hidden-state index is the bond index. This is an algebraic equivalence between the stated finite HMM law and a constrained nonnegative MPS.

    The converse needs care. A generic nonnegative MPS core need not satisfy HMM row-stochastic constraints without a gauge change and a valid normalization; a signed or complex Born MPS can exploit cancellations unavailable to the same size nonnegative HMM. “MPS generalizes an HMM” therefore describes a representation inclusion under explicit constraints, not identical learning dynamics or identical minimal rank.

    Weighted finite automata make the same chain algebra visible for strings:

    \[ f(x_{1:d})=\alpha^\top A[x_1]\cdots A[x_d]\omega. \]

    If \(f(x)\ge 0\) and the total mass over the declared string space is finite and normalized, it defines a probability law. An arbitrary real weighted automaton does not acquire probability semantics merely because its final scalar is easy to compute.

    Autoregressive models¤

    Every strictly positive finite joint law admits the chain-rule factorization

    \[ p(x)=\prod_{i=1}^{d}p(x_i\mid x_{<i}). \]

    An exactly contractible tensor network can calculate each conditional by contracting the unobserved suffix, so its sequential sampler can be written in autoregressive form. This is an algebraic equivalence of the resulting joint law, not an equivalence of parameterizations:

    • a neural autoregressive model predicts a normalized local distribution from a prefix representation;
    • an MPS conditional is a ratio of prefix/suffix contractions;
    • changing the MPS variable order changes its rank requirements;
    • changing the autoregressive order changes its conditional prediction task; and
    • cached contraction environments and a Transformer KV cache have different state, update, and memory costs.

    An autoregressive probability tensor is also a tensor factorization in the broad mathematical sense. Calling every autoregressive network a tensor network would nevertheless discard the structural property that makes MPS contraction and rank analysis useful.

    Probabilistic circuits¤

    Fixing a contraction order turns a tensor network into an arithmetic circuit of sums and products. Conversely, many hierarchical tensor factorizations can be expressed as circuits. The precise correspondence depends on scope structure, sharing, decomposability, smoothness, nonnegativity or squaring, and the query being evaluated.

    Loconte et al. (2025) give a formal circuit account of tensor factorizations and a modular construction for tensorized probabilistic circuits. This supports an algebraic and implementation correspondence for the represented computational graphs. It does not make all circuits tensor trains or make arbitrary tensor-network contractions tractable.

    For a nonnegative smooth, decomposable probabilistic circuit, normalization and marginalization can be linear in circuit size. Squaring a real or complex circuit to obtain a Born law couples copies of the circuit and can increase contraction cost. Loconte, Javaloy, and Vergari (2026) derive orthogonality conditions that recover efficient marginalization for a broader class of squared circuits. The result is structural: an unconstrained squared circuit does not inherit the same cost.

    Graphical models and variable elimination¤

    A factor graph with factors \(\psi_a(x_a)\) has unnormalized mass

    \[ \tilde p(x)=\prod_a\psi_a(x_a). \]

    Its partition function is a tensor-network contraction: variables are tensor indices and factors are tensors. Variable elimination is a contraction order, and the largest intermediate scope controls cost. This is an algebraic equivalence between sum-product inference and contraction.

    The graph and the tensor factorization expose different structure. Graphical models emphasize conditional independence; an MPS emphasizes low unfolding rank in one variable order. A dense distribution with no useful conditional independences may still have a compact MPS approximation, while a sparse graph with large treewidth may be expensive under a poor contraction order.

    Bonnevie and Schmidt (2021) use MPS as a variational family for discrete posteriors, including cases where mean field is too restrictive. That is a hybrid model–inference composition: the target graphical model and the MPS approximation remain distinct objects.

    Latent-variable models, flows, and energy models¤

    Tensor models can appear inside other families without replacing their semantics:

    • a tensor network can parameterize a discrete prior \(p(z)\) in a VAE;
    • a decoder or flow can map a tensor-distributed latent variable into observed space;
    • a nonnegative tensor can be an unnormalized energy model \(\tilde p(x)=\exp[-E(x)]\);
    • a tensor contraction can parameterize a local factor, likelihood, or variational posterior; and
    • a learned codec can turn high-dimensional observations into discrete sites modeled by an MPS.

    These are hybrid compositions. Observed-space likelihood remains incomplete unless every stochastic or invertible map, Jacobian, decoder distribution, and discarded information term is accounted for. Exact latent normalization does not make a lossy encoder an exact density model of its inputs.

    Born machines and quantum generative models¤

    Born machines borrow the probability rule

    \[ p(x)=\frac{|\langle x\mid\psi\rangle|^2} {\langle\psi\mid\psi\rangle}. \]

    This rule can be evaluated entirely on classical hardware when \(\psi\) is a classically stored MPS. In that case, “state,” “amplitude,” and “Born rule” name the algebra; no quantum processor is involved.

    A quantum circuit Born machine instead prepares a physical state and estimates measurement probabilities with shots. It may represent amplitudes that are difficult to contract classically, but finite-shot noise, state preparation, device errors, gradient estimation, and readout enter the system. Shared Born semantics are a distributional analogy. A computational advantage requires a task, accuracy criterion, resource model, classical baseline, and hardware evidence; the word “quantum” is not such evidence.

    Complete worked example: one table, several views¤

    Return to

    \[ \mathcal P= \begin{bmatrix}0.4&0.1\\0.1&0.4\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The same law can be represented as:

    1. a rank-two nonnegative matrix factorization with one binary latent state;
    2. a two-site MPS of bond rank two;
    3. a Born amplitude matrix \(\Psi_{ij}=\sqrt{\mathcal P_{ij}}\);
    4. an autoregressive law \(p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)\) with \(p(x_1)=1/2\) and conditionals \((0.8,0.2)\) or \((0.2,0.8)\); or
    5. a four-leaf normalized probabilistic circuit.

    The equality is at the distribution level. The representations expose different queries, constraints, parameter non-identifiabilities, and routes to larger dimensions. A benchmark result for one representation therefore cannot be transferred to all five.

    2. P1.2 — HMM-to-MPS derivation. Choose a two-state HMM with explicit initial, transition, and binary-emission probabilities. Construct site matrices for a length-three observation law and compute one sequence probability both by summing latent paths and by matrix contraction. Identify the stochastic/nonnegative constraints and boundary vectors. Then give one signed or complex Born core that violates the same-size HMM interpretation while still defining a normalized law after squaring.
    3. P1.3 — Autoregressive correspondence and systems boundary. For the chapter's two-bit law, derive both conditional tables and verify all four products \(p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)\). Show how suffix contractions produce those conditionals from an MPS. Compare the MPS environment cache with a neural autoregressive KV cache in stored state, update operations, batching, and variable-order sensitivity without claiming parameterization or latency equivalence.
    4. P1.4 — Circuit, graph, and hybrid audit. Translate a three-factor chain \(\psi_{12}(x_1,x_2)\psi_{23}(x_2,x_3)\psi_{34}(x_3,x_4)\) into tensors and compare two elimination orders by largest intermediate scope. Draw the resulting arithmetic circuit and state the semiring/query assumptions behind the equivalence. Then place an MPS prior inside a VAE or flow and list every decoder, Jacobian, inference, and information-loss term needed before making an observed-space likelihood claim.
    5. P1.5 — Quantum-advantage evidence record. Compare a classically contracted MPS Born machine with a quantum-circuit Born machine on one declared sampling or likelihood-estimation task. Record represented state class, preparation cost, bond/circuit resources, approximation tolerance, finite shots, gradient estimator, noise/readout correction, classical baseline, hardware, latency, energy, uncertainty, and independent reproduction. Write the strongest claim supported if the only common evidence is an identical Born probability formula.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Compose a tensor prior or posterior with a protein, molecular, language, or scientific generator. Classify each relationship to autoregression, latent variables, energy models, circuits, and quantum implementations; declare which law each component owns; and produce a query/cost ledger that prevents exact latent contraction or shared notation from becoming a family-wide or end-to-end performance claim.

    Retrieval question: when is an MPS sampler genuinely autoregressive?

    At the distribution level, whenever each next-site conditional is computed from the same normalized MPS law and multiplied along the chosen order. It is not thereby the same parameterization or runtime system as a neural autoregressive model.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Tensor networks connect exactly to HMMs, arithmetic circuits, and sum-product inference under stated structural constraints. They connect compositionally to latent-variable models and share Born probability semantics with some quantum generators. The distinctions matter most when a paper moves from representable functions to tractable queries or from classical algebra to hardware claims. Chapter 6 turns those distinctions into an evaluation and reporting protocol.

    6. Evaluation, Applications, and Current Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the full probability, contraction, learning, and relationship development of Chapters 1–5. By the end, you should be able to do the following:

    1. design exact finite tests;
    2. separate representation and contraction error;
    3. compare rank/order choices at matched compute;
    4. audit continuous-data measures; and
    5. report current results at their actual evidence maturity.

    A tensor-network generative model should be evaluated as a probability model, a structured approximation, and an implemented contraction system. Sample quality alone cannot reveal normalization error, likelihood quality, contraction cost, rank sensitivity, or whether the advertised query is exact.

    Likelihood and normalization¤

    For an exactly normalized discrete model, report held-out

    \[ \operatorname{NLL} =-\frac1n\sum_{m=1}^{n}\log p(x^{(m)}). \]

    Bits per variable is

    \[ \operatorname{BPV}=\frac{\operatorname{NLL}}{d\log 2}, \]

    only when each example has the same declared number \(d\) of variables. Images often use bits per dimension, language uses bits per character/token or perplexity, and continuous densities require the base measure and preprocessing. These quantities are not interchangeable.

    Numerically test normalization even when theory guarantees it. For small systems, enumerate all states. For larger chains, compare canonical norm, direct environment contraction, and sampled frequencies on selected marginals. A deviation can expose a broken canonical update, complex-conjugation error, feature-map Gram error, or underflow.

    Marginal and conditional calibration¤

    Exact-query claims deserve query-level tests. Choose subsets \(S\) and compare model marginals \(p(x_S)\) with empirical frequencies or a known simulator law. For conditional generation, evaluate

    • conditional log score or Brier score for discrete events;
    • coverage and sharpness for predictive sets or intervals;
    • constraint satisfaction and probability assigned to the constraint;
    • calibration across common and rare evidence patterns; and
    • latency as the evidence set and query set change.

    Conditioning can create near-zero denominators. Report the policy for impossible or numerically negligible evidence rather than silently renormalizing arbitrary roundoff.

    Sampling checks¤

    Sequential contraction sampling is exact relative to the represented law when every conditional and categorical draw is exact. Validate the implementation by comparing sample frequencies with contracted marginals on a finite test case. Then report:

    • first-sample latency versus amortized throughput;
    • environment-cache construction and update costs;
    • batch size and accelerator utilization;
    • effective and maximum rank during sampling;
    • precision and normalization drift; and
    • any approximate truncation, beam, or top-\(k\) intervention.

    FID, KID, embedding distances, and visual inspection may supplement these checks for image-like observations, but they do not establish tensor likelihood or exact sampling.

    Approximation and capacity diagnostics¤

    A useful rank study reports held-out NLL against:

    1. maximum permitted rank;
    2. realized singular-value spectra and effective ranks;
    3. parameter count;
    4. contraction FLOPs and peak memory; and
    5. several variable orders.

    Compare at least rank one, a moderate rank, and a rank high enough to expose overfitting or computational saturation. Report all tested orders or define a validation-only order-selection protocol. For approximate contraction on trees or loopy networks, vary the truncation budget separately from the learned model rank so representation error and inference error are not conflated.

    Worked evaluation: the two-bit model¤

    For the target table

    \[ \mathcal P= \begin{bmatrix}0.4&0.1\\0.1&0.4\end{bmatrix}, \]

    an auditable unit test proceeds as follows:

    1. enumerate all four states and confirm \(\sum_xp(x)=1\);
    2. verify both one-bit marginals equal \((0.5,0.5)\);
    3. contract the conditional \(p(X_2\mid X_1=0)=(0.8,0.2)\);
    4. draw samples and attach binomial uncertainty to the observed frequency;
    5. verify the exact rank-two NLL approaches \(H(\mathcal P)\approx1.19355\);
    6. verify the best rank-one independent NLL is \(\log4\approx1.38629\); and
    7. confirm that a gauge transform changes cores but not any probability.

    This one test exercises normalization, marginalization, conditioning, sampling, capacity, and gauge invariance. A high-dimensional benchmark should not replace such finite exact tests.

    Applications¤

    Density estimation and compression¤

    MPS, tensor trains, and tensorized circuits provide normalized density estimators when their contractions are exact. Sequential probabilities can drive arithmetic coding; compression rate then evaluates log-loss operationally. The coding distribution, symbol order, precision, headers, and model cost must be included in a fair compression report.

    Missing-data and posterior inference¤

    Arbitrary observed/missing patterns can be answered by contraction when the topology and canonical structure keep those contractions tractable. Tensor trains can also approximate a posterior over many discrete latent assignments. Report approximation error against exact small instances or stronger importance/MCMC baselines, not only the tensor objective.

    Sequences, language, and biological data¤

    Chain structure matches sequences naturally, but fixed length and local physical dimension can be restrictive. Variable-length strings need an end symbol, length model, or weighted-automaton construction with finite total mass. Protein, genomic, and language evaluations must define tokenization, alphabet, homology or contamination splits, and whether generation conditions on length or metadata.

    Images and spatial data¤

    An image MPS requires a one-dimensional ordering or a higher-dimensional tensor topology. Raster, snake, space-filling-curve, patch, and learned orders induce different cut ranks. Report the order and compare it under matched capacity. Pixel likelihood and perceptual quality remain different axes.

    Continuous and scientific distributions¤

    Functional tensor trains and continuous Born models can represent densities, CDFs, and conditional laws through basis functions. Scientific use requires units, support, boundary conditions, symmetries, conservation constraints, and the reference measure. A low NLL in transformed coordinates is not sufficient if the inverse transform violates the intended physical state space.

    • Established. Han et al. (2018) developed MPS Born-machine density estimation and direct sampling. Bonnevie and Schmidt (2021) developed MPS inference for discrete probabilistic models. Novikov et al. (2021) developed tensor-train density estimation with tractable probabilistic queries.
    • Peer-reviewed research. Miller et al. (2021) studied tensor networks for probabilistic sequence modeling. Loconte et al.     Misconception checks¤

    • Exact NLL does not prove useful samples. It measures one declared density and must be supplemented for perceptual, scientific, or task utility.
    • Exact sampling does not mean constant-time sampling. Environment construction, rank, sequence length, batching, and cache updates still cost time and memory.
    • A lower training NLL does not select rank. Order and rank are chosen on held-out data under a declared search budget.
    • Canonical normalization does not excuse numeric tests. Broken retractions, conjugation, basis integrals, or dtype choices can violate the implementation even when the symbolic construction is sound.
    • One hardware benchmark is not a family property. Contraction layout, compiler, sparsity, batch, precision, and device determine measured speed.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Rank and order. Construct a four-bit law whose MPS rank differs under two orders. Compute the relevant unfolding ranks and relate them to a graph cut.
    2. Born versus nonnegative. Find a signed amplitude whose squared law is simple but whose intermediate contractions contain cancellation. Identify which parameters are not observable from basis probabilities.
    3. Contraction planning. Translate a small factor graph into tensors and compare two elimination orders by their largest intermediate tensor.
    4. Continuous density. Choose two nonorthogonal features, compute their Gram matrix, and show numerically how replacing it by the identity changes \(Z\).
    5. Controlled comparison. Compare an HMM, nonnegative MPS, and Born MPS on one sequence dataset with matched split, order, parameter budget, and likelihood implementation.
    6. Systems audit. Implement exact sequential sampling twice—with and without cached right environments—and separate arithmetic count from measured latency.

    Final synthesis¤

    Tensor-network generative models factor exponentially large tables or amplitudes into contractions whose cost is controlled by topology, order, and rank. Nonnegative models obtain probability through positive mass; Born models obtain it by squared magnitude and therefore allow phase, sign, and gauge non-identifiability. Canonical forms can make normalization and sequential sampling efficient, but only for the queries and structures they actually support.

    The family is most compelling when low-rank structure matches the variable order and exact marginals, conditionals, or samples are scientifically useful. It is least compelling when required rank grows rapidly, preprocessing destroys the intended semantics, or a general contraction graph creates prohibitive intermediates. The durable research habit is to state the probability object, contraction, approximation, and implementation separately—and to test all four.
