    This chapter studies support mismatch, mode collapse, continuous and discretized game dynamics, discrete-output gradient estimators, Wasserstein objectives, regularization, and major GAN variants. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    Training Challenges¤

    Training Challenges: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand the logistic game, raw-logit versus probability outputs, and the difference between a population objective and a finite estimator. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. distinguish mode dropping, critic overfitting, gradient saturation, and rotational game dynamics;
    2. state what spectral normalization, WGAN-GP, R1/R2, and TTUR do—and do not—guarantee;
    3. treat update ratios and optimizer settings as protocol-specific hyperparameters;
    4. compare logistic, hinge, least-squares, Wasserstein, and relativistic objectives; and
    5. choose a gradient estimator for continuous, relaxed, or discrete generator outputs; and
    6. design a controlled intervention instead of stacking stabilizers without an ablation.

    Mode Collapse¤

    Mode collapse or mode dropping occurs when the generator assigns too little or no probability to important regions of the target distribution. Severe collapse can make outputs nearly identical, but partial collapse can retain substantial within-mode variety while omitting whole subpopulations.

    Possible symptoms:

    • Many latent inputs produce nearly identical outputs
    • Generated samples cluster into too few semantic or geometric regions
    • Missing modes in the data distribution

    Interventions to test:

    • Minibatch discrimination (Salimans et al., 2016): let the discriminator see batch-wise feature similarities, so it can directly punish "everything looks the same".
    • Unrolled GANs (Metz et al., 2017): unroll \(k\) discriminator update steps when computing the generator gradient, so \(G\) optimises against a "forward-looking" \(D\) rather than the current step.
    • Experience replay / past-sample buffers (Shrivastava et al., 2017): mix in older fakes so \(D\) doesn't forget previously-defeated modes.
    • PacGAN (Lin et al., 2018): give \(D\) packs of \(m\) samples instead of singletons. The paper proves, under its mode-collapse and hypothesis-testing formalisation, that packing penalises collapsed generator distributions more strongly; it does not guarantee that finite neural GAN training will recover every data mode.
    • Top-k generator updates (Sinha et al., 2020): in each generator step, only back-propagate through the top-k most-realistic generated samples in the batch.
    • Mixtures or ensembles: allocate capacity across generators or combine independently trained models, then measure aggregate coverage. Merely selecting one member does not guarantee recovery of missing modes.

    Vanishing Gradients¤

    When the discriminator becomes too confident, it provides vanishing gradients to the generator.

    Problem stated at the right variable. Let \(s=D_{\mathrm{logit}}(G_\theta(z))\) and let the reported probability be \(\sigma(s)\). For the minimax generator term,

    \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial s}\log(1-\sigma(s))=-\sigma(s), \]

    which approaches zero when a generated sample is confidently rejected. By contrast, the non-saturating loss has

    \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial s}\big[-\log\sigma(s)\big]=-(1-\sigma(s)), \]

    which approaches \(-1\) in the same limit. Saying merely that \(\partial\log(1-D)/\partial D\) vanishes would be false; saturation arises after composing the loss with the sigmoid score and generator.

    Solutions:

    1. Non-saturating loss (Goodfellow et al., 2014): use \(\max_G \mathbb{E}[\log D(G(z))]\) instead.
    2. Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017): optimize a Kantorovich–Rubinstein dual objective over an approximately Lipschitz critic. Lipschitz control bounds sensitivity; it does not guarantee that the finite critic supplies a useful nonzero gradient everywhere.
    3. Least Squares GAN (Mao et al., 2017): replace the cross-entropy classifier with the squared-error objective \(\mathbb{E}[(D(G(z)) - 1)^2]\).
    4. Relativistic objectives (Jolicoeur-Martineau, 2018): score real samples relative to generated samples. R3GAN later combines a particular relativistic paired loss with zero-centered regularization and proves a local convergence result under its stated assumptions; that theorem does not transfer automatically to every relativistic loss.

    Training Instability¤

    GAN optimization adds coupled-estimation problems beyond ordinary fixed-target supervised learning:

    The Optimizer Geometry and Coupled Learning Dynamics chapter derives the bilinear rotation example, separates Fisher from empirical-Fisher curvature, and states the local conditions under which common GAN regularizers have convergence evidence.

    Gradients and Geometry in Generative Modeling then separates simultaneous, alternating, extragradient, unrolled, implicit, natural, and transport-pullback derivatives—and explains why positive-definite preconditioning alone does not remove game rotation.

    • No per-latent target in the basic unconditional game—the critic supplies a learned training signal, while evaluation still requires independent definitions of fidelity, coverage, conditioning, and harm
    • Loss landscape continuously shifts as both networks update
    • Stationary points, cycles, and local game behavior depend on the parameterization and update rule; a pure-strategy equilibrium need not exist in a restricted nonconvex parameter game even though mixed-strategy existence and the ideal population equilibrium are separate results
    • Small hyperparameter changes can cause dramatic differences in convergence
    • Two competing objectives must be balanced rather than minimizing a single loss

    Worked dynamical example: one game, three trajectories¤

    Consider the scalar zero-sum game

    \[ \min_\theta\max_\phi V(\theta,\phi), \qquad V(\theta,\phi)=\theta\phi. \]

    Its simultaneous gradient vector field is \((-\phi,+\theta)\). In continuous time,

    \[ \dot\theta=-\phi, \qquad \dot\phi=\theta, \qquad \frac{d}{dt}(\theta^2+\phi^2)=0. \]

    The state circles the equilibrium instead of approaching it. Simultaneous Euler updates with step size \(\eta\) give

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}\theta_{k+1}\\\phi_{k+1}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1&-\eta\\\eta&1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\theta_k\\\phi_k\end{bmatrix}, \qquad \theta_{k+1}^2+\phi_{k+1}^2 =(1+\eta^2)(\theta_k^2+\phi_k^2). \tag{GAN.1}\label{eq:gan-bilinear-simultaneous-radius} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:gan-bilinear-simultaneous-radius}\) makes the discretization failure explicit: every nonzero state spirals outward for every \(\eta>0\). If the discriminator instead uses the just-updated generator value,

    \[ \theta_{k+1}=\theta_k-\eta\phi_k, \qquad \phi_{k+1}=\phi_k+\eta\theta_{k+1}, \]

    the update matrix has determinant one and trace \(2-\eta^2\). For \(0<\eta<2\), its eigenvalues lie on the unit circle: the iterates are bounded under the corresponding invariant quadratic form but still need not converge. Thus the population equilibrium, continuous vector field, simultaneous discretization, and alternating discretization are four different claims.

    The game Jacobian at the equilibrium is

    \[ J= \begin{bmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}, \]

    with eigenvalues \(\pm i\). Purely imaginary directions expose rotation; negative real parts would be needed for local asymptotic stability of the continuous system. Neural GAN Jacobians add self-curvature, cross-player blocks, optimizer state, stochastic noise, and regularizers, but the two-dimensional calculation explains why lowering each player's instantaneous loss is not a convergence proof.

    Discrete outputs require an estimator choice¤

    For a differentiable continuous generator, write \(h_\phi(x)=\ell(D_\phi(x))\). The latent-noise construction then gives the pathwise derivative

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L(\theta) &= \mathbb E_{Z\sim P_Z}[h_\phi(G_\theta(Z))],\\ \nabla_\theta \mathcal L(\theta) &= \mathbb E_{Z\sim P_Z} [\nabla_\theta h_\phi(G_\theta(Z))]. \end{aligned} \]

    when exchanging differentiation and expectation is justified. A hard categorical sample or argmax breaks that differentiable path. Three common responses answer different mathematical problems:

    Table GAN.5. Discrete outputs require an estimator choice
    Route Generator object Gradient and boundary
    Score-function estimator explicit normalized policy \(q_\theta(x)\) unbiased under standard support and integrability conditions, but often high variance
    Continuous relaxation differentiable soft state used by the critic low-variance pathwise gradient for a relaxed game; bias appears when the deployed hard state differs
    Straight-through estimator hard forward state, surrogate backward derivative generally biased; useful behavior is empirical rather than a derivative identity

    For an explicit discrete policy and reward \(R_\phi(x)\) supplied by a fixed critic,

    \[ \nabla_\theta\mathbb E_{X\sim q_\theta}[R_\phi(X)] = \mathbb E_{X\sim q_\theta}\!\left[ R_\phi(X)\nabla_\theta\log q_\theta(X) \right]. \]

    This estimator needs tractable policy log probabilities even though the adversarial objective does not need a data likelihood. A baseline independent of the sampled action can reduce variance without changing the expectation.

    Finite check. Let \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(p)\) with \(p=\sigma(\theta)\) and rewards \(R(1)=r_1\), \(R(0)=r_0\). Then

    \[ J(\theta)=pr_1+(1-p)r_0, \qquad \frac{dJ}{d\theta}=p(1-p)(r_1-r_0). \]

    Because \(\partial_\theta\log q_\theta(X)=X-p\),

    \[ \mathbb E[R(X)(X-p)] =pr_1(1-p)+(1-p)r_0(-p) =p(1-p)(r_1-r_0), \]

    which recovers the exact derivative. The equality does not remove Monte Carlo variance, long-sequence credit assignment, critic drift, or exposure to prefixes omitted by pretraining. Discrete state space, explicit policy probability, and stable adversarial training remain separate properties.

    Stability Techniques

    Architectural:

    • Batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) — except discriminator input / generator output, per the DCGAN guidelines.
    • Spectral normalization (Miyato et al., 2018) — normalize linear operators by an estimated largest singular value. Per-layer bounds compose into a network-level upper bound only after accounting for activations, residual branches, convolution-operator estimation, and other paths; it is not an automatic exact 1-Lipschitz certificate for an arbitrary discriminator.
    • Self-attention for long-range dependencies (SAGAN, Zhang et al., 2019).

    Algorithmic:

    • Two-Time-scale Update Rule (TTUR) (Heusel et al., 2017): update the players on separated stochastic-approximation time scales. The paper proves convergence to a stationary local Nash equilibrium under assumptions on step-size sequences, noise, dynamics, and boundedness; “always use a larger discriminator learning rate” is a practical interpretation, not the theorem itself.
    • Gradient penalties — WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al., 2017), R1/R2 zero-centred GP (Mescheder et al., 2018). In the setting analyzed by Mescheder et al., the one-centred WGAN-GP construction does not generally guarantee local convergence, whereas the zero-centred penalties yield local convergence under the paper's regularity, equilibrium, and update assumptions. This is not a theorem for every architecture and optimizer.
    • Label smoothing (Salimans et al., 2016) — one-sided on real labels only.

    Hyperparameter:

    • Tune learning rates jointly with batch size, loss scale, regularization frequency, and architecture; \(10^{-4}\)-scale values are historical starting points, not a safe universal interval.
    • Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with \(\beta_1=0.5,\beta_2=0.999\) is the DCGAN setting; later recipes commonly use different moments.
    • Treat the number of critic updates per generator update as part of the method. Five critic steps is the WGAN-GP paper's baseline protocol, not a property of all Wasserstein or spectral-normalized GANs.

    GAN Variants¤

    DCGAN: Deep Convolutional GAN¤

    DCGAN (Radford, Metz & Chintala, 2016) established the foundation for stable GAN training using convolutional architectures.

    Architecture Guidelines:

    1. Replace pooling layers with strided convolutions
    2. Use batch normalization in both G and D
    3. Remove fully connected hidden layers
    4. Use ReLU in G (except output), LeakyReLU in D
    5. Use Tanh in G output

    Historical impact: DCGAN supplied a reproducible convolutional baseline and representation-learning evidence for the datasets and architectures studied. Later successful GANs retain some ideas and reject others.

    WGAN: Wasserstein GAN¤

    WGAN (Arjovsky, Chintala & Bottou, 2017) replaces the logistic classification game with a neural approximation to the Kantorovich–Rubinstein dual of Wasserstein-1 distance. The population distance has useful continuity properties under support mismatch; a finite WGAN remains an approximation whose behavior depends on critic capacity, constraint enforcement, and optimization.

    Wasserstein Distance:

    \[ W(p_r, p_g) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(p_r, p_g)} \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \gamma}[\|x - y\|] \]

    Practical Objective (with weight clipping):

    \[ \min_G \max_{D \in \mathcal{D}} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_r}[D(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[D(G(z))] \]

    where the mathematical supremum is over 1-Lipschitz functions. The original algorithm clipped every critic parameter to a bounded interval as a crude proxy; weight clipping neither characterizes nor certifies the full 1-Lipschitz function class.

    Motivations and observed behavior:

    • The critic objective can correlate with sample quality when the critic is trained sufficiently close to its constrained optimum; its raw scale also depends on constraint enforcement
    • The paper observed improved training behavior relative to its baselines, not universal stability
    • Smoother, more informative gradients under support mismatch; mode collapse can still occur because the critic, generator, optimization dynamics, and finite data remain imperfect
    • Works with diverse architectures

    WGAN Terminology

    The discriminator in WGAN is called a critic because it emits an unconstrained real score rather than a class probability. Only the optimized supremum over the admissible function class equals Wasserstein-1 distance. A finite critic value is one witness value and therefore a lower bound, not automatically the distance itself.

    WGAN-GP: Gradient Penalty¤

    WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al., 2017) replaces the original WGAN parameter clipping with a sampled gradient-norm penalty and reports improved behavior for its tested architectures and datasets.

    Key idea and boundary: Under an optimal-transport coupling and regularity assumptions, an optimal Kantorovich potential has unit directional slope along transport segments. WGAN-GP samples straight interpolants between independently drawn real and generated examples and encourages unit gradient norm there. This is a practical surrogate, not a theorem that every optimal critic has norm one almost everywhere under both endpoint distributions.

    Gradient Penalty:

    \[ \lambda \cdot \mathbb{E}_{\hat{x} \sim p_{\hat{x}}}[(\|\nabla_{\hat{x}} D(\hat{x})\|_2 - 1)^2] \]

    where \(\hat{x} = \epsilon x + (1-\epsilon)\tilde{x}\) with \(\epsilon \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\), sampling along straight lines between real and generated data.

    Implementation Details:

    • The paper used \(\lambda=10\) in its main experiments; retune it when architecture, data scale, units, or regularization frequency changes
    • Remove batch normalization from critic (interferes with gradient penalty)
    • The paper commonly used five critic iterations per generator update; this is an experimental setting to report and ablate

    Paper result — Established, protocol-bounded: Gulrajani et al. reported an Inception Score of \(7.86\pm0.07\) for their residual WGAN-GP model on CIFAR-10 and demonstrated training of deep residual critics. Compare only after matching architecture, sample count, preprocessing, and metric implementation; the number is not a family guarantee.

    Conditional GAN (cGAN)¤

    Conditional GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) extend the adversarial framework to enable controlled generation by conditioning both networks on additional information \(y\) (class labels, text, or images).

    Objective:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{cGAN}(G, D) = \mathbb{E}_{x,y \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D(x, y)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z, y}[\log(1 - D(G(z, y), y))] \]

    Applications:

    • Class-conditional generation: Generate specific digit classes in MNIST
    • Attribute manipulation: Change hair color, age, expression in face images
    • Text-to-image: Generate images matching text descriptions

    Pix2Pix: Image-to-Image Translation¤

    Pix2Pix (Isola, Zhu, Zhou & Efros, 2017) learns paired image translation with both adversarial and reconstruction losses.

    Complete Objective:

    \[ G^* = \arg\min_G \max_D \mathcal{L}_{cGAN}(G, D) + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{L1}(G) \]

    where:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{L1}(G) = \mathbb{E}_{x,y,z}[\|y - G(x, z)\|_1] \]

    and the original Pix2Pix experiments used \(\lambda=100\) to balance the terms. Its numerical meaning depends on loss reductions and data scaling.

    Architecture:

    • Generator: U-Net with skip connections that expose high-resolution input features to corresponding decoder stages; they reduce an information bottleneck but do not guarantee preservation
    • Discriminator: PatchGAN (classifies \(N \times N\) patches as real/fake)

    Applications: Edges→photos, labels→scenes, day→night, black-and-white→color

    CycleGAN: Unpaired Translation¤

    CycleGAN (Zhu, Park, Isola & Efros, 2017) enables translation between unpaired image domains using cycle consistency.

    Cycle Consistency Loss:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{cyc}(G, F) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_X}[\|F(G(x)) - x\|_1] + \mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_Y}[\|G(F(y)) - y\|_1] \]

    Total Objective:

    \[ \mathcal{L}(G, F, D_X, D_Y) = \mathcal{L}_{GAN}(G, D_Y, X, Y) + \mathcal{L}_{GAN}(F, D_X, Y, X) + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{cyc}(G, F) \]

    Unpaired marginals do not identify the intended joint correspondence. If both domains are uniform on \(\{0,1\}\), the identity maps \(G(x)=x\), \(F(y)=y\) and the swapped maps \(G(x)=1-x\), \(F(y)=1-y\) both match the marginals and achieve exact cycle consistency. Only task semantics say which correspondence is correct. In image systems, a cycle loss can likewise preserve information through imperceptible or task-irrelevant channels rather than recover the desired causal relation. Paired evaluation, semantic constraints, and application-specific failure tests are therefore required.

    Applications: Horse↔zebra, photo↔painting, summer↔winter, without paired training data

    StyleGAN: Style-Based Generator¤

    StyleGAN (Karras, Laine & Aila, 2019) introduces a style-based generator and evaluates improved control and latent geometry under its face-generation protocol.

    Key Innovations:

    1. Mapping Network: Maps \(z \in \mathcal{Z}\) to an intermediate latent space \(w \in \mathcal{W}\).
    2. Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN) (Huang & Belongie, 2017): injects style at each resolution.
    3. Stochastic Variation: per-pixel noise injection for fine-grained details.
    4. Progressive-growing training inherited from PGGAN (Karras et al., 2018): the original StyleGAN experiments used a generator and discriminator grown from low to high resolution; later StyleGAN versions did not make this a family requirement.

    W-Space Benefits:

    The original StyleGAN paper reports more favorable perceptual-path-length and linear-separability measurements in \(\mathcal W\) than in its input \(\mathcal Z\) under the evaluated models:

    • Perceptual Path Length (PPL) was lower in \(\mathcal W\) than in \(\mathcal Z\) for the paper's reported configurations. PPL measures expected perceptual change along sampled latent interpolation segments; it is not a proof of a globally smooth data manifold.
    • The paper's labeled attributes were more linearly separable under its metric and probe protocol.
    • Manipulation transfer remains an empirical property to test across samples, directions, edit strengths, and subgroups.

    The mapping network pushes the simple input distribution in \(\mathcal Z\) to a learned distribution in \(\mathcal W\). StyleGAN's perceptual-path-length and linear-separability experiments show that this can produce a more useful geometry for the evaluated generator. They do not identify a unique “data factor density,” guarantee disentanglement, or prove that coordinates correspond to independent semantic causes.

    The StyleGAN family:

    • StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020) — addresses characteristic artefacts associated with the original normalization design using weight modulation/demodulation and introduces path-length regularization. It reported FID 2.84 on FFHQ 1024² under the paper's dataset and evaluation protocol.
    • StyleGAN3 (Karras et al., 2021) — alias-free generator that reduces coordinate leakage and "texture sticking" by treating intermediate signals as continuous bandlimited functions. The paper evaluates a translation-equivariant configuration (StyleGAN3-T) and a more restrictive rotation-and-translation-equivariant configuration (StyleGAN3-R); the distinction and measured equivariance errors matter when reproducing the claim.
    • StyleGAN-XL (Sauer, Schwarz & Geiger, 2022) — scales style-based generation to diverse ImageNet classes with projected discriminators, classifier guidance, and class conditioning. Its comparisons were competitive with selected diffusion baselines under reported ImageNet protocols; “matches diffusion” is not meaningful without naming resolution, guidance, metric, and baseline.

    Worked example: select a stabilizer from evidence¤

    Assume training has good held-out precision but low recall, the discriminator separates train real from held-out real, and generator gradients remain finite. This is evidence for critic overfitting plus coverage failure—not saturation. A controlled study might:

    1. freeze architecture, optimizer, data order, and evaluation samples;
    2. add differentiable augmentation or reduce critic capacity to address the train/held-out gap;
    3. separately test an anti-collapse intervention such as packing or minibatch features;
    4. report precision/recall, FID/KID uncertainty, critic generalization, and wall-clock; and
    5. test the interaction only after estimating each main effect.

    Switching to WGAN-GP, changing five hyperparameters, and declaring success from a single FID cannot identify the cause.

    Independent practice and assessment map: training and variants¤

    Diagnose the failed object before choosing an intervention. Each answer must separate population objective, finite critic estimate, generator gradient, coupled update dynamics, and evaluation protocol.

    P1 — Derive the dynamics, choose the estimator, and isolate an intervention¤

    Use the bilinear game \(V(\theta,\phi)=\theta\phi\) as a numerical oracle, then transfer its distinctions to a generator whose outputs may be continuous or categorical.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict which observations distinguish mode dropping, critic overfitting, sigmoid saturation, and rotational dynamics when a run has low discriminator loss, oscillating player losses, finite generator gradients, and low aggregate FID. State which additional measurement can falsify each diagnosis.
    2. P1.2 — Three trajectory calculations. Starting from \((\theta_0,\phi_0)=(1,0)\) with \(\eta=1/2\), compute four steps of continuous-time rotation at matching sample times, simultaneous Euler, and the stated alternating update. Verify the simultaneous radius multiplier, derive the alternating matrix trace and determinant, and distinguish boundedness from convergence.
    3. P1.3 — Stabilizer contract table. For spectral normalization, WGAN-GP, R1/R2, TTUR, packing, and differentiable augmentation, state the controlled object, sampling location, effective units, computational cost, and theorem or empirical boundary. Mark why none alone guarantees mode coverage or global convergence.
    4. P1.4 — Objective and estimator audit. Compare logistic minimax/non-saturating, hinge, least-squares, Wasserstein, and relativistic generator/critic objectives at the level of raw scores and derivatives. For a Bernoulli generator, derive the exact score-function gradient, then contrast its target, bias, and variance with a continuous relaxation and straight-through estimator.
    5. P1.5 — Controlled intervention. Design a factorial or staged study for low held-out recall plus a widening train-versus-held-out critic gap. Freeze data order, architecture, compute, and metric samples; specify baselines, main effects, interactions, uncertainty, stopping rules, and observations that would reject critic regularization or an anti-collapse method.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Apply the study to variable-length categorical protein sequences. Define the normalized generator policy or relaxation, masking and length law, critic inputs, exposure/credit-assignment tests, sequence-level coverage measures, and evidence required before promoting an image-GAN stabilizer to this domain.

    GAN Variants: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    GAN stabilizers encode different hypotheses: Lipschitz sensitivity, local game convergence, critic overfitting, missing diversity, or gradient-estimator variance. Their assumptions, computational cost, and estimator semantics should remain visible. Even a scalar bilinear game separates equilibrium, continuous dynamics, simultaneous discretization, and alternating discretization.

    Retrieval practice: training and regularization
    1. Does spectral normalization prove an arbitrary discriminator is exactly 1-Lipschitz?
    2. What does R1 penalize, and where?
    3. Why is five critic steps not an intrinsic requirement of WGAN?
    4. What must be true before a WGAN critic loss can be interpreted as a distance estimate?
    5. In the bilinear game, why does simultaneous Euler spiral outward although the continuous trajectory preserves radius?
    6. Which additional object must be available for the score-function gradient of a discrete generator?

    Solutions. (1) No; it controls layer operators, while the network bound depends on all paths, nonlinearities, residuals, and estimation accuracy. (2) The squared input-gradient norm on real samples, centered at zero. (3) It is a paper-specific optimization protocol; other constraints and architectures have different time-scale needs. (4) The critic must be trained close enough to the constrained supremum and its constraint implementation and scale must be understood. (5) Simultaneous Euler introduces discretization error: each step multiplies squared Euclidean radius by \(1+\eta^2\). (6) A tractable normalized policy mass \(q_\theta(x)\), or at least its differentiable log probability, is needed in addition to sampled outputs and critic rewards.

    Bridge to Chapter 4. Stable optimization is not the endpoint. The next chapter asks whether the generator covers the target distribution, whether its latent coordinates support interventions, and whether the evaluation protocol can distinguish those properties.
