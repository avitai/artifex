    Flow Model Architectures¤

    Flow Model Architectures: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1's map convention. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. separate the cost of evaluating a map, inverting it, and computing its log-determinant;
    2. explain how structured affine maps and monotone scalar maps become flow primitives;
    3. derive the block-triangular Jacobian of additive and affine coupling layers;
    4. distinguish MAF and IAF by dependency direction and by the sequential direction of computation;
    5. distinguish a low-rank residual determinant from a contractive residual inverse guarantee;
    6. compare coupling, autoregressive, spline, residual, and Transformer flows by scoring and sampling cost; and
    7. identify which architectures are implemented by Artifex and which are research context only.

    Artifex provides implementations of several classical flow architectures, each with different trade-offs between expressiveness, computational efficiency, and ease of use. Research-only architectures are labeled explicitly; conceptual coverage does not imply a runtime export.

    Design primitives: maps before architectures¤

    A flow layer must answer three computational questions:

    1. Can we evaluate \(x=f(u)\)?
    2. Can we recover \(u=f^{-1}(x)\) in the direction the application needs?
    3. Can we compute \(\log|\det J_f(u)|\) or its inverse-map counterpart with a declared error model?

    These questions are independent. A map can have a cheap determinant but an expensive inverse, or an analytic inverse but a dense determinant. Named architectures are best understood as different ways of allocating those costs.

    Structured affine maps¤

    The simplest multivariate bijection is

    \[ x=Au+b, \]

    where \(A\in\mathbb R^{D\times D}\) is nonsingular. Its inverse is \(u=A^{-1}(x-b)\) and its log-volume change is \(\log|\det A|\). This already separates statistical expressivity from computational structure: an unrestricted dense \(A\) can mix every coordinate, but repeatedly factorizing it costs \(O(D^3)\) in general.

    Table FLOW.2. Structured affine maps
    Structure of \(A\) Forward map Inverse map \(\log\lvert\det A\rvert\) What is restricted?
    dense \(O(D^2)\) \(O(D^3)\) factorization, then \(O(D^2)\) per solve \(O(D^3)\) factorization no linear mixing restriction beyond nonsingularity
    triangular \(O(D^2)\) \(O(D^2)\) triangular solve \(O(D)\) from the diagonal dependency order is triangular
    diagonal \(O(D)\) \(O(D)\) \(O(D)\) no coordinate mixing
    permutation \(O(D)\) \(O(D)\) \(0\) in log absolute value reorders coordinates without reshaping density

    The dense row assumes no reusable factorization. If \(A\) is fixed across many evaluations, a cached factorization changes the amortized solve cost; during training, an unconstrained \(A\) changes and its factorization or parameterization remains part of the computation. A cheap log-determinant therefore does not imply a cheap transformation, and conversely.

    Finite worked example. Let

    \[ A=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\1&3\end{bmatrix},\qquad b=\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix},\qquad u=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Then \(x=Au+b=(3,6)^\top\) and \(\det A=6\). Solving the triangular system \(A u=x-b\) recovers \((1,2)^\top\). If \(u\) has density \(p_U\), the output density at this paired point is

    \[ \log p_X(3,6)=\log p_U(1,2)-\log 6. \]

    The minus sign is not a convention to memorize separately: \(A\) expands a unit area by a factor of six, so density per unit output area falls by that factor.

    Monotone scalar maps¤

    An elementwise transform

    \[ x_i=h_i(u_i;\theta_i) \]

    has a diagonal Jacobian and

    \[ \log|\det J_f(u)|=\sum_{i=1}^{D}\log|h_i'(u_i;\theta_i)|. \]

    A continuous scalar map is invertible on its declared domain if it is strictly monotone and maps that domain onto the declared codomain. Positivity of the derivative is a convenient sufficient construction, but it does not by itself state the range or the numerical cost of inversion.

    Table FLOW.3. Monotone scalar maps
    Scalar construction How monotonicity is obtained Forward/inverse behavior Main limitation
    affine \(h(u)=e^a u+b\) positive scale both analytic one layer only shifts and rescales
    positive-weight monotone network monotone activations and constrained paths forward analytic; inverse commonly uses root finding weight constraints can limit the convenient parameterization
    integrated positive derivative \(h(u)=b+\int_0^u [\operatorname{softplus}(g(s))+\epsilon],ds\) forward uses quadrature; inverse nests root finding around integration numerical quadrature and root tolerances enter the density computation
    sum-of-squares polynomial integrate a nonnegative polynomial derivative, with strictness handled explicitly polynomial forward and derivative; inverse generally uses root finding polynomial degree and tails constrain practical behavior
    monotone rational-quadratic spline positive bin widths, heights, and knot derivatives analytic within the selected bin, including a quadratic inverse requires tail and boundary conventions plus bin lookup

    Monotone neural transformations, sum-of-squares flows, and rational-quadratic splines supply increasingly flexible scalar maps (Huang et al., 2018; Jaini et al., 2019; Wehenkel & Louppe, 2019; Durkan et al., 2019). Applied independently, however, scalar maps cannot create dependence between initially independent coordinates. Coupling and autoregressive layers add conditioners that make each scalar map depend on other coordinates while preserving a structured Jacobian.

    This ladder prevents a common category error. A spline is a scalar transformation; a masked autoregressive layer is a dependency structure; a stack plus base distribution is a normalized model; and the same stack can instead parameterize a variational posterior. None of these labels alone determines the full system.

    1. NICE: Pioneering Coupling Layers¤

    NICE (Dinh, Krueger & Bengio, 2014) — Non-linear Independent Components Estimation — introduced additive coupling layers that made normalizing flows practical for high-dimensional data.

    Coupling Layer Mechanism:

    Given input \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\), partition into \((\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2)\):

    \[ \begin{align} \mathbf{y}_1 &= \mathbf{x}_1 \\ \mathbf{y}_2 &= \mathbf{x}_2 + m(\mathbf{x}_1) \end{align} \]

    where \(m\) can be any arbitrary function (typically a neural network).

    Key Properties:

    • Volume-preserving: \(\log|\det(\mathbf{J})| = 0\) (determinant is exactly 1)
    • Efficient inverse: \(\mathbf{x}_1 = \mathbf{y}_1\), \(\mathbf{x}_2 = \mathbf{y}_2 - m(\mathbf{y}_1)\)
    • No Jacobian computation: The triangular structure makes the determinant trivial
    • Flexible coupling function: evaluating the triangular determinant does not require the internal Jacobian of \(m\), but the runtime and memory of the layer still depend on the size and architecture of \(m\)

    When to Use NICE:

    • Fast forward and inverse computations required
    • Volume-preserving transformations are acceptable
    • Starting point for understanding coupling layers
    • Lower-dimensional problems (hundreds of dimensions)

    2. RealNVP: Adding Scale for Expressiveness¤

    RealNVP (Dinh, Sohl-Dickstein & Bengio, 2017 — ICLR) — Real-valued Non-Volume Preserving — extends NICE with affine coupling layers:

    \[ \begin{align} \mathbf{y}_1 &= \mathbf{x}_1 \\ \mathbf{y}_2 &= \mathbf{x}_2 \odot \exp(s(\mathbf{x}_1)) + t(\mathbf{x}_1) \end{align} \]

    where \(s(\cdot)\) and \(t(\cdot)\) are neural networks outputting scale and translation, and \(\odot\) denotes element-wise multiplication.

    Key Properties:

    • Tractable Jacobian: \(\log|\det(\mathbf{J})| = \sum_i s_i(\mathbf{x}_1)\)
    • Efficient inverse: the triangular inverse remains analytic.
    \[ \begin{align} \mathbf{x}_1 &= \mathbf{y}_1 \\ \mathbf{x}_2 &= (\mathbf{y}_2 - t(\mathbf{y}_1)) \odot \exp(-s(\mathbf{y}_1)) \end{align} \]
    • Alternating masks: Alternate which dimensions are transformed across layers
    • No determinant of the internal scale/translation Jacobians is needed: gradients still flow through \(s\) and \(t\) into all of their parameters, but the block-triangular coupling Jacobian makes the log-determinant depend only on the emitted scale values. More capable conditioners can therefore be used without computing their full Jacobian determinant, although their ordinary evaluation cost still matters.

    Multi-Scale Architecture:

    RealNVP introduced hierarchical structure that revolutionized flow-based modeling:

    1. Squeeze operation: Reshape \(s \times s \times c\) tensors into \(\frac{s}{2} \times \frac{s}{2} \times 4c\)
    2. Factor out: After several coupling layers, factor out half the channels to the prior
    3. Continue processing: Transform remaining channels at higher resolution

    This reduces repeated computation on factored-out variables and supports higher-dimensional image experiments, while the exact memory and runtime depend on the number of scales, channels, conditioners, and implementation.

    When to Use RealNVP:

    • Need both fast sampling and density estimation
    • Working with continuous data, especially images
    • Image generation tasks at moderate to high resolution
    • Moderate-dimensional data (hundreds to thousands of dimensions)

    3. Glow: Learnable Permutations¤

    Glow (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018 — NeurIPS) extends RealNVP with three key block-level innovations. Artifex retains these Glow blocks as a single-scale image flow baseline rather than the paper's squeeze/split multi-scale hierarchy.

    Glow Block Architecture:

    Each flow step combines three layers:

    1. ActNorm (Activation Normalization):

    Per-channel affine transformation with trainable parameters:

    \[ y_{i,j,c} = s_c \cdot x_{i,j,c} + b_c \]
    • Data-dependent initialization: normalize first minibatch to zero mean, unit variance
    • Enables training with batch size 1 (critical for high-resolution images)
    • \(\log|\det J| = H \cdot W \cdot \sum_c \log|s_c|\) for \(H \times W\) spatial dimensions

    2. Invertible 1×1 Convolution:

    Learned linear mixing of channels using invertible matrix \(\mathbf{W}\):

    \[ \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{W} \mathbf{x} \]
    • Replaces fixed permutations with learned channel mixing
    • LU decomposition: \(\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{P} \cdot \mathbf{L} \cdot (\mathbf{U} + \text{diag}(\mathbf{s}))\)
    • Determinant: \(\log|\det \mathbf{W}| = \sum_i \log|s_i|\) (reduced to \(O(c)\))
    • The learned mixing was an empirical component of the paper's complete Glow system; an isolated likelihood gain is protocol- and ablation-dependent

    3. Affine Coupling Layer:

    Similar to RealNVP but with the above improvements.

    When to Use Glow in Artifex:

    • Need a fixed-size image flow with learnable channel mixing
    • Want an analytic model-density expression with image-shaped forward and inverse transforms
    • Need ActNorm plus invertible 1×1 convolutions as a stronger image baseline
    • Want a deeper single-scale alternative to RealNVP on image tensors

    Artifex Runtime Note

    The retained implementation is configured through image_shape and blocks_per_scale. If you need the original paper's multi-scale squeeze/split hierarchy, you will need a different runtime.

    4. MAF: Masked Autoregressive Flow¤

    MAF (Papamakarios, Pavlakou & Murray, 2017 — NeurIPS) turns a continuous autoregressive density into an invertible triangular transformation. Let \(x\) denote data and \(z\) a standard-normal base vector. One layer declares

    \[ p(x_i\mid x_{<i}) =\mathcal N\!\left(x_i;\mu_i(x_{<i}),\exp(2\alpha_i(x_{<i}))\right), \]

    which has the data-to-base map

    \[ z_i=(x_i-\mu_i(x_{<i}))\exp[-\alpha_i(x_{<i})]. \]

    The map is triangular: \(z_i\) depends only on \(x_{\le i}\). Its Jacobian is generally not diagonal, because \(\mu_i\) and \(\alpha_i\) can depend on earlier \(x_j\); only its diagonal is needed for the determinant:

    \[ \frac{\partial z_i}{\partial x_i}=\exp[-\alpha_i(x_{<i})], \qquad \log\left|\det\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right| =-\sum_{i=1}^{D}\alpha_i(x_{<i}). \]

    The conditional family must make the scalar inverse explicit. A single Gaussian conditional is unimodal, while stacking autoregressive layers with permutations can create substantially richer joints. Mixture conditionals are another route to flexibility, but a generic mixture CDF need not provide the analytic bijection required by a classical flow.

    Why MADE makes density evaluation parallel¤

    The functions \(\mu_i\) and \(\alpha_i\) are commonly produced by a MADE network (Germain et al., ICML 2015). For a complete observed \(x\), a mask guarantees that output pair \((\mu_i,\alpha_i)\) can see \(x_{<i}\) but not \(x_{\ge i}\). All output pairs can therefore be computed by one feed-forward network evaluation. The binary MADE construction and four-variable mask audit derives the mask inequalities and multiplies the masks to verify every permitted path.

    For the order \((1,2,3)\), the resulting conditional dependency graph is:

    The graph describes conditioner dependencies, not the full computational graph of a particular hidden network. In particular, \(x_i\) enters the scalar transformation for \(z_i\) directly but is forbidden from the network that emits its own \((\mu_i,\alpha_i)\).

    Given arbitrary observed \(x\), the sequence of operations is:

    1. evaluate the masked conditioner once to obtain every \((\mu_i,\alpha_i)\);
    2. compute every \(z_i\) elementwise in parallel;
    3. add the base log-density and the triangular log-determinant.

    Sampling reverses the map:

    \[ x_i=\mu_i(x_{<i})+z_i\exp[\alpha_i(x_{<i})]. \]

    Now \(x_{<i}\) must exist before the parameters for coordinate \(i\) are valid. A basic sampler therefore exposes one coordinate at a time and may require \(D\) masked-network evaluations per layer. Specialized caching can reduce repeated arithmetic, but it cannot remove the declared dependency chain without changing the model or using an approximate parallel inversion.

    Table FLOW.4. Why MADE makes density evaluation parallel
    MAF task Known at the start Direction Network-evaluation structure
    Score arbitrary data all \(x\) \(x\to z\) one masked evaluation per layer; coordinate arithmetic is parallel
    Generate a sample all \(z\), no \(x\) prefix \(z\to x\) sequential in the basic construction
    Score a generated sample sampled \(x\) and recorded transforms already available from generation log-density can be accumulated while sampling

    “One masked evaluation” is a count of network evaluations, not \(O(1)\) arithmetic in dimension, memory, or wall-clock time.

    When to use MAF:

    • Density estimation is the primary goal
    • Sampling speed is less critical
    • Tabular or low-to-moderate dimensional data
    • Need highly expressive transformations
    • All dimensions should interact

    5. IAF: Inverse Autoregressive Flow¤

    IAF (Kingma et al., 2016 — NeurIPS) uses the same kind of masked conditioner but attaches its dependencies to the base vector. With \(z\sim p_0\) already available, define

    \[ x_i=\mu_i(z_{<i})+z_i\exp[\alpha_i(z_{<i})]. \]

    All conditioner inputs \(z\) are known before the layer runs. A MADE-style network can emit all parameter pairs in one evaluation, after which every \(x_i\) is computed elementwise. The same pass supplies the diagonal Jacobian terms needed to evaluate the density of this generated sample. This is why IAF is useful in amortized variational inference: the algorithm needs samples from the approximate posterior and their densities.

    The inverse problem is different. Given an arbitrary external \(x\), recovering \(z_1\) makes \(z_1\) available to compute the parameters for \(z_2\), and so on. Arbitrary-data density evaluation is therefore sequential in the basic IAF construction.

    Table FLOW.5. 5. IAF: Inverse Autoregressive Flow
    IAF task Known at the start Direction Network-evaluation structure
    Generate a sample and its density all \(z\) \(z\to x\) one masked evaluation per layer; coordinate arithmetic is parallel
    Score arbitrary external data all \(x\), no \(z\) prefix \(x\to z\) sequential in the basic construction

    MAF and IAF are not labels for one universal forward direction¤

    Table FLOW.6. MAF and IAF are not labels for one universal forward direction
    Question MAF IAF
    Conditioner reads earlier data \(x_{<i}\) earlier base variables \(z_{<i}\)
    Parallel direction arbitrary-data scoring, \(x\to z\) sampling and sample scoring, \(z\to x\)
    Sequential direction sampling, \(z\to x\) arbitrary-data scoring, \(x\to z\)
    Typical role normalized density model flexible variational posterior transformation
    Shared fact triangular bijection with tractable diagonal triangular bijection with tractable diagonal

    Calling one map “forward” is unsafe unless the author first declares whether forward means base-to-data, model execution, or density evaluation. This book always names the coordinates and direction explicitly.

    When to use IAF:

    • Fast sampling is the primary goal
    • Density is needed mainly for samples produced by the same transformation
    • Variational inference (amortized inference in VAEs)
    • Settings where the base-to-sample direction must be parallel within each layer, after measuring complete latency

    A MADE density estimator is not automatically a flow

    Binary MADE defines normalized Bernoulli conditionals but is not a bijection between equal-dimensional continuous spaces. A continuous MADE with arbitrary mixture conditionals can define a tractable autoregressive density without supplying an analytic inverse. MAF and IAF require scalar transformations whose inverse and Jacobian diagonal are available in the needed direction. The dependency mask is reusable; the probabilistic object is not interchangeable.

    6. Neural Spline Flows¤

    Neural Spline Flows (Durkan, Bekasov, Murray & Papamakarios, 2019 — NeurIPS) use monotonic rational-quadratic splines to create highly expressive yet tractable transformations.

    Rational-Quadratic Spline Transform:

    Each spline maps interval \([-B, B]\) to itself using \(K\) rational-quadratic segments, parameterized by:

    • \(K+1\) knot positions \(\{(x^{(k)}, y^{(k)})\}\)
    • \(K+1\) derivative values \(\{\delta^{(k)}\}\)

    Within segment \(k\), the transformation applies a ratio of quadratic polynomials.

    Key Properties:

    • Strict monotonicity: positive bin widths, heights, and derivatives make each scalar spline invertible on its declared interval, with the boundary/tail rule completing the global map
    • Smooth derivatives: No discontinuities (unlike piecewise-linear)
    • Closed-form operations: Forward evaluation, analytic inverse (quadratic equation), closed-form derivative
    • Piecewise-monotone expressivity: increasing the number of bins can approximate richer scalar monotone transforms, but the required count depends on tail bounds, conditioner capacity, data, and optimisation; 8 or 16 bins are experimental settings, not universal sufficiency results

    Compared to alternatives:

    • vs Affine: a spline emits widths, heights, and derivatives for several bins rather than only a shift and scale; the exact parameter count depends on bin count and boundary convention
    • vs Neural Autoregressive Flows: No iterative root-finding needed
    • vs Flow++: No bisection algorithms required
    • vs Piecewise-linear: Smooth derivatives improve optimization

    Results:

    • Established historical result: the Neural Spline Flows paper reports competitive log likelihood on tabular and image-density benchmarks under its architectures and preprocessing. Numbers such as CIFAR-10 BPD are meaningful only with the dequantisation, split, parameter count, and evaluation convention attached.

    When to Use Neural Spline Flows:

    • Maximum expressiveness with tractability
    • Density estimation on complex distributions
    • Want fewer parameters than Glow
    • Need smooth, differentiable transformations

    7. Residual transformations: determinant and inverse design¤

    A residual transformation has the form

    \[ f(u)=u+F(u). \]

    The identity skip connection does not make an arbitrary residual block invertible. There are at least two distinct ways to make residual transformations useful in flows, and they solve different problems:

    1. give \(J_F\) a low-rank factorization so its determinant is cheap, then establish invertibility for the particular parameterization; or
    2. make \(F\) contractive so invertibility follows globally, then estimate the generally dense log-determinant.

    Low-rank route: the determinant lemma reduces dimension, not assumptions¤

    Let

    \[ F(u)=Vh(Wu+b), \]

    where \(W\in\mathbb R^{M\times D}\), \(V\in\mathbb R^{D\times M}\), \(M\ll D\), and \(h\) acts elementwise. With \(D_h=\operatorname{diag}(h'(Wu+b))\),

    \[ J_f(u)=I_D+V D_h W. \]

    The matrix determinant lemma gives

    \[ \det(I_D+V D_h W)=\det(I_M+D_h W V). \tag{FLOW.1}\label{eq:flow-low-rank-residual-determinant} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:flow-low-rank-residual-determinant}\) replaces a \(D\times D\) determinant with an \(M\times M\) determinant. This can greatly reduce determinant cost when \(M\ll D\). It does not prove that the determinant is nonzero everywhere, supply an analytic inverse, or guarantee that numerical inversion is stable.

    The one-unit case \(M=1\) is the planar flow introduced for variational inference by Rezende & Mohamed (2015):

    \[ f(u)=u+v\tanh(w^\top u+b), \]

    with

    \[ \det J_f(u)=1+\bigl(1-\tanh^2(w^\top u+b)\bigr)w^\top v. \]

    For this activation, \(w^\top v>-1\) is a convenient sufficient condition for strict monotonicity along the affected direction and hence invertibility. The condition is not implied by low rank; it must be enforced, for example by reparameterizing the component of \(v\) parallel to \(w\). Sylvester flows replace the single-unit bottleneck by a structured multi-unit residual block while retaining a reduced determinant (van den Berg et al., UAI 2018).

    Worked two-dimensional planar map. Choose

    \[ w=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix},\qquad v=\begin{bmatrix}1/2\\1\end{bmatrix},\qquad b=0. \]

    Then

    \[ f(u_1,u_2)= \left(u_1+\tfrac12\tanh u_1,\;u_2+\tanh u_1\right), \]

    and

    \[ \det J_f(u)=1+\tfrac12\operatorname{sech}^2(u_1)>0. \]

    At \(u=(0,1)\), the map returns \(x=(0,1)\) but locally expands area by \(3/2\), so the paired output log-density subtracts \(\log(3/2)\). To invert a general \(x\), first solve the strictly increasing scalar equation \(x_1=u_1+\tfrac12\tanh u_1\), then set \(u_2=x_2-\tanh u_1\). The determinant is analytic even though the inverse usually is not.

    Counterexample: rank one is not an inverse certificate

    In one dimension, \(f(u)=u-2\tanh u\) is an identity plus a rank-one residual, yet \(f'(0)=-1\) and \(f'\) changes sign. The map is not globally one-to-one. The determinant lemma evaluates a local volume factor efficiently; it does not turn an arbitrary bottleneck residual network into a bijection.

    Table FLOW.7. Low-rank route: the determinant lemma reduces dimension, not assumptions
    Residual route What makes the determinant tractable? What makes the map invertible? Typical inverse
    planar/Sylvester-style low rank determinant lemma reduces \(D\) dimensions to bottleneck dimension \(M\) case-specific parameter constraints analytic only in special cases; otherwise structured numerical solve
    contractive residual flow convergent log-determinant power series, often with trace estimation a global Lipschitz constant below one Banach fixed-point iteration

    The comparison is architectural, not chronological: neither route is a universally superior replacement for the other. Low rank limits the determinant computation but can bottleneck interactions; contraction permits a dense residual Jacobian but constrains Lipschitz behavior and introduces iterative numerical work.

    Contractive route: a global inverse with estimated volume¤

    Invertible residual networks and residual flows (Behrmann et al., ICML 2019; Chen et al., NeurIPS 2019) instead constrain \(g:\mathbb R^D\to\mathbb R^D\) in

    \[ f(u)=u+g(u) \]

    to have a global Lipschitz constant \(L<1\). Given \(x\), define \(T_x(u)=x-g(u)\). This map is also an \(L\)-contraction, so Banach's fixed-point theorem gives a unique solution of

    \[ u_{k+1}=x-g(u_k),\qquad u_*=x-g(u_*),\qquad f(u_*)=x. \]

    For any initialization, the error decreases geometrically; one useful a posteriori bound is

    \[ \lVert u_k-u_*\rVert \leq \frac{L^k}{1-L}\lVert u_1-u_0\rVert. \]

    In a multilayer residual network, controlling each weight operator and using activations with known Lipschitz constants can bound the product for the complete residual branch. Spectral normalization estimates and rescales operator norms, but a finite power-iteration estimate is not an exact certificate unless its approximation error is controlled. Moreover, blindly forcing every matrix to norm \(c\) is different from projecting only matrices whose norm exceeds a bound.

    The residual Jacobian is generally dense. Under the contraction condition, the matrix log series converges:

    \[ \log\det(I+J_g) =\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{k}\operatorname{tr}(J_g^k). \]

    For a probe \(v\) with \(\mathbb E[vv^\top]=I\),

    \[ \mathbb E[v^\top J_g^k v]=\operatorname{tr}(J_g^k), \]

    so automatic differentiation can estimate each trace without materializing the full Jacobian. Truncating the infinite series gives a biased log-determinant approximation. Randomized Russian-roulette truncation can remove that truncation bias when the survival probabilities and summability conditions justify exchanging the random sum and expectation; finite expected work and finite variance are additional properties to check, not automatic consequences of the name.

    Sufficient is not necessary

    Contractivity is a convenient global certificate, not a characterization of every invertible residual map. For example, \(f(u)=2u\) is invertible even though its residual branch \(g(u)=u\) has Lipschitz constant one. Conversely, a small determinant-estimation error does not certify that an unconstrained residual map is globally one-to-one.

    Use this route when dense coordinate interaction is worth iterative inversion and stochastic or truncated density computation. Report the norm constraint, power-iteration accuracy, inverse stopping rule, trace-probe distribution and count, series truncation rule, and resulting numerical error.

    8. Transformer autoregressive flows: TarFlow and successors¤

    TarFlow (Zhai et al., ICML 2025) applies masked autoregressive flow transformations to image patches with Transformer conditioners and alternates the autoregressive direction across blocks. It is a classical likelihood-based normalizing flow, not a flow-matching ODE. The distinction matters operationally:

    • training and density evaluation can obtain all conditional parameters for a block in a masked parallel evaluation;
    • inverse sampling must reveal autoregressive patches or groups sequentially;
    • Gaussian noise augmentation and post-training denoising in the reported image system change how raw likelihood and perceptual sample quality should be interpreted; and
    • "one inverse call" in an API does not mean one parallel neural evaluation.

    STARFlow (Gu et al., 2025 preprint) scales the TarFlow idea in an autoencoder latent space with a deep–shallow design and guidance. It retains a normalizing-flow objective in its continuous modeled space, while the external codec introduces representation loss and a separate data-space semantics. FARMER (Zheng et al., CVPR 2026) is a 2026 autoregressive-flow-over-pixels system; its accepted result is evidence for that complete architecture and protocol, not proof that all Transformer flows dominate other families.

    Evidence labels

    Peer-reviewed research: TarFlow (ICML 2025) and FARMER (CVPR 2026). Preprint: STARFlow. The page metadata records the verification date. These models are curriculum coverage, not claims about Artifex runtime support.

    Worked example: the RealNVP determinant does not differentiate the conditioner¤

    Take \(x=(x_1,x_2)\) and

    \[ y_1=x_1,\qquad y_2=x_2 e^{s(x_1)}+t(x_1). \]

    Its Jacobian is

    \[ J=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0\\ x_2e^{s(x_1)}s'(x_1)+t'(x_1) & e^{s(x_1)} \end{bmatrix}, \]

    so \(\log|\det J|=s(x_1)\). The derivatives \(s'\) and \(t'\) are present in the lower-left block but disappear from the triangular determinant. During optimization, however, \(s(x_1)\) and \(t(x_1)\) still depend on their neural-network parameters, so ordinary backpropagation absolutely does update both conditioners. "No conditioner Jacobian determinant" does not mean "no conditioner gradient."

    If \(s(x_1)=\log 2\) and \(t(x_1)=x_1\) at \(x=(1,3)\), then \(y=(1,7)\) and the forward log-determinant is \(\log 2\). The inverse returns \(x_2=(7-1)/2=3\) and contributes \(-\log2\).

    Independent practice and assessment map: flow architectures¤

    Treat the scalar transform, dependency structure, inverse algorithm, log-determinant algorithm, conditioner, and complete probabilistic role as separate objects. Count serial network evaluations as well as arithmetic.

    P1 — Design and falsify a tractable flow architecture¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each claim follows from the architecture name alone: a triangular Jacobian makes both map directions parallel; an arbitrary residual block is invertible; an analytic derivative implies an analytic inverse; a masked autoregressive density is automatically a flow; a one-call TarFlow API is one parallel neural evaluation; ActNorm uses every minibatch's statistics; and a cheap determinant makes the complete layer cheap. For each false implication, identify the missing dependency, inverse certificate, boundary rule, or systems measurement.
    2. P1.2 — Primitive cost audit. For a dense, triangular, diagonal, and permutation \(4\times4\) affine map, calculate forward, solve, and log-determinant work with and without a cached factorization. Then specify a monotone rational-quadratic spline's domain, tail rule, positive parameters, bin lookup, derivative, and inverse. Explain which costs come from the primitive and which come from its conditioner.
    3. P1.3 — Coupling derivation and gradient check. For a two-by-two affine coupling layer with declared \(s(x_1)\) and \(t(x_1)\), derive the full Jacobian, inverse, and log-determinant. Evaluate a finite point, then differentiate the likelihood with respect to parameters inside both \(s\) and \(t\). Explain why their input derivatives disappear from the determinant while their parameter gradients do not disappear from training.
    4. P1.4 — MAF/IAF execution trace. Construct a three-variable masked Gaussian layer. For arbitrary-data scoring and base-to-data sampling, list the known prefixes, conditioner calls, scalar operations, and available log-density terms for MAF and IAF. Repeat for a generated sample whose transform trace was retained. State which results depend on caching and why neither direction is constant-cost in dimension.
    5. P1.5 — Residual-flow certificate. For \(f(u)=u+v\tanh(w^\top u+b)\), use the determinant lemma to reduce the Jacobian determinant and construct one parameter setting that loses injectivity. Separately, for \(f(u)=u+g(u)\) with \(\operatorname{Lip}(g)=L<1\), derive a fixed-point inverse error bound and a truncated trace-series estimate. Record which errors are deterministic, stochastic, biased, or certification errors.
    6. P1.6 — Architecture and runtime matrix. Compare NICE, RealNVP, Glow blocks, MAF, IAF, splines, low-rank residual flows, contractive residual flows, and TarFlow by map direction, serial depth, determinant method, inverse method, support assumptions, numerical error, and conditioner cost. Mark each as an Artifex runtime implementation or research context using executable API evidence rather than the prose label alone.
    7. P1.7 — Transfer. Design either an equivariant molecular-coordinate flow or a bounded scientific-parameter posterior flow. Declare the scalar primitive, dependency structure, mixing, support transform, inverse requirement, determinant oracle, symmetry or boundary condition, and deployed query. Give a matched ablation that distinguishes transformation expressivity from conditioner capacity and numerical tolerances.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Architecture design allocates three different costs: map evaluation, inversion, and log-volume computation. Structured affine and scalar maps make the primitive costs visible. Coupling flows buy parallel forward and inverse maps with partial updates; MAF and IAF exchange which direction is sequential; splines improve scalar expressivity; low-rank residual flows reduce determinant dimension but need a separate inverse argument; contractive residual flows obtain that argument while accepting iterative inversion and log-determinant estimation. Transformer flows scale autoregressive conditioners but do not erase their dependency order.

    Retrieval practice: discrete flow architectures
    1. Why may \(s\) and \(t\) be arbitrary neural networks in an affine coupling layer?
    2. Which direction is sequential for MAF density evaluation and sampling?
    3. Why is TarFlow not a one-NFE flow-matching model?
    4. What does ActNorm's data-dependent initialization change after the first batch?
    5. Why does a low-rank residual Jacobian not guarantee an invertible residual map?
    6. How can a scalar map be analytically differentiable yet require numerical inversion?

    Solutions. (1) The unchanged partition makes the complete layer triangular and analytically invertible; only emitted values enter the diagonal determinant. (2) MAF evaluates density in one masked-network pass per layer and samples sequentially; IAF reverses that computational trade-off under the stated parameterization. (3) It is an autoregressive normalizing flow whose inverse exposes patches/groups sequentially; NFE terminology for an ODE vector field would conceal that cost. (4) It initializes learnable affine parameters; subsequent batches do not recompute batch statistics as BatchNorm would. (5) The determinant lemma only reduces the size of a local determinant; that determinant may still vanish or change sign, and a global inverse needs additional constraints. (6) Its forward formula and derivative may be explicit while the equation \(h(u)=x\) has no closed-form solution, so inversion uses a root finder.
