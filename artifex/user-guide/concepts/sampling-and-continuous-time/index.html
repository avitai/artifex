    Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Objectives and Statistical Distances

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • SMC 1 — Sequential Targets and Feynman–Kac Semantics

    • Optimization and Learning Dynamics for Generative Models

    Training specifies a distribution, energy, score, transition, or vector field; a sampler turns that object into draws. Those draws are usually an intermediate object: an estimator turns them into an answer to a declared query. This chapter develops Monte Carlo estimation and uncertainty, exact sampling baselines, variance reduction, importance methods, MCMC, Langevin dynamics, ODE/SDE transport, numerical error, and Schrödinger bridges.

    After this foundation, Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods develops interacting populations across target sequences. It reuses the importance corrections, invariant mutation kernels, and uncertainty distinctions established here.

    Reading routes

    Beginner: query → Monte Carlo average → direct sampling → Markov chains → MH → Gaussian worked example.

    Practitioner: add valid error bars, variance reduction, Gibbs and Metropolis-within-Gibbs design, modern MCMC diagnostics, HMC warmup, solver reporting, and target-versus-numerical error.

    Research: study randomized quasi-Monte Carlo, adaptive and pseudo-marginal kernels, transdimensional moves, stochastic-gradient and kinetic MCMC, multiscale HMC, coupled levels, the Gaussian ULA invariant law, reverse-time drift, and continuity-equation likelihood.

    One Target, Four Ways to Reach It¤

    Sampling methods differ in what they can access and what they return. Keep one target law \(\pi\) fixed while comparing the routes:

    The output boxes are deliberately different. A weighted proposal is not an independent target draw; a chain with the right invariant law may not have mixed; and an ODE endpoint inherits learned-field and solver error.

    • Target error

      Does the mathematical procedure target the intended probability law?

    • Finite-compute error

      Are proposals degenerate, chains unmixed, or particles impoverished?

    • Discretization error

      Does a finite step size change the stationary law or endpoint path?

    • Numerical error

      Do precision, tolerances, unstable ratios, or implementation details alter the calculation?

    Learning Objectives¤

    • distinguish independent sampling, importance weighting, and Markov-chain sampling;
    • separate a target law, a query, a random estimator, and its reported uncertainty;
    • derive the iid Monte Carlo variance and state the conditions behind a normal error interval;
    • compare Rao–Blackwellization, control variates, antithetic coupling, common random numbers, randomized quasi-Monte Carlo, and multilevel coupling;
    • identify whether a procedure returns target draws, weighted particles, an expectation estimate, or a normalizing-constant estimate;
    • distinguish time homogeneity, invariance, uniqueness, distributional convergence, path-average convergence, and finite mixing;
    • derive Metropolis–Hastings acceptance and state its support requirements;
    • construct Gibbs, blocked, collapsed, slice, and Metropolis-within-Gibbs transitions without confusing acceptance with exploration;
    • distinguish warmup, adaptation, initialization bias, retained sampling, and a theoretical mixing time;
    • derive HMC with one mass-matrix convention and interpret rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\), bulk/tail ESS, MCSE, and divergences;
    • separate exact-gradient MCMC from fixed-step and stochastic-gradient approximations;
    • explain dimension matching in reversible-jump MCMC and distinguish simulated annealing from parallel tempering;
    • explain why ULA has no acceptance rate;
    • separate target-distribution error from discretization and mixing error;
    • derive continuity and Fokker–Planck equations at a conceptual level;
    • explain probability-flow ODEs, stochastic transport, and numerical NFE;
    • identify which diagnostics apply to which sampler.

    Chapter Map and Canonical Continuations¤

    This chapter is the mathematical spine for sampling in the book. It owns the family-independent objects: a target law, a statistical query, a random or deterministic estimator, a transition kernel, a continuous-time process, and an error budget. Later chapters do not replace these definitions. They specialize them to a model family, state space, conditioning mechanism, or experimental claim.

    That division prevents two opposite mistakes. Copying the same foundation into every family chapter invites notation drift and contradictory assumptions. Moving every detail into Foundations would erase the model-specific questions that make a method useful. The rule is therefore:

    Derive a reusable mathematical contract here; derive its consequences again where a particular generative model changes the available objects, approximation, or failure mode.

    A first pass needs only five questions

    If sampling is new, leave the continuation map closed. For each method ask: What distribution is the target? What does the procedure return? Which query will be estimated? What makes the answer exact or asymptotically valid? What can go wrong at finite compute? Follow the Beginner reading route at the top of the page, work the finite examples, and return to the map after Section 10.

    Monte Carlo Begins with a Query¤

    A collection of random draws is not yet a statistical answer. Begin with the quantity to be computed. For a probability law \(\pi\) and an integrable observable \(f\), the query

    \[ \mu=\mathbb E_{X\sim\pi}[f(X)] \]

    might represent a generated image's mean intensity, the probability that a sample satisfies a constraint, a posterior moment, an ELBO term, or a metric averaged over generator noise. If \(X_1,\ldots,X_N\) are independent draws from \(\pi\), the ordinary Monte Carlo estimator is

    \[ \widehat\mu_N=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N f(X_i). \]

    When \(\sigma_f^2=\operatorname{Var}_\pi[f(X)]<\infty\),

    \[ \mathbb E[\widehat\mu_N]=\mu, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_N)=\frac{\sigma_f^2}{N}. \]

    The \(N^{-1/2}\) root-mean-square rate is a statement about the number of independent evaluations. It is not a dimension-free difficulty theorem: \(\sigma_f^2\), evaluation cost, tail behavior, and the number of samples needed to discover a rare region can all deteriorate with dimension.

    What an ordinary Monte Carlo error bar assumes¤

    The iid central limit theorem gives

    \[ \sqrt N\,(\widehat\mu_N-\mu) \xrightarrow{d}\mathcal N(0,\sigma_f^2) \]

    under finite-variance regularity. Estimate the variance with Bessel's correction,

    \[ s_f^2=\frac1{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^N \left(f(X_i)-\widehat\mu_N\right)^2, \qquad \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\widehat\mu_N)=\frac{s_f}{\sqrt N}. \]

    The familiar interval

    \[ \widehat\mu_N\ \pm\ z_{1-\alpha/2}\frac{s_f}{\sqrt N} \]

    is therefore asymptotic, not an exact finite-sample confidence interval. It can be badly calibrated for a small \(N\), infinite or very large variance, rare-event indicators, an adaptively chosen stopping time, or dependent samples treated as iid. For MCMC, use a dependence-aware long-run variance or effective sample size; for repeated randomized quasi-Monte Carlo, use independent randomizations; for a deterministic quadrature rule, there is no ordinary sampling variance to estimate.

    Three errors that one standard error cannot combine

    Monte Carlo error is variation caused by finite random computation under a fixed mathematical target and observable. It does not measure model misspecification, learned-parameter uncertainty, dataset sampling error, discretization bias, or floating-point error. A defensible experiment names each error source and reports an estimator appropriate to it.

    Worked finite example: an exact answer can occur by accident¤

    Let \(X\) take values \(-1,0,2\) with probabilities \(1/4,1/2,1/4\), and let \(f(X)=X^2\). Exact summation gives

    \[ \mu=\frac14(1)+\frac12(0)+\frac14(4)=\frac54, \]

    and

    \[ \sigma_f^2 =\left[\frac14(1)+\frac14(16)\right]-\left(\frac54\right)^2 =\frac{43}{16}. \]

    Suppose four independent draws happen to be \((2,0,-1,0)\). Their transformed values are \((4,0,1,0)\), so \(\widehat\mu_4=5/4\), exactly the true answer. But the estimated variance is \(s_f^2=43/12\) and the estimated standard error is \(\sqrt{43/48}\approx0.946\). The point estimate's equality is a coincidence; the realized sample does not justify zero uncertainty. Repeating the experiment with fresh random streams tests Monte Carlo variability, while comparing with the finite exact sum tests correctness.

    1. Direct, Rejection, and Importance Sampling¤

    Direct sampling is a pushforward construction¤

    Direct ancestral sampling follows a normalized factorization. More generally, if \(U\) has a tractable base law and \(T\) is a measurable map, then \(X=T(U)\) has the pushforward law \(T_\#p_U\). The map need not be invertible and need not provide a density. The generalized inverse-CDF construction is the scalar canonical example.

    For independent \(U_1,U_2\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), the Box–Muller construction

    \[ \begin{aligned} R&=\sqrt{-2\log U_1}, & \Theta&=2\pi U_2,\\ Z_1&=R\cos\Theta, & Z_2&=R\sin\Theta \end{aligned} \]

    returns two independent standard Gaussian variables. If \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\) and \(LL^T=\Sigma\), then \(X=\mu+LZ\) has law \(\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)\). These constructions explain the probability map; a production random-number library may use a different Gaussian algorithm and must separately control uniform-generator quality, endpoint handling, precision, and factorization error.

    Rejection sampling exposes its normalizer¤

    Let the normalized target be \(\pi(x)=\gamma(x)/Z\), where \(\gamma\) can be evaluated and \(Z\) may be unknown. If a normalized proposal \(q\) and finite constant \(M\) satisfy

    \[ \gamma(x)\le Mq(x) \]

    everywhere, propose \(X\sim q\), draw \(U\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), and accept when

    \[ U\le\frac{\gamma(X)}{Mq(X)}. \]

    The accepted state has density \(\pi\), and the mean acceptance probability is

    \[ \mathbb P(\text{accept}) =\int q(x)\frac{\gamma(x)}{Mq(x)}\,dx =\frac ZM. \]

    The frequently quoted value \(1/M\) applies only when the envelope was written for an already normalized target, so that \(Z=1\). An invalid bound creates a biased sampler; silently clipping an acceptance probability above one does not repair it.

    Worked failure case: a harmless scale mismatch becomes exponential¤

    Take \(\pi=\mathcal N(0,I_d)\) and \(q=\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I_d)\) with \(\sigma>1\). Their density ratio is

    \[ \frac{\pi(x)}{q(x)} =\sigma^d\exp\!\left[-\frac12\left(1-\sigma^{-2}\right) \lVert x\rVert^2\right], \]

    so the tight rejection constant is \(M=\sigma^d\) and the acceptance probability is \(\sigma^{-d}\). A one-percent scale mismatch gives \(1.01^{-1000}\approx4.77\times10^{-5}\) in one thousand dimensions: about one accepted draw per twenty-one thousand proposals. The algebraic \(N^{-1/2}\) Monte Carlo rate has not disappeared; the cost of obtaining each target draw has exploded.

    Importance sampling changes the output object¤

    Importance sampling estimates

    \[ \begin{aligned} w(x)&=\frac{\tilde p(x)}{q(x)},\\ \mathbb E_p[f(X)] &=\frac{\mathbb E_q[w(X)f(X)]} {\mathbb E_q[w(X)]}, \end{aligned} \]

    where \(\tilde p\) may be unnormalized. Self-normalization introduces finite-sample bias but removes the unknown normalizer. Weight degeneracy is measured through effective sample size, not merely proposal count.

    Coverage Is Non-Negotiable¤

    If \(q(x)=0\) in a region where \(p(x)|f(x)|>0\), importance sampling cannot recover that contribution. Increasing the number of samples does not fix missing support. Even with full support, heavy-tailed weights may have infinite variance, making ordinary error bars unreliable.

    For normalized weights \(\bar w_i=w_i/\sum_jw_j\), the heuristic

    \[ \operatorname{ESS}=\frac{1}{\sum_i\bar w_i^2} \]

    summarizes concentration. It does not prove accurate tail probabilities or proposal coverage. Inspect log weights and repeat the estimate across independent runs.

    Rejection sampling returns independent exact draws when the envelope is valid. Importance sampling returns weighted proposals rather than independent target draws unless followed by resampling, which introduces dependence and duplication.

    The output object constrains the query¤

    Sampling procedures that use the same target density need not return interchangeable objects:

    Table SAMP.1. The output object constrains the query
    Procedure Immediate output Directly supports Does not provide automatically
    Direct or valid rejection sampling Independent target draws Sample statistics and expectations with ordinary Monte Carlo error Density values or an evidence estimate
    Ordinary importance sampling Independent proposal draws with unnormalized weights Unnormalized integrals and, when applicable, an unbiased normalizer estimate Independent target draws
    Self-normalized importance sampling Proposal draws with weights summing to one A finite weighted approximation to normalized expectations An unbiased finite-sample ratio estimator
    MCMC One or more correlated state paths Posterior expectations after initialization and mixing are controlled The target normalizer, independent draws, or a universal burn-in length
    SMC Weighted particles, ancestor indices, and incremental normalizer estimates Filtering, path-space expectations, and normalizer ratios under the chosen construction Recovery of a mode absent from every particle

    Resampling weighted proposals produces an unweighted empirical population, but finite resampling can duplicate high-weight states and discard low-weight ones. It is an approximation to the target population, not a conversion into exact independent draws. The query-first inference map compares these outputs with exact inference and VI.

    For the full estimator algebra—ordinary and self-normalized importance sampling, proposal divergences, multiple proposals, AIS/SMC normalizer ratios, log-scale bias, and diagnostic contracts—continue to Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants.

    Randomized optimization supplies another output class. Independent Gumbel noise for every complete finite configuration followed by an exact argmax gives an exact categorical sample, and matched local Gaussian perturbations followed by an exact quadratic solve give an exact Gaussian sample. Unary or factorwise Gumbels, relaxed argmax operations, truncated max-product, and finite-tolerance linear solves generally change that guarantee. The randomized-optimization derivation states the perturbation field, dependence, solver, and correction contract.

    When the target is created from a base generator by a likelihood, reward, constraint, or verifier, the conditioning and steering volume derives the returned laws of rejection, Doob guidance, SMC twisting, projection, and best-of-\(N\) selection. Those mechanisms share sampling tools but do not share one finite output law.

    When the state is a random event configuration, the output contract must also include cardinality, empty windows, locations or times, marks, and stopping. Point Processes and Random Finite Sets derives count–location sampling, inverse compensators, dominated thinning, branching, spectral DPP sampling, and configuration MCMC. These are distinct uses of the sampling foundations developed here.

    Variance Reduction and Randomness Design¤

    Variance reduction changes how a fixed expectation is estimated. It does not change the target distribution, create a new model family, or excuse an invalid sampler. The basic contract is

    \[ \mathbb E[\widehat\mu_{\mathrm{new}}]=\mu \]

    when unbiasedness is claimed, followed by a cost-matched comparison of variance, bias, and wall-clock work.

    Table SAMP.2. Variance Reduction and Randomness Design
    Method Construction What must be checked
    Rao–Blackwellization Replace a random quantity by its conditional expectation given retained information The conditional expectation is exact or its approximation error is declared
    Control variate Subtract a correlated zero-mean quantity Its expectation is known and coefficient fitting does not reuse data in a way ignored by the error analysis
    Antithetic variables Couple correct marginals to induce negative covariance The transformed outputs, not merely the base noises, are negatively correlated
    Common random numbers Reuse base randomness across two systems It improves a difference only when the induced covariance has the helpful sign
    Stratification or RQMC Cover base-noise space more evenly Smoothness, effective dimension, transformation, randomization, and uncertainty protocol
    Fixed randomness / SAA Optimize one reusable finite-sample objective Convergence to that finite objective is not convergence to the population objective
    Multilevel coupling Telescope across resolutions and couple adjacent levels Cost, bias, and variance decay across levels, plus the finest-level target

    Conditional expectation cannot increase variance¤

    For a square-integrable estimator \(Y\) and retained information \(S\), the law of total variance gives

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(Y) =\mathbb E[\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid S)] +\operatorname{Var}(\mathbb E[Y\mid S]). \]

    Therefore \(\mathbb E[Y\mid S]\) has the same expectation as \(Y\) and no larger variance. Marginalizing a tractable discrete latent variable instead of sampling it, or summing over a local graphical-model state inside a larger estimator, are generative-model examples. Approximate marginalization does not inherit the exact guarantee.

    Control variates make correlation explicit¤

    Suppose \(H\) has known expectation \(\eta\). For any fixed coefficient \(c\),

    \[ Y_c=f(X)-c\bigl(H(X)-\eta\bigr) \]

    has expectation \(\mu\). Its variance is minimized by

    \[ c^*=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(f(X),H(X))} {\operatorname{Var}(H(X))}, \]

    provided \(\operatorname{Var}(H)>0\). A coefficient estimated on the same finite sample can alter small-sample bias and uncertainty; fit on independent data, cross-fit, or analyze the joint estimator. Baselines for score-function gradients are control variates specialized to a derivative query, as developed in Gradients and Geometry.

    Antithetic pairs and common random numbers answer different questions¤

    If \(Y^+\) and \(Y^-\) each have mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), the paired average has

    \[ \operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{Y^++Y^-}{2}\right) =\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\bigl(1+\rho\bigr), \qquad \rho=\operatorname{Corr}(Y^+,Y^-). \]

    The pair helps only when \(\rho<0\) and is worse than two independent draws when \(\rho>0\). A symmetric base law makes \(Z\) and \(-Z\) valid marginals, but it does not prove that a nonlinear generator statistic \(f(G(Z))\) and \(f(G(-Z))\) are negatively correlated.

    Common random numbers instead compare two methods. If \(\widehat\mu_A\) and \(\widehat\mu_B\) share base randomness,

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_A-\widehat\mu_B) =\operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_A) +\operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_B) -2\operatorname{Cov}(\widehat\mu_A,\widehat\mu_B). \]

    A positive covariance sharpens the paired difference even if neither marginal estimate becomes less variable. Report paired and unpaired designs explicitly.

    Worked example: one symmetry, two variance-reduction views¤

    Let \(X\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and estimate \(\mu=\mathbb E[X^2]=1/3\). Ordinary sampling has

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X^2)=\frac15-\frac19=\frac4{45}. \]

    Because \(1-X\) is also uniform, use the antithetic pair

    \[ A(X)=\frac{X^2+(1-X)^2}{2}=X^2-X+\frac12. \]

    Direct integration gives \(\mathbb E[A]=1/3\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(A)=1/180\). By comparison, the average of two independent \(X^2\) values has variance \(2/45\), eight times larger.

    The same estimator follows from the control variate \(H(X)=X\) with known mean \(1/2\). Since

    \[ c^*=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X^2,X)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)}=1, \]

    the optimal controlled value is \(X^2-(X-1/2)=A(X)\). Antithetic coupling and a control variate are different constructions, but here a symmetry makes their estimators coincide.

    Deterministic QMC, randomized QMC, and uncertainty¤

    Write a target expectation as an integral over base noise,

    \[ \mu=\int_{[0,1]^d}h(u)\,du. \]

    Quasi-Monte Carlo (QMC) evaluates \(h\) on a deterministic low-discrepancy point set \(u_{1:N}\). Its error can improve on ordinary Monte Carlo for suitable smoothness, variation, and effective dimension, but the deterministic estimate has no ordinary sampling standard error. Ambient dimension alone is not a complete predictor; the coordinate transformation and the dimensions that dominate variation matter.

    Randomized QMC (RQMC) applies a valid randomization that keeps each point marginally uniform while retaining low-discrepancy structure. With \(R\) independent randomizations, compute one estimate \(\widehat\mu_N^{(r)}\) per randomization and use variation across the \(R\) independent estimates for an error assessment. Treating the \(N\) dependent points within one scramble as iid would be incorrect. Favorable RQMC rates require regularity and are not a universal promise for discontinuous constraints, heavy tails, or high effective dimension.

    QMC has been applied to both score- and pathwise-gradient VI (Buchholz, Wenzel, and Mandt, 2018), while randomized QMC has been combined with quasi-Newton optimization (Liu and Owen, 2021). These results establish method-specific improvements under their assumptions, not that low-discrepancy points dominate iid sampling for every generative objective.

    Fixed randomness creates a finite optimization problem¤

    Let a population objective be

    \[ L(\theta)=\mathbb E_{\epsilon}[\ell(\theta;\epsilon)]. \]

    Sample-average approximation (SAA) fixes draws \(\epsilon_{1:N}\) and optimizes

    \[ \widehat L_N(\theta)=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N \ell(\theta;\epsilon_i). \]

    The resulting objective is deterministic conditional on those draws, so line searches, stopping tests, Hessian-vector products, and quasi-Newton methods can be meaningful for the executed finite problem. A tiny optimization residual for \(\widehat L_N\) does not show that \(\theta\) optimizes \(L\). Validate with fresh random draws or increasing \(N\). Deterministic ADVI applies this idea to a fixed Monte Carlo approximation of a mean-field variational objective; its favorable low-sample results have explicit posterior-family boundaries.

    Multilevel estimators couple resolutions¤

    When \(Y_0,\ldots,Y_L\) approximate the same query at increasing fidelity,

    \[ \mathbb E[Y_L] =\mathbb E[Y_0] +\sum_{\ell=1}^L \mathbb E[Y_\ell-Y_{\ell-1}]. \]

    Multilevel Monte Carlo spends many cheap samples on coarse levels and fewer expensive samples on fine corrections. The identity is elementary; efficiency comes from coupling adjacent levels so that \(Y_\ell-Y_{\ell-1}\) has small variance. In generative modeling, levels can represent solver grids, discretized stochastic paths, or nested inference computations. Estimating \(\mathbb E[Y_L]\) without bias does not remove the gap between the finite \(Y_L\) and a continuum target. Multilevel VI variants instead use related couplings to reduce gradient-estimation cost; their guarantee is tied to the declared hierarchy and optimization assumptions.

    Current generative applications, with bounded claims¤

    Evidence labels and scope

    • Peer-reviewed research — ICLR 2026: Quasi-Monte Carlo Methods Enable Extremely Low-Dimensional Deep Generative Models uses randomized lattice integration to approximate marginal likelihood in specialized one-, two-, and three-dimensional deep latent-variable models.     • Peer-reviewed research — ICLR 2026: Antithetic Noise in Diffusion Models reports negative correlation for paired initial noises \(z\) and \(-z\) across studied generators and uses it for uncertainty estimation.     2. Markov Chains and Stationarity¤

    Markov-kernel learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes finite probability and Monte Carlo estimation. By the end, you should be able to distinguish stationarity, invariance, irreducibility, aperiodicity, ergodicity, and mixing; compute the two-state examples; derive a Metropolis–Hastings kernel; compare scan/block/auxiliary Gibbs constructions; and diagnose adaptation, pseudo-marginal, bottleneck, autocorrelation, and augmentation effects on the estimator rather than only on acceptance rate.

    A Markov chain is best imagined as a moving probability distribution, not one decorative sample path. Applying the kernel repeatedly should move the law toward \(\pi\) while preserving \(\pi\) if it is already reached.

    Invariance describes the self-loop at the target. Mixing describes how quickly the other arrows approach it. Ergodicity connects the long path average to the target expectation.

    A transition kernel \(K(x,dx')\) leaves \(\pi\) invariant if

    \[ \int\pi(dx)K(x,A)=\pi(A). \]

    Detailed balance,

    \[ \pi(x)K(x,x')=\pi(x')K(x',x), \]

    is sufficient but not necessary for invariance. Summing either side over the source state proves \(\pi K=\pi\); irreducibility, aperiodicity, or a mixing-rate bound is not needed for that algebraic implication. Those conditions answer different questions. A stationary target says nothing about whether it is unique, whether a chosen initialization converges to it, or how quickly a finite run explores it.

    Time Homogeneity Is Not Stationarity¤

    A chain is time-homogeneous when it reuses the same transition kernel at every step. It is stationary when its joint law is invariant to a shift in time. For a homogeneous Markov chain, initializing \(X_0\sim\pi\) with \(\pi K=\pi\) makes every marginal equal to \(\pi\) and gives a stationary Markov process. Reusing one kernel from a point-mass initialization does not.

    For a finite transition matrix, keep four statements separate:

    1. Existence: at least one invariant probability vector exists.
    2. Uniqueness: irreducibility is sufficient; more generally, uniqueness holds when there is one closed recurrent class, even if transient states precede it.
    3. Convergence of the time-\(t\) law: a finite irreducible, aperiodic chain converges to its unique invariant law from every initial distribution.
    4. Mixing rate: the convergence theorem does not say that the required \(t\) is computationally useful; bottlenecks can make it enormous.

    On a countably infinite or general state space, finite-state existence no longer transfers automatically. For an irreducible countable chain, positive recurrence is the relevant condition for an invariant probability law; aperiodicity is additionally used for ordinary distributional convergence. Terminology such as “ergodic” varies across fields, so a theorem should list the properties it actually needs.

    Worked Two-State Boundary: Unique but Not Convergent¤

    Consider the deterministic flip chain

    \[ K= \begin{bmatrix} 0&1\\ 1&0 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    It is irreducible and has the unique invariant law \(\pi=(1/2,1/2)\), which also satisfies detailed balance. It is periodic with period two. Starting from state zero,

    \[ \delta_0K^{2t}=\delta_0, \qquad \delta_0K^{2t+1}=\delta_1, \]

    so the time-\(t\) distribution never converges to \(\pi\). Nevertheless, the empirical fraction of visits to either state converges to one half. Thus an invariant law, convergence of marginals, and convergence of path averages are different claims.

    Adding a nonzero self-loop removes the period. For

    \[ K_\varepsilon= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon&1-\varepsilon\\ 1-\varepsilon&\varepsilon \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 0<\varepsilon<1, \]

    the nontrivial eigenvalue is \(2\varepsilon-1\), whose magnitude is below one; the law now converges geometrically to \((1/2,1/2)\). By contrast, the identity kernel is reducible and makes every initial distribution invariant. It has many stationary laws and performs no exploration at all.

    Invariance, Ergodicity, and Mixing¤

    • Invariance: starting at \(\pi\) leaves the next state distributed as \(\pi\).
    • Ergodicity: long-run averages converge to target expectations under suitable conditions.
    • Mixing: the finite-time law approaches \(\pi\) at a useful rate.
    • Monte Carlo efficiency: correlated draws provide enough information about the observables of interest.

    A chain can be invariant but reducible, ergodic but extremely slow, or apparently stable while trapped in one metastable mode. These properties must therefore be checked at the level of the kernel and the scientific query.

    Mixing time is a theorem; warmup is a workflow¤

    For probability laws \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), use the total-variation convention

    \[ \lVert\mu-\nu\rVert_{\mathrm{TV}} =\sup_A|\mu(A)-\nu(A)| =\frac12\int|d\mu-d\nu|. \]

    For a finite chain, the worst-case \(\varepsilon\)-mixing time is

    \[ t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon) =\min\left\{t:\sup_x \lVert K^t(x,\cdot)-\pi\rVert_{\mathrm{TV}}\le\varepsilon\right\}. \]

    This is a property of a specified kernel, target, tolerance, and state space. It is not a number that a trace plot reveals, and it is rarely known for a neural or hierarchical target.

    Practical warmup is different. During warmup, an implementation may move away from deliberately overdispersed initial states and adapt a proposal scale, mass matrix, or trajectory rule. Adaptation usually changes the transition kernel, so warmup iterations are normally excluded from estimators intended for the subsequent fixed-kernel chain. Exclusion is an accounting decision, not a proof that the retained states began in stationarity. The older word burn-in often collapses these distinct ideas:

    Table SAMP.3. Mixing time is a theorem; warmup is a workflow
    Question Correct object What discarding early states does not prove
    Has initialization bias become negligible? distance of the current law from \(\pi\) for relevant queries that every mode was found
    Has adaptation finished? a frozen or theoretically valid adaptive-kernel protocol stationarity of the current state
    Is the retained estimator precise? long-run variance, MCSE, and observable-specific ESS independence of retained draws

    Run multiple chains from meaningfully dispersed initial conditions, retain the adaptation record, and diagnose the retained draws. A single chain that looks stable can simply be stable inside one mode.

    Bottlenecks explain why local movement can be globally slow¤

    For a finite reversible chain, define stationary flow

    \[ Q(A,A^c)=\sum_{x\in A}\sum_{y\notin A}\pi(x)K(x,y) \]

    and conductance

    \[ \Phi=\min_{0<\pi(A)\le 1/2}\frac{Q(A,A^c)}{\pi(A)}. \]

    A small \(\Phi\) means that some set carries appreciable target mass but little one-step probability flow across its boundary. Spectral and conductance bounds make this obstruction quantitative for the chains to which those theorems apply. The general lesson survives beyond the finite reversible setting: excellent local acceptance cannot compensate for a proposal that almost never crosses the scientifically important barrier. Tempering, blocking, auxiliary variables, transport, and nonlocal proposals are alternative attempts to alter that geometry.

    Correlated draws change estimator uncertainty¤

    For a stationary chain and scalar observable \(Y_t=f(X_t)\), define the lag-\(k\) autocovariance

    \[ \gamma_k=\operatorname{Cov}(Y_t,Y_{t+k}). \]

    Under conditions that make the autocovariances summable, the long-run variance is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2 &=\gamma_0+2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\gamma_k,\\ \operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_N) &\approx\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2}{N}. \end{aligned} \]

    When \(\gamma_0>0\), the integrated autocorrelation time and an observable-specific effective sample size are commonly written

    \[ \begin{aligned} \tau_{\mathrm{int}} &=\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2}{\gamma_0} =1+2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\rho_k,\\ N_{\mathrm{eff}} &=\frac{N}{\tau_{\mathrm{int}}}. \end{aligned} \]

    This is not the importance-weight ESS from Section 1. It compares the variance of a correlated path average with an iid average for one declared observable. Different observables can have radically different \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}\) on the same chain; negative autocorrelation can even make \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}>N\) under this variance-equivalence definition. Estimating the infinite sum from a finite, nonstationary, or metastable run requires a window, batch-means, spectral, or related long-run-variance method. A numerical ESS cannot prove that an undiscovered mode was explored.

    Worked two-state example: the same number of states can carry different information¤

    Return to \(K_\varepsilon\) and encode its two states by \(Y_t\in\{-1,+1\}\). At stationarity, this centered observable has \(\gamma_0=1\) and lag correlation

    \[ \rho_k=(2\varepsilon-1)^k. \]

    When \(\varepsilon=3/4\), the eigenvalue is \(1/2\) and the geometric series gives

    \[ \tau_{\mathrm{int}} =1+2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\left(\frac12\right)^k=3. \]

    Thus a path of 900 states carries the same asymptotic variance for the mean of \(Y_t\) as about 300 iid draws. The chain tends to repeat its current state, so adjacent observations are redundant. With \(\varepsilon=1/4\), the eigenvalue is \(-1/2\), \(\tau_{\mathrm{int}}=1/3\), and the same variance-equivalence convention gives \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=2700\). Alternation cancels noise in this particular mean. Neither number says how well an unrelated nonlinear observable mixes, and neither detects a separate mode that the two-state example does not contain.

    The operator view of discrete and continuous processes continues in General Markov Generators. State-space sequences, where observations and latent states arrive through time, continue in Filtering, Smoothing, and State-Space Inference.

    3. Metropolis–Hastings¤

    Suppose only an unnormalized target \(\widetilde\pi(x)\) is evaluable. From the current state \(x\), propose \(x'\sim q(x'\mid x)\) and accept with

    \[ \alpha(x,x')=\min\left( 1, \frac{\tilde\pi(x')q(x\mid x')}{\tilde\pi(x)q(x'\mid x)} \right). \]

    If the proposal is rejected, retain \(x\). The target normalizer cancels, which is why MH is useful for posterior and energy-based targets. The proposal density does not cancel unless it is symmetric.

    The acceptance probability follows from detailed balance. For \(x\ne x'\), accepted proposal flow is

    \[ \pi(x)q(x'\mid x)\min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')q(x\mid x')}{\pi(x)q(x'\mid x)}\right), \]

    which equals the corresponding reverse flow because both reduce to the minimum of the two directional proposal masses. Rejections supply the remaining self-transition probability.

    Acceptance is only one part of validity. The ratio must be defined wherever a move can occur, and the resulting chain must be able to reach every target-relevant region. It is unnecessarily strong to demand \(q(x'\mid x)>0\) for every pair of target states: a local random walk can reach a distant state through several steps. The correct question is whether the multi-step transition graph is irreducible with respect to the target's support, together with the recurrence and regularity conditions required by the claimed convergence theorem.

    Acceptance rate is meaningful here, but a high rate can indicate tiny moves and poor mixing; a low rate can indicate overly aggressive proposals. Autocorrelation, ESS, multiple-chain agreement, and task observables are also needed. The familiar random-walk target near \(0.234\) is an asymptotic result for particular high-dimensional product-like limits, not a universal tuning law.

    A mixture of proposals can be irreducible even when no component is. Likewise, composing invariant kernels—such as a local update followed by a global independence proposal—preserves invariance. Random mixtures and ordered compositions need their own kernel argument; it is not necessary for each component to solve the whole exploration problem.

    Adaptation must preserve an asymptotic contract¤

    Learning a covariance or proposal scale from the chain history can greatly improve performance, but the state process is then generally not a homogeneous Markov chain by itself. A vague instruction to “adapt less over time” is not a theorem. Classical adaptive-MCMC results use explicit conditions such as diminishing adaptation, where consecutive kernels become close, together with a stability condition such as containment. An alternative practical contract is to adapt during warmup, freeze the kernel, and diagnose only the fixed-kernel sampling phase.

    Never update a proposal using future retained states or report fixed-kernel diagnostics as if they automatically applied to an indefinitely adapting algorithm. State which parameters adapt, over what iterations, and under which validity result.

    Exact correction can use a random likelihood estimate¤

    Sometimes \(\widetilde\pi(x)\) contains an intractable likelihood but admits a nonnegative unbiased estimate \(\widehat L(x,U)\) with auxiliary randomness \(U\). Pseudo-marginal MH runs an ordinary MH algorithm on the augmented state \((x,U)\), targeting a joint density proportional to

    \[ p(x)\,m(u\mid x)\widehat L(x,u). \]

    Its \(x\)-marginal is the desired target because integrating over \(u\) returns the exact likelihood. Replacing the likelihood by a fresh noisy estimate inside an otherwise ordinary ratio is not automatically equivalent: the auxiliary randomness must be part of the state and proposal. Nonnegativity and unbiasedness establish the marginal identity; estimator variance controls practical stickiness.

    Delayed-acceptance schemes use a cheap approximation to reject bad proposals before an expensive exact second stage. The two acceptance stages must be derived jointly so that their product preserves the target. “A surrogate was only used for screening” is not sufficient without that correction.

    A learned proposal is not a learned target

    A flow, diffusion, autoregressive model, or variational approximation can propose states. Before correction, its outputs follow the proposal law. Importance weights or a valid MH transition can correct target mismatch under their support assumptions; training the proposer well does not make the correction optional.

    4. Gibbs, Blocks, and Auxiliary Variables¤

    Metropolis–Hastings proposes and corrects a move. Gibbs sampling uses a conditional distribution that is already compatible with the joint target. For \(x=(x_1,\ldots,x_d)\), a single-coordinate Gibbs update draws

    \[ x_j'\sim\pi(x_j\mid x_{-j}) \]

    and keeps \(x_{-j}\) fixed. Viewed as MH on coordinate \(j\), the target and proposal terms cancel, so the acceptance probability is one. That algebra proves invariance of the coordinate update; it says nothing about how far the draw moves or how quickly a sequence of coordinate updates explores the joint law.

    Scan order and parallelism are parts of the kernel¤

    • Systematic scan applies coordinate kernels in a declared order. Their composition is invariant, although it need not be reversible.
    • Random scan chooses a coordinate or block at random and applies its invariant update.
    • Blocked Gibbs draws a group jointly from its full conditional. A good block can cross strong within-group dependence that defeats scalar updates.
    • Metropolis-within-Gibbs replaces an unavailable conditional draw by an MH kernel that leaves that conditional invariant.
    • Parallel Gibbs is valid only for conditionally independent coordinates or for another explicitly derived parallel kernel. Simultaneously drawing dependent coordinates from stale conditionals generally changes the target.

    Blocking trades statistical movement against computation. One exact joint conditional can be expensive; many cheap scalar updates can be highly autocorrelated. The relevant comparison is ESS or MCSE per wall-clock and per target/gradient evaluation for declared observables, not acceptance per update.

    Worked example: a bivariate Gaussian exposes scalar Gibbs slowdown¤

    Let

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}X\\Y\end{bmatrix} \sim\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1&\rho\\\rho&1\end{bmatrix} \right),\qquad |\rho|<1. \]

    The full conditionals are

    \[ X\mid Y=y\sim\mathcal N(\rho y,1-\rho^2), \qquad Y\mid X=x\sim\mathcal N(\rho x,1-\rho^2). \]

    One systematic sweep first draws \(X_{t+1}\) from \(Y_t\) and then \(Y_{t+1}\) from \(X_{t+1}\). Substitution gives

    \[ Y_{t+1}=\rho^2Y_t+\rho\sqrt{1-\rho^2}\,\epsilon_{1,t} +\sqrt{1-\rho^2}\,\epsilon_{2,t}, \]

    with independent standard-normal noises. Thus the sweep-to-sweep lag-one correlation of \(Y\) is \(\rho^2\). It approaches one when \(|\rho|\) approaches one, even though every conditional draw is accepted. Drawing \((X,Y)\) jointly is an exact blocked update and produces an independent target draw in this toy case. Real models rarely offer that ideal block, but the example shows what blocking is trying to approximate.

    Collapsing and Rao–Blackwellization remove different randomness¤

    If a variable can be integrated out analytically, a collapsed sampler runs on the marginal target and reconstructs the removed variable conditionally when needed. This can reduce posterior dependence and dimension, as in collapsed LDA or mixture-model assignment updates. It can also make each update more global and expensive, destroy conditional independences, or complicate parallelism. Collapsing is therefore not universally faster.

    Rao–Blackwellization instead improves an estimator by replacing sampled randomness with a conditional expectation. A chain may remain unchanged while the reported query has lower variance. Keep transition design and estimator design separate.

    Augmentation can make a hard marginal easy¤

    Introduce an auxiliary variable \(U\) and a joint law \(\overline\pi(x,u)\) whose \(x\)-marginal is \(\pi(x)\). Alternating conditionals in the augmented space can be easier than updating \(x\) directly. Data augmentation, latent indicators, Polya–Gamma variables, momentum in HMC, and cluster labels in spin models all follow this pattern. The added variable does not change the desired marginal, but it can improve or worsen the augmented chain's geometry.

    For slice sampling, write an unnormalized nonnegative target as the marginal of

    \[ \overline\pi(x,u)\propto \mathbf 1\{0<u<\widetilde\pi(x)\}. \]

    Given \(x\), sample \(u\) uniformly below \(\widetilde\pi(x)\); given \(u\), sample \(x\) from the horizontal slice \(S_u=\{x:\widetilde\pi(x)>u\}\). One-dimensional stepping-out and shrinkage are implementation strategies for finding and sampling a slice, not the definition of multivariate slice sampling. In several dimensions one may update directions, coordinates, ellipses, or other blocks; there is no rule requiring one auxiliary height per coordinate.

    Cluster algorithms such as Swendsen–Wang augment certain lattice models with random bonds, then update whole connected components. They can reduce critical slowing in important regimes, but “cluster update” does not imply rapid mixing for every graph, temperature, or model.

    Applied instances continue in RBMs and LDA, Bayesian nonparametric partitions, and blocked state trajectories.

    Markov-kernel synthesis¤

    An invariant kernel preserves a target, while convergence also requires access, recurrence, and aperiodicity conditions appropriate to the state space. Metropolis correction, blocking, collapsing, and augmentation create different kernels. Their value is measured through mixing and query-specific Monte Carlo error, not acceptance rate alone.

    5. Langevin Algorithms¤

    Gradient, Hamiltonian, and diagnostic learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes differentiable log targets and Markov-kernel concepts. By the end, you should be able to distinguish ULA, MALA, SGLD, overdamped, and underdamped dynamics; derive leapfrog and Metropolis correction for HMC; explain preconditioning and constrained transformations; interpret rank-normalized \(\widehat R\), ESS, MCSE, divergences, and energy diagnostics; and state the extra contracts for stochastic-gradient, kinetic, transdimensional, or structure- changing chains.

    The overdamped Langevin SDE targeting \(\pi(x)\propto e^{-E(x)}\) is

    \[ \begin{aligned} dX_t &=\nabla_x\log\pi(X_t)dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t\\ &=-\nabla E(X_t)dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t. \end{aligned} \]

    Euler–Maruyama gives unadjusted Langevin (ULA):

    \[ x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\nabla E(x_k)+\sqrt{2\eta}\,\xi_k. \]

    ULA has discretization bias at finite \(\eta\) and no accept/reject step. MALA uses the ULA proposal inside Metropolis–Hastings, correcting discretization bias under exact arithmetic. SGLD replaces full-data gradients with minibatch estimates and typically uses decreasing steps for asymptotic arguments.

    With the convention above, the MALA proposal is

    \[ q_\eta(x'\mid x) =\mathcal N\!\left(x';x+\eta\nabla\log\pi(x),2\eta I\right). \]

    It must use the asymmetric MH ratio

    \[ \alpha(x,x')= \min\left\{1, \frac{\widetilde\pi(x')q_\eta(x\mid x')} {\widetilde\pi(x)q_\eta(x'\mid x)} \right\}. \]

    The reverse proposal mean is evaluated at \(x'\). Treating a Langevin proposal as symmetric silently removes the correction that makes MALA exact under its assumptions.

    Energy need not decrease along a valid stochastic chain. Noise permits uphill moves required by the target distribution.

    ULA, MALA, and SGLD Are Not Interchangeable¤

    • ULA has no accept/reject step and targets a step-size-dependent approximation at fixed \(\eta\).
    • MALA corrects the proposal with an MH test, giving the intended invariant target under standard assumptions and exact computation.
    • SGLD replaces exact log-posterior gradients with stochastic minibatch gradients. Classical asymptotic guarantees use decreasing step sizes; constant-step deep-learning implementations have additional stationary bias.

    Annealed Langevin sampling used with score models is a finite schedule across changing noise levels. It is not a stationary chain targeting one fixed density at every step.

    In an EBM, the same updates become part of a learned negative-phase estimator; replay initialization, energy drift during training, and model change between updates add failure modes that a fixed-target MCMC theorem does not cover. See Langevin sampling in the EBM chapter.

    Preconditioning changes both drift and noise¤

    For a constant symmetric positive-definite mobility \(A\), a preconditioned diffusion can be written

    \[ dX_t=A\nabla\log\pi(X_t)dt+\sqrt{2A}\,dW_t. \]

    Both terms use the same geometry. Scaling only the gradient generally changes the stationary law. If \(A(x)\) depends on position, an additional divergence correction is normally required under the Itô convention; manifold Langevin methods must state that term and the volume convention. An RMSProp- or Adam-shaped update is not automatically a valid Riemannian sampler merely because it rescales a stochastic gradient.

    Discrete spaces require a transition construction, not a formal gradient¤

    A categorical or combinatorial state has no ordinary Euclidean state gradient. Gibbs, locally balanced proposals, continuous-time jump rates, discrete Langevin analogues, and continuous relaxations define different kernels. Some admit MH adjustment; unadjusted variants generally need their own bias result. The discrete iterative-generation chapter develops learned CTMCs, while General Markov Generators owns their operator semantics.

    6. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo¤

    HMC augments position \(x\) with momentum \(p\). This chapter uses one convention throughout:

    \[ \begin{aligned} p&\sim\mathcal N(0,M),\\ H(x,p)&=E(x)+\frac12p^\top M^{-1}p, \end{aligned} \]

    where \(M\) is the mass matrix and momentum covariance. Some software calls \(M^{-1}\) the metric or inverse mass matrix; never transfer a tuned matrix between implementations without checking that convention.

    The joint density is proportional to \(e^{-H(x,p)}\), so marginalizing momentum returns \(\pi(x)\propto e^{-E(x)}\). Hamilton's equations are

    \[ \begin{aligned} \dot x&=M^{-1}p,\\ \dot p&=-\nabla E(x). \end{aligned} \]

    Exact Hamiltonian flow is volume preserving, reversible after a momentum flip, and conserves \(H\). These properties motivate a distant proposal, but the exact flow is rarely available.

    Leapfrog supplies a reversible numerical proposal¤

    For step size \(\epsilon\), one leapfrog step is

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_{n+1/2}&=p_n-\frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla E(x_n),\\ x_{n+1}&=x_n+\epsilon M^{-1}p_{n+1/2},\\ p_{n+1}&=p_{n+1/2}-\frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla E(x_{n+1}). \end{aligned} \]

    After \(L\) steps, negate momentum and accept the proposal with

    \[ \alpha=\min\{1,\exp[-H(x',p')+H(x,p)]\}. \]

    Leapfrog is volume preserving and reversible, which makes this ordinary MH correction valid. A finite integrator almost always has nonzero energy error; that does not mean the transition automatically diverged or must be rejected. Small error is accepted with high probability. A divergence is an implementation diagnostic for unusually large or unstable numerical error, often caused by a step size that is too large for the local curvature.

    A complete fixed-trajectory transition therefore:

    1. draws or refreshes momentum using the declared refresh scheme;
    2. integrates \(L\) leapfrog steps;
    3. applies the MH correction; and
    4. discards momentum if only positions are retained.

    Full independent momentum refresh is standard, but it is not the only valid choice; generalized HMC uses partial refresh with a compatible augmented-state kernel.

    Geometry controls step size and trajectory length¤

    For a Gaussian target \(x\sim\mathcal N(0,\Sigma)\),

    \[ \ddot x=-M^{-1}\Sigma^{-1}x. \]

    The eigenvalues of \(M^{-1}\Sigma^{-1}\) determine the oscillation frequencies. One scalar step size must resolve the fastest direction, while useful movement along slow directions may require a long trajectory. Adapting the coordinate geometry can make those frequencies more similar. Under the convention in this chapter, choosing \(M\) proportional to \(\Sigma^{-1}\) equalizes this ideal Gaussian system; an implementation that adapts an “inverse mass matrix” may store the corresponding covariance-like object instead.

    The lesson is not that one matrix solves nonlinear geometry. Curvature can vary across the state space, as in hierarchical funnels, and separated modes remain separated under local deterministic dynamics. Position-dependent Riemannian HMC introduces a metric \(G(x)\), a log-determinant term, and generalized Hamiltonian equations. Simply inserting a Hessian into the Euclidean update is not RMHMC: a Hessian can be indefinite, and omitting the position-dependent terms changes the target.

    NUTS adapts path length, not all geometry¤

    The No-U-Turn Sampler builds a trajectory and stops expansion when an implementation-specific U-turn condition indicates that continuing would retrace the path. Candidate selection and stopping are constructed together to preserve the target. NUTS removes a fixed user-chosen \(L\); it still needs a step size, a mass-matrix convention, a maximum tree depth, an adaptation protocol, and diagnostics.

    During warmup, dual averaging commonly tunes the step size and windowed estimation tunes the mass matrix. Those iterations belong to an adaptive phase. During retained sampling, the tuned quantities are normally frozen. Tiny-step trajectories can accept almost always while wasting gradient evaluations, so compare ESS and MCSE per gradient evaluation and wall-clock, not acceptance per iteration.

    Constraints require a measure-correct transformation¤

    If \(x=T(u)\) maps unconstrained \(u\in\mathbb R^d\) to a constrained parameter, HMC in \(u\) coordinates must target

    \[ \log\pi_U(u) =\log\pi_X(T(u))+\log|\det J_T(u)|. \]

    The Jacobian is part of the transformed density, not an optional post-processing correction. Simplexes, positive scales, ordered vectors, and correlation matrices each need an appropriate bijection and base measure.

    7. Warmup, Geometry, and MCMC Diagnostics¤

    Diagnostics test particular failure modes; none proves convergence. Use several chains, scientifically relevant observables, and a cost denominator.

    Start chains so disagreement can be seen¤

    Initial states should be valid and should probe meaningfully different regions without relying on impossible or numerically pathological values. Identical initialization can hide between-chain disagreement. Overdispersion is useful only when the resulting warmup can enter the typical set; absurdly diffuse initial states can instead create overflow or waste computation.

    After warmup, inspect rank plots, traces of relevant functions, energy or log density, mode labels, tail events, constraints, and replicated summaries. Trace plots are views of finite paths, not tests with universal pass/fail thresholds.

    Rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\) checks location and scale disagreement¤

    The modern construction first splits each chain, pools the draws of a scalar quantity, replaces them by rank-based normal scores, and compares between- and within-chain variation. A folded version applies the same idea to absolute deviations from the pooled median, making scale disagreement more visible. The reported diagnostic is the more conservative of rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\) and folded-split \(\widehat R\).

    Values near one are necessary evidence of agreement, not proof that every chain found every mode. A rule such as \(\widehat R<1.01\) is a practical screen under a specific workflow, not a theorem that the Monte Carlo error is small. Traditional unsplit, unranked \(\widehat R\) can miss heavy-tail and unequal-scale failures; do not teach it as the default merely because its algebra is shorter.

    ESS and MCSE must match the query¤

    Bulk ESS applies the multi-chain autocorrelation calculation to rank-normalized draws and measures information about central location. Tail ESS uses indicator functions for selected tail quantiles and diagnoses tail-probability accuracy. Local or quantile-specific ESS can target other features. These remain observable-specific variance equivalents.

    For an estimated posterior mean with marginal standard deviation \(s\) and an appropriate ESS,

    \[ \widehat{\operatorname{MCSE}}(\widehat\mu) \approx\frac{s}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{eff}}}}. \]

    Quantiles and tail probabilities need their corresponding localized MCSE, not the mean formula. Report MCSE in the units of the scientific quantity and set a tolerance before interpreting the last decimal place.

    HMC adds algorithm-specific evidence¤

    Record at least:

    • divergent transitions and where they occur;
    • maximum-tree-depth saturation or trajectory-length truncation;
    • acceptance statistic and adapted step size;
    • mass-matrix structure and warmup schedule;
    • rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\), bulk and tail ESS, and MCSE; and
    • gradient evaluations, wall-clock, precision, and chain count.

    Energy diagnostics can reveal poor momentum-to-energy movement. They do not replace target observables. Zero divergences is reassuring only at the declared threshold and precision; increasing the threshold until warnings disappear can hide bias rather than fix geometry.

    Hierarchical funnels require parameterization checks¤

    Consider the generative hierarchy

    \[ V\sim\mathcal N(0,3^2), \qquad X\mid V=v\sim\mathcal N(0,e^v). \]

    The centered coordinates \((v,x)\) contain a narrow neck for negative \(v\) and a wide mouth for positive \(v\). One global HMC step size must negotiate both. Writing

    \[ Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad X=e^{V/2}Z \]

    gives an equivalent prior generative story in \((v,z)\) coordinates. It can make weak-data geometry easier, while the centered form can be better when data strongly identify group effects. This is not a universal “noncentering wins” rule. The complete hierarchical-model discussion is in Bayesian Learning and Model Assessment.

    A diagnostic panel can agree inside the wrong region

    If every chain starts in and remains inside one metastable mode, ranks, \(\widehat R\), and ESS may all look acceptable. Add mode-sensitive initializations, observables, tempering or nonlocal transitions, and application-specific checks. Diagnostics quantify evidence from runs that occurred; they do not reveal an unknown region by magic.

    8. Stochastic-Gradient and Kinetic MCMC¤

    Large datasets make an exact log-posterior gradient expensive. For

    \[ E(\theta)=-\log p(\theta)-\sum_{n=1}^N\log p(x_n\mid\theta), \]

    a minibatch \(B\) gives the unbiased gradient estimator

    \[ \widehat g(\theta) =-\nabla\log p(\theta) -\frac{N}{|B|}\sum_{n\in B}\nabla\log p(x_n\mid\theta). \]

    SGLD inserts this estimator into the Langevin update. The stochastic-gradient noise is not generally Gaussian, constant, or equal to the thermal noise required by the target diffusion. Classical decreasing-step results use explicit regularity and schedule assumptions; constant-step implementations trade asymptotic exactness for throughput and retain gradient-noise and discretization bias. Variance-reduced estimators can reduce that bias and variance but add reference gradients, memory, or periodic full-data passes.

    Momentum produces underdamped dynamics¤

    With mass \(M\) and symmetric friction \(\Gamma\succeq0\), one convenient underdamped Langevin convention is

    \[ \begin{aligned} dX_t&=M^{-1}P_t\,dt,\\ dP_t&=-\nabla E(X_t)\,dt -\Gamma M^{-1}P_t\,dt+\sqrt{2\Gamma}\,dW_t. \end{aligned} \]

    Under regularity conditions its invariant joint density is proportional to \(\exp[-E(x)-p^\top M^{-1}p/2]\). Friction dissipates momentum while calibrated noise restores the intended temperature. Underdamped and overdamped Langevin are different processes; a second-order discretization does not universally require fewer target evaluations on every nonconvex problem.

    Naively replacing HMC gradients by minibatch gradients can inject uncontrolled energy error while also making the exact MH test expensive. SGHMC introduces friction and noise intended to compensate for stochastic-gradient noise. Practical exactness still depends on how that noise is estimated, the discretization, step size, and any omitted acceptance correction. “Uses Hamiltonian momentum” is not equivalent to exact HMC.

    9. Transdimensional and Structure-Changing MCMC¤

    Mixture component counts, change points, graph structures, program traces, and other latent structures can place states in spaces of different dimension. Write the target on a disjoint union

    \[ \mathcal X=\bigcup_m\{m\}\times\mathcal X_m, \qquad \pi(m,x_m). \]

    An ordinary density ratio between \(x_m\in\mathbb R^{d_m}\) and \(x_{m'}\in\mathbb R^{d_{m'}}\) is undefined when the base measures have different dimension. Reversible-jump MCMC augments each direction with random variables so the forward and reverse transformations have matching total dimension.

    Draw \(m'\) with probability \(r(m'\mid m)\) and auxiliary \(u\sim q_{m\to m'}(u\mid x_m)\). A differentiable bijection produces

    \[ (x_{m'}',u')=T_{m\to m'}(x_m,u). \]

    To expose every measure correction without forcing one oversized expression, define four ratios:

    \[ \begin{aligned} R_{\mathrm{target}} &=\frac{\widetilde\pi(m',x_{m'}')}{\widetilde\pi(m,x_m)},\\ R_{\mathrm{move}} &=\frac{r(m\mid m')}{r(m'\mid m)},\\ R_{\mathrm{aux}} &=\frac{q_{m'\to m}(u'\mid x_{m'}')} {q_{m\to m'}(u\mid x_m)},\\ J &=\left|\det\frac{\partial(x_{m'}',u')} {\partial(x_m,u)}\right|. \end{aligned} \]

    The acceptance probability is then

    \[ \alpha=\min\!\left\{1, R_{\mathrm{target}}R_{\mathrm{move}}R_{\mathrm{aux}}J \right\}. \]

    Every factor has a role: target mass, model-move probability, auxiliary proposal, and change of variables. Birth/death and split/merge proposals are special cases. A hand-designed inverse is not enough; dimensions, proposal probabilities, and the Jacobian must agree in both directions.

    Collapsing continuous parameters can turn some structure moves into ordinary discrete MCMC. When analytic integration is unavailable, learned transport maps can make the between-model proposal better matched, but the reversible jump correction remains the source of target invariance. Diagnose model-index occupancy, cross-model round trips, within-model ESS, and sensitivity to move types—not just the aggregate acceptance rate.

    Transdimensional MCMC answers a posterior-over-structures query. It is not the same object as a GFlowNet that amortizes a reward-proportional constructive sampler, an autoregressive structure generator, or a variational posterior, although those models can supply proposals. Continue to Bayesian nonparametrics, random graphs, and GFlowNets for those distinct constructions.

    Gradient samplers couple target geometry to both drift and randomness.     10. Annealing and Tempering¤

    Tempering and continuous-transport learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes importance sampling, Markov kernels, ODEs, and SDEs. By the end, you should be able to construct annealed/tempered paths and their weight updates, distinguish SMC rejuvenation from independent importance sampling, derive continuity and Fokker–Planck evolution, compare deterministic and stochastic transports, allocate numerical-solver and NFE error, and state how a Schrödinger bridge depends on its reference process and endpoint constraints.

    This foundation introduces temperature paths as sampling constructions. The complete estimator treatment—including AIS direction, SMC backward kernels, normalizer-ratio uncertainty, bridge/path alternatives, and reporting—lives in Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants.

    Three similarly named procedures return different objects:

    • Simulated annealing changes temperature while searching for a low-energy state. The nonhomogeneous path is an optimization algorithm; pooling its visited states does not produce samples from the final target.
    • Parallel tempering targets a joint product of distributions at several temperatures, alternates invariant within-temperature transitions with corrected swaps, and retains the cold chain for target expectations.
    • Annealed importance sampling propagates weighted paths through intermediate targets to estimate a normalizer ratio and weighted endpoint expectations.

    Intermediate distributions

    \[ \pi_t(x)\propto p_0(x)^{1-\beta_t}\tilde p(x)^{\beta_t} \]

    connect a tractable base to a target. Annealed importance sampling accumulates weight ratios and estimates normalizing constants; parallel tempering swaps states across temperatures to cross barriers. Schedule spacing matters when adjacent distributions have poor overlap.

    For power-tempered replicas

    \[ \pi_{\beta_i}(x)\propto\widetilde\pi(x)^{\beta_i}, \qquad 0<\beta_0<\cdots<\beta_L=1, \]

    a proposed swap of states \(x_i\) and \(x_j\) has log acceptance ratio and acceptance probability

    \[ \begin{aligned} \log R_{\mathrm{swap}} &=(\beta_i-\beta_j) [\log\widetilde\pi(x_j)-\log\widetilde\pi(x_i)],\\ \alpha_{\mathrm{swap}} &=\min\{1,\exp(\log R_{\mathrm{swap}})\}. \end{aligned} \]

    Within-replica moves and swap moves together preserve the product target. A high swap rate between one adjacent pair does not prove end-to-end movement; report replica round trips, occupancy across modes, and cost across all temperatures. Implement the decision on the log scale to avoid needless overflow or underflow.

    AIS uses transitions that leave each intermediate distribution invariant, but those transitions need not equilibrate fully for the standard importance-weight identity. Poor mixing still causes high-variance weights. Parallel tempering instead maintains chains at multiple temperatures and accepts swaps using an MH ratio; it seeks better exploration rather than directly producing the same normalizing-constant estimator.

    SMC turns one hard correction into a sequence¤

    Sequential Monte Carlo maintains weighted particles across targets \(\pi_t(x)=\gamma_t(x)/Z_t\). In the simplest reweighting step with unchanged particle states, the incremental factor is \(\gamma_t(x)/\gamma_{t-1}(x)\). General SMC samplers also require forward mutation kernels and, in their weight formula, a compatible backward-kernel construction. Propagation moves particles, weighting corrects the proposal, and resampling reallocates finite computation.

    Under standard proper-weighting conditions, multiplying average incremental weights gives an estimator of \(Z_T/Z_0\) that can be unbiased even though a normalized finite-particle posterior expectation is generally biased. Unbiasedness does not imply low variance: if adjacent targets barely overlap, all useful mass may concentrate in one lineage. Del Moral, Doucet, and Jasra (2006) give the general SMC-sampler construction.

    The distinction is developed in two complementary foundations. Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants owns evidence and ratio uncertainty. Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods owns target sequences, resampling offspring laws, particle and genealogical degeneracy, proposals, smoothing, PMCMC, static targets, learning, and generator composition. This sampling volume supplies the invariant mutation kernels and Monte Carlo error concepts reused by both.

    The same logic applies to controlled generation. A path of intermediate targets can introduce a likelihood, constraint, or reward gradually, while an amortized proposal attempts to reduce weight variance. The proposal does not supply correctness by itself; the target sequence and importance weights define what is being approximated. Ou, Pani, and Li (2026) instantiate this pattern for inference-time control of discrete diffusion models.

    Research directions that preserve the contract¤

    Modern learned samplers can serve as coordinates, proposals, or bridge maps without changing the invariant-target logic:

    • Preprint — 2019: NeuTra HMC runs HMC in coordinates defined by a learned invertible transport. The Jacobian-adjusted target and HMC correction preserve the intended target; transport quality controls efficiency.
    • Peer-reviewed research — AISTATS 2023: transport reversible-jump proposals learn reference-space maps for dimension-changing moves. The RJMCMC ratio, not the learned map alone, supplies invariance.
    • Peer-reviewed research — ICLR 2026: neural-transport parallel tempering combines flows or diffusion transports with a tempered system and reports improved sampling and normalizer estimation on its evaluated multimodal targets. The result is protocol-specific; it does not remove overlap or training costs in arbitrary dimensions.
    • Peer-reviewed research — JMLR 2026: WALNUTS adapts leapfrog step size within a NUTS orbit using a reversible construction and reports gains on multiscale targets. This is a new HMC kernel with a specific correctness argument, not permission to change step size arbitrarily inside any trajectory.
    • Peer-reviewed research — COLT 2024: diffusion-based Monte Carlo studies reverse- diffusion sampling from an energy target using nonparametric score estimation. Its complexity guarantees hold under the paper's assumptions; sharing reverse-SDE language with trained diffusion models does not make the two procedures identical.

    Discrete-space research similarly uses locally balanced, discrete-Langevin, cluster, and learned block proposals. Each must declare whether it is Metropolis adjusted, asymptotically exact under a limit, or deliberately biased. The architecture of the proposer never replaces that statement.

    11. Deterministic Transport and the Continuity Equation¤

    The next two sections describe two ways a whole probability law can move through continuous time. Deterministic transport moves particles along a vector field; stochastic transport adds diffusion. Their density equations reveal the difference.

    The shared endpoint label does not assert identical trajectories or finite-step samplers. It marks the design goal; equality requires the declared continuous- time assumptions and exact fields.

    For ODE

    \[ \frac{dX_t}{dt}=u_t(X_t), \]

    the density evolves by

    \[ \partial_t p_t(x)+\nabla\cdot(p_t(x)u_t(x))=0. \]

    Along a trajectory,

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(X_t)=-\nabla\cdot u_t(X_t), \]

    which gives CNF likelihoods after numerical integration. A regular ODE flow is a diffeomorphism: it preserves topology and cannot create disconnected support from a connected base without augmentation or changing assumptions.

    The divergence trace can be exact for structured fields or estimated stochastically with Hutchinson probes:

    \[ \nabla\cdot u(x)=\operatorname{tr}J_u(x) =\mathbb E_\epsilon[\epsilon^TJ_u(x)\epsilon], \]

    for zero-mean probes with identity covariance. This estimator is unbiased for the trace, but numerical integration and finite probes add variance and bias to a computed log likelihood.

    The continuous-flow chapter develops how these identities become learned CNF and flow-matching objectives. The probability-path chapter separates a path of marginals, a coupling, a velocity field, and a particular finite solver.

    12. Stochastic Transport and Fokker–Planck¤

    For Itô SDE

    \[ dX_t=f(X_t,t)dt+g(t)dW_t, \]

    the density obeys

    \[ \partial_t p_t =-\nabla\cdot(fp_t)+\frac12g(t)^2\Delta p_t. \]

    Under regularity assumptions, the reverse-time SDE depends on the marginal score \(\nabla_x\log p_t(x)\), while a probability-flow ODE can share the same one-time marginals with deterministic trajectories. Shared marginals do not mean identical paths, transition kernels, or discretization error.

    For state-independent scalar diffusion \(g(t)\), the reverse-time drift written in decreasing forward time contains the score correction \(-g(t)^2\nabla_x\log p_t(x)\). Sign conventions change when authors reparameterize time from noise to data. Always state time direction before copying a reverse-SDE formula.

    The probability-flow ODE replaces the stochastic diffusion contribution with half the score correction. Its deterministic map can support likelihood computation, while the reverse SDE samples stochastic paths. Exact equality of marginals is a continuous-time theorem; approximate scores and different solvers break exact equality at finite compute.

    For denoising objectives, parameterization conversions, schedules, reverse updates, and solver families, continue to the diffusion mathematical foundation and diffusion sampling chapter. For jumps and rate matrices rather than Brownian diffusion, continue to continuous-time discrete generation.

    13. Numerical Solvers and NFE¤

    Euler, Heun, Runge–Kutta, multistep, exponential, and stochastic solvers trade accuracy, stability, memory, and evaluations. Report:

    • solver and order;
    • fixed grid or adaptive tolerances;
    • accepted and rejected steps;
    • neural-network evaluations, including conditional/unconditional branches;
    • decoder or post-processing cost;
    • wall-clock hardware, precision, batch, and resolution.

    NFE is a sampler statistic, not an architecture-independent speed metric.

    Error Budget for a Learned Continuous-Time Sampler¤

    Separate at least four errors:

    1. model/field error: the learned score or velocity differs from its population target;
    2. path error: the chosen interpolation or forward process is poorly conditioned for the task;
    3. discretization error: finite solver steps approximate the learned dynamics;
    4. numerical/implementation error: precision, tolerances, clipping, batching, or compiler behavior changes the computation.

    Reducing solver tolerance cannot repair a wrong field. Distilling to one step changes the learned object and adds approximation error; it is not merely a lower-order numerical solve.

    Transitions and Few-Step Generation develops the distinction between numerically integrating a field and learning a finite transition directly. Inference accounting defines the compute ledger needed to compare those systems fairly.

    14. Schrödinger Bridges¤

    Given a reference path measure \(R\) and endpoint marginals \(p_0,p_1\), the Schrödinger bridge solves

    \[ P^*=\arg\min_{P:\,P_0=p_0,\,P_1=p_1}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|R). \]

    It is entropy-regularized optimal transport on path space. Iterative proportional fitting alternately adjusts endpoint constraints; diffusion Schrödinger bridge methods learn forward and backward dynamics. The first iteration can recover score-based constructions under particular references, but a full bridge is not synonymous with ordinary diffusion training.

    As reference noise tends toward zero under suitable scaling, Schrödinger bridges connect to deterministic optimal transport. At nonzero noise they solve a different, entropy-regularized path-space problem. Endpoint constraints, reference process, and regularization strength are part of the model—not implementation details.

    The broader construction—including stochastic interpolants, endpoint conditioning, paired translation, and its exact relationship to optimal transport—is developed in Stochastic Interpolants and Schrödinger Bridges.

    Worked Example: ULA for a Standard Gaussian¤

    Sampling capstone learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the complete sampling volume. By the end, you should be able to compute ULA bias and a two-state Metropolis transition, select family-specific diagnostics, use primary references to verify validity conditions, answer the retrieval set with feedback, and route a sampling failure to target/support, proposal/kernel, adaptation, geometry, discretization, or Monte Carlo estimation.

    For \(E(x)=x^2/2\), ULA is

    \[ x_{k+1}=(1-\eta)x_k+\sqrt{2\eta}\,\xi_k. \]

    This AR(1) chain has stationary variance

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{2\eta}{1-(1-\eta)^2} =\frac{1}{1-\eta/2}, \]

    which exceeds the target variance one for finite \(\eta\). The example exposes ULA discretization bias exactly. MALA's accept/reject correction restores the exact target stationary distribution, at the cost of rejected proposals.

    Stability also requires \(|1-\eta|<1\), hence \(0<\eta<2\). A step size can therefore produce a stable but biased chain, an extremely correlated chain, or an unstable process. “The samples look Gaussian” is not enough: compare variance, autocorrelation, and initialization sensitivity.

    Worked Example: A Metropolis Move on a Two-State Target¤

    Reuse the posterior from the binary latent-model example: \(\pi(0)=0.2\), \(\pi(1)=0.8\). Propose switching states with probability one. From state \(0\), the move to state \(1\) is always accepted. From state \(1\), the reverse acceptance probability is

    \[ \alpha(1,0)=\min(1,0.2/0.8)=0.25. \]

    The transition matrix is

    \[ K=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\0.25&0.75\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Checking invariance,

    \[ (0.2,0.8)K=(0.2,0.8). \]

    Starting at state zero gives the finite-time laws

    \[ \begin{aligned} (1,0)&\to(0,1)\\ &\to(0.25,0.75)\\ &\to(0.1875,0.8125)\\ &\to\cdots. \end{aligned} \]

    The more probable state has more rejections because proposed departures move toward lower target density. Those self-transitions are not wasted bookkeeping; they are required for the correct stationary law. The chain estimates posterior occupancy as time grows, while its acceptance ratio has discarded the evidence \(p(X=1)\).

    Summary and Next Step¤

    Sampling quality depends on the target, query, estimator, randomness design, invariance, kernel composition, dependence, mixing, parameterization, discretization, and finite computation. MH correction, Gibbs conditionals, augmentation, HMC geometry, stochastic-gradient scaling, reversible-jump dimension matching, and tempering solve different transition problems. Warmup is not a mixing theorem, acceptance is not exploration, and no diagnostic certifies a missed mode. Ordinary Monte Carlo error, Markov-chain MCSE, model error, and numerical bias answer different questions. ODE and SDE views explain flows and diffusion, while MCMC explains EBMs, posterior inference, and structure uncertainty.
