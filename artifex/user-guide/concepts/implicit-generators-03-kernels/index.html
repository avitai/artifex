    3. Kernel Choice, Estimator Bias, and Statistical Power¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 2's RKHS and estimator derivations. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. interpret bandwidth and feature maps as modeling assumptions;
    2. compare isotropic, anisotropic, multi-kernel, learned, and structured kernels;
    3. account for random-feature, block, and distributed approximation error;
    4. separate generator training from a valid post-selection two-sample test;
    5. design conditional and rare-mode kernel audits; and
    6. calibrate a kernel test under the degenerate null without reusing selection data.

    Bandwidth Is an Inductive Bias¤

    For an RBF kernel, a tiny bandwidth makes almost all distinct high-dimensional samples orthogonal; a huge bandwidth makes them nearly identical. Median-distance heuristics adapt to scale but can fail for multimodal, anisotropic, or mixed-type data.

    A multi-kernel objective

    \[ k(x,y)=\sum_{r=1}^{R}\alpha_r k_r(x,y),\qquad\alpha_r\ge0, \]

    exposes several scales. Learning \(\alpha_r\) jointly with the generator risks choosing kernels that weaken rather than strengthen discrimination unless the optimization is designed as a test-power or adversarial-kernel problem.

    An anisotropic RBF uses \((x-y)^\top\Lambda^{-1}(x-y)\) and therefore encodes relative coordinate scales and correlations. Product kernels can combine continuous, categorical, graph, or sequence components. The resulting kernel is useful only if it is positive definite where required and its invariances match the data—not merely because each component seems reasonable.

    High Dimension and Features¤

    Euclidean distance concentrates in high dimension. Kernels on pretrained perceptual features can improve sample gradients, but they import the encoder's dataset, invariances, and blind spots. For scientific data, use domain invariants, conserved quantities, and structural kernels rather than assuming an image encoder defines fidelity.

    “MMD fails in high dimension” is too broad. Power depends on the alternative, kernel, bandwidth, dimension/sample regime, and studentization. Mean shifts, variance changes, sparse alternatives, and structured functional alternatives can behave differently. Two Sample Testing in High Dimension via Maximum Mean Discrepancy develops joint dimension/sample asymptotics for a studentized statistic; those results do not imply that the median heuristic or a fixed image embedding is powerful for every neural-generator failure.

    The practical response is an alternative suite, not a slogan: inject a missing mode, wrong covariance, heavy tail, local corruption, memorized sample, and domain-specific constraint violation, then estimate which kernels and sample sizes detect each one at controlled type-I error.

    Random Features and Block Estimators¤

    Random Fourier features approximate shift-invariant kernels:

    \[ k(x,y)\approx\phi(x)^T\phi(y). \]

    Then MMD becomes the squared difference of empirical feature means, reducing pairwise cost. Approximation dimension introduces another error source. Block U-statistics trade memory and variance, and distributed reductions must preserve unbiased pairing assumptions.

    With \(R\) random features, the approximate objective is

    \[ \left\|\frac1m\sum_i\phi_R(x_i)-\frac1n\sum_j\phi_R(y_j)\right\|_2^2. \]

    Its cost is roughly linear in samples times \(R\), but it matches the approximate feature map, not the exact kernel. Fixing random features across updates gives a stationary approximate objective; resampling them adds Monte Carlo noise and changes the witness seen at each step.

    Statistical Testing Versus Generator Training¤

    Two-sample testing asks whether finite evidence rejects \(p=q\) at a controlled significance level. Generator training uses a discrepancy as a differentiable objective. A kernel with high hypothesis-test power near one alternative may give poor global optimization geometry. Conversely, a useful training loss is not automatically a calibrated test statistic after parameters were selected on the same data.

    A valid final test needs held-out data or a selection-aware protocol. Choosing bandwidth, feature encoder, checkpoint, and seed to minimize a test-set MMD and then applying the null distribution to the same samples invalidates the nominal type-I error.

    Why the ordinary null is not a standard error bar¤

    Under \(H_0:p=q\), the quadratic unbiased MMD is a degenerate U-statistic: its first-order projection vanishes. Its limiting null is generally a weighted sum of centered chi-square variables rather than an ordinary Gaussian with the same variance formula used under a fixed alternative. Permutation or wild bootstrap calibration is therefore common; linear/block/studentized variants have different limits and power.

    A defensible confirmatory pipeline is:

    1. choose representations, kernels, bandwidth candidates, and alternatives on training data;
    2. select among them on an independent selection split or use a test that adjusts for aggregation;
    3. freeze the complete test before opening the final samples;
    4. compute a permutation/bootstrap/asymptotic threshold valid for that statistic; and
    5. report level, power on controlled alternatives, sample size, and Monte Carlo error.

    MMD Aggregated Two-Sample Test shows one way to aggregate bandwidths while controlling level under its conditions. A Martingale Kernel Two-Sample Test provides a 2026 permutation-free alternative with a limiting standard Gaussian null. These are testing procedures; replacing a minibatch generator loss by one of them is a separate optimization design.

    Conditional MMD¤

    Matching pooled \((x,c)\) samples can hide rare-condition failure. Conditional mean embeddings and stratified objectives compare \(p(x\mid c)\), but continuous/high-dimensional conditions require kernels or learned representations over \(c\). Report performance across condition slices and do not infer conditional calibration from marginal MMD.

    If the condition marginal is the same by construction, equality of the joint laws \(p(x,c)=q(x,c)\) implies equality of the conditional laws for \(p(c)\)-almost every condition. A characteristic product kernel \(k_X(x,x')k_C(c,c')\) can therefore test the joint law. But it weights conditions according to their marginal frequency, so a rare conditional error can have little finite-sample influence. A pooled kernel on \(x\) alone discards \(c\) entirely. A conditional-mean-embedding estimator instead introduces an operator inverse or regularization choice; it is not obtained merely by concatenating \(c\) to \(x\) and writing “conditional MMD.”

    For discrete conditions, report both natural-frequency and macro-averaged stratified MMD. For continuous conditions, declare the condition kernel, localization bandwidth, overlap, boundary behavior, and the region over which the conditional claim is made.

    Practical Kernel Audit¤

    1. Normalize each input dimension or justify the metric.
    2. Plot within-data, within-model, and cross distances.
    3. Sweep bandwidths and feature encoders.
    4. Compare biased, unbiased, and block estimators.
    5. Track gradient magnitude versus sample quality.
    6. Test synthetic alternatives: missing mode, wrong variance, memorization, and corrupted structure.

    Worked example: the median heuristic misses a rare mode¤

    Let \(99\%\) of data lie near zero and \(1\%\) near 100. A model reproduces only the common component. In small minibatches, most batches contain no rare sample, so any empirical MMD is blind to the missing mode. Even when the full-dataset median distance sets a sensible local bandwidth near zero, rare-mode evidence enters only through the few cross terms containing rare points.

    Three fixes address different problems: stratified batches increase rare-mode estimator exposure; a multi-scale kernel changes distance sensitivity; condition- or group-wise metrics expose the failure directly. None changes the generator family or guarantees it allocates the correct \(1\%\) mass.

    Counterexample: the training kernel certifies itself

    Evaluating only with the same learned feature kernel used for training can reward shared blind spots. An independent representation, task metric, or targeted synthetic alternative is required to detect errors outside the training witness class.

    Chapter 3 Synthesis¤

    MMD's formula is short; choosing and estimating the witness space is the real modeling decision. Rare modes can be absent from batches even when the population kernel is characteristic. Chapter 4 turns to IMLE, whose training signal begins from every observed data point rather than every generated sample.

    Independent practice and assessment map: kernels, power, and calibration¤

    Treat kernel design as a scientific measurement design. Predeclare which alternatives matter, how approximation changes the witness, and which untouched data calibrate the final inference.

    P1 — Build and falsify a kernel comparison protocol¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: the median heuristic maximizes power; a sum of characteristic kernels is harmless under arbitrary learned negative weights; random features preserve exact characteristicness at finite \(R\); a permutation p-value remains valid after selecting the smallest one; matching joint \((X,C)\) automatically gives uniform power over rare conditions; and test statistics with identical population MMD have identical null laws. Explain each failure.
    2. P1.2 — Bandwidth/power laboratory. For two specified Gaussian or mixture alternatives, calculate or simulate within-, between-, and cross-kernel similarities across at least five RBF bandwidths. Compare population/large-sample discrepancy, finite estimator variance, pointwise gradient magnitude, and rejection power. Show why the best training bandwidth and best local-test bandwidth need not coincide.
    3. P1.3 — Approximation ledger. Implement exact quadratic, paired linear, block, and fixed-random-feature estimates on nested sample sizes. Hold the kernel and data fixed, separate estimator variance from random-feature error, compare memory/communication, and test whether resampling random features every update changes gradient variance or mean.
    4. P1.4 — Confirmatory test. Design train/selection/test splits for kernel, encoder, bandwidth, generator checkpoint, and seed selection. Specify a permutation, wild-bootstrap, aggregated, or justified asymptotic calibration; derive what is exchangeable under \(H_0\); and write an invalid reuse protocol beside the valid one so the type-I error failure is explicit.
    5. P1.5 — Conditional and rare-mode audit. For conditions with frequencies \((0.90,0.09,0.01)\), construct a generator that fails only in the rare stratum. Compare pooled-\(X\), joint product-kernel, natural-frequency stratified, and macro-stratified statistics. Calculate the probability that a minibatch of size \(m\) contains no rare example and select a batch/sampling scheme with disclosed reweighting.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Design kernels and controlled alternatives for a graph, set, sequence, function, or physical field. Include invariance tests, high-dimensional scaling, structured corruption, approximate/distributed computation, witness visualization, post-selection calibration, uncertainty, and a second representation that can expose the first kernel's blind spots.

    Retrieval practice: kernels and estimators
    1. Does a low minibatch MMD prove mode coverage?
    2. What changes when random features replace the exact kernel?
    3. Why can a huge RBF bandwidth hide distributional differences?
    4. Why is tuning a kernel on the test set incompatible with a naive calibrated test?
    5. What must a conditional MMD evaluation report?

    Solutions. (1) No. The minibatch, representation, kernel, and estimator may miss a rare or structured mode. (2) Training matches a finite approximate feature mean and incurs approximation plus sampling error. (3) Most similarities approach one and their differences/gradients flatten. (4) Selection changes the null distribution and reuses evidence. (5) Condition kernel/representation, condition distribution, stratification or weighting, rare-condition performance, sample counts, and uncertainty.
