    5. Relations to RL, EBMs, Flows, and Diffusion¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the earlier chapters plus introductory reinforcement learning, EBMs, autoregressive models, continuous flows, and diffusion. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. distinguish terminal reward proportionality from expected-return maximization and entropy over trajectories;
    2. compose an EBM reward with a GFlowNet sampler while tracking energy shifts, sampler error, and joint-training feedback;
    3. distinguish constructive graph flow from invertible normalizing flow and continuous-time probability/flow matching;
    4. derive the unique-path autoregressive special case and identify exactly where multi-path semantic objects break sequence-level equivalence;
    5. state the measure, kernel, density-ratio, and discretization obligations for continuous or diffusion-based GFlowNet extensions; and
    6. classify cross-family claims as algebraic equivalence, distributional equivalence under assumptions, population correspondence, shared estimator, analogy, or hybrid composition.

    Reinforcement Learning¤

    Standard RL maximizes expected return and may concentrate on one optimal trajectory. A GFlowNet targets an entire terminal distribution proportional to reward. Entropy-regularized RL can yield related policies under specific state/reward formulations, but objectives, boundary conditions, and trajectory multiplicity must be aligned before claiming equivalence.

    GFlowNet state flows resemble value functions and local losses resemble TD residuals. The learned quantity is conserved probability flow, not expected cumulative reward.

    Under carefully chosen terminal rewards, entropy regularization, state augmentation, and path corrections, soft-RL and GFlowNet objectives can be transformed into one another. Generative Flow Networks as Entropy-Regularized RL and Discrete Probabilistic Inference as Control in Multi-path Environments make such correspondences explicit. The assumptions matter most in multi-path graphs: ordinary maximum-entropy RL assigns entropy to trajectories, so an object with more paths can receive more aggregate mass unless path multiplicity/backward terms correct it.

    Counterexample. If \(x\) and \(y\) have equal terminal utility but \(x\) has ten action sequences and \(y\) has one, a uniformly maximum-entropy trajectory policy can prefer semantic object \(x\) ten-to-one. A reward-proportional GFlowNet with one unit of object reward each targets one-to-one. The methods coincide only after aligning which distribution—trajectory or object—is regularized.

    Energy-Based Models¤

    Setting \(R(x)=e^{-E(x)}\) makes the target the normalized EBM distribution. A GFlowNet becomes an amortized constructive sampler for that energy. The EBM defines relative terminal probability; the GFlowNet adds a state graph and policy. This is hybrid composition, not an identity between arbitrary EBMs and GFlowNets.

    The composition can be trained jointly: GFlowNets for Discrete Probabilistic Modeling couples a learned energy with a GFlowNet sampler. Then two approximations interact—the energy is learned from data and the sampler is learned from the energy. Negative samples from an imperfect sampler can bias or destabilize the energy update; low GFlowNet balance loss alone cannot validate the learned data distribution.

    Energy shifts illustrate the division of labor. Replacing \(E(x)\) by \(E(x)+c\) multiplies all rewards by \(e^{-c}\), changes \(Z\), and leaves normalized terminal probabilities unchanged. A correct GFlowNet policy should be invariant while its learned source flow rescales.

    Normalizing and Probability Flows¤

    Normalizing flows transport continuous density through bijections; flow matching learns continuous vector fields; GFlowNets conserve scalar mass on constructive edges. The shared word “flow” refers to conservation but the state spaces, equations, and likelihoods differ.

    Table GFN.3. Normalizing and Probability Flows
    Family What flows? Governing object Typical likelihood
    Normalizing flow probability density through an invertible map Jacobian/change of variables exact under invertibility and base density
    Flow matching particles/density along continuous time velocity field and continuity equation often via ODE divergence, not the training loss itself
    GFlowNet nonnegative scalar mass through a construction graph node/edge/path conservation terminal object probability may require a path sum

    This is primarily an implementation analogy around conservation. It becomes a deeper mathematical connection only in a specified generalized state space with defined reference measures and kernels.

    Solid arrows denote a specialization or composition with declared objects; dashed arrows require additional assumptions or express only an analogy. The diagram is a claim map, not a family tree in which shared vocabulary proves algorithmic interchangeability.

    Autoregressive Models¤

    Both sample sequential actions. An autoregressive model is trained from observed terminal sequences/objects by normalized conditional likelihood. A GFlowNet can train from terminal rewards without target samples and corrects for multiple construction paths using backward flow. If every object has one canonical trajectory and reward equals a known data probability, the forward policy resembles an AR factorization.

    For a unique-path prefix tree, choose \(R(x)=p_{\text{data}}(x)\) and \(Z=1\). The exact forward policy satisfies

    \[ P_F(x_t\mid x_{<t})= \frac{\sum_{x_{t+1:T}}p_{\text{data}}(x_{1:T})} {\sum_{x_{t:T}}p_{\text{data}}(x_{1:T})} =\frac{p_{\text{data}}(x_{1:t})}{p_{\text{data}}(x_{<t})}, \]

    the usual conditional factorization. This is an algebraic equivalence at the target distribution, not a claim that reward-based TB has the same gradient estimator, data access, or finite-sample behavior as maximum likelihood.

    When several token/action sequences denote the same object, AR likelihood normally models the sequences present in data. A GFlowNet can instead model an object-level reward and sum across constructions. Conversely, if the desired output really is the full sequence including order, there is no multiplicity to correct.

    Diffusion and Continuous-State GFlowNets¤

    GFlowNet balance ideas extend to continuous and diffusion trajectories. Relative trajectory balance for diffusion posteriors trains an amortized sampler for a reweighted diffusion prior. This is a research hybrid; it does not turn every diffusion loss into ordinary discrete TB.

    Continuous and Hybrid State Spaces¤

    Replacing each sum by an integral is insufficient because points in a continuous space have zero probability and densities depend on a reference measure. A Theory of Continuous Generative Flow Networks formulates flows as measures and transition kernels on measurable spaces, covering discrete, continuous, and hybrid cases under explicit assumptions. Conservation becomes equality of measures over sets, while Radon–Nikodym derivatives provide density-like local relations when they exist.

    A continuous construction may append a real-valued coordinate, place a particle, or grow a hybrid object with discrete type and continuous geometry. The implementation must state:

    • the state and action reference measures;
    • whether transition kernels have densities and on which support;
    • the terminal measure associated with reward density;
    • termination and finite-trajectory assumptions;
    • how sampled log densities include Jacobians or action-volume terms.

    Ignoring an action-space volume factor is the continuous analogue of ignoring path multiplicity. A coordinate reparameterization can then change the learned law even though the semantic reward appears unchanged.

    Diffusion paths add stochastic continuous-time dynamics rather than merely continuous actions. Relative TB methods use a pretrained diffusion/path measure as a reference and reweight terminal outcomes. Their backward/forward likelihood ratios and discretization belong to that construction. Equality of terminal targets does not make their finite-step solver, score loss, or path law identical to a DAG GFlowNet.

    Relationship Labels¤

    Table GFN.4. Relationship Labels
    Statement Label
    Terminal flow equals reward and source flow equals \(Z\) algebraic consequence of conservation
    TB and DB target the same terminal law at zero residual population-optimum correspondence
    GFlowNet residuals resemble TD errors implementation/objective analogy
    EBM reward plus GFlowNet policy hybrid composition
    Diffusion posterior fine-tuning with relative TB extended dynamical construction

    A Six-Label Audit¤

    1. Algebraic equivalence: DB telescopes to a TB identity when one compatible state flow and the same policies are used.
    2. Distributional equivalence under assumptions: a unique-path GFlowNet and an exact AR factorization can define the same terminal law.
    3. Population-optimum correspondence: FM, DB, TB, and SubTB have the reward-proportional terminal solution when their respective global constraints and support assumptions hold.
    4. Shared estimator or objective: particular variational/divergence GFlowNet formulations may share score-function estimators with hierarchical VI.
    5. Implementation analogy: node residuals resemble TD residuals; graph flow resembles conservation in other flow families.
    6. Hybrid system composition: an EBM supplies reward to a GFlowNet; a diffusion prior supplies a reference path measure to relative TB.

    Calling every item “equivalent” erases the assumptions that determine whether an algorithm can be substituted for another.

    Worked example: one two-object target, four different path laws¤

    Let semantic objects \(x\) and \(y\) each have target mass \(1/2\), but let \(x\) have two construction paths while \(y\) has one. A GFlowNet may allocate \(x\)'s mass as \((1/4,1/4)\) and \(y\)'s as \(1/2\). A uniform distribution over the three trajectories instead produces semantic law \((2/3,1/3)\). A unique-path autoregressive representation can recover \((1/2,1/2)\) only after choosing one canonical sequence per object. An EBM with \(E(x)=E(y)\) specifies the desired relative object mass but not any of these construction-path allocations.

    Thus the same two-number terminal target can be paired with several path spaces and algorithms. The path law, data access, objective, and estimator must be stated before transferring a theorem or implementation.

    Chapter 5 retrieval Questions¤

    1. Why can maximum-entropy RL and a GFlowNet disagree on a multi-path graph?
    2. What additional object is required to define continuous GFlowNet densities?
    3. Does \(R=e^{-E}\) make an EBM and GFlowNet the same model?
    Complete solutions
    1. Entropy over trajectories rewards path multiplicity, whereas a GFlowNet can target object-level reward after summing paths.
    2. A reference measure (and compatible transition/terminal measures) is needed; density is not coordinate-free.
    3. No. The EBM specifies relative terminal mass; the GFlowNet adds a constructive graph and amortized sampler. This is hybrid composition.

    Chapter 5 Synthesis¤

    GFlowNets borrow sequential policies from RL, unnormalized targets from EBMs, and conservation language from flows, yet define a distinct reward-proportional constructive sampler. Chapter 6 turns that distinction into evaluation and deployment requirements.

    Is a GFlowNet just an RL agent with a diversity bonus?

    No. Its population target is a normalized distribution over terminal objects, and balance accounts for all construction trajectories. Particular entropy-RL formulations may correspond under assumptions.

    Independent practice and assessment map: cross-family relations¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Equal-utility objects \(x,y\) have 8 and 2 construction trajectories. Predict the semantic law under a uniform maximum-entropy trajectory policy and under equal object reward in an exact GFlowNet; calculate both and name the correction an RL correspondence needs.
    2. P1.2 — EBM composition. For energies \((0,\log2,\log4)\), compute the reward-proportional law. Add constant 7 to every energy and derive the changes to reward, \(Z\), and policy. Then explain how a biased GFlowNet negative sampler can affect joint EBM training even though a fixed exact EBM target is unchanged by the shift.
    3. P1.3 — Flow taxonomy. For each of normalizing flow, continuous flow matching, and GFlowNet, state the moving object, governing equation, required normalization/change-of-measure device, and typical likelihood route. Explain which shared statement is mathematical and which is only vocabulary-level analogy.
    4. P1.4 — Autoregressive derivation. On the unique-path binary distribution \(p(00,01,10,11)=(0.1,0.2,0.3,0.4)\), derive every first- and second-token forward conditional from suffix sums. Then merge sequences 01 and 10 into one semantic object and show why sequence likelihood alone no longer supplies its probability without aggregation.
    5. P1.5 — Continuous transfer. An action coordinate changes from \(u\in(0,1)\) to \(v=u^2\). Write the density transformation and identify the missing Jacobian if a continuous GFlowNet reuses the same numerical density in \(v\). State the additional path-measure and discretization ledger needed for a diffusion-relative-TB claim.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Classify six claims: DB telescopes to TB; unique-path AR and GFlowNet share a target; FM/DB/TB share a terminal population solution; a divergence GFlowNet shares a score-function estimator with VI; GFlowNet residuals resemble TD; and an EBM feeds reward to a GFlowNet. For each non-algebraic claim, list one assumption that prevents unconditional substitution.
    Outcome-to-task alignment: valid correspondences¤
    Table GFN.11. Outcome-to-task alignment: valid correspondences
    Learning objective Independently assessed by
    O1 — Separate reward proportionality from return and trajectory entropy; evidence is the unequal-path calculation P1.1
    O2 — Compose EBMs and GFlowNets with error accounting; evidence is the energy-shift and joint-training audit P1.2
    O3 — Distinguish three meanings of flow; evidence is the governing-object taxonomy P1.3
    O4 — Derive and bound the AR special case; evidence is the binary suffix-sum and merge calculation P1.4
    O5 — State continuous/diffusion obligations; evidence is the coordinate-Jacobian and path-measure ledger P1.5
    O6 — Classify relationships precisely; evidence is the six-claim transfer audit P1.6
    Complete solutions to P1
    1. Uniform mass over 10 trajectories gives \(P(x)=8/10\) and \(P(y)=2/10\). Equal object rewards give \(P(x)=P(y)=1/2\), with each object's mass divided among its paths by a backward/path allocation. An entropy-RL correspondence must align object versus trajectory entropy, usually through state augmentation and path/backward corrections; a generic diversity bonus is insufficient.
    2. Rewards are \((1,1/2,1/4)\), \(Z=7/4\), and \(p=(4/7,2/7,1/7)\). Adding 7 multiplies every reward by \(e^{-7}\), so \(Z'=e^{-7}Z\) and normalized probabilities and an exact policy remain unchanged. In joint training, however, an approximate sampler determines which negative states update the learned energy; missing or misweighted modes can bias that update, after which the target energy itself changes.
    3. A normalizing flow transports density through an invertible map and uses the Jacobian change-of-variables formula. Flow matching evolves a density/particles under a velocity field satisfying a continuity equation and commonly evaluates likelihood through ODE divergence integration. A GFlowNet moves nonnegative scalar mass through a constructive graph, using node/edge/path conservation and a terminal path sum. Conservation of mass is a shared mathematical motif; it does not make the state spaces, objectives, Jacobians, kernels, or likelihood computations interchangeable.
    4. \(P_F(x_1=0)=0.3\) and \(P_F(x_1=1)=0.7\). Conditionals are \(P(0\mid0)=1/3\), \(P(1\mid0)=2/3\), \(P(0\mid1)=3/7\), and \(P(1\mid1)=4/7\). Products reproduce the four sequence masses. If 01 and 10 denote one object, its semantic probability is \(0.2+0.3=0.5\); neither individual sequence likelihood is the object likelihood. A canonical unique-path encoding or explicit aggregation is required.
    5. Since \(u=\sqrt v\), \(p_V(v)=p_U(\sqrt v)/(2\sqrt v)\). Copying \(p_U(u)\) numerically into \(v\) omits \(|du/dv|=1/(2\sqrt v)\) and changes probability mass. A diffusion-relative-TB transfer must also state the reference forward path measure, backward/reverse likelihood ratio, terminal tilt, time direction, absolute-continuity support, discretization, and which finite-step algorithm is evaluated.
    6. The labels are, respectively: algebraic equivalence for compatible state flow; distributional equivalence under a unique-path and matched-target assumption; population-optimum correspondence under global constraints and support; shared estimator/objective only for the specified variational formulation; implementation analogy; and hybrid composition. The latter five do not license unconditional substitution because path multiplicity/data access, coverage/parameterization, exact divergence construction, different learned quantities, and sampler–energy approximation feedback remain different.
