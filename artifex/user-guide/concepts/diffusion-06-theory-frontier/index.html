    Comparison with Other Generative Models¤

    Comparison with Other Generative Models: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. classify diffusion–flow, reverse-SDE–probability-flow, stochastic-interpolant, Schrödinger-bridge, EDM, consistency, and adversarial-distillation relationships using explicit assumptions;
    2. compare families at matched operating points; and
    3. attach maturity and protocol boundaries to research results.

    Diffusion vs. GANs¤

    Table DIFF.10. Diffusion vs. GANs
    Aspect Diffusion Models GANs
    Sample quality Strong across many image/video protocols; depends on data, guidance, sampler, codec, and metric Strong on selected image/domain protocols; depends on truncation, objective, regularization, and metric
    Training stability Regression avoids a two-player game but can fail through weighting, precision, conditioning, scale, or optimization Game dynamics require player-specific diagnostics; instability is not inevitable and no fixed stabilizer set is mandatory
    Mode coverage Iterative stochastic models can cover diverse modes, but finite objectives, guidance, data, and solvers can still lose coverage Mode collapse is a known, measurable failure rather than a family certainty
    Inference Speed 20–1000 steps un-distilled; 1–4 steps distilled 1 forward pass
    Training diagnostics Per-time regression, likelihood/path, and sample diagnostics Critic/generalization, game, and sample diagnostics
    Controllability Many guidance, editing, and posterior-sampling constructions; correctness is method-specific Conditional critics and latent editing can be effective; prompt-system comparisons are protocol-specific
    Latent Space Implicit in the noise trajectory; no encoder Implicit; trained encoder optional (ALI, BiGAN)

    Speed comparison contract: report network/decoder calls, parameters and active parameters, resolution, guidance batching, precision, batch size, compiler, hardware, warm-up, peak memory, latency distribution, and achieved quality. A single-pass large DiT can be slower than a smaller multi-step latent model; NFE alone cannot rank systems.

    When to Use Each

    Choose Diffusion: - Quality/diversity paramount - Training stability important - Denoising, super-resolution, inpainting tasks - Computational resources available

    Choose GANs: - Real-time generation required - Single-pass critical (e.g., video style transfer) - Limited inference compute - Interactive applications

    Diffusion vs. VAEs¤

    Table DIFF.11. Diffusion vs. VAEs
    Aspect Diffusion Models VAEs
    Sample quality Objective, representation, sampler, and protocol dependent Observation likelihood and latent/prior design dependent; “VAE blur” is not universal
    Latent space Observed-space diffusion need not have a compact inferred latent; latent diffusion explicitly uses a codec Explicit probabilistic latents, whose interpretability and editability must be measured
    Likelihood Discrete ELBO or probability-flow density estimates under their assumptions; generally approximate numerically ELBO and importance bounds; exact likelihood only when an inference gap vanishes or a tractable model permits it
    Training Regression/path training with method-specific failures Variational training with collapse, likelihood-scale, and inference failures
    Inference Speed Multi-step (1–1000 NFEs) Single forward pass
    Role in modern stacks The generator in latent-space pipelines The codec feeding diffusion (SD-VAE, video VAEs)

    Hybrid Approach: Latent Diffusion¤

    VAEs and diffusion can be alternatives or complementary stages. Stable Diffusion, SDXL, SD3, FLUX.1, and many open video models use an autoencoder-like codec before diffusion or flow matching in latent space. Sora's public report describes compressed spacetime patches but not enough codec detail for a precise VAE classification. A codec can reduce token/spatial cost substantially, but speed, memory, and distortion depend on compression factor, channels, decoder, architecture, and matched-quality protocol; see Representations and Multimodal Systems and the VAE tokenizer section.

    Diffusion vs. Autoregressive Models¤

    Table DIFF.12. Diffusion vs. Autoregressive Models
    Aspect Diffusion Autoregressive
    Generation order Many tokens in parallel per step; arbitrary positions revisited Strictly left-to-right (or fixed scan order)
    Per-image / per-sequence cost \(K\) NFEs × full forward pass (parallel over tokens) \(N\) tokens × full forward pass (sequential)
    Quality (images / video / audio) Often strongest for high-fidelity visual / audio synthesis Strong (VAR, MAR); previously trailed diffusion
    Quality (text) Catching up in selected settings — LLaDA, Mercury, Dream, and ReFusion show competitive 8B-scale results Still the dominant production choice
    Controllability Revising arbitrary positions is natural for several kernels; guidance correctness is method-specific Prefix conditioning is standard, while infilling, constrained decoding, and alternative orderings broaden control
    Variable length Fixed-length outputs (or padded) Natural variable-length

    Complementary strengths. Diffusion, flow, and autoregressive constructions all appear across media and language, with their relative performance depending on representation, conditioning, compute, and evaluation protocol. Diffusion language models are covered in Distillation and Discrete State Spaces → Diffusion Language Models; VAR / MAR systems (Tian et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) illustrate image generation with autoregressive or masked objectives. Modality alone does not determine the family.

    Hybrid Models:

    HART — Hybrid Autoregressive Transformer (Tang et al., 2024):

    • Autoregressive for coarse structure
    • Diffusion for fine details
    • The paper reports up to a 9× inference comparison and 31% lower training cost against its selected diffusion baselines under named image protocols; these are system-level, baseline-specific results rather than composable family constants

    Theoretical Frameworks¤

    The density–latent–energy relationship guide separates diffusion's exact hierarchical latent formulation, noisy marginal scores, and population-optimum denoising identities.

    The probability-paths guide derives the reverse SDE/probability-flow ODE relationship and states why equal continuous-time marginals do not imply equal finite-step samplers.

    Beyond the basic DDPM, score-based, and flow-matching formulations, EDM and conditional flow matching separate several training and sampling choices that are often conflated. Flow matching and rectified flow are covered in Flow Matching and Rectified-Flow Systems; EDM is covered next.

    EDM: Elucidating the Design Space¤

    The EDM line of work — Karras et al., 2022 (NeurIPS) — Elucidating the Design Space and Karras et al., 2024 (CVPR Oral) — Analyzing and Improving Training Dynamics — provides a common framework separating the otherwise-tangled design choices (preconditioning, schedule, sampler, EMA):

    Optimal Preconditioning:

    Normalizing inputs/outputs of network for better training:

    \[ D_\theta(x, \sigma) = c_{\text{skip}}(\sigma) x + c_{\text{out}}(\sigma) F_\theta(c_{\text{in}}(\sigma) x; c_{\text{noise}}(\sigma)) \]

    Karras / EDM \(\rho\)-schedule for \(N\) sampling levels:

    \[ \sigma_i = \left( \sigma_{\max}^{1/\rho} + \frac{i}{N-1} \left(\sigma_{\min}^{1/\rho}-\sigma_{\max}^{1/\rho}\right) \right)^\rho, \qquad i=0,\ldots,N-1. \]

    The exponent \(\rho\) controls how solver steps concentrate across noise scales; EDM commonly uses \(\rho=7\) for image experiments. Geometric interpolation \(\sigma(t)=\sigma_{\min}^{1-t}\sigma_{\max}^{t}\) is a different log-linear schedule. Schedule quality depends on the trained model, solver, discretization budget, and data—not a universal ordering over “linear” or “cosine” schedules.

    The EDM paper reports FID 1.79 on CIFAR-10 under its sampler and evaluation protocol. EDM2 analyzes training dynamics, introduces magnitude-preserving architectural changes and post-hoc EMA reconstruction, and reports FID 1.81 on ImageNet 512² under its evaluated configuration. These values are reference points for the named experiments, not timeless rankings or matched cross-dataset comparisons.

    For distillation, diffusion language models, post-training, and video, see Distillation, Flow Matching, and Discrete State Spaces. For backbone architectures see Beyond U-Net above.

    Distillation, Flow Matching, and Discrete State Spaces¤

    The transitions and few-step guide separates progressive, consistency, adversarial, distribution-matching, and inverse distillation by the teacher behavior each objective actually preserves.

    Diffusion research spans inference acceleration, DiT scaling, language and video state spaces, conditioning, and preference/reward post-training. This section organizes those threads using primary papers and surveys including Yang et al., Diffusion Models: A Comprehensive Survey of Methods and Applications, Efficient Diffusion Models, Efficient Diffusion Models: A Survey, and the Survey of Video Diffusion Models.

    Few-Step Distillation: From 50 Steps to 1¤

    Distillation and transition-learning methods target one- to few-evaluation generation. Their quality, teacher cost, and exact NFE accounting are protocol-specific:

    Table DIFF.13. Few-Step Distillation: From 50 Steps to 1
    Method Year Idea Steps Notable result
    Progressive Distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022) 2022 Iteratively halve the teacher's step count. 4 FID 3.0 on CIFAR-10.
    Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023) 2023 Self-consistency map: every point on a trajectory decodes to the endpoint. 1–2 FID 3.55 → 2.51 → 1.73 (CTM, Kim et al., 2024).
    Latent Consistency Models / LCM-LoRA (Luo et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023) 2023 Distill latent-diffusion trajectories; LCM-LoRA represents the consistency student as a low-rank adapter. 2–4 The papers report 768² generation and a named training-cost protocol.
    Adversarial Diffusion Distillation (ADD) / SDXL Turbo (Sauer et al., 2023) 2023 Add a discriminator with a frozen DINOv2 backbone to score distillation. 1–4 Reported quality and latency comparisons are system- and prompt-protocol specific.
    Score Identity Distillation (SiD / SiDA) (Zhou et al., ICML 2024) 2024 Use score-related identities; SiDA adds an adversarial real-data signal. 1 Paper-reported FID 1.499 on CIFAR-10 and 1.110 on ImageNet 64² under distinct protocols.
    DMD / DMD2 (Yin et al., 2024 — NeurIPS Oral) 2024 Distribution-matching objectives; DMD2 adds a real-data adversarial loss and revised optimization. 1 Paper-reported FID 1.28 on ImageNet 64² and 8.35 on zero-shot COCO 2014 under distinct protocols.
    Diffusion2GAN (Kang et al., 2024) 2024 Distill into a conditional GAN with E-LatentLPIPS. 1 The paper evaluates one-step high-resolution generation under named teacher and perceptual metrics.
    APT — Adversarial Post-Training (Lin et al., 2025) 2025 Extend adversarial post-training to one-step video with an approximated R1 term. 1 The paper reports comparisons with disclosed and closed video systems; its broad comparison label does not define a reproducible model class.
    Few-step SiD (Zhou et al., 2025) 2025 Generalize the SiD objective to multiple student transitions. 2–4 Quality changes with the evaluated step count and training protocol.

    Adversarial losses define one branch of diffusion distillation alongside consistency, progressive, score-identity, and distribution-matching objectives. A discriminator in the student loss creates a hybrid composition; it does not erase the diffusion teacher or make every few-step student a GAN. See GANs Explained — Adversarial Distillation.

    Flow Matching and Rectified-Flow Systems¤

    Continuous-time flow models are common in large-scale visual generators, especially when paired with Transformer backbones.

    Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2023 — ICLR) trains a vector field \(v_\theta(x, t)\) to regress against a target conditional probability path (Gaussian, optimal-transport, etc.):

    • Simulation-free conditional regression — the standard conditional flow-matching loss does not solve the learned ODE during each training example.
    • Path-dependent optimization — numerical conditioning and learned error depend on the probability path, coupling, time distribution, weight, architecture, and optimizer; stability relative to DDPM must be measured.
    • Declared endpoint couplings — conditional paths can be built from independent, optimal-transport-inspired, minibatch, or problem-specific couplings. Stochastic interpolants (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Albergo et al., 2024) provide a broader ODE/SDE construction.

    Rectified Flow (Liu, Gong & Liu, 2023 — ICLR) is a special case that learns ODEs following straight paths between noise and data:

    \[ \frac{dx}{dt} = v_\theta(x, t), \]

    where the conditional trajectories are straight lines \(\psi_t = (1-t)x_0 + t\, x_1\). The reflow operation iteratively straightens trajectories:

    1. Sample endpoint pairs \((x_0, x_1)\) from an initial data–reference coupling.
    2. Train \(v_\theta\) on straight conditional paths between those paired endpoints; the learned marginal trajectories may still curve.
    3. Solve the learned ODE to induce a new coupling between reference and generated endpoints.
    4. Retrain on straight conditional paths under the induced coupling.
    5. Repeat.

    InstaFlow (Liu et al., 2023) evaluates one-step text-to-image generation after reflow and distillation and reports roughly 0.12 seconds per image in its disclosed hardware setting. The latency is not architecture- or hardware-independent.

    System scale: rectified-flow and flow-matching objectives appear in MMDiT-based Stable Diffusion 3 / 3.5 (Esser et al., 2024), FLUX.1 (Black Forest Labs, 2024), and several video systems. Product names do not establish the exact objective, weighting, or sampler without a corresponding technical disclosure.

    Representation Alignment for DiT Training¤

    REPA (Yu et al., ICLR 2025 Oral) aligns selected DiT activations to a frozen self-supervised encoder. The paper reports a 17.5× reduction in training iterations for a named SiT-XL comparison and guided FID 1.42 on ImageNet 256². This is not a hardware-independent wall-clock factor or an unguided architecture result. The representation volume derives the general auxiliary objective, its compute ledger, and a failure case in which the teacher collapses a distinction required by generation. Follow-ups test U-Net placement, relational and spectral losses, internal diagnostics, and joint perception–generation systems under their own protocols.

    Distillation, Flow Matching, and Discrete State Spaces: Diffusion Language Models¤

    Discrete diffusion applies categorical corruption and iterative refinement to text and other token sequences. D3PM, absorbing-mask models, and continuous-time categorical processes lead to several language-model constructions:

    • LLaDA (Nie et al., 2025) — a masked-diffusion LLM trained from scratch at 8B parameters, competitive with LLaMA3-8B on in-context learning and instruction following after SFT.
    • Mercury (Inception Labs, 2025) — a commercial-scale diffusion LLM with multi-token parallel decoding, reporting substantially lower latency than autoregressive baselines in selected settings.
    • ReFusion (2025) — interleaves diffusion-based selection with autoregressive infilling, enabling KV-cache reuse; 18× speedup on average.
    • Dream / SEDD and the broader masked-diffusion-LM family report competitive scaling on reasoning and language benchmarks at similar parameter counts, but the broad production comparison with AR LLMs is still early.

    Masked/discrete diffusion LMs can update many positions per evaluation, trading sequential token dependence for repeated full-sequence passes. Latency still scales with sequence length through attention/state computation, refinement evaluations, remasking, cacheability, and hardware; it is not decoupled from length. See Discrete Iterative Generation for peer-reviewed theory and sampler-correctness boundaries.

    Open-Source Video Diffusion¤

    Several public video systems—including HunyuanVideo, Wan, Mochi-1, CogVideoX, and LTX-Video—combine a spatiotemporal codec with a U-Net, DiT, or multimodal transformer and a declared diffusion or flow objective. They do not all use the same codec, target, attention pattern, conditioning path, or sampler, so the diagram below is a component template rather than a classification of every release.

    The full per-model list with citations and benchmark numbers is in Diffusion Model Variants → Video Diffusion Models. The VAE side of this stack (CV-VAE, WF-VAE, IV-VAE, OD-VAE) is covered in the VAE explainer's tokenizer section.

    Post-Training: RLHF, DPO, Reward Models for Diffusion¤

    Post-training is one major research axis alongside pretraining, architecture, representations, data, and sampling:

    • DPO for diffusion (Wallace et al., 2024 — Diffusion-DPO) — direct preference optimisation in \(x_0\)-space, no separate reward model needed.
    • DDPO (Black et al., 2024) — REINFORCE-style RL with reward models (CLIP, aesthetic, OCR).
    • Reward-guided distillation (Kim et al., 2024) — fine-tune the distilled student against a reward model.
    • RAFT and related reward-model fine-tuning form a broad thread. Public systems change preference data, captioning, filtering, base model, and inference jointly, so ecosystem-wide quality gains cannot be attributed to reward fine-tuning without controlled ablations.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • FreqFlow (Ren et al., CVPR 2026) adds frequency-aware branches and time-dependent weighting to latent flow matching and reports FID 1.38 on class-conditional ImageNet 256 under its protocol. Peer-reviewed research: this is a flow-matching system result, not a cross-protocol ranking of all diffusion families.
    • BiFM (Dai et al., CVPR 2026) jointly learns forward inversion and reverse generation over time intervals for few-step editing. Peer-reviewed research: “one-step inversion” is learned under its objective and evaluated tasks, not exact inversion of arbitrary images through any flow model.
    • WaDi (Wang et al., CVPR 2026) uses low-rank rotations for parameter-efficient variational score distillation. Peer-reviewed research: its one-step COCO results and roughly 10% trainable-parameter claim are architecture/protocol bounded.
    • EDA (Qiu et al., CVPR 2026) studies EDM-style design choices with non-Gaussian and spatially structured corruptions for image restoration. Peer-reviewed research: the paper shows that Gaussian corruption is a design choice rather than a family definition in its restoration settings; it does not establish one arbitrary-noise schedule as universally superior for unconditional generation.
    • APEX (Liu et al., 2026) derives a discriminator-free condition-shifted update described as GAN-aligned for one-step flow generation. Preprint: objective correspondence does not make it an external-discriminator GAN, and author benchmarks await independent reproduction.

    A Brief Note on Critiques of Diffusion¤

    Several lines of work question which parts of diffusion are essential:

    • MAR (Li et al., 2024) shows masked-AR generation can match diffusion quality.
    • VAR (Tian et al., 2024) — visual autoregression with next-scale prediction; first AR model to beat DiT on ImageNet.
    • LDM-without-VAE (2025) — argues that a VAE bottleneck can hurt its diffusion setup and explores a VAE-free latent construction.

    These are not replacements for diffusion as a whole. They are controlled opportunities to ask which benefit came from the probability path, architecture, representation, objective, transition parameterization, or systems implementation rather than attributing it to a family name.

    Worked example: equal marginals do not imply equal algorithms¤

    Assume an exact reverse SDE and its probability-flow ODE start from the same terminal distribution. At every continuous time they have the same marginal density, but conditional on one initial noise the ODE follows one deterministic trajectory while the SDE produces a distribution over trajectories. Euler integration of the ODE and Euler–Maruyama integration of the SDE then introduce different finite-step errors. Adding CFG changes the field again. Therefore “same marginals under exact dynamics” is a distributional equivalence under assumptions, not algebraic equivalence of samplers or equality at a fixed NFE.

    Independent practice and assessment map: theory and research¤

    Label each cross-family relationship by its strongest justified mathematical or compositional type, then bound every empirical result by maturity, protocol, and reproduction evidence.

    P1 — Build an assumption-bearing relationship and evidence ledger¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Classify each claim before consulting a benchmark: reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE are the same algorithm; VP \(v\) is every flow-matching velocity; every stochastic interpolant is a Schrödinger bridge; EDM is only a sampling schedule; a consistency map is an exact coarse ODE solver; a DiT result establishes a diffusion-objective result; an adversarially distilled student is a pure GAN with no diffusion dependency; equal endpoint marginals identify the same coupling; and a one-step preprint establishes a family-wide speed ranking. For each, choose algebraic identity, population-optimum correspondence, distributional equivalence, variational or control construction, composition/hybrid, controlled empirical comparison, loose analogy, or false claim, and list the assumptions needed to upgrade it.
    2. P1.2 — Matched family comparison. Design a comparison among one diffusion/score model, flow-matching model, GAN or adversarial student, VAE-based generator, and autoregressive model. Match dataset, representation or codec, conditioning, output resolution, training and distillation compute, active parameters, hardware, latency definition, and evaluation sample count. Specify fidelity, coverage, likelihood/bounds where meaningful, alignment, calibration, and uncertainty, then state which axes cannot be collapsed into one ranking.
    3. P1.3 — Derive the correspondence boundary. For a declared VP Gaussian path, derive the relation among conditional noise, marginal score, VP angular velocity, reverse-SDE drift, and probability-flow velocity. For a linear rectified-flow path, derive its conditional velocity separately. Identify the time rescaling or path condition required for a VP-\(v\) relation, then show why one coarse numerical step need not preserve any continuous-time marginal equivalence. Add an assumption table locating stochastic interpolants, entropy-regularized Schrödinger bridges, EDM preconditioning/sampling choices, and consistency maps relative to those exact identities without collapsing their objectives or finite algorithms.
    4. P1.4 — Evidence maturity audit. Select one peer-reviewed checkpoint, one preprint, and one technical report from the chapter. Record claim, source type, dataset, model and active parameters, codec, objective, guidance, sampler, NFE/calls, hardware, metric implementation, uncertainty, released artifacts, independent reproduction, and verification date. Rewrite each headline as the strongest warranted statement and define the evidence that would change its maturity label.
    5. P1.5 — Transfer. Recast one diffusion–flow, diffusion–AR, or diffusion–adversarial relationship for a masked language model, protein generator, or molecular system. Declare the state space, path or kernel, coupling, trained target, finite sampler, comparison baseline, scientific or semantic constraints, and evidence maturity. Give one mathematical or executable counterexample that prevents analogy from being reported as equivalence.

    Distillation, Flow Matching, and Discrete State Spaces: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    The modern landscape is organized better by probability path, state space, trained field, and transition than by brand names. Diffusion, flow matching, consistency, and adversarial distillation can share estimators or compose in a system without becoming identical methods.

    Retrieval practice: theory and research
    1. Which relationship holds between an exact reverse SDE and its probability-flow ODE?
    2. Why is FreqFlow not evidence that every flow model has FID 1.38?
    3. What does BiFM add beyond ordinary deterministic inversion?
    4. Why is APEX both GAN-aligned and discriminator-free without contradiction?

    Solutions. (1) Equal one-time marginals under the score/SDE assumptions, not identical paths or finite algorithms. (2) The result belongs to a particular architecture, dataset, conditioning, guidance, sampler, and metric protocol. (3) It trains interval-conditioned forward and reverse fields with bidirectional consistency for its few-step tasks. (4) Its derived update has an adversarial objective correspondence but obtains the signal from condition-shifted flow fields rather than an external discriminator.
