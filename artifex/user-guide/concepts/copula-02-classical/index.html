    2. Classical Families, Rank Dependence, and Tails¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and elementary multivariate Gaussian and Student distributions. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Use reference copulas and bounds correctly: distinguish independence, comonotonicity, bivariate countermonotonicity, and invalid higher-dimensional lower envelopes.
    2. O2 — Compare elliptical dependence: translate correlation parameters into rank and tail behavior for Gaussian and Student-\(t\) copulas.
    3. O3 — Audit Archimedean validity: connect generator conditions, dimension, exchangeability, rotations, and asymmetric tail dependence.
    4. O4 — Recognize extreme-value structure: distinguish tail dependence from max-stability and stable tail-dependence functions.
    5. O5 — Interpret dependence summaries: separate Pearson correlation, rank concordance, pairwise tails, and the full multivariate copula.
    6. O6 — Select and transfer a family: justify a dependence family from symmetry, tail, dimension, and query requirements rather than its name or central fit alone.

    Bounds and reference copulas¤

    Every \(d\)-copula obeys the Fréchet–Hoeffding bounds

    \[ \max\!\left(1-d+\sum_{j=1}^d u_j,0\right) \le C(u_1,\ldots,u_d)\le \min_j u_j. \]

    The upper bound \(M(u)=\min_j u_j\) is a copula in every dimension and represents comonotonic variables \(U_1=\cdots=U_d\). The lower bound is the countermonotone copula \(W(u,v)=\max(u+v-1,0)\) only in two dimensions; for \(d\ge3\) the displayed lower envelope is generally not a copula. There is no universal multivariate analogue in which every pair can be perfectly countermonotone.

    The independence copula

    \[ \Pi(u_1,\ldots,u_d)=\prod_{j=1}^d u_j \]

    has density one. These references are useful exact baselines, but real dependence need not lie on a one-dimensional path between them.

    Elliptical copulas¤

    For correlation matrix \(R\), the Gaussian copula is

    \[ C_R^{\mathrm G}(u) =\Phi_R\!\left(\Phi^{-1}(u_1),\ldots,\Phi^{-1}(u_d)\right), \]

    where \(\Phi_R\) is a centered multivariate normal CDF with correlation \(R\). The marginal variances and means disappear under the transform; only \(R\) remains. Sampling is simple: draw \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,R)\) and set \(U_j=\Phi(Z_j)\).

    For a nondegenerate bivariate Gaussian copula with \(|\rho|<1\),

    \[ \lambda_L=\lambda_U=0. \]

    This does not mean the variables are independent. It says that asymptotic co-exceedance vanishes at the tail limit. Finite-threshold joint risk can still be substantial.

    The Student-\(t\) copula replaces the Gaussian vector by a standardized multivariate \(t\) vector with degrees of freedom \(\nu\). In the bivariate case its symmetric tail coefficient is

    \[ \boxed{ \lambda_L=\lambda_U =2t_{\nu+1}\!\left( -\sqrt{\frac{(\nu+1)(1-\rho)}{1+\rho}} \right), } \]

    where \(t_{\nu+1}\) is a univariate Student-\(t\) CDF. Shared radial scale variation creates joint extremes even when ordinary correlation matches a Gaussian copula.

    Archimedean copulas¤

    An Archimedean construction uses a decreasing generator \(\psi:[0,\infty)\to[0,1]\):

    \[ C(u_1,\ldots,u_d) =\psi\!\left(\psi^{-1}(u_1)+\cdots+\psi^{-1}(u_d)\right). \]

    Not every decreasing function is valid in every dimension. The precise finite-dimensional requirement is \(d\)-monotonicity under the standard generator conventions; complete monotonicity supports all dimensions through a Laplace-transform representation. This constraint is central when a neural network parameterizes \(\psi\).

    For the bivariate Clayton family,

    \[ C_\theta(u,v) =\left(u^{-\theta}+v^{-\theta}-1\right)^{-1/\theta}, \qquad \theta>0, \]

    with

    \[ \lambda_L=2^{-1/\theta}, \qquad \lambda_U=0. \]

    It emphasizes lower-tail association. The Gumbel family instead has upper-tail dependence and zero lower-tail dependence under its usual positive-dependence parameter range. Rotations of bivariate copulas move tail behavior among corners, but a rotation also changes the interpretation of concordance and should be reported.

    Archimedean families are parsimonious and interpretable. A single exchangeable generator is often too restrictive in high dimensions; nested or hierarchical constructions broaden the structure but require compatibility conditions among generators.

    Extreme-value copulas¤

    Bivariate extreme-value copulas can be written

    \[ C(u,v)=\exp\{-\ell(-\log u,-\log v)\}, \]

    where the stable tail-dependence function \(\ell\) is convex, homogeneous of order one, and satisfies

    \[ \max(x,y)\le\ell(x,y)\le x+y. \]

    These copulas arise as limits for componentwise maxima and encode max-stability:

    \[ C(u^{1/m},v^{1/m})^m=C(u,v). \]

    An arbitrary tail-dependent copula is not necessarily an extreme-value copula. Max-stability is the distinguishing structural property.

    Rank dependence is copula dependence—under continuity¤

    For continuous bivariate variables, Kendall's tau and Spearman's rho depend only on \(C\):

    \[ \tau=4\int_{[0,1]^2}C(u,v)\,dC(u,v)-1, \]
    \[ \rho_S=12\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 \bigl(C(u,v)-uv\bigr)\,du\,dv. \]

    Strictly increasing marginal transformations preserve the copula and these rank measures. Ties change their empirical and population normalization; formulas from the continuous case cannot simply be reused for count data.

    Complete worked example: equal correlation, unequal extremes¤

    Choose a Gaussian copula and a \(t\) copula with the same \(\rho=0.5\). For the Gaussian copula, \(\lambda_U=0\). For the \(t\) copula with \(\nu=4\),

    \[ \lambda_U =2t_5\!\left(-\sqrt{\frac{5(1-0.5)}{1+0.5}}\right) =2t_5(-\sqrt{5/3})\approx0.253. \]

    Both models can have similar central elliptical contours and the same linear correlation parameter, yet the limiting probability of a joint upper-tail event is qualitatively different. A validation set concentrated near the center may fail to distinguish them; a tail-risk query can distinguish them immediately.

    Choose structure before a family label¤

    This is not an automatic selector. It prevents a common inversion of reasoning: fitting a familiar family first and only afterward asking what kind of dependence it was capable of expressing.

    Independent practice and assessment map: classical copulas¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. A Gaussian copula and a \(t_4\) copula both use \(\rho=0.5\). Predict which has larger asymptotic upper-tail dependence, then compute the two coefficients using the formulas in this chapter.
    2. P1.2 — Refute a false multivariate bound. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Let \(W_3(u)=\max(u_1+u_2+u_3-2,0)\). Compute its signed volume on \((1/2,1]^3\) and use the result to decide whether \(W_3\) is a three-copula.
    3. P1.3 — Read tail asymmetry. Type: derive · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For a Clayton copula with \(\theta=2\), compute \(\lambda_L\) and \(\lambda_U\), then state which joint corner the unrotated family emphasizes.
    4. P1.4 — Translate correlation into concordance. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 8 min. A Gaussian copula has \(\rho=1/2\). Use \(\tau=(2/\pi)\arcsin\rho\) to compute Kendall's tau and explain why neither number identifies a non-Gaussian copula.
    5. P1.5 — Check max-stability. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. At \((u,v)=(0.25,0.81)\) and \(m=2\), verify \(C(u^{1/m},v^{1/m})^m=C(u,v)\) for both the independence and comonotone copulas. Explain why passing this check at one point is not a proof for an arbitrary model.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Choose an initial family class for a three-variable flood-risk problem with strong upper-corner association, weak lower-corner association, and nonexchangeable pairs. State which simple families you reject, what evidence you would inspect, and how little tail data limits the claim.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The Gaussian coefficient is zero. For \(t_4\), \(\lambda_U=2t_5(-\sqrt{5/3})\approx0.253\), so the Student copula has the larger asymptotic upper-tail dependence. Check. Both models remain dependent in their centers; the comparison concerns a limiting conditional exceedance probability.
    2. P1.2. On the upper corner, \(W_3(1,1,1)=1\); each of the three corners with one coordinate \(1/2\) has value \(1/2\); all corners with two or three coordinates \(1/2\) have value zero. The signed box volume is \(1-3(1/2)+0-0=-1/2\). A CDF cannot assign negative probability, so \(W_3\) is not a three-copula. Check. The bivariate lower Fréchet bound does not extend as a universal copula in higher dimensions.
    3. P1.3. \(\lambda_L=2^{-1/2}=1/\sqrt2\approx0.7071\) and \(\lambda_U=0\). The unrotated family emphasizes joint small uniforms, corresponding to the lower-left corner. Check. Rotations can move the emphasized corner but change the reported family orientation.
    4. P1.4. \(\tau=(2/\pi)(\pi/6)=1/3\). The value \(\rho=1/2\) is the latent Gaussian correlation parameter and the arcsine relation maps it to copula concordance within the Gaussian family. Many non-Gaussian copulas can share \(\tau=1/3\) while differing in tails and higher-order structure. Check. One scalar dependence summary cannot identify a whole CDF.
    5. P1.5. For independence, \(\Pi(0.5,0.9)^2=(0.45)^2=0.2025=0.25\cdot0.81\). For comonotonicity, \(M(0.5,0.9)^2=0.5^2=0.25=M(0.25,0.81)\). Both reference copulas are max-stable. Check. An arbitrary candidate must satisfy the identity for every point and positive integer or equivalent functional conditions; one numerical point is only a test case.
    6. P1.6. A defensible start is a nonexchangeable vine whose upper-tail-dependent pair families and rotations can differ by edge, or an asymmetric multivariate extreme-value construction if componentwise maxima are the scientific object. A one-parameter exchangeable Archimedean family is too restrictive, and a Gaussian copula has zero asymptotic tail dependence. A symmetric \(t\) copula may miss corner and pair asymmetry. Compare finite-threshold exceedances, conditional tails, held-out scores, structure sensitivity, and uncertainty. Rubric. Full credit rejects families for stated structural reasons and limits extrapolation because rare joint floods provide few effective tail observations.

    Chapter 2 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • A correlation matrix specifies a Gaussian copula, not an arbitrary copula with those pairwise correlations.
    • Zero asymptotic tail dependence does not imply negligible risk at a finite threshold.
    • The lower Fréchet envelope is not a copula in dimensions three and above.
    • Pairwise tail coefficients do not identify the full multivariate tail law.
    • A flexible family fitted by likelihood can still extrapolate tail behavior from very little information.
    Retrieval questions
    1. What property distinguishes an extreme-value copula from a generic tail-dependent copula?
    2. Why can Gaussian and \(t\) copulas with the same \(\rho\) imply different joint risk?
    3. What generator condition is needed for a \(d\)-dimensional Archimedean copula?
    4. Why is the bivariate countermonotone construction not a universal \(d\)-dimensional lower copula?

    Solutions. (1) Max-stability, equivalently the homogeneous stable tail-dependence representation. (2) The \(t\) copula has a shared random radial scale and nonzero symmetric tail dependence for finite \(\nu\), whereas the nondegenerate Gaussian copula has zero asymptotic tail dependence. (3) \(d\)-monotonicity under the standard generator convention; complete monotonicity suffices for every dimension. (4) Three or more uniforms cannot in general make every coordinate pair perfectly countermonotone, and the lower envelope fails the required multivariate volume condition.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Copula families encode structural decisions about exchangeability, symmetry, tail association, and dimensional scaling. Elliptical, Archimedean, and extreme-value constructions answer different questions. Rank summaries are useful projections, not identifiers of the whole copula.

    Why Chapter 3 follows. Family behavior matters only after the marginal transform, estimation objective, conditional query, and uncertainty calculation have been made explicit.
