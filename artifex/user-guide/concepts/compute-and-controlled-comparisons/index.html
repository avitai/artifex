    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Reproducibility and Generative-Model Research Methods

    A quality result without a resource boundary is incomplete. Parameters, active parameters, training tokens/examples, optimizer steps, FLOPs, NFEs, candidate samples, verifier calls, precision, memory, communication, hardware, and wall-clock describe different costs. A controlled comparison must also align data, resolution, conditioning, guidance, post-training, tuning budget, and evaluation.

    This volume builds a full accounting model for training and inference across generative families, then turns it into fair experimental design, Pareto analysis, and evidence-aware comparison.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1 and 3 for training/inference cost vocabulary.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–5 and the reporting templates.
    Research: all chapters, including profiler reconciliation, lifecycle amortization, factorial ablations, and 2026 adaptive-compute evidence.

    1. Parameters, Tokens, FLOPs, and Time to Quality¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes basic training-loop terminology. By the end, you should be able to distinguish parameter count, active parameters, tokens, examples, training FLOPs, and time to quality; reproduce the chapter's accounting identity; explain why compute-optimality is conditional on a budget and scaling regime; and audit a mixture-of-experts report without equating total and active capacity.

    Parameter counts¤

    Report at least:

    • total parameters stored;
    • trainable parameters for the run;
    • active parameters per token/example for sparse/MoE models;
    • non-model parameters such as optimizer states;
    • auxiliary encoders, codecs, discriminators, reward/verifier models, and EMA copies.

    For an MoE with \(E\) experts and top-\(k\) routing, total expert parameters grow with \(E\) while active expert parameters per token grow roughly with \(k\). Routing, shared layers, load imbalance, communication, and capacity padding mean active parameter count is not compute by itself.

    Data exposure¤

    Record unique examples/tokens, total examples/tokens processed, epochs or repetition distribution, batch size, sequence/resolution/duration, augmentation, curriculum, rejected data, and tokenizer/codec. Two models trained on the same named dataset may see different effective distributions and numbers of tokens.

    FLOP conventions¤

    A floating-point operation count depends on convention. A multiply–accumulate may be counted as one MAC or two FLOPs. State the convention and whether embeddings, attention, normalization, activation, optimizer, recomputation, sparse zero work, and communication are included.

    For a dense decoder-only Transformer, the heuristic

    \[ C_{train}\approx 6ND \]

    with \(N\) parameters and \(D\) training tokens approximates forward plus backward matrix-multiply work under regimes where parameter multiplications dominate. It is not exact for long-context attention, MoE, convolutions, diffusion U-Nets/DiTs at varying resolution, multimodal codecs, or optimizer overhead. Use operator-level profiling/model FLOP analysis for the final report.

    The accounting identity¤

    Let \(F_{step}\) be counted FLOPs per optimizer step and \(S\) completed steps:

    \[ C_{train}=\sum_{s=1}^{S}F_{step,s}. \]

    Wall time is not FLOPs:

    \[ T_{wall}=T_{compute}+T_{communication}+T_{input}+T_{compile} +T_{checkpoint}+T_{evaluation}+T_{idle/recovery}. \]

    Report both. FLOPs aid algorithmic comparison; time captures systems effects. Development sweeps and failed runs are separate research cost from final-run compute and should be disclosed when the claim concerns total project efficiency.

    Time to quality¤

    Comparing fixed steps can reward a slower-converging method; comparing best final quality can ignore cost. Plot quality against cumulative tokens, FLOPs, energy, and wall-clock. Predefine a target-quality threshold and report time/compute to reach it, plus failure rate across seeds.

    Compute-optimality is conditional¤

    Chinchilla scaling empirically studied dense autoregressive language models under its data/model/compute regime. Its fitted allocation is influential but not a universal law for diffusion, MoE, multimodal, repeated-data, post-training, or inference-dominated deployments.

    Worked MoE report¤

    A model stores 100B parameters, of which 20B are shared and 80B across 16 experts. Top-2 routing activates about 10B expert parameters plus 20B shared: roughly 30B active per token, not 100B and not simply \(2/16\times100B\). Report the actual layer structure, router, expert capacity, padding, and achieved load balance.

    Retrieval question: why are parameter counts insufficient?

    Reuse, sparsity, sequence/resolution, activations, optimizer, routing, communication, and number of evaluations determine compute and memory. Equal parameters can have very different costs.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Training cost has algorithmic and systems layers. Chapter 2 develops memory, communication, precision, and utilization—the reasons equal FLOPs do not imply equal time.

    2. Memory, Parallelism, Precision, and Hardware¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and elementary accelerator terminology. By the end, you should be able to construct a model/optimizer/activation memory ledger, separate data, tensor, pipeline, and expert parallelism, explain how precision changes numerical behavior as well as storage, compute utilization from declared work, and design a reproducible hardware-timing protocol.

    Memory ledger¤

    Peak training memory includes:

    \[ M=M_{weights}+M_{grads}+M_{optimizer}+M_{activations} +M_{temporary}+M_{communication}+M_{runtime}. \]

    If weights use \(b_w\) bytes, \(M_{weights}=N b_w\), but mixed precision may retain master weights. Adam-like optimizers commonly store multiple moments. Activation memory depends on batch, sequence/spatial tokens, width, layers, checkpointing, and attention implementation. Measure peak allocated and reserved memory; do not estimate only weights.

    Inference adds KV cache for AR attention, persistent codec/condition encoders, sampler states, and batched conditional/unconditional activations. Quantized weights do not imply quantized KV cache or activations.

    Parallelism changes the computation system¤

    • data parallelism replicates parameters and synchronizes gradients;
    • tensor parallelism shards operators and adds collectives;
    • pipeline parallelism introduces bubbles/microbatches;
    • expert parallelism routes tokens across devices;
    • sequence/context parallelism shards long activations;
    • optimizer/parameter sharding reduces memory with communication.

    Report device topology, interconnect, nodes/devices, parallel strategy, global/microbatch, accumulation, and fault/restart policy. A speedup from more devices is not algorithmic efficiency; give strong/weak scaling efficiency.

    Precision and numerical behavior¤

    State weight, activation, gradient, optimizer, accumulation, and communication dtypes. BF16, FP16, FP8, INT8, INT4, and mixed formats differ by hardware and operator. Quantization changes memory/bandwidth and potentially quality; include calibration/fine-tuning data and the exact quality delta.

    Utilization¤

    Model FLOPs utilization is commonly

    \[ MFU=\frac{\text{estimated model FLOPs per second}} {\text{advertised peak FLOPs per second across devices}}. \]

    It depends on both the model-FLOP convention and applicable peak for the chosen dtype/sparsity. Hardware FLOPs utilization may count additional kernels. Report throughput directly alongside utilization.

    Low utilization can result from memory bandwidth, small matrices/batches, communication, input stalls, kernel launch, dynamic routing, compilation, or imbalance. High utilization does not mean low energy or good sample quality.

    Reproducible hardware timing¤

    Warm up compilation/caches; synchronize asynchronous devices; separate cold and steady state; report software/compiler/kernel versions; fix or describe batching/concurrency; include percentile latency; and repeat. Cloud instance name alone does not determine clocks, topology, contention, or power limits.

    MLPerf illustrates rule-defined time-to-quality system benchmarking. The June 2026 Training v6.0 release is an Official benchmark release, not evidence that one submitted system dominates every custom workload.

    Worked memory trap¤

    A 7B model at 4-bit weights appears to need 3.5 GB. Serving may still require higher-precision scales/metadata, activations, runtime workspace, and a KV cache growing with batch and context. “Fits in 4 GB” from weight arithmetic is not a deployment measurement.

    Retrieval question: what makes a latency number portable?

    Model/checkpoint, input/output sizes, batch/concurrency, precision, sampler, hardware/topology, software versions, warmup, synchronization, and percentile protocol.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Memory and wall-clock emerge from the whole hardware/software execution. Chapter 3 gives family-specific inference ledgers so “one sample” and “one step” become comparable.

    3. Inference Accounting Across Generative Families¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the sampling mechanisms of the major model families. By the end, you should be able to define the unit of work for autoregressive, diffusion/flow, direct latent, MCMC, and search-based generation; distinguish NFE, token steps, proposals, and visible requests; include codecs, guidance, verifiers, and retrieval in the system boundary; and compare latency and throughput under matched quality constraints.

    Autoregressive generation¤

    Separate:

    • prefill input tokens, time to first token, and prefill throughput;
    • decode output tokens, inter-token latency, and decode throughput;
    • batch/concurrency and request-length distribution;
    • KV-cache dtype/memory and cache hits;
    • speculative draft/target calls, acceptance rate, and verified tokens;
    • best-of-\(N\), beam width, verifier/reward calls, and postprocessing.

    With KV caching, decode avoids recomputing all prior hidden states but attention reads a growing cache. Throughput and latency trade off with batching. Efficient Transformer inference demonstrates that partitioning, memory, quantization, and batch regime jointly set performance.

    Diffusion and flow sampling¤

    NFE counts calls to the learned score/noise/vector-field network. A solver step may use multiple stages/calls; adaptive solvers can reject steps. Classifier-free guidance often evaluates conditional and unconditional branches: one batched invocation can still perform roughly two sample-equivalents of model FLOPs and memory.

    Report:

    \[ C_{sample}=\sum_{j=1}^{NFE}F_{model}(shape_j) +F_{codec}+F_{condition}+F_{guidance}+F_{post}. \]

    Different timesteps may use different resolutions/experts/caches, so NFE alone can mislead. Give total FLOPs and wall time. For stochastic samplers specify random draws and churn/corrector calls; for ODE likelihood include divergence evaluations.

    Direct and latent generators¤

    GAN/VAE decoders can generate in one forward pass, but report latent sampling, mapping networks, cascades, super-resolution, rejection/truncation, and codec. Latent diffusion adds encoder/decoder cost; amortized codec cost can be cached during training but not always at user-facing inference.

    MCMC, EBMs, and GFlowNets¤

    Count burn-in, retained samples, thinning, chains, gradient evaluations, accept/reject, autocorrelation/effective sample size, replay initialization, and reward/oracle calls. A thousand correlated steps are not a thousand independent samples.

    For GFlowNets count policy steps per object, forward/backward evaluations, reward evaluations, and invalid/rejected trajectories. Variable construction length requires a distribution, not one step number.

    Search, verifiers, and test-time scaling¤

    Total inference compute includes every candidate, branch, critic, judge, simulator, tool, and retry. If \(N\) samples each use \(F\) generator FLOPs and a verifier costs \(V\),

    \[ C_{total}=N(F+V)+C_{aggregation}, \]

    not the cost of the selected sample. Report quality–compute curves and whether failed/filtered candidates are charged.

    End-to-end latency¤

    Include tokenization, upload/download, retrieval, queueing, safety filters, codec, serialization, and tool execution for deployed-system claims. Kernel-only time answers a different question and should be labeled.

    Retrieval question: when is NFE comparable?

    When each evaluation uses comparable model shape/cost and all conditional branches, solver stages, corrections, codecs, and search candidates are accounted. Otherwise total FLOPs/time are needed.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Inference cost is a tree of model and auxiliary evaluations. Chapter 4 uses that ledger to define controlled model, method, and system comparisons.

    4. Controlled Comparisons and Ablations¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes basic experimental design. By the end, you should be able to name a comparison estimand, construct a matched-budget table, distinguish an ablation from an uncontrolled model replacement, use paired randomness when appropriate, and diagnose guidance, tuning, data, or evaluation asymmetries that invalidate a claimed compute advantage.

    Name the estimand¤

    Decide whether the experiment estimates the effect of:

    • a training objective;
    • architecture/backbone;
    • representation/tokenizer/codec;
    • data/curation;
    • sampler/solver/decoding;
    • guidance/conditioning;
    • post-training/distillation;
    • systems implementation;
    • complete product pipeline.

    Hold other causes fixed or explicitly treat them as factors. Comparing two published checkpoints with different data, scale, codec, post-training, and sampler estimates a bundle—not one method.

    Matching table¤

    Table COMP.1. Matching table
    Axis Control/report
    data source snapshot, filters, tokens/examples, epochs
    resolution/duration exact shapes, frame/sample rate, crops
    conditions encoders, prompt set, dropout, guidance
    model total/active/trainable params, codec, auxiliaries
    training FLOPs, steps, batch, optimizer, tuning budget
    inference output length, NFE/candidates, latency budget
    evaluation sample count, metric code/features, human protocol
    post-training SFT/preference/RL/distillation data and compute

    Matched-budget designs¤

    Useful designs include equal training FLOPs, equal wall-clock on one system, equal data exposure, equal parameter/memory budget, equal inference FLOPs/latency, or equal total lifecycle cost. No single match is universally fair; report several Pareto points when conclusions change.

    Ablations¤

    An ablation should remove or vary one component within the same pipeline, retrain when the component affects training, use multiple seeds, and report interactions. If components \(A,B\) interact, the effect

    \[ \Delta_A=L(A=1,B=b)-L(A=0,B=b) \]

    depends on \(b\). A \(2\times2\) factorial reveals interaction; one-at-a-time ablations can miss it.

    Negative ablations matter: compare added complexity with parameter/compute-matched simple baselines, random/frozen versions, stronger tuning, and increased guidance/steps/data.

    Hyperparameter fairness¤

    Equal tuning trials can be unfair when search spaces differ; unlimited tuning for the proposed method and copied defaults for baselines is worse. Define search spaces, selection metric/split, number/adaptivity of trials, total tuning compute, and baseline expertise. Retune baselines under changed data/resolution.

    Paired randomness¤

    Share data order, prompts, initial noise, evaluation examples, or seeds where scientifically valid to reduce variance, while retaining independent training replicates. Do not share randomness if it advantages one sampler or violates its intended distribution.

    Guidance confounding¤

    Guidance Matters, ICLR 2026, supplies a concrete Peer-reviewed research case: comparisons among text-to-image guidance methods can be confounded by insufficient CFG-scale matching. Report guidance curves, not one hand-selected point.

    Retrieval question: what does a published-checkpoint comparison establish?

    It compares complete released systems under your inference/evaluation protocol. It generally cannot isolate architecture or objective because training data, scale, post-training, and implementation differ.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Controlled comparisons begin with a causal estimand and matched resource/data axes. Chapter 5 turns multiple budgets into Pareto frontiers and lifecycle decisions.

    5. Pareto Frontiers, Scaling, Energy, and Lifecycle Cost¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–4 and elementary cost analysis. By the end, you should be able to identify dominated systems on a multi-objective frontier, compute a training–inference break-even point, fit and qualify a scaling curve, separate device energy from lifecycle impact, and explain how utilization, retraining, reliability, and rebound effects alter deployment conclusions.

    Pareto analysis¤

    A configuration is dominated if another is at least as good on every reported axis and better on one. Plot quality versus latency, throughput, memory, training compute, inference compute, and safety/coverage rather than collapsing all into an arbitrary scalar.

    Use iso-quality cost and iso-cost quality comparisons. Show curves across batch, precision, guidance, NFE, candidate count, and output length. A “fastest” claim needs the operating region.

    Training–inference amortization¤

    For \(Q\) deployed generations,

    \[ C_{lifecycle}=C_{research}+C_{train}+C_{post} +Q\,C_{inference}+C_{maintenance}. \]

    Distillation may add training but reduce per-query inference. Break-even query count between systems \(A,B\) follows by solving their lifecycle costs. Include retraining frequency, verifier/retrieval services, and energy/operations when material.

    Scaling curves¤

    Fit empirical scaling laws only over the measured regime, give uncertainty/residuals, and test held-out scales. Parameter, data, and compute effects confound if co-scaled. Emergent-looking threshold behavior can result from metric granularity or prompting. Extrapolation is a model, not an observed result.

    Energy and carbon¤

    Measure energy by integrating system power over time or using validated telemetry:

    \[ E_{kWh}=\frac1{3.6\times10^6}\int P(t)dt. \]

    Report scope: accelerator only or whole node/cluster, networking/storage, PUE, location/time-dependent carbon intensity, embodied hardware if claimed, and uncertainty. FLOPs do not uniquely determine energy. Green AI argues for efficiency reporting; carbon comparisons require transparent boundaries.

    Rebound and utilization¤

    Lower per-sample cost can increase total usage. Report aggregate expected volume for deployment claims. Higher batch utilization reduces energy/sample but can worsen latency; choose service-level objectives and show their trade-off.

    Reliability cost¤

    Include failed jobs, checkpoint/restart overhead, corrupted samples, retries, fallback models, and tail latency. A method that succeeds 80% of runs has a higher expected cost to a usable result than its best run suggests.

    Worked distillation break-even¤

    Teacher A requires 100 units to train and 10 per sample. Distilled B requires the same teacher plus 50 distillation units and 2 per sample. B breaks even when \(150+2Q<100+10Q\), or \(Q>6.25\). Quality and maintenance must also match; seven samples is only the compute break-even in this toy ledger.

    Retrieval question: why is one efficiency number inadequate?

    Training and inference trade off; latency and throughput depend on load; memory and hardware constrain deployment; quality/safety vary with settings; and lifecycle query volume changes the optimum.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Efficiency is a multi-objective lifecycle property. Chapter 6 audits 2026 adaptive-compute results and supplies complete reporting exercises.

    6. Research and Reporting Practice¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the complete accounting framework. By the end, you should be able to produce a minimum compute card, reconstruct a result's system boundary from accepted or official evidence, design a controlled reproduction with an uncertainty budget, and refuse comparisons whose quality target, workload, hardware, precision, or amortization horizon is not commensurable.

    Accepted and official evidence¤

    • Test-Time Iterative Error Correction, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a quality/extra-compute trade-off across reported efficient diffusion settings; correction iterations must be charged to inference.
    • Guidance Matters, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for treating CFG scale as a controlled comparison axis.
    • Compute Where it Counts, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for learned per-token compression/budget actions in reported autoregressive regimes; controller overhead and hardware realization remain part of end-to-end cost.
    • Diffusion Probe, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for early prediction/pruning of generation quality in reported T2I workflows; seed selection changes total candidates and must count discarded paths.
    • MLPerf Training v6.0, June 2026 — Official benchmark release with rule-defined full-system time-to-quality results, including new sparse-computation benchmarks; conclusions stay within its workloads/rules.

    Minimum compute card¤

    Report:

    1. total/active/trainable parameters and auxiliaries;
    2. unique and processed data units;
    3. training/post-training/tuning FLOPs and wall-clock;
    4. devices, topology, precision, software, parallelism;
    5. peak memory and throughput/utilization;
    6. inference workload distribution, NFE/tokens/candidates/verifiers;
    7. latency percentiles, throughput, concurrency, batch;
    8. quality/safety metrics and uncertainty at every cost point;
    9. energy/carbon boundary if claimed;
    10. failed runs and lifecycle assumptions.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Reconcile a dense Transformer's \(6ND\) estimate with an operator profiler and explain residuals.
    2. Build a byte-level training memory ledger and verify peak measurement under checkpointing/sharding.
    3. Compare two diffusion solvers by step, true NFE, FLOPs, and wall-clock including CFG/codec.
    4. Benchmark AR prefill/decode separately across batch, context, output length, and KV dtype.
    5. Design a \(2\times2\) objective/architecture factorial under matched training FLOPs.
    6. Plot a quality–latency–memory Pareto surface and identify dominated configurations.
    7. Compute lifecycle break-even for distillation under uncertain query volume.
    8. Reproduce one 2026 efficiency claim and charge every discarded candidate/controller/probe call.
    Exercise 3 solution criterion

    Instrument every model invocation and its input shape, count conditional/unconditional branches and multi-stage solver calls, include text/codec overhead, synchronize timing, and compare quality at matched total FLOPs and latency. If conclusions differ from equal-step or equal-NFE rankings, report all three rather than choosing the favorable one.

    Cumulative retrieval: compute and controlled comparison
    1. Why can equal parameter count, equal FLOPs, and equal wall time each define a different comparison?
    2. What belongs in an end-to-end inference boundary for a latent diffusion system with guidance and reranking?
    3. How do you compute the query count at which a more expensive training run becomes cheaper overall?
    4. Which fields are indispensable in a minimum compute card?

    Solutions. (1) They constrain capacity, arithmetic work, and hardware/system realization respectively, and none fixes the others or equalizes quality. (2) Text/condition encoding, guided model evaluations, solver, latent codec, candidate count, reranker, transfers, and filtering. (3) Equate total lifecycle costs and solve training-cost difference divided by per-query cost savings, including recurring costs where material. (4) Model and active parameters, data exposure, training/inference work definition, hardware, precision, software, parallelism, utilization or timing method, tuning budget, quality target, uncertainty, and system boundary.

    Final synthesis¤

    Compute claims are measurement claims. Parameters, FLOPs, memory, NFE, latency, throughput, energy, and lifecycle volume answer different questions. Controlled comparisons isolate a declared causal factor under matched data and resource axes; Pareto curves reveal where no universal winner exists.

    Continue to Reproducibility and Generative-Model Research Methods, where uncertainty, independent reproduction, ablations, negative results, evidence maturity, and maintenance turn experiments into durable scientific knowledge.
