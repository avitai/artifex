    3. Shortcut Models, Average Velocity, and MeanFlow¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes velocity-based transport and Chapter 2's map composition view. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate velocity objects: distinguish instantaneous velocity, endpoint displacement, interval-average velocity, and a learned shortcut.
    2. O2 — Derive the MeanFlow identity: differentiate a finite displacement along a trajectory with correct total derivatives and time orientation.
    3. O3 — Verify shortcut recursion: derive the relation between one long update and two short updates for a step-conditioned predictor.
    4. O4 — Audit training compute: identify Jacobian--vector products, stop-gradient targets, bootstrap networks, and hidden model evaluations.
    5. O5 — Diagnose guidance and interval shift: test whether deployed guidance scales, path curvature, and time intervals match training.
    6. O6 — Transfer average motion: construct a finite average-velocity oracle for a nonlinear, conditional, or constrained system.

    For an exact ODE trajectory and \(r<t\), define average velocity

    \[ u(x_t,r,t)=\frac{x_t-x_r}{t-r} =\frac{1}{t-r}\int_r^t v(x_\tau,\tau)d\tau. \]

    Then

    \[ x_r=x_t-(t-r)u(x_t,r,t). \]

    Differentiating along the trajectory gives

    \[ u=v(x_t,t)-(t-r)D_tu, \]

    where \(D_tu\) is the total derivative, including state motion. This identity is the basis of MeanFlow, a NeurIPS 2025 oral, which trains average rather than instantaneous velocity and can learn one-step generation from scratch.

    The total derivative is not merely \(\partial_tu\):

    \[ D_tu(x_t,r,t) =\partial_tu(x_t,r,t) +v(x_t,t)^\top\nabla_xu(x_t,r,t). \]

    For vector-valued \(u\), the second term is a Jacobian--vector product. Dropping it changes the identity whenever the predicted average varies with state. Likewise, differentiating while holding the observed tensor \(x_t\) fixed answers a different question from differentiating along the trajectory. Autodiff code must make that distinction visible.

    The identity can be read in two directions. Given exact \(v\), it constrains \(u\). Given a parameterized \(u_\theta\), it yields an induced instantaneous target involving \(u_\theta\) and its derivative. The original MeanFlow objective and later reformulations make different optimization choices around this self-dependence. The CVPR 2026 paper Improved Mean Flows explicitly reformulates the objective and treats guidance as conditioning. That is evidence that training-objective and guidance design matter; it is not a retroactive guarantee for every average-velocity implementation.

    The derivative target typically uses automatic differentiation/Jacobian-vector products and stop-gradient choices. “One NFE at inference” does not mean the training objective costs one ordinary forward pass.

    Worked nonlinear average velocity¤

    For \(\dot x=x^2\) with \(x(0)=x_0\) and \(t<1/x_0\),

    \[ x_t=\frac{x_0}{1-tx_0}. \]

    The average velocity over \([0,t]\) is

    \[ u(x_t,0,t)=\frac{x_t-x_0}{t}=x_0x_t. \]

    It differs from both endpoint velocities: \(v(x_0)=x_0^2\) and \(v(x_t)=x_t^2\). For \(x_0=1\) and \(t=1/2\), the start, average, and end velocities are \(1\), \(2\), and \(4\). Yet the finite update \(x_t-tu=2-(1/2)2=1\) returns the exact start. The example also exposes an interval boundary: the flow blows up at \(t=1/x_0\), so no globally valid long shortcut exists across that singularity.

    Shortcut models¤

    Shortcut Models, ICLR 2025, condition a flow model on a desired step size and enforce recursive self-consistency: a long shortcut should agree with two shorter shortcuts. This supports variable NFE in one model.

    For a forward update written

    \[ S_d(x,t)=x+d\,s_\theta(x,t,d), \]

    agreement between one \(2d\) shortcut and two \(d\) shortcuts requires

    \[ s_\theta(x,t,2d) =\frac12\left[ s_\theta(x,t,d) +s_\theta(S_d(x,t),t+d,d) \right]. \]

    This is a finite recursion. A stop-gradient or EMA copy is commonly used in the bootstrapped target, so specify which side receives gradients. Grounding at small \(d\) links the recursion to the data/path objective; recursion alone again admits coherent but wrong families.

    MeanFlow uses a differential average-velocity identity; shortcut training uses a finite interval-doubling relation. They share a flow-map goal but are not the same loss.

    For a constant field \(v(x,t)=c\), both targets reduce to \(c\) at every interval. For curved dynamics, they constrain different quantities: MeanFlow relates an average to a local total derivative, while shortcut recursion compares nested finite predictions. Agreement on the constant-field oracle is therefore a minimum implementation check, not evidence of general equivalence.

    Directness versus distillation¤

    MeanFlow is self-contained: it does not require a pretrained teacher by definition. A MeanFlow-based student can still be used for distillation, but that is a hybrid application. Direct training can avoid teacher inference cost while taking on derivative estimation and optimization sensitivity.

    Guidance¤

    Classifier-free guidance is nonlinear at the level of finite maps. Combining conditional and unconditional instantaneous velocities and then integrating need not equal combining separately learned average velocities. Guidance formulation, scale, training dropout, and NFE must be reported.

    If the deployed instantaneous field is

    \[ v_w=v_{\mathrm{uncond}} +w(v_{\mathrm{cond}}-v_{\mathrm{uncond}}), \]

    then its trajectory \(x_t^{(w)}\) depends on \(w\). The average velocity integrates \(v_w\) along that guided trajectory. In general it is not \(u_{\mathrm{uncond}}+w(u_{\mathrm{cond}}-u_{\mathrm{uncond}})\) because the three averages are evaluated on different state paths. Equality holds for special shared or affine trajectories, not by linearity of the displayed field alone. Test unseen \(w\) values and count whether conditional/unconditional branches are separate or fused.

    Failure cases¤

    • Large intervals combine heterogeneous path segments and increase regression difficulty.
    • One-step maps can lose fine modes even when FID is good.
    • Derivative targets can be noisy or memory intensive.
    • Training on a narrow interval distribution can make arbitrary-NFE sampling unreliable.
    • A direct map may produce good endpoints while failing interpolation, inversion, or editing.

    Interval-generalization grid¤

    Do not collapse interval testing into one average metric. Cross at least:

    Table FEW.11. Interval-generalization grid
    Axis In-distribution Stress test
    start time sampled training grid held-out boundary/interior times
    interval length trained lengths longer, shorter, and non-dyadic lengths
    state source teacher/data path model-generated off-trajectory state
    guidance trained scale/dropout unseen scale and negative/strong guidance
    composition direct trained jump repeated and mixed-length jumps
    precision training precision deployment precision and compiler

    Report endpoint quality and direct-versus-composed residual separately. A shortcut can be useful at one fixed budget without being reliable as an arbitrary-step neural operator.

    Retrieval practice: shortcut and average velocities
    1. Why is an interval-average velocity not generally equal to the instantaneous velocity at either endpoint?
    2. What evidence is required before calling a method teacher-free?
    3. How can classifier-free guidance invalidate an unguided one-step equivalence claim?
    4. Which test reveals whether a shortcut model composes across unseen interval lengths?

    Solutions. (1) It represents integrated displacement over a finite interval and depends on trajectory curvature. (2) Its targets and optimization must not require a pretrained teacher, teacher-generated trajectories, or cached teacher outputs. (3) Guidance changes the vector field or denoising target, so the learned unguided map may no longer integrate the deployed dynamics. (4) Evaluate held-out start/end times and compare direct long transitions with compositions of shorter transitions.

    Misconception checks: average velocities and shortcuts¤

    • “Average velocity is the velocity at the midpoint.” Only special fields or quadrature identities make that exact.
    • “Teacher-free means target-free.” Direct methods still use corrupted data, analytic fields, JVPs, EMA targets, or bootstrapping.
    • “Recursive agreement grounds the model.” A wrong family can compose perfectly; small-step/data grounding is separate.
    • “Linear classifier-free guidance commutes with finite maps.” The guided state trajectory changes with the scale.

    Independent practice and assessment map: average motion and shortcuts¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: nonlinear velocity order. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For \(\dot x=x^2\), \(x_0=1\), and \(t=1/2\), order the start, average, and endpoint velocities before computing them.
    2. P1.2 — Derive the MeanFlow identity. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Differentiate \(x_r=x_t-(t-r)u(x_t,r,t)\) along the trajectory with \(r\) fixed and recover the total-derivative identity.
    3. P1.3 — Verify shortcut recursion. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Compose two updates \(S_d\) and derive the target for \(s_\theta(x,t,2d)\).
    4. P1.4 — Count a JVP objective. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A training step evaluates \(u_\theta\) once, computes one JVP through it, evaluates an EMA target once, and uses no teacher rollout. State what “one-NFE inference” omits from this training-cost description and whether the method is teacher-distilled.
    5. P1.5 — Falsify guidance commutation. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 15 min. Give a condition under which guided average velocities do commute with linear combination and a nonlinear condition under which they need not.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to constrained motion. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. For motion on a circle, define an intrinsic interval-average displacement, its branch-cut limitation, and a direct-versus-composed test.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. \(x_{1/2}=2\). The start velocity is \(1^2=1\), the average is \((2-1)/(1/2)=2\), and the endpoint velocity is \(2^2=4\). Thus \(1<2<4\). Check. Subtracting half the average from the endpoint returns the start: \(2-(1/2)2=1\).
    2. P1.2. Differentiate with \(x_r\) and \(r\) fixed: \(0=v(x_t,t)-u-(t-r)D_tu\). Rearranging gives \(u=v-(t-r)D_tu\), with \(D_tu=\partial_tu+v^\top\nabla_xu\). Check. Holding \(x_t\) fixed would omit the JVP and would not differentiate along a solution.
    3. P1.3. Two short updates give \(x+d s(x,t,d)+d s(S_d(x,t),t+d,d)\). A long update gives \(x+2d s(x,t,2d)\). Equating and dividing by \(2d\) yields the displayed half-sum. Check. Evaluating both short predictions at the original state would not compose the map.
    4. P1.4. “One NFE” describes one principal-network call in the deployed endpoint update. It omits a training forward, JVP/backward graph, EMA-target forward, activation memory, and optimizer work. With no pretrained generative teacher or teacher rollout, the described objective is bootstrapped/direct rather than teacher-distilled. Rubric. The EMA is still a target-producing network and must be logged.
    5. P1.5. If conditional and unconditional velocities are constant, or otherwise share a path on which linear combination commutes with integration, their interval averages combine linearly. With state-dependent fields, changing \(w\) changes the trajectory on which each field is evaluated, so the average of the combined guided field need not equal a combination of separately learned averages. Check. Linearity in field values is not linearity in flow maps.
    6. P1.6. For points within the circle's injectivity radius, use \(u=\operatorname{Log}_{x_t}(x_r)/(r-t)\) in the tangent space and recover with the exponential map. Near antipodes, the logarithm has multiple shortest branches, so the target must declare a branch, retain stochasticity, or restrict intervals. Compare one long exponential-map update with two shorter intrinsic updates using geodesic distance. Check. Renormalizing Euclidean chords defines a different finite algorithm.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Average velocities and shortcut identities learn integrated motion rather than hoping a local field extrapolates over a long interval. Their guarantees attach to exact identities and population objectives; practical derivative targets and map consistency remain approximate. Chapter 4 turns from deterministic maps to expressive stochastic transition kernels and causal generation orders.
