    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Conditioning, Steering, Constraints, and Selection

    Autoregressive models generate discrete objects one position at a time. Discrete iterative models instead begin from a masked, corrupted, or simple reference state and update many positions across several rounds. This creates a continuum between one-shot parallel prediction and fully sequential generation.

    The family includes masked iterative refinement, discrete denoising diffusion, continuous-time Markov chains (CTMCs), ratio/score-based discrete models, and discrete flow matching. These methods share code patterns but do not automatically define the same joint distribution or likelihood.

    How to read this book

    Read continuously for the full distinction between objective, transition model, and sampler. Numbered pages are exact projections of this source. Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–3. Practitioner: Chapters 1–5 plus the sampler audit. Research: all chapters, rederiving the reverse transition for a two-state chain.

    Keep four objects separate throughout:

    1. the data law \(p_{\mathrm{data}}\) that the learner is trying to approximate;
    2. the training query—a mask, noisy state, time, or source–target pair;
    3. the learned estimator—clean-token probabilities, reverse probabilities, ratios, rates, or posteriors;
    4. the sampler that turns repeated model calls into an output distribution.

    Two systems may share an estimator while using different samplers, or share a sampler skeleton while estimating different mathematical objects. Neither fact establishes equality of their output laws.

    The vertical route is deliberate: representation and data law determine the legal states; the training query determines the estimand; the learned network is only one input to a transition kernel; and the repeated kernel determines the deployed output law. Skipping a row is the source of many false equivalences in this family.

    1. Masked Modeling and Iterative Refinement¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional probability, cross-entropy, and the autoregressive chain rule. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. distinguish a masked conditional objective from a normalized sequence likelihood;
    2. test compatibility of full conditionals in a small finite example;
    3. separate a compatible Gibbs sampler from an order-dependent pseudo-Gibbs chain;
    4. write the trajectory law induced by a confidence/remasking policy;
    5. explain how the mask law and loss normalization change the population objective; and
    6. audit confidence, commitment, revision, and stopping as parts of the sampler.

    The simplest intuition is “fill the blanks, inspect the result, and revise.” The formal object is more specific: a conditional predictor is queried along a stochastic sequence of partially observed states, and the query policy helps determine the generated law.

    Let \(x=(x_1,\ldots,x_D)\) with tokens in vocabulary \(\mathcal V\). A mask \(m\subseteq\{1,\ldots,D\}\) replaces selected positions with a special token. A bidirectional model predicts

    \[ p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{\bar m},m),\qquad i\in m. \]

    A typical training loss samples a mask and averages cross-entropy over masked positions:

    \[ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{mask}} =\mathbb E_{x,m} \left[-\frac1{|m|}\sum_{i\in m} \log p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{\bar m},m)\right]. \]

    The mask distribution is part of the objective. Uniform mask ratios, independent masking, contiguous spans, and task-shaped masks train different conditional queries.

    Suppose first that a mask size \(K\) is drawn and then a subset is drawn uniformly among the \(K\)-subsets. Even if every coordinate appears equally often marginally, the learner sees different jointly visible context at different \(K\). A model trained almost entirely with one missing token is not thereby trained to denoise a fully masked sequence. Conversely, a model trained mostly at high mask ratios may receive too few nearly complete contexts to make precise final corrections.

    The normalization by \(|m|\) also matters. It gives each sampled example roughly equal total weight. Omitting it gives high-mask examples more token-loss weight. Neither convention is universally correct; the intended diffusion integral, query distribution, and evaluation protocol decide which estimator is being approximated.

    Masked Conditionals Do Not Automatically Define One Joint¤

    An arbitrary collection of conditionals can be mutually incompatible: there may be no normalized \(p(x)\) whose conditionals equal all model outputs. Pseudolikelihood is statistically principled under assumptions when it trains compatible local conditionals from a parameterized joint; a generic masked Transformer need not have that construction.

    Therefore “masked-language-model likelihood” must name the quantity actually evaluated. Summed masked cross-entropy is not generally exact sequence log likelihood.

    For two binary variables \(X,Y\), compatible positive full conditionals must satisfy an odds-ratio cycle:

    \[ \frac{\operatorname{odds}(X=1\mid Y=1)} {\operatorname{odds}(X=1\mid Y=0)} = \frac{\operatorname{odds}(Y=1\mid X=1)} {\operatorname{odds}(Y=1\mid X=0)}. \]

    To see why, both sides equal the joint cross-product ratio \(p_{11}p_{00}/(p_{10}p_{01})\). Now set \(P(X=1\mid Y=0)=0.2\) and \(P(X=1\mid Y=1)=0.8\); the left side is \((4)/(1/4)=16\). If instead \(P(Y=1\mid X=0)=0.2\) and \(P(Y=1\mid X=1)=0.5\), the right side is \(1/(1/4)=4\). No strictly positive joint distribution has all four conditionals. A neural masked model can approximate this incompatible table because nothing in ordinary masked cross-entropy enforces the cycle.

    This does not make masked training invalid. It identifies the semantics: the network is a collection of useful conditional estimators until an explicit diffusion, order-mixture, Gibbs, or other generative construction supplies a joint law.

    From conditionals to a chain is not the same as recovering their joint¤

    Three constructions are often described informally as “Gibbs-like,” but their probabilistic claims differ:

    Table DISC.8. From conditionals to a chain is not the same as recovering their joint
    Construction What is normalized What can be claimed
    compatible positive full conditionals with an ergodic Gibbs scan every coordinate update and one common joint the common joint is invariant; under ergodicity the chain converges to it
    incompatible positive conditionals with a fixed scan every coordinate update and the resulting Markov chain a stationary chain law may exist, but it can depend on scan order and need not have the supplied conditionals
    confidence-based parallel refinement proposal marginals plus an adaptive selection/remasking policy the complete trajectory defines an output law; it is not ordinary Gibbs unless its transition kernel is derived as such

    Dependency networks make the middle distinction explicit. On a finite state space, positive local conditionals used in an ordered pseudo-Gibbs scan define an irreducible, aperiodic Markov chain with a unique stationary law. Inconsistency has not disappeared: the stationary law can change when the scan order changes, and its conditionals can differ from the local predictors that generated it.

    This connection is useful precisely because it is limited. It explains how local predictors can be turned into a sampler, but it does not license calling masked cross-entropy a joint likelihood or treating confidence filling as a Gibbs transition.

    MaskGIT-Style Decoding¤

    MaskGIT begins with every image token masked. At each iteration:

    1. predict distributions for all currently masked positions in parallel;
    2. sample or select token proposals;
    3. compute a confidence score for each proposal;
    4. keep the most confident subset fixed;
    5. remask the rest according to a decreasing schedule.

    If \(M_t\) is the number of masked positions after iteration \(t\), a schedule \(M_t\approx D\gamma(t/T)\) controls the compute/quality trade-off. A cosine schedule is an empirical design choice, not a probabilistic necessity.

    If \(S_t\) denotes the partially masked state, \(A_t\) the selected update set, and \(X_{A_t}'\) the proposals, then a complete sampler has trajectory probability

    \[ P_\theta(\tau) =P(S_0)\prod_{t=0}^{T-1} \pi_\theta(A_t\mid S_t) P_\theta(X_{A_t}'\mid S_t,A_t) P(S_{t+1}\mid S_t,A_t,X_{A_t}'). \]

    The policy \(\pi_\theta\) may depend on confidence produced by the same network. Marginalizing this expression over all trajectories that end at \(x\) defines the sampler's output probability, but that sum is generally intractable. Multiplying only the accepted proposal probabilities silently omits selection, remasking, and alternative trajectories.

    Confidence Is Part of the Sampler¤

    Maximum probability, sampled-token probability, entropy, margin, and calibrated uncertainty give different commit orders. Once a token is fixed forever, an early error may be irreversible. Remasking previously committed positions increases correction ability but changes the transition kernel.

    Temperature and classifier-free guidance affect confidence as well as token values. If confidence is computed after guidance or temperature scaling, the sampler changes its order of commitment—not just its diversity.

    Confidence is not automatically calibrated probability. Maximum softmax probability tends to favor low-entropy positions, but a position can be confidently wrong under distribution shift. Entropy treats multimodal but well-specified positions as uncertain. Margin ignores the mass below the top two candidates. A principled comparison therefore freezes the model and varies only the policy while measuring both final quality and transition behavior.

    Worked Four-Token Refinement¤

    Suppose the initial state is [M, M, M, M]. The model proposes (A,B,C,D) with confidences \((0.95,0.55,0.80,0.40)\). If the schedule keeps two tokens, positions 1 and 3 become [A,M,C,M]. The next prediction conditions both remaining positions on \(A\) and \(C\).

    This gains bidirectional context relative to raster decoding. It does not guarantee that the final sample probability equals the product of proposal probabilities: later predictions, confidence selection, and remasking depend on the whole stochastic trajectory.

    Now suppose positions 2 and 4 are strongly coupled: the data contains only (B,D) and (E,F), each with probability \(1/2\). Marginal proposals sample them independently, producing a valid pair only half the time. A second refinement round can repair the mismatch if it sees and may revise both positions; irreversible parallel commitment cannot. This finite example isolates the source of the speed–faithfulness trade-off without blaming model capacity.

    Images, Audio, and Video Tokens¤

    Masked generation usually operates on learned discrete codes. The tokenizer determines sequence length, reconstruction ceiling, local semantics, and code usage. Image/video sample quality is therefore a property of the codec plus prior plus sampler. A fast token sampler cannot recover details discarded by the codec.

    Chapter 1 Synthesis¤

    Masked objectives teach flexible conditionals; iterative refinement turns them into a sampler. The sampler can be effective without possessing a tractable normalized joint. Chapter 2 introduces explicit discrete Markov corruption, where reverse transitions and likelihood bounds are derived from a generative process.

    Independent practice and assessment map: masked conditionals and policies¤

    Keep conditional compatibility, chain normalization, stationary law, trajectory probability, and deployed policy in separate rows. A normalized coordinate predictor does not make these rows interchangeable.

    P1 — Build and falsify a masked-refinement generator¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict which statements are necessarily true: equal marginal mask frequency gives the same objective; positive full conditionals define one compatible joint; an incompatible pseudo-Gibbs scan has no stationary law; changing scan order cannot matter; multiplying accepted-token probabilities gives the sample probability; and temperature changes only diversity. For every false statement, name the missing joint, weighting, policy, or trajectory factor.
    2. P1.2 — Compatibility calculation. Reproduce the binary odds-ratio contradiction in the chapter, then construct one compatible positive \(2\times2\) table and recover all four conditionals. Verify the common cross-product ratio and state which positivity assumption the finite argument uses.
    3. P1.3 — Stationary-law intervention. Form transition matrices for deterministic-order and random-scan pseudo-Gibbs updates using the incompatible conditional table. Numerically solve each stationary law, compare its induced conditionals with the supplied predictors, and test whether reversing the deterministic scan changes the result.
    4. P1.4 — Trajectory ledger. For the four-token worked example, enumerate update-set selection, proposal, remasking, tie-breaking, and stopping factors for two rounds. Contrast irreversible confidence, stochastic confidence perturbation, and revisable remasking while holding logits and random seeds fixed.
    5. P1.5 — Mask-law experiment. Compare fixed one-token masking, uniform mask size, and independent Bernoulli masking. Derive the induced weight of each visible-context size with and without division by \(|m|\), then design a calibration report by mask ratio and jointly missing pattern.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Specify a masked iterative generator for an image-code grid, audio-codebook stack, graph, or protein sequence. Declare the representation, legal masks/edits, conditional query, compatibility claim, update policy, revision rule, constraint handling, output-law semantics, and a finite oracle that can expose simultaneous-dependence error.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why does changing the mask-size distribution change the population objective even when every coordinate is masked equally often?
    2. What equality must compatible positive binary full conditionals satisfy?
    3. Which factors are missing if one multiplies only accepted token probabilities in confidence decoding?
    4. When can a second refinement round repair an error created by simultaneous independent proposals?
    Complete solutions
    1. The jointly visible context changes with mask size, so the conditional queries and their weighting change.
    2. The conditional odds-ratio change for \(X\) across \(Y\) must equal that for \(Y\) across \(X\); both equal the joint cross-product ratio.
    3. The update-set policy, remasking/transition rule, and probability of alternative trajectories are omitted.
    4. Only when the later state exposes the inconsistency and the policy permits the coupled positions to change. Irreversible commitment removes that correction path.

    The bridge to Chapter 2 is semantic: masked refinement supplies a generator only after a policy is specified, whereas discrete diffusion starts from a forward Markov model whose reverse likelihood terms can be derived.

    2. Discrete Denoising Diffusion¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter uses finite Markov chains, categorical distributions, KL divergence, and the ELBO. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. construct and compose row-stochastic corruption matrices;
    2. quantify terminal mixing and distinguish a terminal marginal from its chosen reference law;
    3. derive the exact one-step posterior from the Markov factorization;
    4. distinguish clean-token, previous-token, ratio, and direct reverse-kernel parameterizations;
    5. explain when a jointly learned, non-Markovian forward construction still supports a variational objective; and
    6. identify which reported quantity is an exact likelihood, a variational bound, an auxiliary denoising loss, or an external-model score.

    Unlike heuristic masked refinement, conventional discrete denoising diffusion first specifies a normalized Markov joint over an entire latent path:

    \[ q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)=\prod_{t=1}^Tq(x_t\mid x_{t-1}). \]

    The reverse model then defines \(p_\theta(x_{0:T})=p(x_T)\prod_{t=1}^Tp_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\). The model has joint semantics even when its marginal likelihood requires summing over an exponentially large set of paths.

    The solid path is the known training-side variational distribution. The dashed path is the learned generative model. They share a state space and time index, but their conditionals are different objects; replacing one with the other without a Bayes derivation is a semantic error.

    For \(K\) categories, represent a token as a one-hot row vector. A forward transition matrix \(Q_t\in\mathbb R^{K\times K}\) has nonnegative entries and rows summing to one:

    \[ q(x_t\mid x_{t-1})=\operatorname{Cat}(x_t;x_{t-1}Q_t). \]

    The cumulative transition is

    \[ \bar Q_t=Q_1Q_2\cdots Q_t, \qquad q(x_t\mid x_0)=\operatorname{Cat}(x_t;x_0\bar Q_t). \]

    D3PMs generalize uniform categorical corruption to structured matrices, including nearest-neighbor and absorbing-mask transitions.

    For a uniform replacement law with replacement probability \(\beta_t\),

    \[ Q_t=(1-\beta_t)I+\beta_t\frac{\mathbf 1\mathbf 1^\top}{K}. \]

    Terminal mixing is a measurable approximation¤

    Let \(U=\mathbf 1\mathbf 1^\top/K\) and \(\alpha_t=1-\beta_t\). Since \(U^2=U\) and \(IU=UI=U\), the cumulative uniform-replacement kernel has the exact form

    \[ \bar Q_t=\bar\alpha_t I+(1-\bar\alpha_t)U, \qquad \bar\alpha_t=\prod_{s=1}^t\alpha_s. \]

    For a clean token \(i\) this gives

    \[ q(x_t\mid x_0=i) =\bar\alpha_t\,\delta_i+(1-\bar\alpha_t)\operatorname{Unif}(K), \]

    so its exact total-variation distance from the uniform reference is

    \[ D_{\mathrm{TV}}\!\left(q(x_t\mid x_0=i),\operatorname{Unif}(K)\right) =\bar\alpha_t\left(1-\frac1K\right). \]

    This calculation separates three statements that are often blurred together: the chain has a uniform stationary law, the finite-time terminal marginal is close to that law, and the reverse model is initialized from that law. Only the first is automatic from the kernel. For independently corrupted sequences, a small one-token discrepancy can accumulate across coordinates; it should be bounded at the joint level used by the sampler. An absorbing chain instead approaches a point mass on the mask token and has different support and initialization behavior.

    For an absorbing mask \(m\) appended to the vocabulary, ordinary token \(i\) stays at \(i\) with probability \(1-\beta_t\) and moves to \(m\) with probability \(\beta_t\), while \(Q_t(m,m)=1\). The first chain approaches a full-support uniform reference; the second approaches a point mass. Their endpoint, support, posterior behavior, and numerical analysis are therefore different even though both can train a clean-token predictor.

    Corruption Families¤

    • Uniform replacement: drift toward the uniform distribution.
    • Absorbing mask: tokens transition to a mask state and remain there in forward time.
    • Embedding-neighbor transitions: prefer semantically or geometrically nearby categories.
    • Application-specific kernels: preserve graph, ordinal, or chemical structure when valid.

    The stationary/reference distribution, mixing rate, and reverse difficulty depend on \(Q_t\). A vocabulary-level uniform replacement is not the discrete analogue of small Gaussian noise in every semantic sense.

    Exact Forward Posterior¤

    The posterior over the previous token is available during training because \(x_0\) is known. Under the row-vector convention,

    \[ q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0) \propto (x_tQ_t^\top)\odot(x_0\bar Q_{t-1}). \]

    The normalizer is the forward probability of \(x_t\) given \(x_0\). A neural model may predict \(x_0\), \(x_{t-1}\), transition logits, or another parameterization and then construct a reverse distribution.

    The normalized vector is

    \[ q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0) =\frac{(x_tQ_t^\top)\odot(x_0\bar Q_{t-1})} {x_0\bar Q_t x_t^\top}. \]

    This follows directly from Bayes' rule:

    \[ q(x_{t-1}=j\mid x_t,x_0) =\frac{Q_t(j,x_t)\,[x_0\bar Q_{t-1}]_j} {[x_0\bar Q_t]_{x_t}}. \]

    The denominator proves why matrix order and row/column conventions cannot be changed casually. Under a column-vector convention the transposes and multiplication order change.

    An \(x_0\)-predicting network can induce a reverse kernel by marginalizing the analytic posterior over predicted clean tokens:

    \[ p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t) =\sum_{\hat x_0} q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,\hat x_0) p_\theta(\hat x_0\mid x_t). \]

    This is a parameterization, not an assertion that \(\hat x_0\) was independently sampled. Directly predicting \(p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\) is also normalized but does not automatically share the same inductive bias or auxiliary clean-token loss.

    Variational Bound¤

    For reverse model \(p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\) and prior \(p(x_T)\), the negative ELBO decomposes into a terminal prior term, intermediate KL terms, and reconstruction at the first step:

    \[ -\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ELBO}} =D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(x_T\mid x_0)\|p(x_T)) +\sum_{t=2}^{T} \mathbb E_q D_{\mathrm{KL}} \bigl(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\|p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\bigr) -\mathbb E_q\log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_1). \]

    Auxiliary cross-entropy on \(x_0\) can improve optimization but changes the combined objective. Report whether likelihood uses the pure bound or an added training term.

    The derivation begins by inserting the known forward chain as a variational distribution:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x_0) =\log\mathbb E_{q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)} \frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)} \ge \mathbb E_q\log\frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)}. \]

    Regrouping this Jensen bound yields the terminal, intermediate, and reconstruction terms above. The inequality can be loose because the forward path posterior need not equal the learned model posterior. Reporting \(\exp(-\mathrm{ELBO}/D)\) as “perplexity” must retain the fact that it is a bound and must use the same tokenizer and reduction as the comparison.

    Worked Two-State Chain¤

    Let

    \[ Q=\begin{bmatrix}0.9&0.1\\0.2&0.8\end{bmatrix}. \]

    \[ q(x_1=0\mid x_2=1,x_0=0) \propto0.9\times0.1=0.09, \]
    \[ q(x_1=1\mid x_2=1,x_0=0) \propto0.1\times0.8=0.08. \]

    After normalization the posterior is approximately \((0.529,0.471)\). The observed corruption endpoint does not uniquely reveal the intermediate state.

    The posterior calculation also catches a common intuition error. Although state 1 is less likely after the first step, it is more likely to remain 1 at the second step. Replacing the posterior with the forward marginal \((0.9,0.1)\) would discard this endpoint evidence and train the wrong reverse target.

    Parallel Factorization and Its Error¤

    For a vector of tokens, many implementations corrupt positions independently and factorize each reverse step across positions conditioned on the full noisy state. Within-step sampling is parallel, while dependencies are mediated across iterations. Fewer iterations expose more simultaneous conditional independence; more iterations allow dependencies to propagate but cost more NFEs.

    The distinction can be written explicitly. A factorized reverse step uses

    \[ p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t) =\prod_{i=1}^D p_\theta(x_{t-1}^i\mid x_t), \]

    which may still model global context through the shared network input \(x_t\). It cannot, however, represent residual dependence among coordinates conditional on that same \(x_t\) within one draw. Multiple smaller steps introduce new shared states through which those dependencies can propagate. Blockwise or structured reverse kernels offer an intermediate point at greater sampling cost.

    Learning the forward construction without losing tractability¤

    A fixed Markov corruption is sufficient for a diffusion ELBO, but it is not necessary. The forward construction is used during training to sample noisy marginals and evaluate posterior targets. A more general construction remains usable when it provides

    1. efficient samples from \(q_\phi(z_t\mid x)\);
    2. tractable posteriors \(q_\phi(z_s\mid z_t,x)\) for \(s=t-1\); and
    3. consistent marginals and posteriors, including the clean and reference boundary conditions.

    Forward-Learned Discrete Diffusion (FLDD, ICLR 2026) uses the reverse-time factorization

    \[ q_\phi(z_{0:T}\mid x) =q_\phi(z_T\mid x) \prod_{t=1}^{T}q_\phi(z_{t-1}\mid z_t,x). \]

    This is a normalized variational path distribution but is generally non-Markovian in forward time, because the posterior factors condition on the clean datum \(x\). The paper learns factorized categorical noisy marginals

    \[ q_\phi(z_t\mid x)=\prod_{i=1}^{D} \operatorname{Cat}\!\left(z_t^i;u_\phi^i(x,t)\right) \]

    and couples adjacent marginals to construct consistent tractable posteriors. The reverse generator remains factorized by coordinate given the current full state. Joint optimization can therefore adapt the forward target

    \[ q_\phi(z_s\mid z_t) =\mathbb E_{q_\phi(x\mid z_t)} \left[q_\phi(z_s\mid z_t,x)\right] \]

    toward a conditional that the efficient reverse family can approximate in fewer transitions.

    This does not mean that an arbitrary learned corruption is valid. Boundary conditions, normalization, marginal–posterior consistency, gradient estimation through categorical samples, and the variational terms all remain part of the model. It also does not remove dependencies from the generated data law: each factorized reverse conditional can depend on the entire current sequence, and dependencies propagate across transitions.

    Peer-reviewed research. FLDD reports better few-step likelihood or sample quality than fixed-forward baselines on the paper's image, molecule, and language experiments. The conclusion is bounded by its forward parameterization, factorized reverse family, datasets, step budgets, and evaluation protocols; it is not evidence that learned forward processes dominate every fixed kernel.

    3. Continuous-Time Markov Chains and Discrete Scores¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the forward equation for finite Markov chains and basic continuous-time probability. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. derive reverse jump rates by equating probability flux;
    2. explain why a discrete score is indexed by an allowed transition rather than by a numeric token coordinate;
    3. test whether local edge ratios can arise from one globally compatible distribution;
    4. derive uniformization and distinguish it from Euler stepping, event simulation, and tau-leaping;
    5. analyze absorbing endpoints, support, and initialization mismatch; and
    6. separate ratio-estimation, numerical, parallelization, clipping, and initialization errors.

    A CTMC does not “move continuously between tokens.” Its state is piecewise constant and changes at random jump times. Continuous time describes the clock and instantaneous rate law, not a Euclidean interpolation between category IDs.

    A time-dependent CTMC uses generator matrix \(R_t\) with

    \[ R_t(i,j)\ge0\quad(i\ne j), \qquad R_t(i,i)=-\sum_{j\ne i}R_t(i,j). \]

    For row probability vector \(p_t\), the forward master equation is

    \[ \frac{dp_t}{dt}=p_tR_t. \]

    For a short interval \(\Delta t\), transition probability is approximately \(I+\Delta tR_t\), provided the step is small enough. Matrix exponentials give exact transitions for constant rates but are infeasible on enormous joint state spaces.

    The diagonal constraint has a probabilistic meaning. From state \(i\), the probability of no jump during a short interval is \(1-\Delta t\sum_{j\ne i}R_t(i,j)+o(\Delta t)\), while the probability of jumping to \(j\) is \(\Delta tR_t(i,j)+o(\Delta t)\). A learned matrix with a positive off-diagonal entry but an independently predicted diagonal need not conserve probability.

    This factorization is a debugging tool. A ratio estimator can be correct while a coarse numerical kernel is wrong, and a normalized numerical kernel can faithfully simulate incorrectly estimated rates. The output law belongs to the whole composition.

    Reverse-Time Rates¤

    If the forward process can jump from \(i\) to \(j\), the reverse rate from \(j\) to \(i\) depends on the marginal density ratio:

    \[ \bar R_t(j,i)=R_t(i,j)\frac{p_t(i)}{p_t(j)}, \qquad i\ne j, \]

    when \(p_t(j)>0\). This ratio is the discrete counterpart of the score information used in continuous diffusion. It is not a gradient with respect to token IDs.

    The formula follows from local flux. Forward trajectories carry probability from \(i\) to \(j\) at instantaneous rate \(p_t(i)R_t(i,j)\). Viewed backward, the same trajectory segment leaves \(j\) for \(i\), so

    \[ p_t(j)\bar R_t(j,i)=p_t(i)R_t(i,j). \]

    Dividing by \(p_t(j)\) gives the off-diagonal reverse rate; its diagonal is then set to the negative sum of reverse exit rates. This is a time-reversal identity, not a detailed-balance assumption. Detailed balance would additionally assert equality of opposing fluxes for a stationary distribution and would make the stationary forward and reverse generators coincide.

    Discrete Score and Score Entropy¤

    On a state graph, a discrete score can be represented by ratios between neighboring states:

    \[ s_t(x,y)=\frac{p_t(y)}{p_t(x)}. \]

    SEDD develops score entropy, an objective for estimating such ratios in discrete diffusion. The method avoids pretending categorical labels live in Euclidean space. Ratio parameterization, neighbor structure, and rate kernel jointly define the model.

    For a positive distribution \(p\), positive estimator \(s_\theta(x,y)\), and nonnegative edge weights \(w_{xy}\), score entropy can be written

    \[ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{SE}} =\mathbb E_{x\sim p}\sum_{y\ne x}w_{xy} \left[ s_\theta(x,y) -\frac{p(y)}{p(x)}\log s_\theta(x,y) +K\!\left(\frac{p(y)}{p(x)}\right) \right], \]

    where \(K(a)=a(\log a-1)\) is independent of \(\theta\). For every edge with positive weight, the pointwise population minimizer is \(s^*(x,y)=p(y)/p(x)\). The ratio-containing display defines the divergence but is not directly computable from data samples. The implicit equivalent, up to a constant, is

    \[ \mathbb E_{x\sim p}\sum_{y\ne x} \left[w_{xy}s_\theta(x,y)-w_{yx}\log s_\theta(y,x)\right]. \]

    On enormous spaces even this neighbor sum can be expensive. Denoising score entropy instead uses known forward transition probabilities conditioned on \(x_0\), yielding a Monte Carlo objective whose population target is the noisy marginal ratio. The equality depends on the corruption kernel and support assumptions; “ratio model” alone does not establish it.

    Local ratios must agree around cycles¤

    If positive edge values really equal ratios from one distribution, they obey

    \[ s(x,y)s(y,x)=1 \]

    and, for every directed cycle \(x_0\to x_1\to\cdots\to x_m=x_0\),

    \[ \prod_{k=0}^{m-1}s(x_k,x_{k+1})=1. \]

    The product telescopes because each probability appears once in the numerator and once in the denominator. Conversely, on a connected graph, positive reciprocal edge ratios whose product is one on every cycle define vertex weights up to one global scale: choose a root weight, multiply ratios along a path, and use cycle consistency to prove path independence before normalizing.

    This gives a finite-state compatibility test analogous to Chapter 1's conditional-compatibility test. A neural ratio field can be positive on every edge and still fail it. Multiplying such estimates into rates produces a valid CTMC after the diagonal is repaired, but not necessarily the exact time reversal of any single claimed marginal law.

    Sampling: Gillespie and Tau-Leaping¤

    The exact stochastic simulation algorithm samples a waiting time from the total exit rate and then chooses one jump. On a factorized high-dimensional process this can require many events. Tau-leaping advances a finite interval by sampling several approximately independent events using rates frozen or approximated over the interval.

    Large leaps reduce sequential steps but increase error through simultaneous changes and stale rates. Adaptive steps use total rates, uncertainty, or error estimates. A claim of “32 steps” must state whether a step permits one event, multiple coordinate jumps, correctors, or model reevaluation.

    Four commonly conflated samplers are:

    • Gillespie/event simulation: sample one exact waiting time and event for the current finite-rate CTMC; exact between model-rate changes but potentially many events;
    • uniformization: dominate exit rates by \(\Lambda\), sample Poisson event counts, and use the stochastic kernel \(I+R/\Lambda\); exact for a fixed finite generator when implemented without truncation;
    • Euler stepping: use \(I+hR_t\) once per grid interval, requiring \(h\) small enough for nonnegative probabilities;
    • tau-leaping: freeze or approximate rates over an interval and allow multiple events, reducing network calls at a discretization cost.

    For a fixed finite generator \(R\), choose \(\Lambda\ge\max_i[-R(i,i)]\) and define

    \[ P=I+\frac{R}{\Lambda}. \]

    \(P\) is row-stochastic, and the exact transition semigroup admits the uniformization identity

    \[ e^{tR} =e^{-\Lambda t}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\Lambda t)^n}{n!}P^n =\mathbb E_{N\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\Lambda t)}[P^N]. \]

    Thus uniformization changes the clock representation, not the target process. Truncating the sum after \(M\) terms introduces at most the omitted Poisson-tail mass in row-wise total variation. Time-varying learned rates require interval-wise domination or another solver analysis; the fixed-generator identity cannot simply be reused across an interval in which \(R_t\) changes.

    Model reevaluation frequency is distinct from simulated event count. A tau leap may generate many coordinate changes from one network call; an event simulator may reuse the same rates for several events only when the rate law permits it.

    Absorbing-Mask CTMC¤

    If each token jumps to a mask at rate \(\beta(t)\) and never leaves it in forward time, then survival probability is

    \[ P(x_t=x_0)=\exp\left(-\int_0^t\beta(s)ds\right). \]

    Reverse time unmasking uses learned ratios/posteriors. Several coordinates can be unmasked per numerical step, connecting CTMC diffusion to masked iterative generation. The probabilistic construction clarifies transition rates that a heuristic confidence schedule leaves implicit.

    Pure absorbing corruption has a singular endpoint: all mass eventually reaches the mask state. Analyses may use finite terminal time, early stopping, or a surrogate initialization near the point mass. These devices are part of the theorem and implementation. Treating the terminal point mass as a full-support prior can make a KL expression infinite or reverse ratios undefined.

    Boundary and Support Issues¤

    Ratios are undefined when the denominator probability is zero. Forward corruption is chosen to make relevant states reachable and ratios estimable. Clipping ratios stabilizes computation but changes reverse rates; report it as sampler/model approximation.

    Worked two-state reverse CTMC¤

    Let

    \[ R=\begin{bmatrix}-1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad p_0=(0.8,0.2). \]

    The forward marginal is

    \[ p_t=\left(0.5+0.3e^{-2t},\;0.5-0.3e^{-2t}\right). \]

    At \(t=(\log2)/2\), \(p_t=(0.65,0.35)\). The reverse rate from current state 0 to previous state 1 is

    \[ \bar R_t(0,1)=R(1,0)\frac{p_t(1)}{p_t(0)} =\frac{0.35}{0.65}\approx0.538. \]

    The opposing reverse rate is \(0.65/0.35\approx1.857\). Although the forward rates are symmetric, the time-reversed rates at this nonstationary marginal are not. At stationarity \(p=(0.5,0.5)\), both reverse rates return to 1.

    This example separates three objects: \(R\) is known forward corruption, the marginal ratio changes with time, and \(\bar R_t\) is the generative rate. A network usually estimates the middle object and the implementation constructs the third.

    Continuous flow matching learns a velocity field for a probability path. On a discrete space, probability moves through jump rates rather than deterministic Euclidean velocity. A discrete probability path \(p_t\) satisfies

    \[ \frac{dp_t}{dt}=p_tR_t \]

    for some generator \(R_t\). Many generators can realize the same marginal path; choosing rates determines finite-time trajectories and sampling cost.

    One marginal path, many event mechanisms¤

    Even on two states, a desired marginal derivative does not determine unique rates. Write \(a_t=R_t(0,1)\) and \(b_t=R_t(1,0)\). Then

    \[ \dot p_t(1)=p_t(0)a_t-p_t(1)b_t. \]

    For any nonnegative \(b_t\) that makes \(a_t=[\dot p_t(1)+p_t(1)b_t]/p_t(0)\) nonnegative, the same \(p_t\) is realized. Increasing both opposing fluxes while preserving their difference raises activity: trajectories jump more often even though the marginal curve is unchanged. In larger state graphs, the choice of edges and cycle flows creates still more freedom.

    For a single categorical coordinate, couple a source token \(X_0\) and data token \(X_1\), and define the conditional interpolation

    \[ p_t(a\mid X_0,X_1) =(1-\kappa_t)\delta_{X_0}(a)+\kappa_t\delta_{X_1}(a), \qquad \kappa_0=0,\quad\kappa_1=1. \]

    Let \(p_{1\mid t}(a\mid z)\) be the posterior probability that the endpoint token is \(a\) given current state \(z\). One forward generating probability velocity is

    \[ u_t(a,z) =\frac{\dot\kappa_t}{1-\kappa_t} \left[p_{1\mid t}(a\mid z)-\delta_z(a)\right]. \]

    For a sufficiently small \(h\), the categorical update is \(X_{t+h}\sim\delta_{X_t}(\cdot)+h,u_t(\cdot,X_t)\). The components of \(u_t\) sum to zero, but this coordinate-level object is not literally one full-state \(|\mathcal V|^D\times|\mathcal V|^D\) rate matrix. Safe step sizes or an equivalent rate construction are needed to keep every update probability nonnegative.

    Conditional-to-Marginal Learning¤

    As in continuous flow matching, construct a conditional path given a target \(x_1\) and derive conditional transition information. A neural model predicts a denoiser/posterior from the current state; averaging conditional targets at the population optimum yields the marginal object needed for generation under the construction's assumptions.

    The regression identity is ordinary conditional expectation. If a training target is \(Y=\delta_{X_1}\) and the model minimizes categorical cross-entropy from noisy state \(X_t\), then the unrestricted population minimizer is

    \[ \hat p^*_{1\mid t}(a\mid X_t) =P(X_1=a\mid X_t). \]

    Substituting this posterior into the conditional velocity and averaging over endpoint pairs conditioned on the current state gives the marginal velocity. Finite data, finite network capacity, optimization error, and off-training-path states break the population argument. “Flow matching is simulation-free” refers to target construction during training; sampling still simulates discrete updates.

    More explicitly, if \(u_t(a,z\mid X_0,X_1)\) is a valid conditional probability velocity, then the marginal velocity at occupied state \(z\) is

    \[ u_t^{\mathrm{marg}}(a,z) =\mathbb E\!\left[ u_t(a,z\mid X_0,X_1) \mid X_t=z \right]. \]

    Multiplying by \(P(X_t=z)\) and summing over endpoint pairs recovers the derivative of the marginal probability through the law of total expectation. Cross-entropy learns the endpoint posterior needed inside this expectation; it does not directly regress an arbitrary full-state matrix. The derivation also explains the off-path problem: if a coarse sampler visits states with negligible training probability, the conditional expectation target there is weakly identified.

    Discrete Flow Matching develops general discrete probability paths and sampling formulas using learned posterior parameterizations. This is a common Markov-generator construction, not a claim that all masking heuristics optimize the same flow objective.

    Different source–target couplings can induce the same endpoint marginals but different conditional targets. An independent coupling is easy to sample; a structured or optimal coupling can shorten transitions or preserve semantics but may itself require computation or approximation. Scheduler changes after training can be valid only when the learned posterior and path formula support that transformation.

    Order-Agnostic Autoregression¤

    An order-agnostic objective samples an ordering or revealed subset and predicts unobserved variables given observed ones. If generation samples a complete ordering and follows normalized conditionals associated with that ordering, it defines a joint conditional on the order; averaging over orders defines a mixture.

    For permutation \(\pi\) of \(D\) coordinates,

    \[ p_\theta(x\mid\pi) =\prod_{k=1}^D p_\theta\!\left(x_{\pi_k}\mid x_{\pi_{<k}},\pi\right), \qquad p_\theta(x)=\sum_\pi p(\pi)p_\theta(x\mid\pi). \]

    Each conditional product is normalized if every factor is normalized. The mixture is therefore normalized, although evaluating it exactly may require summing over \(D!\) orders. If the network omits \(\pi\) but is trained on revealed subsets, the intended consistency across multiple paths to the same subset becomes another modeling question.

    If generation instead fills many positions independently and chooses future subsets by confidence, the resulting law includes the adaptive policy. It should not be assigned an autoregressive likelihood unless that trajectory probability is derived and marginalized correctly.

    The branches can share parameters and training examples while defining different generated distributions. Order randomization is exogenous in the left branch; confidence decisions are endogenous in the right branch.

    State Graphs and Superposition¤

    Discrete tokens may change one coordinate at a time, whole blocks, graph edges/nodes, or structured actions. The generator matrix encodes allowed transitions. Superposing generators

    \[ R_t=R_t^{(1)}+R_t^{(2)} \]

    combines independent event types when off-diagonal rates remain valid. This supports mixed operations such as mask/unmask, substitution, insertion, and deletion, but variable-length state spaces require careful non-explosion and normalization assumptions.

    Superposition is algebraically exact for generators defined on the same state space and clock. It does not make separately trained neural rate estimates jointly calibrated. If deletion changes sequence alignment, an insertion rate must be interpreted on the resulting state, not on stale pre-deletion indices. A finite total exit rate is needed to rule out infinitely many jumps in finite time.

    Few-Step and Adaptive Generation¤

    Confidence, entropy, total exit rate, or predicted change can determine which coordinates update. Adaptive policies can allocate compute to uncertain regions. They can also bias samples because update decisions depend on model outputs. Evaluate the final sampler, not only token denoising accuracy.

    There are three different routes to fewer network evaluations:

    1. larger numerical steps, which increase discretization and simultaneous-event error;
    2. better transition kernels or correctors, which reuse the same trained estimator but change inference;
    3. training or distillation for large jumps, which changes the learned estimator and consumes extra training compute.

    A consistency objective on discrete posterior bridges belongs to the third category. It should not be described as merely applying a continuous probability-flow ODE solver: a categorical sample space has no ordinary deterministic ODE trajectory through intermediate token values.

    Text¤

    Text tokens are nominal categories with syntax and semantics that are not captured by numeric proximity. Uniform, masking, and embedding-neighbor corruption make different assumptions. Parallel refinement supports infilling and bidirectional constraints naturally, while autoregressive decoding provides an exact chain-rule likelihood and causal prefix semantics.

    Token perplexity is comparable only when tokenization, likelihood/bound definition, and reduction match. A denoising cross-entropy at sampled noise levels is not automatically perplexity.

    Masked language models naturally expose both left and right context, but unconditional variable-length generation still needs a length law or a semi-autoregressive/block construction. Padding to a fixed maximum and later deleting pads is not innocuous if the model treats padding as a semantic state. Deletion–Insertion Diffusion instead defines 2026 CTMC processes on the union of finite sequence lengths; its dynamic-programming objective and reported efficiency belong to that particular construction, not to masked diffusion generally.

    Uniform-state corruption permits substitutions and therefore revision. Pure absorbing masking only removes information in forward time and ordinarily commits clean tokens in reverse time. Generalized Interpolating Discrete Diffusion combines masking and uniform noise to study revisable language generation. Its compute-matched results are peer-reviewed research evidence for that implementation, not a proof that mixed corruption is optimal.

    Images and Video¤

    Discrete visual models depend on a tokenizer such as VQ-VAE/VQGAN-style codecs. The prior models token distributions; the decoder determines visual reconstruction. Report codebook size, grid shape, temporal compression, reconstruction metrics, prior metrics, and end-to-end sample metrics.

    MAGVIT extends masked generation to multiple video tasks. Such systems are representation–prior–sampler stacks rather than a single masked Transformer result.

    Let encoder \(E\) map pixels \(y\) to codes \(x\), prior \(p_\theta(x)\) generate codes, and decoder \(D\) return \(\hat y=D(x)\). End-to-end error has at least two sources:

    \[ \text{data}\xrightarrow{E}\text{code distribution} \xrightarrow{p_\theta\text{ approximation}}\text{generated codes} \xrightarrow{D}\text{generated media}. \]

    Reconstruction FID measures a codec-related ceiling; prior likelihood or code accuracy measures the token model; end-to-end FID or human evaluation measures their composition. Changing the codec can shorten the token sequence and improve sampler latency while worsening reconstruction, so tokens/second alone is not an end-to-end speed–quality result.

    Audio¤

    Neural audio codecs produce multiple codebooks or residual-quantization streams. Parallel generation across time and codebook levels must respect dependencies. Coarse-to-fine schedules, delayed patterns, and hybrid AR/iterative decoders expose different latency-quality trade-offs.

    If codebook \(r\) encodes the residual left by codebooks \(1{:}r-1\), sampling every codebook independently at one time step discards the codec's causal construction. A model may instead reveal coarse streams first, offset streams in time, or generate groups autoregressively and refine within a group. Report both acoustic duration per generated token and sequential network depth; a short codec sequence can dominate an apparent algorithmic advantage.

    Graphs, Molecules, and Proteins¤

    State spaces include node/edge labels, sequences, 3-D coordinates, and validity constraints. A discrete transition must preserve or repair valence, connectivity, grammar, or sequence/structure consistency. Projecting invalid states after each step changes the Markov kernel unless included in the model.

    For formal-language constraints under arbitrary-order infilling, Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations separates completion feasibility, syntactic support, semantic validity, and preservation of the unconstrained model law.

    The complete Random Graphs and Relational Generators volume supplies the graph-law semantics underneath these transitions, including relabeling invariance, variable node count, sparse exchangeability, and the difference between one adjacency representation and an unlabeled graph.

    For an unlabeled graph, node-index permutations represent the same object. An equivariant denoiser satisfies that relabeling the noisy graph relabels its edge/node predictions consistently. This is separate from validity: an equivariant network can still propose impossible valence. Three strategies have different semantics:

    1. restrict the state graph so invalid edits have zero rate;
    2. learn over a broad state space and reject or repair at the end;
    3. project after each transition and include that projection in the defined kernel.

    Only the first guarantees validity by construction. The second changes acceptance and may be inefficient; the third can be principled but must be analyzed and trained as the actual transition process.

    Mixed objects need mixed generators. A molecule can have discrete atom/bond types and continuous coordinates. Alternating a categorical jump kernel with an equivariant coordinate SDE defines a composition; jointly trained rates and scores do not automatically correspond to one exact reverse process unless derived from a shared forward law.

    Conditioning and Guidance¤

    Conditions enter through cross-attention, concatenation, adapters, or conditional normalization. Classifier-free guidance can combine conditional and unconditional logits or denoising targets, but the scale convention and normalization must be stated. Guidance may sharpen condition adherence while reducing diversity or distorting transition rates.

    For ratio estimators, one multiplicative analogue of log-space guidance is

    \[ s_{\mathrm{guided}}(x,y\mid c) =s_{\mathrm{uncond}}(x,y)^{1-w} \,s_{\mathrm{cond}}(x,y\mid c)^w. \]

    This remains positive but need not be a globally compatible ratio field, and inserting it into reverse rates changes total exit rates. Combining normalized clean-token logits and then rebuilding a reverse kernel is a different operation. Comparisons must state target, guidance equation, scale \(w\), whether confidence is computed before or after guidance, and whether conditional/unconditional branches double NFE or are batched.

    The guidance chapter derives the finite-state Doob transform. Discrete Guidance Matching, published at ICLR 2026, derives exact target transition rates for its discrete-flow construction. Its exactness statement concerns the derived rate under the learned guidance objects; network approximation and time discretization remain separate errors.

    Prerequisites and capstone objectives¤

    This chapter assumes all previous chapters plus basic experimental design. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. implement a target-aware training loop without conflating mask CE, ELBO, ratio, and flow losses;
    2. audit a sampler as a stochastic kernel, including numerical and policy approximations;

    The capstone principle is simple: the trained network is not the deployed generator. The deployed generator is the network, representation, transition law, numerical method, guidance, constraints, stopping rule, and randomness contract composed together.

    Moving upward in realism adds confounders. Moving downward in this ladder removes them. A credible diagnosis reports both directions: controlled oracle evidence for attribution and end-to-end evidence for relevance.

    The derivative layer is equally composite. Masked cross-entropy, transition likelihoods, ELBO terms, trajectory rewards, and straight-through discrete surrogates require different estimators.     Training Checklist¤

    1. State the corruption/mask distribution and time sampling law.
    2. State the target: clean token, previous token, ratio, rate, or transition logits.
    3. Define loss weighting and whether it is an ELBO, auxiliary CE, score entropy, or flow objective.
    4. Verify padding and mask tokens are not conflated unless intended.
    5. Test transition matrices for nonnegativity and row normalization; test CTMC generators for zero row sums.
    6. Measure accuracy/calibration across noise levels and mask ratios, not only the average.
    7. Audit tokenizer/codebook utilization for non-text modalities.

    A family-neutral training skeleton is:

    repeat
    draw clean object x0 and condition c
    draw training query: mask m, time t, or source endpoint x_source
    sample corrupted/intermediate state xt from the declared path
    predict the declared target from (xt, t, c)
    evaluate the matching loss and importance/time weight
    update parameters; record loss by time, corruption, length, and condition
until the data, optimization, and compute budget is exhausted

    Every noun in this pseudocode is part of the experiment. If time is sampled from proposal \(r(t)\) while the intended objective integrates weight \(w(t)\), an unbiased Monte Carlo estimate uses \(w(t)/r(t)\). Clipping that weight may improve stability but changes the optimized objective. Likewise, sampling mask ratios uniformly is not equivalent to sampling time uniformly when mask survival is a nonlinear function of time.

    Sampler Audit¤

    Record the full state transition:

    • which positions are eligible to change;
    • whether proposals are sampled or argmax;
    • confidence computation and tie-breaking;
    • remasking/commit schedule;
    • temperature, top-k/top-p, and guidance;
    • number of model evaluations and corrector steps;
    • whether previously fixed tokens can change;
    • termination rule and invalid-state handling.

    Two papers using the same network objective but different confidence schedules can define materially different generators.

    A useful sampler skeleton makes policy decisions explicit:

    state = draw_reference(length, condition, rng)
for interval in schedule:
    estimate = model(state, interval.time, condition)
    kernel = build_transition(estimate, interval, guidance, constraints)
    state = sample_kernel(kernel, rng)
    state = optional_corrector_or_projection(state, estimate, rng)
return decode(state)

    For reproducibility, use a stateless or explicitly split random-key discipline. Proposal sampling, confidence perturbation, event counts, tie-breaking, and decoder stochasticity require separate recorded streams. Batched samples should not accidentally share a mask pattern or Poisson event count unless that coupling is intentional.

    Error decomposition by intervention¤

    It is tempting to write one additive “diffusion error,” but the terms interact. A more defensible diagnostic separates them experimentally:

    • reference/terminal error: the initialized law differs from the intended terminal marginal;
    • estimator error: learned posterior, ratio, or rate differs from its population target;
    • time-discretization error: a finite step approximates a time-varying process;
    • parallel-kernel error: independently sampled simultaneous coordinates omit conditional dependence;
    • policy error: confidence selection or adaptive scheduling changes the intended transition law;
    • intervention error: guidance, clipping, rejection, or validity projection changes rates or probabilities;
    • representation error: tokenizer or codec excludes or distorts relevant outputs.

    Use exact finite-state posteriors to remove estimator error, very fine steps to estimate discretization limits, one-coordinate updates to remove parallel factorization, and codec reconstructions to bound representation error. The interventions will not make the errors perfectly additive, but they identify which subsystem causes a discrepancy.

    Evaluation Contract¤

    Table DISC.6. Evaluation Contract
    Claim Required evidence
    Faster than AR same tokenizer/backbone/condition, wall clock, batch, hardware, quality operating point
    Better likelihood exact joint, variational bound, or surrogate identified; same tokenization/reduction
    Better infilling mask distribution, constraint satisfaction, diversity, and human/task evaluation

    Likelihood and sample evaluation answer different questions. An ELBO evaluates a model/path objective; samples evaluate the numerical and policy composition. A strong held-out bound can coexist with a poor few-step sampler. Conversely, a guided sampler can score well on preferences while no longer sampling the unguided model whose bound was reported.

    For discrete language, report at least held-out likelihood or a clearly named bound, entropy, external-model generative perplexity with the evaluator named, diversity/duplication, task success, and human judgments where appropriate. External-model perplexity is not likelihood under the generator and inherits evaluator bias. For structured science domains, add validity, novelty relative to train data, property calibration, and downstream experimental uncertainty.

    An oracle finite-state benchmark is unusually valuable here. Choose a small HMM or graphical model with exact \(p(x_0)\) and exact denoising posteriors; run the production sampler with the oracle denoiser; enumerate its output distribution; then compute total variation, KL where support allows, transition statistics, and aggregate text-style metrics. This reveals whether a seemingly good metric can hide sampler-induced distributional error.

    Failure Modes¤

    • Incompatible masked conditionals: good local CE but incoherent iterative samples.
    • Early commitment: confident wrong tokens constrain all later rounds.
    • Oscillation: remasked tokens alternate between locally plausible states.
    • Parallel-factorization artifacts: mutually dependent positions update inconsistently.
    • Schedule mismatch: the model is evaluated at mask ratios/noise states poorly represented in training.
    • Codec bottleneck: prior improvements cannot exceed reconstruction support.
    • Guidance collapse: condition adherence rises while diversity falls.
    • Terminal mismatch: reverse sampling starts from an approximation that is not close enough under the metric used by the theorem.
    • Rate explosion or clipping: small marginal probabilities create large reverse ratios, then implementation clipping silently defines a different process.
    • Length/padding leakage: a fixed-length model learns padding artifacts or an evaluation strips states the model treated probabilistically.
    • Cache illusion: nominally parallel generation repeatedly recomputes clean context and is slower than a cache-aware baseline.

    Research Directions¤

    Active directions include adaptive CTMC solvers, state-size-independent error analysis under structured assumptions, discrete consistency/few-step maps, learned transition graphs, insertion/deletion processes, mixed discrete–continuous generators, and post-training for reasoning. Research claims should distinguish peer-reviewed empirical results from theory under ideal ratio error.

    Recent sampling theory studies tau-leaping convergence under score-entropy control, including structure-adaptive rates for masking processes. Such results are peer-reviewed research and assumption-bound; they do not certify an arbitrary large language model with clipped approximate ratios.

    Three COLT papers establish complementary results. Optimal Inference Schedules for Masked Diffusion Models gives an exact expected-divergence characterization for a distribution and unmasking schedule, proves that competing with the distribution-specific optimum can require strong prior knowledge, and relates achievable parallelism to total and dual total correlation. Efficient Sampling with Discrete Diffusion Models gives sharp/adaptive tau-leaping guarantees under score-entropy control: its uniform-process complexity has unavoidable ambient-dimension dependence in general, while its masking result adapts through an effective-total-correlation quantity. Quantized Transition Diffusion reformulates a continuous target through spatial quantization and density ratios, using truncated uniformization to obtain its stated total-variation query bound. These are peer-reviewed research results. Their metrics, corruption processes, estimator assumptions, and algorithms differ; none follows merely from using a masked Transformer.

    This 2026 theory complements, rather than supersedes, NeurIPS 2025 sampler guarantees for tau-leaping, Euler, and Tweedie-style methods and absorbing-process analysis. Successively improved bounds use different assumptions and algorithms; quoting only asymptotic iteration counts without the divergence, dimension, vocabulary, score-error, and initialization terms is not a meaningful comparison.

    Categorical Flow Maps (ICML 2026) take a different few-step route: a self-distilled map moves probability toward a predicted endpoint in the simplex, allowing continuous-map distillation techniques while ultimately generating categorical objects. The paper reports image, molecular-graph, and text experiments, including one-step settings. This is peer-reviewed empirical research for its parameterization and protocols—not evidence that its continuous simplex trajectory is the same object as the CTMC token trajectory in Chapter 3, nor that one-step quality is distributional correctness.

    Peer-Reviewed Language-Model Research¤

    The following results were verified against their ICLR/ICML 2026 OpenReview decisions on 2026-07-18. They address different objectives, samplers, and evaluations and should not be collapsed into one “diffusion LM” result:

    • AR-to-diffusion conversion: Efficient-DLM: From Autoregressive to Diffusion Language Models, and Beyond in Speed (ICML 2026) continues pretraining an autoregressive model with block-wise attention and position-dependent masking. Its reported 8B accuracy/throughput gains are comparisons against named models under the paper's serving protocol, not a theorem that converted DLMs dominate AR models.
    • Parallel-sampling theory: Diffusion Language Models are Provably Optimal Parallel Samplers (ICLR 2026) proves simulation results in a formal parallel-sampling model, using polynomial-length chain-of-thought and, for optimal space, revision or remasking. The theorem is about representational/sequential-depth capability; it does not give a trained finite network an optimal wall-clock sampler.
    • Variable-length state spaces: Beyond Masks: Efficient, Flexible Diffusion Language Models via Deletion-Insertion Processes (ICLR 2026) defines deletion and insertion CTMCs over the union of all finite sequence lengths, avoiding mandatory mask/pad states and deriving a likelihood-bounded objective with dynamic-programming subsequence counts.
    • Few-step distillation: Ultra-Fast Language Generation via Discrete Diffusion Divergence Instruct (ICLR 2026) distils a pretrained discrete-diffusion LM with an integral-KL objective and reports protocol-specific perplexity/acceleration trade-offs. The teacher, student, NFE, entropy, and extra training cost remain part of the system comparison.
    • Sampler correctness: Is Your Diffusion Sampler Actually Correct? A Sampler-Centric Evaluation of Discrete Diffusion Language Models (ICML 2026) uses an exact HMM denoiser to isolate sampling error. Its experiments show that few-step parallel samplers can be distributionally wrong even with an oracle denoiser and that NLL, generative perplexity, or MAUVE need not reveal the mismatch. This directly motivates the sampler audit above.
    • Predictor–corrector inference: The Diffusion Duality, Chapter II: Ψ-Samplers and Efficient Curriculum (ICLR 2026) generalizes discrete predictor–corrector sampling across noising processes and reports protocol-bounded gains for uniform-state language and image diffusion. These experiments challenge a universal preference for absorbing masks; they do not prove one corruption dominates across data and compute regimes.
    • Single-stage few-step consistency: Consistent Diffusion Language Models (ICML 2026) train agreement in expectation across exact discrete posterior bridges rather than importing a nonexistent categorical probability-flow ODE. Its few-step results are author experiments under the paper's model/data protocols, and its claimed limiting relationships require their stated bridge construction.
    • Architecture–sampler co-design: Efficient Parallel Samplers for Recurrent-Depth Models and Their Connection to Diffusion Language Models (ICML 2026 Spotlight) reinterprets refinement in recurrent-depth transformers to advance token positions while latent computation continues. The reported speedup applies to named 3.5B recurrent-depth models and hardware budgets; it is not a generic conversion of fixed-depth AR models.
    • Any-order/AR hybrid: Esoteric Language Models: A Family of Any-Order Diffusion LLMs (ICML 2026) connects masked diffusion to any-order autoregression and uses causal attention to obtain exact KV caching and exact likelihood for its construction while interpolating between parallel and sequential generation. Its reported speed–quality frontier is an empirical result under the paper's model, schedule, and serving protocol; it does not make exact caching or exact likelihood available to arbitrary bidirectional masked denoisers.

    Together these papers sharpen four separate questions: what distribution/objective is learned, what transition kernel is executed, how architecture exposes parallel work, and what latency/quality operating point is measured. Progress on one does not answer the other three.

    Cumulative worked study: an oracle denoiser with a wrong sampler¤

    Let the length-two data law be

    \[ p_{\mathrm{data}}(00)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(11)=\tfrac12, \qquad p_{\mathrm{data}}(01)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(10)=0. \]

    At the fully masked state [M,M], an oracle clean-token denoiser returns marginal probability \(1/2\) for 0 and 1 at both positions. A one-step parallel sampler draws the positions independently, so its output law is uniform over all four strings. Its total-variation distance from data is

    \[ \operatorname{TV}(p_{\mathrm{parallel}},p_{\mathrm{data}}) =\frac12\sum_x|p_{\mathrm{parallel}}(x)-p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)| =\frac12. \]

    The denoiser is exact; all error comes from the factorized simultaneous kernel. An order sampler that first draws either coordinate from \((1/2,1/2)\) and then draws the other from its exact conditional produces only 00 or 11 and is exact. Thus “perfect token predictions” and “perfect joint samples” are different claims.

    Now build the full experiment:

    1. train a network on masks from this known law and compare its conditionals with the oracle;
    2. substitute the oracle into each production sampler to isolate sampler error;
    3. sweep number of updated coordinates, remasking, and NFE;
    4. report exact TV/KL where supported, pair agreement, entropy, and an external aggregate score;
    5. repeat with a perturbed denoiser to measure interaction between estimator and sampler errors.

    The design scales conceptually to an HMM, where forward–backward inference supplies exact masked posteriors while dynamic programming supplies the target sequence law. It also explains the 2026 sampler-centric result without treating a controlled HMM as a quantitative prediction for every large language model.

    Source-tracing checklist¤

    Before carrying a result from a paper into a comparison table, record:

    1. venue status and exact paper version;
    2. data, split, tokenizer/codec, context or grid size, and conditional setting;
    3. whether the reported likelihood is exact, a bound, or an external-model score;
    4. corruption/path, target parameterization, loss weighting, and auxiliary losses;
    5. sampler, schedule, NFE definition, changing-token count, correctors, guidance, and clipping;
    6. parameter and active-parameter counts, training tokens/FLOPs, batch, precision, hardware, and wall clock;
    7. entropy/diversity, memorization, constraint, and human-evaluation controls;
    8. whether the result is an author report, independent reproduction, partial reproduction, or unverified claim.

    For a theorem, replace benchmark fields with the state space, target metric, error model, initialization, regularity/support assumptions, hidden constants, and oracle access. A theorem about sequential query complexity and an experiment about accelerator latency belong in adjacent columns, not one undifferentiated “speed” cell.

    Final Synthesis¤

    Discrete iterative generation replaces a fixed left-to-right order with a learned path through corrupted or partially observed states. Masked refinement supplies flexible bidirectional conditionals; D3PMs supply explicit discrete Markov chains and ELBOs; CTMC models supply reverse jump rates from density ratios; discrete flow matching supplies a general probability-path view. Parallelism comes from changing many coordinates per round, but it introduces within-step independence and discretization choices. A complete scientific description must name the state space, corruption/path, target, transition law, sampler, and likelihood semantics.
