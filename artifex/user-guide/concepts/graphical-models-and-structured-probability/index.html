    Graphical Models and Structured Probability¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Classical Generative Models and Graphical Structure
    • Bayesian Learning and Model Assessment
    • Latent Variables and Inference
    • Objectives and Statistical Distances

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Graphical Inference 1 — Factors, Graphs, and Queries
    • Graphical Inference and Message Passing

    A graphical model is a probability model whose structure is exposed through a graph. The graph can make a large joint law intelligible: it shows which local conditional distributions or factors define the law, which conditional independences follow for every compatible parameter choice, and which variables must interact when a query is answered. It does not, by itself, make inference cheap, establish causality, or turn arbitrary node scores into a normalized distribution.

    This volume develops that distinction from a four-variable finite model. Directed graphs first encode a normalized generative process. Separation then turns graph topology into conditional-independence statements. Undirected graphs replace local conditionals with potentials and a global normalizer. Conditional random fields, pseudolikelihood, mixed graphs, factor graphs, plates, and latent projections are introduced only after their probability objects are explicit. The final chapter combines these pieces into a model-design workflow and routes specialized constructions to the rest of the book.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–3 explain directed and undirected laws through finite tables, diagrams, and exact enumeration.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–4 add model learning, conditional structured laws, normalization, and diagnostics.
    Research: read all six chapters, including positivity boundaries, Markov equivalence, composite-likelihood uncertainty, chain-graph semantics, and latent projections.

    Evidence convention

    Definitions and independently derived finite identities are Established. Archival results are Peer-reviewed research. A diagrammatic analogy is never promoted to a distributional or causal equivalence. Every graph conversion states which probability laws, independence statements, or queries it preserves.

    A visual grammar for this volume¤

    The arrows describe a design discipline, not a new probabilistic graph. Begin with the generated variables and their support. State the law and the meaning of every local term. Use a graph to expose structure. Only then choose a query and an algorithm.

    Recurring notation¤

    Table GRAPH.1. Recurring notation
    Symbol Meaning
    \(G=(V,E)\) a graph with vertices \(V\) and edges \(E\)
    \(X_V=(X_v:v\in V)\) random variables indexed by graph vertices
    \(\operatorname{pa}(v),\operatorname{ch}(v)\) parents and children of \(v\) in a directed graph
    \(\operatorname{ne}(v)\) neighbors of \(v\) in an undirected graph
    \(X_A\perp X_B\mid X_C\) conditional independence under a declared distribution
    \(\psi_c(x_c)\) nonnegative potential on factor or clique scope \(c\)
    \(\gamma(x)=\prod_c\psi_c(x_c)\) unnormalized mass or density
    \(Z=\int\gamma(x)\,\mu(dx)\) partition function relative to base measure \(\mu\)
    \(\mathcal I(G),\mathcal I(p)\) conditional-independence statements encoded by \(G\) or satisfied by \(p\)

    For continuous variables, every density is relative to a declared reference measure. For deterministic or hard-constrained factors, zeros in the support are part of the model and invalidate results that assume a strictly positive density.

    1. Directed Graphs as Generative Laws¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know joint, marginal, and conditional probability. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish a directed graph from the conditional distributions that parameterize it;
    • derive a joint law from normalized local conditionals;
    • generate an exact prior sample in topological order;
    • read local, ordered, and global Markov statements without causal overreach; and
    • use a plate to show repetition and parameter sharing without mistaking it for an algorithm.

    A graph is structure; the conditionals define the law¤

    Let \(G\) be a directed acyclic graph, or DAG. A topological order places every parent before its children. Associate each vertex \(v\) with a conditional probability kernel

    \[ p_v(x_v\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(v)}). \]

    The directed graphical model is the joint law

    \[ p(x_V)=\prod_{v\in V}p_v(x_v\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(v)}). \]

    Each factor is locally normalized over \(x_v\). Iterated summation or integration in reverse topological order therefore proves that the product is globally normalized. This is stronger than drawing arrows: replacing a conditional distribution by an arbitrary nonnegative score generally destroys the proof.

    The same DAG can host tabular, linear-Gaussian, neural, mixture, or deterministic conditionals. Those parameterizations can differ radically in expressivity and compute while sharing the same graph-level conditional independences.

    Running model: two possible causes and two measurements¤

    Consider a monitoring system with four binary variables:

    • \(F=1\) means a component is faulty;
    • \(O=1\) means the machine is overloaded;
    • \(A=1\) means a general alarm fires; and
    • \(S=1\) means a targeted inspection signal is positive.

    The model assumes independent prior causes, an alarm affected by both causes, and a targeted signal affected only by the fault:

    Its joint distribution is

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(f,o,a,s) &=p(f)p(o)\\ &\quad\times p(a\mid f,o)p(s\mid f). \end{aligned} \]

    Use

    \[ p(F=1)=0.1, \qquad p(O=1)=0.2, \]

    and the alarm table

    Table GRAPH.2. Running model: two possible causes and two measurements
    \(f\) \(o\) \(p(A=1\mid f,o)\)
    0 0 \(0.01\)
    1 0 \(0.80\)
    0 1 \(0.60\)
    1 1 \(0.95\)

    with \(p(S=1\mid F=0)=0.1\) and \(p(S=1\mid F=1)=0.9\). The graph alone contains none of these numbers. The tables alone do not make their shared conditional-independence structure as visible as the graph.

    Exact generation by ancestral sampling¤

    One valid topological order is \(F,O,A,S\). An exact prior sample is obtained by:

    1. draw \(F\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(0.1)\);
    2. draw \(O\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(0.2)\) independently;
    3. draw \(A\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(p(A=1\mid F,O))\); and
    4. draw \(S\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(0.1+0.8F)\).

    Every completed trace has the product probability above. Repeating the procedure produces independent samples if the random draws are independent. Ancestral sampling answers an unconditional generation query. Conditioning on \(A=1\) is a different task and usually cannot be implemented by simply clamping \(A\) while retaining the same forward sampler.

    Parameter count and structural economy¤

    Suppose every vertex has \(K\) states and at most \(r\) parents. A full joint table over \(d=|V|\) variables requires \(K^d-1\) free probabilities. A tabular DAG requires at most

    \[ d(K-1)K^r \]

    free parameters. The reduction is exponential in \(d\) only when \(r\) remains small. Sparse arrows do not guarantee that the local conditional functions are simple, statistically identifiable, or computationally cheap.

    Three equivalent Markov views for a DAG law¤

    For distributions that factorize over a DAG, the following descriptions agree under the standard finite or density-kernel formulation:

    1. Ordered Markov property. Given its parents, a node is independent of its earlier nonparents in a topological order.
    2. Local Markov property. Given its parents, a node is independent of its nondescendants that are not parents.
    3. Global Markov property. Sets separated by the DAG's d-separation criterion are conditionally independent.

    The graph guarantees only statements in \(\mathcal I(G)\). Particular parameter values may create additional independences. Conversely, a distribution that violates even one graph-encoded independence is not Markov with respect to that graph.

    Plates show indexed repetition¤

    Let \(\theta\) be a global parameter and let each observation have a local latent state \(Z_n\):

    \[ p(\theta,z_{1:N},x_{1:N}) =p(\theta)\prod_{n=1}^{N}p(z_n\mid\theta)p(x_n\mid z_n,\theta). \]

    The plate states which variables repeat and which parameter is shared. It does not express sequential recurrence, declare exchangeability beyond the displayed factorization, choose an inference method, or imply that mini-batch subsampling is unbiased for every objective.

    Complete finite checks¤

    The running model gives a direct normalization check:

    \[ \sum_{f,o,a,s}p(f)p(o)p(a\mid f,o)p(s\mid f)=1, \]

    because the sums over \(s\) and \(a\) each remove a normalized conditional, followed by the normalized root laws. It also implies

    \[ F\perp O, \qquad S\perp\{O,A\}\mid F \]

    only where the second statement is interpreted through the graph and joint law. The alarm's two parents are marginally independent but, as Chapter 2 shows, generally become dependent after the alarm is observed.

    Counterexample: the first-order Markov product

    A first-order chain has

    \[ p(x_{1:T})=p(x_1)\prod_{t=2}^{T}p(x_t\mid x_{t-1}). \]

    Writing the final product as \(\prod_t p(x_t\mid x_{1:t-1})\) is merely the unrestricted chain rule unless the Markov equality is stated. The two conditionals are equal under the first-order assumption; the notation should not hide that assumption.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A DAG plus normalized local kernels defines a normalized joint law and an ancestral sampler. Its edges encode a family of factorizations and graph-implied independences, not one neural architecture, one inference procedure, or a causal claim. Plates compress repeated structure but do not execute the model.

    Retrieval practice: directed laws
    1. Why is a product of DAG conditionals normalized without a global partition function?
    2. Can two models with the same DAG use different conditional families?
    3. What query does ancestral sampling answer?
    4. What does a plate fail to specify?

    Solutions. (1) Sum or integrate child variables in reverse topological order; each normalized conditional contributes one. (2) Yes. The graph fixes parent scopes, not the tabular, Gaussian, neural, or deterministic parameterization. (3) Exact unconditional sampling from the joint law. (4) It does not select an inference, optimization, or sampling algorithm, and it does not represent arbitrary recurrence.

    Bridge. The DAG factorization generates samples locally, but posterior evidence can connect variables that were independent in the prior. Continue continuously or open Chapter 2.

    2. Separation, Colliders, and Graphical Claims¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand the directed factorization in Chapter 1. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • apply d-separation to chains, forks, colliders, and descendants of colliders;
    • compute a node's Markov blanket in a DAG;
    • distinguish an I-map, minimal I-map, perfect map, and faithful distribution;
    • recognize Markov-equivalent DAGs; and
    • explain why statistical arrows are not automatically causal arrows.

    Active and blocked trails¤

    A trail ignores arrow direction while tracing adjacent vertices. Given a conditioning set \(C\), a trail is active when:

    • every noncollider on the trail is outside \(C\); and
    • every collider on the trail has itself or at least one descendant in \(C\).

    Two vertex sets are d-separated by \(C\) when no active trail connects them. For every distribution Markov to the DAG,

    \[ A\mathbin{\perp_G}B\mid C \quad\Longrightarrow\quad X_A\perp X_B\mid X_C. \]

    The implication is a sound statement about every compatible distribution. Its converse for one fitted parameter vector requires additional conditions.

    Removing conditioned vertices from a DAG is not a correct general separation algorithm because conditioning on a collider can open rather than close a trail. Bayes-ball and ancestral-moralization procedures implement the full rule.

    Worked explaining-away calculation¤

    In the monitoring model, \(F\to A\leftarrow O\) is a collider. Before observing the alarm,

    \[ F\perp O. \]

    The alarm probability is

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(A=1) &=0.0072+0.064\\ &\quad+0.108+0.019\\ &=0.1982. \end{aligned} \]

    Average the alarm probability over overload when the component is faulty:

    \[ r_F=0.8(0.80)+0.2(0.95)=0.83. \]

    The fault-and-alarm mass is therefore

    \[ p(F=1,A=1)=0.1r_F=0.083. \]

    Consequently,

    \[ p(F=1\mid A=1) =\frac{0.083}{0.1982} \approx0.419. \]

    If overload is also observed, it already explains much of the alarm. The two fault-specific contributions to the alarm evidence at \(O=1\) are

    \[ q_1=0.1(0.95)=0.095. \]

    For a nonfaulty component, the corresponding mass is

    \[ q_0=0.9(0.60)=0.540. \]

    Hence \(p(A=1\mid O=1)=q_1+q_0=0.635\), and

    \[ p(F=1\mid A=O=1) =\frac{q_1}{q_1+q_0}. \]

    Substitution gives approximately \(0.150\).

    Observing one possible cause reduces belief in the other after their common effect is fixed. This is explaining away. The sign and size of the change depend on the conditional table; the graph guarantees dependence can be induced, not that it must have a particular numerical direction for every parameterization.

    Selection is conditioning too¤

    Suppose a dataset includes a machine only when \(A=1\). Analysis inside the selected dataset conditions on a common effect of fault and overload. A correlation between \(F\) and \(O\) can therefore appear even though their population priors are independent. This is collider or selection bias, not evidence that one cause generates the other.

    The same issue arises when inclusion, response, filtering, survival, or successful decoding is affected by multiple variables. A selection variable belongs in the generative model whenever its mechanism changes the distribution being analyzed.

    Markov blankets¤

    For a vertex \(v\) in a DAG, its Markov blanket contains:

    \[ \operatorname{mb}(v) =\operatorname{pa}(v) \cup\operatorname{ch}(v) \cup\bigcup_{u\in\operatorname{ch}(v)} \left(\operatorname{pa}(u)\setminus\{v\}\right). \]

    Given this set, \(X_v\) is conditionally independent of every other vertex. In the running model,

    \[ \operatorname{mb}(F)=\{A,S,O\}. \]

    The co-parent \(O\) belongs because conditioning on their common child \(A\) couples the parents. A Markov blanket is a distributional shielding set under the model, not necessarily a minimal predictive feature set in finite data and not a causal adjustment set by default.

    Four levels of graphical fit¤

    Let \(\mathcal I(G)\) be the independences implied by a graph and \(\mathcal I(p)\) those satisfied by a distribution.

    Table GRAPH.3. Four levels of graphical fit
    Term Requirement What it permits
    I-map \(\mathcal I(G)\subseteq\mathcal I(p)\) the distribution may have extra independences
    minimal I-map removing any graph edge breaks the I-map property another nonnested graph may still be smaller or equally valid
    perfect map \(\mathcal I(G)=\mathcal I(p)\) graph and distribution independence models agree exactly
    faithful distribution usually \(\mathcal I(p)=\mathcal I(G)\) for the declared graph excludes cancellations that create extra independences

    Faithfulness is an assumption about a graph-distribution pair. It is not a consequence of factorization. For example, a linear path \(X\to M\to Y\) plus a direct edge \(X\to Y\) can have coefficients whose total effects cancel, producing an independence not visible from the graph.

    Markov equivalence¤

    Two DAGs are Markov equivalent when they encode the same conditional-independence model. For DAGs, this occurs exactly when they share:

    1. the same undirected skeleton; and
    2. the same unshielded colliders, also called v-structures.

    Thus \(X\to M\to Y\), \(X\leftarrow M\to Y\), and \(X\leftarrow M\leftarrow Y\) are Markov equivalent: all encode \(X\perp Y\mid M\). The collider \(X\to M\leftarrow Y\) is not equivalent because it instead encodes marginal independence of \(X\) and \(Y\).

    Observational conditional independences generally identify an equivalence class, not a unique arrow orientation. Interventions, time order, functional restrictions, background knowledge, or other assumptions are needed to go further.

    Statistical DAGs and causal DAGs are different declarations¤

    A statistical DAG asserts factorization and conditional independence. A causal DAG additionally interprets each local conditional as a stable mechanism under a declared intervention semantics. The same drawn arrow can therefore carry different meaning in two books or software systems.

    Do not infer any of the following from statistical factorization alone:

    • that a parent is a manipulable cause;
    • that deleting incoming arrows computes an intervention;
    • that latent common causes are absent;
    • that conditioning on a Markov blanket estimates a causal effect; or
    • that observational fit identifies individual counterfactuals.

    Those questions belong to Causal Generative Models, where observational, interventional, and counterfactual laws are separate objects.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    D-separation converts a DAG into distribution-wide independence claims. Chains and forks are blocked by conditioning on their middle vertex; colliders behave in the opposite way. I-maps, faithfulness, and Markov equivalence clarify what graph structure does and does not identify. Causal semantics require an additional model contract.

    Retrieval practice: separation
    1. When does conditioning open a trail rather than block it?
    2. Why is a co-parent in a DAG Markov blanket?
    3. What two graphical features characterize DAG Markov equivalence?
    4. Does a perfectly fitted observational DAG identify interventions?

    Solutions. (1) When the conditioned vertex is a collider on that trail, or when a descendant of the collider is conditioned on. (2) Conditioning on the shared child can couple its parents. (3) The same skeleton and the same unshielded colliders. (4) No. Intervention semantics and identifying assumptions are additional to observational factorization.

    Bridge. Directed graphs normalize through local conditionals. Symmetric spatial and relational interactions are often easier to state as undirected potentials, which changes both the normalization and learning problem. Continue continuously or open Chapter 3.

    3. Undirected Laws, Potentials, and Energies¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand factorization, support, and conditional independence. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • construct a normalized undirected law from nonnegative potentials;
    • state the positivity boundary in the Hammersley–Clifford theorem;
    • derive full conditionals and Markov blankets from local factors;
    • interpret zeros in a Gaussian precision matrix; and
    • distinguish an energy value, a normalized probability, and a sample.

    Local compatibility, global normalization¤

    An undirected graphical model, Markov random field, or MRF associates nonnegative potentials with scopes such as cliques or factors:

    \[ \gamma(x)=\prod_{c\in\mathcal C}\psi_c(x_c), \qquad \psi_c(x_c)\ge 0. \]

    Relative to a base measure \(\mu\), this product defines a probability law only if

    \[ 0<Z=\int\gamma(x)\,\mu(dx)<\infty, \qquad p(x)=\frac{\gamma(x)}{Z}. \]

    Unlike a DAG conditional, a potential need not sum or integrate to one over any argument. Multiplying one potential by a positive constant changes \(Z\) by the same constant but leaves \(p\) unchanged. Potentials are therefore not individually identifiable without a gauge or parameter convention.

    For a finite undirected graph, clique potentials make the graph separation rule immediate: if a set \(C\) separates \(A\) from \(B\), conditioning on \(X_C\) splits all remaining factors between the two sides, so \(X_A\perp X_B\mid X_C\).

    Hammersley-Clifford and the support boundary¤

    For a strictly positive finite mass function, the following are equivalent:

    • the distribution satisfies the undirected global Markov property; and
    • the distribution factorizes into positive potentials over graph cliques.

    This is the Hammersley–Clifford theorem. Strict positivity is not decoration. When the law assigns zero mass to some configurations, a clique factorization still implies the associated Markov properties under its conditions, but the converse can fail. A hard constraint should therefore be stated as part of the support or as an explicit zero-valued factor rather than inferred from conditional independence alone.

    Counterexample boundary: hard parity

    Let three binary variables have positive mass only when \(X_1\oplus X_2\oplus X_3=0\). Conditional probabilities can become undefined on zero-probability events, and support restrictions can create dependencies not summarized by ordinary positive-density reasoning. Do not apply a positivity-based equivalence after silently deleting the forbidden states.

    Energy is a parameterization, not a probability¤

    Positive potentials can be written as exponentials of local negative energies:

    \[ \psi_c(x_c)=\exp[-E_c(x_c)], \qquad E(x)=\sum_c E_c(x_c), \]

    which gives

    \[ p(x)=\frac{\exp[-E(x)]}{Z}. \]

    An energy may be negative, zero, or positive. Adding a constant \(b\) to every energy changes \(Z\) by \(e^{-b}\) and leaves the distribution unchanged. Only energy differences affect probability ratios:

    \[ \log\frac{p(x)}{p(x')}=-E(x)+E(x'). \]

    A low-energy configuration need not be typical in high dimension because a vast number of moderately higher-energy configurations may carry more total probability mass. An optimizer that finds \(\arg\min_x E(x)\) returns a mode, not a representative sample.

    Worked two-spin Ising model¤

    Let \(X_1,X_2\in\{-1,+1\}\) and

    \[ \gamma(x_1,x_2)=\exp(Jx_1x_2), \qquad J=\log 2. \]

    Equal spins have weight \(2\) and unequal spins have weight \(1/2\):

    Table GRAPH.4. Worked two-spin Ising model
    \((x_1,x_2)\) \(\gamma(x_1,x_2)\) \(p(x_1,x_2)\)
    \((-1,-1)\) \(2\) \(0.4\)
    \((-1,+1)\) \(1/2\) \(0.1\)
    \((+1,-1)\) \(1/2\) \(0.1\)
    \((+1,+1)\) \(2\) \(0.4\)

    Thus \(Z=5\). The local conditional does not require the global partition function:

    \[ p(X_1=+1\mid X_2=x_2) =\frac{e^{Jx_2}}{e^{Jx_2}+e^{-Jx_2}} =\sigma(2Jx_2). \]

    For \(x_2=+1\), this probability is \(4/5\). This cancellation makes single-site Gibbs updates and pseudolikelihood possible even when \(Z\) is hard to compute.

    Undirected separation and Markov blankets¤

    In an undirected graph, \(C\) separates \(A\) and \(B\) when every path from \(A\) to \(B\) passes through \(C\). For a positive law that is Markov to the graph,

    \[ C\text{ separates }A\text{ and }B \quad\Longrightarrow\quad X_A\perp X_B\mid X_C. \]

    The Markov blanket of a vertex is simply its neighbor set. Its full conditional uses only factors that mention that vertex:

    \[ p(x_v\mid x_{V\setminus\{v\}}) \propto \prod_{c:v\in c}\psi_c(x_c). \]

    This is a local normalization identity. It neither evaluates the global partition function nor guarantees that repeated local updates mix rapidly.

    Gaussian graphical models¤

    For a nonsingular Gaussian law

    \[ p(x)\propto \exp\left( -\frac12x^\top\Lambda x+\eta^\top x \right), \]

    the precision matrix \(\Lambda=\Sigma^{-1}\) must be symmetric positive definite. For distinct indices \(i,j\),

    \[ \Lambda_{ij}=0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad X_i\perp X_j\mid X_{V\setminus\{i,j\}}. \]

    Zeros in the covariance matrix instead encode marginal uncorrelatedness, which equals marginal independence only for a joint Gaussian. Sparse covariance, sparse precision, and sparse directed regression matrices represent different graphical objects.

    For a three-node Gaussian chain, a tridiagonal precision matrix may yield a dense covariance matrix. The endpoints can be marginally correlated while remaining conditionally independent given the middle node. This is expected, not a contradiction.

    Sparse precision is computational structure as well as independence structure. When a non-Gaussian observation model is coupled to a latent Gaussian Markov random field, Graphical Inference and Message Passing develops integrated nested Laplace approximation and states the sparsity, hyperparameter-dimension, and posterior-geometry limits under which that deterministic approximation is appropriate.

    Table GRAPH.5. Directed versus undirected generation

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    An MRF defines a global law through local nonnegative potentials and a partition function. Positivity makes undirected Markov properties and clique factorization equivalent; hard constraints require separate care. Energy is a nonunique score parameterization, and local conditionals can be tractable even when global normalization is not.

    4. Learning, Normalization, and Structured Conditionals¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know maximum likelihood, latent-variable marginalization, and statistical distances. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • state when complete-data DAG learning separates by local conditional;
    • derive the positive- and negative-phase terms in an undirected likelihood gradient;
    • distinguish pseudolikelihood from likelihood and calibrate its uncertainty correctly;
    • define a conditional random field and its input-dependent normalizer; and
    • explain label bias without equating local normalization with short context.

    Complete-data DAG learning: the modular case¤

    For independent complete observations \(x^{(1:N)}\) from a DAG,

    \[ \log p_\theta(x^{(1:N)}) =\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{v\in V} \log p_{\theta_v} \left(x_v^{(n)}\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(v)}^{(n)}\right). \]

    The optimization separates by vertex when the parameter blocks \(\theta_v\) are disjoint and the regularizer or prior also separates. Parameter tying, hierarchical priors, neural modules shared across vertices, or constraints across conditional tables couple the problems even when every variable is observed.

    For a tabular conditional, the maximum-likelihood estimate is the empirical conditional frequency when the parent configuration has positive count. Zero-count parent configurations are unidentified by likelihood. Smoothing, hierarchy, or a structured parameterization supplies information; the graph alone does not.

    Hidden variables recouple local terms¤

    With a latent variable \(Z_n\) and observation \(X_n\),

    \[ p_\theta(x_n)=\sum_{z_n}p_{\theta_Z}(z_n)p_{\theta_X}(x_n\mid z_n). \]

    The logarithm of this sum does not decompose into separate objectives for \(\theta_Z\) and \(\theta_X\). EM uses posterior expected sufficient statistics; direct gradients use the identity

    \[ \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x) =\mathbb E_{p_\theta(z\mid x)} \left[\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x,z)\right] \]

    when differentiation and marginalization may be exchanged. Exactness of the identity does not make the posterior expectation cheap.

    Undirected maximum likelihood¤

    Consider an exponential-family MRF

    \[ p_\theta(x)= \exp\!\left(\theta^\top\phi(x)-A(\theta)\right), \qquad A(\theta)=\log\int e^{\theta^\top\phi(x)}\,\mu(dx). \]

    For the average log likelihood, the gradient is

    \[ \nabla_\theta\ell(\theta) =\frac1N\sum_{n=1}^{N}\phi(x^{(n)}) -\mathbb E_{p_\theta}[\phi(X)]. \]

    The empirical or positive phase is directly observed. The model or negative phase requires expectation under the current normalized law. At an attained interior optimum in a minimal regular family, the corresponding moments match. Approximate MCMC, variational, score-matching, contrastive, and ratio-estimation methods replace or avoid this expectation under different assumptions; they are not interchangeable estimators of finite-sample likelihood.

    Pseudolikelihood is a composite objective¤

    The nodewise pseudolikelihood for one fully observed vector is

    \[ \operatorname{PL}_\theta(x) =\prod_{v\in V}p_\theta(x_v\mid x_{V\setminus\{v\}}), \]

    and the empirical objective sums its log over cases. Each full conditional has only a local normalizer because factors not containing \(v\) cancel. The product is generally not the joint probability \(p_\theta(x)\) and need not sum to one over complete configurations.

    Maximum pseudolikelihood can be consistent under identifiability, correct specification, sampling, support, and regularity conditions appropriate to the model. This is not a universal finite-sample equivalence to maximum likelihood. Its asymptotic covariance uses sensitivity and variability matrices:

    \[ \operatorname{Avar}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{PL}}) =H^{-1}JH^{-1}, \]

    not a likelihood Fisher-information formula unless a special identity makes them coincide. Calibration should therefore use composite-likelihood or estimating-equation theory, a justified bootstrap, or another method matched to the sampling design.

    Counterexample: conditionals are not a free joint model

    Independently fitting one predictor for every \(X_v\mid X_{-v}\) need not produce mutually compatible conditionals for any joint distribution. A shared MRF parameterization can enforce compatibility; separate black-box conditionals generally cannot assume it.

    Conditional random fields¤

    A conditional random field, or CRF, directly models a structured output \(Y\) given an observed input \(X=x\):

    \[ p_\theta(y\mid x) =\frac{\exp s_\theta(x,y)}{Z_\theta(x)}, \qquad Z_\theta(x)=\sum_{y'}\exp s_\theta(x,y'). \]

    For a log-linear score \(s_\theta(x,y)=\theta^\top\phi(x,y)\), the conditional log likelihood is concave in \(\theta\) and the negative log likelihood is convex. A deep nonlinear score generally removes that convexity. In either case, the gradient contains a feature or score-gradient expectation under \(p_\theta(y\mid x)\) for every input.

    A CRF is a normalized conditional probability model. A structured energy predictor that returns only

    \[ \hat y(x)=\arg\min_y E_\theta(x,y) \]

    may be useful without defining probabilities. Calling it a CRF requires a finite normalizer and a declared base measure or state space.

    Local and global normalization¤

    A locally normalized sequence model has

    \[ p(y_{1:T}\mid x) =\prod_{t=1}^{T}p(y_t\mid y_{<t},x), \]

    where each decision normalizes over its legal next states. A globally normalized model assigns complete-path scores and normalizes once over all paths. The distinction is normalization scope, not merely whether the network sees future input tokens: a local conditional may use all of \(x\) and still be locally normalized.

    Local softmax probabilities are invariant to adding a history-dependent constant to all next-state logits. Consequently, absolute score mass discarded at one state cannot be recovered later. In constrained state graphs, states with few outgoing transitions can retain probability regardless of weak downstream evidence. This family of representational effects is label bias.

    At state \(a\), the only legal transition has local probability one regardless of its absolute score. A globally normalized path model can compare that weak complete path against both paths through \(b\). Lookahead features can mitigate some local decisions but do not make local and global normalization algebraically identical.

    Learning and sampling are separate choices¤

    Table GRAPH.6. Learning and sampling are separate choices
    Model A common training query A common generation query
    DAG local or joint likelihood ancestral sampling
    latent DAG marginal likelihood or ELBO sample latents, then descendants
    MRF/EBM likelihood, score, ratio, or contrastive objective MCMC or specialized exact sampler
    CRF conditional likelihood or structured surrogate conditional sampling or MAP given \(x\)

    The training objective need not supply the deployment sampler. Conversely, a sampler that leaves a target law invariant does not estimate its parameters. Keep the probability object, estimator, optimizer, and sampling transition distinct.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Complete-data DAG learning separates only under parameter and prior modularity. Latent marginalization and global normalizers recouple local factors. Pseudolikelihood replaces joint likelihood with a composite conditional objective and needs its own uncertainty theory. CRFs normalize complete output structures conditional on inputs; label bias concerns local normalization, not merely limited context.

    5. Representations, Conversions, and Latent Projections¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know directed and undirected Markov properties. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish refactoring a function from preserving an independence model;
    • moralize a DAG for a declared inference or separation task;
    • state when directed and undirected graphs can be perfect maps of the same independence model;
    • interpret factor graphs, chain graphs, and ADMGs under explicit conventions; and
    • route circuits, relational models, and probabilistic programs to their specialized volumes.

    Four preservation questions¤

    Before converting any graphical representation, ask what must remain unchanged:

    1. Function preservation: is the same normalized or unnormalized function represented?
    2. Distribution preservation: is the same law obtained after normalization or marginalization?
    3. Independence preservation: are exactly the same conditional-independence statements visible?
    4. Algorithm preservation: does the same complexity, schedule, or sampler remain valid?

    These are different claims. A conversion may preserve a joint law while hiding independences, or preserve a factor product while changing the graph on which a message algorithm runs.

    Factor graphs expose factor scopes¤

    If

    \[ \gamma(x)=\prod_{a\in\mathcal F}f_a(x_a), \]

    a bipartite factor graph has one variable vertex for every \(X_v\), one factor vertex for every \(f_a\), and an edge whenever \(v\in a\). It records the exact factor scopes rather than only pairwise adjacency.

    A DAG becomes a factor graph by creating one factor for each local conditional. An MRF becomes one by exposing its chosen potentials. Multiple factorizations can yield the same function, and merging or splitting factors can change message complexity without changing \(\gamma\).

    Forney or normal factor graphs place factors at vertices and variables on edges; equality constraints copy a variable into multiple factors. They are useful computational diagrams, but their edge directions, when drawn, need a declared signal or generative interpretation rather than inheriting DAG semantics automatically.

    Moralization is query-aware information loss¤

    To use undirected separation for a DAG query \(A\perp B\mid C\):

    1. retain \(A\cup B\cup C\) and their ancestors;
    2. connect every pair of parents with a common child;
    3. drop arrowheads; and
    4. test ordinary graph separation by \(C\).

    Connecting co-parents is moralization. Moralizing the entire DAG can add edges created by descendants irrelevant to a particular query and thereby hide valid independences. Taking the ancestral subgraph first preserves the d-separation test for that query.

    Moralization also helps compile directed factors into undirected cliques for elimination. It need not produce a perfect undirected independence map of the original DAG.

    Chordality and perfect directed/undirected maps¤

    A v-structure \(X\to M\leftarrow Y\) has no undirected perfect map: the DAG encodes \(X\perp Y\) but generally not \(X\perp Y\mid M\). An undirected chain encodes the opposite conditional pattern. Conversely, a chordless undirected four-cycle has no DAG perfect map without adding an independence or an edge.

    An undirected graph admits an orientation with no new unshielded colliders exactly when it is chordal, also called decomposable. A perfect elimination order then turns clique factorizations into directed conditionals while preserving the independence model. Chordality is also what allows junction-tree compilation without first adding fill edges.

    Why a generic undirected-to-directed dummy node needs conditions¤

    It is tempting to encode every potential \(\psi_c(x_c)\) as the probability of an observed child. A binary conditional must, however, lie in \([0,1]\). On finite support, choose a positive base law \(q(x)\) and constants \(M_c\) such that the likelihood factors are bounded. Conditioning on auxiliary events can then reproduce a target proportional to

    \[ q(x)\prod_c\frac{\psi_c(x_c)}{M_c}. \]

    This is a likelihood-evidence construction under explicit boundedness and base-law conditions. It is not a general local conversion for arbitrary continuous, unbounded, or hard-constrained potentials. Independently, any ordinary joint distribution can be written as a saturated DAG by the chain rule, but that construction can discard the MRF's sparse semantics and computational advantages.

    Chain graphs need a Markov convention¤

    A graph containing directed and undirected edges is not fully specified by saying “chain graph.” Several non-equivalent Markov interpretations exist, including Lauritzen–Wermuth–Frydenberg and alternative AMP or multivariate-regression semantics. A valid model declaration must name:

    • the allowed edge types and forbidden cycles;
    • the chain components;
    • the factorization or kernel associated with each component; and
    • the separation rule.

    Under an LWF-style block-recursive construction, directed edges order chain components and an undirected conditional random field defines each component given its parents. Switching conventions can change the independence model even when the picture is unchanged.

    Latent projection and ADMGs¤

    Marginalizing latent variables from a DAG can produce observed independences that no observed-variable DAG represents compactly. An acyclic directed mixed graph, or ADMG, uses directed edges \(X\to Y\) and bidirected edges \(X\leftrightarrow Y\). In a latent projection:

    • a directed observed edge records a directed path through latent nonendpoints; and
    • a bidirected edge records an appropriate latent common-cause path with arrowheads at both observed endpoints.

    Conditional independence is read by m-separation, an extension of d-separation. An ADMG edge is a statement in a marginal graph; it does not assert that one scalar latent parent exists for that pair, fix a unique structural equation model, or identify counterfactual coupling.

    A bidirected covariance graph can use missing edges to encode marginal independences, while a causal or latent-projection ADMG uses bidirected edges within a different mixed-graph Markov semantics. Similar arrowheads do not make the two objects interchangeable.

    6. From Model Design to Research Audit¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand all previous chapters. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to design, learn, query, and audit a structured probability model without conflating its graph, architecture, objective, or algorithm.

    The eight-part model contract¤

    For any graphical generative model, record:

    1. Variables and support. What is random, observed, latent, selected, repeated, or fixed?
    2. Reference measure. Is the law a mass function, Euclidean density, manifold density, point-process law, or mixed measure?
    3. Factorization. Which terms are normalized kernels and which are unnormalized potentials?
    4. Graph semantics. DAG, MRF, factor graph, chain graph under which convention, ADMG, plate, or computation graph?
    5. Query. Prior sample, conditional, marginal, evidence, MAP, structure, intervention, or counterfactual?
    6. Estimator. Likelihood, variational, score, ratio, pseudolikelihood, Bayesian posterior, or another declared objective?
    7. Algorithm. Enumeration, elimination, messages, MCMC, SMC, optimization, compilation, or amortization?
    8. Validation. Which exact small case, calibration target, convergence diagnostic, and misspecification check can falsify the implementation?

    This record prevents a common category error: an attractive graph can describe factor scopes while leaving the distribution improper, the query unidentified, or the proposed algorithm inexact.

    Capstone: the monitoring model from generation to diagnosis¤

    Return to

    \[ p(f,o,a,s)=p(f)p(o)p(a\mid f,o)p(s\mid f). \]

    Suppose both sensors are positive. Enumerating the four \((f,o)\) states gives unnormalized posterior weights:

    Table GRAPH.8. Capstone: the monitoring model from generation to diagnosis
    \(f\) \(o\) \(p(f)p(o)p(A=1\mid f,o)p(S=1\mid f)\)
    0 0 \(0.9(0.8)(0.01)(0.1)=0.00072\)
    0 1 \(0.9(0.2)(0.60)(0.1)=0.01080\)
    1 0 \(0.1(0.8)(0.80)(0.9)=0.05760\)
    1 1 \(0.1(0.2)(0.95)(0.9)=0.01710\)

    The evidence probability is

    \[ p(A=1,S=1)=0.08622. \]

    Hence

    \[ p(F=1\mid A=1,S=1) =\frac{0.05760+0.01710}{0.08622} \approx0.866. \]

    This calculation supplies several independent tests:

    • ancestral samples should approach the declared joint frequencies;
    • exact enumeration must normalize to one;
    • an elimination or message implementation must reproduce \(0.866\);
    • posterior samples should match all four normalized table entries; and
    • removing the \(F\to S\) edge changes both the factorization and the posterior, whereas changing the inference schedule should not change an exact answer.

    A counterfactual implementation audit¤

    Suppose code labels the arrow \(F\to A\) as causal and answers “what if the component were repaired?” by setting \(F=0\) in the observational conditional. That result is justified only if the model has declared stable structural mechanisms and the repair intervention replaces the \(F\) mechanism as assumed. A statistical DAG alone supports

    \[ p(A\mid F=0), \]

    not automatically

    \[ p(A\mid \operatorname{do}(F=0)). \]

    If fault status is confounded with an unmodeled maintenance regime, the two can differ. The correct response is to expand or qualify the causal model, not to infer intervention semantics from arrow direction.

    Diagnostics by abstraction layer¤

    Table GRAPH.9. Diagnostics by abstraction layer
    Layer A useful check A failure means
    probability law finite normalization or analytic integral the stated factors do not define the claimed law
    graph semantics hand-checked separation and factor scopes arrows or factors encode the wrong assumptions
    estimator population optimum and finite estimator derivation training may target a different object
    inference exact enumeration on a small instance implementation or approximation error
    sampler invariant-law check plus mixing diagnostics transitions may not represent target samples
    learning simulation-based parameter recovery under identifiability optimizer, estimator, or model can fail even on-model
    criticism discrepancies not used to fit the same feature blindly the model may miss scientifically relevant structure

    Passing one row never certifies the others. A perfectly normalized misspecified model can generate poor data. A sampler can mix well for the wrong energy. An accurate conditional predictor can be useless for an intervention.

    Misconception checks¤

    If the graph is sparse, is exact inference cheap?

    No. Sparse local degree can still produce large intermediate factors. Treewidth, state cardinalities, factor structure, and the query determine exact inference cost.

    Does an absent edge mean marginal independence?

    Not generally. In an undirected model it usually means conditional independence given all other vertices; in a DAG the relevant statement depends on d-separation. In a covariance graph, a missing bidirected edge can encode marginal independence under that graph's semantics.

    Does an MRF energy have to be positive?

    No. The unnormalized density \(e^{-E(x)}\) is positive for finite real energy, and additive energy constants cancel after normalization.

    Is pseudolikelihood just a fast way to compute likelihood?

    No. It is a different composite objective built from local conditionals. It can be statistically useful under stated conditions, but its efficiency and uncertainty differ from maximum likelihood.

    Are all arrows causal?

    No. A causal interpretation requires structural mechanisms, intervention semantics, and identifying assumptions beyond a statistical DAG factorization.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Unfaithful parameterization. Construct a linear-Gaussian DAG with two directed paths from \(X\) to \(Y\) whose effects cancel. List the graph-implied and parameter-specific independences.
    2. Hard constraint. Define a three-bit even-parity law. Determine which full conditionals exist on supported contexts and identify where a positivity-based theorem cannot be invoked.
    3. Composite likelihood. For the two-spin Ising model, derive the pseudolikelihood estimate from repeated complete observations and compare its finite objective with exact likelihood.
    4. Global normalization. Build a two-step state graph with unequal outgoing degrees. Show which history-dependent score shifts a local softmax discards and a global path normalizer retains.
    5. Latent projection. Start with a DAG containing one latent common cause and one latent mediator. Compute the observed ADMG and verify two m-separation queries.
    6. Causal boundary. Give two SCMs with the same observational DAG law but different counterfactual coupling. Explain which graph-level checks cannot distinguish them.

    Final synthesis and next steps¤

    Graphical models turn a large probability law into local objects with explicit structural meaning. Directed graphs provide normalized generative kernels and ancestral samples. D-separation identifies graph-wide conditional independences, including the exceptional behavior of colliders. Undirected graphs provide symmetric potentials, energies, and local full conditionals but usually introduce a global normalizer. Learning, conversion, and causal interpretation each add assumptions that a drawing cannot supply.

    The next foundation volume, Graphical Inference and Message Passing, takes correctly declared factor scopes and derives elimination, exact tree messages, junction trees, loopy approximations, and learned solvers. Use Energy-Based Models for nonlinear energies and MCMC training, Probabilistic Circuits for representation-level tractability, Random Graphs and Relational Generators when the graph itself is generated, and Causal Generative Models when mechanisms and interventions—not only conditional independence—define the query.
