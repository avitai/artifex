    2. Leakage, Contamination, and Deduplication¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and standard train/evaluation splits. By the end, you should be able to distinguish exact, near-duplicate, semantic, temporal, and entity leakage; select split units that match the generalization claim; explain false-positive and false-negative costs in deduplication; and design contamination audits spanning pretraining, post-training, retrieval, benchmarks, and generated evaluation data.

    Different overlap problems¤

    • Exact duplicate: identical bytes or normalized text.
    • Near duplicate: resized/cropped/re-encoded image, paraphrase, template variant, clip overlap.
    • Semantic duplicate: same fact/problem/scene with different surface form.
    • Entity leakage: the same person, patient, molecule scaffold, repository, or video appears across splits.
    • Temporal leakage: future information enters training or preprocessing.
    • Benchmark contamination: test questions, solutions, labels, or derived discussions enter training.
    • Evaluator leakage: the judge/embedding model was trained on the benchmark or generator outputs.

    Each needs different detection. Hashing catches exact identity; MinHash/suffix arrays catch long text overlap; perceptual hashes and learned embeddings catch media similarity; entity/time/source-group splits address shared causes.

    Duplicates reweight the objective¤

    Maximum likelihood on a multiset uses empirical probability

    \[ \hat p_D(x)=\frac{count_D(x)}{|D|}. \]

    Repeating one record \(k\) times multiplies its contribution. Duplicates therefore change the intended distribution, gradient frequency, memorization risk, and train/test independence. They are not merely wasted storage.

    Deduplicating language-model data found reduced verbatim emission and more reliable validation in its study. Kandpal et al. connect duplication to extraction risk. Deduplication mitigates one risk factor; unique rare records can still be memorized.

    Split before or after augmentation?¤

    Group original entities/sources first, then derive augmentations within their assigned split. Randomly splitting crops, frames, translations, or paraphrases lets sibling derivatives cross the boundary. For time series, split chronologically before windows; overlapping windows can share almost all content.

    Benchmark contamination audit¤

    Maintain a registry of test assets, normalized variants, canaries, publication dates, and likely web derivatives. Audit:

    1. exact/n-gram overlap;
    2. paraphrased questions and answer rationales;
    3. source documents from which tests were derived;
    4. code repositories and unit tests;
    5. benchmark discussion pages and model-generated solutions;
    6. post-training/RL and retrieval corpora, not only pretraining.

    Contamination detection has false positives and false negatives. Report thresholds, examples, coverage, and an uncontaminated/private or newly collected confirmation set.

    Data leakage through preprocessing¤

    Fitting a tokenizer, normalizer, feature selector, codec, imputer, or dedup index on all data can leak evaluation distribution. Unsupervised does not mean harmless. Fit learned preprocessing on training data unless the protocol explicitly studies transductive access.

    Worked video split¤

    One 10-minute video is cut into overlapping 5-second clips every second, then randomly split. Adjacent train/test clips share 80% of frames. A generator can retrieve background/actors and appear to generalize. Assign the entire source video—or higher-level event/person—to one split before clipping.

    Contamination-aware reporting¤

    Report clean and flagged subsets, not only one corrected score. State whether contaminated examples were removed before any model choice. If discovered after tuning, a new confirmatory set is preferable to retroactively redefining the same test.

    Retrieval question: why can semantic deduplication harm data?

    A threshold can collapse legitimate rare variants, minority dialects, or distinct scientific cases. Audit deletion rates by subgroup/source and preserve a reversible mapping rather than assuming every nearby embedding is redundant.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Overlap changes training weights, privacy, and independence. Chapter 3 formalizes the privacy threats that remain even after careful deduplication.
