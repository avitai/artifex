    Comparing Flows with Other Generative Models¤

    Comparing Flows with Other Generative Models: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes you can identify the modeled state space and the complete sampling/scoring pipeline. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. compare families without transferring guarantees from one object—density, objective, sampler, or codec—to another;
    2. design a likelihood evaluation with explicit base measure and preprocessing;
    3. compare sequential depth, NFE, FLOPs, wall-clock, memory, and post-processing separately;
    4. test inversion and density code with analytic or finite-difference oracles; and
    5. write a protocol-bounded deployment recommendation rather than a family slogan.

    The density–latent–energy relationship guide derives a flow's exact normalized energy and explains what becomes difficult when its invertible structure is hidden.

    Flows vs VAEs: Exact Likelihood vs Learned Compression¤

    Table FLOW.10. Flows vs VAEs: Exact Likelihood vs Learned Compression
    Aspect Normalizing Flows VAEs
    Likelihood Exact expression for tractable classical bijections / numerical divergence integral for CNFs when evaluated Usually an ELBO; specialized estimators can tighten or estimate likelihood
    Dimensionality Equal for ordinary bijections; augmentation, surjection, injection, and codecs change the construction Latent dimension is a design choice and need not always be smaller
    Sample Quality Earlier image flows trailed perceptual generators; TarFlow strengthens classical autoregressive NFs, while MeanFlow and rectified/flow-matching systems pursue diffusion-level generation through different objectives Basic pixel-likelihood decoders can average uncertainty; perceptual, adversarial, discrete, and hierarchical variants can be sharp
    Training Maximum likelihood (classical) or vector-field regression (flow matching) ELBO (reconstruction + KL)
    Mode Coverage Must be measured; forward-KL pressure and invertibility do not guarantee semantic coverage in finite models Must be measured; posterior collapse and decoder misspecification are distinct issues
    Generation Speed Coupling inverse, autoregressive inverse, and ODE/flow-map models have different sequential costs Decoder cost depends on architecture and any autoregressive or iterative likelihood
    Role in modern stacks The generator in latent-space pipelines (SD3, FLUX.1) The codec feeding flow / diffusion priors

    When to Choose:

    • Use classical flows when a tractable normalized density is essential and its base measure, preprocessing, support, and downstream semantics have been validated; numerical inversion alone is not a lossless codec
    • Use VAEs when compressed latent representations provide value for downstream tasks, interpretability matters, or computational constraints favor smaller latent spaces
    • Hybrid f-VAEs: Combine both—VAEs with flow-based posteriors or decoders

    Flows vs GANs: Mode Coverage vs Sample Quality¤

    Table FLOW.11. Flows vs GANs: Mode Coverage vs Sample Quality
    Aspect Normalizing Flows GANs
    Sample Quality Architecture- and protocol-dependent: TarFlow improves classical NF image generation; MeanFlow and rectified flow provide separate transport-based routes Strong in many image settings; adversarial training also appears inside diffusion distillation
    Training Stability No minimax game, but log-scale saturation, stiffness, estimator variance, and numerical error can destabilize training Minimax dynamics add failure modes; regularizers and optimizer choices are protocol-dependent
    Likelihood Exact (classical) / estimated by ODE divergence integral (FM / CNF) None (implicit)
    Mode Coverage Not guaranteed by normalized density or invertibility Mode collapse is a known risk, not an inevitable outcome
    Standard evaluation Negative log-likelihood, BPD FID, Inception Score, KID
    Consistency Reconstructive (classical flows) None — no encoder

    When to Choose:

    • Use classical flows when a tractable normalized model density and invertible representation are central, after validating support, finite-precision inverse error, mode coverage, and anomaly-score behavior. Maximum likelihood and invertibility do not guarantee stable optimisation, semantic mode coverage, or reliable OOD detection.
    • Use GANs when an adversarially trained implicit generator fits the task and measured quality/latency trade-off; one-step flow maps and VAEs show that a GAN is not the only one-pass route.
    • Hybrid: some fast diffusion/flow students use adversarial or distribution-matching losses. A hybrid system should name its teacher, student, objective, and sampler rather than being classified by only one component.

    Flows vs Diffusion: Speed vs Quality with Converging Trajectories¤

    Historical Trade-off:

    • Diffusion: historically strong perceptual results with iterative samplers ranging from very few to many evaluations
    • Classical flows: direct invertible sampling, with coupling and autoregressive architectures having different sequential depth; early image systems often trailed perceptual generators under their protocols

    Additional objectives modify the trade-off:

    1. Rectified flows (Liu et al., 2023) — straight paths enabling few-step generation; powers SD3 and FLUX.1.
    2. Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023) — simulation-free vector-field regression for chosen paths, with precise correspondences to some diffusion probability paths.
    3. TarFlow (Zhai et al., ICML 2025) — a Transformer-based masked autoregressive normalizing flow with strong reported likelihood and sample-quality results. Its inverse generates patches sequentially; it is neither flow matching nor a one-NFE parallel sampler.
    4. Mean Flows (Geng et al., NeurIPS 2025 Oral) — FID 3.43 at 1 NFE on ImageNet 256² with no distillation, no curriculum.
    5. CFG-Zero* (Fan et al., 2025) — flow-specific guidance closing the conditional-generation gap with diffusion.
    Table FLOW.12. Flows vs Diffusion: Speed vs Quality with Converging Trajectories
    Aspect Classical Flows (NICE/RealNVP/Glow) Modern Flow Matching Diffusion Models
    Sampling Speed Architecture-dependent: coupling flows can invert in one parallel pass; MAF/TarFlow-style inverses are sequential Usually multiple ODE evaluations; MeanFlow targets one-step generation Sampler-dependent: one/few-step trained or distilled models through long iterative samplers
    Sample Quality Protocol-dependent; TarFlow revived strong image results for autoregressive NFs Competitive under several image/video protocols; no family-wide order Protocol-dependent, with a mature ecosystem of samplers and conditioning methods
    Likelihood Exact via change of variables Estimated by ODE divergence integral ELBO / probability-flow ODE estimates
    Training Stability Single-objective MLE but architecture/numerics can fail Regression avoids minimax dynamics but target variance and stiffness matter Regression objectives are often robust, but weighting, precision, and systems choices matter
    Jacobian computation Required (structured \(O(D)\)) Not required (regression objective) Not required
    Architectural constraints Invertibility plus tractable map/determinant and ordinarily equal dimensions Vector field must match state shape, conditioning, regularity, and solver interface; backbone need not itself be invertible Score/denoiser must match corruption and sampler parameterization; backbone need not itself be invertible

    When to Choose:

    • Use classical flows when you need a tractable change-of-variables model density, controlled invertibility, or direct latent encoding. For anomaly detection and model comparison, validate that likelihood tracks the target notion rather than background statistics; finite-precision reconstruction is not mathematically lossless coding by itself.
    • Use flow matching when its path design, regression target, and measured solver/few-step trade-off fit the task. SD3, FLUX.1, and MeanFlow are influential but do not define a universal 2026 default.
    • Use diffusion when you need the most mature post-training tooling (DPO, RLHF, ControlNet, IP-Adapter) — though most of these are now being ported to flow matching.
    • Hybrids are common: flow matching trains a CNF vector field, and some chosen Gaussian paths admit score/diffusion target conversions. Generator Matching formalizes broader generator correspondences without making finite implementations identical.

    Common Implementation Pitfalls¤

    • Forgetting Preprocessing

      # WRONG: Direct training on uint8 images
flow.train(images_uint8)

# ONE VALID CONTINUOUS-DENSITY PIPELINE:
# dequantize, then use a declared support transform and its log-Jacobian
images = dequantize(images_uint8)
images = logit_transform(images, alpha=0.05)
flow.train(images)

    • Computing Determinants Directly

      # WRONG: Direct determinant (overflow!)
det = jnp.linalg.det(jacobian)
log_det = jnp.log(det)

# CORRECT: Log-space computation
sign, log_det = jnp.linalg.slogdet(jacobian)
# Or for triangular matrices:
log_det = jnp.sum(jnp.log(jnp.abs(jnp.diagonal(jacobian))))

    • Ignoring Invertibility Checks

      # Monitor reconstruction error
x_reconstructed = flow.inverse(flow.forward(x))
error = jnp.mean(jnp.abs(x - x_reconstructed))

# Choose tolerances from dtype, transformation depth, and value scale.
tolerance = inverse_tolerance(dtype=x.dtype, depth=flow.num_layers)
if error > tolerance:
    warnings.warn(f"Poor invertibility: {error}")

    • Wrong Coupling Architecture

      # Compare conditioner capacity under a fixed transformation count and budget.
shallow = MLP([512, output_dim])
deeper = MLP([512, 512, 512, output_dim])
# Select with held-out likelihood, inverse diagnostics, memory, and latency;
# no fixed depth is sufficient for every distribution.

    Evaluation Metrics¤

    Density-Estimation Metrics (Classical Flows)¤

    For a declared discrete-image convention, bits per dimension is typically \(-\log_2 P_\theta(x)/D\) or an explicitly named upper bound on that quantity. A continuous \(-\log_2 p_\theta(x)/D\) has units tied to coordinates and is not automatically the same metric. Historical tables from Glow, Flow++, and Neural Spline Flows remain valuable only when read with their paper-specific preprocessing and estimator.

    Table FLOW.13. Density-Estimation Metrics (Classical Flows)
    Reported quantity What is exact or estimated? Required qualifiers
    Continuous flow log density Exact analytic expression for a tractable bijection, up to floating-point arithmetic Units/base measure, support transform, dtype, map direction
    Uniform-dequantisation BPD Monte Carlo estimate of an upper bound on discrete NLL Number of noise samples, \(K\) levels, rescaling constants, split
    Variational-dequantisation BPD Importance/variational bound whose tightness depends on \(q_\phi(u\mid x)\) Dequantizer, sample count, bound type, joint or separate training
    CNF log density Numerical ODE integral, often with stochastic trace estimation Solver, tolerances/grid, NFE, probe distribution/count, error check
    Compression rate Code length from a complete coder, not likelihood alone Quantization, entropy coder, bits-back/side information, implementation overhead

    Do not assume a fixed BPD improvement from switching uniform to variational dequantisation. Its value depends on the dataset, flow, dequantizer, optimization, and evaluation estimator.

    Sample Quality (Flow Matching / TarFlow / Mean Flows)¤

    Modern flow-matching DiTs are evaluated on the same metrics as diffusion models:

    • FID (Heusel et al., 2017) — Fréchet Inception Distance.
    • CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024) — CLIP-feature MMD; better correlated with human perception.
    • CLIPScore — alignment for text-conditional generation.
    • Inception Score (IS) — legacy class-conditional metric.

    Reference results from Conditional, Rectified, and Average-Velocity Flow Objectives must retain their sampler context: TarFlow reports diffusion-comparable image quality from a Transformer MAF but samples autoregressively; MeanFlow reports FID 3.43 at one network evaluation on ImageNet 256² in its evaluated class-conditional setting. Cross-paper numbers are not rankings unless data, conditioning, guidance, compute, and metric implementations match.

    Test Quantities Specific to Flows¤

    • Round-trip error — test both map directions over representative and adversarial scales with dtype-aware absolute/relative tolerances; passing this test does not validate the log-determinant.
    • Log-determinant oracle — on small dimensions, compare the analytic result with slogdet(jacfwd(f)(x)) or finite differences. Test signs and batch reductions separately.
    • Per-layer log-determinants and condition estimates — shifts, heavy tails, or non-finite values can precede training failure, but thresholds are model-specific.
    • CNF density consistency — tighten solver tolerances, vary trace probes, and compare forward/reverse integration on a low-dimensional analytic system.
    • Path geometry — report kinetic energy, curvature/discretisation diagnostics, and endpoint quality together. Constant speed alone does not prove a correct transport.

    Production Considerations¤

    Inference Cost¤

    Table FLOW.14. Inference Cost
    Method Sequential model evaluations What must be reported
    Coupling flow (Glow / NSF) Usually one composed inverse pass Layer count, image/latent shape, batch, precision, and hardware
    Autoregressive NF (MAF / TarFlow inverse) One or more masked-model passes per autoregressive unit/group Ordering, number of groups/patches, caching, denoiser/post-processing, and hardware
    Flow matching with an ODE solver Solver-dependent, often tens before acceleration Solver, tolerances or fixed grid, NFE, guidance, and hardware
    Rectified/reflowed or distilled flow Often 1–4 evaluations in the targeted regime Teacher/student setup, reflow/distillation, exact checkpoint, and quality operating point
    MeanFlow One evaluation in the headline setting Training recipe, guidance, architecture, benchmark protocol, and measured wall-clock

    Production acceleration has several distinct levers: solver design and caching, reflow, distillation, and objectives such as MeanFlow. TarFlow belongs to the separate autoregressive-normalizing-flow branch and must be benchmarked by sequential inverse cost rather than labeled “1 NFE.”

    Memory and Quantisation¤

    • Classical flows need the activations required by their ordinary backward pass and whatever statistics compute the structured log-determinant; they do not generally materialize or store a full Jacobian. Mixed precision safety depends on scales, inverses, LU/triangular operations, and accumulation dtype.
    • Post-training integer quantisation can break inverse consistency or bias log-determinants, but it is not categorically impossible. Compare density, round trips, condition numbers, and downstream decisions with a higher-precision reference.
    • Flow-matching DiTs often use diffusion-style mixed precision and quantization, but no format is universally safe. Validate attention, normalization, time conditioning, vector-field error, codec error, and complete trajectories.

    Common Production Issues¤

    • Drift on out-of-distribution inputs — classical flows assign well-defined likelihoods to OOD inputs but those likelihoods can be higher than in-distribution ones (Nalisnick et al., 2019) — a counter-intuitive failure mode that complicates OOD detection from \(\log p_\theta\) alone.
    • Numerical reversibility — long bijector stacks and ODE solves accumulate error. Choose tolerances in the modeled space from dtype, scale, and downstream sensitivity; there is no universal pixel-space threshold.
    • Adaptive ODE solvers can raise NFE on stiff trajectories. Fixed-step Euler or higher-order Runge–Kutta/multistep methods offer predictable budgets, but solver suitability depends on the vector field and time parameterization; a diffusion solver name is not automatically valid for every flow ODE.

    Worked example: compare three "fast flows" without hiding cost¤

    Suppose a coupling flow uses 24 coupling blocks, a TarFlow inverse reveals 256 patches, and a MeanFlow checkpoint uses one DiT evaluation plus one codec decode. Calling all three "one call" is uninformative. A controlled report records:

    Table FLOW.15. Worked example: compare three "fast flows" without hiding cost
    System Sequential neural depth Other required work Density semantics
    Coupling NF 24 composed conditioner evaluations, with parallel work inside each block permutations, support transforms analytic modeled continuous density
    TarFlow autoregressive patch/group schedule times blocks, with possible caching noise augmentation/denoiser if used analytic augmented continuous-model density under the declared pipeline
    MeanFlow one or a few interval-conditioned DiT evaluations guidance branches and codec decode not automatically evaluated; requires a separate divergence/path calculation if meaningful

    Benchmark them on the same batch, hardware, precision, compilation/warmup policy, representation, and quality target. NFE alone cannot compare these architectures.

    Independent practice and assessment map: flow evaluation and deployment¤

    Compare complete probability-and-compute protocols at matched operating points. Do not let likelihood, inversion, NFE, a perceptual metric, or an API-call count stand in for the other acceptance dimensions.

    P1 — Produce a protocol-bounded flow recommendation¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each observation proves the stated conclusion: lower continuous NLL proves better discrete compression; exact invertibility proves good OOD detection; one NFE proves lowest latency; forward-KL training proves semantic coverage; a higher-order solver always improves endpoint quality; and a passed round trip proves a correct density. For every invalid implication, supply a counterexample and the missing measurement.
    2. P1.2 — Matched cross-family comparison. Design a comparison among a coupling flow, autoregressive flow, flow-matching model, diffusion model, VAE-based generator, and GAN or adversarial student. Match dataset, representation/codec, conditioning, parameter and active-parameter counts, training compute, quality operating point, hardware, precision, and evaluation sample count. Separate likelihood/bounds, fidelity, coverage, alignment, calibration, uncertainty, and failure severity.
    3. P1.3 — Likelihood and BPD protocol. For a tiny discrete image, compute and label continuous log density, uniform-dequantization bound, variational-dequantization bound, CNF numerical estimate, and an actual code length where a coder is specified. Record base measure, units, constants, sample/probe count, solver, and uncertainty. Construct two numerically similar BPD values that are not comparable because their conventions differ.
    4. P1.4 — Implementation falsification suite. For a small affine coupling and a low-dimensional CNF, define analytic forward/inverse states, signed log-determinants/divergences, automatic-differentiation or finite-difference oracles, batch reductions, solver-refinement checks, adversarial scales, and dtype comparisons. Show which tests can pass while a different contract still fails.
    5. P1.5 — Complete systems ledger. Compare a 24-block coupling inverse, a 256-patch TarFlow inverse, a 32-step guided ODE sampler, and a one-evaluation MeanFlow with codec. Count serial backbone/conditioner calls, guidance branches, auxiliary calls, transforms, decoder/post-processing, FLOPs, peak memory, memory traffic, compilation, warmup, and measured latency. Vary component costs until the ranking changes.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Write a deployment decision for likelihood-based anomaly detection on scientific sensors or conditional protein/material generation. Declare the query, state space, family, support/codec, objective, scorer/sampler, calibration set, shift types, physical constraints, compute budget, baselines, abstention/failure policy, and evidence maturity. State one result that would reverse the recommendation.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    The useful comparison unit is a complete protocol: data representation, probability object, training objective, sampler/scorer, guidance, post-processing, compute, and metric. Likelihood and inversion are valuable capabilities, not automatic guarantees of OOD detection, semantic coverage, compression, or perceptual quality.

    Retrieval practice: evaluation and production
    1. Why can two papers report "BPD" for nonidentical quantities?
    2. What two independent tests validate an affine coupling implementation?
    3. Why is one NFE not a wall-clock claim?
    4. What evidence is needed before using flow likelihood as an anomaly score?

    Solutions. (1) They may use continuous density versus a discrete-mass bound, different dequantizers/constants, or different estimators. (2) Round-trip inversion and an independent Jacobian/log-determinant oracle. (3) Network size, guidance evaluations, sequential groups, codec, memory traffic, precision, compilation, and hardware remain. (4) A shift-specific labeled evaluation, score calibration, threshold protocol, baselines, uncertainty, and failure analysis; normalized likelihood alone is insufficient.
