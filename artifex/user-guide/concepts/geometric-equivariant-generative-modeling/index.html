    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Representations and Multimodal Generative Systems

    Geometric data is not an ordinary vector with unusual formatting. Sets have no canonical order; molecular coordinates are unchanged in meaning by global translation and rotation; orientations live on Lie groups; meshes and graphs have topology; gauge-dependent local frames should not change predictions. A generator must define probability, noise, dynamics, and evaluation on the correct space.

    This volume develops symmetry and geometry across autoregressive models, diffusion, flows, EBMs, and mixed Markov generators. It also separates mathematical symmetry from physical validity: an equivariant molecule can still be chemically impossible.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–2, 4–6.
    Research: all chapters, including quotient spaces, gauge choices, chirality, topology, and evidence appraisal.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to state invariance/equivariance at the function and distribution levels, define probability and noise on manifolds or quotient spaces, derive symmetry constraints for scores and vector fields, distinguish parity from rotation equivariance, and test geometric validity separately from chemical or physical validity.

    1. Group Actions, Invariance, and Equivariance¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes multivariable calculus and elementary probability; it introduces the group language it uses. By the end, you should be able to state invariance and equivariance for functions and probability laws, derive how a Euclidean score transforms under an orthogonal action, distinguish \(SO(3)\) from \(O(3)\) when chirality matters, and decide what exact architecture and data augmentation each do and do not guarantee.

    Let a group \(G\) act on inputs through \(\rho_X(g)\) and outputs through \(\rho_Y(g)\). A function is equivariant when

    \[ f(\rho_X(g)x)=\rho_Y(g)f(x), \]

    and invariant when the output action is trivial:

    \[ f(\rho_X(g)x)=f(x). \]

    A scalar molecular energy should be invariant to rigid motion. A coordinate score or velocity should rotate with the molecule and be translation invariant in a centered representation.

    Distributional symmetry¤

    For a volume-preserving action such as rotation or permutation, an invariant density satisfies

    \[ p(\rho(g)x)=p(x). \]

    For a general coordinate transformation, density also changes by the Jacobian relative to the chosen base measure. “Invariant density” without a measure is incomplete.

    If \(g\) acts orthogonally and \(p(gx)=p(x)\), then the Euclidean score obeys

    \[ s(gx)=g,s(x). \]

    For non-orthogonal actions, scores transform as covectors with inverse-transpose Jacobians plus any density-Jacobian term.

    Equivariant dynamics¤

    If a vector field satisfies

    \[ v_t(gx)=g,v_t(x) \]

    and the ODE has a unique solution, its flow commutes with the action:

    \[ \Phi_{t\to s}(gx)=g\Phi_{t\to s}(x). \]

    Analogous results for SDEs require equivariant drift and compatible diffusion/noise. An equivariant denoiser with non-invariant base noise or a non-equivariant numerical projection can still break output symmetry.

    Permutations, rotations, and reflections¤

    • \(S_n\) handles reindexing of set elements or graph nodes.
    • \(SO(3)\) contains proper rotations and preserves handedness.
    • \(O(3)\) adds reflections.
    • \(SE(3)\) combines proper rotations and translations.
    • \(E(3)\) includes translations and all orthogonal transformations, including reflections.

    Choosing \(E(3)\) when mirror images have different semantics can erase chirality. Choosing \(SE(3)\) does not automatically make a model chirality-sensitive; features and architecture must retain parity information.

    Worked score transformation¤

    Let \(p(x)\propto\exp(-\|x\|^2/2)\) on \(\mathbb R^3\). Then \(s(x)=-x\). For \(R\in SO(3)\),

    \[ s(Rx)=-Rx=Rs(x). \]

    By contrast, a constant score \(s(x)=e_1\) is not rotation equivariant and would privilege a laboratory axis.

    Data augmentation versus architectural symmetry¤

    Augmentation encourages empirical invariance over sampled transformations. Exact equivariance enforces a functional identity for every represented group element, up to numerical error. Augmentation can cover approximate or unknown symmetries; architecture can improve efficiency but may be restrictive or expensive. They are complementary, not universally ordered.

    Retrieval practice: symmetry contracts
    1. What additional object must be named before calling a density invariant under a non-volume-preserving transformation?
    2. Why does an invariant density under an orthogonal action have an equivariant score?
    3. Give one failure caused by choosing \(O(3)\) when the task is only \(SE(3)\)-symmetric.
    4. Why can augmentation and an equivariant architecture not be treated as interchangeable guarantees?

    Solutions. (1) The base measure and its Jacobian transformation. (2) Differentiating the invariant log-density applies the action to its gradient. (3) Reflections can identify or average chiral states that have different semantics. (4) Augmentation constrains sampled training transformations empirically, whereas architecture enforces a represented functional identity; neither fixes an incompatible base law, sampler, or numerical projection.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Symmetry is a relationship among group actions on inputs, outputs, probability measures, and dynamics. The exact group matters, especially for reflections and chirality. Chapter 2 applies these definitions to sets, graphs, point clouds, meshes, and coordinate networks.

    2. Sets, Graphs, Point Clouds, and Equivariant Architectures¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter uses Chapter 1's action and distributional-symmetry definitions. By the end, you should be able to specify permutation-equivariant set and graph updates, explain why an equivariant network does not by itself define an invariant graph law, construct a translation-removing center-of-mass projection, and identify the ordering, topology, and discretization assumptions introduced by point-cloud and mesh architectures.

    Permutation-equivariant sets¤

    A set generator should not depend on an arbitrary storage order. For fixed cardinality, a joint density satisfies

    \[ p(x_{pi(1)},\ldots,x_{pi(n)})=p(x_1,\ldots,x_n). \]

    Elementwise maps with symmetric aggregation give permutation-equivariant updates. Autoregressive set generation introduces an order as a latent construction choice; order randomization or order marginalization is needed if the terminal object is unordered.

    Variable cardinality requires a count distribution, stopping rule, birth/death process, or insertion model. Padding tokens are an implementation representation, not actual elements.

    Graphs¤

    Graphs combine permutation symmetry with discrete topology. A graph generator may model adjacency, node/edge types, and coordinates. Relabeling nodes must relabel every associated tensor consistently. Canonicalization can reduce duplicate representations but may be discontinuous or costly.

    Message passing is permutation equivariant but can be unable to distinguish some non-isomorphic graphs. Higher-order, subgraph, spectral, or positional features increase expressivity while creating their own symmetry and scaling questions.

    Random Graphs and Relational Generators develops the complementary probability contract: labeled versus unlabeled mass, automorphism multiplicity, exchangeability, sparse graph laws, likelihood, and graph-specific evaluation. An equivariant architecture can parameterize that contract, but it does not define the graph law by itself.

    Point clouds and Euclidean equivariance¤

    E(n)-Equivariant GNNs, ICML 2021, update features using invariant pairwise distances and coordinates using relative vectors. A schematic layer is

    \[ m_{ij}=\phi_e(h_i,h_j,\|x_i-x_j\|^2,e_{ij}), \]
    \[ x_i'=x_i+\sum_j(x_i-x_j)\phi_x(m_{ij}). \]

    Relative vectors rotate/refelect equivariantly; distance-derived scalars are invariant. Centering or relative coordinates handle translations.

    Tensor-field and steerable networks carry higher-order irreducible representations rather than only scalars and vectors. They can express angular information more richly but require Clebsch–Gordan/tensor-product operations and careful parity conventions.

    Meshes and surfaces¤

    Meshes have vertices, connectivity, and sometimes an embedding. Remeshing changes discretization without changing the underlying surface. A method can be permutation equivariant yet sensitive to triangulation quality. Intrinsic operators avoid some embedding dependence; extrinsic curvature and orientation require additional structure.

    Worked center-of-mass projection¤

    For coordinates \(x_i\in\mathbb R^3\), define

    \[ \bar x=\frac1n\sum_i x_i, \qquad \tilde x_i=x_i-\bar x. \]

    Translation \(x_i\mapsto x_i+a\) leaves \(\tilde x_i\) unchanged. A Gaussian prior sampled independently and then centered lives on the \((3n-3)\)-dimensional zero-center-of-mass subspace; treating it as a full \(3n\)-dimensional density gives the wrong base measure.

    Retrieval practice: structured architectures
    1. What extra modeling choice is required when an unordered set has variable cardinality?
    2. Why is message passing equivariance insufficient to specify a graph generator?
    3. Which linear projection removes the center-of-mass translation mode for equal-mass points?
    4. Name one way an autoregressive construction can recover an order-invariant terminal law.

    Solutions. (1) A count law, stopping rule, or equivalent birth/death construction. (2) The sample space, node-count law, probability target, ordering convention, and sampler remain unspecified. (3) Subtract the coordinate mean, equivalently apply \(I-\frac1n\mathbf1\mathbf1^\top\) along the point axis. (4) Marginalize or randomize construction orders, or prove that a canonical order preserves the intended law.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Permutation symmetry, Euclidean symmetry, topology, and cardinality are distinct. An architecture must transform every coupled feature consistently, while the probabilistic model must avoid counting arbitrary labels as different objects. Chapter 3 moves from embedded structures to intrinsic manifolds, Lie groups, and gauges.

    3. Manifolds, Lie Groups, Quotients, and Gauge Choices¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the symmetry contracts from Chapters 1–2 and basic differential geometry terminology. By the end, you should be able to distinguish ambient from intrinsic probability, place noise and velocities in tangent spaces, state the local limitations of exponential and logarithm maps, explain cover and quotient multiplicities such as \(q\sim -q\) for rotations, and identify when gauge choices or parallel transport enter a generative algorithm.

    Intrinsic probability¤

    On a Riemannian manifold \((\mathcal M,g)\), densities are defined relative to Riemannian volume. The score is

    \[ s(x)=\operatorname{grad}_{\mathcal M}\log p(x) \in T_x\mathcal M. \]

    Velocities lie in point-dependent tangent spaces. Brownian motion uses the Laplace–Beltrami operator; replacing it with ambient Gaussian noise followed by projection generally defines a different process.

    Riemannian Score-Based Generative Modelling extends time reversal and score matching to manifold-valued diffusion. Riemannian Flow Matching uses geodesic conditional paths and tangent vector fields.

    Exponential and logarithm maps¤

    The exponential map sends a tangent vector to the manifold:

    \[ \operatorname{Exp}_x:T_x\mathcal M\to\mathcal M. \]

    The logarithm is locally inverse but may be multivalued at the cut locus. On a sphere, antipodal points have many shortest geodesics. A training target using \(\operatorname{Log}_x(y)\) must specify branch selection and handle singular regions.

    Lie groups¤

    Rotations live on \(SO(3)\); rigid poses on \(SE(3)\). Group multiplication, inverse, Lie algebra coordinates, and Haar/Riemannian measures matter. Quaternions double-cover \(SO(3)\): \(q\) and \(-q\) represent the same rotation, so a Euclidean density on quaternion coordinates can double-count or introduce seams.

    \(SE(3)\) is noncompact, combining compact rotations and unbounded translations. Rotation and translation scales require a metric or product construction; there is no neutral choice that makes units irrelevant.

    Quotient spaces¤

    If configurations related by \(G\) represent the same object, the true state may be a quotient \(\mathcal X/G\). Centering removes translations but rotations remain. Alignment selects representatives from orbits; discontinuities occur when the representative is ambiguous. Modeling the quotient intrinsically, using invariant coordinates, or retaining an equivariant lifted space are different designs.

    Gauge equivariance¤

    On a curved domain, local frames can be chosen arbitrarily. A gauge transformation changes the frame, not the physical field. Gauge-equivariant networks ensure components transform consistently under local frame changes. Global rotation equivariance alone does not guarantee local gauge independence.

    Parallel transport¤

    Vectors in \(T_x\mathcal M\) and \(T_y\mathcal M\) cannot be averaged directly without identifying tangent spaces. Parallel transport depends on a path and connection. This becomes essential for manifold MeanFlow, momentum, covariance, and long-step maps.

    Retrieval practice: intrinsic geometry
    1. Why is ambient Gaussian noise followed by projection generally not Brownian motion on a manifold?
    2. What fails at the cut locus when a method uses \(\operatorname{Log}_x(y)\) as a unique target?
    3. Why can a Euclidean density over unit quaternions double-count rotations?
    4. When must vectors be parallel transported before they are compared or averaged?

    Solutions. (1) Projection changes the drift, covariance, and reference measure rather than directly realizing the Laplace–Beltrami diffusion. (2) The logarithm can be multivalued or singular, so a branch and exceptional-set policy are required. (3) \(q\) and \(-q\) encode the same element of \(SO(3)\). (4) When they live in tangent spaces at different base points and no common trivialization has been justified.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Manifold models require intrinsic measures, tangent dynamics, chart-aware numerics, and explicit choices for logs, metrics, covers, and gauges. Chapter 4 shows how the major generator families implement these requirements.

    4. Diffusion, Flows, Autoregression, and Energies on Structured Spaces¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–3 plus the basic mechanics of diffusion, flows, autoregression, and energy-based models. By the end, you should be able to audit the symmetry of each family's base law and transition mechanism, distinguish tangent or centered noise from ambient noise, state where likelihood Jacobians must be computed, explain how autoregressive order interacts with symmetry, and test whether an invariant energy is paired with a compatible sampler.

    Equivariant diffusion¤

    Equivariant Diffusion for Molecule Generation, ICML 2022, combines an E(3)-equivariant denoiser with centered coordinate diffusion and categorical atom features. For an invariant noisy density and isotropic compatible corruption, the score is equivariant.

    Noise design is part of the theorem. Independent ambient noise can violate constraints; centered noise removes translation; tangent noise stays on a manifold; categorical corruption handles atom or edge types.

    Equivariant flows¤

    An equivariant invertible map pushes an invariant base distribution to an invariant target. E(n)-Equivariant Normalizing Flows integrate equivariant dynamics. Exact likelihood requires the Jacobian/divergence on the actual constrained space, not an ambient determinant with zero translation modes.

    Manifold flow matching learns tangent velocities along chosen paths. Numerical retractions may replace exact exponential maps, introducing geometric integration error.

    Autoregressive geometry¤

    AR models can generate atoms, residues, graph edits, torsion angles, or local frames sequentially. The order provides tractable conditionals but may break permutation symmetry unless it is canonical, randomized, or marginalized. Internal coordinates encode some invariances but introduce tree/order dependence and singularities.

    A local-frame protein model must transform predictions when the frame changes. Generating invariant distances alone may not determine a realizable or chiral 3D structure.

    Geometric EBMs¤

    An invariant energy \(E(gx)=E(x)\) induces an invariant density relative to an invariant measure. Its gradient is equivariant in Euclidean orthogonal settings; on manifolds use the Riemannian gradient. Products of invariant energies remain invariant, but MCMC proposals and constraints must also respect geometry.

    Mixed topology and coordinates¤

    A molecule or mesh can change topology through discrete jumps while coordinates evolve through equivariant drift/diffusion. The joint Markov generator must couple both. Coordinate-only continuous flows cannot create a different number of atoms or change discrete connectivity without augmentation or events.

    Worked invariant energy¤

    For particles,

    \[ E(x)=\sum_{i<j}\phi(\|x_i-x_j\|) \]

    is permutation invariant for identical particles and \(E(3)\) invariant. Forces \(-\nabla_{x_i}E\) are equivariant and sum to zero, giving translation-related momentum conservation for this closed pair-potential form. A generic equivariant neural force need not be conservative unless it is the gradient of an energy.

    Retrieval practice: geometric model families
    1. What three ingredients, beyond an equivariant denoiser, are needed for an invariant diffusion output law?
    2. On which space must a flow's Jacobian or divergence be evaluated for exact likelihood?
    3. Why can an autoregressive geometric model break permutation symmetry?
    4. Does an invariant energy guarantee invariant samples? Explain.

    Solutions. (1) A symmetry-compatible base/noise law, transition or solver, and any projection or conditioning operation. (2) The actual constrained manifold or quotient with its declared measure, not an ambient space containing null modes. (3) Its construction order can assign different probabilities to equivalent orderings unless randomized, canonicalized with justification, or marginalized. (4) No; initialization, proposal kernels, discretization, and constraint handling must also commute with the action.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Every family can be geometric, but the symmetry must extend through base distributions, noise, objectives, samplers, and likelihood measures. Chapter 5 studies semantic and physical constraints that group equivariance alone does not solve.

    5. Chirality, Topology, and Physical Constraints¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the group and manifold distinctions developed earlier. By the end, you should be able to separate rotational symmetry from reflection symmetry, identify chirality and topology information lost by an inadequate representation, distinguish architectural equivariance from physical validity, formulate hard and soft constraint mechanisms, and diagnose conflicts among symmetry, realizability, conservation, and numerical projection.

    Chirality and parity¤

    Reflections turn a chiral object into its enantiomer. If the task distinguishes enantiomers, full \(E(3)\) invariance is too strong. Pairwise distances cannot distinguish mirror images. Oriented volumes such as

    \[ ((x_j-x_i)\times(x_k-x_i))\cdot(x_l-x_i) \]

    change sign under reflection and encode handedness. Parity-aware scalar/pseudoscalar and vector/pseudovector representations can retain this information.

    Evaluation must specify whether alignment permits only \(SO(3)\) rotations or also reflections. Reflection-permitting RMSD can report a perfect match for the wrong enantiomer.

    Topology and realizability¤

    Valid pairwise distances must form a positive-semidefinite Gram structure with the correct embedding dimension. Independently generated distances can violate triangle inequalities or fail to embed. Meshes need consistent incidence and orientation; graphs may need connectivity, valence, genus, or planarity constraints.

    An ODE flow on a fixed smooth manifold is a diffeomorphism under standard uniqueness assumptions and cannot by itself perform discrete topology changes. Jump processes, stratified spaces, variable-cardinality models, or augmented representations are needed.

    Physical laws¤

    Equivariance expresses behavior under coordinate transformations. It does not automatically enforce:

    • energy or momentum conservation;
    • symplectic/Hamiltonian structure;
    • incompressibility or divergence constraints;
    • holonomic bonds and collision avoidance;
    • thermodynamic equilibrium weights;
    • quantum or chemical validity.

    These require conservative parameterizations, constrained integration, projection, energy models, or domain-specific checks.

    Periodic and gauge systems¤

    Periodic boundary conditions identify coordinates modulo a lattice. Minimum-image distances can be discontinuous at cell boundaries; fractional coordinates change under cell transformations. Crystals additionally require space-group symmetry, lattice validity, composition, and periodic neighbor construction.

    Gauge fields live on connections/edges and transform locally. A gauge-invariant observable such as a loop product differs from a gauge-equivariant field component. Confusing global rotation with local gauge symmetry yields incorrect architectures.

    Proteins and molecules¤

    Protein generation combines sequence, backbone frames, torsions, side chains, bonds, and global geometry. Metrics should include designability, clashes, bond geometry, secondary structure, novelty, sequence recovery, and external structure prediction—with leakage and circularity audits.

    Molecular validity needs valence, connectedness, stereochemistry, conformer energy, and sometimes equilibrium reweighting. A force-field score can be biased outside its training domain; low predicted energy is not experimental stability.

    Worked constraint conflict¤

    Projecting every generated point independently onto a sphere preserves the radius constraint but can break an equivariant joint flow and distort density by a many-to-one map. An intrinsic tangent flow preserves the manifold continuously; a projection method must account for its induced measure and numerical bias.

    Retrieval practice: physical and topological validity
    1. Why do pairwise distances fail to distinguish some chiral structures?
    2. Give one reason a geometrically equivariant molecular sample can still be invalid.
    3. What distributional change can rejection or projection introduce even when every retained sample is feasible?
    4. Why should topology-changing moves be reported separately from coordinate updates?

    Solutions. (1) Reflection preserves pairwise distances while reversing handedness. (2) Valence, steric, energetic, conservation, or domain-specific constraints may be violated. (3) Rejection reweights by acceptance probability, while projection pushes mass through a generally many-to-one map. (4) They change different parts of the state space and require distinct proposal, reversibility, and likelihood arguments.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Symmetry narrows the hypothesis class but does not certify topology, chirality, chemistry, or physics. Valid generation combines group-aware probability with domain constraints and external validation. Chapter 6 defines the corresponding evidence and evaluation contract.

    6. Evaluation and Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the preceding geometric chapters and basic experimental design. By the end, you should be able to construct a layered evaluation that separates symmetry, geometry, physical validity, distributional fidelity, and utility; design transformation and counterexample tests; report numerical and constraint violations with uncertainty; and classify geometric-generation claims by evidence maturity without promoting architectural labels into physical guarantees.

    Evaluation layers¤

    1. Symmetry residual: compare \(f(gx)\) with \(g f(x)\) across group samples and numerical solvers.
    2. Distributional invariance: transform generated samples and test law/metric stability.
    3. Geometry: constraint violation, tangent drift, geodesic error, chart/cut-locus failures, and remeshing sensitivity.
    4. Topology/cardinality: validity, connectivity, embedding, incidence, and edit statistics.
    5. Domain validity: stereochemistry, energy, clashes, conservation, designability, or task-specific constraints.
    6. Generative quality: likelihood with the correct volume measure, coverage, novelty, conditional accuracy, and memorization.
    7. Compute: irreducible-representation cost, neighbor construction, solver NFE, projection/retraction cost, memory, and hardware.

    Report whether evaluation aligns translations, rotations, permutations, and reflections. Alignment can remove genuine errors or hide symmetry violations.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • GP-EquiFlow, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for SE(3)-equivariant flow matching with vector-valued GP priors on trajectory prediction; not a universal prior result.
    • Learning Manifold Data with Flow Matching, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research theory tying a specified flow-matching transformer's complexity to intrinsic structure.
    • Geometry-Aware Image Flow Matching, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for hyperspherical image/latent assumptions under reported protocols; not proof all image distributions lie on one sphere.
    • LieFlow, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for discovering symmetry distributions on Lie groups; learned support remains data/model dependent.
    • Riemannian MeanFlow, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for average velocities via parallel transport and log-map tangent representations on tested manifolds.
    • Elign, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for aligning equivariant molecular diffusion with learned force fields; force-field fidelity bounds the physical claim.

    Submitted, withdrawn, and workshop-only geometry papers are not promoted to this tier.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Prove score equivariance for an orthogonally invariant density.
    2. Construct two enantiomers with identical pairwise distances and opposite oriented volume.
    3. Compare intrinsic spherical Brownian motion with ambient noise plus normalization.
    4. Measure composition and equivariance residuals of a numerical flow solver.
    5. Build a hybrid bond-jump/coordinate-flow generator and identify splitting errors.
    6. Reproduce one 2026 claim with symmetry, validity, compute, and independent-reproduction reporting.
    Exercise 1 solution

    From \(\log p(Rx)=\log p(x)\), differentiate with respect to \(x\): \(R^T s(Rx)=s(x)\). Multiplying by \(R\) and using \(RR^T=I\) gives \(s(Rx)=Rs(x)\).

    Cumulative retrieval: geometric generation
    1. In what order should symmetry, geometric, physical, distributional, and task-level checks be interpreted?
    2. What counterexample distinguishes equivariance from physical validity?
    3. Which implementation components must be transformed together in a symmetry test?
    4. What evidence is needed before claiming that a geometric model improves scientific utility rather than only sample appearance?

    Solutions. (1) Treat them as separate layers; passing an earlier layer does not imply a later one. (2) An exactly equivariant molecule with impossible valence or severe steric clashes. (3) Inputs, conditioning variables, local frames, outputs, noise, and any solver or projection state. (4) A task-specific protocol with appropriate baselines, controlled system boundaries, uncertainty, validity checks, and independent or peer-reviewed evidence proportional to the claim.

    Final synthesis¤

    Geometric generation is probability modeling on structured spaces, not merely adding an equivariant layer. Group actions, measures, bases, tangent spaces, topology, parity, noise, and solvers must agree. Physical and scientific validity then require constraints beyond symmetry.
