    Evaluating Generative Models¤

    • Research Methods 1 — Questions and Claims

    Generative evaluation asks several questions at once: Does the model assign probability correctly? Are samples faithful and diverse? Does conditioning work? Are uncertainty and rare events calibrated? Do outputs help the intended task? Do people prefer them for the reason the metric claims? No scalar answers all of these.

    This volume develops likelihood, ELBO, bits per dimension, perplexity, FID, KID, CMMD, precision/recall, FVD, MAUVE, calibration, task metrics, human studies, learned judges, statistical uncertainty, protocol control, and the research evidence needed to interpret them.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2 for likelihood and sample metrics.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–5; use the protocol checklist before comparing systems.
    Research: all chapters, including estimator bias, judge validity, metric gaming, and sampler-specific evaluation.

    Reader orientation¤

    Evaluation vector¤

    Report an evaluation vector rather than a single leaderboard number:

    \[ E=(\text{density},\text{fidelity},\text{coverage},\text{conditioning}, \text{calibration},\text{generalization},\text{utility},\text{safety},\text{cost}). \]

    Every coordinate depends on a data distribution, representation, estimator, and finite protocol.

    Five claims that must not be collapsed¤

    An evaluation can support one claim while leaving another untouched:

    Table EVAL.1. Five claims that must not be collapsed
    Claim Object being evaluated Evidence that can address it
    density fit probabilities assigned by \(Q\) to fresh draws from \(P\) held-out proper score or a justified likelihood bound
    sample fidelity validity or quality of individual draws under a declared observer or rule blinded ratings, domain tests, or validated features
    coverage which regions and rare events of \(P\) receive mass under \(Q\) recall, stratified tests, or support-sensitive discrepancies
    non-copying how \(Q\) depends on the particular training sample train/holdout/generated three-sample audits and extraction tests
    task utility decisions made with generated data or simulated rollouts a fixed downstream learner, planner, endpoint, and deployment distribution

    The object changes from one row to the next. A likelihood result cannot become a human-preference result by interpretation, and a sample-distance result cannot become evidence of non-memorization without using the training set.

    1. Likelihood, Bounds, BPD, and Perplexity¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes probability density, latent-variable bounds, and basic coding terminology. By the end, you should be able to distinguish exact test likelihood from ELBO/IWAE estimates, convert likelihood to bits per dimension or perplexity with the correct representation, explain why likelihood is neither typicality nor utility, and reproduce the finite-mixture and tokenizer counterexamples that reverse naive rankings.

    Test log-likelihood¤

    For held-out examples \(x^{(i)}\sim p_{test}\), average negative log-likelihood is

    \[ NLL=-\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\log p_\theta(x^{(i)}). \]

    It estimates cross-entropy from the test distribution to the model. The irreducible entropy of the data is unknown, so NLL differences—not absolute “percent correct” interpretations—are usually meaningful. Report units (nats/bits), reduction (per example/token/dimension), preprocessing, and confidence intervals over independent examples.

    Exact likelihood applies only when the model and dequantization/observation semantics define it. A probability-flow ODE likelihood is a numerical estimate depending on solver tolerance and divergence estimation; an EBM likelihood needs a partition-function estimate; an implicit generator may have no tractable density.

    The canonical treatment of importance sampling, AIS, SMC, bridge and path methods, nested sampling, log bias, and shared-normalizer uncertainty is Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants. This chapter uses those estimators as inputs to an evaluation claim rather than duplicating their algorithms.

    ELBO is a bound¤

    For a latent model,

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\mathcal L(x) +D_{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)), \]

    so

    \[ \mathcal L(x)= \mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] -D_{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) \le\log p_\theta(x). \]

    Comparing ELBOs confounds model likelihood with inference gap. IWAE/importance estimates can tighten the bound, but estimator variance, sample count, and proposal quality must be reported. A diffusion variational bound similarly need not track perceptual sample quality.

    Bits per dimension¤

    For a \(D\)-dimensional discretized example with NLL in nats,

    \[ BPD=\frac{NLL}{D\log 2}. \]

    This is an average code-length-like quantity under the stated discretization/dequantization. Image BPD is incomparable across bit depth, color space, crop, dimension definition, or dequantization convention. Continuous densities can exceed one, so differential log-density is coordinate dependent.

    Perplexity¤

    With \(M\) evaluated tokens and total token NLL,

    \[ PPL=\exp\left(-\frac1M\sum_{t=1}^M\log p(x_t\mid x_{<t})\right). \]

    Perplexity is the exponential of per-token cross-entropy. It changes with tokenizer and vocabulary: splitting one word into several easier tokens changes both numerator allocation and denominator. Compare perplexity only with the same tokenization, normalization, context handling, and test text. Bits per byte/character improves cross-tokenizer comparability but still depends on encoding and corpus.

    Likelihood is not typicality or utility¤

    High-dimensional models can assign higher likelihood to some out-of-distribution inputs while typical in-distribution samples have lower pointwise density. Typicality considers where probability mass concentrates, not only the mode. Typicality testing and likelihood regret address specific OOD settings; neither makes likelihood a universal semantic metric.

    Forward cross-entropy penalizes missing data modes strongly but can reward modeling imperceptible low-level statistics. It says nothing direct about conditional instruction following, safety, or sampling cost.

    This limitation should not be misstated as “likelihood is meaningless.” For a discrete target \(P\) and any model \(Q\) with adequate support,

    \[ \mathbb E_{X\sim P}[-\log Q(X)] =H(P)+D_{KL}(P\|Q). \]

    Consequently the population log score is minimized by \(Q=P\). It is a proper answer to a density-estimation question. The mistake is to substitute that answer for an application-specific notion of sample quality. This distinction is formalized more generally by proper-scoring-rule theory and by the application-relative analysis in Theis, van den Oord, and Bethge.

    Worked finite-mixture reversal¤

    Consider four observable mixture components. A panel has specified a display score \(u(x)\) from \(0\) to \(5\); this is deliberately a utility, not a probability.

    Table EVAL.2. Worked finite-mixture reversal
    component \(P\) \(Q_D\) \(Q_S\) \(u\)
    ordinary A \(0.48\) \(0.48\) \(0.09\) \(2\)
    ordinary B \(0.48\) \(0.48\) \(0.09\) \(2\)
    showcase \(0.02\) \(0.02\) \(0.80\) \(5\)
    artifact \(0.02\) \(0.02\) \(0.02\) \(0\)

    Because \(Q_D=P\), its population NLL is \(H(P)=0.861\) nats. Direct substitution gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{NLL}_P(Q_S) &=-\sum_x P(x)\log Q_S(x)\\ &=2.394\ \text{nats}. \end{aligned} \]

    The log score correctly prefers \(Q_D\). Samples from \(Q_S\), however, have the higher declared display utility:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E_{Q_D}[u(X)]&=2.02,\\ \mathbb E_{Q_S}[u(X)]&=4.36. \end{aligned} \]

    There is no contradiction. \(Q_D\) is the better model of the population, while \(Q_S\) is better for this panel's presentation objective and badly undercovers the two ordinary components. Changing the panel, the utility, or a best-of-\(N\) selection rule changes the second question without changing the first. Report both criteria when both claims matter.

    Worked tokenizer counterexample¤

    Suppose a corpus contains only ab, with probability one. Tokenizer A uses one token ab; a perfect model has PPL \(1\). Tokenizer B uses a,b; the perfect conditionals also give PPL \(1\). Now assign sequence probability \(0.25\): A has PPL \(4\), while B distributes NLL over two tokens and has PPL \(2\). Same sequence likelihood, different perplexity.

    Retrieval question: when can two BPD numbers be compared?

    Only when data, dimensionality, quantization/dequantization, preprocessing, log base, and likelihood estimator semantics match. Otherwise the normalization hides a changed task.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Likelihood metrics test probabilistic coding under declared semantics. Chapter 2 develops sample-based distances, whose result instead depends on feature representation and estimator.

    2. Distributional Sample Metrics¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes two-sample comparison and feature embeddings. By the end, you should be able to state the estimands and finite-sample biases of FID, KID, MMD, CMMD, precision, recall, density, and coverage; identify dependence on feature extractor and reference set; use memorization and metric-blindness counterexamples; and choose modality-appropriate distributional diagnostics.

    Fréchet Inception Distance¤

    FID embeds real and generated images with a fixed Inception network, fits Gaussians with means/covariances \((\mu_r,\Sigma_r)\) and \((\mu_g,\Sigma_g)\), and computes

    \[ FID=\|\mu_r-\mu_g\|_2^2 +\operatorname{Tr}\left(\Sigma_r+\Sigma_g -2(\Sigma_r^{1/2}\Sigma_g\Sigma_r^{1/2})^{1/2}\right). \]

    Lower is better. This is the squared 2-Wasserstein distance between the fitted feature Gaussians, not between raw image distributions. It ignores non-Gaussian differences beyond first two feature moments. Estimates are sample-size biased and sensitive to image resizing, compression, feature implementation, precision, and reference statistics.

    Always report sample count, feature extractor/version, preprocessing, reference split/statistics, conditional/unconditional sampling, truncation/guidance, and whether duplicates were removed. Compare at equal counts or use a controlled extrapolation protocol.

    KID and MMD¤

    Kernel Inception Distance is squared MMD in Inception features, commonly with a polynomial kernel. For samples \(x_i\sim P,y_j\sim Q\), an unbiased estimator excludes diagonal terms:

    \[ \widehat{MMD}_u^2= \frac{1}{m(m-1)}\sum_{i\ne j}k(x_i,x_j) +\frac{1}{n(n-1)}\sum_{i\ne j}k(y_i,y_j) -\frac{2}{mn}\sum_{i,j}k(x_i,y_j). \]

    Unbiased does not mean low variance or representation independent. Kernel and bandwidth determine which differences are visible.

    CMMD¤

    CMMD computes MMD in CLIP embeddings and was proposed after demonstrating FID failures under certain distortions. It can better align with text/image semantics in tested settings, but inherits CLIP's blind spots, biases, and implementation choices. It does not replace conditional compositional tests or human studies.

    Precision, recall, density, and coverage¤

    Generative precision approximates the fraction of generated features near the real-data manifold; recall approximates how much of the real manifold generated samples cover. Improved precision/recall uses \(k\)-nearest-neighbor neighborhoods in feature space.

    These metrics expose fidelity–coverage trade-offs hidden by one FID number. They depend on \(k\), feature geometry, sample size, and manifold estimator. A model can memorize training images and score high precision; holdout comparisons and nearest-neighbor/memorization audits remain necessary.

    Worked memorizer: valid classes, copied identities¤

    Let the population be uniform over eight identities, \(\{a_1,\ldots,a_4,b_1,\ldots,b_4\}\). The training set contains \(\{a_1,a_2,b_1,b_2\}\) and an independent test set contains the remaining four. Define a smoothed memorizer with \(\epsilon=0.04\). Its mass on each training identity is \((1-\epsilon)/4=0.24\), and its mass on each held-out identity is \(\epsilon/4=0.01\):

    \[ Q_M(x)= \begin{cases} 0.24, & x\in D_{\mathrm{train}},\\ 0.01, & x\in D_{\mathrm{test}}. \end{cases} \]

    Let \(Q_G\) be uniform over all eight identities. Their exact NLLs are

    Table EVAL.3. Worked memorizer: valid classes, copied identities
    model train NLL held-out NLL
    smoothed memorizer \(Q_M\) \(1.427\) \(4.605\)
    population model \(Q_G\) \(2.079\) \(2.079\)

    Here \(1.427=-\log 0.24\), \(4.605=-\log 0.01\), and \(2.079=\log 8\).

    Training likelihood favors the memorizer; held-out likelihood exposes its failure. Now let an evaluator retain only the class feature \(\phi(a_i)=A,\phi(b_i)=B\). Both generators induce the exact class law \((1/2,1/2)\). A feature metric that sees only \(\phi\) reports perfect class coverage even though almost all \(Q_M\) samples repeat training identities.

    This is why copying is a three-distribution question involving the training sample, an independent sample from the target, and generated samples. The three-sample test of Meehan et al. formalizes one such audit. Later analysis shows that global copying statistics can miss local copying and establishes sample requirements for local tests (Bhattacharjee, Dasgupta, and Chaudhuri).

    The conclusion must still match the application. Excess repetition relative to an independent population sample is evidence of dependence on the training sample, not automatically a privacy breach; if the true population itself has a small finite support, repeats can be correct. Audit exact and semantic neighbors, per-region copying, held-out performance, and privacy attacks separately. Current TMLR evidence finds heterogeneous sample-level memorization in tabular diffusion models, but its detector and mitigation results are not a universal guarantee for other modalities or generators.

    Video, audio, and text¤

    • FVD applies a Fréchet Gaussian comparison to spatiotemporal video embeddings (Unterthiner et al.); clip length, frame rate, crop, encoder, and temporal sampling are part of the metric.
    • Fréchet Audio Distance analogously uses audio embeddings; sample rate, loudness, segment duration, and encoder domain matter.
    • MAUVE summarizes divergence frontiers between generated and human text in quantized embedding space; featurizer, quantization, length, and decoding affect it.

    No generic image metric should be renamed and applied to scientific fields without validating that its representation preserves the field's important errors.

    Worked metric blindness¤

    If a feature extractor maps a red square and blue square to the same vector, any FID/KID/CMMD computed solely on that representation sees no color error. The estimator may be mathematically correct while the representation makes the task invisible. Validate metrics using controlled corruptions relevant to the application.

    Retrieval question: what are the four layers of a sample metric?

    Sampling protocol, preprocessing, feature representation, and statistical comparison/estimator. A score change can arise at any layer.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Distributional metrics compare finite samples through a chosen representation. A separate train/holdout/generated audit is needed to evaluate copying. Chapter 3 adds conditional correctness, calibration, task utility, and safety dimensions that marginal sample quality cannot reveal.

    3. Conditional Fidelity, Calibration, and Task Utility¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional probability and decision evaluation. By the end, you should be able to measure condition adherence separately from marginal quality, include guidance and decoding in the evaluated system, assess calibration and diversity jointly, design task-specific synthetic-data or world- model utility tests, and separate safety performance from generic sample fidelity.

    Conditional evaluation¤

    A conditional generator models \(p(x\mid c)\). Marginal FID can be excellent while the model ignores \(c\). Evaluate:

    1. within-condition fidelity/coverage;
    2. alignment between output and condition;
    3. controllability under counterfactual changes to \(c\);
    4. diversity conditional on the same \(c\);
    5. leakage between reference and condition encoders.

    Text-image similarity detects broad alignment but often misses counting, negation, spatial relations, identity, and attributes. Use compositional prompt suites with object detectors/VQA only after validating those evaluators, and manually audit their disagreement regions.

    Guidance and decoding are part of the system¤

    Classifier-free guidance, temperature, top-\(p\), beam search, rejection sampling, best-of-\(N\), verifiers, and safety filters change the output distribution. A model comparison at independently optimized guidance values is a system comparison, not an isolated training-method comparison. Report curves across guidance/compute as well as a predeclared operating point.

    Guidance Matters, ICLR 2026, is Peer-reviewed research evidence that guidance baselines and scale matching can reverse conclusions in text-to-image guidance comparisons. The general lesson is protocol control, not that one guidance scale is universally optimal.

    The cross-family control volume defines the output laws of guidance, constraints, selection, retrieval, retries, and fallbacks. Evaluation should use that declared law as the system under test and retain raw candidates so selection effects can be localized.

    Calibration¤

    For events \(A\) assigned probability \(q\), calibration requires empirical frequency near \(q\) among comparable predictions. Token calibration, sequence calibration, semantic-event calibration, and interval coverage are different. A next-token model can be calibrated tokenwise while its beam-selected answers are overconfident.

    For continuous forecasts, proper scoring rules such as log score, Brier score for events, CRPS for scalar distributions, and energy score for multivariate distributions reward honest probabilities under assumptions. Reliability diagrams and ECE require bin choices; report adaptive/fixed bins and uncertainty. Temperature scaling is a classification baseline, not a universal fix for structured generation.

    Diversity¤

    Distinguish:

    • aleatoric diversity appropriate for one condition;
    • semantic coverage across conditions/data modes;
    • superficial variation that preserves the same content;
    • invalid or unsafe variation.

    Pairwise feature distance can reward noise. Self-BLEU can penalize legitimate repeated syntax. Conditional entropy is attractive but usually intractable. Use multiple representations and task annotations.

    Task-specific evaluation¤

    Scientific and operational outputs need domain validity:

    • molecules: validity, uniqueness, novelty, synthesizability, property calibration, scaffold split;
    • proteins: structural/functional assays, sequence identity controls, wet-lab validation;
    • weather: proper probabilistic scores, extremes, spatial spectra, conservation, lead-time curves;
    • code: isolated execution, tests, security, nondeterminism, pass@\(k\) protocol;
    • world models: action consistency, rollout calibration, planning regret, constraint violations;
    • causal generation: intervention and counterfactual identification, not realism alone.

    Task metrics may be proxy-optimized. Keep an untouched confirmatory endpoint and report failure cases outside the proxy.

    Synthetic-data utility is query-relative¤

    For train-on-synthetic, test-on-real (TSTR), train a fixed learner \(f_S=\mathcal A(D_S)\) on generated labeled data and evaluate it on an untouched real test set. Compare it with the same learner, tuning budget, and sample budget trained on real data. Also report real-plus-synthetic augmentation, learning curves, subgroup results, calibration, and more than one plausible learner. Train-on-real, test-on-synthetic can diagnose a different direction of shift; it is not a substitute for real held-out evaluation.

    A finite construction shows the scope of the claim. Let real data be \(X=(Y,N)\), where \(Y\) and a nuisance feature \(N\) are independent fair bits, and let the prediction label be \(Y\). A synthetic generator preserves balanced \(Y\) but always emits \(N=0\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} P(y,n)&=\tfrac14,\\ Q(y,n)&=\tfrac12\,\mathbf 1\{n=0\}. \end{aligned} \]

    The classifier \(f(x)=x_1\) trained on synthetic data has \(100\%\) accuracy on real data. Nevertheless,

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{TV}(P,Q) &=\frac12\sum_{y,n}|P(y,n)-Q(y,n)|\\ &=\frac12. \end{aligned} \]

    Perfect TSTR for predicting \(Y\) proves utility for that learner and task, not joint-distribution fidelity. A task that predicts \(N\), a causal query involving \(N\), or a subgroup analysis can fail completely. Recent work on synthetic-data evaluation likewise separates predictive utility from richer distributional and causal targets (Amad et al., NeurIPS 2025).

    World-model utility passes through a planner¤

    World-model evaluation must fix the policy or planner that consumes the model. Suppose a safe action always returns \(1\), whereas risky returns \(2\) with probability \(0.99\) and \(-100\) with probability \(0.01\). The true values are

    \[ \begin{aligned} V(\text{safe})&=1,\\ V(\text{risky})&=0.99(2)+0.01(-100)\\ &=0.98. \end{aligned} \]

    A sample of \(50\) risky transitions contains no catastrophe with probability \(0.99^{50}\approx0.605\). A model fitted to that sample can have zero observed one-step error, predict value \(2\), and make the planner choose the wrong action. The rare transition matters because planning changes which errors are queried. This is a finite instance of the documented mismatch between one-step model objectives and downstream control performance (Lambert et al.).

    Report at least four layers: held-out proper scores stratified by state and action; multi-step calibration and constraint failures; return or regret in the real environment or a separately validated simulator; and sensitivity across planners, horizons, and compute budgets. Stress rare events and action sequences proposed by the planner, not only trajectories from the data-collection policy. The world-model volume develops this model–planner–environment boundary in detail.

    Safety evaluation¤

    Measure unsafe propensity with prompt/source stratification, severity, uncertainty, and false positives from filters. Report both pre-filter model behavior and post-filter system behavior where permitted. A safety filter can improve observed harm metrics while hiding loss of benign coverage.

    Retrieval question: why is CLIP alignment insufficient?

    It evaluates similarity in one learned representation, not exact compositional truth, diversity, calibration, safety, or whether the output causally responds to each part of the condition.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Conditional systems need alignment, diversity, calibration, utility, and safety—not only marginal realism. Synthetic-data and world-model utility remain relative to a learner, planner, task, and deployment distribution. Chapter 4 develops human and learned-judge protocols for properties automated metrics cannot establish.

    4. Human Evaluation and Learned Judges¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes basic experimental design. By the end, you should be able to operationalize a human-evaluation construct, choose sampling and rater units, build a randomized pairwise protocol, compute uncertainty under clustered ratings, represent disagreement rather than erase it, and validate learned judges against population, position, leakage, and reward-model biases.

    Start with the construct¤

    Define what raters judge: realism, prompt faithfulness, aesthetics, usefulness, harmlessness, preference, or domain correctness. “Overall quality” mixes constructs differently across people and contexts. Use separate questions before an overall preference, and specify whether raters can abstain or tie.

    Pairwise protocol¤

    Pairwise comparison often reduces scale calibration burden. Randomize left/right position and system identity, blind metadata, sample prompts and stochastic outputs before judging, and avoid showing only curated examples. Balance pair order and include attention/consistency checks without silently removing inconvenient raters.

    A Bradley–Terry model uses

    \[ p(i\succ j)=\sigma(\beta_i-\beta_j). \]

    Its scalar skills assume a consistent transitive comparison structure. Check cycles, prompt/rater interactions, ties, and model fit. Elo is an online update procedure whose result can depend on ordering and settings; give pairwise counts or a fitted model with uncertainty, not only an opaque rating.

    Sampling unit and uncertainty¤

    If outputs share prompts, raters, or model checkpoints, judgments are clustered. Treating every click as independent understates uncertainty. Use hierarchical/mixed models or bootstrap at the highest sampled unit relevant to generalization (often prompts, then outputs/raters). Report number of unique prompts, outputs per prompt, raters, judgments, exclusions, and confidence/credible intervals.

    Rater population and disagreement¤

    Eligibility, language, culture, accessibility, domain expertise, pay, device, and exposure change judgments. Disagreement may reveal plural preferences rather than noise. Report inter-rater distributions and subgroup results when ethical/sample-size constraints allow. Domain correctness in medicine or science cannot be replaced by generic crowd preference.

    Learned judges and reward models¤

    Model judges reduce cost but inherit training data, positional/style bias, self-family preference, contamination, and vulnerability to adversarial phrasing. Validate against held-out human judgments, report judge version/prompt/decoding, randomize positions, test reference-free versus reference-based settings, and keep a human audit sample.

    Correct Looks Better, ICML 2026, is Peer-reviewed research evidence that pairwise rankings can recover accuracy rankings in its controlled free-form conversions, especially relative to direct weak judging. It does not prove pairwise judges are unbiased on open-ended subjective generation.

    GT-SVJ, CVPR 2026, is Peer-reviewed research for repurposing a video generative transformer as a temporally aware reward model under its benchmark/annotation protocol. Synthetic corruptions define what the judge learns to notice; other physical or semantic failures require separate validation.

    Preference-model bias¤

    Reward models trained from pairwise preferences estimate the annotation process under a sampling policy, not universal human utility. Selection, annotator aggregation, and model outputs determine the learned reward. Optimizing against it creates distribution shift and reward hacking. Maintain fresh human evaluation outside the reward's training distribution.

    Worked clustered-data error¤

    Suppose 1,000 judgments come from 10 prompts with 100 near-identical outputs each. A click-level confidence interval pretends \(n=1000\) independent task draws. For prompt generalization, effective independent support is closer to 10 prompt clusters. Resample prompts, not only clicks.

    Retrieval question: when is a model judge acceptable evidence?

    When its construct validity and agreement are established on held-out, representative human data; its version/protocol is fixed; biases are probed; uncertainty is reported; and conclusions remain bounded to that validation domain.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Human evaluation is an experiment, and learned judging is a measured instrument. Chapter 5 integrates protocol controls, uncertainty, multiple comparisons, and anti-gaming practices.

    5. Controlled Protocols, Statistics, and Metric Gaming¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–4 and elementary statistical inference. By the end, you should be able to predeclare an estimand and comparison boundary, match training and inference budgets, select uncertainty intervals at the correct sampling unit, test metric implementations, detect reference contamination and Goodhart effects, and write a protocol card that makes reruns and negative results interpretable.

    Predeclare the comparison¤

    Before running systems, fix the research question, primary endpoint, data split, prompt sampling, number of stochastic samples, decoding/guidance, compute budget, exclusion rules, and statistical analysis. Exploratory sweeps are valuable, but the final confirmatory comparison needs an untouched evaluation or explicit multiplicity correction.

    Match the system boundary¤

    Compare one of these, and say which:

    • training objectives at matched architecture/data/compute;
    • base models under one sampler/decoder;
    • best achievable quality under a latency budget;
    • complete deployed systems including filters/retrieval/verifiers;
    • Pareto frontiers over quality, diversity, cost, and safety.

    Matching parameter count while one system uses much more training or inference compute is not controlled. Matching “steps” while NFEs differ is not controlled.

    Uncertainty¤

    Use paired tests when systems share prompts/examples. Report effect size and interval, not only \(p\)-value. Bootstrap the correct unit; use multiple seeds for training variability; distinguish sample-generation variance, metric-estimator variance, dataset/prompt variance, and checkpoint variance.

    For \(K\) reported metrics and many model variants, false discovery grows. Predeclare a primary metric, adjust confirmatory tests, or label analyses exploratory. Do not select the best seed/checkpoint on the final test metric.

    Two-sample tests are finite decision procedures¤

    A two-sample test observes \(X_{1:m}\sim P\) and \(Y_{1:n}\sim Q\) and tests

    \[ \begin{aligned} H_0 &: P=Q,\\ H_1 &: P\ne Q. \end{aligned} \]

    Its level \(\alpha\) controls the probability of rejection under \(H_0\); its power at a particular alternative is \(\Pr_{P,Q}(\text{reject }H_0)\). Neither a \(p\)-value nor non-rejection is the posterior probability that \(H_0\) is true. Characteristic-kernel MMD can identify equality at the population level, while the finite test still has kernel-, sample-size-, and alternative-dependent power (Gretton et al.).

    For a concrete power limit, let \(P(B=1)=0.01\) and \(Q(B=1)=0.02\), and draw \(50\) bits from each distribution. The distributions differ, yet the probability that neither sample contains a single \(1\) is

    \[ (0.99)^{50}(0.98)^{50}\approx0.220. \]

    On this event the observed samples are two identical all-zero sequences. No observed between-sample difference reveals the rare-event discrepancy; rejection could then arise only from prespecified randomization rather than evidence in these observations. More samples improve power against this rare-event alternative; a feature map that discards \(B\) makes it invisible at every sample size. Therefore report the alternative or minimum effect size used for power analysis, the test statistic and representation, calibration method, sample sizes, achieved effect and interval, and all repeated tests.

    Table EVAL.4. Two-sample tests are finite decision procedures
    Outcome Defensible interpretation
    reject \(H_0\) the samples provide evidence against equality under the declared test and assumptions
    do not reject \(H_0\) this experiment did not resolve a difference of the tested kind
    small empirical discrepancy the measured effect is small at this representation and sample size
    estimated high power for \(\Delta\) the design would usually detect the prespecified alternative \(\Delta\)

    Nothing in a two-sample statistic makes it unusable during training. Exact permutation calibration or a quadratic-time kernel statistic may be too costly to run after every update, but linear-time approximations, minibatch discrepancies, and checkpoint diagnostics are available. MMD has even served as a differentiable training objective for implicit generators (Li, Swersky, and Zemel, 2015). What changes is the claim: a noisy minibatch discrepancy is a training signal, not automatically a level-\(\alpha\) sequential test. Reusing validation samples, inspecting many checkpoints, or stopping adaptively requires an explicit sequential or multiplicity protocol before inferential error guarantees can be attached.

    Retrieval question: what does failure to reject establish?

    Only that the chosen statistic did not cross its rejection threshold in this finite experiment. It does not establish equality, adequate coverage, or sufficient power for an unspecified alternative.

    Metric implementation tests¤

    Validate a metric with controlled perturbations:

    1. duplicates/memorization;
    2. mode removal;
    3. blur/noise/compression;
    4. label or condition permutation;
    5. semantic relation/count changes;
    6. temporal shuffle/speed change;
    7. unsafe rare events;
    8. sample-count variation.

    A metric that does not respond appropriately cannot support the intended claim even if canonical.

    Gaming and Goodhart's law¤

    Once a metric guides training, checkpoint selection, or publication, models adapt to its blind spots. Reward models are especially direct optimization targets. Separate development and audit metrics, rotate adversarial tests, monitor distribution shift, and preserve human/domain endpoints.

    Reference-set contamination¤

    Memorized or near-duplicate reference examples can improve feature distances and human preference. Deduplicate train/test/reference sets at exact and semantic levels, document thresholds, and run nearest-neighbor disclosure. Generated captions or synthetic references can leak the evaluator model's style.

    Protocol card¤

    Every result should state:

    task dataset/split/modality/resolution/condition
    system checkpoint, parameters/active parameters, filters
    sampling seed policy, temperature/guidance, NFE, best-of-\(N\)

    Retrieval question: what variance sources should not be pooled blindly?

    Training-seed/checkpoint, prompt/data, stochastic decoding, rater, and metric-estimator variance arise at different hierarchy levels and support different generalization claims.

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • Guidance Matters, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for guidance-scale and baseline control in text-to-image evaluation.
    • ImageDoctor, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for multi-aspect grounded diagnosis of plausibility, alignment, aesthetics, and overall quality; learned diagnostic validity remains benchmark-bound.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Hold sequence likelihood fixed while changing tokenization; quantify perplexity changes.
    2. Compute FID and KID versus sample count and extrapolate/plot estimator behavior.
    3. Construct a feature-invisible corruption and demonstrate metric blindness.
    4. Evaluate the same conditional model over a guidance curve; plot fidelity, diversity, alignment, and NFE.
    5. Run a blinded pairwise study with ties, prompt-cluster bootstrap, and a Bradley–Terry fit; inspect cycles.
    6. Compare a learned judge with human ratings on a held-out corruption suite, including adversarial examples.
    7. Build a sampler oracle to separate model/denoiser and finite-sampler error.
    8. Write a complete protocol card for a published benchmark claim and identify missing comparability fields.
    9. Reproduce the eight-identity memorizer and compare identity-aware with class-only features.
    10. Design a two-sample test for the rare-bit example; plot power against sample size and effect size.
    11. Evaluate one synthetic dataset on two prediction tasks whose sufficient features differ.
    12. Couple a learned transition model to two planners and test whether model rankings change.
    Exercise 2 solution sketch

    Draw nested generated subsets at several counts while keeping real reference statistics and preprocessing fixed. Repeat subset draws to estimate sampling variance. FID usually changes systematically with finite count, whereas unbiased KID centers more stably but may have broad variance. Never compare one model at 10k samples with another at 50k as if sample count were irrelevant.

    Cumulative retrieval: generative-model evaluation
    1. Why can likelihood, sample realism, coverage, conditional fidelity, and task utility rank the same systems differently?
    2. What is the experimental unit when each of several raters evaluates many outputs from the same prompts?
    3. How can a memorizing generator obtain a favorable distributional feature score?
    4. What minimum information makes an evaluation comparison reproducible and resistant to metric gaming?

    Solutions. (1) They measure different properties and weight support, representation, conditioning, and decisions differently. (2) Prompts/outputs and raters form crossed or nested clusters; individual ratings are not automatically independent units. (3) Copied training examples can match feature moments and appear realistic while failing novelty or identity-level coverage checks. (4) A declared construct and estimand, system boundary, data/reference provenance, metric implementation, sampling units, uncertainty, budgets, seeds, preprocessing, selection rules, and all reported metrics including failures.

    Final synthesis¤

    Generative evaluation is measurement science. Likelihood tests density semantics; sample metrics test distributions through representations; copying audits compare generated, training, and held-out samples; conditional and calibration metrics test response to information; task endpoints test utility through a learner or planner; human and model judges measure declared constructs; statistical protocols bound uncertainty. Credible results state which object was measured and resist turning one valid answer into a claim about a different object.

    Structured objects require additional declarations. For example, Random Graphs and Relational Generators derives invariant graph-distribution tests, descriptor-MMD failure modes, likelihood conventions, constraint reporting, and the distinction between generating a relational graph and making a causal claim.

    Copula Models gives the corresponding dependence protocol: validate marginal uniformity separately from joint fit, test Rosenblatt residual independence, qualify tail estimates by threshold and uncertainty, and recombine the pieces only for the observed-scale query.

    Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations gives the structured-derivation protocol: separate marginalized yield density, latent-tree recovery, finite termination, syntax, types, execution, behavioral equivalence, constrained-law distortion, and end-to-end compute.

    Tensor-Network and Born Probability Models gives the contraction-model protocol: test normalization and exact queries on finite cases, report topology, order, ranks, gauges, base measure, contraction plan and numeric precision, and separate represented-law error from approximate-contraction and systems error.
