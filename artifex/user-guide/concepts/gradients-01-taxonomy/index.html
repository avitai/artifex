    1. A Taxonomy of Gradient Objects¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Multivariable derivatives, probability densities, and expected objectives.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Type a gradient claim: name its objective, variable, averaging law, geometry, and estimator or solver.
    2. O2 — Separate vector spaces: distinguish parameter, state, game, implicit, functional, and search gradients by domain and codomain.
    3. O3 — Separate estimator from geometry: identify when two estimators target one Euclidean derivative and when a metric transforms that derivative.
    4. O4 — Audit pullbacks and shapes: verify the Jacobian or adjoint needed to transport a state-space field to parameters.
    5. O5 — Detect target changes: recognize relaxation, truncation, detachment, and straight-through rules that alter or replace a declared derivative.
    6. O6 — Transfer the contract: specify all five fields for a new generative-model training or sampling update.

    The five-field gradient contract¤

    Write a gradient claim as

    \[ \mathsf G =\bigl( \text{objective}, \text{differentiation variable}, \text{averaging law}, \text{geometry}, \text{estimator or solver} \bigr). \]

    For example, the pathwise VAE encoder gradient may be described as:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathsf G_{\mathrm{VAE}} =\bigl(&-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\ \phi,\ \varepsilon\sim p(\varepsilon),\\ &\text{Euclidean parameter metric},\ \text{reparameterized Monte Carlo and reverse AD}\bigr). \end{aligned} \]

    Changing any field can change the mathematical object. Using the same neural architecture, automatic-differentiation system, or optimizer name does not restore equality.

    Ten recurring meanings¤

    Table GRAD.1. Ten recurring meanings
    Name in this volume Differentiation variable Canonical object Typical generative use
    Objective parameter gradient model parameters \(\theta\) \(\nabla_\theta J(\theta)\) likelihood, ELBO, denoising, flow matching
    Monte Carlo gradient estimator parameters of an expectation random estimate of \(\nabla_\theta\mathbb E[f]\) latent variables, discrete choices, stochastic paths
    State score or energy force sample/state \(x\) \(\nabla_x\log p(x)\) or \(-\nabla_xE(x)\) score models, Langevin sampling, guidance
    Riemannian or natural gradient tangent coordinate under metric \(G\) \(G^\dagger\nabla J\) statistical families and manifold constraints
    Game gradient field several players' parameters stacked signed partial derivatives GANs and learned critics
    Implicit or hypergradient outer parameter through an inner solution total derivative via unrolling or an implicit system learned inference, bilevel critics, sampler tuning
    Solver or path sensitivity parameters through numerical dynamics tangent, adjoint, or pathwise derivative CNFs, neural ODEs/SDEs, differentiable samplers
    Trajectory reward gradient parameters of a sampled sequence or path law likelihood-ratio sum with return or advantage AR, diffusion, and flow post-training
    Functional gradient function, map, density, or measure first variation in a chosen function space Stein particles, boosting, probability flows
    Search-distribution gradient parameters of \(q_\psi(a)\) score/natural gradient of expected reward black-box generator and design search

    “Stochastic gradient” can mean a minibatch estimator of a deterministic parameter gradient or a score-function estimator through a random variable. “Score gradient” can mean the data score \(\nabla_x\log p(x)\) or an outer-product curvature built from parameter scores \(\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x)\). The subscripts are not optional decoration; they identify different vector spaces.

    The map below is a practical type checker. A familiar word such as “score” is not enough: follow the variable first, then ask whether another map is required before the resulting vector can be used elsewhere.

    Estimators are not geometries¤

    Suppose

    \[ J(\theta)=\mathbb E_{Z\sim q_\theta}[f_\theta(Z)]. \]

    The score-function and pathwise identities can estimate the same Euclidean parameter derivative under their respective regularity conditions. They do not define different population objectives merely because their sample formulas differ. Conversely, a finite-temperature relaxation or a straight-through rule may estimate the derivative of a relaxed objective or a custom surrogate rather than \(\nabla_\theta J\).

    Once an estimator \(\widehat g\) is available, a natural, mirror, or other preconditioned method may transform it. Thus

    \[ \widehat d=(\widehat G+\lambda I)^{-1}\widehat g \]

    contains at least two approximation problems: whether \(\widehat g\) estimates the intended gradient and whether \(\widehat G\) represents the intended metric.

    Training gradients versus sampling gradients¤

    An EBM makes the distinction concrete. Its parameter likelihood gradient is

    \[ \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x), \]

    whereas Langevin sampling uses the state score

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_\theta(x)=-\nabla_xE_\theta(x). \]

    The first changes the model; the second moves a sample under a fixed model. Differentiating through several sampler steps introduces a third object: a total derivative of a downstream loss through the numerical chain. Its validity depends on truncation, random-number treatment, and the role of the sampler.

    Worked scalar separation¤

    Let \(E_\theta(x)=\tfrac12\theta x^2\) with \(\theta>0\). The normalized model is \(\mathcal N(0,1/\theta)\). For one observed \(x\), the parameter score is

    \[ \partial_\theta\log p_\theta(x) =\frac{1}{2\theta}-\frac{x^2}{2}. \]

    The state score used by Langevin dynamics is

    \[ \partial_x\log p_\theta(x)=-\theta x. \]

    At \(\theta=2\) and \(x=1\), these are \(-1/4\) and \(-2\). They differ in value, units, dimension, and purpose. Calling both “the model gradient” loses the information required to implement either correctly.

    The noun after gradient matters

    Parameter, state, functional, game, and hypergradients live in different spaces. Before comparing two methods, verify that their vectors can even be added without a pullback, projection, or other declared conversion.

    Independent practice and assessment map: typed gradient objects¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. A correct answer must type the mathematical object before discussing implementation quality.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: two meanings of score. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 7 min. For \(E_\theta(x)=\tfrac12\theta x^2\), predict whether the parameter score and state score at \(\theta=3,x=2\) have the same sign, units, and purpose. Then compute both for the normalized model.
    2. P1.2 — Complete two five-field contracts. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. Fill the objective, variable, averaging law, geometry, and estimator or solver for (a) a pathwise VAE encoder update and (b) Langevin sampling from a fixed EBM. State when “objective” is a state-space sampling target rather than a training loss.
    3. P1.3 — Separate estimator from geometry. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 9 min. A score-function estimator and a reparameterized estimator both target \(\nabla_\phi J\). A colleague calls the first “Euclidean” and the second “natural.” Diagnose the claim and write the additional operation that would create a natural direction.
    4. P1.4 — Audit a generator pullback. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 8 min. Let \(g_\theta:\mathbb R^m\to\mathbb R^n\) have \(p\) parameters and let \(v(x)\in\mathbb R^n\) be a teacher state field. Give the shapes of \(J_\theta g_\theta(z)\), \(v\), and the parameter pullback. Explain why \(v\) cannot be added directly to a parameter gradient when \(n=p\) happens numerically.
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose a silent target change. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. A discrete latent model samples a hard category in the forward pass and backpropagates through a softmax probability in the backward pass. Identify the exact derivative that has not yet been justified and list two experiments that would reveal the surrogate's effect.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to guided diffusion. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. Type a classifier-guided diffusion sampling update. Name the state variable, unconditional and classifier fields, law being approximated, geometry, time weighting or guidance scale, and finite solver. Then name the distinct parameter gradient used to train the classifier.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The normalized model is \(\mathcal N(0,1/\theta)\). Its parameter score is \(1/(2\theta)-x^2/2=1/6-2=-11/6\); its state score is \(-\theta x=-6\). Both are negative here, but agreement of sign is accidental. The first has parameter-covector units and changes model precision during training; the second has inverse-state units and moves a sample under fixed \(\theta\). Check. Changing \(x\) can reverse or change their relative magnitudes without making the objects equal.
    2. P1.2. For a pathwise VAE encoder update: objective \(-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x)\); variable \(\phi\); law \(x\sim p_D\), \(\varepsilon\sim p(\varepsilon)\) with \(z=T_\phi(x,\varepsilon)\); Euclidean parameter geometry unless another metric is declared; reparameterized Monte Carlo plus reverse AD. For Langevin: target \(p_\theta(x)\propto e^{-E_\theta(x)}\) with fixed \(\theta\); variable \(x\); law is the current sampler state plus Gaussian increments; Euclidean state geometry; Euler--Maruyama or another declared integrator using \(-\nabla_xE_\theta\). The “objective” field describes the sampling target or free energy, not an encoder loss. Check. Only the first update changes learned parameters.
    3. P1.3. Under their conditions, score-function and pathwise formulas are alternative estimators of the same Euclidean coordinate derivative. Neither is natural merely because of how randomness is differentiated. A natural direction requires \(d=G^\dagger\nabla_\phi J\) or an approximate solve \((\widehat G+\lambda I)d=\widehat g\) for a declared metric. Check. Estimator variance and metric approximation are separate error axes.
    4. P1.4. With output-by-parameter convention, \(J_\theta g\in\mathbb R^{n\times p}\), \(v\in\mathbb R^n\), and \(J_\theta g^\top v\in\mathbb R^p\). Equal integer dimensions do not identify vector spaces: changing sample units and changing parameters have different meanings and coordinate transformations. Check. The transpose is the pullback from a state covector to parameter coordinates.
    5. P1.5. The backward softmax rule has not established the derivative of the expected hard-category objective; it defines a straight-through surrogate and may not be integrable. Enumerate a tiny support to compare its expectation with the exact gradient or a score estimator, and compare bias, variance, optimization, and final discrete metrics against a declared continuous relaxation across temperatures. Check. Good downstream performance does not retroactively make the surrogate unbiased.
    6. P1.6. At noise level \(t\), the state is \(x_t\); fields are \(s_t(x_t)\) and \(\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(y\mid x_t)\); the intended conditional field is their sum before optional guidance scaling; the law is the reverse-time conditional path induced by the corruption convention; geometry is state-space Euclidean unless a manifold metric is declared; the finite method specifies noise schedule, scale, and reverse SDE/ODE integrator. The classifier is trained separately with a parameter gradient \(\nabla_\varphi\mathbb E[\ell(y,c_\varphi(x_t,t))]\). Rubric. Full credit does not call the classifier state gradient a gradient of \(\varphi\).

    Chapter 1 synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    A gradient is fully specified only after its objective, variable, averaging law, geometry, and estimator are named. This typed contract prevents common category errors: confusing training with sampling, estimator variance with statistical geometry, or a surrogate derivative with the derivative of the declared population target.

    Retrieval questions
    1. What five fields identify a gradient claim?
    2. Why are pathwise and score-function rules not two geometries?
    3. What is the difference between an EBM parameter score and state score?

    Solutions. (1) Objective, differentiation variable, averaging law, geometry, and estimator or solver. (2) They are estimators of a parameter derivative; geometry is a separate rule for measuring or transforming that derivative. (3) The parameter score trains \(\theta\) while the state score moves \(x\) under fixed \(\theta\).
