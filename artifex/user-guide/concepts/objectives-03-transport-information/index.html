    8. Optimal Transport Objectives¤

    Transport, information, and composite-objective learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes couplings, KL divergence, and latent-variable learning. By the end, you should be able to distinguish primal transport, entropic regularization, and learned-critic approximations; derive rate–distortion and information-bottleneck operating points; separate VAE rate, distortion, and actual code length; and audit composite objectives for scale, estimator, identifiability, and Pareto tradeoffs rather than reading coefficients as model semantics.

    Optimal transport introduces an explicit geometric cost for moving mass. A small distance therefore means little only relative to that chosen ground cost; raw pixels, learned features, molecular coordinates, and manifold geodesics encode different notions of a useful move.

    The primal transport problem is

    \[ W_c(p,q)=\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(p,q)}\mathbb E_{(x,y)\sim\pi}[c(x,y)]. \]

    Choosing a coupling affects path geometry and regression variance. Entropic regularization trades exact transport cost for smoother, computationally tractable couplings and leads to Sinkhorn algorithms and Schrödinger bridges. A minibatch OT coupling is itself an approximation whose bias depends on batch construction.

    The dynamic Benamou–Brenier formulation writes quadratic transport as

    \[ W_2^2(p_0,p_1)= \inf_{(p_t,v_t)}\int_0^1\mathbb E_{x\sim p_t}\|v_t(x)\|^2dt \]

    subject to \(\partial_t p_t+\nabla\cdot(p_tv_t)=0\) and the endpoint distributions. Flow matching may learn a vector field along a chosen probability path without solving this minimum-action problem. Using an OT coupling can straighten paths and reduce target variance, but “flow matching” and “optimal transport” are not synonyms.

    9. Rate–Distortion and Information Bottlenecks¤

    The Information, Coding, and Generative Models volume supplies the source-coding theorem, mutual-information prerequisites, operational channel distinctions, and description-length boundaries. This section retains the objective-centered derivation needed to compare generative training losses.

    Lossless coding asks the decoder to recover a discrete message exactly. Lossy coding permits a reconstruction \(\hat X\) and must therefore state what counts as an error. For a block \(X^n\), an encoder emits a finite bitstring \(M\) and a decoder maps it to \(\hat X^n\). Two operational quantities are

    \[ \begin{aligned} R_n &=\frac{\mathbb E[|M|]}{n},\\ D_n &=\frac1n\mathbb E\!\left[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}d(X_i,\hat X_i) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The rate counts transmitted bits, including whatever the protocol requires. The distortion is an expected task-declared cost, such as squared error, a perceptual feature distance, or a domain-specific penalty. Zero distortion means exact recovery only when \(d(x,\hat x)=0\) implies \(x=\hat x\).

    For a memoryless source and the usual asymptotic block-coding assumptions, Shannon's rate–distortion function is

    \[ R(D_0)= \inf_{r(\hat x\mid x):\, \mathbb E[d(X,\hat X)]\leq D_0} I(X;\hat X). \]

    It gives the minimum asymptotic rate compatible with expected distortion at most \(D_0\) under the declared source and distortion. It is not the loss of a particular neural codec, and one finite architecture need not attain it. The original fidelity-criterion coding theorem is due to Shannon (1959).

    Lagrangians select operating points¤

    Constrained coding problems are commonly trained with either

    \[ R+\lambda D \qquad\text{or}\qquad D+\beta R. \]

    These conventions invert the role and units of the multiplier: \(\lambda\) prices distortion in bits, whereas \(\beta\) prices rate in distortion units. Increasing \(\beta\) in the second convention favors lower rate, not universally “better compression,” because the chosen distortion may ignore important information. Lagrangian sweeps recover supported points of an ideal convex trade-off; finite data, quantization relaxations, non-convex networks, and imperfect optimization can leave gaps or dominated solutions.

    Learned codecs have several rates¤

    Let a learned encoder quantize to \(\hat z\) and let an entropy model be \(m_\psi(\hat z)\). Its model-based ideal rate is

    \[ R_{\mathrm{model}} =\mathbb E[-\log_2m_\psi(\hat Z)]. \]

    An arithmetic or ANS implementation turns those probabilities into a bitstream. Actual rate also includes finite-precision and termination overhead, side information, model headers when they are not shared, container metadata, and padding. Token count, latent scalar count, vocabulary size, empirical token entropy, entropy-model cross-entropy, and measured file size are consequently different quantities. End-to-end learned compression optimizes this rate proxy jointly with distortion; see Ballé, Laparra, and Simoncelli (2017) and the later scale-hyperprior construction of Ballé et al. (2018).

    VAE rate, distortion, and bits back¤

    Latent probabilistic models often use the variational quantities

    \[ R_{\mathrm{var}} =\mathbb E_xD_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z\mid x)\|p(z)), \qquad D_{\mathrm{NLL}} =-\mathbb E_{x,z}\log p(x\mid z). \]

    With base-two logarithms, their sum is the negative ELBO in bits. A naive two-part code appears to pay the cross-entropy of \(q(z\mid x)\) under \(p(z)\) plus the residual code under \(p(x\mid z)\). A bits-back construction uses initial random coder state to choose \(z\sim q(z\mid x)\) and recovers information corresponding to \(-\log_2q(z\mid x)\) during decoding. Its net ideal expected cost is therefore

    \[ \begin{aligned} -\operatorname{ELBO}_2(x) &=\mathbb E_{q(z\mid x)}\!\left[ -\log_2p(x,z)\right.\\ &\qquad\left.+\log_2q(z\mid x) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    This is a coding interpretation with conditions, not a claim that an ordinary VAE checkpoint is already a compressor. A concrete implementation needs a discrete coding construction, synchronized probability tables, an initial bit reservoir, and a strategy such as message chaining to amortize startup cost. Approximate inference and finite coder precision create overhead. Townsend, Bird, and Barber (2019) demonstrate a practical lossless bits-back construction using ANS.

    Changing \(\beta\) in a \(\beta\)-VAE optimizes

    \[ D_{\mathrm{NLL}}+\beta R_{\mathrm{var}}. \]

    This is a variational compression trade-off, not a guarantee of disentanglement and not automatically the operational rate–distortion curve of a deployed codec. Learned representations must be evaluated both for reconstruction and for how easily a downstream prior models their latents.

    The variational rate decomposes into mutual information plus aggregate-posterior mismatch:

    \[ \mathbb E_xD_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z\mid x)\|p(z)) =I_q(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z)\|p(z)). \]

    Thus a lower variational rate can mean less instance information, better prior matching, or both. It upper-bounds \(I_q(X;Z)\), but it is not simply “the information in the latent” unless the aggregate posterior matches the prior. Distortion also depends on the decoder likelihood: pixel MSE corresponds to a fixed-variance Gaussian assumption, not a universal perceptual distance.

    Distortion is not perception or utility¤

    Two reconstructions can have the same average distortion while differing in realism, semantic correctness, or downstream value. Conversely, a perceptually plausible decoder may replace an uncertain detail with a realistic but incorrect one. The perception–distortion result of Blau and Michaeli (2018) formalizes a conflict between reconstruction distortion and distributional perceptual quality under its assumptions. Scientific, medical, identity, text, and control applications must report fidelity to the particular information that matters rather than treating visual plausibility as a universal distortion measure.

    Misconception check

    A tokenizer maps a \(256\times256\) image to \(16\times16\) categorical tokens from a vocabulary of size 8192. Has the image been compressed to exactly \(256\log_2 8192=3328\) bits?

    Solution. No. That is a nominal fixed-width index count. Actual storage depends on the token distribution, context-dependent entropy model, entropy coder, side information, headers, and implementation overhead. If the tokens are never serialized—for example, they only reduce a latent generator's compute—there may be no bitstream at all.

    10. Composite Objectives and Identifiability¤

    Modern systems often combine reconstruction, adversarial, perceptual, score, preference, and regularization terms:

    \[ \mathcal J(\theta)=\sum_j\lambda_j\mathcal J_j(\theta). \]

    This sum generally does not minimize one named divergence unless a derivation proves it. Scale depends on reduction, feature dimension, noise level, and critic state, so coefficient values are not portable defaults. Report each term, its units/reduction, gradient norms or conflicts, and ablations.

    A reward or constraint can also define a new endpoint target \(\pi(x)\propto p_0(x)w(x)\). That change of measure is distinct from adding \(w\) as an uncalibrated training penalty. The target-law chapter separates likelihood conditioning, exponential tilting, hard feasibility, and the algorithms used to approximate each target.

    Even if a discrepancy identifies the data distribution over observations, it need not identify latent variables, causal mechanisms, or a unique parameter vector. Distributional identifiability and representation identifiability are different research questions.

    Transport and information retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: transport, rate, and composite losses
    1. Which choice makes an optimal-transport distance depend on geometry rather than only probability mass?
    2. How does entropic regularization change the coupling objective?
    3. Why is the expected KL in a VAE not automatically the number of bits written by a practical codec?
    4. What should be reported when a composite loss improves one metric and worsens another?

    Solutions. (1) The ground cost/metric and its scale. (2) It trades transport cost against coupling entropy or KL to a reference, smoothing the solution and changing the estimand at finite regularization. (3) Actual coding also depends on the aggregated latent distribution, prior mismatch, bits-back protocol, quantization, entropy coder, and overhead. (4) The full Pareto tradeoff, coefficient normalization, estimator variance, training budget, uncertainty, and the intended decision criterion rather than a single selected score.

    Transport and information synthesis¤

    Transport objectives require a geometry and coupling class. Information Lagrangians choose operating points rather than universally optimal models, and learned codecs distinguish variational rates from realized code lengths. Composite losses combine estimands with scale and optimization interactions, so their coefficients must be treated as an explicit multi-objective design.
