    Advanced Topics¤

    Advanced Topics: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes basic hierarchical probability models, importance sampling, and change of variables. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. write the joint, top-down posterior factorization, and layerwise ELBO of a hierarchical VAE;
    2. distinguish a chain-rule ordering from the restrictions imposed by an inference architecture;
    3. diagnose latent groups using conditional information, prior mismatch, and interventions rather than KL alone;
    4. separate legitimate unused capacity from harmful variational pruning;
    5. identify which hierarchical conditionals are sequential and which coordinates can be sampled in parallel;
    6. distinguish one global sequence code from a per-time latent stochastic process and select filtering or smoothing inference;
    7. explain IWAE monotonicity and its inference-gradient trade-off;
    8. distinguish a flow posterior, learned prior, diffusion prior, and codec-plus-prior system; and
    9. evaluate tokenizer and codec claims across reconstruction, latent-distribution, downstream-generation, systems, and evidence layers.

    Structured decoders can replace a free-form observation parameterization with a probability law over valid derivations. The Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations volume develops grammar VAEs, syntax-directed attributes, molecular hypergraph grammars, and the boundary between syntactic support and semantic correctness.

    Hierarchical VAEs¤

    Model family and notation¤

    Let \(z_{>\ell}=z_{\ell+1:L}\) denote the latent groups above group \(\ell\), where \(z_L\) is farthest from the observation. A general top-down hierarchical VAE defines

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\theta(x,z_{1:L}) &=p_\theta(z_L)\\ &\quad\times \prod_{\ell=1}^{L-1}p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell})\\ &\quad\times p_\theta(x\mid z_{1:L}). \end{aligned} \]

    The Markov special case uses only \(z_{\ell+1}\) in each conditional and often only \(z_1\) in the observation model. Skip-connected hierarchies allow every lower group and the likelihood to depend on all groups above them. Those are different probabilistic models, even if their neural backbones look similar.

    The word hierarchical also does not guarantee semantic ordering. Spatial resolution, receptive field, prior dependencies, and decoder paths can encourage coarse-to-fine allocation, but the ELBO is invariant to many latent reparameterizations and does not name “global” or “local” factors.

    Top-down and bottom-up are posterior factorizations¤

    A variational family aligned with the generative ordering is

    \[ \begin{aligned} q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x) &=q_\phi(z_L\mid x)\\ &\quad\times \prod_{\ell=1}^{L-1}q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{>\ell}). \end{aligned} \]

    This is usually implemented by a deterministic bottom-up feature pass followed by stochastic top-down sampling. At each group, an inference network combines data-dependent evidence with the conditional prior supplied by already sampled higher groups.

    Ladder VAE gives the bottom-up network a likelihood-message interpretation and combines Gaussian messages with top-down Gaussian priors. Residual posterior parameterizations instead predict corrections to prior parameters. BIVA adds a stochastic bottom-up path as well as the stochastic top-down path (Maaløe et al., 2019).

    The ordering itself is not a theorem that top-down inference is always more accurate. The chain rule also permits

    \[ \begin{aligned} q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x) &=q_\phi(z_1\mid x)\\ &\quad\times \prod_{\ell=2}^{L}q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{<\ell}). \end{aligned} \]

    With unrestricted conditionals, either ordering can represent the exact posterior. Differences arise after architectural restrictions:

    Table VAE.11. Top-down and bottom-up are posterior factorizations
    Inference construction What it assumes or exposes Characteristic limitation
    Mean-field \(\prod_\ell q(z_\ell\mid x)\) no posterior dependence among groups cannot represent explaining-away dependence
    Restricted bottom-up \(q(z_1\mid x)\prod_{\ell>1}q(z_\ell\mid z_{\ell-1})\) information is compressed successively upward need not match the top-down conditional-prior structure
    Full chain-rule bottom-up conditions on all sampled lower groups expressive in principle but can be costly and awkward for per-prior corrections
    Top-down with bottom-up evidence matches the prior ordering and exposes a local posterior-versus-prior comparison remains approximate; higher-group errors propagate downward
    Bidirectional or iteratively refined expands inference computation or variational flexibility adds latency, memory, optimization, or estimator complexity

    The layerwise ELBO¤

    For the matched top-down factorization, the KL chain rule gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal B(x) ={}&\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x)} [\log p_\theta(x\mid z_{1:L})]\\ &-D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_\phi(z_L\mid x)\,\|\,p_\theta(z_L) \right)\\ &-\sum_{\ell=1}^{L-1} \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z_{>\ell}\mid x)} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{>\ell}) \,\|\, p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \right) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    Define the per-record group rate

    \[ R_\ell(x)= \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z_{>\ell}\mid x)} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{>\ell}) \,\|\, p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \right) \right], \]

    with the expectation omitted for the top group. Then the total rate is \(\sum_\ell R_\ell(x)\). Each lower-group term must be averaged over its random parents; evaluating it only at posterior means is a different diagnostic.

    Worked two-layer Gaussian hierarchy¤

    Consider the scalar model

    \[ \begin{aligned} Z_2&\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\\ Z_1\mid Z_2=z_2&\sim\mathcal N(z_2,1),\\ X\mid Z_1=z_1&\sim\mathcal N(z_1,1). \end{aligned} \]

    Marginally, \(X\sim\mathcal N(0,3)\). For the observation \(x=3\), exact Gaussian conditioning gives the top-down posterior

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(z_2\mid x=3) &=\mathcal N\!\left(1,\frac23\right),\\ p(z_1\mid x=3,z_2) &=\mathcal N\!\left(\frac{3+z_2}{2},\frac12\right). \end{aligned} \]

    Using these exact conditionals as \(q\), the two group rates are

    \[ \begin{aligned} R_2(3) &=\frac13+\frac12\log\frac32 \approx0.536\ \text{nats},\\ R_1(3) &=\mathbb E_{p(z_2\mid x=3)} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( p(z_1\mid x=3,z_2)\,\|\,p(z_1\mid z_2) \right) \right]\\ &=\frac13+\frac12\log2 \approx0.680\ \text{nats}. \end{aligned} \]

    The lower rate is an expectation over \(Z_2\); it is not the KL obtained by substituting only \(z_2=1\). The posterior marginal is \(Z_1\mid X=3\sim\mathcal N(2,2/3)\), so

    \[ \mathbb E_q[\log p(x=3\mid Z_1)] =-\frac12\log(2\pi)-\frac56. \]

    Subtracting both rates yields

    \[ \mathcal B(3) =-\frac12\log(6\pi)-\frac32 =\log p(x=3). \]

    The equality is expected because \(q\) is exact. The example also shows why a group-rate dashboard is more informative than total KL: both stochastic groups carry posterior information, and their contributions are quantitatively separable.

    What does a group KL measure?¤

    Let \(r(x,z)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)\) be the inference joint, and let \(r(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell})\) be its conditional aggregate. Averaging a group rate over data gives the exact decomposition

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)}R_\ell(x) ={}&I_r(X;Z_\ell\mid Z_{>\ell})\\ &+\mathbb E_{r(z_{>\ell})} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( r(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \,\|\, p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \right) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The first term is conditional information about the observation. The second is conditional aggregate-posterior mismatch. Consequently:

    • a large group KL need not mean that the group conveys much information about \(x\); prior mismatch can dominate;
    • a small group KL means the encoder contributes little data-dependent update beyond higher groups, but does not by itself reveal whether this is an appropriate optimum or an inference/optimization failure; and
    • equal KL across groups is neither required by probability theory nor evidence that the groups learned different semantic scales.

    A layerwise activity dashboard¤

    Record diagnostics per stochastic group and, where practical, per channel or dimension:

    Table VAE.12. A layerwise activity dashboard
    Diagnostic Question it answers Required qualification
    Mean and quantiles of \(R_\ell(x)\) How much posterior-to-prior update occurs, and for which records? state reductions, units, and whether parent samples or means were used
    \(I_r(X;Z_\ell\mid Z_{>\ell})\) estimate How much conditional data information is carried? name the estimator and its bias/variance limitations
    Conditional aggregate mismatch Is rate spent matching a poor conditional prior? explain how \(r(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell})\) was estimated
    Active-channel statistic Which coordinates vary with data? state the statistic and threshold; raw activation variance is not invariant to scaling
    Posterior/prior scale and residual-mean histograms Is the encoder making stable corrections, or producing variance/mean pathologies? stratify by group size and spatial scale
    Resample, clamp, or prior-replace intervention What changes when the group stops carrying record-specific information? compare reconstruction, likelihood estimate, samples, and task behavior
    Gradient and update norms Can the group receive a usable learning signal? separate encoder, prior, and decoder paths
    Ancestral truncation or partial resampling Which structure is fixed by higher groups and supplied by lower groups? this is an empirical interpretation, not a semantic guarantee

    The classic “active unit” statistic often thresholds \(\operatorname{Var}_x\mathbb E_{q(z_j\mid x)}[z_j]\). In a hierarchy, movement of a lower marginal posterior mean can be inherited entirely from \(x\)-dependent higher-group states, even when the lower conditional posterior equals its prior. That is not new conditional evidence at the lower group. Prefer residual posterior-versus-prior statistics or conditional interventions alongside any marginal active-unit count. No threshold such as \(0.01\), \(0.02\), or \(0.1\) is universal across parameterizations or reductions.

    Variational pruning: unused capacity or failed optimization?¤

    A group is pruned in the inference sense when its approximate conditional posterior closely matches its conditional prior for almost all records. Calling that outcome over-pruning requires evidence that the unused capacity harms the declared goal. The distinction is operational:

    Table VAE.13. Variational pruning: unused capacity or failed optimization?
    Observation Legitimate unused capacity Evidence of harmful pruning
    Near-zero group rate remaining groups already model the relevant variation a stronger inference/training procedure activates the group and improves held-out model or task criteria
    Decoder intervention removing or prior-resampling the group has negligible effect the decoder is sensitive to the group, but the encoder cannot infer it reliably
    Model fit held-out likelihood and posterior predictive checks do not improve with the group the model underfits or misses long-range/multiscale structure that the group was introduced to capture
    Representation goal no required information is lost conditional information, interventions, or downstream probes show a missing factor
    Stability the group remains unused across seeds and adequate optimization activation depends strongly on initialization, warm-up, or gradient path

    Yeung et al. (2017) demonstrate that inactive factors can persist even when a VAE underfits, but also show why merely lowering the KL weight or forcing more “active” units is insufficient: extra coordinates may memorize, duplicate information, or worsen prior generation. More active units are not an objective by themselves.

    Interventions and what they change¤

    Table VAE.14. Interventions and what they change
    Intervention Mechanism Scientific reporting boundary
    KL warm-up or cyclical schedules temporarily reduce the rate gradient so reconstruction paths form state the schedule and whether the final objective returns to the ELBO
    Free bits or capacity floors stop rewarding rate reductions below a group budget the optimized objective differs wherever the floor is active
    KL balancing during warm-up reweight group KL gradients to keep weak groups trainable equal group KL is a heuristic target during optimization, not a property of the optimum
    Residual posterior parameterization predict corrections relative to conditional-prior parameters changes conditioning and numerical behavior, not the probabilistic ELBO identity
    Bidirectional or iterative inference enlarge or refine the approximate posterior computation compare inference latency, memory, and amortization/approximation gaps
    Weaker, masked, or restricted decoder prevent the likelihood path from bypassing latents changes the model family and possibly attainable likelihood
    Learned or richer conditional prior reduce aggregate mismatch and improve generation from prior states a lower KL can reflect better prior fit rather than less information
    Group removal or redesign eliminate demonstrably redundant capacity or change scale allocation retrain matched controls; post-hoc deletion alone changes the model

    NVAE used KL balancing only during warm-up and returned the weights to one for ordinary ELBO training; its residual Gaussian parameterization and spectral regularization are separate design choices (Vahdat & Kautz, 2020). Do not combine all of these changes and attribute an improvement to “the hierarchy” without ablations.

    Autoregressive conditionals and parallel generation¤

    A top-down hierarchy is sequential across any dependent groups, but it can be parallel inside a conditionally factorized group. The actual sampling critical path is determined at several layers:

    Table VAE.15. Autoregressive conditionals and parallel generation
    Component Factorized form Sampling consequence
    Across latent groups \(p(z_L)\prod_{\ell<L}p(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell})\) at least the dependency depth of the latent DAG
    Within one group \(\prod_jp(z_{\ell j}\mid z_{>\ell})\) coordinates can be drawn in parallel after parents are known
    Autoregressive group prior \(\prod_jp(z_{\ell j}\mid z_{\ell,<j},z_{>\ell})\) sequential over the chosen within-group ordering
    Factorized observation decoder \(\prod_ip(x_i\mid z_{1:L})\) output coordinates can be drawn in parallel given latents
    Autoregressive observation decoder \(\prod_ip(x_i\mid x_{<i},z_{1:L})\) sequential over observations and can reduce pressure to use latents
    Tree or multiscale hierarchy conditionally independent nodes at each depth parallel by level only if the declared graph and implementation permit it

    A sufficiently deep hierarchical latent model can represent an autoregressive distribution through the degenerate construction \(z_i=x_i\), an autoregressive prior over \(z\), and an identity decoder. This is an algebraic inclusion construction, not evidence that an arbitrary trained VAE learned the same factorization or sampling cost. Conversely, grouping conditionally independent details below a shared parent can shorten the critical path relative to a flat autoregressive ordering. Never infer \(O(\log D)\) generation merely from the word “hierarchical”; state the graph depth, within-group dependencies, decoder, batch size, and hardware.

    Architectures and research boundaries¤

    Intended benefits to test:

    • different latent groups can represent information at different spatial or semantic scales, but coarse-to-fine ordering is not guaranteed;
    • conditional priors can allocate rate adaptively across groups;
    • conditionally independent coordinates can reduce the ancestral critical path; and
    • added expressiveness may improve likelihood or reconstructions while increasing posterior-design, numerical-stability, and optimization difficulty.

    Representative deep hierarchies include:

    • Ladder VAE recursively combines bottom-up approximate-likelihood messages with top-down conditional priors.
    • BIVA adds stochastic bottom-up variables and generative skip paths to a bidirectional hierarchy.
    • NVAE uses deep Gaussian groups, residual posterior parameters, depthwise-separable decoder cells, temporary KL balancing, and spectral regularization; each contribution needs its own ablation.
    • Very Deep VAE / VDVAE (Child, 2021) scales stochastic depth and demonstrates fast, protocol-specific image sampling relative to the PixelCNN baselines it tested.
    • HQ-VAE (Takida et al., 2024) gives stacked discrete codebooks a variational treatment; discrete codebook collapse is related to, but not identical with, Gaussian group pruning.

    The design space remains open rather than converging to one hierarchy recipe. An ICLR 2026 study adds training-only spectral constraints to hierarchical learned image compression, explicitly targeting cross-scale frequency allocation (Cong et al., 2026). An ICASSP 2026 study combines amortized initialization with iterative posterior refinement in a transform-separated HVAE for its inverse-problem setting (Penninga & van Sloun, 2026). These peer-reviewed results establish application-specific constructions, not a universal hierarchy, semantic ordering, or speed advantage.

    Sequence VAEs: One Global Code or a Latent Process?¤

    The phrase sequence VAE does not determine where stochastic state lives. Two common constructions answer different questions.

    One latent variable for the whole sequence¤

    A global-code model can use

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\theta(z,x_{1:T}) &=p(z)\prod_{t=1}^{T}\\[-2pt] &\quad p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t},z). \end{aligned} \]

    An offline posterior model may condition on the whole sequence as \(q_\phi(z\mid x_{1:T})\).

    The latent variable can capture sequence-level properties such as speaker, style, topic, or a global plan. The decoder remains autoregressive when its conditional depends on \(x_{<t}\): drawing \(z\) once does not make token generation parallel. A bidirectional sequence encoder is valid for offline posterior inference because the complete observation is known, even though the generative model must sample in causal order. For online inference, the available conditional would instead be \(q(z\mid x_{1:t})\) and changes as evidence arrives.

    A stochastic latent state at each time¤

    A latent-process model can factorize more generally as

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\theta(z_{1:T},x_{1:T}) &=\prod_{t=1}^{T}\left[ p_\theta(z_t\mid z_{<t},x_{<t})\right]\\[-2pt] &\quad\times\left[ p_\theta(x_t\mid z_{\le t},x_{<t})\right]. \end{aligned} \]

    VRNN implements these histories through a recurrent state. A filtering inference model uses only data available through the current time,

    \[ \begin{aligned} &q_\phi^{\mathrm{filter}}(z_{1:T}\mid x_{1:T})\\ &\quad=\prod_{t=1}^{T} q_\phi(z_t\mid z_{<t},x_{\le t}), \end{aligned} \]

    whereas an offline smoother may use future observations,

    \[ \begin{aligned} &q_\phi^{\mathrm{smooth}}(z_{1:T}\mid x_{1:T})\\ &\quad=\prod_{t=1}^{T} q_\phi(z_t\mid z_{<t},x_{1:T}). \end{aligned} \]

    The smoother can reduce posterior uncertainty about an earlier state using later evidence, but it is unavailable for a strictly online decision at time \(t\). The ELBO contains one expected observation term and one conditional KL per time step; those KLs are averaged over inferred histories. Global collapse and per-time collapse are therefore different diagnostics. A model may use a global code while ignoring local innovations, or the reverse.

    The choice is about the desired probability law:

    • Use a global code when one persistent random variable is a defensible account of shared sequence variation.
    • Use a latent process when new stochastic innovations, evolving uncertainty, or state-space queries matter.
    • Combine global and local variables when both are needed, and report their rates and interventions separately.
    • Do not infer superior likelihood, long-range memory, or calibrated forecast uncertainty merely from adding stochastic states. Compare against an autoregressive baseline and classical or neural state-space models under the same observation likelihood and evaluation horizon.

    Worked two-step sequence: future evidence updates a shared cause¤

    Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and let independent noise bits satisfy \(E_t\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(0.1)\). Define \(X_t=Z\mathbin{\mathsf{xor}}E_t\). Marginally, each observation is a fair bit, but the global latent couples the two times:

    \[ \Pr(X_1=X_2) =0.9^2+0.1^2 =0.82. \]

    After observing \(X_1=1\), filtering gives \(\Pr(Z=1\mid X_1=1)=0.9\). After the future observation \(X_2=1\) arrives, smoothing gives

    \[ \Pr(Z=1\mid X_1=1,X_2=1) =\frac{0.9^2}{0.9^2+0.1^2} =\frac{81}{82}\approx0.9878. \]

    If instead each time has an independent fair latent \(Z_t\) and the same local noise channel, \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) are independent fair bits and \(\Pr(X_1=X_2)=1/2\). Matching one-time marginals therefore does not establish the same sequence law. Temporal posterior inference must be checked against joint and predictive queries, not framewise reconstruction alone.

    Structured amortization is a separate design axis¤

    Mean-field, autoregressive, state-space, message-passing, and flow-based posteriors can all be amortized. The architecture should encode the conditional dependencies needed by the query rather than treating a wider encoder as a substitute for structure. Amortized Structural Variational Inference, listed by AISTATS 2026, combines a structured variational family with context-aware amortization and reports lower variational or amortization gaps in its studied graphical and time-series models. Those results are specific to its models, neighborhoods, estimators, and experiments; they do not establish one universally best sequence posterior.

    Retrieval practice: sequence VAEs
    1. Does a global sequence latent remove an autoregressive decoder's token-by-token sampling path?
    2. What information distinguishes filtering from smoothing?
    3. In the worked binary model, why does the second observation change the posterior for the same global \(Z\)?
    4. Can matching every frame marginal prove that two models learned the same sequence distribution?

    Solutions. (1) No. The decoder still conditions on previously generated observations. (2) Filtering uses observations only through the current time; smoothing can use the complete observed sequence to revise earlier states. (3) Both observations share the same cause, so the later bit supplies another likelihood factor for \(Z\). (4) No; the global and independent-local constructions above have identical fair one-frame marginals but equality probabilities 0.82 and 0.5.

    Importance Weighted VAE (IWAE)¤

    Burda et al. (2016) use multiple samples to get tighter bounds on the log-likelihood:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{IWAE}} = \mathbb{E}_{z_{1:K} \sim q} \left[ \log \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \frac{p(x, z_k)}{q(z_k|x)} \right] \]

    Under the standard IWAE construction, the expected bound is non-decreasing in \(K\) and converges toward the log marginal likelihood under suitable integrability conditions. It is strictly tighter only when the added importance samples provide non-degenerate information; equality is possible, including when the proposal is the exact posterior. Larger \(K\) also raises compute and can reduce the signal-to-noise ratio of inference-network gradients even while tightening the bound (Rainforth et al., 2018). Treat \(K\) as an estimator/optimisation trade-off and report it.

    Normalizing Flow VAE¤

    Rezende & Mohamed (2015) replace the Gaussian posterior with flexible distributions via invertible transformations:

    \[ q_\phi(z|x) = q_0(z_0|x) \left|\det \frac{\partial f}{\partial z_0}\right|^{-1} \]

    where \(f\) is an invertible function. Common flow families: Real NVP (Dinh et al., 2017), MAF (Papamakarios et al., 2017), IAF (Kingma et al., 2016) — well-suited as a posterior because sampling is fast — and Glow (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018).

    Benefits:

    • More expressive posterior families within the support, topology, invertibility, and architecture constraints of the chosen flow
    • Potentially better approximation of the true posterior and a tighter ELBO; neither is automatic after finite optimisation
    • downstream generation can improve if posterior fit was the relevant bottleneck; this is not guaranteed

    Trade-off: Increased compute during training; some flows (MAF) are slow to sample, others (IAF) are slow to evaluate density.

    VampPrior and Learned Priors¤

    The standard \(\mathcal{N}(0, I)\) prior can be mismatched with the aggregate posterior \(q_\phi(z)=\int q_\phi(z\mid x)p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)dx\). Then samples from some prior regions may decode poorly, while some encoded regions may receive too little prior mass. VampPrior (Tomczak & Welling, 2017) replaces \(p(z)\) with a mixture \(p_\lambda(z) = \tfrac{1}{K}\sum_{k} q_\phi(z\mid u_k)\) parameterised by \(K\) learned pseudo-inputs \(u_k\). The original work reports improved bounds and samples in its settings; component count, shared encoder coupling, and evaluation cost are part of the comparison. Diffusion priors (Wehenkel & Louppe, 2021; Vahdat et al., 2021 — LSGM) replace a simple prior with an iterative latent generator, changing both training and sampling cost.

    VAEs as Codecs and Latent Components¤

    One influential role is lossy learned compression for a separate latent generator. Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), SDXL (Podell et al., 2024), and documented image/video systems use an autoencoder-like codec followed by a diffusion, flow-matching, or autoregressive prior. Product names do not establish that every codec is a classical VAE; closed reports may omit architecture, loss, data, or latent scaling details.

    The cross-family Representations and Multimodal Generative Systems volume develops the corresponding rate–distortion, discrete-token, temporal-codec, semantic-alignment, and multimodal evaluation questions without assuming every codec is a classical VAE.

    Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs)¤

    The original latent-diffusion work uses a KL-regularized autoencoder with spatial downsampling and combines reconstruction, perceptual, adversarial, and mild KL terms (Rombach et al., 2022). Exact coefficients, latent scaling, channels, and losses are checkpoint-specific and should be read from the implementation/model card rather than elevated to a family constant. The downstream diffusion or flow model—not the simple reference prior alone—is the operational latent generator.

    Diffusion–VAE Hybrids¤

    Beyond the LDM "VAE-then-diffusion" pipeline, several hybrids tie the two more tightly:

    • DiffuseVAE (Pandey et al., 2022) conditions a diffusion model on a VAE reconstruction; speed and quality depend on both stages and the sampler.
    • LSGM (Vahdat et al., 2021) trains a diffusion model as the prior of an NVAE, jointly maximising a single ELBO.
    • Diffusion autoencoders (Preechakul et al., 2022 — DiffAE) combine semantic encodings with conditional diffusion decoding; representation and sample claims are protocol-specific.
    • Generalisation theory (Wang et al., 2025) gives a unified information-theoretic analysis of when VAE encoders + diffusion generators generalise, formalising what the empirical LDM literature has been observing.

    Foundation-Model Tokenizers¤

    A complementary direction uses frozen or aligned vision-foundation encoders and trains a decoder or adapter (ICLR 2026 poster; CVPR 2026). It trades end-to-end codec freedom for inherited features. Whether those features improve conditioning, reconstruction, generation, or transfer depends on the downstream task and protocol; the results do not prove KL is irrelevant.

    Latent Space Properties and Interpretation¤

    Name the latent claim before selecting a metric¤

    The phrase “good latent space” can refer to at least five different objects:

    Table VAE.17. Name the latent claim before selecting a metric
    Latent role Relevant question
    Inference state Does \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) approximate the model posterior required by the query?
    Codec state Which observation details can the encoder–decoder preserve, and at what rate?
    Generative state Can the learned prior place mass where the decoder produces the required data distribution?
    Downstream feature Can a declared adapter recover target \(Y\) from a chosen posterior statistic with finite data?
    Semantic or causal factor Is the coordinate meaning identified under explicit supervision, environment, intervention, or structural assumptions?

    These roles may share one tensor without sharing one guarantee. In particular, the ELBO does not state that a posterior mean is a linearly accessible feature, that a coordinate is a true causal variable, or that an augmentation should be ignored. The representation evaluation matrix separates reconstruction, generative adequacy, target sufficiency, accessibility, invariance/equivariance, disentanglement, identifiability, compression, and causal adequacy.

    Exact reconstruction deserves a precise qualification. If a deterministic decoder satisfies \(X=d(Z)\), then every target generated from \(X\) remains recoverable from \(Z\) by composing its predictor with \(d\). It need not be recoverable by a linear probe or from a small labeled sample. Conversely, a one-bit feature can be sufficient for one target while intentionally discarding most of \(X\). The finite XOR example makes both boundaries explicit.

    Continuity and Interpolation¤

    A common hope is that a VAE learns a useful continuous latent representation, but the following are empirical properties to test, not consequences of the ELBO alone:

    • Local decoder continuity: a continuous neural decoder maps sufficiently small latent perturbations to small output-parameter changes, but perceptual change can still be sharp.
    • Useful interpolation: linear or spherical paths may decode smoothly, yet can traverse low-density or semantically implausible regions.
    • Prior coverage: samples from the prior may decode well when the aggregate posterior and prior are suitably aligned; posterior holes and low-quality prior regions can remain.

    Interpolation therefore diagnoses the learned geometry; it does not prove disentanglement, semantic linearity, topology preservation, or global coverage.

    Testing interpolation:

    # Encode two images
z1 = encoder(x1)[0]  # Take mean, ignore variance
z2 = encoder(x2)[0]

# Interpolate
alphas = jnp.linspace(0, 1, num=10)
z_interp = [(1 - alpha) * z1 + alpha * z2 for alpha in alphas]

# Decode interpolated points
x_interp = [decoder(z) for z in z_interp]

    Disentanglement: Independent Factors of Variation¤

    An idealized axis-aligned disentangled representation assigns declared generative factors to separate coordinates, for example:

    • \(z_1\): Object class (digit identity)
    • \(z_2\): Rotation angle
    • \(z_3\): Stroke width
    • \(z_4\): Position
    • ...

    Axis alignment is a relationship between learned coordinates and named factors. It is stronger than independence of the aggregate posterior. The total correlation

    \[ \operatorname{TC}_q(Z) =D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q(z)\,\middle\|\,\prod_j q(z_j)\right) \]

    vanishes when the learned coordinates are statistically independent, but a measure-preserving transformation can mix the intended factors while retaining independent coordinates. The finite XOR construction above has exactly this property. If the scientific factors are themselves dependent, forcing independence can also misrepresent their structure.

    Achieving disentanglement:

    • Treat β-VAE (\(\beta>1\)) as one rate-control baseline, not an automatic disentangler.
    • Use β-TCVAE or FactorVAE when you want to reweight only the total-correlation component of the KL.
    • Use datasets with known generative factors for factor-based metrics; natural-data evaluation requires operational labels, interventions, or downstream tasks rather than assuming ground truth.
    • Add supervision or weak supervision when semantic identification matters. Locatello et al., 2019 show that unsupervised disentanglement is not identifiable without inductive biases; different equally likely representations can encode the same observations.

    Positive identifiability results are assumption-specific. For example, iVAE uses an observed auxiliary variable and a conditionally factorized latent prior to identify a class of nonlinear latent models up to a restricted equivalence. This does not turn an ordinary unconditional VAE into an identifiable model. Controlled pairs, temporal changes, environments, and interventions can supply other symmetry-breaking information, but their support and mechanism assumptions must be stated.

    Measuring disentanglement:

    • MIG (Mutual Information Gap) — gap between the top-2 latents most informative about each ground-truth factor; introduced with β-TCVAE.
    • SAP (Separated Attribute Predictability) (Kumar et al., 2018) — gap between the top-2 latents that best predict each factor.
    • DCI (Disentanglement, Completeness, Informativeness) (Eastwood & Williams, 2018) — three-metric framework based on a probe regressor's importance matrix.
    • FactorVAE score (Kim & Mnih, 2018) — accuracy of a majority-vote classifier predicting which factor is held fixed in a batch.

    When to Use VAEs¤

    VAEs Excel When:

    • You need explicit latent variables for posterior inference or downstream representations and will validate their structure on the target task
    • A single-objective latent-variable baseline and amortized inference match the task
    • You want both generation and reconstruction capabilities
    • You need a representation whose task utility, calibration, and intervention behavior will be explicitly evaluated
    • The chosen likelihood and latent structure fit the data, regardless of modality

    Example Applications:

    • Medical image anomaly detection via reconstruction error (Zimmerer et al., 2019)
    • Molecular design with controllable chemical properties (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018)
    • Semi-supervised learning with limited labels (Kingma et al., 2014 — M2 model)
    • Data compression and denoising (Ballé et al., 2018)
    • Recommendation systems (Liang et al., 2018 — Mult-VAE)

    When to Use GANs¤

    GANs Excel When:

    • A direct implicit generator and adversarial objective fit the data and latency target
    • You need a fast direct generator; an encoder is optional and can be learned through inversion, an auxiliary encoder, or a bidirectional architecture
    • You can evaluate game dynamics, coverage, and memorization under the target protocol

    Limitations:

    • No inferred latent representation by default; nevertheless, GANs have an explicit input latent space and models such as StyleGAN exhibit useful interpolation and editing structure
    • Training instability (mode collapse, oscillation)
    • No amortized reconstruction path by default; optimization-based inversion or a jointly trained encoder can provide one

    When to Use Diffusion Models¤

    Diffusion Models Excel When:

    • Your main objective is image/video generation rather than amortised latent inference or codec reconstruction. Diffusion and flow systems are relevant comparisons, but quality claims must be bounded by dataset, conditioning, guidance, sampler, and verification date.
    • Computational cost is acceptable.
    • You need excellent mode coverage and diversity (Ho et al., 2020 — DDPM; Song et al., 2021 — score-based).

    Limitations:

    • Iterative sampling cost depends on solver, NFE, distillation, representation, guidance, and hardware.
    • End-to-end cost may exceed or undercut a VAE/GAN pipeline depending on decoder and quality target; measure it.
    • Often paired with a learned autoencoder in latent image/video systems; pixel-space, discrete-token, and other representation choices remain active alternatives.

    Worked four-layer tokenizer audit¤

    Suppose Codec A improves PSNR but a fixed latent diffusion prior trained for equal steps obtains worse conditional fidelity than with Codec B. This is not contradictory. PSNR tests reconstruction means; the prior also depends on latent scale, covariance, spatial/token length, semantic organization, and optimization conditioning. A complete audit reports: (1) reconstruction metrics and bitrate; (2) aggregate latent statistics and prior mismatch; (3) downstream generator quality, coverage, and convergence under matched compute; and (4) encode/decode latency and memory. Only layer 3 answers which codec better serves that generator.

    Independent practice and assessment map: modern latent systems¤

    Analyze the complete dependency graph before choosing a method. Distinguish tighter bounds, richer inference, learned priors, codec changes, and downstream generators even when a system combines all five.

    P1 — Audit a hierarchical sequence codec with a learned latent prior¤

    A video system uses two global latent groups \(z_2,z_1\), per-frame latent states \(s_{1:T}\), a flow posterior for \(z_1\), and an independently trained diffusion prior over compressed frame latents.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether near-zero KL in \(z_2\) proves harmful pruning, whether a larger IWAE sample count guarantees a better encoder gradient, and whether the diffusion prior turns the codec into one algebraically equivalent VAE. State the missing test or assumption in each case.
    2. P1.2 — Hierarchical derivation. Write a top-down generative joint, a valid posterior factorization, and the layerwise ELBO for \(z_2,z_1,s_{1:T}\). For each KL term, identify its conditioning variables, conditional-information contribution, and aggregate prior-mismatch contribution. Mark the sequential and conditionally parallel sampling steps.
    3. P1.3 — Temporal-query audit. Draw the dependency graph for a global sequence code plus a Markov latent process. Specify filtering, fixed-lag smoothing, and full smoothing posteriors, and give a two-time-step example in which future evidence changes the posterior of the earlier state without changing the generative direction.
    4. P1.4 — Approximation taxonomy. Classify IWAE, a flow posterior, VampPrior, a diffusion prior, and structured amortization by the gap or system component each changes. State which objects alter the generative model, variational family, bound, gradient estimator, or separately trained downstream prior.
    5. P1.5 — Codec and systems study. Design a four-layer comparison covering reconstruction/rate, latent statistics and prior mismatch, downstream generation under matched compute, and encode/decode latency and memory. Include a critical-path table for global groups, frame states, flow inversion, and diffusion steps.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Transfer the design to irregular clinical time series. Choose a global code, latent process, or both; define the missing-observation likelihood and causal/offline inference query; and state the calibration, intervention, privacy, and systems evidence required for deployment.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Modern “VAE” systems range from probabilistic latent models to lightly KL-regularized codecs, VQ tokenizers, foundation-encoder adapters, sequence-level codes, latent stochastic processes, and hybrids with diffusion priors or decoders. The label does not identify the objective. A hierarchy or temporal model specifies conditional dependencies and a sampling critical path; it does not guarantee semantic scales, useful activity in every group, long-range memory, or calibrated forecast uncertainty. Layerwise and per-time KL terms combine conditional data information with prior mismatch, so pruning and collapse must be diagnosed through held-out criteria, residual statistics, gradients, and interventions. Importance weighting, flow posteriors, structured amortization, and learned priors address different approximation gaps, while tokenizer research must be evaluated through both reconstruction and downstream generation.

    Retrieval practice: modern latent systems
    1. Can a tighter IWAE bound weaken the inference-network gradient signal?
    2. Is a codec plus diffusion prior algebraically one VAE?
    3. What four layers should a tokenizer comparison report?
    4. Why can a foundation encoder improve downstream generation while worsening pixel reconstruction?
    5. Why does a top-down posterior factorization not prove that top-down inference is universally more expressive than bottom-up inference?
    6. What two quantities make up a data-averaged hierarchical group KL?
    7. When does a near-zero group KL become evidence of harmful pruning rather than legitimate unused capacity?
    8. Which condition permits parallel sampling inside a latent group?
    9. What does exact reconstruction imply about information-theoretic target sufficiency, and what does it not imply about a finite linear probe?
    10. Why can zero aggregate-posterior total correlation coexist with semantically entangled coordinates?
    11. How does a global sequence latent differ from a per-time latent process?
    12. Why can an offline smoother revise an earlier latent state when a filter cannot?

    Solutions. (1) Yes; larger \(K\) can tighten the bound while reducing inference-gradient signal-to-noise under standard estimators. (2) No; it is hybrid composition unless a particular joint variational objective establishes a stronger relation. (3) Reconstruction/rate, latent statistics, downstream generator results, and systems cost. (4) It may preserve semantic or invariant features useful to the prior while discarding low-level details rewarded by pixel metrics. (5) Both directions are chain-rule factorizations and can represent the exact posterior with unrestricted conditionals; architecture and optimization restrictions create the practical difference. (6) Conditional mutual information \(I(X;Z_\ell\mid Z_{>\ell})\) and conditional aggregate-posterior mismatch to the prior. (7) When matched controls show that better inference or optimization activates the group and improves the declared held-out model, generation, or task criterion. (8) Its coordinates must be conditionally independent, or otherwise sampleable in parallel, after their parent groups are known. (9) An unrestricted predictor can compose with the exact decoder, but the target may remain inaccessible to the declared linear adapter or finite sample. (10) A measure-preserving bijection can mix the intended factors while leaving the learned coordinates independent, as in the finite XOR example. (11) A global latent is drawn once and captures shared sequence variation; a latent process introduces stochastic state or innovations across time. Either may still use an autoregressive observation decoder. (12) The smoother conditions on later observations that are unavailable to a causal filter at the earlier time.
