    Optimization and Learning Dynamics for Generative Models¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Latent Variables and Inference
    • Objectives and Statistical Distances
    • Information, Coding, and Generative Models
    • Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Gradients and Geometry 1 — Gradient Taxonomy
    • Gradients and Geometry 2 — Natural Gradients

    A generative objective is a mathematical target. A gradient estimator is a random procedure for estimating a derivative of that target. An optimizer turns those estimates into parameter updates. A training process couples the optimizer to data sampling, latent sampling, competing players, numerical precision, distributed execution, and stopping rules. These layers are related, but none determines the others.

    This volume develops that separation from scalar examples to stochastic computation graphs, latent-variable bounds, adversarial games, black-box distribution search, and optimal transport. The recurring question is not merely whether a loss decreased. It is: which mathematical object was optimized, with which estimator, in which geometry, under which finite-compute protocol, and what does the resulting point imply about the generative model?

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1 and 2 establish derivative semantics and stochastic-gradient estimators. Read the scalar examples before the implementation patterns.

    Practitioner: Chapters 1–4 cover automatic differentiation, estimator diagnostics, optimizer state, game dynamics, EM, and variational training. Chapter 6 supplies the transport distinctions needed for WGANs, flow matching, and distributional losses.

    Research: read all six chapters, including biased discrete estimators, singular Fisher geometry, non-convergent games, generalized EM conditions, search-distribution collapse, entropic-transport bias, and the current structured-optimizer evidence boundary.

    Evidence convention

    Independently rederived identities and theorems under explicit assumptions are Established. Accepted archival results are Peer-reviewed research. Current conference submissions and workshop results are Preprint unless an official accepted record exists. An optimizer benchmark never becomes a family-wide recommendation without matched model, data, token or image budget, precision, hardware, and wall-clock accounting.

    Six layers of a learning system¤

    Changing a lower layer can change the effective learning problem. A stop-gradient changes the derivative program. A straight-through rule changes the estimator. Gradient accumulation changes the stochastic batch but need not equal a larger physical batch when stateful layers or clipping intervene. Alternating discriminator updates define a different discrete dynamical system from simultaneous updates. Mixed precision and loss scaling can change which updates survive numerically.

    1. Derivatives Are Programs with Contracts¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know the scalar chain rule and basic matrix multiplication. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish a derivative from an optimization update;
    • compute Jacobian-vector and vector-Jacobian products;
    • choose forward or reverse automatic differentiation from input/output dimensions;
    • explain what a stop-gradient does mathematically;
    • identify nondifferentiability, numerical, and mutation boundaries; and
    • audit a generative training step before treating its gradient as the derivative of the stated objective.

    Three things often called “backpropagation”¤

    For a program \(f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^m\), the derivative at \(x\) is a linear map \(J_f(x)\). Automatic differentiation (AD) evaluates products with that map by applying the chain rule to elementary program operations. Reverse-mode AD is the computational mechanism commonly called backpropagation when the output is scalar. The optimizer is a separate stateful algorithm that consumes the resulting gradient.

    Table OPT.2. Three things often called “backpropagation”
    Layer Example What it does not specify
    Objective negative ELBO how its expectation is estimated
    Derivative transform reverse-mode VJP learning rate or optimizer state
    Optimizer AdamW whether the supplied gradient is unbiased
    Training loop accumulation, clipping, EMA the population optimum

    This separation follows the established AD account of Baydin et al.: AD differentiates a numerical program, rather than symbolically simplifying an expression or approximating a derivative by finite differences.

    JVPs and VJPs¤

    The Jacobian-vector product propagates a tangent \(v\) forward:

    \[ \operatorname{JVP}_f(x;v)=J_f(x)v. \]

    Its cost is roughly one forward evaluation per tangent direction, independent of output dimension. Forward mode is therefore attractive when the number of differentiated inputs is small.

    The vector-Jacobian product propagates a cotangent \(u\) backward:

    \[ \operatorname{VJP}_f(x;u)=u^\top J_f(x). \]

    For scalar \(f\), choosing \(u=1\) returns \(\nabla f(x)^\top\). Reverse mode is attractive when a scalar loss depends on many parameters, which is the dominant neural-training shape.

    Worked scalar graph: value, derivative, and update¤

    Consider

    \[ J(\theta)=\tfrac12(\theta^2-a)^2, \]

    and define intermediate values \(u=\theta^2\), \(r=u-a\), and \(J=r^2/2\). At \(\theta=2\) and \(a=3\):

    \[ u=4,\qquad r=1,\qquad J=\tfrac12. \]

    Reverse accumulation starts with \(\bar J=1\) and gives

    \[ \bar r=r=1,\qquad \bar u=\bar r=1,\qquad \bar\theta=\bar u(2\theta)=4. \]

    Thus \(J'(2)=4\). Gradient descent with learning rate \(\eta=0.1\) would produce \(\theta^+=1.6\). The number \(4\) is the derivative; \(1.6\) is an optimizer-dependent next iterate. Momentum, clipping, weight decay, or a natural-gradient metric would produce other iterates from the same derivative.

    Detachment changes the derivative program¤

    Let \(\operatorname{sg}(u)\) return the value \(u\) in the forward pass but have derivative zero. Replacing \(r=\theta^2-a\) by

    \[ r=\theta^2-\operatorname{sg}(a) \]

    does nothing when \(a\) is a constant. Replacing a trainable occurrence,

    \[ J(\theta)=\theta\operatorname{sg}(\theta), \]

    preserves the forward value \(\theta^2\) but yields derivative \(\operatorname{sg}(\theta)=\theta\), not \(2\theta\). Stop-gradient is therefore not a memory hint. It defines a custom derivative and may sever terms required by the original objective.

    A correct forward pass does not imply a correct gradient

    Custom VJPs, gradient reversal, straight-through rules, implicit differentiation, and detached targets can all preserve forward values while changing derivatives. Their mathematical contract must state the derivative being computed and whether it remains exact, implicit, biased, or deliberately surrogate.

    Higher derivatives, implicit states, and memory¤

    A Hessian-vector product can be evaluated without materializing a dense Hessian by differentiating a gradient-direction product:

    \[ H_J(\theta)v=\nabla_\theta\bigl(\nabla_\theta J(\theta)^\top v\bigr). \]

    This supports curvature diagnostics, conjugate-gradient solves, and implicit differentiation. If \(z^*(\theta)\) is defined by a stationary equation \(G(z^*,\theta)=0\) and \(\partial G/\partial z\) is invertible locally, then

    \[ \frac{d z^*}{d\theta} =- \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial z}\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial G}{\partial\theta}. \]

    Unrolling an iterative solver and differentiating through every step instead computes the derivative of the finite algorithm. Implicit differentiation computes the derivative of a converged solution under regularity conditions. They agree only when the finite solver has reached the relevant fixed point and the derivative limit is valid.

    Reverse mode stores or reconstructs intermediate values. Rematerialization or gradient checkpointing trades extra forward computation for lower activation memory. It should preserve the mathematical gradient for deterministic recomputation, but random-number reuse, mutable state, data-dependent control flow, and nondeterministic kernels can break exact replay.

    Nondifferentiability and floating-point boundaries¤

    A ReLU, maximum, quantizer, categorical sample, sorting operation, or constrained projection may be nondifferentiable on some inputs. Software chooses a subgradient, returns zero, defines a custom rule, or refuses differentiation. None of these choices proves that the resulting update optimizes the desired discrete or constrained objective.

    Numerical differentiation also has a contract. Central finite differences,

    \[ \frac{J(\theta+h v)-J(\theta-h v)}{2h}, \]

    are valuable for testing directional derivatives, but truncation error dominates for large \(h\) and floating-point cancellation dominates for very small \(h\). A gradient check should sweep several scales and compare a directional product, not demand bitwise equality from every coordinate.

    Generative-model audit¤

    Before trusting an AD result, ask:

    1. Is the differentiated scalar the intended objective, including every reduction and sign?
    2. Which random samples are held fixed during differentiation?
    3. Are target-network, teacher, critic, or sampler parameters intentionally detached?
    4. Does a custom derivative preserve the true derivative or introduce a surrogate?
    5. Are the forward and backward dtypes sufficient for the relevant scale?
    6. Does rematerialization replay the same randomness and state?
    7. Are constrained parameters represented by a differentiable parameterization or projected after the update?

    Chapter 1 synthesis and retrieval¤

    AD evaluates derivatives of programs. JVPs and VJPs expose the same Jacobian through different products. Reverse mode is efficient for scalar neural losses, but a detached or custom-gradient program may have a derivative different from the visible forward expression. Optimizer state, clipping, precision, and distributed execution enter only after the derivative contract is known.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why is backpropagation not an optimizer?
    2. For \(f:\mathbb R^{10^9}\to\mathbb R\), which AD direction is usually appropriate?
    3. What derivative does \(\theta\operatorname{sg}(\theta)\) have?
    4. When can differentiating through a finite solver disagree with implicit differentiation?

    Solutions. (1) Backpropagation is reverse accumulation of derivatives; an optimizer separately maps those derivatives and its state to an update. (2) Reverse mode, because one cotangent sweep yields the scalar gradient. (3) \(\theta\), because the detached occurrence contributes zero derivative. (4) When the finite solver has not converged, the fixed point is not locally regular, randomness or state differs, or differentiation and the solver limit cannot be exchanged.

    Bridge. Exact derivatives of deterministic programs are only part of generative learning. Chapter 2 asks how to differentiate expectations over data, latent variables, and discrete choices.

    2. Stochastic Gradient Estimators¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand expectations, conditional probability, and Chapter 1. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • derive score-function and pathwise estimators;
    • state the regularity conditions hidden by moving derivatives through integrals;
    • construct valid baselines and control variates;
    • distinguish unbiasedness, consistency, variance, and mean-squared error;
    • explain the objective changed by continuous relaxations and straight-through rules; and
    • select diagnostics for data, latent, timestep, and multi-particle sampling.

    The central problem¤

    Many generative objectives have the form

    \[ J(\theta)=\mathbb E_{Z\sim q_\theta}[f_\theta(Z)]. \]

    Two sources of parameter dependence appear: the integrand \(f_\theta\) and the law \(q_\theta\). Interchanging differentiation and integration requires regularity—for example, differentiability almost everywhere and an integrable local dominating function. Writing \(\nabla\mathbb E=\mathbb E\nabla\) without such conditions can fail when support moves or tails do not dominate the derivative.

    The review by Mohamed et al. organizes Monte Carlo gradient estimation around pathwise, score-function, and measure-valued constructions. Stochastic computation graphs make the dependency and credit-assignment structure explicit (Schulman et al.).

    Score-function estimator¤

    When \(q_\theta\) has parameter-independent support and differentiation is valid,

    \[ \begin{aligned} \nabla_\theta J(\theta) &=\int \nabla_\theta\bigl(q_\theta(z)f_\theta(z)\bigr)\,dz\\ &=\mathbb E_{q_\theta} \left[f_\theta(Z)\nabla_\theta\log q_\theta(Z) +\nabla_\theta f_\theta(Z)\right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The first term is the likelihood-ratio or score-function estimator. It applies to discrete choices because it does not differentiate a sampled category. Its variance can be severe when delayed costs are multiplied by long score sums.

    A baseline is valid because a term has zero expectation¤

    For a constant \(b\) independent of the sampled \(Z\),

    \[ \mathbb E_{q_\theta} \left[b\nabla_\theta\log q_\theta(Z)\right] =b\nabla_\theta\int q_\theta(z)\,dz=0. \]

    Thus \(f(Z)\) can be replaced by \(f(Z)-b\) without biasing the score term. In a structured stochastic graph, a baseline may depend on variables that do not descend from the scored random choice, subject to the corresponding conditional zero-mean argument. An arbitrary learned function of downstream samples is not automatically a valid baseline. A general control variate must have known zero expectation or must add its analytically known expectation back.

    Pathwise estimator¤

    Suppose \(Z=g_\theta(\epsilon)\) for parameter-free \(\epsilon\sim s\). Then

    \[ J(\theta)=\mathbb E_{\epsilon\sim s} \left[f_\theta(g_\theta(\epsilon))\right] \]

    and ordinary differentiation through \(g_\theta\) gives

    \[ \nabla_\theta J =\mathbb E_{\epsilon} \left[ \nabla_z f_\theta(z)\big|_{z=g_\theta(\epsilon)} \nabla_\theta g_\theta(\epsilon) +\nabla_\theta f_\theta(g_\theta(\epsilon)) \right]. \]

    This is the VAE reparameterization pattern. It often has lower variance than a score estimator because it uses local derivative information, but no universal variance ordering exists. Discontinuous integrands, heavy tails, ill-conditioned transforms, or parameter-dependent support can reverse the comparison.

    Worked Gaussian comparison¤

    Let \(Z\sim\mathcal N(\theta,1)\) and \(f(Z)=Z^2\). The exact objective is \(J(\theta)=\theta^2+1\), so \(J'(\theta)=2\theta\).

    With \(Z=\theta+\epsilon\), the pathwise one-sample estimator is

    \[ g_{\mathrm{PW}}=2Z, \qquad \mathbb E[g_{\mathrm{PW}}]=2\theta, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(g_{\mathrm{PW}})=4. \]

    The score estimator is

    \[ g_{\mathrm{SF}}=Z^2(Z-\theta). \]

    At \(\theta=0\), both are unbiased, but \(g_{\mathrm{PW}}=2\epsilon\) has variance \(4\), while \(g_{\mathrm{SF}}=\epsilon^3\) has variance \(\mathbb E[\epsilon^6]=15\). This example demonstrates a variance reduction, not a theorem that pathwise estimators always win.

    Worked discrete estimator: unbiased score, biased straight-through¤

    Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(p)\) and \(f(Z)=Z^2\). Because \(Z^2=Z\) on the discrete support,

    \[ J(p)=\mathbb E[Z^2]=p, \qquad \frac{dJ}{dp}=1. \]

    The score estimator is

    \[ g_{\mathrm{SF}} =Z^2\left(\frac{Z}{p}-\frac{1-Z}{1-p}\right), \]

    whose expectation is one. A straight-through rule that treats the hard sample as if \(dZ/dp=1\) differentiates \(Z^2\) as \(2Z\), giving expectation \(2p\). It is exact only at the accidental value \(p=1/2\) and is biased elsewhere.

    This is the essential straight-through contract: the forward pass uses a discrete or hard operation, while the backward pass substitutes a chosen surrogate derivative. The common identity backward rule supplies derivative one; it does not replace the derivative by the function \(g(x)=x\). The estimator may be practically useful, but it is not the derivative of the original discrete expectation unless separately proved.

    Continuous relaxations¤

    Gumbel–Softmax and Concrete variables replace a categorical vertex with a differentiable simplex-valued sample at temperature \(\tau>0\) (Jang et al., Maddison et al.). At finite temperature, pathwise differentiation targets the relaxed objective. As \(\tau\) decreases, samples become closer to vertices, but derivatives can become ill-conditioned and the finite-temperature optimization path need not converge to a good discrete solution.

    A hard forward sample paired with a soft backward pass adds a straight-through bias. REBAR combines score and relaxation terms to obtain an unbiased control-variate estimator under its construction (Tucker et al.). RELAX, Rao–Blackwellization, antithetic estimators, and later variants explore the same bias–variance–cost design space. Their benchmark ranking depends on category count, number of particles, model depth, relaxation temperature, and compute.

    Sampling axes in generative training¤

    One minibatch gradient may average randomness from:

    • data records or sequences;
    • data augmentation and token masking;
    • latent variables or importance particles;
    • diffusion times, noise levels, and perturbations;
    • negative samples or contrastive reference draws;
    • critic or discriminator minibatches;
    • Monte Carlo trajectories and MCMC states; and
    • distributed workers and reduced-precision arithmetic.

    Variance should be decomposed or ablated across these axes. Increasing latent particles while keeping data examples fixed answers a different question from increasing the data batch. Reusing common random numbers can reduce the variance of a difference but correlates estimates. Antithetic sampling helps only when the coupling induces useful negative correlation.

    Bias, variance, and optimization are separate¤

    For estimator \(g\) of target gradient \(\mu\),

    \[ \mathbb E\lVert g-\mu\rVert^2 =\lVert\mathbb E[g]-\mu\rVert^2 +\operatorname{tr}\operatorname{Cov}(g). \]

    An unbiased estimator can be unusably noisy. A biased estimator can give a lower mean-squared error and faster finite-time optimization. Neither fact licenses calling the biased estimate exact. Report gradient cost, number of samples, bias evidence, variance, downstream objective, and wall-clock jointly.

    Chapter 2 synthesis and retrieval¤

    Score estimators differentiate probability mass and work for discrete samples. Pathwise estimators differentiate a parameterized sample transformation. Baselines and control variates preserve the target only through explicit zero-expectation identities. Relaxations and straight-through rules may change the objective or derivative. The correct choice is a bias–variance–cost decision tied to the full stochastic graph.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why can a score estimator handle a categorical sample?
    2. What makes a baseline valid?
    3. Which objective does a finite-temperature Gumbel–Softmax pathwise gradient target?
    4. In the Bernoulli example, why is the identity straight-through estimate biased?

    Solutions. (1) It differentiates the log probability of the realized category rather than the category value. (2) Its score-weighted contribution has zero conditional expectation, or a control variate's known expectation is restored. (3) The continuously relaxed objective at that temperature. (4) The true derivative is one, whereas the surrogate estimate has expectation \(2p\).

    Bridge. Chapter 2 supplied gradients. Chapter 3 studies how update geometry and coupled players turn those gradients into trajectories that may converge, cycle, or diverge.

    3. Optimizer Geometry and Coupled Learning Dynamics¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand gradients, covariance, and eigenvalues. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish update direction, preconditioner, regularizer, and parameter averaging;
    • compute a natural-gradient step for a finite model;
    • state when Fisher, generalized Gauss–Newton, Hessian, and empirical Fisher do or do not coincide;
    • diagnose gradient clipping and weight decay by their actual operations;
    • analyze a bilinear two-player game; and
    • bound claims about structured optimizers by their model and systems evidence.

    An optimizer defines a dynamical system¤

    Plain stochastic gradient descent has

    \[ \theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\eta_t g_t. \]

    Momentum adds a state variable; adaptive methods rescale coordinates; matrix methods transform blocks; exponential moving averages create evaluation parameters distinct from training parameters. Even with identical \(g_t\), these rules define different trajectories.

    For heavy-ball momentum,

    \[ m_t=\beta m_{t-1}+g_t, \qquad \theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-\eta m_t. \]

    Adam-type methods maintain first and second raw-moment estimates and apply bias correction. AdamW applies decoupled parameter shrinkage in addition to the adaptive gradient update. Adding \(\lambda\lVert\theta\rVert^2/2\) to the objective and using ordinary weight decay coincide for SGD under a simple parameterization, but they generally do not coincide after coordinate-wise adaptive preconditioning.

    Clipping has multiple meanings¤

    Global-norm clipping computes the norm over the complete gradient tree,

    \[ \lVert g\rVert_2 =\sqrt{\sum_k\lVert g^{(k)}\rVert_F^2}, \qquad \widetilde g=g\min\left(1,\frac{c}{\lVert g\rVert_2+\delta}\right). \]

    Per-tensor norm clipping, coordinate clipping, adaptive gradient clipping, and clipping after worker reduction are different operations. Clipping each shard before all-reduce is not generally equivalent to clipping the reduced global gradient. Gradient clipping also biases a stochastic gradient unless the clipping event has special symmetry. It is a stability device, not a proof that the original expected gradient is preserved.

    Geometry from a local distributional constraint¤

    Suppose parameters define a regular family \(p_\theta\). A small parameter change has local KL expansion

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\theta\|p_{\theta+\Delta}) &=\tfrac12\Delta^\top F(\theta)\Delta\\ &\quad+o(\lVert\Delta\rVert^2), \end{aligned} \]

    where

    \[ \begin{aligned} F(\theta) &=\mathbb E_{X\sim p_\theta}\!\left[ s_\theta(X)s_\theta(X)^\top \right],\\ s_\theta(X)&=\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(X). \end{aligned} \]

    The Fisher matrix is positive semidefinite. It can be singular when parameters are redundant or the model is locally unidentifiable. Linearizing \(J\) and constraining the local KL yields

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Delta^* &=-\alpha F^\dagger g,\\ \alpha &=\sqrt{\frac{2\epsilon}{g^\top F^\dagger g}}. \end{aligned} \]

    on the identifiable tangent space, provided the gradient lies in the relevant range. A damped practical step replaces the pseudoinverse by a solve with \(F+\lambda I\), which improves conditioning but changes the exact geometry.

    This construction is the natural gradient introduced by Amari. Exact invariance concerns a smooth reparameterization of the same statistical manifold and an exact metric-aware step in the infinitesimal limit. Finite learning rates, damping, block approximations, coordinate exclusions, and optimizer state weaken or destroy that guarantee.

    The later Gradients and Geometry in Generative Modeling volume applies this foundation separately to parameter, state, game, implicit, functional, natural, and search gradients across model families. It is placed after the family volumes because those applications require choosing among several inequivalent objectives and probability laws inside one system.

    Worked Bernoulli natural gradient¤

    Let \(p=\sigma(\theta)\) be a Bernoulli success probability and minimize cross-entropy to a target success frequency \(r\):

    \[ J(\theta)=-r\log p-(1-r)\log(1-p). \]

    The ordinary logit gradient is \(g_\theta=p-r\). The Fisher information in logit coordinates is \(F_\theta=p(1-p)\). Hence the natural direction is

    \[ F_\theta^{-1}g_\theta=\frac{p-r}{p(1-p)}. \]

    Because \(dp/d\theta=p(1-p)\), a small natural step in \(\theta\) induces

    \[ \Delta p\approx p(1-p)\Delta\theta=-\eta(p-r). \]

    The geometry removes the sigmoid's local coordinate scaling. Near \(p=0\) or \(1\), however, \(F\) becomes nearly singular and an undamped finite step can explode. The local metric derivation does not justify arbitrary step sizes.

    Fisher, generalized Gauss–Newton, Hessian, and empirical Fisher¤

    These matrices must not be used as synonyms:

    • the Fisher averages model score outer products with outcomes drawn from the model distribution;
    • the empirical Fisher commonly averages score outer products at observed training outcomes;
    • the generalized Gauss–Newton matrix pulls back output-loss curvature through the network Jacobian; and
    • the Hessian differentiates the complete scalar objective twice and can be indefinite.

    Under suitable conditional exponential-family likelihoods and matching loss conventions, Fisher and generalized Gauss–Newton structures can coincide. They do not coincide for arbitrary objectives. The empirical Fisher is generally neither the Fisher nor a consistent Hessian surrogate away from special conditions, as demonstrated by Kunstner, Balles, and Hennig. Martens gives the precise second-order and invariance connections and their approximation limits.

    A singular metric is information, not just a numerical nuisance

    If two parameter directions define the same local distribution, Fisher has a null direction. Adding damping makes a solve possible but does not make the statistical model identifiable. Report whether singularity comes from symmetry, redundant parameterization, saturation, insufficient support, or finite-sample approximation.

    Two-player objectives are not scalar minimization¤

    A GAN often defines

    \[ \min_\theta\max_\phi V(\theta,\phi). \]

    Its simultaneous gradient vector field is

    \[ G(\theta,\phi) =\begin{bmatrix} \nabla_\theta V\\ -\nabla_\phi V \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Convergence depends on the Jacobian of this vector field, not merely on a scalar loss trace. Alternating updates, unequal learning rates, multiple critic steps, gradient penalties, and stale distributed parameters define different games.

    Worked bilinear game: why gradients rotate¤

    Take \(V(x,y)=xy\) with \(min_x\max_y\). Simultaneous descent–ascent gives

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}x_{t+1}\\y_{t+1}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1&-\eta\\\eta&1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}x_t\\y_t\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The squared radius evolves as

    \[ x_{t+1}^2+y_{t+1}^2 =(1+\eta^2)(x_t^2+y_t^2), \]

    so every positive fixed step spirals outward even though \((0,0)\) is the saddle. Loss oscillation here is structural, not ordinary minibatch noise.

    An extragradient step first evaluates a look-ahead point,

    \[ \widetilde x=x-\eta y, \qquad \widetilde y=y+\eta x, \]

    then updates with the look-ahead field:

    \[ x^+=x-\eta\widetilde y=(1-\eta^2)x-\eta y, \qquad y^+=y+\eta\widetilde x=\eta x+(1-\eta^2)y. \]

    Its radius multiplier is \(1-\eta^2+\eta^4\), below one for \(0<\eta<1\). This finite example explains why anticipatory or consensus-like methods can damp rotational components. It does not establish global convergence for a nonlinear nonconvex–nonconcave GAN.

    Mescheder, Geiger, and Nowozin show that unregularized GAN training need not converge when data and generator distributions lie on lower-dimensional manifolds, and that commonly used WGAN or WGAN-GP updates with finitely many discriminator steps are not automatically convergent. A successful critic-loss curve is therefore not a general convergence certificate.

    Structured and matrix optimizers: evidence boundary¤

    Tensor- or matrix-aware preconditioners use parameter shape instead of treating every coordinate independently. Shampoo maintains per-axis preconditioners and has stochastic-convex theory plus neural experiments (Gupta, Koren, and Singer). SOAP runs an Adam-like update in a Shampoo-derived eigenbasis and reports large-batch language-model gains in its accepted study (Vyas et al.). Muon-family methods orthogonalize matrix updates; MuonBP studies block-periodic orthogonalization for distributed throughput, and Muon Optimizes Under Spectral Norm Constraints analyzes a spectral-norm-constrained interpretation. These are peer-reviewed results on their declared protocols.

    These results expand the optimizer design space, but they do not establish a universal successor to AdamW. Evidence must name parameter shapes, exclusions for embeddings or scalars, batch regime, width scaling, precision, communication topology, step and token budget, wall-clock, memory, and validation target. The SPlus whitening-optimizer preprint includes diffusion training among its controlled objectives; it remains preprint evidence and should be labeled as such.

    Practical trajectory diagnostics¤

    Track at least:

    • population proxy and held-out objective, not training loss alone;
    • gradient and update norms before and after clipping, by parameter group;
    • effective step ratio \(\lVert\Delta\theta\rVert/(\lVert\theta\rVert+\delta)\);
    • overflow, skipped-update, and loss-scale histories;
    • estimator variance across data, latent, and timestep axes;
    • critic and generator vector-field or Jacobian diagnostics on controlled small models;
    • EMA and live-parameter evaluation separately; and
    • steps, examples or tokens, FLOPs, wall-clock, memory, and energy under matched stopping rules.

    Chapter 3 synthesis and retrieval¤

    An optimizer is a stateful dynamical system. Natural gradients use a distributional metric that can be singular; approximate curvature and the empirical Fisher must retain their own definitions. Coupled games can rotate even when each player follows its gradient. Structured optimizers are promising empirical designs whose claims remain protocol-specific.

    4. Bound Optimization, EM, and Latent-Variable Learning¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand latent-variable marginalization and the ELBO. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • derive EM as coordinate optimization of a free-energy bound;
    • distinguish exact E-steps, generalized M-steps, variational EM, Monte Carlo EM, and amortized inference;
    • state the monotonicity and convergence conclusions each version supports;
    • compute one complete finite-mixture update;
    • explain why posterior mean imputation loses uncertainty; and
    • diagnose boundary collapse and non-identifiability in latent generative models.

    One identity organizes the chapter¤

    For observed \(x\), latent \(z\), parameters \(\theta\), and any distribution \(q(z)\) with compatible support,

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\underbrace{ \mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x,z)]+H(q) }_{\mathcal F(q,\theta)} +D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q(z)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)\bigr). \]

    The free energy \(\mathcal F\) is a lower bound because the KL term is nonnegative. Classical EM alternates:

    1. E-step: set \(q^{t+1}(z)=p_{\theta^t}(z\mid x)\), making the bound tight at \(\theta^t\);
    2. M-step: choose \(\theta^{t+1}\) that maximizes \(\mathcal F(q^{t+1},\theta)\).

    This coordinate view, developed clearly by Neal and Hinton, separates the posterior update from the parameter update.

    Why exact EM is monotone¤

    After the E-step,

    \[ \mathcal F(q^{t+1},\theta^t)=\log p_{\theta^t}(x). \]

    An exact M-step gives

    \[ \mathcal F(q^{t+1},\theta^{t+1}) \geq \mathcal F(q^{t+1},\theta^t). \]

    Finally,

    \[ \log p_{\theta^{t+1}}(x) \geq \mathcal F(q^{t+1},\theta^{t+1}). \]

    Combining the three inequalities proves nondecreasing observed-data likelihood for exact EM under the stated support and optimization conditions (Dempster, Laird, and Rubin).

    It does not prove convergence to a local maximum. Under regularity conditions, limit points can be stationary; saddles, boundary points, non-isolated ridges, and singularities remain possible. Wu's convergence analysis states the additional compactness, continuity, differentiability, or unimodality conditions required for stronger conclusions.

    Worked two-component Bernoulli mixture¤

    Let a latent class \(Z\in\{1,2\}\) have weights \(\pi_1=\pi_2=1/2\). Conditional on \(Z=k\), an observation \(X\in\{0,1\}\) is Bernoulli with \(\mu_1=0.2\) and \(\mu_2=0.8\). For data \(x=(1,1,0)\), responsibilities are

    \[ r_{nk} =\frac{\pi_k\mu_k^{x_n}(1-\mu_k)^{1-x_n}} {\sum_j\pi_j\mu_j^{x_n}(1-\mu_j)^{1-x_n}}. \]

    For each observed one, \((r_{n1},r_{n2})=(0.2,0.8)\). For the observed zero, the responsibilities are \((0.8,0.2)\). Effective component counts are therefore

    \[ N_1=1.2, \qquad N_2=1.8. \]

    The M-step gives

    \[ \pi_1^+=0.4, \qquad \pi_2^+=0.6, \]

    and

    \[ \mu_1^+=\frac{0.2+0.2}{1.2}=\frac13, \qquad \mu_2^+=\frac{0.8+0.8}{1.8}=\frac89. \]

    These fractional sufficient statistics are posterior expectations. Assigning every point to its most likely class would define hard EM or classification EM, a different algorithm with different monotonicity semantics.

    Posterior expectation is not mean imputation¤

    For a Gaussian complete-data term, sufficient statistics can include both \(Z\) and \(ZZ^\top\). If

    \[ m=\mathbb E[Z\mid x], \qquad S=\operatorname{Cov}(Z\mid x), \]

    then

    \[ \mathbb E[ZZ^\top\mid x]=S+mm^\top. \]

    Replacing a missing or latent variable by its posterior mean keeps only \(mm^\top\) and discards \(S\). Mean imputation therefore does not generally implement the E-step. This distinction matters in factor analysis, linear dynamical systems, missing-data Gaussians, and latent scientific models.

    Generalized, variational, stochastic, and Monte Carlo EM¤

    Table OPT.3. Generalized, variational, stochastic, and Monte Carlo EM
    Method E-like step M-like step Guaranteed quantity under conditions
    Exact EM exact posterior exact maximization nondecreasing observed likelihood
    Generalized EM exact posterior any step increasing the bound nondecreasing observed likelihood
    Variational EM optimize within restricted \(q\) family optimize bound nondecreasing chosen ELBO, not necessarily likelihood
    Monte Carlo EM sampled approximation to E statistics maximization of noisy surrogate needs increasing accuracy or specific stochastic conditions
    Stochastic-approximation EM recursive sufficient-statistic estimate parameter update convergence under step-size, stability, and model conditions
    Amortized variational training learned \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) joint or alternating gradient updates finite optimized ELBO; inference and optimization gaps remain

    An inexact or stochastic step can reduce the monitored likelihood without implying an error in the EM identity. The finite approximation, optimizer, numerical precision, or estimator may have violated the monotone-coordinate conditions. Conversely, a monotone ELBO can improve while true likelihood falls if the variational gap changes.

    MM is the larger pattern¤

    Minorize–maximize constructs \(G(\theta\mid\theta^t)\) such that

    \[ G(\theta\mid\theta^t)\leq J(\theta), \qquad G(\theta^t\mid\theta^t)=J(\theta^t), \]

    for a maximization objective. Maximizing the surrogate ensures \(J(\theta^{t+1})\geq J(\theta^t)\). For minimization, majorization reverses the inequality. EM is an MM instance when the latent posterior constructs the tangent lower bound to the log likelihood.

    The word “bound” is incomplete without its direction and optimized quantity. A lower bound is useful for maximizing a target; an upper bound is useful for minimizing one. A local quadratic approximation need not majorize or minorize globally.

    Failure cases that the likelihood trace can hide¤

    • Mixture singularity: a Gaussian component can collapse onto one data point with variance approaching zero and likelihood diverging.
    • Label symmetry: permuting component labels gives equivalent parameters, so coordinate convergence is not identifiability.
    • Dead component: near-zero responsibility can freeze a component without showing a numerical error.
    • Posterior collapse: a powerful decoder can make the latent posterior match the prior while achieving a good variational objective.
    • Amortization gap: the inference network may fail to reach the best member of its own variational family for each observation.
    • Approximation gap: even the best restricted \(q\) may be far from the exact posterior.

    Priors, variance floors, constrained parameterizations, component-splitting diagnostics, multiple initializations, held-out likelihood estimates, posterior predictive checks, and per-datapoint inference refinement address different failure modes.

    Chapter 4 synthesis and retrieval¤

    EM is coordinate optimization of a latent-variable free-energy bound. Exact E and M steps yield monotone likelihood, while convergence to a maximum needs additional conditions. Variational and Monte Carlo variants optimize or estimate different finite quantities. Posterior expectations preserve uncertainty that point imputation discards.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why does exact EM increase observed likelihood?
    2. Does monotone likelihood imply convergence to a local maximum?
    3. Which quantity is monotone in restricted variational EM?
    4. Why is posterior mean imputation insufficient for Gaussian latent statistics?

    Solutions. (1) The E-step makes a lower bound tight at the old parameters, the M-step raises that bound, and likelihood lies above it. (2) No; limit points may be stationary, singular, boundary, or non-isolated unless stronger assumptions hold. (3) The selected ELBO under successful coordinate improvement. (4) It drops the posterior covariance term in \(\mathbb E[ZZ^\top\mid x]\).

    5. Search Distributions, Black-Box Objectives, and Structured Selection¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand sampling, maximum likelihood, and finite optimization. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish a search distribution from a learned data distribution;
    • derive the elite update used by an estimation-of-distribution or cross-entropy method;
    • explain premature collapse and smoothing;
    • place evolution strategies and Bayesian optimization within generative workflows without treating them as model families;
    • state the assumptions behind greedy submodular guarantees; and
    • separate diversity models such as determinantal point processes from arbitrary subset heuristics.

    When the objective is a query-only function¤

    Let \(R(a)\) score a design, prompt, molecule, simulator configuration, sampler schedule, or generated candidate \(a\). The score may be nondifferentiable, supplied by a physical experiment, or accessible only through a costly evaluation. A black-box optimizer chooses queries; it does not automatically define a probabilistic model of the data that originally trained the generator.

    Three layers often coexist:

    The base generator may be a language model, diffusion model, flow, GFlowNet, or simulator. The search distribution may adapt toward high-reward regions. A final selector may enforce diversity or constraints. Only a declared probability law over outputs makes any one of these a probabilistic generator.

    Estimation-of-distribution algorithms¤

    An estimation-of-distribution algorithm (EDA) maintains \(q_\psi(a)\), samples candidates, evaluates them, and fits \(q_\psi\) to a selected or weighted subset. The cross-entropy method is a prominent instance: choose a reward threshold \(\gamma_t\), define elite indicators

    \[ w_i=\mathbf 1\{R(a_i)\geq\gamma_t\}, \]

    and update

    \[ \psi_{t+1} \in\arg\max_\psi \sum_i w_i\log q_\psi(a_i). \]

    For a general exponential family this is a weighted maximum-likelihood fit. The family need not be Gaussian. Continuous Gaussian CEM, categorical EDAs, autoregressive search models, and structured distributions retain different dependence assumptions.

    Worked Bernoulli elite update¤

    Suppose candidates are three-bit strings sampled from independent Bernoulli coordinates with initial probabilities \((1/2,1/2,1/2)\). Four sampled candidates have rewards equal to their number of ones:

    Table OPT.4. Worked Bernoulli elite update
    Candidate Reward
    100 1
    110 2
    011 2
    111 3

    Keeping the two highest-reward candidates \(011\) and \(111\) gives maximum-likelihood coordinate probabilities

    \[ \widehat p^+=(1/2,1,1). \]

    After one update, the second and third coordinates have collapsed and cannot explore zero again. With smoothing

    \[ p^+=(1-\alpha)p+\alpha\widehat p^+, \]

    and \(\alpha=1/2\), the update is \((1/2,3/4,3/4)\). Smoothing delays collapse but does not guarantee global exploration. Probability floors, entropy regularization, mixture search families, restart rules, and importance weighting encode different exploration choices.

    Relation to reward-proportional generation¤

    Elite fitting concentrates on an upper level set defined by a changing threshold. It does not generally sample terminal objects in proportion to a fixed reward. GFlowNets instead target a reward-proportional terminal distribution through flow constraints. Reward-weighted regression, the cross-entropy method, best-of-\(N\) selection, rejection sampling, and GFlowNet training can share samples or components without being algebraically equivalent.

    Evolution strategies¤

    Evolution strategies optimize the parameters of a search distribution using ranked or weighted perturbations. For Gaussian search \(\theta+\sigma\epsilon\), \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\), a smoothed objective

    \[ J_\sigma(\theta)=\mathbb E_\epsilon[R(\theta+\sigma\epsilon)] \]

    has score-style gradient

    \[ \nabla_\theta J_\sigma(\theta) =\frac1\sigma\mathbb E_\epsilon \left[R(\theta+\sigma\epsilon)\epsilon\right] \]

    under the usual regularity conditions. This is a gradient of the Gaussian-smoothed reward, not necessarily the original nonsmooth function.

    CMA-ES adapts covariance and step size using evolution paths and selected perturbations (Hansen and Ostermeier). Natural evolution strategies also use a Fisher geometry on a search distribution. The methods are related, but CMA-ES is not simply identical to one generic natural-gradient update; its cumulation, rank weighting, covariance adaptation, and step-size control define a specific algorithm.

    Bayesian optimization is a decision loop, not a generator replacement¤

    Bayesian optimization maintains a surrogate posterior over a costly objective and uses an acquisition rule to choose the next query. In a generative workflow it can tune a small number of expensive hyperparameters, calibrate a simulator, or search a costly sampling schedule. Its surrogate uncertainty concerns the black-box objective under its model assumptions; it is not the generative model's predictive uncertainty.

    High-dimensional neural parameters are usually a poor direct Bayesian-optimization domain. The method is most defensible when the query budget is small, each evaluation is expensive, the search dimension and conditional structure are controlled, and acquisition optimization is itself audited.

    Submodular selection¤

    A set function \(F:2^V\to\mathbb R\) is submodular when it has diminishing returns:

    \[ F(A\cup\{e\})-F(A) \geq F(B\cup\{e\})-F(B) \]

    whenever \(A\subseteq B\) and \(e\notin B\). For a normalized, monotone, submodular \(F\) under a cardinality constraint \(|S|\leq k\), greedy selection achieves at least \(1-1/e\) of the optimum (Nemhauser, Wolsey, and Fisher). Remove monotonicity, normalization, exact marginal evaluation, or the cardinality constraint, and the guarantee changes.

    In generative modeling, a submodular surrogate may select a diverse prompt set, training subset, evaluation panel, or generated batch. Its approximation guarantee applies to the declared set function, not automatically to downstream FID, likelihood, scientific discovery, or human utility.

    Determinantal point processes¤

    A finite \(L\)-ensemble determinantal point process (DPP) assigns subset probability

    \[ P(Y=S)=\frac{\det L_S}{\det(I+L)}, \]

    for positive-semidefinite \(L\). Determinants favor subsets whose feature vectors span volume, combining quality and repulsion. DPPs are genuine probability models with exact normalization, marginals, conditioning, and sampling for the finite formulation—not merely a diversity penalty (Kulesza and Taskar).

    A log-determinant set objective can be submodular under suitable conditions, but DPP sampling, MAP subset selection, and greedy maximization are different queries. More generally, a log-submodular energy may have tractable MAP structure while its partition function and marginal probabilities remain difficult.

    Current generative uses and evidence boundaries¤

    Black-box gradient estimators appear in reward fine-tuning of diffusion chains, sampler-schedule search, simulator calibration, and molecular design. The RLR preprint proposes a recursive likelihood-ratio optimizer for diffusion fine-tuning; its unbiasedness and variance claims belong to its stated chain rearrangement and assumptions, and independent reproduction remains a separate question. The HSO diffusion schedule-search preprint reports low-NFE gains from a hierarchical search procedure; those are sampler-, dataset-, metric-, and implementation-specific claims, not a theorem about schedule optimization.

    Audit protocol for black-box generative optimization¤

    Report:

    1. the base generator and whether it is updated;
    2. the black-box score, noise, calibration, and failure susceptibility;
    3. query count, parallelism, wall-clock, and hardware;
    4. search distribution, support, floors, smoothing, and restart rules;
    5. reuse of training, validation, or evaluation data;
    6. selection-induced optimism and held-out reevaluation;
    7. diversity and constraint semantics; and
    8. whether the returned object is one optimum, a set, or samples from a target law.

    Chapter 5 synthesis and retrieval¤

    Search distributions allocate costly evaluations. EDAs and CEM fit selected candidates and can collapse prematurely. Evolution strategies differentiate a smoothed search objective or adapt a distribution through specialized rules. Bayesian optimization models the black-box score, not the generated-data law. Submodular and DPP constructions supply structured selection or probabilistic diversity only under explicit assumptions.

    6. Optimal Transport as an Objective and a Construction¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand probability measures, couplings, and change of variables. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish assignment, Monge maps, Kantorovich couplings, and dynamic transport;
    • compute a finite optimal coupling;
    • state existence and moment conditions for Wasserstein distances;
    • derive the one-dimensional quantile formula and separate \(W_1\) from the Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic;
    • explain entropic bias, Sinkhorn stabilization, and Sinkhorn divergence; and
    • connect transport to GANs, flows, diffusion, and probability paths without claiming finite-algorithm equivalence.

    Transport begins with a cost and two marginals¤

    Let \(P\) live on \(\mathcal X\), \(Q\) on \(\mathcal Y\), and \(c:\mathcal X\times\mathcal Y\to[0,\infty]\) be a measurable cost. A coupling \(\pi\in\Pi(P,Q)\) is a joint law with marginals \(P\) and \(Q\). The Kantorovich problem is

    \[ \operatorname{OT}_c(P,Q) =\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(P,Q)} \int c(x,y)\,d\pi(x,y). \]

    This formulation can split mass. It asks for an optimal joint law, not necessarily a deterministic generator.

    The Monge problem instead searches for a map \(T\) satisfying \(T_\#P=Q\):

    \[ \inf_{T:T_\#P=Q} \int c(x,T(x))\,dP(x). \]

    A Monge map need not exist. If \(P=\delta_0\) and \(Q=(\delta_{-1}+\delta_1)/2\), every deterministic \(T\) sends the single source atom to one point, so it cannot produce \(Q\). A Kantorovich coupling can split that source mass between both targets.

    Change of variables has a fixed direction¤

    If \(P\) has density \(p\), \(T\) is a differentiable bijection, and \(Q=T_\#P\) has density \(q\), then

    \[ q(T(x))\left|\det J_T(x)\right|=p(x), \]

    or equivalently

    \[ q(T(x))=\frac{p(x)}{|\det J_T(x)|}. \]

    Multiplying \(p(x)\) by the forward Jacobian determinant reverses the formula. The map-direction convention is the same one used in normalizing flows.

    Worked two-by-two transport problem¤

    Let

    \[ P=\tfrac12\delta_0+\tfrac12\delta_2, \qquad Q=\tfrac12\delta_1+\tfrac12\delta_3, \]

    with cost \(c(x,y)=|x-y|\). A coupling is a nonnegative matrix with row and column sums \((1/2,1/2)\). Write

    \[ \pi(a)= \begin{bmatrix} a&1/2-a\\ 1/2-a&a \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 0\leq a\leq1/2. \]

    The cost matrix is

    \[ C= \begin{bmatrix} 1&3\\ 1&1 \end{bmatrix}, \]

    so total cost is

    \[ \langle C,\pi(a)\rangle =a+3(1/2-a)+(1/2-a)+a=2-2a. \]

    It is minimized at \(a=1/2\), yielding the monotone coupling \(0\mapsto1\), \(2\mapsto3\) with cost one. The crossed assignment has cost two. The optimizer depends on the cost: a semantic or geodesic cost could prefer a different coupling than Euclidean distance.

    Wasserstein distances¤

    For real \(p\geq1\) and probability measures with finite \(p\)th moments,

    \[ W_p(P,Q) =\left( \inf_{\pi\in\Pi(P,Q)} \int \lVert x-y\rVert^p\,d\pi(x,y) \right)^{1/p}. \]

    \(p\) need not be an integer. Without finite \(p\)th moments, the value may be infinite. A generic transport cost does not necessarily produce a metric; the Wasserstein metric properties rely on the ground metric and exponent conditions.

    In one dimension,

    \[ W_p^p(P,Q) =\int_0^1 \left|F_P^{-1}(u)-F_Q^{-1}(u)\right|^pdu. \]

    For \(p=1\) there is also

    \[ W_1(P,Q)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|F_P(t)-F_Q(t)|\,dt. \]

    This is the area between CDFs. The Kolmogorov–Smirnov statistic is the maximum vertical gap \(\sup_t|F_P(t)-F_Q(t)|\). They are not equal in general and have different units.

    Worked \(W_1\) versus KS counterexample¤

    Let \(P=\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and \(Q=\operatorname{Uniform}(0,2)\). Their CDF gap is \(t/2\) on \([0,1]\) and \(1-t/2\) on \([1,2]\). Therefore

    \[ \operatorname{KS}(P,Q)=\frac12, \]

    while

    \[ \begin{aligned} W_1(P,Q) &=\int_0^1\frac t2\,dt\\ &\quad+\int_1^2\left(1-\frac t2\right)dt\\ &=\frac12. \end{aligned} \]

    The numbers happen to match at scale two. Stretch \(Q\) to \(\operatorname{Uniform}(0,L)\) with \(L>1\): KS becomes \(1-1/L\), whereas \(W_1=(L-1)/2\). One is dimensionless and bounded by one; the other has the units and scale of the sample space. Equality at one setting is accidental.

    Duality and WGAN boundaries¤

    For suitable metric spaces,

    \[ W_1(P,Q) =\sup_{\lVert f\rVert_{\mathrm{Lip}}\leq1} \left(\mathbb E_P[f(X)]-\mathbb E_Q[f(Y)]\right). \]

    WGAN replaces the full 1-Lipschitz function class by a parameterized critic and approximately enforces the constraint (Arjovsky, Chintala, and Bottou). Weight clipping, gradient penalty, spectral normalization, and other controls define different critic classes. WGAN-GP penalizes selected input-gradient norms and improved empirical stability in its experiments (Gulrajani et al.); it does not guarantee that a finite critic equals the exact Kantorovich potential or that every generator step follows an exact Wasserstein gradient.

    Entropic regularization¤

    For discrete or suitably dominated laws, entropic OT solves

    \[ \operatorname{OT}_{c,\varepsilon}(P,Q) =\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(P,Q)} \left\{ \mathbb E_\pi[c(X,Y)] +\varepsilon D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi\|P\otimes Q) \right\}. \]

    The regularizer favors diffuse couplings and enables Sinkhorn matrix scaling (Cuturi). It changes the optimization problem. In particular,

    \[ \operatorname{OT}_{c,\varepsilon}(P,P) \]

    is generally not zero under common conventions, so the regularized cost is biased as a discrepancy. The debiased Sinkhorn divergence is

    \[ S_{c,\varepsilon}(P,Q) =\operatorname{OT}_{c,\varepsilon}(P,Q) -\tfrac12\operatorname{OT}_{c,\varepsilon}(P,P) -\tfrac12\operatorname{OT}_{c,\varepsilon}(Q,Q). \]

    Sinkhorn divergences interpolate useful geometric and statistical regimes, with precise positive-definiteness and convergence results under their conditions (Feydy et al.). The \(n^{-1/2}\) sample-complexity behavior has a constant that deteriorates with regularization and dimension-dependent smoothness quantities; it is not a dimension-free free lunch (Genevay et al.).

    Numerical stability and finite estimates¤

    Sinkhorn iterations alternate marginal rescalings. Small \(\varepsilon\) produces sharply varying kernels and numerical underflow. Log-domain updates, potential centering, epsilon scaling, warm starts, stopping on marginal residuals, and higher precision can be essential. Reporting “Sinkhorn converged” requires the regularization, iteration cap, marginal error, dual gap or other criterion, dtype, and debiasing convention.

    Minibatch transport compares empirical measures and can be a biased estimate of a population discrepancy. Reusing a batch for optimization and evaluation can hide sample overfitting. Exact empirical Wasserstein distances suffer a high-dimensional sample-complexity curse; learned ground costs and feature embeddings change the geometry rather than merely accelerating the same quantity.

    Dynamic transport and probability paths¤

    For quadratic cost under appropriate regularity, the Benamou–Brenier formulation writes

    \[ W_2^2(P_0,P_1) =\inf_{(\rho_t,v_t)} \int_0^1\int \lVert v_t(x)\rVert^2\rho_t(x)\,dx\,dt \]

    subject to

    \[ \partial_t\rho_t+\nabla\cdot(\rho_t v_t)=0, \qquad \rho_0=P_0, \quad \rho_1=P_1. \]

    This turns static coupling into minimum kinetic action (Benamou and Brenier). Flow matching can regress a vector field for a chosen probability path; rectified flow can iteratively straighten couplings; diffusion follows stochastic paths; Schrödinger bridges add an entropic reference-process constraint. Shared endpoints, or even shared marginal paths, do not make their finite training objectives, vector fields, solvers, or discretization errors identical.

    Brenier maps and existence qualifications¤

    For quadratic cost on Euclidean space, if the source is absolutely continuous and the measures satisfy appropriate finite-moment conditions, the optimal map is almost everywhere the gradient of a convex potential. This is not a theorem that every neural transport must be invertible, smooth everywhere, or representable by a finite convex network. Atomic sources, manifolds, unequal dimensions, nonquadratic costs, and constraints require other formulations.

    Neural OT research extends maps and couplings across incomparable spaces, unbalanced mass, stochastic conditionals, and entropic objectives. The VarEOT workshop preprint proposes a variational reformulation for continuous entropic OT; it is evidence about one proposed solver, not an established replacement for Sinkhorn or a universal generative method. A continuous Gromov--Wasserstein benchmark submission documents solver difficulties; its review status and benchmark scope must remain visible.

    Transport audit for generative claims¤

    Every transport claim should state:

    1. source and target measures and their sample access;
    2. ground cost, units, feature representation, and invariances;
    3. Monge map, Kantorovich coupling, stochastic kernel, or dynamic path;
    4. moment, absolute-continuity, compactness, or regularity assumptions;
    5. primal, dual, semi-dual, or entropic objective and sign convention;
    6. parameterized function class and constraint enforcement;
    7. empirical estimator, batch size, debiasing, and numerical tolerance;
    8. whether the result concerns an objective value, a coupling, a conditional sampler, or generated samples; and
    9. finite solver, NFE, wall-clock, and evaluation protocol.

    Chapter 6 synthesis and retrieval¤

    Kantorovich transport optimizes over couplings; Monge transport restricts to deterministic maps. Wasserstein metrics need moment and ground-space conditions. Entropic regularization changes the objective and introduces self-cost, which Sinkhorn divergence corrects. Dynamic OT connects distributions through a minimum-action path, but shared probability paths do not erase estimator and solver differences across flows, diffusion, and bridge methods.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why can the atomic Monge example have no feasible map while a coupling exists?
    2. What is the correct forward change-of-variables identity?
    3. How does \(W_1\) differ from the KS statistic in one dimension?
    4. Why subtract entropic self-costs in Sinkhorn divergence?

    Solutions. (1) A deterministic map cannot split one source atom, whereas a joint coupling can. (2) \(q(T(x))|\det J_T(x)|=p(x)\). (3) \(W_1\) integrates the absolute CDF gap and has sample-space units; KS takes its supremum and is dimensionless. (4) Regularized OT generally assigns nonzero cost to a law compared with itself, so the subtraction removes that entropic bias under the stated construction.

    Complete-volume synthesis¤

    Generative learning is not one optimization problem. Deterministic AD, Monte Carlo gradients, discrete surrogates, adaptive preconditioners, adversarial games, latent-variable coordinates, black-box search distributions, subset objectives, and probability couplings expose different mathematical objects. Their connections become useful only after objective, estimator, geometry, finite algorithm, and evidence protocol are kept separate.

    The most reliable workflow is therefore layered:

    1. declare the generative object and query;
    2. write the population target and assumptions;
    3. derive the finite estimator and its bias–variance contract;
    4. specify the derivative or surrogate program;
    5. define optimizer state and coupled update order;
    6. measure numerical and systems behavior; and
    7. evaluate the resulting generative model independently of training loss.
