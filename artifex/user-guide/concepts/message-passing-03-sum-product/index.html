    3. Sum-Product on Trees¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be able to multiply and marginalize finite factors and follow the elimination calculation in Chapter 2. By the end, you should be able to:

    • derive variable-to-factor and factor-to-variable messages;
    • explain why the recipient's incoming message is excluded;
    • compute exact node and factor marginals on an acyclic factor graph;
    • separate sum-product, max-product, and sampling queries; and
    • implement normalization and log-domain updates without changing the target.

    Two local update rules¤

    For a factor graph, let \(N(i)\) be the factors adjacent to variable \(X_i\) and \(N(a)\) the variables in factor \(f_a\). A variable-to-factor message multiplies information arriving from every other neighboring factor:

    \[ m_{i\to a}(x_i) =\prod_{b\in N(i)\setminus\{a\}}m_{b\to i}(x_i). \]

    A factor-to-variable message incorporates the factor and sums out its other arguments:

    \[ m_{a\to i}(x_i) =\sum_{x_{a\setminus i}}f_a(x_a) \prod_{j\in N(a)\setminus\{i\}}m_{j\to a}(x_j). \]

    The recipient is excluded because its message already summarizes evidence from that side of the cut. Multiplying it back into the outgoing message would immediately double-count the same subtree.

    Once both directions of every edge have been computed on a tree,

    \[ b_i(x_i)\propto\prod_{a\in N(i)}m_{a\to i}(x_i), \]

    and

    \[ b_a(x_a)\propto f_a(x_a) \prod_{i\in N(a)}m_{i\to a}(x_i). \]

    After normalization, these beliefs are the exact node and factor marginals. This is the sum-product algorithm on an acyclic factor graph.

    Why two passes are enough¤

    Remove any edge from a tree. The graph splits into two components. A message across that edge is the partial sum of every factor on the sender's component, expressed as a function of the boundary variable. A leaf-to-root pass computes all summaries toward an arbitrary root; a root-to-leaf pass computes the opposite summaries. Every complete configuration contributes exactly once.

    An implementation may use a postorder traversal followed by a preorder traversal, or any schedule that sends a message only after all prerequisite messages are ready. For a static tree, each directed edge message is needed once. Parallel synchronous updates reach all nodes after a number of rounds controlled by the tree diameter.

    Worked example: recover all three marginals¤

    The leaf messages into \(B\) from Chapter 2 are

    \[ r_{A\to B}=(7,5), \qquad r_{C\to B}=(6,9). \]

    Their product gives

    \[ b_B\propto(42,45). \]

    To send the right-hand evidence through \(B\) toward \(A\),

    \[ r_{B\to A}(a)=\sum_b f_{AB}(a,b)r_{C\to B}(b) =(27,33). \]

    Multiplying by \(u_A\) yields

    \[ b_A\propto(2,1)\odot(27,33)=(54,33). \]

    Similarly,

    \[ r_{B\to C}(c)=\sum_b r_{A\to B}(b)f_{BC}(b,c) =(19,17), \]

    so

    \[ b_C\propto(1,4)\odot(19,17)=(19,68). \]

    Every belief sums to the same \(Z=87\) before normalization, matching enumeration. The messages are precisely cached elimination factors, now reused for several queries.

    Message scale and the normalizer¤

    Messages can be multiplied by arbitrary positive constants without changing normalized beliefs. Normalizing every message prevents underflow, but discarding all scale factors loses the normalizer. To recover \(Z\) or a sequence likelihood, store the normalization constants under a consistent convention or evaluate one calibrated root expression before normalizing it.

    For positive factors, log-domain updates replace products by sums and factor marginalization by logsumexp:

    \[ \log m_{a\to i}(x_i) =\operatorname{logsumexp}_{x_{a\setminus i}} \left[ \log f_a(x_a)+ \sum_{j\in N(a)\setminus\{i\}}\log m_{j\to a}(x_j) \right]. \]

    Zero factors represent impossible configurations and become \(-\infty\) in log space. Code must define how an all-impossible message is reported rather than silently producing NaN after normalization.

    Chains, hidden Markov models, and state-space inference¤

    Forward-backward in a hidden Markov model is sum-product on a chain. A forward message combines the filtered state with a transition and new emission; a backward message summarizes later observations. Their product gives a smoothed marginal. Viterbi replaces summation by maximization and stores backpointers. Forward-filtering backward-sampling uses calibrated conditionals to draw a complete posterior path.

    These computations answer different queries:

    Table MSG.5. Chains, hidden Markov models, and state-space inference
    Operation Replaced algebra Result
    sum-product sum and product exact marginals and normalizer on a tree
    max-product / max-sum max and product / addition in log space max-marginals; global MPE requires compatible traceback
    backward sampling normalized calibrated factors exact posterior sample on a tree
    coordinatewise marginal mode argmax of each exact marginal minimum per-node 0–1 loss; need not be a legal or globally most probable joint state

    Sum and max cannot be interchanged indiscriminately. Marginal MAP, which sums nuisance variables and maximizes query variables, needs an order compatible with those operations and is generally harder than either all-sum marginalization or all-max MPE.

    Posterior modes answer different decision problems¤

    The distinction is visible in a four-row posterior. Let three binary variables obey an even-parity constraint, so only \(000\), \(011\), \(101\), and \(110\) are legal, with probabilities

    Table MSG.6. Posterior modes answer different decision problems
    State \(000\) \(011\) \(101\) \(110\)
    Posterior probability \(0.27\) \(0.18\) \(0.30\) \(0.25\)

    The unique globally most probable explanation is

    \[ x^{\mathrm{MPE}}=101. \]

    The singleton posterior probabilities of state \(1\) are

    \[ p(X_1=1)=0.55, \qquad p(X_2=1)=0.43, \qquad p(X_3=1)=0.48. \]

    Choosing the most probable value independently at each coordinate therefore returns \(100\), which has posterior probability zero because it violates the parity factor. That coordinatewise rule is still a valid Bayes action when the loss is Hamming loss: each bit is scored separately. MPE instead minimizes posterior expected exact-match \(0\)–\(1\) loss, where the entire assignment must be correct.

    Max-product computes max-marginals. In this example their values are

    \[ \begin{aligned} \zeta_1(0),\zeta_1(1)&=(0.27,0.30),\\ \zeta_2(0),\zeta_2(1)&=(0.30,0.25),\\ \zeta_3(0),\zeta_3(1)&=(0.27,0.30). \end{aligned} \]

    Their unique local maximizers stitch together as \(101\), the MPE. With tied max-marginals, independently breaking ties can again create an incompatible assignment; a tree traceback or another globally consistent reconstruction is required.

    Marginal MAP introduces a third decision. For query variables \(X_Q\) and nuisance variables \(X_R\),

    \[ x_Q^*=\arg\max_{x_Q}\sum_{x_R}p(x_Q,x_R\mid e). \]

    It maximizes posterior mass after eliminating \(X_R\); it need not equal the restriction of a full MPE assignment. The elimination schedule must respect which variables are summed and which are maximized because those operations do not generally commute. Thus “run max-product” is incomplete until the query, loss, tie rule, and reconstruction procedure have been named.

    Exactness contract for tree messages¤

    The standard exact guarantee needs:

    1. an acyclic factor graph after incorporating evidence;
    2. factors representing the declared nonnegative finite sum or compatible integral;
    3. every required message computed from the correct incoming messages;
    4. exact local sum/product arithmetic, apart from stated floating-point error; and
    5. a query supported by the chosen algebra.

    A graph that merely looks tree-like, a truncated number of rounds, pruned states, low-rank message compression, quadrature, or particle messages changes the guarantee. Those may be excellent approximations, but they must be named.

    Misconception check

    Exact singleton marginals do not determine the joint law, and choosing the most probable state of every singleton can violate a global constraint. Use factor marginals, a traceback procedure, or posterior sampling when dependencies matter.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Tree messages are cached partial eliminations. Two directed messages per edge provide all local marginals, and a consistent scale convention recovers the normalizer. Chapter 4 preserves this reuse on a loopy original graph by clustering variables into a junction tree.

    Retrieval practice: exact tree messages
    1. Why does \(m_{i\to a}\) exclude \(m_{a\to i}\)?
    2. What do the running example's two incoming messages to \(B\) multiply to?
    3. Does normalizing every message make the algorithm approximate?
    4. Why is a coordinatewise marginal mode not necessarily the MPE state?
    5. Which loss makes coordinatewise posterior modes optimal?

    Solutions. (1) To avoid returning and double-counting information that came from the recipient's side. (2) \((7,5)\odot(6,9)=(42,45)\). (3) No; positive rescaling leaves normalized beliefs unchanged, although scale factors must be retained to recover \(Z\). (4) It optimizes each coordinate separately and can ignore joint constraints or dependence. (5) Additive Hamming loss, with one \(0\)–\(1\) penalty per coordinate.

    Bridge. Loops prevent an edge from separating the graph into independent sides. Exact inference can restore a tree by making each node a cluster rather than a single variable. Continue continuously or open Chapter 4.
