    1. From Infinitesimal Dynamics to Finite Transitions¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes elementary ODEs, Markov kernels, and iterative generative sampling. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Type the learned object: distinguish an infinitesimal field, exact propagator, deterministic flow map, stochastic kernel, and finite program.
    2. O2 — Apply composition laws: compose deterministic maps and Markov kernels with one explicit time convention.
    3. O3 — Falsify a coarse solver claim: compare exact and Euler updates for stability, sign, and global error.
    4. O4 — Diagnose hidden branching: construct a conditional transition that a deterministic conditional mean cannot represent.
    5. O5 — Audit the compute unit: separate outer steps, network stages, guidance branches, internal-head calls, and end-to-end latency.
    6. O6 — Allocate error: distinguish reference-dynamics, finite-object, estimation, composition, numerical, and systems error in a new method.

    Let an ODE have flow map

    \[ \Phi_{t\to s}(x_t)=x_s, \qquad \frac{dx_t}{dt}=v_t(x_t). \]

    For \(r\le s\le t\), exact maps satisfy

    \[ \Phi_{t\to r} =\Phi_{s\to r}\circ\Phi_{t\to s}, \qquad \Phi_{t\to t}=I. \]

    Under uniqueness they are invertible with \(\Phi_{s\to t}=\Phi_{t\to s}^{-1}\). A network that approximates a finite map need not satisfy identity, composition, or invertibility without explicit construction or training.

    For a Markov process, a transition kernel replaces the deterministic map:

    \[ K_{t\to r}(x_t,dx_r). \]

    Chapman–Kolmogorov gives

    \[ K_{t\to r}=K_{s\to r}K_{t\to s}. \]

    Deterministic maps are Dirac kernels. Stochastic kernels can represent multiple possible destinations from one current state, which matters when a coarse transition hides unresolved latent choices.

    For time-inhomogeneous dynamics, write the propagator as \(\Phi_{s,t}\) with source time first:

    \[ x_t=\Phi_{s,t}(x_s),\qquad \Phi_{s,t}=\Phi_{u,t}\circ\Phi_{s,u},\qquad s\leq u\leq t. \]

    The corresponding kernel convention is

    \[ (K_{s,u}K_{u,t})(x,A) =\int K_{u,t}(y,A)K_{s,u}(x,dy) =K_{s,t}(x,A). \]

    This book sometimes generates from a high noise time back toward data time. In that direction the indices decrease, but the rule is unchanged: the rightmost map acts first. Writing source and destination on every finite object prevents a large class of sign and composition errors.

    Five distinct finite objects¤

    Table FEW.3. Five distinct finite objects
    Object Typical notation Deterministic? What it answers
    exact ODE flow \(\Phi_{s,t}(x)\) yes under uniqueness where this state moves under the declared field
    Markov transition \(K_{s,t}(x,dy)\) not necessarily the conditional law at a later time
    endpoint predictor \(f(x_t,t)\) usually a clean endpoint estimate from this state
    learned two-time map \(F_\theta(x_s,s,t)\) usually an approximate jump between requested times
    inference program \(\widehat K^{(m)}_{0,1}\) possibly the law produced after all \(m\) deployed operations

    Equality of endpoints for one initial distribution does not identify the transition kernel. Equality of transition kernels on all states and time pairs is stronger; equality of a finite implementation additionally fixes random number generation, schedules, precision, and auxiliary operations.

    Why Euler one-step collapses¤

    Euler applied over a large interval uses

    \[ x_0\approx x_1-v_1(x_1). \]

    The local velocity is not generally the integrated displacement. If several conditional paths cross at \(x_1\), a regression velocity is their average. A single large Euler jump can move toward a conditional mean rather than follow any valid trajectory.

    There are two independent failures here. Numerical failure occurs when a local field is evaluated over an interval outside the solver's accuracy or stability region. Representation failure occurs when the desired coarse conditional law is not a Dirac mass but the model outputs only one vector.

    Worked stability counterexample: valid flow, invalid coarse step¤

    For the stable scalar ODE \(\dot x=-4x\) over one unit of time,

    \[ \Phi_{0,1}(x)=e^{-4}x\approx0.0183x, \qquad x_1^{\mathrm{Euler}}=(1-4)x=-3x. \]

    The exact solution preserves sign and contracts magnitude. One Euler step reverses sign and triples magnitude. The learned vector field can be exact while the one-step solver is catastrophically wrong. Four Euler steps with \(h=1/4\) collapse immediately to zero; eight steps give \((1/2)^8x=0.00390625x\). More steps do not create monotone accuracy unless the method and step size lie in a useful regime.

    Worked branching counterexample: the conditional mean is not a sample¤

    Let a hidden variable \(Z\in\{-1,+1\}\) be equally likely. Suppose a coarse state forgets \(Z\) and is always \(X_s=0\), while the endpoint is \(X_t=Z\). Then

    \[ K_{s,t}(0,\cdot)=\tfrac12\delta_{-1}+\tfrac12\delta_{+1}, \qquad \mathbb E[X_t\mid X_s=0]=0. \]

    A squared-error deterministic predictor returns zero, a point outside the support of the correct conditional endpoint law. Initial-noise diversity does not repair this conditional query because the current state has already erased the branch. A stochastic head, retained latent state, or finer transition is needed.

    Four meanings of “one step”¤

    1. One solver step: possibly several network stages, as in Runge–Kutta.
    2. One NFE: one evaluation of the principal neural network.
    3. One outer transition: may contain an internal stochastic head or repeated substeps.
    4. One direct map: one network maps base noise to an output.

    Guidance may require conditional and unconditional evaluations; self-conditioning, rejection, or decoding adds cost. Every speed claim must state NFE, wall time, batch, precision, hardware, and auxiliary models.

    An NFE is itself ambiguous unless the counted network is named. A Heun step can use two evaluations of one backbone; classifier-free guidance can use two logical branches fused into one launch; a transition head can perform several cheap internal evaluations after one expensive backbone call. Report both logical work and realized execution.

    Four acceleration axes and one systems overlay¤

    A smaller step count can result from four different interventions:

    1. Numerical solver or schedule: retain a learned local field but change where and how it is evaluated.
    2. Learned finite transition: replace repeated local evaluations with a trained endpoint map, average velocity, student, or stochastic kernel.
    3. State representation: generate in a compressed or structured state and pay for a codec, renderer, or cascade.
    4. Training target or path: change the field, parameterization, weighting, interpolation, or endpoint objective that is learned.

    Architecture, kernels, caching, quantisation, batching, and hardware are a systems overlay that changes the cost of each evaluation. These axes can be combined, but they answer different questions. A solver comparison can hold the checkpoint fixed; a distilled map, latent generator, or newly trained path usually cannot.

    For outer transition \(i\), let \(m_i\) be its backbone stages, \(b_{ij}\) its logical guidance branches, and \(\ell_{ij}\) its launched calls after branch fusion. Then

    \[ E_{\mathrm{branch}}=\sum_{i,j}b_{ij}, \qquad L_{\mathrm{backbone}}=\sum_{i,j}\ell_{ij}. \]

    Neither count includes an internal transition head, codec, cascade, reward model, rejection loop, or reranker unless those are reported separately. A fair record therefore includes outer transitions, branch-equivalent evaluations, actual launches, head and auxiliary calls, tensor shapes, peak memory, end-to-end latency, and—when the model changed—training or distillation cost.

    The diffusion sampling chapter gives an explicit counterexample: a hypothetical eight-step system fuses two guidance branches into eight launches and finishes in 116 ms, while a four-step, two-stage system executes the branches serially in sixteen launches and finishes in 180 ms. Both perform sixteen branch-equivalent evaluations. The example does not rank their quality; it proves that the reported outer-step count alone does not order latency.

    Error ledger for a finite generator¤

    For a metric or discrepancy \(d\), a useful decomposition is conceptual rather than universally additive:

    \[ E_{\mathrm{total}} \leadsto E_{\mathrm{reference}} +E_{\mathrm{representation}} +E_{\mathrm{estimation}} +E_{\mathrm{composition}} +E_{\mathrm{numerical}} +E_{\mathrm{systems}}. \]
    • \(E_{\mathrm{reference}}\) asks whether the chosen path or teacher ends at the intended distribution.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{representation}}\) asks whether the model class can express the required finite map or kernel.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{estimation}}\) covers finite data, stochastic optimization, and imperfect auxiliary targets.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{composition}}\) measures how learned local errors propagate across repeated transitions.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{numerical}}\) covers a solver or internal sampler.
    • \(E_{\mathrm{systems}}\) covers finite precision, codecs, guidance plumbing, rejection, and implementation drift.

    Do not add unlike quantities numerically without a theorem. The ledger is a causal debugging partition: change one layer at a time and use exact small systems to isolate it.

    Worked linear system¤

    For \(dx/dt=ax\), the exact map over interval \(h\) is \(e^{ah}x\). Euler gives \((1+ah)x\). The local error is \(O(h^2)\) and compounds over steps. Learning \(e^{ah}\) directly can be exact for this system, but the learned map must cover every \(h\) and state encountered.

    Retrieval practice: generators and finite transitions
    1. How are a generator \(L_t\) and a finite transition \(P_{s,t}\) related?
    2. Why does a large Euler step generally fail to equal the exact one-step map?
    3. Name four distinct claims that may be hidden by the phrase “one step.”
    4. Which error term remains even if the learned finite map is exact but implemented approximately?

    Solutions. (1) The transition is the time-ordered evolution induced by integrating or exponentiating the generator, not the generator itself. (2) Euler retains only a local first-order approximation and accumulates curvature and stability error over a large interval. (3) One numerical solver step, one learned transition, one network evaluation, or one visible system call. (4) Implementation or numerical realization error, including precision, decoding, and system overhead.

    Misconception checks: finite transitions¤

    • “An exact field plus one evaluation gives an exact endpoint.” Only special fields and integrators have that property.
    • “A deterministic global map cannot generate a multimodal marginal.” It can, because its initial noise varies; the limitation concerns a multimodal conditional law after information has been discarded.
    • “A Markov kernel is a matrix.” A matrix is the finite-state case; kernels also act on continuous, structured, and mixed state spaces.
    • “Fewer outer transitions means less compute.” Internal heads, guidance, model size, codecs, and hardware can reverse the ordering.

    Independent practice and assessment map: finite transitions¤

    Attempt the prediction before calculating. Label every answer as a dynamics, finite-object, program, or deployed-system claim.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: coarse Euler stability. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For \(\dot x=-4x\), predict the sign of one Euler update with \(h=1\) and of the exact update, then compute their multipliers.
    2. P1.2 — Compose an affine flow. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For \(\dot x=ax+b\), derive \(\Phi_{s,t}\) for \(a\ne0\) and verify \(\Phi_{s,t}=\Phi_{u,t}\circ\Phi_{s,u}\).
    3. P1.3 — Diagnose a collapsed branch. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For the hidden-sign example, compute the squared-error-optimal deterministic endpoint and its total-variation distance from the true conditional law.
    4. P1.4 — Reconcile four compute counts. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A sampler uses four outer transitions, two Runge--Kutta stages per transition, two guidance branches fused per stage, and one decoder. Report outer transitions, branch-equivalent backbone evaluations, launched backbone calls, and auxiliary calls.
    5. P1.5 — Allocate a failed experiment. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 15 min. A student matches analytic training targets but degrades only after repeated fp16 composition. Assign the strongest supported error categories and propose two isolation tests.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to discrete generation. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. For a masked-token model, define the finite conditional kernel, the meaning of an outer step and an NFE, and one example in which parallel unmasking discards dependence that sequential refinement could retain.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. Euler multiplies by \(1-4=-3\), so it reverses sign. The exact flow multiplies by \(e^{-4}\approx0.0183156\), so it preserves sign and contracts. Check. The failure is caused by the finite step; the vector field itself is exact and stable.
    2. P1.2. Solving the shifted system gives \(\Phi_{s,t}(x)=e^{a(t-s)}x+\frac ba(e^{a(t-s)}-1)\). Substituting \(\Phi_{s,u}\) into \(\Phi_{u,t}\) makes the exponential factors multiply and the two constant terms combine to \(\frac ba(e^{a(t-s)}-1)\). At \(s=t\) the map is identity. Check. With \(b=0\) it reduces to the linear example.
    3. P1.3. Squared loss returns the conditional mean zero. The model law is \(\delta_0\) and the target is \((\delta_{-1}+\delta_{+1})/2\). Taking \(A=\{-1,+1\}\) yields \(|1-0|=1\), the maximum possible TV distance. Check. Zero regression loss relative to the conditional mean is not distribution matching.
    4. P1.4. There are four outer transitions. Two stages and two logical branches give \(4\cdot2\cdot2=16\) branch-equivalent evaluations. Fusion launches one batch per stage, so there are \(4\cdot2=8\) backbone launches, plus one auxiliary decoder call. Check. A timing comparison still needs tensor shapes, precision, batch, and hardware.
    5. P1.5. Analytic-target agreement weakens an estimation explanation. Failure only under repeated fp16 use implicates composition and finite-precision systems error. Compare one versus many compositions at the same precision, then repeat fp16/fp32/fp64 with identical states. An exact high-precision reference separates numerical drift from map approximation. Rubric. Claiming that training data are inadequate without a changed-data control is unsupported.
    6. P1.6. Let \(K_k(x,dy)\) be the conditional distribution of the next partially unmasked sequence given the current sequence and condition. One outer step applies one scheduled parallel refinement; one NFE evaluates the named denoiser once. If two masked tokens must agree, simultaneous independent predictions from the all-mask state can disagree, while revealing the first and conditioning the second on it can preserve equality. Check. A coupled stochastic parallel kernel could also preserve dependence; “parallel” alone is not the theorem.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Finite maps and kernels obey composition laws that infinitesimal fields satisfy only after integration. Few-step learning moves numerical integration error into approximation, self-consistency, or distillation error. Chapter 2 develops consistency and two-time map objectives.
