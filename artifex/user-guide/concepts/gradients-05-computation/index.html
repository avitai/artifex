    5. Computing Natural Steps at Model Scale¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Automatic differentiation, stochastic gradients, linear solves, and damping.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Specify matrix-free curvature: distinguish model-Fisher, empirical-Fisher, and generalized-Gauss--Newton products.
    2. O2 — Execute a natural solve: compute a damped linear solution and scale it by a declared local trust radius.
    3. O3 — Validate derivative programs: use JVP/VJP duality and directional finite-difference or Taylor checks before trusting curvature products.
    4. O4 — Audit structured approximations: state the factorization, rank, block, sampling, staleness, and damping assumptions in K-FAC/FANG-like steps.
    5. O5 — Separate solver from geometry error: interpret residuals, predicted movement, and measured divergence as different diagnostics.
    6. O6 — Design a scalable report: account for precision, communication, memory, factor batches, wall-clock, and matched-compute generative behavior.

    Never materialize a giant Fisher without a reason¤

    For score \(s_\theta(X)=\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(X)\),

    \[ Fv =\mathbb E_{X\sim p_\theta} \left[s_\theta(X)\bigl(s_\theta(X)^\top v\bigr)\right]. \]

    This identity permits stochastic Fisher-vector products. In neural conditional models, generalized Gauss--Newton products may be obtained by JVP/VJP compositions without explicitly forming scores. The two products coincide only under the likelihood and loss conditions established for that model.

    An empirical average at observed outcomes is often easier to compute, but it is the empirical Fisher, not automatically an unbiased model-Fisher estimator. Kunstner, Balles, and Hennig demonstrate why that substitution can fail as curvature.

    Validate the derivative program before the metric¤

    A curvature routine composes derivative programs, so a low linear-solver residual cannot expose a wrong JVP, VJP, sign, detach, or batching convention. For a differentiable map \(y=f(\theta)\) with Jacobian \(J\), test the adjoint identity on random directions \(v\) and cotangents \(u\):

    \[ \langle u,Jv\rangle =\langle J^\top u,v\rangle. \]

    Then compare the implemented JVP against a central directional difference,

    \[ Jv \approx \frac{f(\theta+h v)-f(\theta-h v)}{2h}, \]

    over a range of \(h\). Equivalently, a first-order Taylor remainder should initially decrease quadratically as \(h\) shrinks, before floating-point error dominates. Run these checks on the exact precision, parameter pytree, masks, random seeds, and batch reduction used by the Fisher or GGN routine. The automatic-differentiation survey provides the computational-graph foundations; the JAX autodiff cookbook demonstrates JVP/VJP constructions and numerical checking.

    These tests validate the derivative of the implemented program. They do not prove that the program represents the intended population objective, metric, or continuous-time equation. That separate semantic check is why both program and law-level diagnostics are required.

    A matrix-free trust-region step¤

    A defensible finite procedure makes each approximation visible:

    1. estimate a gradient \(g\) for the declared scalar objective;
    2. define a routine for \(v\mapsto \widehat Fv\) using samples from the declared law;
    3. approximately solve \((\widehat F+\lambda I)d=g\), for example by conjugate gradients;
    4. measure the undamped predicted law movement \(\widehat\delta=\tfrac12d^\top\widehat Fd\);
    5. choose \(\alpha\leq1\) so \(\alpha^2\widehat\delta\leq\epsilon\);
    6. propose \(\theta^+=\theta-\alpha d\); and
    7. verify the actual objective, divergence proxy, and held-out generative behavior before accepting a large step.
    g = stochastic_objective_gradient(theta)
Fv = model_fisher_vector_product(theta, declared_model_samples)
d = conjugate_gradient(v -> Fv(v) + damping * v, g)
predicted_kl = 0.5 * dot(d, Fv(d))
scale = min(1, sqrt(radius / max(predicted_kl, tiny)))
candidate = theta - scale * d

    The damping term stabilizes the solve but is a coordinate-dependent algorithmic choice. The trust-region estimate is local. Both facts belong in experimental reports.

    Worked two-parameter solve¤

    Let

    \[ F=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad g=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The exact natural direction is

    \[ \begin{aligned} d&=F^{-1}g\\ &=\frac13 \begin{bmatrix}1&-1\\-1&4\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}\\ &=\begin{bmatrix}-1/3\\7/3\end{bmatrix}. \end{aligned} \]

    Indeed \(Fd=g\), and \(d^\top Fd=g^\top d=13/3\). The Euclidean descent direction \(-g=(-1,-2)\) and natural descent direction \(-d=(1/3,-7/3)\) even point to different parameter-space quadrants. That is not paradoxical: the metric says that equal coordinate lengths do not represent equal changes of law.

    Structured approximations¤

    K-FAC. For a neural layer, K-FAC approximates a Fisher block by a Kronecker product of an activation covariance and a back-propagated output-gradient covariance. The two smaller factors can be regularized and inverted efficiently. The construction is neither the exact dense Fisher nor merely a diagonal adaptive optimizer. Its factorization, damping, stale statistics, parameter grouping, and distributed update schedule all affect the resulting direction (Martens and Grosse).

    FANG and energy models. Grosse and Salakhutdinov's factorized natural-gradient method approximates inverse-Fisher structure for restricted Boltzmann machines. The paper is especially instructive because the Fisher is a covariance of model sufficient statistics, while estimating those statistics still depends on the quality of persistent model samples. A sophisticated solve does not repair a biased negative phase.

    Diagonal and low-rank methods. A diagonal score-second-moment estimate can be useful, but it discards correlations and may be an empirical rather than model Fisher. Low-rank approximations cannot represent curvature outside their subspace without an additional diagonal or damping term. Names such as “natural,” “second order,” and “adaptive” do not settle these distinctions.

    Numerical and distributed diagnostics¤

    Record at least:

    • the law and sampling distribution used for \(\widehat F\);
    • model Fisher, empirical Fisher, or generalized Gauss--Newton semantics;
    • damping, factorization, rank, update frequency, and excluded parameters;
    • conjugate-gradient residual and iteration count;
    • \(g^\top d\), \(d^\top\widehat Fd\), and the predicted versus measured KL proxy;
    • curvature-estimation batch size separately from gradient batch size;
    • factor eigenvalue ranges and the fraction dominated by damping;
    • precision used for factors, solves, and parameter updates; and
    • wall-clock, memory, communication, and generative evaluation at matched compute.

    Dash et al. introduce gradient-regularized natural-gradient variants and report results on their declared vision and language protocols (peer-reviewed study). It is peer-reviewed research, not evidence that one such optimizer universally improves AR, VAE, EBM, GAN, diffusion, and flow training.

    Independent practice and assessment map: matrix-free natural steps¤

    Treat the derivative program, curvature identity, linear solve, and accepted finite update as four separately testable layers.

    1. P1.1 — Compute a Fisher-vector product. Type: derive · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 7 min. A Bernoulli model uses logit \(\eta\) with \(q=0.25\). Compute its scalar model Fisher and \(Fv\) for \(v=2\) from the score-outer-product identity.
    2. P1.2 — Verify the two-parameter solve. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 9 min. For the displayed \(F\) and \(g\), solve \(Fd=g\), verify the residual, and compute \(g^\top d\) and \(d^\top Fd\) independently.
    3. P1.3 — Predict before computing: scale by a trust radius. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 9 min. Using P1.2, predict whether the full step fits the radius, then compute the undamped predicted divergence \(\widehat\delta=\tfrac12d^\top Fd\) and scale \(\alpha\) for radius \(\epsilon=0.1\). State what must be measured after the step.
    4. P1.4 — Distinguish model and empirical Fisher. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. A Bernoulli classifier predicts \(q=0.8\) and the observed target is \(y=1\). Compute the logit model Fisher and the single-observation score outer product. Explain why the latter is not a model expectation.
    5. P1.5 — Validate JVP and VJP code. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 15 min. Specify an adjoint test and a directional Taylor or central-difference test for \(f:\mathbb R^p\to\mathbb R^n\). Give the expected error trend and one failure these tests cannot detect.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer: audit a distributed K-FAC run. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Design a report that can distinguish factorization error, stale-statistic error, solve error, damping domination, communication cost, and downstream generative benefit.

    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. For a Bernoulli logit, \(F=\operatorname{Var}(X)=q(1-q)=0.25(0.75)=0.1875\). Therefore \(Fv=0.1875(2)=0.375\). Check. Equivalently, average \((X-q)[(X-q)v]\) over both model outcomes.
    2. P1.2. \(d=(-1/3,7/3)^\top\). Multiplication gives \(Fd=(1,2)^\top=g\), hence zero exact residual. \(g^\top d=-1/3+14/3=13/3\), and because \(Fd=g\), \(d^\top Fd=d^\top g=13/3\). Check. Agreement of the two quadratic calculations catches sign and transpose errors.
    3. P1.3. \(\widehat\delta=\tfrac12(13/3)=13/6\). Thus \(\alpha=\sqrt{0.1/(13/6)}=\sqrt{0.6/13}\approx0.2148\). After proposing the step, measure the actual declared divergence, objective change, and held-out generative behavior; reject or shrink when the local model is inaccurate. Check. The CG residual cannot substitute for these measurements.
    4. P1.4. The model Fisher is \(q(1-q)=0.16\). The observed logit score is \(1-q=0.2\), whose outer product is \(0.04\). The first averages both possible targets under the current model; the second conditions on the one observed target. Check. With observed \(y=0\), the square would instead be \(0.64\), illustrating data dependence.
    5. P1.5. Draw compatible \(u\in\mathbb R^n\) and \(v\in\mathbb R^p\) and verify \(\langle u,Jv\rangle\approx\langle J^\top u,v\rangle\). Compare \(Jv\) with \([f(\theta+hv)-f(\theta-hv)]/(2h)\) across logarithmically spaced \(h\); error should decrease approximately as \(h^2\) before roundoff rises. Match production precision, masks, batches, and randomness. These tests cannot show that \(f\) is the scientifically intended objective or that a GGN equals the desired Fisher. Rubric. Passing code differentiation is necessary, not semantic proof.
    6. P1.6. Record the exact metric and sampling law; factor block definitions, covariance batches, ranks, eigenvalues, update age, and excluded parameters; CG residuals and iteration counts; damping relative to factor eigenvalues; predicted and measured KL; gradient and update norms; bytes, synchronization time, memory, precision, and wall-clock; and likelihood/sample metrics across seeds at matched examples and tuning budget. Ablate factor freshness, rank, damping, and solve tolerance separately. Rubric. Each approximation receives its own diagnostic rather than one end loss.

    Chapter 5 synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Large-scale natural-gradient methods are approximation stacks: stochastic law samples, a matrix-vector product or structured factorization, a regularized solve, and a finite step rule. Correctness requires naming every layer.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why is a score outer product on observed targets not automatically the model Fisher?
    2. What does conjugate-gradient residual measure, and what does it not measure?
    3. Why should the undamped quadratic form be reported when damping is used?

    Solutions. (1) The model Fisher averages outcomes under the model law; observed targets follow the empirical data law. (2) It measures numerical accuracy for the chosen linear system, not whether the chosen matrix is the right Fisher. (3) Damping can dominate the direction; the undamped form estimates movement in the intended statistical metric.

    Bridge. With the computational contract in place, Chapter 6 asks how the Fisher decomposes—or becomes difficult—in normalized, latent, and energy model families.
