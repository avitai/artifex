    Objectives and Statistical Distances¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    A training loss is not merely a score to minimize: it determines which information about the data distribution is observable to the optimizer, how finite samples are weighted, and which model errors are easy to detect. This chapter separates mathematical discrepancies from their estimators and from the neural architectures used to optimize them.

    Reading routes

    Beginner: compare KL, GAN density ratios, MMD, and score matching, then do both worked examples.
    Practitioner: focus on estimator bias, critic constraints, kernel/feature choice, loss reductions, and diagnostics.
    Research: derive each population optimum and then list which assumptions fail for a finite minibatch, finite network, and finite sampler.

    Start with the Information Available to the Optimizer¤

    Suppose the data place mass around two separated regions while a generator currently reaches only one. A learning rule can discover that omission only through the information its objective exposes. Likelihood reads density at data points; a classifier estimates a ratio; an IPM searches for a witness; MMD fixes a kernel witness class; score matching compares local log-density derivatives; transport couples mass across space.

    The diagram is not a tournament bracket. Each branch changes the statistical and computational contract.

    • Population object

      The discrepancy, proper score, divergence, or identifying equation defined for full probability laws.

    • Estimator

      The finite-sample statistic, variational bound, regression target, or Monte Carlo approximation actually computed.

    • Parameterization

      The generator, critic, kernel, representation, or score network that limits the functions available.

    • Optimization path

      The finite updates, regularization, sampling error, and numerical choices that determine the trained result.

    Throughout the chapter, read every claim at all four levels. Equality at a population optimum does not make two finite training procedures equivalent.

    Learning Objectives¤

    • distinguish forward and reverse KL without reducing every failure mode to them;
    • derive the optimal discriminator in the original GAN;
    • compare f-divergences, IPMs, Wasserstein distance, MMD, score matching, and NCE;
    • distinguish a divergence from a metric and Hellinger distance from its square;
    • state density-ratio objectives at the measure level, including singular support;
    • analyze support mismatch and distinguish smoothing, support-compatible discrepancies, and representation changes;
    • explain conditional regression used by diffusion and flow matching;
    • distinguish operational bitrate, Shannon rate, entropy-model cross-entropy, a VAE's variational rate, and token count;
    • identify when a claimed divergence interpretation requires an optimal auxiliary model or population limit.

    1. Forward and Reverse KL¤

    What KL can see

    Forward KL averages under \(p\), so every data-supported region matters. Reverse KL averages under \(q\), so it sees only regions the model visits. This difference explains the zero-avoiding and zero-forcing intuitions; it does not by itself diagnose a particular neural optimizer.

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)=\mathbb E_p\left[\log\frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right]. \]

    Forward KL \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\|p_\theta)\) is infinite when the model assigns zero density where data have positive density, motivating “zero avoiding” or coverage-seeking language. Reverse KL \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\theta\|p_{\mathrm{data}})\) is infinite when the model samples where data density is zero, motivating “zero forcing” or mode-seeking language.

    These descriptions are asymptotic intuitions, not diagnoses of every trained model. Capacity, optimization, support smoothing, estimator bias, finite data, and architecture can dominate. In particular, vanilla GAN mode collapse should not be explained as direct reverse-KL minimization.

    A Two-Mode Thought Experiment¤

    Let \(p\) put equal mass on two separated modes while \(q_\theta\) can represent only one narrow mode. Forward KL is infinite if the omitted data mode has positive mass and \(q\) assigns it zero probability. Reverse KL can remain finite when \(q\) stays inside either supported mode, so it may prefer one mode over placing mass between them. If \(q\) instead has fixed full support, neither divergence is infinite and the optimum depends on its parameterization. “Coverage-seeking” and “mode-seeking” summarize this geometry; they do not predict a particular neural training run.

    KL Is Not a Metric¤

    KL is asymmetric and violates the triangle inequality. Jensen–Shannon divergence symmetrizes through a mixture. Define

    \[ \begin{aligned} m&=\tfrac12(p+q),\\ D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p\|q)=\tfrac12D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|m)+\tfrac12D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|m). \end{aligned} \]

    It remains finite and bounded by \(\log2\) for natural logarithms, including disjoint supports. That boundedness can produce saturated discrimination and weak generator gradients when supports are separated.

    2. f-Divergences and Density-Ratio Estimation¤

    The ratio \(p(x)/q(x)\) is the central object in this section. If neither density can be evaluated, a classifier can estimate a monotone transform of that ratio from labeled samples—but only to the extent that its class, training data, and optimization approximate the population discriminator.

    For convex \(f\) with \(f(1)=0\),

    \[ D_f(p\|q)=\mathbb E_q\left[f\left(\frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right)\right]. \]

    The Measure-Level Definition Includes Singular Mass¤

    The compact density formula assumes a common dominating measure. Such a measure always exists for two probability laws—for example \(\mu=P+Q\)—with Radon--Nikodym derivatives \(p=dP/d\mu\) and \(q=dQ/d\mu\). Then

    \[ D_f(P\|Q)=\int q(x)f\!\left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right)\mu(dx), \]

    where the integrand at \(q(x)=0\) is defined by the lower-semicontinuous extended-value limit. This convention is not cosmetic. Forward KL is infinite when \(P\) assigns positive mass to a region where \(Q\) assigns none, whereas JS remains finite because each law is compared with their mixture. A neural classifier trained on samples does not remove these measure-level facts; it approximates a variational witness under a finite function class and dataset.

    Strict convexity of \(f\) at one supplies the usual population identification \(D_f(P\|Q)=0\Rightarrow P=Q\). Symmetry and the triangle inequality are separate properties and usually fail. The word “distance” in an algorithm name should therefore not be treated as a metric theorem.

    Hellinger, Total Variation, and Chi-Squared Conventions¤

    Using the same dominating measure, define Hellinger distance by

    \[ H^2(P,Q)=\frac12\int(\sqrt p-\sqrt q)^2\,d\mu. \]

    The unsquared distance is

    \[ H(P,Q)=\sqrt{H^2(P,Q)}. \]

    Total variation has equivalent density and event forms:

    \[ d_{\mathrm{TV}}(P,Q)=\frac12\int|p-q|\,d\mu. \]

    Equivalently,

    \[ d_{\mathrm{TV}}(P,Q)=\sup_A|P(A)-Q(A)|. \]

    The Hellinger distance \(H\) is a metric; its square \(H^2\) generally does not satisfy the triangle inequality. Both are bounded and independent of the chosen dominating measure, but a paper must say which one it reports. Total variation is also a metric and has the operational event interpretation shown above. Neither metric supplies a geometry between nearby observations unless that geometry enters through the laws or another construction.

    One Pearson chi-squared convention is

    \[ r_{\chi^2}(x)=\frac{(p(x)-q(x))^2}{q(x)}. \]

    Then

    \[ \chi^2(P\|Q)=\int r_{\chi^2}(x)\,\mu(dx). \]

    with value \(+\infty\) when the required absolute continuity fails. Some sources reverse \(P,Q\) or insert a factor of one half. Orientation and scale must be recorded before comparing a result or mapping it to a discriminator loss.

    Under the regularity conditions of convex duality, the variational representation used by f-GAN (Nowozin et al., 2016) is

    \[ \mathcal J_f(T)=\mathbb E_p[T(X)]-\mathbb E_q[f^*(T(X))]. \]

    The divergence is the best admissible value of this witness objective:

    \[ D_f(p\|q)=\sup_{T\in\operatorname{dom}f^*}\mathcal J_f(T), \]

    where the supremum ranges over all admissible measurable functions. Replacing that class by a neural family \(\mathcal T\) gives a lower bound,

    \[ \sup_{T\in\mathcal T}\mathcal J_f(T)\le D_f(p\|q). \]

    Equality therefore belongs to the unrestricted population problem, not automatically to a trained critic. Alternating finite neural updates optimize a game approximation, not the exact divergence at every step.

    The critic has two roles that must not be conflated: it is an estimator of a density-ratio-related variational objective, and it supplies gradients to move generator samples. A critic can classify well while providing geometrically unhelpful gradients. Regularization and feature architecture alter both roles.

    Original GAN discriminator¤

    For

    \[ V(D,G)=\mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}}\log D(x) +\mathbb E_{p_g}\log(1-D(x)), \]

    pointwise maximization gives

    \[ D^*(x)=\frac{p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)}{p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)+p_g(x)}. \]

    Substitution gives the population value

    \[ -\log4+2D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\|p_g). \]

    The non-saturating generator loss changes gradients while retaining the same ideal fixed point; it should not be described as simply minimizing the same finite-step objective.

    If real examples have class prior \(\pi\) and generated examples have prior \(1-\pi\), the Bayes-optimal classifier satisfies

    \[ D^*_{\pi}(x) =\frac{\pi p(x)}{\pi p(x)+(1-\pi)q(x)}, \qquad \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} =\frac{1-\pi}{\pi}\frac{D^*_{\pi}(x)}{1-D^*_{\pi}(x)}. \]

    With equal class priors, the prior correction disappears and the optimal discriminator logit is

    \[ \log\frac{D^*(x)}{1-D^*(x)} =\log\frac{p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)}{p_g(x)}. \]

    This is the density-ratio connection. It assumes densities relative to a common reference measure and a population-optimal discriminator. The numeric discriminator loss alone cannot diagnose mode coverage, memorization, or equilibrium.

    The same identity has a specialized dependence interpretation in Copula Models: after exact continuous marginal transforms, the product reference is uniform and the density ratio is the copula density. Finite classifier calibration, normalization, marginal validity, and sampling remain separate obligations.

    3. Integral Probability Metrics¤

    Instead of estimating a density ratio, an IPM asks for a function whose expected value differs most between the two laws. The function class determines what differences are visible and how reliably that witness can be estimated.

    An IPM compares expectations over a function class \(\mathcal F\):

    \[ d_{\mathcal F}(p,q) =\sup_{f\in\mathcal F}\left|\mathbb E_p[f(X)]-\mathbb E_q[f(X)]\right|. \]

    The function class defines the discrepancy. Wasserstein-1 uses 1-Lipschitz functions through Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality:

    \[ W_1(p,q)=\sup_{\|f\|_L\le1} \left(\mathbb E_p f-\mathbb E_qf\right). \]

    WGAN approximates this with a neural critic and an imperfect Lipschitz constraint. Weight clipping, gradient penalties, spectral normalization, and other regularizers enforce different approximations; none proves mode collapse impossible.

    The primal Wasserstein-1 distance is the minimum expected transport cost \(\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(p,q)}\mathbb E_\pi\|X-Y\|\). Unlike JS, it varies continuously as two disjoint point masses move through space. That gives a useful geometric signal in idealized settings. A neural WGAN critic estimates a restricted dual and can still be biased, underoptimized, or violate global Lipschitzness.

    4. Maximum Mean Discrepancy¤

    MMD fixes the witness geometry through a kernel. This removes the alternating critic optimization used by a GAN, but it does not remove the modeling choice: bandwidth and representation decide which sample differences count as nearby.

    For an RKHS \(\mathcal H\) with kernel \(k\),

    \[ \operatorname{MMD}^2(p,q) =\mathbb E_{x,x'\sim p}k(x,x') +\mathbb E_{y,y'\sim q}k(y,y') -2\mathbb E_{x\sim p,y\sim q}k(x,y). \]

    Characteristic kernels make population MMD zero only when \(p=q\). Finite-sample estimators may be biased or unbiased; kernel bandwidth and representation determine test power. GMMNs train an implicit generator directly with this discrepancy, showing that implicit generation does not require an adversarial critic.

    For samples \(x_{1:m}\sim p\) and \(y_{1:n}\sim q\), the V-statistic includes diagonal kernel terms, while the unbiased U-statistic removes \(i=j\) terms within each sample set. The unbiased estimate of squared MMD can be negative at finite sample size even though population \(\operatorname{MMD}^2\ge0\). This is estimator fluctuation, not a negative population distance.

    Kernel choice defines which discrepancies are visible. An RBF kernel on raw pixels may be dominated by low-level distances; a kernel on pretrained features inherits that encoder's invariances and biases. Calling feature-space MMD “distribution-free” hides a representation choice.

    A Discrepancy Value Is Not Yet a Two-Sample Test¤

    MMD can be a differentiable population objective, a minibatch training loss, a descriptive validation statistic, or the statistic inside a calibrated hypothesis test. These uses are not interchangeable. A level-\(\alpha\) test needs a null distribution or valid resampling/asymptotic calibration, a declared estimator, and a rule fixed independently of the observed test outcome. Selecting the kernel on the same data to maximize separation, checking the test repeatedly during training, or stopping when it rejects changes the decision procedure and can invalidate its nominal level unless the calibration accounts for that adaptivity. The full protocol appears in Generative Model Evaluation.

    One Comparison Spine: Which Witness Can See the Difference?¤

    Sample-based objectives can look similar in code because each compares expectations under data and model samples. Their mathematical contracts differ in the witness class, the penalty or constraint imposed on that witness, and whether the witness is fixed, solved analytically, or learned by alternating optimization.

    Table OBJ.1. One Comparison Spine: Which Witness Can See the Difference?
    Comparison Population form What defines the witness What equality identifies Optimization consequence
    \(f\)-divergence variational form \(\sup_T\{\mathbb E_pT-\mathbb E_q f^*(T)\}\) all admissible \(T\) for equality; often a restricted neural class in practice \(p=q\) when the chosen \(f\) is strictly convex at \(1\) and the unrestricted divergence is zero the conjugate penalty is part of the objective; a restricted or underoptimized critic supplies only a lower bound
    integral probability metric \(\sup_{h\in\mathcal F}(\mathbb E_ph-\mathbb E_qh)\) for symmetric \(\mathcal F\) the declared function class \(\mathcal F\) depends on whether \(\mathcal F\) separates probability laws the constraint on \(\mathcal F\) defines the geometry; there is generally no density-ratio interpretation
    finite feature moments \(\|\mathbb E_p\phi-\mathbb E_q\phi\|_2\) a fixed finite feature map \(\phi:\mathcal X\to\mathbb R^d\) equality of the declared feature means, not generally equality of distributions the maximizing linear witness is analytic; no alternating critic is required
    MMD \(\|\mu_p-\mu_q\|_{\mathcal H_k}\) the unit ball of the RKHS for a fixed kernel \(k\) \(p=q\) for characteristic \(k\) on the stated domain and measure class the population witness is analytic and the empirical loss is direct; learning the kernel adversarially creates a different game

    Finite moment matching is itself an IPM over the linear class \(h_w(x)=w^\top\phi(x)\) with \(\|w\|_2\le1\). MMD extends this construction to a possibly infinite-dimensional RKHS feature map. An \(f\)-divergence bound is not merely another IPM: the \(q\)-expectation contains the nonlinear conjugate \(f^*(T)\) rather than the same witness appearing in a difference of expectations. These are algebraic distinctions, not names for interchangeable neural losses.

    Four levels should consequently be written separately:

    1. the population discrepancy, defined after its supremum or analytic witness is solved;
    2. the auxiliary optimum for the current model distribution;
    3. the implemented generator loss while the current auxiliary model is held fixed; and
    4. the optimization path produced by finite samples, restricted networks, update ratios, and optimizer state.

    Agreement at the population optimum does not make levels 3 or 4 identical. The finite example near the end of this chapter computes the distinction explicitly.

    Discrepancy retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: KL, ratios, IPMs, and MMD
    1. Why do forward and reverse KL respond differently to missing support and extra modes?
    2. What information does a density-ratio classifier estimate at population optimum?
    3. How does an IPM depend on its critic function class?
    4. When is an empirical MMD equal to zero without proving equality of population laws?

    Solutions. (1) Forward KL averages under the data law and heavily penalizes assigning too little model density where data occur; reverse KL averages under the model and can avoid unsupported data regions while penalizing model mass in low-data-density areas. (2) A monotone transform of the data/model density ratio under the declared class priors and classification loss. (3) The class determines which expectation differences are visible and whether the discrepancy identifies the full law. (4) With finite samples, a non-characteristic kernel, biased estimator coincidences, or degenerate features; population identification additionally requires a characteristic kernel and assumptions.

    Discrepancy synthesis¤

    Every discrepancy exposes only information available through its sampling, density-ratio, score, coupling, or critic interface. Direction, support, function class, estimator, and finite optimization determine behavior. Naming a divergence does not by itself specify the trained generator.

    Support Mismatch: Three Remedies, Three Different Contracts¤

    Support, score, ratio, and regression learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes density ratios, gradients, and conditional expectation. By the end, you should be able to diagnose singular support mismatch, distinguish smoothing from changing discrepancy or representation, derive score matching and noise-contrastive objectives, identify their boundary and normalization assumptions, and explain how conditional regression can learn a marginal target only after the relevant conditional expectation is taken.

    Support mismatch is not one scalar pathology. It is a mismatch among a target law, a model law, a reference measure, and the topology induced by the objective. A probability distribution can be perfectly valid while having no density with respect to ambient Lebesgue measure. For example, a distribution on a curve in \(\mathbb R^2\) may have a density with respect to arc length but be singular with respect to two-dimensional area. The first question is therefore not “does the density exist?” but “with respect to which measure, and which comparison is the optimizer estimating?”

    A finite example that becomes a singular continuous example¤

    Begin with two probability laws on four points in \(\mathbb R^2\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} P &=\tfrac12\delta_{(-1,0)}+\tfrac12\delta_{(1,0)},\\ Q_h &=\tfrac12\delta_{(-1,h)}+\tfrac12\delta_{(1,h)}. \end{aligned} \]

    For every \(h\ne0\), the supports are disjoint. Consequently,

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|Q_h) &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(Q_h\|P)=\infty,\\ D_{\mathrm{JS}}(P\|Q_h) &=\log 2. \end{aligned} \]

    Neither value records whether the two rows of points are a millimeter or a kilometer apart. By contrast, with Euclidean transport cost,

    \[ W_1(P,Q_h)=|h|. \]

    Pairing each point with the point directly above or below it gives the upper bound \(W_1\le |h|\). The function \(f(x_1,x_2)=\operatorname{sign}(h)x_2\) is 1-Lipschitz, so the Kantorovich--Rubinstein dual gives the matching lower bound. Thus \(Q_h\) converges to \(P\) in \(W_1\) as \(h\to0\), while JS remains at its maximum until the supports coincide. This is the elementary geometry behind the continuity result used by Wasserstein GANs; it is not a claim that a finite neural critic computes \(W_1\) exactly.

    Now replace the two horizontal coordinates by a continuous random variable \(U\sim\operatorname{Unif}[-1,1]\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} X&=(U,0),\\ Y_h&=(U,h). \end{aligned} \]

    The laws of \(X\) and \(Y_h\) are supported on parallel line segments. Both are singular with respect to two-dimensional Lebesgue measure, and they are mutually singular when \(h\ne0\). The same coupling and dual witness still prove \(W_1(\mathcal L(X),\mathcal L(Y_h))=|h|\). The finite construction therefore exposes the same distinction as a continuum-supported model: a comparison based on density ratios can saturate or become infinite even while the model law approaches the target weakly.

    This example establishes a problem, not a universal winner. There are at least three materially different responses.

    Remedy 1: smooth both laws¤

    Let \(S_\sigma P\) denote the law of \(X+\varepsilon\) for \(X\sim P\) and \(\varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I)\). Gaussian convolution turns any finite measure on \(\mathbb R^d\) into a smooth, strictly positive density, so it removes the zero-overlap singularity in the example. A spread objective can then compare

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(S_\sigma P\|S_\sigma Q). \]

    Because the Gaussian characteristic function is nowhere zero, \(S_\sigma P=S_\sigma Q\) implies \(P=Q\). Smoothing can therefore preserve the population zero set even though the loss compares blurred laws. This is the identification argument behind Spread Divergence. Common support alone does not guarantee that every KL is finite for arbitrary heavy-tailed laws; the required log-ratio integrability or tail conditions must still be checked.

    The bandwidth \(\sigma\) changes what finite data and finite networks can resolve. Large \(\sigma\) joins separated regions and supplies broad gradients but suppresses fine structure. Small \(\sigma\) is closer to the endpoint law but can restore sharp ratios, high estimator variance, and difficult low-noise learning. Diffusion objectives use multiple noise levels rather than pretending that one bandwidth solves both global movement and local detail.

    The smoothing kernel is also a geometric modeling choice. For a law subject to known equality constraints, isotropic noise moves both normal to and along the constraint set. A 2026 ICML result instead perturbs along normal spaces and projects samples back; it proves exact recovery for linear constraints and gives a curvature-dependent total-variation bound for nonlinear manifolds under its stated reach and regularity assumptions (Keegan and Ruthotto, 2026). The lesson is not that one perturbation is universal. It is that the noise law and any recovery map belong in the model specification.

    Remedy 2: choose a comparison that remains informative without overlap¤

    Wasserstein distance and some kernel discrepancies can compare singular laws directly. Their assumptions differ:

    • On a compact metric space, convergence in \(W_1\) is equivalent to weak convergence. On an unbounded space, \(W_p\) convergence additionally controls the relevant \(p\)th moments; finite \(W_1\) requires finite first moments.
    • A characteristic kernel makes population MMD zero only when the laws are equal. Characteristicness alone does not guarantee that MMD metrizes weak convergence on every topological space. The kernel, its boundedness and continuity properties, and the class of probability measures matter.
    • A neural WGAN replaces the full 1-Lipschitz function class by a trained, regularized critic family. A learned-kernel MMD likewise replaces a fixed identifying witness class by an optimization problem. These implementations may supply useful gradients, but their finite objectives need not equal the named population distances.

    The kernel two-sample analysis makes the statistical trade-off explicit: a witness class must be rich enough to identify unequal distributions yet restricted enough to estimate from finite samples. “Works on disjoint supports” therefore means that the population quantity is defined and can vary; it does not mean that every empirical estimator has high power or every generator receives a non-vanishing gradient.

    Remedy 3: learn a state space, then model its law¤

    A two-stage system first learns an encoder \(E:\mathcal X\to\mathcal Z\) and decoder \(G:\mathcal Z\to\mathcal X\), then fits a latent model \(R\) to the encoded law \(E_\#P\). Its output law is \(G_\#R\). This changes the space in which distribution learning occurs; it does not make the original support question disappear.

    For a concrete error decomposition, suppose \(G\) is \(L\)-Lipschitz and all laws have finite first moments. Coupling each \(X\sim P\) with its reconstruction \(G(E(X))\), then using stability of \(W_1\) under a Lipschitz map, gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} W_1(P,G_\#R) &\le W_1(P,G_\#E_\#P)\\ &\quad +W_1(G_\#E_\#P,G_\#R)\\ &\le \mathbb E_{X\sim P}\|X-G(E(X))\|\\ &\quad +L\,W_1(E_\#P,R). \end{aligned} \]

    The two terms are different failure modes: codec distortion and latent distribution error. Perfect population reconstruction together with \(R=E_\#P\) recovers \(P\). Finite autoencoders and finite latent models need not satisfy either condition.

    Representation-level matching can also destroy identification. If \(E(x)=0\) for every \(x\), then \(E_\#P=E_\#Q\) for all \(P\) and \(Q\), so every identifying latent discrepancy is zero even when the observation laws are unrelated. More generally, a non-injective encoder can erase distinctions that matter for generation. Exact two-step recovery results require assumptions such as reconstruction almost surely, matched intrinsic dimension, sufficient regularity, and a correctly learned encoded law (Loaiza-Ganem et al., 2022). The related Wasserstein interpretation is an upper-bound result under its own cost, measurability, and population assumptions; it is not a blanket theorem that any learned codec optimizes observation-space Wasserstein distance (Loaiza-Ganem et al., 2024).

    Latent diffusion and learned-token systems instantiate this decomposition operationally: a codec fixes the state space, and a separate generator learns its aggregate latent law. Reconstruction quality, latent learnability, and decoded sample quality must all be measured. A low latent loss cannot certify information that the encoder removed.

    3. Optimization: does the parameterized loss provide a useful path from the current model to the optimum?

    A theorem answering the first question does not answer the other two. Conversely, a deliberately biased finite critic may provide useful gradients even when it is not an accurate divergence estimator. Objective selection should state which of these properties is being claimed.

    5. Score Matching and Fisher Divergence¤

    From global normalization to local slope

    A score field describes how log density changes around a point. Because the derivative of a global normalizing constant is zero, score matching can train an unnormalized model without evaluating that constant. It still needs suitable boundary conditions, data-space geometry, and an estimator of the population score.

    The score is \(s_p(x)=\nabla_x\log p(x)\). Fisher divergence compares scores:

    \[ D_F(p\|q)=\frac12\mathbb E_p\|s_p(X)-s_q(X)\|^2. \]

    Hyvärinen score matching removes the unknown data score by integration by parts, under boundary and differentiability conditions:

    \[ \mathcal J(\theta) =\mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}} \left[\frac12\|s_\theta(x)\|^2+\nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)\right] +\text{constant}. \]

    Denoising score matching perturbs data and regresses the conditional perturbation score. Its population optimum is the marginal perturbed-data score through conditional expectation. It avoids the data Hessian but introduces a noise-level/path design.

    For Gaussian corruption \(x_t=\alpha_t x_0+\sigma_t\epsilon\), the conditional score is

    \[ \nabla_{x_t}\log p(x_t\mid x_0) =-\frac{x_t-\alpha_t x_0}{\sigma_t^2} =-\frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t}. \]

    The marginal score is its posterior conditional expectation given \(x_t\); the two are not equal sample by sample. This distinction is the reason a network trained on conditional targets can recover a marginal field at the population optimum.

    Integration by parts requires differentiability and vanishing or controlled boundary terms. On bounded domains, manifolds, or discrete spaces, ordinary Euclidean score matching must be modified. A low score-matching loss at noisy levels also does not automatically imply accurate normalized likelihood at the data endpoint.

    6. Noise-Contrastive Estimation¤

    NCE turns unnormalized density estimation into classification between data and known noise. If data-to-noise ratio is \(1:k\), the optimal probability that a sample came from data is

    \[ P(y=1\mid x)=\frac{p_\theta(x)}{p_\theta(x)+k p_n(x)}. \]

    Parameterizing an unnormalized \(p_\theta\) and, when needed, its log normalizer allows consistent estimation under suitable noise, identifiability, and asymptotic conditions. Poor noise overlap produces an easy classifier but weak density-ratio information.

    NCE differs from negative sampling used as a heuristic embedding objective: consistency as a density estimator depends on the exact classification construction, known noise law, treatment of the normalizer, and asymptotic regime. The number and quality of noise samples trade compute against estimator variance.

    6.1 The unnormalized-model objective family¤

    For \(p_\theta(x)=e^{-E_\theta(x)}/Z_\theta\), the likelihood gradient removes explicit evaluation of \(Z_\theta\) but introduces an exact model expectation. The main alternatives are transformations of this obstacle, not progressively more approximate spellings of one algorithm:

    and a Stein discrepancy is the supremum of \(|\mathbb E_P[\mathcal T_{p_\theta}f]|\) over a declared class. It inherits the useful cancellation \(s_\theta=-\nabla E_\theta\), but zero identifies equality only when the operator and witness class are sufficiently rich under their regularity assumptions. The reusable lesson is to report population identification, finite estimation, and parameter optimization as three separate claims.

    7. Conditional Regression as Marginal Learning¤

    Diffusion and flow matching often create tractable conditional targets \(u(x_t\mid x_1)\) and train

    \[ \mathbb E\|u_\theta(x_t,t)-u(x_t\mid x_1)\|^2. \]

    The squared-error optimum is

    \[ u^*(x,t)=\mathbb E[u(x_t\mid x_1)\mid x_t=x], \]

    which is the required marginal field under the construction's assumptions. This conditional-to-marginal regression principle unifies several objectives, but it does not make their paths, samplers, or finite-network errors identical.

    If the loss is weighted by \(w(t,x_t)\), the optimum becomes a weighted conditional expectation unless the weight depends only on variables held fixed in the conditioning. Seemingly harmless loss weights can therefore change the learned field, not merely its optimization emphasis.

    Support remedies are modeling decisions: smoothing, changing the comparison, and learning a representation answer different questions. Scores and ratios can avoid explicit normalizers but introduce derivative, boundary, noise, and conditioning contracts.     8. Optimal Transport Objectives¤

    Transport, information, and composite-objective learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes couplings, KL divergence, and latent-variable learning. By the end, you should be able to distinguish primal transport, entropic regularization, and learned-critic approximations; derive rate–distortion and information-bottleneck operating points; separate VAE rate, distortion, and actual code length; and audit composite objectives for scale, estimator, identifiability, and Pareto tradeoffs rather than reading coefficients as model semantics.

    Optimal transport introduces an explicit geometric cost for moving mass. A small distance therefore means little only relative to that chosen ground cost; raw pixels, learned features, molecular coordinates, and manifold geodesics encode different notions of a useful move.

    The primal transport problem is

    \[ W_c(p,q)=\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(p,q)}\mathbb E_{(x,y)\sim\pi}[c(x,y)]. \]

    Choosing a coupling affects path geometry and regression variance. Entropic regularization trades exact transport cost for smoother, computationally tractable couplings and leads to Sinkhorn algorithms and Schrödinger bridges. A minibatch OT coupling is itself an approximation whose bias depends on batch construction.

    The dynamic Benamou–Brenier formulation writes quadratic transport as

    \[ W_2^2(p_0,p_1)= \inf_{(p_t,v_t)}\int_0^1\mathbb E_{x\sim p_t}\|v_t(x)\|^2dt \]

    subject to \(\partial_t p_t+\nabla\cdot(p_tv_t)=0\) and the endpoint distributions. Flow matching may learn a vector field along a chosen probability path without solving this minimum-action problem. Using an OT coupling can straighten paths and reduce target variance, but “flow matching” and “optimal transport” are not synonyms.

    9. Rate–Distortion and Information Bottlenecks¤

    The Information, Coding, and Generative Models volume supplies the source-coding theorem, mutual-information prerequisites, operational channel distinctions, and description-length boundaries. This section retains the objective-centered derivation needed to compare generative training losses.

    Lossless coding asks the decoder to recover a discrete message exactly. Lossy coding permits a reconstruction \(\hat X\) and must therefore state what counts as an error. For a block \(X^n\), an encoder emits a finite bitstring \(M\) and a decoder maps it to \(\hat X^n\). Two operational quantities are

    \[ \begin{aligned} R_n &=\frac{\mathbb E[|M|]}{n},\\ D_n &=\frac1n\mathbb E\!\left[ \sum_{i=1}^{n}d(X_i,\hat X_i) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The rate counts transmitted bits, including whatever the protocol requires. The distortion is an expected task-declared cost, such as squared error, a perceptual feature distance, or a domain-specific penalty. Zero distortion means exact recovery only when \(d(x,\hat x)=0\) implies \(x=\hat x\).

    For a memoryless source and the usual asymptotic block-coding assumptions, Shannon's rate–distortion function is

    \[ R(D_0)= \inf_{r(\hat x\mid x):\, \mathbb E[d(X,\hat X)]\leq D_0} I(X;\hat X). \]

    It gives the minimum asymptotic rate compatible with expected distortion at most \(D_0\) under the declared source and distortion. It is not the loss of a particular neural codec, and one finite architecture need not attain it. The original fidelity-criterion coding theorem is due to Shannon (1959).

    Lagrangians select operating points¤

    Constrained coding problems are commonly trained with either

    \[ R+\lambda D \qquad\text{or}\qquad D+\beta R. \]

    These conventions invert the role and units of the multiplier: \(\lambda\) prices distortion in bits, whereas \(\beta\) prices rate in distortion units. Increasing \(\beta\) in the second convention favors lower rate, not universally “better compression,” because the chosen distortion may ignore important information. Lagrangian sweeps recover supported points of an ideal convex trade-off; finite data, quantization relaxations, non-convex networks, and imperfect optimization can leave gaps or dominated solutions.

    Learned codecs have several rates¤

    Let a learned encoder quantize to \(\hat z\) and let an entropy model be \(m_\psi(\hat z)\). Its model-based ideal rate is

    \[ R_{\mathrm{model}} =\mathbb E[-\log_2m_\psi(\hat Z)]. \]

    An arithmetic or ANS implementation turns those probabilities into a bitstream. Actual rate also includes finite-precision and termination overhead, side information, model headers when they are not shared, container metadata, and padding. Token count, latent scalar count, vocabulary size, empirical token entropy, entropy-model cross-entropy, and measured file size are consequently different quantities. End-to-end learned compression optimizes this rate proxy jointly with distortion; see Ballé, Laparra, and Simoncelli (2017) and the later scale-hyperprior construction of Ballé et al. (2018).

    VAE rate, distortion, and bits back¤

    Latent probabilistic models often use the variational quantities

    \[ R_{\mathrm{var}} =\mathbb E_xD_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z\mid x)\|p(z)), \qquad D_{\mathrm{NLL}} =-\mathbb E_{x,z}\log p(x\mid z). \]

    With base-two logarithms, their sum is the negative ELBO in bits. A naive two-part code appears to pay the cross-entropy of \(q(z\mid x)\) under \(p(z)\) plus the residual code under \(p(x\mid z)\). A bits-back construction uses initial random coder state to choose \(z\sim q(z\mid x)\) and recovers information corresponding to \(-\log_2q(z\mid x)\) during decoding. Its net ideal expected cost is therefore

    \[ \begin{aligned} -\operatorname{ELBO}_2(x) &=\mathbb E_{q(z\mid x)}\!\left[ -\log_2p(x,z)\right.\\ &\qquad\left.+\log_2q(z\mid x) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    This is a coding interpretation with conditions, not a claim that an ordinary VAE checkpoint is already a compressor. A concrete implementation needs a discrete coding construction, synchronized probability tables, an initial bit reservoir, and a strategy such as message chaining to amortize startup cost. Approximate inference and finite coder precision create overhead. Townsend, Bird, and Barber (2019) demonstrate a practical lossless bits-back construction using ANS.

    Changing \(\beta\) in a \(\beta\)-VAE optimizes

    \[ D_{\mathrm{NLL}}+\beta R_{\mathrm{var}}. \]

    This is a variational compression trade-off, not a guarantee of disentanglement and not automatically the operational rate–distortion curve of a deployed codec. Learned representations must be evaluated both for reconstruction and for how easily a downstream prior models their latents.

    The variational rate decomposes into mutual information plus aggregate-posterior mismatch:

    \[ \mathbb E_xD_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z\mid x)\|p(z)) =I_q(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z)\|p(z)). \]

    Thus a lower variational rate can mean less instance information, better prior matching, or both. It upper-bounds \(I_q(X;Z)\), but it is not simply “the information in the latent” unless the aggregate posterior matches the prior. Distortion also depends on the decoder likelihood: pixel MSE corresponds to a fixed-variance Gaussian assumption, not a universal perceptual distance.

    Distortion is not perception or utility¤

    Two reconstructions can have the same average distortion while differing in realism, semantic correctness, or downstream value. Conversely, a perceptually plausible decoder may replace an uncertain detail with a realistic but incorrect one. The perception–distortion result of Blau and Michaeli (2018) formalizes a conflict between reconstruction distortion and distributional perceptual quality under its assumptions. Scientific, medical, identity, text, and control applications must report fidelity to the particular information that matters rather than treating visual plausibility as a universal distortion measure.

    Misconception check

    A tokenizer maps a \(256\times256\) image to \(16\times16\) categorical tokens from a vocabulary of size 8192. Has the image been compressed to exactly \(256\log_2 8192=3328\) bits?

    Solution. No. That is a nominal fixed-width index count. Actual storage depends on the token distribution, context-dependent entropy model, entropy coder, side information, headers, and implementation overhead. If the tokens are never serialized—for example, they only reduce a latent generator's compute—there may be no bitstream at all.

    10. Composite Objectives and Identifiability¤

    Modern systems often combine reconstruction, adversarial, perceptual, score, preference, and regularization terms:

    \[ \mathcal J(\theta)=\sum_j\lambda_j\mathcal J_j(\theta). \]

    This sum generally does not minimize one named divergence unless a derivation proves it. Scale depends on reduction, feature dimension, noise level, and critic state, so coefficient values are not portable defaults. Report each term, its units/reduction, gradient norms or conflicts, and ablations.

    A reward or constraint can also define a new endpoint target \(\pi(x)\propto p_0(x)w(x)\). That change of measure is distinct from adding \(w\) as an uncalibrated training penalty. The target-law chapter separates likelihood conditioning, exponential tilting, hard feasibility, and the algorithms used to approximate each target.

    Even if a discrepancy identifies the data distribution over observations, it need not identify latent variables, causal mechanisms, or a unique parameter vector. Distributional identifiability and representation identifiability are different research questions.

(4) The full Pareto tradeoff, coefficient normalization, estimator variance, training budget, uncertainty, and the intended decision criterion rather than a single selected score.

    Transport and information synthesis¤

    Transport objectives require a geometry and coupling class. Information Lagrangians choose operating points rather than universally optimal models, and learned codecs distinguish variational rates from realized code lengths. Composite losses combine estimands with scale and optimization interactions, so their coefficients must be treated as an explicit multi-objective design.

    Worked Example: MMD for Two Point Masses¤

    Objective-selection capstone learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the discrepancy, support, transport, and information chapters. By the end, you should be able to compute MMD on a finite example, convert a calibrated classifier into a density-ratio estimate and generator update, choose an objective from available information and support geometry, and use the checklist to separate the population discrepancy from its estimator, optimization, and deployed sampling procedure.

    Let \(p=\delta_0\), \(q=\delta_a\), and use the RBF kernel \(k(x,y)=\exp(-(x-y)^2/(2\sigma^2))\). Then

    \[ \operatorname{MMD}^2(p,q) =2-2\exp\left(-\frac{a^2}{2\sigma^2}\right). \]

    For \(|a|\gg\sigma\), MMD approaches \(2\) and its gradient with respect to \(a\) becomes small; for \(\sigma\) too large, distinct points look similar. Multi-scale kernels mitigate but do not remove representation and bandwidth choices.

    Worked Example: From a Classifier to a Ratio, Value, and Generator Step¤

    Let the data law be \(p=(0.75,0.25)\) and let the model family be \(q_\theta=(\theta,1-\theta)\) on outcomes \((a,b)\). Begin at \(\theta=0.5\) and construct a balanced real-versus-generated classification problem. Its joint class masses are

    Table OBJ.4. Worked Example: From a Classifier to a Ratio, Value, and Generator Step
    Outcome real class, \(\tfrac12p(x)\) generated class, \(\tfrac12q_{0.5}(x)\)
    \(a\) \(0.375\) \(0.25\)
    \(b\) \(0.125\) \(0.25\)

    Dividing each real-class mass by the total mass at that outcome gives the Bayes classifier:

    \[ D^*(a)=\frac{0.75}{0.75+0.5}=0.6, \qquad D^*(b)=\frac{0.25}{0.25+0.5}=\frac13. \]

    Its odds recover the density ratios:

    \[ \frac{D^*(a)}{1-D^*(a)}=1.5=\frac{p_{\mathrm{data}}(a)}{p_g(a)}, \]
    \[ \frac{D^*(b)}{1-D^*(b)}=0.5=\frac{p_{\mathrm{data}}(b)}{p_g(b)}. \]

    With unequal class priors, these raw odds would also contain the prior odds; the correction \((1-\pi)/\pi\) is essential. The classifier is therefore not merely labeling individual samples. At its population optimum, with declared priors, it represents a pointwise relation between the two laws.

    Now keep the equal-prior convention used by the original GAN value, which omits factors of \(1/2\). Substitution gives

    \[ V(D^*,q_{0.5})=-1.318650 =-\log4+2D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p\|q_{0.5}), \]

    so \(D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p\|q_{0.5})=0.033822\) nats. This is the optimized population value for the current \(q\); it is not yet a prescription for a finite generator update.

    Frozen-Discriminator Losses Are Different Functions¤

    To see why, freeze the two classifier outputs and compare two generator losses. The minimax generator term is

    \[ L_{\mathrm{MM}}(\theta) =\theta\log(1-0.6)+(1-\theta)\log(1-1/3), \]

    whereas the non-saturating loss is

    \[ L_{\mathrm{NS}}(\theta) =-\theta\log0.6-(1-\theta)\log(1/3). \]

    At \(\theta=0.5\) their values and derivatives are

    \[ \begin{array}{c|c|c} &L(0.5)&dL/d\theta\\ \hline L_{\mathrm{MM}}&-0.660878&\log0.6=-0.510826\\ L_{\mathrm{NS}}&0.804719&\log(5/9)=-0.587787. \end{array} \]

    Gradient descent increases \(\theta\) in both frozen-classifier calculations, toward the target value \(0.75\), but the objectives and gradients are not equal. Reoptimizing \(D\) after \(\theta\) changes produces yet another function of \(\theta\). Equating a frozen-discriminator derivative with the derivative of the optimized value requires the relevant envelope-theorem assumptions and the same underlying objective; it cannot be assumed for the non-saturating replacement.

    Finally, a finite classifier can memorize sample identity, and alternating neural updates need not track \(D^*_{q_\theta}\). Held-out discrimination, regularization, generator-side coverage metrics, and game dynamics therefore remain necessary even though every population number in this example is exact.

    Summary and Next Step¤

    Objectives expose different information through different estimators. Divergence names apply only when their auxiliary optima and assumptions hold. Support mismatch can be addressed by smoothing, a support-compatible discrepancy, or a learned state space, but each route changes a different object and carries different identification and optimization conditions. Rate–distortion analysis likewise requires separating transmitted bits, model cross-entropy, variational KL, reconstruction cost, perception, and task utility.
