    7. Diagnostic Assessment and Personalized Next Routes¤

    Prerequisites and learning objectives for Chapter 7¤

    This chapter assumes all earlier first-course chapters. After it, you should be able to:

    1. demonstrate the first-course outcomes without relying on recognition alone;
    2. locate a specific conceptual gap from your response, not merely a total score;
    3. choose a next route through the complete book; and
    4. state what evidence would show that you are ready to advance.

    How to take the diagnostic¤

    Use a blank page or text file. Do not consult earlier chapters during the first attempt. Write an explanation or calculation for every item; a familiar-looking answer is not evidence of recall. Then compare with the solution and mark the route tag for every item you could not justify.

    The tags are:

    • O — objects and role separation;
    • P — probability and notation;
    • M — mathematics and optimization;
    • I — latent inference and estimation;
    • F — family comparison; and
    • C — executable computation.

    There is deliberately no universal pass percentage. Missing one foundational denominator can matter more than missing two family names. Route by the pattern of errors and the explanations you can reconstruct.

    First-course diagnostic¤

    1. [O] A paper defines an energy \(E_\theta(x)\), trains it by score matching, and generates with Langevin dynamics. Identify the model component, objective, and sampler.
    2. [O] Give one reason a generator may support sampling but not normalized scoring.
    3. [P] Using the tile table, calculate \(p(X_1=0\mid X_2=0)\).
    4. [P] Explain why a continuous density value of \(2.4\) is not an invalid probability.
    5. [M] If a scalar loss has derivative \(-3\) and the step size is \(0.1\), what gradient-descent update is proposed? State one thing this update does not guarantee.
    6. [M] For \(W\in\mathbb R^{5\times3}\) and \(\mathbf x\in\mathbb R^3\), give the shape of \(W\mathbf x\).
    7. [I] In \(p_\theta(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)\), describe the generation direction and the posterior-inference question.
    8. [I] Why can two latent-variable models have the same marginal law for \(X\) but different latent meanings?
    9. [F] Contrast a GAN generator with a density flow using one similarity and one non-equivalence.
    10. [F] Contrast autoregressive and diffusion generation using one similarity and one non-equivalence.
    11. [C] In the executable example, why are pmf and sample_tile different objects?
    12. [C] Modify the program to draw 10,000 tiles and propose a diagnostic that compares generated frequencies with the four model masses.
    Diagnostic solutions
    1. \(E_\theta\) defines the unnormalized model object; score matching is the training objective; Langevin dynamics is the sampler.
    2. An implicit generator can map noise to samples without an invertible map or tractable change-of-variables density.
    3. The circle marginal is \(0.45+0.15=0.60\), so \(p(X_1=0\mid X_2=0)=0.45/0.60=0.75\).
    4. Density is measured relative to a reference measure; probability is an integral over a set. A narrow interval can have density above one while its integrated probability remains at most one.
    5. \(\theta_{t+1}=\theta_t-0.1(-3)=\theta_t+0.3\). This local step does not guarantee a global optimum, generalization, or even a lower loss when the step is too large.
    6. The product lies in \(\mathbb R^5\).
    7. Generation draws \(z\) from its prior and then \(x\mid z\); inference asks which \(z\) values are plausible after observing \(x\), namely \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\).
    8. Marginalization can erase distinctions among joint decompositions; observed \(X\) alone need not identify a unique latent coordinate or causal interpretation.
    9. Both push base noise through a learned map. A density flow constrains the map to provide an inverse and Jacobian density contract; a typical GAN uses adversarial training and exposes no normalized likelihood.
    10. Both produce a sequence of conditional updates. AR usually appends observed components in an order; diffusion iteratively transforms a noisy whole-state representation along a time path.
    11. pmf specifies the four masses; sample_tile is one algorithm that converts uniform randomness into a draw from those masses.
    12. Repeatedly call sample_tile(rng.random()), count each returned state, divide by 10,000, and compare the empirical frequencies with pmf. A useful diagnostic reports deviations together with sampling uncertainty instead of demanding exact equality.

    If no tag repeats and you can explain all twelve answers, begin the autoregressive foundations for the most direct neural example of normalized generation, then follow the six-family route in the landscape. If your application already determines a family, enter that family's Chapter 1 and use its local prerequisite panel instead.

    The complete mathematical foundation remains available¤

    The first course is an on-ramp, not a replacement for the foundation. The complete route develops probability, classical models, Bayesian learning, latent inference, objectives, information and coding, graphical models, message passing, filtering and smoothing, sampling, sequential Monte Carlo, optimization, and notation. Follow it continuously when you need proof-level coverage or broad research preparation. Follow local diagnostic links when you need just-in-time foundations for one family.

    Cumulative performance task¤

    Choose a tiny object with at most eight states—for example, two coin flips, a three-bit pattern, or a two-step categorical sequence—and produce a one-page model card containing:

    1. the outcome space and supported queries;
    2. a normalized probability table or an explicit statement that no normalized law is exposed;
    3. one marginal and one conditional calculation;
    4. separate model, objective, estimator, optimizer, and sampler descriptions;
    5. executable generation code with at least two invariants;
    6. a comparison with one of AR, VAE, GAN, EBM, diffusion, or flow; and
    7. an evaluation plan that states data separation, metric, and uncertainty.

    The artifact passes when another reader can reproduce the calculation and generation result, identify every role, and tell which claims are exact, estimated, or approximate. This is stronger evidence of readiness than recognizing definitions in isolation.

    Chapter 7 retrieval practice¤

    One day after finishing the first course, reconstruct from memory:

    1. the seven generative-system roles;
    2. the joint-to-conditional tile calculation;
    3. the object–learning–generation sentence for each of six families; and
    4. the difference between learning, inference, sampling, and evaluation.

    Use the diagnostic tags again for anything you cannot reconstruct. Delayed retrieval is part of the route, not an optional epilogue.

    Chapter 7 synthesis¤

    You are ready to leave the on-ramp when you can explain the role distinctions, compute a finite conditional, compare the six families structurally, and run a small generator. The diagnostic routes missing knowledge to a specific part of the complete book; it does not force every newcomer through every foundation volume before encountering a model family.
