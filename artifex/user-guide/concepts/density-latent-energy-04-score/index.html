    4. Energy–Score Duality and Integrability¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes multivariable gradients and elementary line integrals; score-matching objectives are introduced rather than assumed. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive energy–score duality: obtain \(s=-\nabla E\) on the interior of positive support under a fixed coordinate system and base measure.
    2. O2 — Test score validity: separate local cross-partial, global topology, boundary, disconnected-support, and normalizability conditions.
    3. O3 — Transform score objects: account for coordinate Jacobians, reference measures, manifolds, and discrete neighbor ratios.
    4. O4 — Compare score estimators: distinguish explicit, denoising, sliced, ratio-based, and noise-contrastive objectives and their population targets.
    5. O5 — Audit finite samplers: separate invariant-law results from discretization bias, burn-in, autocorrelation, and multimodal mixing.
    6. O6 — Transfer conditional composition: decide when guidance or a sum of learned fields corresponds to a normalizable target.

    For a differentiable positive density

    \[ p(x)=\frac{e^{-E(x)}}{Z}, \]

    the score is

    \[ s(x)=\nabla_x\log p(x)=-\nabla_xE(x), \]

    because \(Z\) is constant with respect to \(x\). This is algebraic equivalence. The score discards the additive energy constant and the partition function.

    The equation is an interior statement. At a hard support boundary, where the density vanishes, \(\log p\) may diverge and a classical score may not exist. Distributional derivatives, reflecting diffusions, or manifold gradients can be appropriate, but each defines a different mathematical contract.

    Scores determine density only under conditions¤

    On a connected domain, an exact score determines log density up to a constant. But an arbitrary learned vector field \(v(x)\) is a score only if it is integrable. On a simply connected domain with sufficient smoothness, a necessary and sufficient local condition is symmetry of cross-partials:

    \[ \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_j} =\frac{\partial v_j}{\partial x_i}. \]

    Topology and boundary behavior also matter. Neural score estimators need not be exactly conservative at finite capacity, even if their population target is a true score.

    Three further checks prevent false reconstruction claims:

    • Disconnected support: integration recovers one additive log-density constant per connected component. The field alone cannot determine relative component masses.
    • Nontrivial topology: zero local curl does not guarantee zero circulation around holes; all closed-loop periods must be compatible.
    • Normalizability: even an exact gradient such as \(v(x)=2x\) integrates to \(\log\tilde p(x)=x^2+C\), whose exponential is not integrable on \(\mathbb R\). Conservativity is necessary but not sufficient for a probability density on an unbounded domain.

    Worked non-integrable field¤

    Consider \(v(x,y)=(-y,x)\). Its cross-partials are

    \[ \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial y}=-1, \qquad \frac{\partial v_2}{\partial x}=1. \]

    They disagree, so no scalar log density on \(\mathbb R^2\) has this score. Integrating around the unit circle gives nonzero circulation. A sampler driven by this field may rotate probability mass rather than ascend any log density.

    Coordinates, reference measures, manifolds, and discrete states¤

    For a differentiable coordinate change \(y=f(x)\) with inverse \(g\), the score of the transformed density relative to Euclidean volume is

    \[ s_Y(y)=J_g(y)^\top s_X(g(y)) +\nabla_y\log|\det J_g(y)|. \]

    Thus score vectors do not transform by the first term alone. Changing the base measure similarly adds the derivative of the log Radon–Nikodym factor. On a Riemannian manifold, the intrinsic score is a Riemannian gradient relative to a declared volume measure; embedding-space gradients can contain irrelevant normal components. Riemannian score-based generative modeling is a primary example of making this geometry explicit.

    For discrete \(x\), \(\nabla_x\log p(x)\) is not canonically defined. A graph of allowed moves can instead carry ratios

    \[ r(x,y)=\frac{p(y)}{p(x)}=e^{-[E(y)-E(x)]}. \]

    Reciprocity and cycle consistency replace continuous zero-curl checks. Recent discrete diffusion modeling by data-ratio estimation develops a ratio-based objective for discrete state spaces; it should not be described as an ordinary Euclidean score without this translation.

    Score matching¤

    Explicit score matching minimizes

    \[ \mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}} \left[\frac12\|s_\theta(x)\|^2+ \nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)\right] \]

    under boundary assumptions, avoiding the model partition function. Denoising score matching perturbs data and regresses a noisy conditional score; after expectation, its population minimizer is the noisy marginal score.

    This is a population-optimum correspondence. Finite noise schedules, weighting, parameterization, architecture, and optimization alter estimator behavior.

    The objectives solve related but nonidentical estimation problems:

    Table DLE.13. Score matching
    Method Training signal Population object under assumptions Main computational boundary
    explicit score matching data score eliminated by integration by parts clean-data score divergence or Hessian trace; boundary conditions
    denoising score matching analytic corruption score noisy-marginal score noise-level coverage and weighting
    sliced score matching random directional projections score through projected trace estimates projection variance and regularity
    noise-contrastive estimation data versus known-noise classification density ratio and normalized parameter estimate reference coverage and class-prior calibration
    discrete ratio objectives neighbor transitions or ratios declared discrete ratio field graph connectivity and cycle consistency

    Hyv\u00e4rinen's original score-matching paper derives the integration-by- parts objective. Sliced score matching replaces full trace computation with projections, while noise-contrastive estimation estimates a density relative to a declared noise law. Calling all three “the same loss” would erase their targets and estimators.

    Langevin sampling¤

    Using the score in unadjusted Langevin dynamics,

    \[ x_{k+1}=x_k+\frac{\eta}{2}s(x_k)+\sqrt{\eta}\,\epsilon_k, \qquad \epsilon_k\sim\mathcal N(0,I), \]

    targets \(p\) only in an ideal limiting sense under regularity and ergodicity; finite step size causes bias unless corrected, and multimodal mixing can be slow. Knowing a score locally is not the same as having an efficient global sampler.

    Even the one-dimensional standard Gaussian exposes the finite-step boundary. With \(s(x)=-x\), unadjusted Langevin becomes

    \[ x_{k+1}=(1-\eta/2)x_k+\sqrt\eta\,\epsilon_k. \]

    For \(0<\eta<4\), its stationary variance is \(1/(1-\eta/4)\) rather than one. The bias vanishes only as \(\eta\to0\); a Metropolis correction can restore the target invariant law but changes cost and rejection behavior.

    Conditional scores and exponential tilting¤

    Bayes' rule gives

    \[ \nabla_x\log p(x\mid y) =\nabla_x\log p(x)+\nabla_x\log p(y\mid x), \]

    because the evidence is constant in \(x\). Thus classifier guidance adds a likelihood score. More generally, tilting a base density by reward \(r(x)\),

    \[ p_\beta(x)\propto p_0(x)e^{\beta r(x)}, \]

    adds \(\beta\nabla r(x)\) to the score where differentiable. Approximate guidance fields, guidance scaling, and time-dependent noisy classifiers introduce modeling error; the identity alone does not validate arbitrary guidance.

    The identity also inherits support. A tilt of \(p_0\) cannot create probability where \(p_0=0\), and a conditional score is meaningful only where \(p(x,y)>0\) and the required derivatives exist. Adding two approximate fields can violate conservativity even when each was trained toward a valid population score.

    Independent practice and assessment map: score validity and sampling¤

    Work from field to potential to normalizer to sampler. Stopping after a local derivative check is not a complete probability argument.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: circulation. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. For \(v(x,y)=(-y,x)\), predict whether it is a score, compute the two cross-partials, and evaluate its line integral around the counterclockwise unit circle.
    2. P1.2 — Diagnose missing component masses. Type: explain · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Let \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) be translated copies of the same smooth density on two disjoint open intervals. Show why every mixture \(p_\alpha=\alpha q_1+(1-\alpha)q_2\), \(0<\alpha<1\), has the same within-component score for all \(\alpha\), and identify the missing information.
    3. P1.3 — Transform a score nonlinearly. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Let \(X\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(Y=e^X\). Derive \(p_Y(y)\) and \(s_Y(y)\) for \(y>0\), then reproduce the score with the inverse-Jacobian transformation rule.
    4. P1.4 — Compare estimation contracts. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For explicit score matching, denoising score matching, sliced score matching, NCE, and a discrete ratio objective, state the training signal, population object, and one assumption or computational bottleneck for each.
    5. P1.5 — Quantify finite Langevin bias. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For the standard-normal score and step size \(\eta=0.4\), derive the stationary variance of unadjusted Langevin. Compare it with the target variance and state what a Metropolis correction changes.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a discrete cycle. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. A four-node directed cycle reports clockwise ratios \((2,3,1/2,1/4)\). Test cycle consistency, decide whether these can be exact ratios of one positive mass function, and design a projection or diagnostic for noisy learned ratios on a larger graph.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. \(\partial v_1/\partial y=-1\) while \(\partial v_2/\partial x=1\), so the field is not conservative. On the unit circle, \(v=(-\sin t,\cos t)\) and \(d(x,y)=(-\sin t,\cos t)dt\), hence \(\oint v\cdot d\ell=\int_0^{2\pi}1\,dt=2\pi\). Check. A gradient field would have zero integral around this closed loop.
    2. P1.2. On the first interval, \(\log p_\alpha=\log\alpha+\log q_1\), so differentiation removes \(\log\alpha\) and yields \(\nabla\log q_1\); on the second, it similarly removes \(\log(1-\alpha)\). Thus every \(\alpha\) has the same within-component field. The field lacks the relative component mass \(\alpha/(1-\alpha)\). Check. One integration constant is needed per connected component.
    3. P1.3. For \(y>0\), \(p_Y(y)=(y\sqrt{2\pi})^{-1}\exp[-(\log y)^2/2]\) and \(s_Y(y)=-(\log y+1)/y\). The transformation rule gives \((1/y)s_X(\log y)+d[-\log y]/dy=(1/y)(-\log y)-1/y\), the same answer. Check. Omitting the Jacobian term loses \(-1/y\).
    4. P1.4. Explicit score matching uses data with integration by parts to target the clean score but needs divergence traces and boundary conditions. Denoising uses analytic corruption scores to target a noisy marginal but depends on corruption and weighting. Sliced matching uses random projections to estimate the same trace-based target with projection variance. NCE classifies data against known noise to estimate a density ratio and normalized model parameter under reference coverage and class-prior calibration. A discrete objective estimates declared neighbor ratios and needs connected moves plus cycle consistency. Rubric. Full credit names five distinct signals and targets.
    5. P1.5. Here \(a=1-0.4/2=0.8\), so stationary variance is \(0.4/(1-0.8^2)=0.4/0.36=10/9\), larger than the target one by \(1/9\). A Metropolis acceptance step can restore the target invariant distribution under its conditions, but adds proposal rejection and evaluation cost. Check. An exact score did not remove finite-step bias.
    6. P1.6. The cycle product is \(2\cdot3\cdot(1/2)\cdot(1/4)=3/4\ne1\), so no positive mass function has exactly these four ratios. For noisy ratios, fit node log masses by least squares to edge log-ratio differences, fix one gauge constant per component, and report residual cycle errors on a cycle basis or held-out edges. Rubric. Full credit detects inconsistency and proposes a global, not edgewise-only, diagnostic.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Energy gradients are scores, but scores erase normalization and require integrability to correspond to a density. Score matching and denoising recover true scores at population optima under assumptions; finite samplers add another approximation layer. Chapter 5 uses these facts to classify exact and inexact cross-family statements.

    Can the score recover log likelihood?

    It can recover log-density differences by path integration when the field is exact and the domain is connected. Absolute normalization still requires a constant, and non-conservative estimation error makes the integral path-dependent.
