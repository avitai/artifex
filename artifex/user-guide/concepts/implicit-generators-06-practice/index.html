    6. Comparison, Evaluation, and Research Practice¤

    Capstone objectives¤

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. choose an implicit objective from a declared failure model;
    2. compare population discrepancy, estimator, gradient, and systems cost;
    3. evaluate thin support, conditional coverage, latent mass, and memorization;
    4. separate training witnesses from confirmatory evaluation witnesses;
    5. classify theory, estimator results, and complete-generator benchmarks by evidence maturity; and
    6. design a reproducible implicit-generator study with falsifiable exit criteria.

    Objective Comparison¤

    Table IMPL.10. Objective Comparison
    Method Learned auxiliary model Coverage pressure Main computation Density access
    GAN discriminator/critic depends on game and regularization alternating neural updates no
    GMMN/MMD optional feature encoder/kernel parameters population discrepancy; minibatch dependent pairwise kernels or features no
    learned-kernel MMD adversarial/test-power feature kernel learned witness dependent min--max updates plus kernel pairs no
    IMLE nearest-neighbor index/feature metric each data point selects a candidate latent pool generation + matching no
    Sliced Wasserstein random or learned projections across observed projections projection + sorting no
    max/generalized sliced optimized or nonlinear slice family worst/represented slices slice optimization + sorting no
    Sinkhorn divergence ground cost and iterative transport solver minibatch coupling geometry pairwise cost + Sinkhorn iterations no
    Finite moment matching feature map \(\phi\) only represented statistics feature means no
    Rank-based/dual-ISL rank/projection and density basis choices estimator-specific ranks plus basis/density estimation auxiliary estimate, not exact pushforward
    Particle sliced transport directions and particle system represented particles repeated projection, sorting, particle updates no
    Explicit likelihood model posterior/normalizer may be needed forward-KL population pressure log density or bound yes/estimated

    No row dominates. GAN critics can learn task-relevant witnesses but create a game. MMD is a single minimization but kernel-sensitive. IMLE directly covers data examples but pays matching cost. Sliced methods exploit one-dimensional OT but need enough projections. Explicit likelihood brings density semantics but may restrict architecture or require inference.

    Choose from the failure you need to expose¤

    • Missing broad moments or known invariants: begin with explicit features or structured MMD.
    • Unknown but smooth sample differences with moderate dimension: characteristic multi-scale kernels are a defensible baseline.
    • Data examples receive no nearby samples: test IMLE-style data-to-model matching while measuring latent mass and memorization.
    • High-dimensional OT is too costly but linear projections are meaningful: use sliced objectives with projection-count audits.
    • Novel graph/molecule regions matter: design thin-support splits and domain-validity checks rather than relying on aggregate moments.
    • Pointwise density or calibrated posterior queries are requirements: an implicit sampler alone is the wrong interface; add a justified estimator or select another family.

    This is a routing aid, not an automatic model selector. The final choice still depends on state-space geometry, generator gradient access, sample size, compute, and the weakest failure that the deployment cannot tolerate.

    Evaluation Contract¤

    For an implicit generator, report:

    1. sample quality and diversity with modality-appropriate metrics;
    2. precision/recall or density/coverage-style diagnostics;
    3. nearest-neighbor and membership/memorization checks;
    4. condition-wise performance for conditional models;
    5. objective estimator settings: kernel, features, bandwidth, projection count, latent pool, and distance;
    6. latency, parameter count, training compute, and sampling cost;
    7. multiple seeds and uncertainty;
    8. failure cases that the chosen witness class cannot detect.

    Use at least one independent representation and targeted alternatives. FID-like feature distances, MMD, precision/recall, and nearest-neighbor metrics can share encoders and therefore share blind spots. Metrics estimated from the same finite reference set are correlated; multiple numbers do not imply independent evidence.

    Estimand, estimator, selection, and uncertainty¤

    Every reported number should identify four layers:

    Table IMPL.11. Estimand, estimator, selection, and uncertainty
    Layer Example for MMD/KID Example for IMLE coverage
    estimand population feature-kernel MMD prior mass or expected nearest-neighbor distance
    estimator U-statistic at stated sample counts finite generated pool and search procedure
    selection encoder/kernel/checkpoint chosen before final evaluation distance, pool size, and checkpoint chosen before final evaluation
    uncertainty repeated sampling, bootstrap, permutation/test interval binomial/multinomial mass interval plus resampled proximity

    Kernel Inception Distance is an unbiased U-statistic estimate of squared MMD in a particular Inception feature space with a commonly used polynomial kernel. Unbiased does not mean low-variance, representation-independent, or calibrated after selecting the best checkpoint by KID on the same samples. The complete metric derivation and FID/KID comparison are in Generative Model Evaluation.

    For a confirmatory MMD test, report the null-calibration method and controlled type-I error. For descriptive KID/MMD model comparison, report equal sample counts, nested-sample stability, repeated draws, and an interval for the difference. These answer different questions; a confidence interval for a metric difference is not automatically a level-controlled equality test.

    For structured outputs, validity and novelty are not enough. Vertical Validation, UAI 2024 constructs thin-support graph splits and reweighting to evaluate extrapolative regions and memorization. It is an evaluation methodology, not proof that any particular graph generator generalizes to undiscovered chemistry.

    Production Considerations¤

    Sampling is often one generator pass, but preprocessing, conditioning encoders, rejection filters, and decoders must be included in latency. Deterministic generators require latent randomness for diversity; accidental fixed seeds or collapsed latent paths can make a healthy model appear deterministic.

    Monitor distribution drift in the same feature space used for training and independent spaces. Reusing one encoder for objective and evaluation can hide shared blind spots.

    Record RNG algorithm and seed handling, latent dimension/distribution, truncation or rejection, batch-dependent layers, conditioning dropout, and post-processing. Exported inference can differ from training because BatchNorm statistics, stochastic augmentation, or feature normalization changed. A sampler that only works with a large rejection budget is not one-pass in end-to-end cost.

    Research Directions¤

    • witness spaces that adapt without unstable adversarial games;
    • linear/subquadratic distribution comparisons with reliable gradients;
    • conditional and structured kernels for graphs, sets, molecules, and functions;
    • coverage objectives that distinguish generalization from memorization;
    • uncertainty and calibrated inference for implicit simulators;
    • hybrid likelihood/implicit training whose claimed statistical target is explicit.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • Peer-reviewed research testing: Chatterjee and Ramdas, ALT 2026 derive a martingale MMD statistic with a limiting standard-Gaussian null and consistency against fixed alternatives, reducing calibration cost in the large-sample regime. It is a two-sample testing result, not a new generator objective or a guarantee after adaptive checkpoint/kernel reuse.
    • Peer-reviewed research theory: Thurin, Boyer, and Nadjahi, COLT 2026 derive non-asymptotic rates for an iterative slice-matching scheme; their tractable rate analysis is for Gaussian distributions with fresh random orthonormal bases. This is distribution-matching theory for that regime, not evidence that a particular neural generator dominates contemporary diffusion or flow systems.
    • Peer-reviewed research estimator: dual-ISL, AISTATS 2026 Spotlight introduces a convex density-space rank discrepancy and Bernstein-based explicit approximation with convergence results. Convexity in density space does not make a neural generator's parameter optimization convex, and the explicit approximation is not an exact implicit likelihood.

    The absence of a large 2026 standalone GMMN/IMLE benchmark wave is itself a reason to avoid manufactured “SOTA” claims. These objectives remain relevant inside few-shot generation, learned representations, particle transport, simulator inference, graph evaluation, and hybrid training. Research coverage should follow the active statistical ideas, not relabel unrelated diffusion/flow systems as moment-matching generators.

    Research Exercises¤

    1. Plot the point-mass MMD gradient across several bandwidths.
    2. Train a one-dimensional generator on a two-mode mixture with MMD and IMLE; compare mode occupancy and latent allocation.
    3. Construct two distributions with equal mean/variance but different tails and show a quadratic feature loss cannot distinguish them.
    4. Measure sliced-Wasserstein error as projection count grows.
    5. Replace raw distance in IMLE with a non-injective feature map and identify the new false matches.
    Exercise sketches
    1. Small bandwidths vanish far away; large bandwidths flatten local distinctions.
    2. IMLE assigns each datum a neighbor; MMD behavior depends on kernel and batch. Neither guarantees balanced latent mass.
    3. A standardized heavy-tailed or symmetric discrete distribution supplies a counterexample.
    4. Monte Carlo variance falls, but compute increases and high-dimensional alternatives may need many directions.
    5. Any two points sharing the same feature become indistinguishable to the matching objective.

    Cumulative worked study: an implicit molecular graph generator¤

    Suppose a one-pass sampler produces molecular graphs and the research goal is novelty in low-density property regions.

    1. State space: define graph isomorphism, atom/bond validity, disconnected components, stereochemistry, and property computation.
    2. Training witnesses: use graph kernels or learned features for MMD, or data-to-sample feature matching for IMLE. Record whether features are injective over relevant structures—they usually are not.
    3. Estimator: report graph batch size, quadratic/block approximation, kernel scales, latent-pool size, projection count, and search recall.
    4. Coverage: measure frequency mass over known scaffolds and property bins, not only whether each held-out graph has one neighbor.
    5. Thin support: construct a property/scaffold-aware split following the motivation of vertical validation; keep model selection separate from the final held-out region.
    6. Scientific boundary: generated novelty is not synthesizability, activity, or safety. Oracle prediction error and selection bias must accompany downstream claims.

    The study can compare MMD and IMLE fairly only when generator capacity, feature representation, sampling budget, and evaluation are controlled. Otherwise it compares entire pipelines rather than objectives.

    Independent practice and assessment map: implicit-system evaluation¤

    The capstone unit is the complete stochastic system: data and split, sampler, objective witness, estimator, gradient route, search/solver, deployed sampling policy, evaluation witnesses, and claim maturity.

    P1 — Defend an implicit generator end to end¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Classify each statement as a population theorem under named assumptions, estimator property, optimization claim, systems claim, empirical result, or invalid inference: characteristic MMD prevents mode collapse; IMLE covers each population mode; more slices monotonically improve trained samples; unbiased KID is noise-free; exact nearest-neighbor search validates mass; a density auxiliary recovers the exact pushforward likelihood; and one 2026 discrepancy theorem ranks complete generators.
    2. P1.2 — Objective decision record. Given a declared failure model with missing rare modes, incorrect physical invariants, and a fixed compute budget, compare fixed/learned MMD, IMLE, sliced/max/generalized objectives, Sinkhorn, and an explicit-density baseline. Record population target, finite estimator, gradient interface, asymptotic and measured cost, likely blind alternative, and a predeclared rejection rule. Choose one primary and one falsification objective.
    3. P1.3 — Controlled training comparison. Design a matched study over at least three objectives that fixes generator capacity, data, augmentation, optimizer-search budget, stopping opportunities, and evaluation sample counts. Account for pairwise kernels, latent-pool generation/search, projection/sort operations, Sinkhorn iterations, auxiliary networks, compilation, memory, communication, and end-to-end wall-clock. Report uncertainty across seeds and compute-quality Pareto fronts.
    4. P1.4 — Independent evaluation battery. Use a representation not used by the training loss, a domain-specific constraint oracle, precision/recall or density/coverage, condition-wise and rare-region mass, memorization/membership checks, and KID/MMD with equal sample counts. Inject controlled alternatives to estimate sensitivity, separate descriptive intervals from level-controlled tests, and disclose correlated metrics and shared encoders.
    5. P1.5 — Research evidence audit. Select one established foundation, one estimator/theory result, and one complete-system benchmark. For each, record exact claim, assumptions, state space, samples, generator class, witness, estimator, solver, compute, uncertainty, artifact availability, and independent reproduction. Rewrite any claim that transfers a discrepancy theorem directly to neural-system dominance.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Produce a preregistered protocol and model card for an implicit generator of graphs, molecules, scientific fields, policies, images, or another modality not used in P1.2. Include support and conditioning, latent law, gradient access, objective and solver, deployment sampling/rejection, thin-support split, contamination controls, safety/scientific boundaries, exit criteria, and a finite oracle that can falsify the implementation before scaling.

    Final Synthesis¤

    Implicit generation is a density-access category, not a synonym for GANs. GMMNs compare kernel mean embeddings; IMLE assigns generated candidates to every data point; sliced methods compare projected laws; Sinkhorn objectives differentiate through regularized transport; feature matching chooses explicit witnesses. Each method replaces an intractable pointwise likelihood with a sample-based view of distributional error. The research obligation is to make that view visible: specify its population target, witness, estimator, gradient route, solver/search approximation, deployed sampling law, and blind spots.

    Cumulative retrieval practice: the complete implicit-generator volume
    1. Distinguish a law without a density from a law whose density is merely unavailable.
    2. What does characteristicness guarantee for MMD, and what does it not guarantee?
    3. Why can unbiased MMD estimation coexist with biased model evaluation?
    4. Derive the small-noise bridge from a generated Gaussian mixture to nearest-neighbor IMLE.
    5. Why do finite sliced projections and finite feature moments have blind alternatives?
    6. What evidence is required before claiming coverage of a rare scientific region?
    7. Why does sample access not imply pathwise-gradient access?
    8. Why is the quadratic MMD U-statistic's null not handled by an ordinary alternative-standard-error calculation?
    9. What does \(1-(1-\pi)^M\) reveal about IMLE pool coverage?
    10. Why does Sinkhorn divergence subtract two self-costs?

    Solutions. (1) A singular law has no density relative to the chosen base measure; another law may have one that cannot be computed. (2) Population zero iff equality for the stated domain/measures; it does not guarantee finite-sample power or useful gradients. (3) A fixed-model estimator can be unbiased while adaptive training/model selection and reuse of data bias held-out conclusions. (4) Negative scaled log-sum-exp of Gaussian-kernel distances tends to their minimum as \(\sigma\to0\). (5) Finitely many witnesses leave distribution changes outside their span/features. (6) Region-aware splits, sufficient samples, mass/coverage and validity metrics, independent witnesses, uncertainty, memorization checks, oracle error, and prospective validation where appropriate. (7) A sampler may contain discrete, rejected, external, or hidden stateful operations and may expose neither a differentiable path nor a trace probability. (8) Under equality the U-statistic is degenerate and has a different, generally non-Gaussian limiting null; use a statistic-specific permutation, bootstrap, or justified asymptotic calibration. (9) It is the probability that a pool of size \(M\) visits a region of generator mass \(\pi\), showing how a huge pool can find a neighbor for a tiny-mass mode. (10) Entropic self-transport is generally nonzero; subtracting both self-costs removes that entropic bias from the discrepancy.

    Source-tracing checklist¤

    For every claim, record the sampling program and latent law; density/base-measure statement; discrepancy and population assumptions; empirical estimator and bias/variance; kernel/features/bandwidth/projections/pool/search; gradient route; solver, stopping tolerance, and approximation; generator and auxiliary models; data/split/conditioning; sampling and rejection cost; evaluation representations and sample counts; compute/hardware; seeds/uncertainty; publication status/version; and independent reproduction. Never cite a discrepancy theorem as a complete neural-system ranking.
