    An energy-based probabilistic model assigns a scalar \(E_\theta(x)\) and defines a density or mass function proportional to \(\exp[-E_\theta(x)]\) relative to a declared base measure, provided the partition function is finite. Scalar scoring, constrained optimization, score fields, and equilibrium dynamics may also use an object called an energy, but they do not inherit normalized-density semantics without the corresponding assumptions.

    • Energy Functions

      Parameterize relative weights through a scalar energy, with normalization and learnability determined by the state space and objective

    • Unnormalized Density

      Broad scalar architectures are possible, provided the induced density is normalizable on the declared state space

    • Scores and Composition

      Supports scoring, generation, classification, or composition in suitable objectives; no one capability guarantees the others

    • Transition-Based Sampling

      Classical normalized EBMs use MCMC; modern energy/equilibrium systems may use different iterative dynamics with different target semantics

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    Energy-Based Models (EBMs), formalised in the influential tutorial by LeCun et al. (2006) and developed as deep generative models by work including Du & Mordatch (2019), assign scalar energies to configurations. A normalized EBM defines \(p_\theta(x)\propto e^{-E_\theta(x)}\) relative to a declared base measure when the partition function is finite. Other systems use an energy only for ranking, constraints, optimization, or a learned vector field; those should not inherit normalized-density claims automatically.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand densities or probability masses, base measures, expectations, differentiation under an integral, and MCMC stationarity. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. distinguish a scalar energy from a normalized probability distribution;
    2. state the support, base measure, and integrability condition required for \(Z_\theta<\infty\);
    3. compute a finite-state partition function and probability ratio;
    4. derive the energy–score identity and its additive-constant invariance; and
    5. explain why low-energy optimization is not automatically Boltzmann sampling.

    Consequences of energy parameterization¤

    An energy parameterization exposes relative compatibility before it exposes a normalized probability or an efficient sampler:

    • Relative energy is the primitive - the model compares configurations before the partition function turns those comparisons into probabilities
    • Training determines which contrasts are learned - finite data, negative samples, corruptions, or score information do not constrain every off-data region equally
    • Sampling uses a transition kernel - valid MCMC balances drift, noise, acceptance rules where present, and sufficient mixing rather than moving monotonically downhill
    • Broad architectural freedom - many scalar networks are possible, but support, symmetry, differentiability, and normalizability still constrain a probabilistic EBM

    Geometric interpretation: level sets, modes, and stochastic motion¤

    For a differentiable energy on a continuous state space, contours of equal \(E_\theta(x)\) are level sets, local minima are modes of the associated Boltzmann law, and \(-\nabla_xE_\theta(x)\) points in the direction of steepest local energy decrease. This geometry is useful, but it does not by itself define a valid sampler.

    1. Scoring. \(E_\theta(x)\) orders configurations by relative energy. A trained model should assign useful relative scores on the regions and queries for which it was evaluated; “all non-data have high energy” is not guaranteed from finite training.

    2. Normalization. The integral or sum of \(\exp[-E_\theta(x)]\) must be finite. The partition function turns relative weights into a probability law but is commonly intractable.

    3. Sampling. A valid stochastic sampler may move toward higher energy. Its noise, acceptance rule, temperature, discretization, stationarity, and mixing determine whether it targets the Boltzmann law. Pure gradient descent instead seeks a local optimum or equilibrium.

    4. Learning. Maximum likelihood, contrastive divergence, score matching, noise-contrastive estimation, and Stein objectives use different information and approximations. They should not be described as interchangeable merely because each trains a scalar network.

    Relative energies determine probability ratios without evaluating \(Z_\theta\), but normalized likelihood training still requires its gradient or an alternative objective, and generation still requires a sampler or other constructive dynamics.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Boltzmann Distribution¤

    EBMs define probability distributions through the Boltzmann distribution:

    \[ p_\theta(x) = \frac{e^{-E_\theta(x)}}{Z_\theta} \]

    where:

    • \(E_\theta(x)\) is the energy function parameterized by neural network with weights \(\theta\)
    • \(Z_\theta = \int_{\mathcal X} e^{-E_\theta(x')}\,\mu(dx')\) is the partition function relative to base measure \(\mu\)
    • Lower energy \(E_\theta(x)\) corresponds to higher probability \(p_\theta(x)\)

    The exponential ensures nonnegative unnormalized density, while \(p(x_1)/p(x_2)=\exp[-E(x_1)+E(x_2)]\) cancels \(Z\). Adding an \(x\)-independent constant to every energy leaves the normalized distribution unchanged.

    The Intractable Partition Function¤

    The partition function \(Z_\theta\) poses the fundamental challenge in EBM training:

    \[ Z_\theta = \int e^{-E_\theta(x)} dx \]

    This integral sums over all possible configurations of \(x\), which becomes intractable for high-dimensional data:

    • Images (256×256×3): \(Z_\theta\) requires integration over \(196{,}608\)-dimensional space
    • Text (512 tokens, vocab 50k): \(Z_\theta\) requires summing over \((50{,}000)^{512} \approx 10^{2414}\) configurations

    The intractability of \(Z_\theta\) makes maximum likelihood training directly infeasible and necessitates alternative training methods.

    Energy Functions and Neural Networks¤

    The energy function can use a broad scalar-output architecture. On an unbounded continuous state space, however, a bounded or decreasing-at-infinity energy can make \(Z_\theta=\infty\); architecture plus domain must yield a normalizable model if normalized probability is claimed.

    Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) Energy:

    \[ E_\theta(x) = \text{MLP}_\theta(x) \in \mathbb{R} \]
    class MLPEnergyFunction(nnx.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_dims, input_dim, *, rngs):
        self.layers = nnx.List([])
        for dim in hidden_dims:
            self.layers.append(nnx.Linear(input_dim, dim, rngs=rngs))
            input_dim = dim
        self.output = nnx.Linear(input_dim, 1, rngs=rngs)

    def __call__(self, x):
        for layer in self.layers:
            x = nnx.relu(layer(x))
        return self.output(x).squeeze(-1)  # Scalar energy

    Convolutional Energy (for images):

    \[ E_\theta(x) = \text{CNN}_\theta(x) \in \mathbb{R} \]
    class CNNEnergyFunction(nnx.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_dims, input_channels, *, rngs):
        self.conv_layers = nnx.List([])
        for out_channels in hidden_dims:
            self.conv_layers.append(
                nnx.Conv(input_channels, out_channels,
                        kernel_size=(3, 3), rngs=rngs)
            )
            input_channels = out_channels
        self.global_pool = lambda x: jnp.mean(x, axis=(1, 2))
        self.output = nnx.Linear(hidden_dims[-1], 1, rngs=rngs)

    def __call__(self, x):
        for conv in self.conv_layers:
            x = nnx.relu(conv(x))
        x = self.global_pool(x)
        return self.output(x).squeeze(-1)

    Key properties:

    • Scalar output - energy function outputs single real number per input
    • No invertibility requirement, but domain, integrability, regularity, and desired symmetries remain requirements
    • Learned landscape - network parameters define the energy surface topology

    Worked example: normalization on three states¤

    Let \(\mathcal X=\{a,b,c\}\) with counting measure and energies \((0,\log2,\log4)\). Then unnormalized weights are \((1,1/2,1/4)\), \(Z=7/4\), and

    \[p(a)=\frac47,\qquad p(b)=\frac27,\qquad p(c)=\frac17.\]

    The ratio \(p(a)/p(c)=4=e^{-E(a)+E(c)}\) does not require computing \(Z\). Adding 100 to all three energies multiplies numerator and partition function by \(e^{-100}\) and leaves every probability unchanged. If instead \(\mathcal X=\mathbb R\) and \(E(x)=0\), then \(Z=\int_{\mathbb R} 1\,dx=\infty\): a perfectly valid scalar function is not a normalized EBM.

    Independent practice and assessment map: foundations¤

    Keep the scalar energy, reference measure, normalized law, spatial score, optimizer, and transition kernel separate. Show the measure or finite sum behind every normalization claim.

    P1 — Audit an energy model from scalar score to sampler¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: adding a constant to every energy changes the normalized law; every scalar network defines a normalized EBM; a global energy minimum is a typical Boltzmann sample; and a negative energy gradient is already a valid MCMC kernel. Name the missing object or assumption in every false statement.
    2. P1.2 — Finite normalization and gauge. On \(\mathcal X=\{a,b,c\}\) with counting measure, let \(E_\theta=(0,\theta,2\theta)\) and set \(\theta=\log 2\). Compute \(Z_\theta\), all three probabilities, \(p(a)/p(c)\), and the same quantities after adding an arbitrary constant \(\lambda\) to every state. Identify what the ratio and normalized law can and cannot reveal about \(\lambda\).
    3. P1.3 — Support, measure, and integrability. With Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb R\), determine for which \(\alpha>0\) the energy \(E_\alpha(x)=\alpha\log(1+x^2)\) has finite partition function. Contrast the answer with the same formula on a bounded interval and with counting measure on the integers. State why declaring only the scalar formula is insufficient.
    4. P1.4 — Score, optimization, and sampling. For \(E_{\mu,\sigma,\lambda}(x)=(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)+\lambda\), derive the spatial score and show its gauge invariance. Compare the limiting behavior of gradient descent, calibrated ULA, and MALA: specify which returns a mode, which has fixed-step bias, and which has the Gaussian law as an invariant target under its assumptions.
    5. P1.5 — Transfer. Recast the audit for molecular conformations represented by Cartesian coordinates. Declare the state space or symmetry quotient, chirality convention, base measure, temperature, normalizability condition, invariant energy, equivariant force, sampler, and one tractable test that would falsify a claimed Boltzmann interpretation.

    Mathematical Foundation: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    An energy becomes a probability model only after specifying domain, base measure, finite partition function, and sampling semantics. Relative scores, modes, and normalized samples are related but distinct outputs.

    Retrieval practice: energy semantics
    1. Why does an additive constant not change a normalized EBM?
    2. What fails for \(E(x)=0\) on \(\mathbb R\)?
    3. Why is gradient descent on \(E\) not an MCMC sampler?

    Solutions. (1) The same multiplicative factor appears in the unnormalized density and \(Z\). (2) The partition function diverges with respect to Lebesgue measure. (3) It deterministically seeks low-energy points and lacks the transition/noise or acceptance mechanism needed to preserve the full Boltzmann distribution.

    Bridge to Chapter 2. The model semantics are simple; estimating its parameters and sampling its normalized law are not. The next chapter derives the likelihood gradient and audits the bias introduced by CD, PCD, ULA, score matching, and NCE.

    Maximum Likelihood: The Ideal but Intractable Approach¤

    The maximum likelihood objective seeks to maximize the log-probability of observed data:

    \[ \max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log p_\theta(x)] \]

    Substituting the Boltzmann distribution:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) = -E_\theta(x) - \log Z_\theta \]

    Taking gradients with respect to \(\theta\):

    \[ \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) = -\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x) - \nabla_\theta \log Z_\theta \]

    The first term \(-\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x)\) is tractable—just backpropagation through the energy function evaluated at data \(x\).

    The second term is problematic:

    \[ \nabla_\theta \log Z_\theta = \frac{1}{Z_\theta} \nabla_\theta Z_\theta = \frac{1}{Z_\theta} \int \nabla_\theta e^{-E_\theta(x')} dx' \]
    \[ = -\int \frac{e^{-E_\theta(x')}}{Z_\theta} \nabla_\theta E_\theta(x') dx' = -\mathbb{E}_{x' \sim p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x')] \]

    Substituting this result gives the positive/negative-phase identity

    \[ \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) = -\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x) + \mathbb{E}_{x'\sim p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x')]. \]

    The minus sign in front of \(\log Z_\theta\) is essential: maximum likelihood lowers data energy and, through the model expectation, raises energy where the current model places too much mass.

    This requires expectations under the current normalized model. Exact iid model samples would make a Monte Carlo gradient unbiased; finite MCMC, a changing parameter, and a discretized transition generally add different errors. The alternatives below do not all approximate the same finite-sample computation. Each removes a nuisance quantity by changing the statistical problem.

    The objective tree: what each transformation removes¤

    Start from normalized maximum likelihood, then ask which unavailable quantity prevents its use. A transition kernel approximates the model expectation; integration by parts turns density comparison into derivative comparison; binary classification estimates a density ratio; and a Stein identity restricts comparison to a witness class.

    Table EBM.2. The objective tree: what each transformation removes
    Route Population object What is removed What is introduced Finite-computation boundary
    Exact MLE gradient \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\|p_\theta)\) Explicit evaluation of \(Z_\theta\) Expectation under the normalized current model Monte Carlo is unbiased only for exact samples; MCMC adds initialization, mixing, and discretization error
    CD or PCD Short-run or persistent transition law An equilibrated negative-phase draw Kernel, initialization, chain length, and parameter drift The usual update omits how the transition law depends on \(\theta\) and is generally a biased likelihood gradient
    Basic score matching Fisher divergence \(Z_\theta\), model samples, and the unknown data score after integration by parts Smooth positive density, boundary decay, and a divergence or Hessian trace Empirical data averaging can be unbiased while automatic differentiation and trace computation remain costly
    Denoising score matching Fisher divergence at a corrupted marginal Clean data score and model sampling Corruption kernel, noise level or path, and loss weighting At fixed nonzero noise the population target is the corrupted law, not the clean endpoint
    Sliced score matching Projected Fisher divergence Full Jacobian trace Projection distribution and trace-estimation variance A finite number of directions is a stochastic estimator; identification needs a nondegenerate projection law
    NCE Logistic data-versus-noise classification Partition-function integration and model sampling Evaluable noise density, support overlap, class ratio, and a normalizer parameter Population consistency does not prevent poor finite conditioning when classification is too easy
    Negative sampling Task-specific binary surrogate Full softmax or a more exact classification construction Chosen negative law and altered target It is not automatically a consistent normalized-density estimator

    This table separates three levels. A population identity states what an unrestricted objective identifies. An estimator statement describes expectations over finite data, negative samples, projections, or witness evaluations. An optimization statement concerns the path followed by a parameterized energy, sampler, or discriminator. Agreement at one level does not imply agreement at the other two.

    Running example: one quadratic energy, many objectives¤

    Let the data law be \(P=\mathcal N(m,v)\) with \(v>0\), and define

    \[ E_{a,b,c}(x)=\frac12 a x^2-bx+c,\qquad a>0. \]

    Completing the square gives

    \[ Z_{a,b,c}=e^{-c+b^2/(2a)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}, \qquad p_{a,b}(x)=\mathcal N\!\left(x;\frac ba,\frac1a\right). \]

    The normalized law is independent of the additive constant \(c\). Both population MLE and basic score matching therefore recover

    \[ a^*=\frac1v,qquad b^*=\frac mv. \]

    NCE can additionally identify the intercept that makes the unnormalized expression integrate to one,

    \[ c^*=\frac{(b^*)^2}{2a^*}+\frac12\log\frac{2\pi}{a^*}. \]

    The same example will expose where the finite CD, DSM, sliced, NCE, and Stein constructions differ.

    Contrastive Divergence: Practical Approximation¤

    Contrastive Divergence (CD) (Hinton, 2002 — Neural Computation) approximates the intractable expectation using short MCMC chains:

    Algorithm:

    1. Positive phase: Sample data \(x^+ \sim p_{\text{data}}\), compute \(\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x^+)\).
    2. Negative phase: Initialise \(x^- = x^+\) (or from a persistent buffer), run \(k\) MCMC steps to obtain a model sample \(x^-\), compute \(\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x^-)\).

    3. Gradient of the negative log-likelihood:

      \[ \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{CD}} \;\approx\; \nabla_\theta E_\theta(x^+) \;-\; \nabla_\theta E_\theta(x^-). \]

      Equivalently, the SGD parameter update is \(\theta \leftarrow \theta - \alpha\, \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{CD}}\), which lowers the energy on data and raises it on model samples.

    Why it works:

    • Positive gradient pushes down energy at data points
    • Negative gradient pushes up energy at model samples
    • Equilibrated chains estimate the model expectation when their draws are representative of the current \(p_\theta\)
    • Short chains instead use a transition law near their initialization; they need not approximate the equilibrium expectation
    • Persistent chains (PCD) reuse state across iterations and can reduce burn-in, but must still track a moving target and mix between modes

    If \(K_\theta\) is the transition kernel and \(P_k=P K_\theta^k\), the formal contrast associated with CD-\(k\) is

    \[ \operatorname{CD}_k(\theta) =D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|p_\theta)-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_k\|p_\theta). \]

    Because \(P_k\) itself depends on \(\theta\), differentiating this expression includes a transition-law term. The usual endpoint update omits that term.

    CD-\(k\) is generally not the likelihood gradient

    The familiar positive-minus-negative update treats the sampled endpoint as constant when differentiating. It is not generally the gradient of either likelihood or the complete \(\operatorname{CD}_k\) expression above. The estimator approaches the equilibrium negative phase only when the transition mixes to the current model under the required limits. PCD can reduce initialization bias; persistence alone proves neither mixing nor unbiasedness (Hinton, 2002).

    Running example: a one-step chain moves the exact target¤

    Initialize the calibrated ULA step at \(X_0\sim\mathcal N(m,v)\) and use the exact quadratic parameters \(a^*=1/v\), \(b^*=m/v\):

    \[ X_1=\left(1-\frac{\delta}{2v}\right)X_0 +\frac{\delta m}{2v}+\sqrt{\delta}\,\xi, \qquad \xi\sim\mathcal N(0,1). \]

    The mean stays equal to \(m\), but

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X_1) =\left(1-\frac{\delta}{2v}\right)^2v+\delta =v+\frac{\delta^2}{4v}. \]

    Thus a one-step CD negative phase has the wrong second moment even when the model already equals the data. The resulting \(a\) update is nonzero for every fixed \(\delta>0\). This is discretization bias, not evidence that the optimum should move. An exact Ornstein--Uhlenbeck transition or a valid Metropolis correction preserves the Gaussian target; taking appropriate step-size and long-chain limits can also remove the discrepancy.

    Persistent Contrastive Divergence (PCD)¤

    PCD (Tieleman, 2008 — ICML) maintains a persistent sample buffer across training iterations:

    Algorithm:

    1. Initialize buffer: \(\mathcal{B} = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_M\}\) with random samples
    2. Each training iteration:
      • Sample data batch \(\{x^+_i\}\)
      • Sample buffer batch \(\{x^-_j\} \sim \mathcal{B}\)
      • Run \(k\) MCMC steps from each \(x^-_j\) to get updated \(\tilde{x}^-_j\)
      • Update buffer: \(\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \cup \{\tilde{x}^-_j\}\)
      • For negative log likelihood, compute \(\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \nabla E(x^+_i)-\frac{1}{N}\sum_j \nabla E(\tilde{x}^-_j)\). The opposite sign is the approximate log-likelihood gradient.
      • Update parameters
    def persistent_contrastive_divergence(
    energy_fn, real_samples, sample_buffer, rng_key,
    n_mcmc_steps=60, step_size=0.01, noise_scale=0.005
):
    """Persistent Contrastive Divergence training step."""
    batch_size = real_samples.shape[0]
    sample_shape = real_samples.shape[1:]

    # Sample initial points from buffer
    init_samples = sample_buffer.sample_initial(batch_size, rng_key, sample_shape)

    # Run MCMC chain
    final_samples = langevin_dynamics(
        energy_fn, init_samples, n_mcmc_steps, step_size, noise_scale, rng_key
    )

    # Update buffer with new samples
    sample_buffer.push(final_samples)

    return init_samples, final_samples

    Advantages over CD:

    • Longer effective chain history: chains continue across parameter updates rather than restarting at data every iteration
    • Potentially lower negative-phase bias: only when the persistent chains track the changing model sufficiently well
    • Reusable diagnostics: buffer age, chain traces, and reinitialisation sensitivity can reveal failures; persistence does not guarantee mixing or stable training

    Langevin Dynamics: MCMC Sampling¤

    The canonical sampling chapter derives ULA, MALA, SGLD, preconditioning, stochastic-gradient error, and their diagnostics. This section specializes them to a learned energy whose target moves during training.

    Langevin dynamics (Welling & Teh, 2011 — ICML) provides the MCMC sampler for EBMs. The continuous-time SDE has the well-calibrated discretisation

    \[ x_{t+1} = x_t - \frac{\epsilon}{2} \nabla_x E_\theta(x_t) + \sqrt{\epsilon} \, \xi_t, \qquad \xi_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I), \]

    whose continuous-time diffusion has \(p_\theta\) as a stationary law under regularity conditions. Fixed-step unadjusted Langevin has discretization bias; recovering the target additionally requires an appropriate long-time and vanishing-step limit, or a Metropolis correction.

    Some deep-EBM training recipes instead use an SGLD-style decoupled step where the gradient step size \(\eta\) and noise scale \(\sigma\) are tuned independently:

    \[ x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta\, \nabla_x E_\theta(x_t) + \sigma\, \xi_t, \]

    Clipping and choosing \(\eta,\sigma\) independently generally change the stationary law. Such dynamics may be useful as a negative-sample heuristic, but must not be reported as exact Boltzmann sampling. The implementation below exposes both coefficients and therefore needs protocol-specific validation.

    Two components:

    1. Gradient descent \(-\eta \nabla_x E_\theta(x_t)\) moves toward lower energy.
    2. Brownian motion \(\sigma \xi_t\) explores the space and prevents the chain from collapsing onto a single mode.
    def langevin_dynamics(
    energy_fn, init_samples, n_steps, step_size, noise_scale, rng_key
):
    """Sample from EBM using Langevin dynamics."""
    x = init_samples

    for step in range(n_steps):
        # Compute energy gradient
        energy_grad = jax.grad(lambda x: jnp.sum(energy_fn(x)))(x)

        # Gradient descent step
        x = x - (step_size / 2) * energy_grad

        # Add noise
        rng_key, noise_key = jax.random.split(rng_key)
        noise = jax.random.normal(noise_key, x.shape) * noise_scale
        x = x + noise

        # Clip to valid range
        x = jnp.clip(x, -1.0, 1.0)

    return x

    Convergence: Under regularity and ergodicity conditions, an appropriate infinite-time/vanishing-discretisation limit targets \(p_\theta\). Fixed-step ULA has discretisation bias, finite chains have transient and mixing error, and independently tuning noise, clipping the state, or changing the drift generally changes or removes the claimed stationary target.

    Score Matching: Avoiding Sampling¤

    Score Matching (Hyvärinen, 2005 — JMLR) bypasses MCMC sampling by matching the score function (gradient of log-density):

    \[ \psi_\theta(x) = \nabla_x \log p_\theta(x) = -\nabla_x E_\theta(x) \]

    The population Fisher divergence is

    \[ \mathcal{D}_{F}(p_{\mathrm{data}}\|p_\theta) =\frac12\mathbb{E}_{p_{\mathrm{data}}} \left\|s_{\mathrm{data}}(X)-s_\theta(X)\right\|^2. \]

    It contains the unknown data score. Expanding the square and integrating the cross term by parts gives, up to a constant independent of \(\theta\),

    \[ \mathcal J_{\mathrm{SM}}(\theta) =\mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}} \left[\frac12\|s_\theta(X)\|^2+\nabla_x\!\cdot s_\theta(X)\right] =\mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}} \left[\frac12\|\nabla_xE_\theta(X)\|^2-\Delta_xE_\theta(X)\right]. \]

    This identity needs differentiable densities on a common continuous support and boundary terms that vanish or are otherwise controlled. Ordinary Euclidean score matching is not valid unchanged for discrete variables, arbitrary bounded domains, singular data laws, or manifolds. Matching scores identifies a normalized density only on connected support components under the necessary regularity; disconnected components can retain undetermined relative masses.

    Advantages:

    • No sampling required during training
    • No partition function computation needed
    • Avoids a negative-phase Markov chain, although Hessian traces or stochastic trace estimators can still be expensive

    Disadvantages:

    • Second derivatives enter the Hyvärinen objective through a Laplacian or Jacobian trace; forming a full Hessian is unnecessary, but exact trace evaluation can still scale poorly.
    • Generation still requires MCMC at test time (or a learned sampler).
    • Denoising and sliced score matching replace the inaccessible or expensive term with a different estimator; they introduce noise-path or random-projection choices rather than eliminating statistical error.

    Running example: basic score matching reaches the MLE solution¤

    For the quadratic energy, \(s_{a,b}(x)=b-ax\) and \(\partial_xs_{a,b}=-a\), so

    \[ \mathcal J_{\mathrm{SM}}(a,b) =\mathbb E_P\left[\frac12(b-aX)^2-a\right]. \]

    The first-order conditions are \(b=am\) and \(av=1\). Hence \(a^*=1/v\) and \(b^*=m/v\), exactly the population MLE parameters. This equivalence is a statement about this well-specified, regular population problem; it does not make their finite estimators or optimization dynamics identical.

    Denoising score matching: the conditional target learns a marginal score¤

    Denoising Score Matching (Vincent, 2011 — Neural Computation): A practical variant adds noise to data:

    \[ \mathcal J_{\mathrm{DSM}}(\theta;\sigma) =\frac12\mathbb E_{X\sim P,\,\widetilde X\sim q_\sigma(\cdot\mid X)} \left\|s_\theta(\widetilde X) -\nabla_{\widetilde x}\log q_\sigma(\widetilde X\mid X)\right\|^2. \]

    For \(\widetilde X=X+\sigma\epsilon\), the conditional target is \((X-\widetilde X)/\sigma^2=-\epsilon/\sigma\). Squared-error regression returns its conditional expectation given \(\widetilde X\), which is the score of the corrupted marginal \(q_\sigma(\widetilde x)=\int q_\sigma(\widetilde x\mid x)p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)dx\). The conditional target and marginal score are therefore related by a population conditional expectation; they are not equal sample by sample.

    For the running Gaussian, \(q_\sigma=\mathcal N(m,v+\sigma^2)\). A quadratic score fitted at one fixed nonzero noise level recovers

    \[ a_\sigma^*=\frac1{v+\sigma^2},\qquad b_\sigma^*=\frac m{v+\sigma^2}, \]

    not the clean parameters. Score-based diffusion instead learns a time-indexed family of corrupted-marginal scores and couples it to a reverse-time sampler. Its clean-generation claim depends on the whole path, endpoint limit, score accuracy, and numerical solver—not on a single fixed-noise DSM objective (Song & Ermon, 2019 — NeurIPS).

    Sliced score matching: estimate a trace with directions¤

    Let \(V\) be independent of \(X\), with \(\mathbb E[VV^\top]\) positive definite. Sliced score matching projects the score difference before squaring. Under the same integration-by-parts conditions, its implicit form is

    \[ \mathcal J_{\mathrm{SSM}}(\theta) =\mathbb E_{X,V} \left[ \frac12\bigl(V^\top s_\theta(X)\bigr)^2 +V^\top\bigl(\nabla_xs_\theta(X)\bigr)V \right]. \]

    It uses Hessian-vector or Jacobian-vector products instead of materializing a full Hessian (Song et al., 2020 — UAI). With \(\mathbb E[VV^\top]=I\), the second term is also a Hutchinson estimator of \(\nabla\cdot s_\theta\). The expectation is exact; a finite number of directions has Monte Carlo variance.

    In one dimension, write the projection as \(R\). If \(\mathbb E[R^2]=1\), then

    \[ \mathcal J_{\mathrm{SSM}}(a,b) =\mathbb E[R^2]\, \mathbb E_P\left[\frac12(b-aX)^2-a\right] =\mathcal J_{\mathrm{SM}}(a,b). \]

    A Rademacher direction \(R\in\{-1,+1\}\) has \(R^2=1\) and adds no projection variance in this scalar example. That convenient collapse does not extend to a single arbitrary direction in higher dimensions.

    Noise Contrastive Estimation (NCE)¤

    NCE (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010 — AISTATS) frames training as binary classification:

    Setup: Given data samples \(\{x_i\}\) and noise samples \(\{\tilde{x}_j\}\) from distribution \(p_n\), train a classifier to distinguish them.

    Let \(k\) be the number of noise examples per data example. Write \(\bar E_\theta\) for an energy without its free additive intercept, and parameterize a candidate log-density as \(\log p_{\theta,c}(x)=-\bar E_\theta(x)-c\), where \(c\) is learned rather than obtained by integration. Equivalently, the complete energy is \(E_{\theta,c}=\bar E_\theta+c\). The model posterior probability that a mixture observation came from data is

    \[ h_{\theta,c}(x) =\frac{e^{-\bar E_\theta(x)-c}}{e^{-\bar E_\theta(x)-c}+k p_n(x)} =\sigma\!\left(-\bar E_\theta(x)-c-\log p_n(x)-\log k\right). \]

    NCE objective:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{NCE}} =-\mathbb{E}_{X\sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log h_{\theta,c}(X)] -k\mathbb{E}_{Y\sim p_n}[\log(1-h_{\theta,c}(Y))]. \]

    At the unrestricted population optimum, matching the true and modeled class posterior implies \(p_{\theta,c}=p_{\mathrm{data}}\) wherever the noise law supplies the required support. In a well-specified identifiable model this yields a consistent parameter estimator under the theorem's asymptotic conditions (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010 — AISTATS). That statement does not say a finite classifier is well conditioned. When \(p_n\) scarcely overlaps the data, labels become easy while the useful density-ratio region receives little information.

    Advantages:

    • Avoids evaluating the partition-function integral by estimating its log-normalizer parameter jointly, under NCE's identifiability and noise-coverage conditions
    • Simple implementation like standard classification
    • Requires a known, samplable noise density with adequate support; a Gaussian is only one option and is unsuitable for many discrete domains

    When to use:

    • Problems where an evaluable noise distribution overlaps the data sufficiently and the NCE estimator is appropriate
    • Density-ratio or normalization estimation where direct model MCMC is undesirable
    • Not every representation-level “contrastive learning” loss is NCE for a normalized generative model

    Running example: NCE also fixes the additive intercept¤

    For \(P=\mathcal N(m,v)\) and a noise law that has positive density on \(\mathbb R\), the population NCE posterior is represented exactly by the quadratic family at

    \[ a^*=\frac1v,\qquad b^*=\frac mv,qquad c^*=\frac{(b^*)^2}{2a^*}+\frac12\log\frac{2\pi}{a^*}. \]

    Substitution makes \(e^{-E_{a^*,b^*,c^*}(x)}\) equal to the normalized data density, so the NCE logit becomes \(\log p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)-\log p_n(x)-\log k\). MLE and score matching could not identify \(c\) because normalized probabilities and input scores cancel an additive energy constant. NCE identifies it only because the classification parameterization and known noise density put the absolute scale back into the problem.

    Negative sampling is a related construction, not a synonym¤

    The skip-gram negative-sampling objective, for example, retains positive and sampled-negative logistic terms but does not preserve every ingredient of NCE as a normalized-density estimator. It is a simplified objective for learning representations, with its own negative distribution and population target (Mikolov et al., 2013 — NeurIPS). Calling any loss with negative examples “NCE” silently imports consistency, normalization, and density-ratio claims that may not apply. To make a generative claim, specify the class mixture, the evaluable noise law, the normalizer treatment, and the recovered probabilistic object.

    Connection to adversarial density-ratio estimation¤

    With equal class priors and fixed laws \(P\) and \(Q\), the population-optimal binary discriminator satisfies

    \[ D^*(x)=\frac{p(x)}{p(x)+q(x)}, \qquad \operatorname{logit}D^*(x)=\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}. \]

    This is the shared density-ratio identity behind NCE and the original GAN discriminator (Goodfellow et al., 2014 — NeurIPS). The roles differ. NCE contrasts data with a fixed, evaluable noise density and can learn a normalizer; a GAN usually contrasts data with a learned implicit generator whose density need not be evaluable. Their population-optimal classifiers correspond algebraically, but their generator objectives, finite discriminators, and alternating optimization do not become equivalent.

    Stein Discrepancies: Compare Through Witness Functions¤

    For a differentiable one-dimensional target \(p_\theta\), define the Langevin Stein operator

    \[ (\mathcal T_{p_\theta}f)(x) =s_\theta(x)f(x)+f'(x). \]

    If \(f(x)p_\theta(x)\) vanishes at the boundary, integration by parts gives \(\mathbb E_{p_\theta}[\mathcal T_{p_\theta}f]=0\). A Stein discrepancy evaluates the violation under the data law:

    \[ \mathcal D_{\mathrm{Stein}}(P,p_\theta;\mathcal F) =\sup_{f\in\mathcal F} \left|\mathbb E_{X\sim P}[(\mathcal T_{p_\theta}f)(X)]\right|. \]

    The partition function disappears because \(s_\theta=-\partial_xE_\theta\). What replaces it is a choice of operator, boundary condition, and witness class \(\mathcal F\). For suitable rich classes, zero discrepancy identifies the target. For a restricted class, zero means only that the selected Stein moments vanish.

    The running quadratic example makes this visible. With \(f_0(x)=1\),

    \[ \mathbb E_P[\mathcal T_{p_{a,b}}f_0(X)]=b-am, \]

    which detects a mean mismatch but is zero for every \(a\) satisfying \(b=am\). Adding \(f_1(x)=x\) gives

    \[ \mathbb E_P[\mathcal T_{p_{a,b}}f_1(X)] =bm-a(m^2+v)+1. \]

    After \(b=am\), the second condition becomes \(1-av=0\) and recovers the variance. One witness was insufficient; the pair identifies this two-parameter Gaussian family.

    Kernel Stein discrepancies take \(\mathcal F\) to be a unit ball in a vector-valued RKHS, producing a sample statistic from target scores and kernel derivatives without target samples or \(Z_\theta\) (Liu, Lee & Jordan, 2016 — ICML). The kernel still matters: commonly used KSDs can fail to detect non-convergence even for Gaussian targets, whereas convergence-determining results require appropriate kernels and target assumptions (Gorham & Mackey, 2017 — ICML). A finite near-zero KSD is therefore evidence relative to its kernel, estimator, sample size, and assumptions—not an unconditional certificate that two distributions are equal.

    Worked example: verify the likelihood-gradient sign¤

    For a two-state EBM with \(E_\theta(a)=0\) and \(E_\theta(b)=\theta\), suppose the observation is \(b\). Then \(p_\theta(b)=e^{-\theta}/(1+e^{-\theta})\) and

    \[\frac{d}{d\theta}\log p_\theta(b)=-1+p_\theta(b).\]

    The general identity gives \(-\partial_\theta E(b)+\mathbb E_p[\partial_\theta E(X)]=-1+p(b)\) because \(\partial_\theta E(a)=0\) and \(\partial_\theta E(b)=1\). Gradient ascent decreases \(\theta\), lowering the observed state's energy. A negative-log-likelihood optimizer uses the opposite sign. This one-line finite model is an effective unit test for implementation conventions.

    Joint Energy-Based Models (JEM)¤

    Joint Energy-Based Models (JEM): Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. derive a joint energy from classifier logits;
    2. distinguish conditional softmax normalization over labels from global normalization over inputs and labels;
    3. choose architecture constraints for sets, graphs, manifolds, and scientific states; and
    4. audit whether a claimed scalar energy is normalizable and differentiable for its sampler.

    For structured scientific states, the geometric and equivariant generation guide derives invariant energies, equivariant gradients, constrained-space measures, and the distinction between equivariance and conservative physics.

    For graph-valued energies, Random Graphs and Relational Generators separates an invariant energy parameterization from the induced labeled or unlabeled graph law, its partition function, and the Markov chain used to sample it.

    Joint Energy-Based Models (Grathwohl, Wang, Jacobsen, Duvenaud, Norouzi & Swersky, 2020 — ICLR) unify generative and discriminative modeling in a single framework.

    The Key Insight¤

    A standard classifier \(p(y|x)\) can be reinterpreted as an energy model:

    \[ p_\theta(y|x) = \frac{e^{f_\theta[x](y)}}{\sum_{y'} e^{f_\theta[x](y')}} \]

    where \(f_\theta(x) \in \mathbb{R}^C\) are logits.

    Define energy as:

    \[ E_\theta(x, y) = -f_\theta[x](y) \]

    Then the joint distribution becomes:

    \[ p_\theta(x, y) = \frac{e^{f_\theta[x](y)}}{Z_\theta}, \quad Z_\theta = \sum_{y'} \int e^{f_\theta[x'](y')} dx' \]

    And the marginal data distribution:

    \[ p_\theta(x) = \frac{\sum_y e^{f_\theta[x](y)}}{Z_\theta} \]

    Training JEM¤

    Hybrid objective combining classification and generation:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{JEM}} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{class}}(f_\theta)}_{\text{discriminative}} + \lambda \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{gen}}(p_\theta)}_{\text{generative}} \]

    where:

    • \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{class}} = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y) \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log p_\theta(y|x)]\) (cross-entropy)
    • \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{gen}} = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log p_\theta(x)]\) (MLE via SGLD)

    Training algorithm:

    1. Sample batch \(\{(x_i, y_i)\}\) from labeled data
    2. Classification update: Compute cross-entropy loss, backprop
    3. Generative update:
    4. Generate samples via SGLD
    5. Compute CD gradient
    6. Update parameters
    7. Alternate or combine both losses

    Capabilities of JEM:

    • Classification

      The original and successor papers report classification results on named image benchmarks; competitiveness is protocol-specific

    • Generation

      Realistic samples — historically below GAN quality on CIFAR-10 (FID 38), reaching FID ≈ 9–11 with stabilised training (Zhang et al., 2023).

    • Out-of-Distribution Detection

      Energy or estimated likelihood can be evaluated as an OOD score, but dataset direction, tuning split, threshold, and semantic shift determine whether it works

    • Adversarial Robustness

      Some JEM protocols report improved robustness relative to selected baselines; threat model and attack strength must be reproduced

    • Calibration

      Calibration is measurable and protocol-dependent, not implied by joint normalization

    • Hybrid Discriminative-Generative

      Leverages both labeled and unlabeled data

    Challenges:

    • Training instability: SGLD can fail to produce quality samples
    • Computational cost: Requires MCMC sampling during training
    • Hyperparameter sensitivity: Step size, noise scale, buffer size critical

    Zhang et al. (2023) study more stable JEM training; see Foundational Refinements. “Stable” there is an empirical result under the reported initialization, curriculum, and adaptive-SGLD protocol, not a family theorem.

    Energy Function Design Principles¤

    Key considerations when designing energy functions:

    1. Scalar output: \(E_\theta: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}\) must map inputs to single energy value
    2. Normalizability and boundary behavior: a scalar output alone does not make \(Z\) finite
    3. Symmetry and state geometry: invariance of \(E\) should induce the intended equivariance of \(-\nabla E\)
    4. Per-sample semantics: batch-coupled normalization can make one sample's energy depend on other chain members
    5. Differentiability and numerical scale: input-gradient samplers require stable derivatives in the precision actually deployed

    class CNNEnergyFunction(nnx.Module):
    def __init__(
        self, hidden_dims, input_channels=3,
        activation=nnx.silu, *, rngs
    ):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv_blocks = nnx.List([])

        in_channels = input_channels
        for out_channels in hidden_dims:
            self.conv_blocks.append(EnergyBlock(
                in_channels, out_channels, activation=activation, rngs=rngs
            ))
            in_channels = out_channels

        self.global_pool = lambda x: jnp.mean(x, axis=(1, 2))
        self.fc_layers = nnx.List([
            nnx.Linear(hidden_dims[-1], hidden_dims[-1]//2, rngs=rngs),
            nnx.Linear(hidden_dims[-1]//2, 1, rngs=rngs),
        ])
        self.activation = activation

    def __call__(self, x, *, deterministic=True):
        for block in self.conv_blocks:
            x = block(x)
        x = self.global_pool(x)
        for i, fc in enumerate(self.fc_layers):
            x = fc(x)
            if i < len(self.fc_layers) - 1:
                x = self.activation(x)
        return x.squeeze(-1)

class EnergyBlock(nnx.Module):
    """Residual block with GroupNorm for energy function."""
    def __init__(
        self, in_channels, out_channels,
        kernel_size=3, stride=2,
        use_residual=False, activation=nnx.silu, *, rngs
    ):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv = nnx.Conv(
            in_channels, out_channels,
            kernel_size=(kernel_size, kernel_size),
            strides=(stride, stride), padding="SAME", rngs=rngs
        )
        num_groups = next(
            group for group in range(min(32, out_channels), 0, -1)
            if out_channels % group == 0
        )
        self.norm = nnx.GroupNorm(
            out_channels, num_groups=num_groups, rngs=rngs
        )
        self.activation = activation
        self.use_residual = use_residual and (in_channels == out_channels) and (stride == 1)

    def __call__(self, x):
        residual = x
        x = self.conv(x)
        x = self.norm(x)
        x = self.activation(x)
        if self.use_residual:
            x = x + residual
        return x

    Worked example: from two logits to a joint EBM¤

    Let a two-class classifier output \(f(x)=(x,-x)\) for scalar \(x\). The conditional is \(p(y=1\mid x)=e^x/(e^x+e^{-x})\), and the marginal energy implied by the logits is \(E(x)=-\log(e^x+e^{-x})\). On all of \(\mathbb R\), \(e^{-E(x)}=e^x+e^{-x}\) is not integrable, so the conditional classifier is valid while the proposed global joint density is not. Restricting \(x\) to a bounded domain or adding confining input-energy structure changes that conclusion. Conditional normalization over labels never proves global normalization over inputs.

    Sample Buffer Management¤

    Persistent buffers are one important PCD mechanism, not a universal EBM requirement. Their contents, replacement rule, age distribution, and restart probability are part of the estimator.

    Buffer operations:

    class SampleBuffer:
    def __init__(self, capacity=8192, reinit_prob=0.05):
        self.capacity = capacity
        self.reinit_prob = reinit_prob
        self.buffer = []

    def sample_initial(self, batch_size, rng_key, sample_shape):
        """Sample starting points for MCMC."""
        if len(self.buffer) < batch_size:
            # Not enough samples - return random noise
            return jax.random.normal(rng_key, (batch_size, *sample_shape))

        index_key, reinit_key, noise_key = jax.random.split(rng_key, 3)

        # Sample from buffer
        indices = jax.random.choice(index_key, len(self.buffer), (batch_size,))
        # This pedagogical buffer is host-managed and intentionally not jitted.
        samples = jnp.stack([self.buffer[int(i)] for i in indices])

        # Reinitialize some samples with noise
        reinit_mask = jax.random.uniform(reinit_key, (batch_size,)) < self.reinit_prob
        noise = jax.random.normal(noise_key, samples.shape)
        broadcast_shape = (batch_size,) + (1,) * (samples.ndim - 1)
        samples = jnp.where(reinit_mask.reshape(broadcast_shape), noise, samples)

        return samples

    def push(self, samples):
        """Add new samples to buffer."""
        for sample in samples:
            if len(self.buffer) >= self.capacity:
                self.buffer.pop(0)  # Remove oldest
            self.buffer.append(sample)

    Reinitialization probability: \(p_{\text{reinit}}=0.05\) is a historical deep-EBM setting. Tune and report it; excessive restarts can prevent chains from tracking the model, while too few can hide disconnected modes.

    Common Training Issues¤

    • Mode Collapse

      Symptom: Generated samples lack diversity, all similar

      Tests: verify initialization and latent use, compare independent chains and restarts, measure mode occupancy, then ablate buffer, chain length, or tempering

    • Energy Explosion

      Symptom: Energy values grow unbounded, NaN losses

      Tests: account for additive-energy invariance, locate the first nonfinite gradient, then test an explicitly scaled regularizer or optimizer change

    • Poor Sample Quality

      Symptom: Samples look like noise or blurry averages

      Tests: separate model error from sampler error using longer, adjusted, or tempered chains and tractable marginals before adding capacity

    • Training Instability

      Symptom: Oscillating losses, sudden divergence

      Tests: identify whether parameters, negative chains, precision, or data units diverge; change one factor in a seeded comparison

    Monitoring and Diagnostics¤

    Essential metrics:

    1. Energy values: Monitor \(\mathbb{E}[E(x_{\text{data}})]\) and \(\mathbb{E}[E(x_{\text{gen}})]\)
    2. Should satisfy \(E(x_{\text{data}}) < E(x_{\text{gen}})\)

    3. Define the reported gap explicitly. With \(\Delta_E=E_{\text{gen}}-E_{\text{data}}\), a positive value matches this ordering; the opposite subtraction has the opposite sign. A growing gap alone is not proof of a normalized or well-mixed model.

    4. MCMC diagnostics:

    5. Acceptance rate applies to Metropolis-adjusted samplers such as MALA or HMC, not ULA/SGLD, which accept every numerical update by construction.
    6. Energy and observable traces should fluctuate after burn-in rather than decrease monotonically: stochastic samplers must sometimes move uphill to represent the target distribution.

    7. Track multiple-chain mixing, autocorrelation/effective sample size, between-chain agreement, and sensitivity to step size and chain length. A visually attractive short chain is not a convergence diagnostic.

    8. Sample quality: Visual inspection, FID/IS scores

    9. Gradient norms: Should be stable, not exploding

    real_data = batch['x']
    real_energies = model.energy(real_data)
    real_energy = real_energies.mean()

    # Generate samples
    fake_data = generate_samples_via_mcmc(model, real_data.shape[0], rngs)

          f"Loss: {loss:.3f}")

    return loss

    Worked example: validate ULA on a Gaussian before images¤

    For \(E(x)=x^2/2\), the target is \(\mathcal N(0,1)\) and ULA gives

    \[x_{t+1}=(1-\epsilon/2)x_t+\sqrt\epsilon\,\xi_t.\]

    This AR(1) chain has stationary variance

    \[\frac{\epsilon}{1-(1-\epsilon/2)^2}=\frac{1}{1-\epsilon/4},\]

    which exceeds one at every fixed \(\epsilon>0\). An implementation test should recover this variance, show convergence toward one as \(\epsilon\downarrow0\), and verify that MALA removes the fixed-step stationary bias when its acceptance ratio is implemented correctly.

    Foundational Refinements¤

    Before discussing energy–flow and implicit-energy research, three earlier pieces set the table:

    • Improved CD (Du, Li, Tenenbaum & Mordatch, 2021 — ICML) — adds a KL-divergence term to address gradient bias, plus data augmentation, multi-scale processing, and reservoir-sampled buffers. Achieves Inception Score 8.30 on CIFAR-10.
    • Diffusion Recovery Likelihood (DRL) (Gao, Song, Poole, Wu & Kingma, 2021 — ICLR) — trains a sequence of EBMs at progressively noisier scales, each one a tractable conditional that recovers the previous scale; bridges EBMs with diffusion.
    • Cooperative Diffusion Recovery Likelihood (CDRL) (Zhu et al., 2024 — ICLR) — pairs each scale's EBM with an explicit initialiser model, dramatically improving CIFAR-10 / ImageNet generation while retaining tractable training.
    • Persistently Trained Diffusion-Assisted EBMs (Yu et al., 2023) — combines persistent training with diffusion-assisted sampling for long-run stability, post-training generation, and superior OOD detection.
    • Stabilized JEM (Zhang et al., 2023) — addresses JEM training instability via better initialisation, curriculum learning, and adaptive SGLD; first stable JEM training on ImageNet-scale data.

    Energy Matching: Unifying Flows and EBMs¤

    Energy Matching (Balcerak et al., 2025 — NeurIPS) is a key scalar-potential construction connecting transport and equilibrium objectives:

    • Far from the data manifold, samples follow irrotational, optimal-transport paths from noise to data — like flow matching.
    • Near the data manifold, an entropic energy term takes over and guides the system into a Boltzmann equilibrium — like a classical EBM.
    • A single scalar potential parameterises both regimes; no time conditioning, no auxiliary generators, no second network.

    The result substantially outperforms prior EBMs on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet generation while retaining simulation-free training away from the data manifold. The framework also supports interaction-energy terms for diverse-mode exploration, demonstrated on protein generation.

    Equilibrium Matching: Implicit Energy via Gradient Descent¤

    Preprint: Equilibrium Matching (EqM) (Wang & Du, arXiv v1, 2025) discards the time-conditional dynamics of diffusion / flow matching. At the 2026-07-12 verification cutoff, this guide could verify the arXiv manuscript and an earlier ICLR submission record, but not a final archival proceedings version; its benchmark and theoretical claims remain author-reported here.

    • Learn a time-invariant equilibrium field; the paper includes implicit-gradient and explicitly parameterised energy variants. A learned vector field must satisfy integrability conditions before it can be identified globally with \(\nabla E_\theta(x)\).
    • At inference, samples are produced by gradient descent on the learned energy landscape with adjustable step sizes, adaptive optimisers, and adaptive compute — replacing fixed-horizon ODE solvers with general optimisation.
    • The manuscript reports FID 1.90 on class-conditional ImageNet 256×256 against its listed baselines. A cross-paper “matched compute” conclusion requires aligned training data, architecture, parameter counts, guidance, NFE, metric implementation, and hardware; the number alone does not establish it.

    The manuscript also evaluates partially noised denoising, OOD scoring, and image composition. These are task-specific empirical claims. Gradient descent toward equilibria is an implicit generation algorithm, not a proof that iterates are samples from a normalized Boltzmann density \(p(x)\propto e^{-E(x)}\).

    Energy-Based Diffusion Language Models (EDLM)¤

    EDLM (NVIDIA, 2025 — ICLR) attacks discrete-text diffusion's approximation gap by attaching a full-sequence energy model at each diffusion step:

    • The standard discrete-diffusion factorisation \(p(x_0 \mid x_t) \approx \prod_i p(x_0^{(i)} \mid x_t)\) breaks down for long sequences.
    • EDLM replaces the per-token approximation with a sequence-level energy \(E_\theta(x_0, x_t)\) trained to score full reconstructions, then samples via NCE-style contrastive training plus importance-weighted decoding.
    • Closes a substantial chunk of the perplexity gap to autoregressive language models on PTB, OWT, and LM1B.

    This places EBMs at the centre of the discrete-diffusion-LM line of work alongside LLaDA and Mercury — see the Diffusion explainer.

    ARMs are Secretly EBMs¤

    Preprint: Autoregressive Language Models are Secretly Energy-Based Models (Blondel et al., 2025/2026) establishes an explicit bijection between autoregressive conditional functions and a corresponding energy representation in function space under its construction:

    • The mapping corresponds to a special case of the soft Bellman equation in maximum-entropy reinforcement learning.
    • The paper uses the mapping to analyze supervised equivalence, EBM-to-ARM distillation error, and lookahead. It does not make ordinary left-to-right sampling into generic global-energy MCMC or prove that every RLHF method is best implemented as energy shaping.

    A companion 2026 paper (A Theoretical Lens for RL-Tuned LLMs via EBMs) develops the practical consequences for RLHF analysis.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • Scalable Energy-Based Models via Adversarial Training (Yin et al., ICLR 2026) replaces JEM's SGLD negative sampling with an adversarially trained generator in a hybrid joint model and reports ImageNet 256² generation, robust classification, and counterfactual experiments. Peer-reviewed research: these are results of the complete adversarial EBM system under its protocol, not proof that a standalone scalar energy is cheap to sample.
    • Energy-Based Transformers are Scalable Learners and Thinkers (Gladstone et al., ICLR 2026 Oral) trains iterative energy minimization across modalities and reasoning tasks. Peer-reviewed research: its “thinking” terminology describes the paper's iterative inference construction and evaluations; it is not synonymous with normalized-density sampling.
    • \(\pi_0\)-EqM (Liu et al., 2026) replaces a flow-matching action decoder with EqM in a VLA stack and reports task-dependent control results. Preprint: adaptive inference depth is a system result, and success need not be monotone in an energy residual.

    Maximum-Entropy IRL for Diffusion Models¤

    MaxEnt IRL for Diffusion (Anonymous, 2024 — NeurIPS) interprets diffusion sample-quality fine-tuning as inverse reinforcement learning with an EBM-shaped reward:

    • Especially helpful when the diffusion sampler uses few steps, where standard losses become harder to optimise.
    • Connects EBMs back to the post-training-of-diffusion thread (DPO, DDPO, SiD).

    Method-Specific Implementation Patterns¤

    The methods above do not reduce to one validated recipe. A useful comparison checklist is:

    1. State whether the model defines a normalized density, an unnormalised scalar energy, an integrable gradient, or only an implicit equilibrium field.
    2. State whether training uses MLE/CD, score matching, a flow-derived regression target, NCE, or a sequence-level reranking energy.
    3. State whether generation uses MCMC with a stationary target, deterministic optimisation, an ODE/SDE, or proposal-and-rerank.
    4. If a diffusion/flow initializer or teacher is used, account for its training and inference cost rather than attributing the complete system to a standalone EBM.

    Some objectives avoid differentiating through long negative-phase chains, but they change the target or system composition. They do not universally solve mixing, normalization, integrability, or finite-sample bias.

    Worked example: classify three “energy” systems¤

    1. \(p(x)=e^{-E(x)}/Z\) trained by MLE and sampled with validated MALA is a normalized EBM with approximate finite-chain sampling.
    2. A flow model whose velocity is constrained to \(-\nabla E\) shares a scalar potential and may have an algebraic or dynamical relationship under assumptions, but its finite ODE solver is not MCMC.
    3. A diffusion student reranked by an energy head is a hybrid composition; the student, energy, proposal, and reranking rule jointly define deployed behavior.

    Calling all three “the same EBM” would erase their likelihoods, targets, and samplers.

    Evaluation Metrics¤

    Capstone objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. evaluate normalization estimates, sample quality, mixing, OOD scoring, robustness, calibration, compute, and deployment precision without treating any one axis as a proxy for all others; and
    2. produce an evidence-bounded research report for a new energy system.

    EBMs are evaluated on density-estimation, sample-quality, out-of-distribution-detection, and representation-quality axes:

    Density-Estimation Metrics¤

    The partition function \(Z_\theta\) makes exact likelihood intractable, so EBMs use bounds and ratios:

    The algorithms and uncertainty semantics are owned by Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants. This EBM section records only the consequences for energy-model evaluation.

    • Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) — estimates \(Z_\theta\) using a sequence of intermediate distributions. Accuracy depends on schedule, transitions, chains, and mixing; repeat runs and compare schedules rather than treating one estimate as ground truth.
    • Reverse AIS and bidirectional Monte Carlo — reverse procedures have different guarantees. Stochastic upper bounds on a marginal likelihood require reverse runs initialized from an exact target/posterior sample, as in Grosse, Ghahramani & Adams (2015). RAISE instead gives a conservative stochastic lower bound on the log-likelihood of an approximation to the model (Burda, Grosse & Salakhutdinov, 2015). Generic “reverse AIS” does not automatically bracket \(Z\).
    • NCE log-Z estimate — when training with NCE, the learned \(c = \log Z\) parameter is itself an estimate.
    • Energy-gap reporting — state the subtraction order. If \(\Delta_E=\mathbb{E}[E_\theta(x_{\mathrm{sample}})]-\mathbb{E}[E_\theta(x_{\mathrm{data}})]\), the desired ordering gives a positive value. The reverse definition is negative. Neither gap estimates \(Z_\theta\) or proves good mixing.

    Sample-Quality Metrics¤

    EBM sampling is unconditional by default; the standard image-generation metrics apply:

    • FID (Heusel et al., 2017), Inception Score, CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024).
    • Protocol-qualified reference points from Energy–Flow Constructions and Implicit EBMs include the EqM preprint's author-reported FID 1.90 on class-conditional ImageNet 256² (Wang & Du, 2025) and CDRL's comparisons on CIFAR-10. Reproduce the cited evaluation configuration before comparing either number with another family.
    • For language, EDLM (NVIDIA, 2025) reports perplexity competitive with similar-scale autoregressive LMs.

    Out-of-Distribution Detection¤

    Energy is a useful candidate OOD score, not a reliable OOD detector by construction. An energy function is learned for a particular objective; low energy can reflect background statistics, model misspecification, or unseen high-confidence regions rather than semantic in-distribution membership:

    • Report AUROC, AUPR, and FPR at a declared TPR on named in/out datasets, corruption severities, and tuning splits. Do not transfer a threshold or an aggregate result to a new domain without validation.
    • Likelihood-ratio scores (Ren et al., 2019) — divide by the energy of a "background" model to fix the well-known counter-intuitive higher-likelihood-on-OOD problem (cf. Nalisnick et al., 2019).

    Representation Quality (JEM, conditional EBMs)¤

    • Linear probing on top of the JEM classifier head.
    • Adversarial robustness under a declared threat model and strong attacks such as PGD/AutoAttack. Some JEM successors report improvements over named baselines; re-evaluate adaptive attacks against the released model.
    • Calibration using ECE plus proper scoring rules and reliability plots. Joint-energy training does not guarantee calibration under distribution shift.

    Production Considerations¤

    Inference Cost¤

    EBM inference cost is determined by the sampler, energy-network architecture, state dimension, and convergence criterion. The research landscape contains several qualitatively different regimes:

    Table EBM.8. Inference Cost
    Sampling regime Per-step work Main limitation
    ULA / SGLD One energy gradient per step Discretization bias, autocorrelation, and potentially long burn-in
    MALA / HMC Energy gradients plus accept/reject bookkeeping More work per transition; tuning and diagnostics remain essential
    Tempered / recovery-likelihood ladders Multiple energy or recovery evaluations across noise levels Additional states improve movement but increase cost
    Learned or equilibrium-style optimizers Method-specific iterative updates Report the actual algorithm, stopping rule, NFE, hardware, and benchmark; there is no universal latency

    Equilibrium-style methods explore adaptive optimization as a sampler, but that does not create a generic “one-step distilled EBM” category. Any such claim must name a concrete trained sampler and distinguish approximate optimization toward low energy from sampling the normalized Boltzmann distribution.

    Quantisation and Deployment¤

    • FP16 / BF16 can work for the energy network, but are not universally safe: energy differences and input gradients can be more sensitive than class logits. Validate chains, gradients, and acceptance statistics against an FP32 reference.
    • INT8 PTQ is risky when the sampler differentiates the quantized energy, because small energy/gradient errors can change trajectories or acceptance decisions. Mixed-precision or quantization-aware designs need sampler-level validation; “quantize only the forward pass” is not a coherent rule when the backward input gradient is computed through that same forward graph.
    • Distillation of EBMs into a single-step student is an active research area (see Equilibrium Matching's appendix on adaptive-NFE sampling).

    Common Pitfalls in Production¤

    • Long-run sample collapse — if chains drift to a narrow basin, diagnose model landscape, temperature, transition kernel, and initialization. Restarts or equilibrium-style optimization change behavior but do not prove the normalized target is represented.
    • Energy explosion during fine-tuning — absolute energy has an additive gauge, but nonfinite values and gradients are real failures. Centering, an explicitly scaled energy regularizer, optimizer changes, or architectural confinement should be tested with their effect on the objective disclosed.
    • OOD detection drift — the AUROC on a held-out OOD set should be tracked as a training metric, not just a final-evaluation one.

    For the broader cross-family picture and how EBMs fit alongside diffusion / flow / VAE / GAN / AR systems, see The Generative Modeling Landscape.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    Energy-Based Models provide a principled framework for learning probability distributions through energy functions. Their practical behavior depends jointly on the energy parameterization, training objective, negative-sample mechanism, and sampler; contrastive, score-based, regression-based, and hybrid methods make different trade-offs rather than forming a single progression.

    Core Principles¤

    • Energy Landscape

      Model assigns scalar energy to configurations—low energy = high probability

    • Boltzmann Distribution

      \(p(x) \propto e^{-E(x)}\) connects energy to probability via exponential

    • MCMC Sampling

      Generate samples by traversing energy landscape with Langevin dynamics

    • Flexible Architecture

      Broad scalar networks are possible, but normalized models still require finite \(Z\), declared support, geometry, and stable derivatives

    When to Use EBMs¤

    Consider EBMs when:

    • Flexible unnormalized density modeling where energies, ratios, constraints, or compositional structure matter more than cheap exact normalized likelihood
    • OOD or anomaly scoring experiments when representative anomalies, held-out shifts, calibration data, and failure costs are available; neither \(p(x)\) nor \(E(x)\) alone certifies safety
    • Hybrid tasks combining generation and classification (JEM)
    • Structured prediction with complex dependencies

    Prefer another or hybrid approach when the validated sampler misses the latency budget, normalized likelihood is required but \(Z\) cannot be estimated adequately, or available data/compute and domain evidence strongly favor another family. These are operating-point decisions; VAEs and diffusion models do not have a universal sample-efficiency advantage.

    Open Research Directions¤

    Interpretive synthesis: the following are research hypotheses, not established forecasts.

    • Energy-flow connections — Energy Matching (Balcerak et al., 2025) and the EqM preprint (Wang & Du, 2025) motivate scalar-potential or equilibrium-field alternatives to time-dependent flows. Whether large production generators adopt explicit energy heads remains open.
    • EBM dual of language models — the ARM–EBM preprint provides one rigorous function-space lens for lookahead and distillation. Whether it yields better post-training algorithms than policy or probabilistic views is open.
    • Energy-based discrete diffusion for text — EDLM reports reduced gaps to selected autoregressive baselines on named datasets. Extension to multimodal systems is a hypothesis, not an established trajectory.
    • One model, many tasks — EqM reports generation, OOD scoring, denoising, and composition experiments; future work must test whether one fitted model remains competitive under controlled multi-task protocols.
    • Scientific applications — scalar energies are attractive for constraints, composition, and physical priors in protein, molecular, materials, and physics problems. They compete and hybridise with diffusion and flow methods; no family naturally dominates across those domains.

    Cumulative worked example: audit a scientific EBM¤

    Suppose a molecular model outputs an invariant scalar energy and uses clipped unadjusted Langevin steps to generate conformations. A complete audit should:

    1. declare coordinates, quotient symmetries, chirality handling, base measure, temperature, and whether the energy is normalizable;
    2. verify force equivariance and finite-difference input gradients;
    3. validate ULA or MALA on tractable harmonic wells in the same units;
    4. compare multiple initializations, step sizes, chain lengths, adjusted and tempered kernels, and effective sample sizes;
    5. measure structural validity, energy/observable marginals, coverage, memorization, and held-out thermodynamic quantities;
    6. distinguish clipped optimization samples from the claimed Boltzmann distribution; and
    7. compare diffusion, flow, and autoregressive baselines under matched data, compute, constraints, and wall-clock.
