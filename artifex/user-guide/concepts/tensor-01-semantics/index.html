    1. Probability Tensors, Factorizations, and Born Semantics¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes finite probability tables and elementary matrix algebra. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. define nonnegative and Born tensor models;
    2. derive their normalizers;
    3. explain internal interference without invoking quantum hardware; and
    4. use a finite table to distinguish independence from a rank-constrained dependency.

    The probability tensor is the generated law¤

    For \(d\) finite variables, the joint probability mass function is an order-\(d\) tensor

    \[ \mathcal P[x_1,\ldots,x_d] =p(X_1=x_1,\ldots,X_d=x_d), \]

    with \(\mathcal P[x]\ge0\) and \(\sum_x\mathcal P[x]=1\). If every variable has \(K\) states, the full table has \(K^d\) entries. A tensor factorization is useful only when the target law has exploitable low-rank or sparse structure in the chosen topology and coordinate system.

    The model family is not “an array passed through einsum.” The free indices must correspond to observed variables, the internal indices and contraction graph must be declared, and the resulting scalar must define a valid probability after the chosen positivity and normalization construction.

    Three probability constructions¤

    Nonnegative contraction¤

    Let every core entry be nonnegative and let their full contraction be \(f_\theta(x)\ge0\). Then

    \[ p_\theta(x)=\frac{f_\theta(x)}{Z_\theta}, \qquad Z_\theta=\sum_x f_\theta(x). \]

    Local stochastic normalization can make \(Z_\theta=1\) in HMM-like parameterizations. Arbitrary nonnegative cores need a global contraction for \(Z_\theta\). Nonnegativity alone does not imply normalization.

    Born contraction¤

    A Born model permits signed or complex cores and defines

    \[ \boxed{ p_\theta(x)=\frac{|\Psi_\theta(x)|^2}{Z_\theta}, \qquad Z_\theta=\sum_x|\Psi_\theta(x)|^2. } \]

    Internal contraction paths can cancel before the magnitude is squared. A global phase \(e^{i\gamma}\Psi\) leaves the distribution unchanged, and even an independent phase \(e^{i\gamma(x)}\) for every completed configuration leaves \(p(x)\) unchanged if the parameterization can realize it. Computational-basis data therefore do not identify amplitude phase in general.

    Locally purified construction¤

    Introduce an auxiliary index \(a\) and marginalize a squared amplitude:

    \[ p_\theta(x) =\frac{1}{Z_\theta} \sum_a |\Phi_\theta(x,a)|^2. \]

    This is a latent-variable model with nonnegative output by construction. It contains a Born model when the auxiliary dimension is one and can offer different rank trade-offs from both nonnegative and pure Born networks. Glasser et al. (2019) prove representation separations among nonnegative MPS, Born models, and locally purified states. The result is incomparability, not a universal winner.

    Complete worked example: a dependent two-bit table¤

    Consider

    \[ \mathcal P= \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 & 0.1\\ 0.1 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Both marginals are \((0.5,0.5)\), but \(P(X_1=X_2)=0.8\), so the variables are not independent. The determinant is \(0.15\ne0\), hence the probability matrix has ordinary matrix rank two; a rank-one product model cannot represent it.

    A nonnegative latent factorization uses \(H\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and conditionally independent noisy copies. Let

    \[ \varepsilon=\frac{1-\sqrt{0.6}}{2}. \]

    Given \(H=h\), set \(X_i=h\) with probability \(1-\varepsilon\) and flip with probability \(\varepsilon\). Then

    \[ P(0,1)=\varepsilon(1-\varepsilon)=0.1, \]

    and each diagonal cell is

    \[ \tfrac12\bigl[(1-\varepsilon)^2+\varepsilon^2\bigr]=0.4. \]

    A Born representation may take the amplitude matrix \(\Psi_{ij}=\sqrt{\mathcal P_{ij}}\). It is already normalized because \(\sum_{ij}|\Psi_{ij}|^2=1\), and it also has rank two. Different semantics do not remove the rank needed by this particular table.

    Internal cancellation is not negative probability¤

    Let

    \[ H=\frac1{\sqrt2} \begin{bmatrix} 1&1\\ 1&-1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Factor a two-site amplitude as \(\Psi=HH=I\). The off-diagonal amplitudes vanish because two internal paths cancel. After normalization, the Born law puts mass \(1/2\) on 00 and 11. Replacing the negative entry by its absolute value gives a dense all-ones product and a uniform law instead. The negative sign is inside an amplitude computation; the final probabilities remain nonnegative.

    Chapter 1 counterexamples and misconception checks¤

    • Small parameter count is not small error. A low-rank network can badly miss a law whose relevant unfolding ranks are large.
    • Born is not quantum execution. Classical MPS contractions implement the same squared-amplitude semantics without qubits or hardware shots.
    • A nonnegative score is not yet a PMF. It must also have finite positive normalizer under the declared support.
    • Phase is not directly learned from basis probabilities. Many amplitudes induce the same observed law.
    • Tensor rank is not one number. CP rank, Tucker ranks, TT ranks, and ranks across different variable orders answer different representation questions.

    Independent practice and assessment map: tensor probability semantics¤

    Keep the probability tensor, factorization, normalizer, phase, rank notion, and execution substrate separate. Verify every validity or compactness claim on a finite law before generalizing it.

    P1 — Construct and falsify tensor probability models¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: nonnegative cores define a normalized PMF; squaring an amplitude removes the need for a normalizer; a low-parameter tensor network has low approximation error; two amplitudes with the same magnitudes are statistically identifiable from basis samples; a Born model requires quantum hardware; and one reported tensor rank determines all relevant factorization costs. For every false statement, name the missing normalization, support, cut, phase, approximation, or systems assumption.
    2. P1.2 — Finite-law ledger. For \(P=\begin{bmatrix}0.3&0.2\\0.1&0.4\end{bmatrix}\), verify normalization, compute both marginals, test independence by determinant and by a cell-product identity, and determine the ordinary matrix rank. Construct the nonnegative score \(f=P\) and Born amplitude \(\Psi_{ij}=\sqrt{P_{ij}}\); calculate both normalizers and explain why the two parameterizations share a law without sharing internal semantics.
    3. P1.3 — Cancellation and phase. Multiply the two Hadamard matrices in the chapter, enumerate the two internal paths contributing to each output amplitude, and normalize the resulting Born law. Replace the negative entry by its magnitude and recompute the law. Then multiply each completed amplitude by a configuration-dependent unit phase and prove that basis probabilities remain unchanged while intermediate contractions may change.
    4. P1.4 — Purification boundary. Build a two-bit locally purified model with a binary auxiliary index by specifying amplitudes \(\Phi(x,a)\), summing \(|\Phi(x,a)|^2\) over \(a\), and normalizing the four visible masses. Show how auxiliary dimension one recovers a Born model, then explain why this containment does not prove that purified, Born, or nonnegative MPS has the smallest rank for every target.
    5. P1.5 — Support and identifiability audit. Give one finite nonnegative contraction with \(Z=0\), one with a finite positive \(Z\) but zero mass on observed data, and two gauge- or phase-related amplitudes with the same PMF. For each, state whether the failure concerns probabilistic validity, likelihood support, parameter identifiability, or numerical representation, and give a finite test that detects it.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Define a tensor probability model for a short protein motif or a discretized physical configuration. Declare variables, alphabet, support, reference measure, order/topology, nonnegative/Born/purified semantics, normalizer, rank cuts, exact queries, and classical or quantum execution. Provide an enumerated small-instance oracle and identify which claims would need new evidence after increasing length or state dimension.

    Retrieval question: what changes when a score becomes a Born amplitude?

    Solution. The model squares a real or complex amplitude before normalization. This guarantees nonnegativity but doubles the contraction used for norms and marginals, permits cancellation among internal amplitude paths, introduces phase and gauge nonidentifiability, and does not specify whether execution is classical or quantum.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Tensor probability models compress a joint table; Born models compress an amplitude whose squared magnitude is the table. Positivity, normalization, rank, phase, and hardware are separate contracts. Chapter 2 turns the abstract factorization into the matrix-product-state chain, where every bond has a precise algebraic and statistical role.
