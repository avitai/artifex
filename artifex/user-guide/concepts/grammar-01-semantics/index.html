    1. Derivations, Yields, and Probability Mass¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes discrete probability, trees, and finite sums. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Identify the random object: distinguish a grammar's language, a law over derivations, and the induced law over observed yields or executions.
    2. O2 — Compute tree and yield mass: multiply local rule probabilities for a named finite derivation and sum compatible derivations for an observation.
    3. O3 — Treat ambiguity as latent structure: separate recognition, Viterbi decoding, marginal likelihood, and the posterior over derivations.
    4. O4 — Audit recursive consistency: solve a finite-termination fixed point and distinguish almost-sure termination from finite expected size.
    5. O5 — Normalize weighted grammars: distinguish local rule normalization, a finite global partition function, and probability lost to infinite paths.
    6. O6 — Transfer the semantic contract: specify the derivation, observation map, normalization, termination, and ambiguity policy for a new domain.

    From a context-free grammar to a PCFG¤

    A context-free grammar rewrites one nonterminal at a time. A PCFG associates a probability \(\theta_{A\to\beta}\) with each production and normally requires

    \[ \sum_{r:\operatorname{lhs}(r)=A}\theta_r=1 \qquad\text{for every productive nonterminal }A. \]

    For a finite derivation tree \(t\), the standard context-free independence assumption gives

    \[ \boxed{p(t)=\prod_{r\in t}\theta_r,} \]

    where repeated uses of a rule contribute repeated factors. This is a probability law over trees only if recursive expansion terminates almost surely. The grammar's string law is the pushforward through the yield map:

    \[ \boxed{ p(x)=\sum_{t:\operatorname{yield}(t)=x}p(t). } \]

    The tree is therefore a discrete latent variable. If the grammar is ambiguous, replacing the sum by the most probable tree changes the model.

    Ambiguity is probability, not parser noise¤

    Two trees may have the same terminal frontier because productions associate in different ways, use different latent categories, or construct different hidden objects that serialize identically. Ambiguity can be intentional: it lets the model represent alternative explanations. It can also make parameters weakly identified and inference expensive.

    Keep four quantities separate:

    1. recognition, whether any derivation yields \(x\);
    2. the probability of one named derivation \(p(t)\);
    3. the marginal yield probability \(p(x)\); and
    4. the posterior over derivations \(p(t\mid x)=p(t)/p(x)\) for compatible \(t\).

    The maximum-probability derivation need not yield the maximum-probability string, because a string supported by many moderate-probability trees can have more total mass than one supported by a single high-probability tree.

    Local rule normalization does not prove global consistency¤

    Consider the one-nonterminal grammar

    \[ S\to SS\quad\text{with probability }\rho, \qquad S\to a\quad\text{with probability }1-\rho. \]

    Every expansion chooses a locally normalized alternative. Yet the number of child nonterminals has mean \(2\rho\). The finite-termination probability \(q\) is the smallest solution in \([0,1]\) of

    \[ q=(1-\rho)+\rho q^2. \]

    Hence

    \[ q= \begin{cases} 1, & \rho\le \tfrac12,\\[2mm] \dfrac{1-\rho}{\rho}, & \rho>\tfrac12. \end{cases} \]

    At \(\rho=0.6\), only \(q=2/3\) of the mass reaches a finite string; the remaining \(1/3\) belongs to infinite derivations. At the critical value \(\rho=1/2\), termination still occurs almost surely, but expected tree size is infinite. This branching-process boundary is why a maximum length or emergency stop in an implementation is not merely an engineering detail: it changes the sampled law unless the model already conditions on that event.

    For a multitype PCFG, termination probabilities form a system of polynomial fixed-point equations. Spectral-radius tests on the mean progeny matrix give a standard consistency criterion under the corresponding accessibility and branching-process assumptions. The exact assumptions must accompany the test. Booth and Thompson's consistency theory and later algorithmic consistency work make this global requirement explicit.

    The following audit keeps three statements separate. Local normalization is a property of one expansion decision; almost-sure termination is a property of the infinite recursive process; finite expected cost is stronger still.

    Weighted grammars and partition functions¤

    A weighted context-free grammar assigns \(w_r\ge0\) without requiring local sums of one. A tree has weight \(w(t)=\prod_{r\in t}w_r\). If the total finite weight

    \[ Z_S=\sum_{t\text{ finite from }S}w(t) \]

    is finite and positive, then \(p(t)=w(t)/Z_S\). For a grammar without problematic cycles, the subtree normalizers satisfy formal fixed-point equations

    \[ Z_A=\sum_{A\to\beta}w_{A\to\beta} \prod_{B\in\beta\cap\mathcal N}Z_B, \]

    with one factor per nonterminal occurrence. A finite solution is not guaranteed. PCFGs and weighted grammars are therefore related but not interchangeable: local normalization, global normalization, and almost-sure termination are three separate claims.

    Complete worked example: an ambiguous finite yield¤

    Set \(\rho=1/3\) in the binary grammar above. A tree yielding aa uses one binary rule and two terminal rules, so

    \[ p(\texttt{aa})=\frac13\left(\frac23\right)^2=\frac4{27}. \]

    The string aaa has two binary tree shapes. Each uses two binary rules and three terminal rules:

    \[ p(t_1)=p(t_2)= \left(\frac13\right)^2\left(\frac23\right)^3 =\frac8{243}. \]

    Consequently,

    \[ p(\texttt{aaa})=p(t_1)+p(t_2)=\frac{16}{243}, \qquad p(t_1\mid\texttt{aaa})=p(t_2\mid\texttt{aaa})=\frac12. \]

    Returning \(8/243\) as the string probability would silently replace marginalization by Viterbi decoding.

    Independent practice and assessment map: derivation semantics¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: escaped mass. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 7 min. In the binary grammar \(S\to SS\) with probability \(\rho=0.6\) and \(S\to a\) otherwise, predict whether ancestral generation finishes almost surely. Then solve for the finite-termination probability and escaped mass.
    2. P1.2 — Sum an ambiguous yield. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. At \(\rho=1/3\), compute the probability of the string aaaa. State the number of ordered binary tree shapes, the mass of one shape, and the marginalized string mass.
    3. P1.3 — Separate three queries. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 7 min. A yield has three compatible derivations with masses \(0.06\), \(0.03\), and \(0.01\). Report recognition, Viterbi mass, marginal yield probability, and the posterior over the three trees.
    4. P1.4 — Normalize a weighted grammar. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. A weighted grammar has \(w_{S\to SS}=1/4\) and \(w_{S\to a}=1/2\). Solve \(Z=1/2+(1/4)Z^2\), select the finite tree-series solution, and compute the normalized probability of the one-node tree.
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose emergency stopping. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Set \(\rho=1/2\) and allow depth at most one. Compare (a) the original grammar conditioned on this depth event with (b) a sampler that draws the root rule normally but forcibly changes every depth-one nonterminal to a. Compute the probabilities of the one-leaf and two-leaf trees under both procedures.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Specify a probabilistic graph-rewrite grammar for finite molecules. Name the derivation object, observation map, ambiguity source, termination condition, normalization claim, and the additional evidence needed before calling its generated graphs chemically valid.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The grammar is supercritical. Its smallest fixed point is \(q=(1-0.6)/0.6=2/3\), so finite derivations receive mass \(2/3\) and infinite derivations receive \(1/3\). Check. The two local rule probabilities still sum to one; that fact did not prevent escaped mass.
    2. P1.2. Four ordered leaves have \(C_3=5\) full binary tree shapes. Each uses three binary and four terminal rules, so its mass is \((1/3)^3(2/3)^4=16/2187\). Thus \(p(\texttt{aaaa})=5(16/2187)=80/2187\approx0.03658\). Check. Returning \(16/2187\) would be a one-tree or Viterbi answer, not the yield likelihood.
    3. P1.3. Recognition is true because compatible derivations exist. The Viterbi mass is \(0.06\); the marginal yield probability is \(0.06+0.03+0.01=0.10\); and the posterior is \((0.6,0.3,0.1)\). Check. Only the posterior entries are normalized conditional tree probabilities.
    4. P1.4. The roots are \(Z=2\pm\sqrt2\). Iteration from zero selects \(Z=2-\sqrt2\approx0.585786\) as the finite tree-series sum. The one-node tree has weight \(1/2\), hence probability \((1/2)/(2-\sqrt2)=(2+\sqrt2)/4\approx0.853553\). Check. Choosing the larger algebraic root would not equal the nonnegative series over finite trees.
    5. P1.5. Under the original grammar, the one-leaf tree has mass \(1/2\) and the two-leaf tree has mass \((1/2)^3=1/8\); conditioning on their total \(5/8\) gives probabilities \((4/5,1/5)\). The forced sampler chooses the terminal or binary root with probabilities \((1/2,1/2)\) and then overwrites child choices, so it gives \((1/2,1/2)\). Check. An emergency stop can be operationally useful while still changing the declared distribution.
    6. P1.6. One valid contract samples a finite ordered rewrite trace from typed attachment rules, then forgets construction order and node labels to obtain a molecular graph. Alternative traces or isomorphic serializations create ambiguity, so graph probability sums their masses unless the grammar uses a proven canonical derivation. Almost-sure construction termination needs a recursion or size-bound argument; global normalization must include every admitted trace. Valence preservation establishes only declared graph invariants. Chemical stability, synthesizability, target-property calibration, and shift robustness require separate domain evidence. Rubric. Full credit names all six requested layers and does not equate parseability with chemistry.

    Counterexamples and misconception checks¤

    • “A CFG is a generative probability model.” A CFG defines support. It needs weights, a normalization contract, and a sampling process to define a law.
    • “Rule probabilities sum to one, so the grammar is normalized.” Recursive mass can escape to infinite derivations.
    • “One string has one parse.” Ambiguous grammars deliberately permit many derivations with the same yield.
    • “Syntactic validity implies semantic validity.” A parseable program can still be ill typed, raise an exception, diverge, or violate a domain law.
    Retrieval question: where is the latent variable?

    The derivation tree is latent when only its yield or execution result is observed. The observation likelihood sums the probabilities of every compatible finite derivation; selecting one parse answers a different query.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A probabilistic grammar assigns mass first to constructive derivations and then pushes that mass through a many-to-one observation map. Ambiguity requires a sum, and recursion requires a termination proof. Chapter 2 turns those sums into dynamic programs for likelihoods, posteriors, constraints, and exact samples.
