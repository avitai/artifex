    5. Loopy Belief Propagation and Variational Messages¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know the exact tree updates and why a tree edge separates two components. By the end, you should be able to:

    • run the sum-product updates iteratively on a graph with cycles;
    • separate convergence, fixed-point validity, and marginal accuracy;
    • relate interior belief-propagation fixed points to stationary points of the Bethe free energy under the required conditions;
    • derive scalar Gaussian messages in information form and state their tree and loopy guarantees;
    • use damping and residual diagnostics without treating them as correctness proofs; and
    • distinguish loopy BP, generalized BP, variational message passing, expectation propagation, and amortized inference.

    Reusing tree equations on a loopy graph¤

    Loopy belief propagation applies the Chapter 3 update equations repeatedly even though an edge no longer separates independent subtrees. Information can return around a cycle in a later iteration, so the tree proof no longer applies. One must answer three separate questions:

    1. Convergence: do the messages approach a fixed point under the declared schedule and damping?
    2. Validity: do the resulting beliefs normalize and satisfy the local marginal-consistency constraints?
    3. Accuracy: how close are those beliefs or the estimated normalizer to the exact target query?

    A converged run can be inaccurate. A nonconverged run can occasionally yield a useful early iterate. Locally consistent beliefs need not correspond to any global joint distribution on a loopy graph.

    Worked counterexample: convergence without exact marginals¤

    Let \(A,B,C\in\{0,1\}\) form a triangle. Give each edge the same compatibility factor

    \[ f(x_i,x_j)= \begin{bmatrix} 3&1\\ 1&3 \end{bmatrix}, \]

    and use unary factors \(u_A=(2,1)\) and \(u_B=u_C=(1,1)\). Exact enumeration gives \(Z=108\) and

    \[ p(A)=\left(\frac23,\frac13\right), \qquad p(B)=p(C)=\left(\frac{11}{18},\frac7{18}\right) \approx(0.61111,0.38889). \]

    Starting all directed messages uniformly and applying normalized synchronous sum-product updates converges for this instance. Its fixed-point beliefs are approximately

    \[ b_A\approx(0.70691,0.29309), \qquad b_B=b_C\approx(0.63794,0.36206). \]

    The messages converged, the beliefs are normalized, and the answer is still wrong. This finite example rejects the common inference “the residual is small, therefore the posterior is accurate.”

    Bethe free energy: a precise but bounded connection¤

    For normalized candidate factor beliefs \(b_a(x_a)\) and node beliefs \(b_i(x_i)\) satisfying local marginal consistency, the Bethe free energy can be written

    \[ F_{\mathrm B}(b) =\sum_a\sum_{x_a}b_a(x_a) \log\frac{b_a(x_a)}{f_a(x_a)} +\sum_i(1-d_i)\sum_{x_i}b_i(x_i)\log b_i(x_i), \]

    where \(d_i\) is the factor-graph degree of variable \(i\). On a tree, the Bethe entropy decomposition is exact and minimizing this objective recovers the exact marginals and log normalizer. On a loopy graph, it is an approximation.

    Under positivity, interiority, and regularity conditions, loopy-BP fixed points correspond to stationary points of the constrained Bethe free energy (Yedidia, Freeman, and Weiss, 2005). This does not guarantee that an iteration converges, that the stationary point is unique or globally optimal, or that its beliefs are accurate.

    Scheduling, damping, and numerical practice¤

    Common stabilizers include:

    • normalize messages or subtract a log-message offset after every update;
    • update serially, in residual-priority order, or by a graph coloring instead of fully synchronously;
    • damp a normalized update \(\widehat m\) using
    \[ m^{(t+1)}=(1-\lambda)m^{(t)}+\lambda\widehat m^{(t+1)}, \qquad 0<\lambda\le1; \]
    • detect oscillation and track both maximum message residual and belief change;
    • set an explicit iteration and wall-clock budget; and
    • compare with exact enumeration on small induced subgraphs or with an independent approximate method.

    Convex damping in probability space and interpolation in log space are different algorithms. State which one is used. Damping changes the update dynamics, not the target factor graph, and supplies no universal convergence or accuracy guarantee.

    What can be guaranteed in special cases?¤

    Guarantees depend on model class. Weak interactions can make the update a contraction. For Gaussian graphical models, walk-summability is a useful sufficient condition for convergence; when Gaussian BP converges under the standard valid-model conditions, its means can be exact while variance estimates need not be (Malioutov, Johnson, and Willsky, 2006). A statement proved for Gaussian means must not be transferred to categorical marginals, MAP, or normalizer estimates.

    Generalized belief propagation clusters short loops into regions, trading larger messages for a richer free-energy approximation. At the limit of a valid exact junction tree, clustering recovers exact inference. Intermediate region graphs need carefully chosen counting numbers and consistency constraints; “uses bigger clusters” is not a complete guarantee.

    Gaussian messages are sparse elimination in information form¤

    A Gaussian Markov random field can be written

    \[ p(x)\propto \exp\!\left(-\frac12x^\top Jx+h^\top x\right), \qquad J\succ0. \]

    Zeros in the precision matrix \(J\) encode missing pairwise edges. For scalar nodes, write the pair interaction as \(\exp(-J_{ij}x_ix_j)\). A Gaussian message from \(i\) to \(j\) has canonical form

    \[ m_{i\to j}(x_j) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac12P_{i\to j}x_j^2+q_{i\to j}x_j\right). \]

    Combining the unary term at \(i\) with every incoming message except the one from \(j\) gives the cavity parameters

    \[ P_{i\setminus j}=J_{ii}+\sum_{k\in N(i)\setminus\{j\}}P_{k\to i}, \qquad q_{i\setminus j}=h_i+\sum_{k\in N(i)\setminus\{j\}}q_{k\to i}. \]

    When \(P_{i\setminus j}>0\), integrating out \(x_i\) is a one-dimensional Schur complement:

    \[ \boxed{ P_{i\to j}=-\frac{J_{ij}^2}{P_{i\setminus j}}, \qquad q_{i\to j}=-\frac{J_{ij}q_{i\setminus j}}{P_{i\setminus j}} } \]

    The negative message precision is not a negative posterior variance. A message is an unnormalized partial integral and need not be a normalizable density by itself. The combined belief must have positive precision

    \[ P_i=J_{ii}+\sum_{k\in N(i)}P_{k\to i}, \qquad q_i=h_i+\sum_{k\in N(i)}q_{k\to i}, \]

    and then \(\mathbb E[X_i]=q_i/P_i\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=1/P_i\).

    Consider the chain

    with

    \[ J= \begin{bmatrix} 2&-1&0\\ -1&3&-1\\ 0&-1&2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad h= \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Both leaf-to-center messages have

    \[ (P_{1\to2},q_{1\to2}) =(P_{3\to2},q_{3\to2}) =\left(-\frac12,\frac12\right). \]

    Thus \(P_2=2\) and \(q_2=1\), giving mean \(1/2\) and variance \(1/2\). The message from node \(2\) to node \(1\), after absorbing node \(3\), is

    \[ (P_{2\to1},q_{2\to1}) =\left(-\frac25,\frac15\right), \]

    so node \(1\) has precision \(8/5\), information \(6/5\), mean \(3/4\), and variance \(5/8\). Direct linear algebra confirms

    \[ J^{-1}h= \begin{bmatrix}3/4\\1/2\\3/4\end{bmatrix}, \qquad J^{-1}=\frac18 \begin{bmatrix} 5&2&1\\ 2&4&2\\ 1&2&5 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    On a Gaussian tree these messages are exact sparse elimination. On a loopy Gaussian graph, convergence is a separate question. Walk-summability is one useful sufficient condition; when standard Gaussian BP converges under its valid-model conditions, the means can be exact while the marginal variances remain approximate (Malioutov, Johnson, and Willsky, 2006). Block messages, reparameterization, damping, and alternative splittings change the convergence analysis.

    Do not transfer that theorem to a nonlinear or non-Gaussian factor graph merely because every local message has been projected to a Gaussian. Deep Gaussian factor graphs (Peer-reviewed research, ICML 2024) learn within a Gaussian-message construction; Gaussian Ensemble Belief Propagation (Peer-reviewed research, ICLR 2025) uses ensemble and low-rank local approximations for black-box factors. A 2026 preprint derives Gaussianization with graph, factor, uncertainty, and asymptotic assumptions. None of these results makes an arbitrary locally Gaussianized loopy solver exact.

    Expectation propagation replaces one site through a tilted law¤

    Expectation propagation (EP) uses a different approximation object from mean-field VI. Write a target and tractable approximation as

    \[ p(z)=\frac{1}{Z}f_0(z)\prod_{n=1}^{N}f_n(z), \qquad q(z)=\frac{1}{Z_q}f_0(z)\prod_{n=1}^{N}t_n(z), \]

    where each tractable site \(t_n\) approximates the effect of \(f_n\). One EP update performs four distinct operations:

    \[ \begin{aligned} q_{\setminus n}(z) &\propto \frac{q(z)}{t_n(z)} &&\text{remove the old site},\\ \widetilde p_n(z) &\propto f_n(z)q_{\setminus n}(z) &&\text{form the tilted law},\\ q^{\mathrm{proj}}_n &=\arg\min_{r\in\mathcal Q} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\widetilde p_n\|r) &&\text{project by moment matching},\\ t_n^{\mathrm{new}}(z) &\propto\frac{q^{\mathrm{proj}}_n(z)}{q_{\setminus n}(z)} &&\text{replace the site}. \end{aligned} \]

    If \(\mathcal Q\) is a regular exponential family and the tilted moments exist, the forward-KL projection matches its sufficient-statistic expectations. The factor \(f_n\) itself need not belong to that family; the required tilted integrals may still need quadrature, analytic identities, or another approximation. Minka's corrected UAI paper and thesis give the foundational construction.

    This local forward-KL projection is not the global reverse-KL objective of ordinary ELBO VI. Repeated site replacement may converge to a fixed point of an EP energy under the applicable formulation, but EP can oscillate, produce improper cavity or site parameters, or converge to an inaccurate approximation. Damping a site in natural-parameter space can stabilize the iteration without proving accuracy. Assumed-density filtering is the one-pass relative that folds each factor into the current approximation without later removing and revising its site.

    Power EP changes the amount of a site removed and the factor power inserted; limits connect to variational constructions under particular alpha-divergence conventions. Alpha symbols and divergence directions differ across papers, so an implementation must state its cavity, tilted law, energy, and limiting convention rather than claim that EP “is forward KL.” Stochastic EP compresses many tied sites into a global approximation to reduce memory (Li, Hernández-Lobato, and Turner); black-box alpha methods define another scalable objective (Hernández-Lobato et al.). These are related algorithms, not interchangeable names.

    Nearby methods are not synonyms¤

    Table MSG.8. Nearby methods are not synonyms
    Method Approximation object Update principle Important boundary
    loopy BP locally consistent factor/node beliefs repeated sum-product; Bethe stationary points at fixed points under conditions may not converge or be exact
    generalized BP region beliefs region-graph messages and richer free energy region choice affects cost, validity, and accuracy
    mean-field / variational message passing chosen variational family, often factorized or conjugate-exponential coordinate optimization of an ELBO minimizes a declared variational objective, not Bethe by default
    expectation propagation product of tractable sites approximating factors cavity removal, tilted distribution, moment projection moment integrals and convergence may fail
    MCMC or SMC weighted or correlated samples invariant transitions or sequential reweighting Monte Carlo error and mixing replace deterministic fixed-point error
    amortized neural inference learned map from observations/model description to posterior parameters training over a task distribution generalization, calibration, and approximation gaps must be measured

    The latent-variable inference volume develops ELBO and amortization gaps; the sampling volume develops Monte Carlo correctness; and normalizer estimation treats uncertainty in evidence estimates. Message passing is one branch of that larger inference decision.

    A diagnostic record¤

    For every approximate message run, record:

    • target factors, evidence, variable cardinalities, and any zeros or hard constraints;
    • update equations, schedule, initialization, damping convention, and stopping rule;
    • message and belief residual traces, oscillation, and numerical repairs;
    • the query extracted from beliefs and whether it needs a normalizer estimate;
    • exact small-case comparisons or an independently justified reference; and
    • sensitivity to initialization, order, damping, precision, and iteration budget.

    Misconception check

    A fixed point is an algorithmic event. Posterior correctness is a probabilistic claim. Connect them only with a theorem whose model, graph, update, and query assumptions match the implementation.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Loopy BP trades the exponential clique width of exact inference for fixed-size local iterations. Convergence, local consistency, and accuracy remain distinct. Variational, sampling, and amortized alternatives optimize or approximate different objects. Chapter 6 asks when to compile the exact algebra, differentiate through it, or learn a replacement.

    Retrieval practice: approximate messages
    1. Can converged loopy messages produce incorrect marginals?
    2. What does the Bethe connection say—and not say—about a BP fixed point?
    3. Does damping alter the declared factorized target law?
    4. Why is an MPNN with learned messages not automatically loopy BP?
    5. Can an individual Gaussian canonical message have negative precision?
    6. Which KL direction appears in one EP moment projection, and does that make the whole EP iteration a global forward-KL minimization?

    Solutions. (1) Yes; the triangle example does. (2) Under stated conditions it is a stationary point of a constrained approximate free energy; this does not prove convergence, uniqueness, global optimality, or exactness. (3) No; it changes the iterative algorithm, though an early-stopped output changes the approximation. (4) Its messages and readout need not implement probabilistic sum-product or correspond to beliefs in any normalized model. (5) Yes. A message is an unnormalized partial integral; only the combined cavity and belief precisions used as Gaussian laws must be positive. (6) The local projection minimizes \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\widetilde p_n\|q)\) and therefore matches exponential-family moments. Repeated cavity removal and site replacement are not generally one global \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)\) minimization.

    Bridge. Repeated inference can reuse more than messages: it can retain the whole sum-product computation or train a solver over a distribution of queries. Continue continuously or open Chapter 6.
