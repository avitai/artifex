    5. Representations, Conversions, and Latent Projections¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know directed and undirected Markov properties. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish refactoring a function from preserving an independence model;
    • moralize a DAG for a declared inference or separation task;
    • state when directed and undirected graphs can be perfect maps of the same independence model;
    • interpret factor graphs, chain graphs, and ADMGs under explicit conventions; and
    • route circuits, relational models, and probabilistic programs to their specialized volumes.

    Four preservation questions¤

    Before converting any graphical representation, ask what must remain unchanged:

    1. Function preservation: is the same normalized or unnormalized function represented?
    2. Distribution preservation: is the same law obtained after normalization or marginalization?
    3. Independence preservation: are exactly the same conditional-independence statements visible?
    4. Algorithm preservation: does the same complexity, schedule, or sampler remain valid?

    These are different claims. A conversion may preserve a joint law while hiding independences, or preserve a factor product while changing the graph on which a message algorithm runs.

    Factor graphs expose factor scopes¤

    If

    \[ \gamma(x)=\prod_{a\in\mathcal F}f_a(x_a), \]

    a bipartite factor graph has one variable vertex for every \(X_v\), one factor vertex for every \(f_a\), and an edge whenever \(v\in a\). It records the exact factor scopes rather than only pairwise adjacency.

    A DAG becomes a factor graph by creating one factor for each local conditional. An MRF becomes one by exposing its chosen potentials. Multiple factorizations can yield the same function, and merging or splitting factors can change message complexity without changing \(\gamma\).

    Forney or normal factor graphs place factors at vertices and variables on edges; equality constraints copy a variable into multiple factors. They are useful computational diagrams, but their edge directions, when drawn, need a declared signal or generative interpretation rather than inheriting DAG semantics automatically.

    Moralization is query-aware information loss¤

    To use undirected separation for a DAG query \(A\perp B\mid C\):

    1. retain \(A\cup B\cup C\) and their ancestors;
    2. connect every pair of parents with a common child;
    3. drop arrowheads; and
    4. test ordinary graph separation by \(C\).

    Connecting co-parents is moralization. Moralizing the entire DAG can add edges created by descendants irrelevant to a particular query and thereby hide valid independences. Taking the ancestral subgraph first preserves the d-separation test for that query.

    Moralization also helps compile directed factors into undirected cliques for elimination. It need not produce a perfect undirected independence map of the original DAG.

    Chordality and perfect directed/undirected maps¤

    A v-structure \(X\to M\leftarrow Y\) has no undirected perfect map: the DAG encodes \(X\perp Y\) but generally not \(X\perp Y\mid M\). An undirected chain encodes the opposite conditional pattern. Conversely, a chordless undirected four-cycle has no DAG perfect map without adding an independence or an edge.

    An undirected graph admits an orientation with no new unshielded colliders exactly when it is chordal, also called decomposable. A perfect elimination order then turns clique factorizations into directed conditionals while preserving the independence model. Chordality is also what allows junction-tree compilation without first adding fill edges.

    Why a generic undirected-to-directed dummy node needs conditions¤

    It is tempting to encode every potential \(\psi_c(x_c)\) as the probability of an observed child. A binary conditional must, however, lie in \([0,1]\). On finite support, choose a positive base law \(q(x)\) and constants \(M_c\) such that the likelihood factors are bounded. Conditioning on auxiliary events can then reproduce a target proportional to

    \[ q(x)\prod_c\frac{\psi_c(x_c)}{M_c}. \]

    This is a likelihood-evidence construction under explicit boundedness and base-law conditions. It is not a general local conversion for arbitrary continuous, unbounded, or hard-constrained potentials. Independently, any ordinary joint distribution can be written as a saturated DAG by the chain rule, but that construction can discard the MRF's sparse semantics and computational advantages.

    Chain graphs need a Markov convention¤

    A graph containing directed and undirected edges is not fully specified by saying “chain graph.” Several non-equivalent Markov interpretations exist, including Lauritzen–Wermuth–Frydenberg and alternative AMP or multivariate-regression semantics. A valid model declaration must name:

    • the allowed edge types and forbidden cycles;
    • the chain components;
    • the factorization or kernel associated with each component; and
    • the separation rule.

    Under an LWF-style block-recursive construction, directed edges order chain components and an undirected conditional random field defines each component given its parents. Switching conventions can change the independence model even when the picture is unchanged.

    Latent projection and ADMGs¤

    Marginalizing latent variables from a DAG can produce observed independences that no observed-variable DAG represents compactly. An acyclic directed mixed graph, or ADMG, uses directed edges \(X\to Y\) and bidirected edges \(X\leftrightarrow Y\). In a latent projection:

    • a directed observed edge records a directed path through latent nonendpoints; and
    • a bidirected edge records an appropriate latent common-cause path with arrowheads at both observed endpoints.

    Conditional independence is read by m-separation, an extension of d-separation. An ADMG edge is a statement in a marginal graph; it does not assert that one scalar latent parent exists for that pair, fix a unique structural equation model, or identify counterfactual coupling.

    A bidirected covariance graph can use missing edges to encode marginal independences, while a causal or latent-projection ADMG uses bidirected edges within a different mixed-graph Markov semantics. Similar arrowheads do not make the two objects interchangeable.

    Representation routing¤

    Table GRAPH.7. Representation routing
    Representation Canonical purpose Continue in
    plate diagram repeated indexed variables and shared parameters Classical Generative Models
    factor graph explicit product scopes and message computation Graphical Inference
    probabilistic circuit compiled structure with query-specific tractability properties Probabilistic Circuits
    relational or open-universe model objects, relations, identity, and variable-size worlds Random Graphs and Relational Generators and Bayesian Nonparametrics
    probabilistic program a measure on execution traces plus conditioning semantics Extended Landscape
    conditional structured predictor a conditional law or decision rule over coupled outputs Probability and Generative Modeling and Energy-Based Models
    structural causal model mechanisms, interventions, and counterfactual worlds Causal Generative Models

    Probabilistic programs are not merely large static DAGs: stochastic control flow, recursion, continuous choices, and variable-dimensional traces require program semantics and inference machinery. Relational templates are not merely plates: grounding, identity uncertainty, and an unknown number of objects change the state space. Specialized volumes develop these objects without overloading one diagram language.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    A graphical conversion must name what it preserves. Factor graphs refactor products; moralization supports particular separation and inference constructions; chordality governs perfect DAG–MRF conversions; chain graphs require a Markov convention; and ADMGs summarize latent-variable margins without identifying a unique latent mechanism.

    Retrieval practice: representations
    1. Why moralize an ancestral subgraph rather than always moralizing the full DAG?
    2. When can an undirected graph be oriented without losing its independence model?
    3. Why is “mixed graph” not a complete probability-model specification?
    4. What does a bidirected edge in a latent-projection ADMG fail to identify?

    Solutions. (1) Irrelevant descendants can introduce moral edges that hide a valid query-specific separation. (2) When it is chordal and a perfect elimination order supplies an orientation without new unshielded colliders. (3) Different chain-graph and mixed-graph Markov conventions assign different factorizations and separation rules to the same edge picture. (4) It does not identify the number, state space, distribution, or structural equations of latent common causes, nor an individual counterfactual coupling.
