    5. A Conversion Table with Relationship Labels¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the preceding interfaces. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Apply six relationship labels: distinguish algebraic equivalence, distributional equivalence under assumptions, population-optimum correspondence, shared estimator, implementation analogy, and hybrid composition.
    2. O2 — Preserve the computational contract: state the hidden sum, integral, inverse, or sampler introduced by a mathematically valid arrow.
    3. O3 — Derive AR–energy conversions: retain exact normalization in the forward direction and suffix-partition costs in the reverse direction.
    4. O4 — Interpret density-ratio bridges: recover an optimal-discriminator ratio while retaining the reference density and class priors.
    5. O5 — Connect energy, score, and transport: separate local, pointwise, and global path information and their learned conversions.
    6. O6 — Audit unification claims: reject arrows that silently change support, base measure, probability object, optimum, or finite algorithm.

    The six labels are not interchangeable¤

    Table DLE.17. The six labels are not interchangeable
    Label What may be shared What must not be inferred automatically
    algebraic equivalence the same object after exact rewriting equal evaluation cost after structure is hidden
    distributional equivalence under assumptions the same probability law the same paths, support outside assumptions, or finite solver
    population-optimum correspondence an ideal minimizer after expectation equal sample losses, variance, capacity, or optimization
    shared estimator one numerical estimator or identity identical models, objectives, or semantics
    implementation analogy a reusable computational motif probability or optimum equivalence
    hybrid composition a new system using multiple interfaces preservation of either component's original target without proof

    Exact identities¤

    Table DLE.2. Exact identities

    normalized AR normalized EBM algebraic equivalence exact joint, \(Z=1\) energy form hides factorization
    invertible flow normalized EBM algebraic equivalence exact density energy form hides inverse/Jacobian structure
    differentiable EBM score \(-\nabla E\) algebraic equivalence local log-density derivatives loses additive constant and \(Z\)

    finite diffusion chain hierarchical latent joint algebraic formulation path joint and \(x_0\) marginal high-dimensional latent integration

    Assumption-dependent correspondences¤

    • A generic energy becomes AR conditionals only after suffix marginalization. This is a distributional equivalence with potentially intractable computation.
    • A score field determines a density up to normalization only when it is integrable and normalizable.
    • Denoising conditional-score regression and marginal-score regression share a population optimum under the denoising identity, not the same sample-level target.
    • Probability-flow ODEs and reverse SDEs can share time marginals under regularity and exact scores; their paths and finite solvers differ.
    • A sufficiently expressive transport may map a base law to an EBM target, but learning it is not implied by existence.

    AR–EBM correspondences without overclaiming¤

    An AR model supplies a globally normalized energy. An EBM supplies conditionals through ratios of prefix/suffix partition functions. They are two representations of the same normalized law after those quantities exist, but their inductive biases and accessible computations differ.

    Sequence-level energy reweighting of an AR reference,

    \[ p_R(x)\propto p_{\mathrm{AR}}(x)e^{R(x)}, \]

    is a product-of-experts or exponential tilt. Sampling it with rejection, MCMC, sequential importance sampling, or policy optimization is an additional algorithm. Adding \(R\) to token logits greedily is generally not exact sequence-level sampling.

    Worked density ratios and discriminators¤

    For normalized \(p\) and \(q\),

    \[ \log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}=E_q(x)-E_p(x). \]

    An optimal binary discriminator estimates a transformed density ratio at population optimum. This connects adversarial learning to energy differences, but a finite discriminator need not be an integrable log-density model, and the ratio alone needs a reference density to recover either distribution.

    With class priors \(\pi_p\) and \(\pi_q\), the population-optimal discriminator is

    \[ D^*(x)=\frac{\pi_p p(x)}{\pi_p p(x)+\pi_q q(x)}, \qquad \log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} =\operatorname{logit}D^*(x)+\log\frac{\pi_q}{\pi_p}. \]

    For equal priors the prior correction vanishes. Recovering \(p\) still requires a normalized reference \(q\) whose support covers \(p\); a ratio on regions never seen from either class is not identified by the classification experiment.

    Mixture scores require posterior responsibilities¤

    For a differentiable mixture \(p_{\mathrm{mix}}=\sum_k\pi_kp_k\), its score is

    \[ s_{\mathrm{mix}}(x) =\sum_k r_k(x)s_k(x), \qquad r_k(x)=\frac{\pi_kp_k(x)}{\sum_j\pi_jp_j(x)}. \]

    The weights are posterior component responsibilities and depend on \(x\). Therefore mixture scores are not plain sums of component scores. By contrast, a normalized product of experts has score \(\sum_k\alpha_ks_k\) wherever all factors are positive and differentiable. Confusing union-like mixture composition with intersection-like product composition is a common source of invalid guidance formulas.

    Energy–flow–score triangle¤

    The three interfaces solve different subproblems:

    • an energy evaluates an unnormalized target;
    • a score supplies a local direction field;
    • a flow supplies a global transport and possibly a normalized density.

    Algorithms that distill an energy or score into a flow are hybrid compositions. Success depends on proposal coverage, trajectory learning, and target evaluation; the mathematical existence of all three objects does not give a free conversion algorithm.

    A flow is global: it assigns a coupled map from base samples to target samples. An energy is pointwise up to normalization, and a score is local and loses component constants. Moving from a score to a flow therefore requires solving a dynamical or transport problem; moving from a flow to a score additionally requires a differentiable inverse and tractable Jacobian. Those operations are the content of an algorithm, not consequences of drawing a triangle.

    Independent practice and assessment map: labeled conversions¤

    Use the weakest label justified by the equations and assumptions. Then list the computational operation that the arrow still requires.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: label six arrows. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 12 min. Label these claims: \(E=-\log p\) for normalized \(p\); reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE sharing time marginals under exact-score regularity; denoising and marginal-score regression sharing a population minimizer; Hutchinson projections reused to estimate traces in two objectives; U-Net blocks reused across diffusion and adversarial models; and a learned flow distilled from an EBM.
    2. P1.2 — Expose a reverse AR cost. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For a dense energy on \(d\) bits, write the first AR conditional with suffix partitions and count the complete assignments appearing in its two numerator values before any structure or reuse is exploited.
    3. P1.3 — Recover a discriminator ratio. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. At one \(x\), an optimal discriminator trained with equal class priors outputs \(0.8\). Compute \(p(x)/q(x)\) and \(E_q(x)-E_p(x)\). Repeat the log-ratio formula for priors \(\pi_p=1/4\), \(\pi_q=3/4\) without assuming the numerical output stays \(0.8\).
    4. P1.4 — Diagnose mixture-score addition. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Let \(p=0.75\mathcal N(-1,1)+0.25\mathcal N(1,1)\). At \(x=0\), compute component responsibilities, component scores, and the mixture score. Compare with an unweighted sum of component scores.
    5. P1.5 — Compare the energy–score–flow triangle. Type: compare · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. For a target with two disconnected components of unknown relative mass, state what an exact energy, exact within-component score, and exact invertible flow must each encode. Identify the missing operation in score-to-flow conversion.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a paper audit. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Select one published “unified generative modeling” claim. For every arrow in its main diagram, record source object, target object, relationship label, assumptions, preserved quantity, hidden computation, and finite experimental evidence. Reject or weaken any arrow that fails the contract.

    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup

    Table DLE.20. Outcome-to-task alignment: labeled conversions
    Learning objective Independently assessed by

    O2 — Preserve the computational contract P1.2, P1.5, P1.6
    O3 — Derive AR–energy conversions P1.2

    O5 — Connect energy, score, and transport P1.4, P1.5
    O6 — Audit unification claims P1.1, P1.6
    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The labels are, respectively: algebraic equivalence; distributional equivalence under assumptions; population-optimum correspondence; shared estimator; implementation analogy; and hybrid composition. Check. Reusing architecture does not imply a probability identity, while distillation creates a new learned system rather than an algebraic rewrite.
    2. P1.2. \(p(x_1=b)=Z_2(b)/Z_1\) with \(Z_2(b)=\sum_{x_{2:d}}e^{-E(b,x_{2:d})}\). Each of the two values of \(b\) contains \(2^{d-1}\) complete assignments, \(2^d\) total before reuse. Check. The chain rule guarantees existence but supplies no faster summation algorithm.
    3. P1.3. Equal priors give \(p/q=D/(1-D)=0.8/0.2=4\), hence \(E_q-E_p=\log(p/q)=\log4\) for normalized energies. With general priors, \(\log(p/q)=\operatorname{logit}D+\log(\pi_q/\pi_p)=\operatorname{logit}D+\log3\). Check. The changed priors generally change the optimal numerical \(D\).
    4. P1.4. At zero the component densities are equal, so responsibilities are \((0.75,0.25)\). The scores are \(-1\) and \(1\), giving mixture score \(0.75(-1)+0.25(1)=-0.5\). The unweighted sum is zero and is wrong. Check. A product score would add component scores; a mixture score averages them with posterior responsibilities.
    5. P1.5. A normalized energy encodes within-component shape and relative component constants, though its global partition may be unknown. An exact within-component score encodes local shapes but not the mass split. An invertible flow encodes a global coupling whose base-region volumes assign both shapes and component masses, subject to topology/support constraints. Score-to-flow conversion must choose component masses and solve a transport or dynamical problem. Check. Integrating local paths alone cannot choose the global coupling.
    6. P1.6. A complete audit is a row per arrow with the seven requested fields and a source-backed experimental check. Algebraic identities need derivations; distributional arrows need assumptions; optimum arrows need expectation identities; finite algorithms need error and cost evidence. Rubric. Full credit weakens individual arrows independently instead of accepting a diagram because one arrow is correct.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Many strong unification claims contain a true identity wrapped around an omitted computational problem. The relationship label forces the claim to name whether it preserves a distribution, an optimum, an estimator pattern, or only an implementation analogy. Chapter 6 turns these connections into safe composition and evaluation rules.

    What is omitted by saying every EBM has an AR factorization?

    Computing each conditional may require exponentially large suffix partition functions. Existence of a chain-rule factorization does not imply tractable conditional evaluation.
