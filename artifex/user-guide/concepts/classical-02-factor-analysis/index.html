    4. Factor Analysis and Matrix Factorization¤

    Factor and source-model learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes latent-variable likelihoods and linear algebra. By the end, you should be able to derive the factor-analysis covariance and posterior, identify rotation/scale ambiguities, distinguish probabilistic PCA from generic matrix factorization, compare mixture/local factor models and probabilistic CCA, and state the assumptions that identify linear or auxiliary-variable nonlinear ICA rather than treating every latent coordinate as a source.

    A finite mixture uses a discrete latent to choose among component distributions. A factor model uses a continuous latent vector to induce dependence among observed coordinates. In factor analysis,

    \[ z\sim\mathcal N(0,I_L), \qquad x\mid z\sim\mathcal N(Wz+\mu,\Psi), \]

    where \(W\in\mathbb R^{D\times L}\) and \(\Psi\) is usually diagonal. Marginalizing \(z\) gives

    \[ p(x)=\mathcal N\!\left(x;\mu,WW^\top+\Psi\right). \]

    The graph is almost identical to the mixture plate; the state space and factor family changed. The assignment is now a continuous vector and the observation factor is linear Gaussian:

    This visual similarity isolates the mathematical change responsible for analytic Gaussian marginalization instead of making factor analysis look like an unrelated list of formulas.

    The low-rank term \(WW^\top\) explains covariance shared across features; the diagonal term \(\Psi\) represents feature-specific residual variation. The model therefore uses \(O(DL+D)\) covariance parameters rather than the \(O(D^2)\) entries of an unrestricted covariance matrix when \(L\ll D\).

    The posterior is also Gaussian. With \(M=I_L+W^\top\Psi^{-1}W\),

    \[ p(z\mid x)=\mathcal N\!\left( z; M^{-1}W^\top\Psi^{-1}(x-\mu), M^{-1} \right). \]

    This exactness comes from linear-Gaussian closure. Replacing \(Wz+\mu\) by a nonlinear neural decoder preserves the directed graph \(z\to x\) but usually destroys the analytic marginal and posterior. That replacement leads toward the generative model used by a VAE; adding \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) supplies an approximate inference model rather than changing the latent joint by itself (Kingma and Welling, 2014).

    Worked Two-Dimensional Factor Model¤

    Let \(L=1\), \(D=2\), \(W=(1,1)^\top\), \(\mu=0\), and \(\Psi=I_2\). Then

    \[ z\sim\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad x\mid z\sim\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}z\\z\end{bmatrix},I_2 \right), \]

    so the observed covariance is

    \[ WW^\top+I_2= \begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The two coordinates are correlated even though their observation noises are independent: both inherit the same latent factor. For the observation \(x=(2,0)^\top\),

    \[ M=1+W^\top W=3, \qquad p(z\mid x)=\mathcal N\!\left(z;\frac23,\frac13\right). \]

    The value \(2/3\) is a posterior mean, not a deterministically recovered code. Its variance \(1/3\) records uncertainty left after seeing the noisy coordinates. Generation runs in the other direction: sample one \(z\), then add an independent noise draw to each coordinate.

    Probabilistic PCA and Identifiability¤

    Probabilistic PCA restricts \(\Psi=\sigma^2I\). Maximum-likelihood loading directions span the principal subspace, while the probabilistic model also supplies a noise scale, a density, missing-data handling, and posterior uncertainty (Tipping and Bishop, 1999). It is not identical to applying deterministic PCA and then declaring its coordinates random.

    Factor coordinates are not unique. For any orthogonal \(R\),

    \[ (WR)(WR)^\top=WW^\top, \]

    so rotating \(W\) and the latent coordinate leaves the observed law unchanged under an isotropic latent prior. Sparsity, non-Gaussian independent components, supervision, temporal structure, or other constraints can reduce the equivalence class, but a named axis is not automatically an identified concept.

    Matrix Factorization Needs a Probability Model¤

    A deterministic low-rank approximation writes a data matrix as \(X\approx UV^\top\) and minimizes a reconstruction loss. Non-negative matrix factorization additionally constrains the factors. These are useful representation or optimization methods, but neither is a generative probability model until a likelihood, priors or parameter constraints, and a sampling procedure are declared.

    For example, probabilistic matrix factorization may specify

    \[ u_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\lambda_u^{-1}I), \quad v_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\lambda_v^{-1}I), \quad x_{ij}\mid u_i,v_j\sim\mathcal N(u_i^\top v_j,\sigma^2) \]

    for observed entries. This model has global collections of row and column latents; posterior inference couples them through the observed matrix. A Poisson or multinomial likelihood is often more appropriate for counts, and simplex or non-negativity constraints change both semantics and inference. Topic models are one structured count-data branch of this idea. Deep decoders replace bilinear maps with networks, but they still require a declared observation law if likelihood or posterior claims are made.

    A probabilistic non-negative factorization for counts can instead use Gamma-distributed non-negative factors and a Poisson observation law, for example

    \[ u_{ik}\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(a_u,b_u), \quad v_{jk}\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(a_v,b_v), \quad x_{ij}\mid u_i,v_j \sim\operatorname{Poisson}\!\left(\sum_k u_{ik}v_{jk}\right). \]

    Now prior sampling, count generation, posterior inference, and held-out predictive likelihood are defined. Gamma–Poisson conjugacy can support coordinate or augmented-variable updates, but integrating both factor matrices is not thereby trivial. Different NMF losses correspond to different observation/noise assumptions when a probabilistic interpretation exists; non-negativity alone does not select one. Cemgil (2009) develops the Poisson/Gamma construction and separates maximum-likelihood NMF updates from full Bayesian inference.

    Counterexample: a reconstruction factorization is not yet a generator

    An algorithm that returns matrices \(U,V\) minimizing \(\|X-UV^\top\|_F^2\) has not specified how to sample a new row, how missing entries are distributed, what noise measure the squared loss represents, or whether \(U\) and \(V\) are parameters or random variables. Calling it “generative” before those choices are made hides the probability space.

    Mixtures of Local Factor Models¤

    One linear subspace may be too rigid for curved or heterogeneous data. A mixture of factor analyzers introduces a component \(c\) and local factor \(z\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} c &\sim \operatorname{Categorical}(\pi),\\ z\mid c=k &\sim \mathcal N(0,I),\\ x\mid z,c=k &\sim \mathcal N(W_kz+\mu_k,\Psi_k). \end{aligned} \]

    After marginalizing \(z\), component \(k\) has covariance \(W_kW_k^\top+\Psi_k\). The model is therefore a constrained Gaussian mixture whose local low-rank covariance needs \(O(DL)\) rather than \(O(D^2)\) parameters per component when \(L\ll D\). It can approximate a curved distribution using several local subspaces, but component labels, local rotations, and redundant components remain non-identifiable. A finite mixture of local linear maps is not one globally invertible flow. The original exact-EM construction makes this local-density interpretation explicit (Ghahramani and Hinton, 1996).

    Paired Views and Probabilistic CCA¤

    For paired observations \((x,y)\), a shared latent model writes

    \[ z\sim\mathcal N(0,I),\qquad x\mid z\sim\mathcal N(W_xz+\mu_x,\Psi_x),\qquad y\mid z\sim\mathcal N(W_yz+\mu_y,\Psi_y). \]

    The views are conditionally independent given \(z\), and their cross-covariance is \(W_xW_y^\top\). Under the probabilistic CCA restrictions, maximum likelihood recovers the classical canonical subspaces—not unique named coordinates. View-private latent variables are needed when each view contains structured variation absent from the other; otherwise the shared code is forced to explain both common and private variation. More than two views, nonlinear decoders, or non-Gaussian observation laws change the model and usually the inference problem, even when the training objective still contains a correlation term. The Gaussian latent-variable equivalence to classical CCA is an assumption-specific maximum-likelihood result (Bach and Jordan, 2005).

    Exponential-Family Factors and Nonlinear Decoders¤

    Factor analysis extends beyond real-valued Gaussian observations. A conditional exponential-family model can use

    \[ p(x_d\mid z) = h_d(x_d)\exp\!\left\{ T_d(x_d)^\top\eta_d(z)-A_d(\eta_d(z)) \right\}, \]

    with a linear natural parameter \(\eta_d(z)=W_dz+b_d\) for Bernoulli, count, or categorical data. The likelihood remains normalized, but a Gaussian prior is generally no longer conjugate to this observation law. A deterministic "exponential-family PCA" point estimate of every \(z_n\) is therefore not the same procedure as integrating those latents or maintaining a non-degenerate variational posterior.

    Collins, Dasgupta, and Schapire (2001) derive the point-estimation and Bregman-loss construction. The normalized conditional model above is a probabilistic extension; it should not be attributed every property of that deterministic objective without an inference and parameter-learning specification.

    Replacing \(W_dz+b_d\) by a neural network gives the decoder side of a VAE-like latent model; adding an encoder specifies an inference algorithm. Replacing it by a Gaussian-process function gives a GP latent-variable model. In the classical GP-LVM, latent inputs for the training cases are often optimized rather than produced by an amortized encoder. Sampling a new observation still requires a declared latent prior and the appropriate integration over the GP function or posterior. A nonlinear map increases flexibility; it does not by itself identify the latent coordinates (Lawrence, 2005).

    Independent Components and the Source-Separation Contract¤

    Independent component analysis (ICA) changes the latent assumption rather than merely rotating PCA after the fact. In the square noiseless model,

    \[ z_j\ \text{independent}, \qquad x=Az, \qquad B=A^{-1}, \]

    so a continuous density transforms as

    \[ p_X(x) = \left|\det B\right| \prod_{j=1}^{D}p_j\!\left((Bx)_j\right). \]

    The determinant is essential: optimizing only independent-looking recovered coordinates is not maximum likelihood for a general unmixing matrix. In practice, centering and whitening can reduce the remaining search to an orthogonal rotation, after which likelihood, non-Gaussian contrast functions, or independence objectives estimate the axes.

    Worked finite example: six observations, one exact unmixing rule¤

    Let \(Z_1\) be uniform on \(\{-1,+1\}\), let \(Z_2\) have probabilities \((1/4,1/2,1/4)\) on \(\{-2,0,+2\}\), and make the two sources independent. Mix them with

    \[ \begin{aligned} A&= \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix},\\[4pt] B=A^{-1} &=\frac12 \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}. \end{aligned} \]

    For example, \(z=(1,2)^\top\) produces \(x=(3,-1)^\top\), and \(Bx=(1,2)^\top\) recovers the source exactly. Enumerating the \(2\times3\) latent states gives six observation locations with the corresponding product masses. The observed coordinates are mixtures; the recovered coordinates are independent by construction.

    This example does not prove that a learning algorithm will recover \(B\) from finite samples. Classical linear ICA identification additionally requires an appropriate non-degenerate mixing model and non-Gaussian independent sources, with at most one Gaussian source under the standard theorem. Even then, permutation and nonzero scaling (including sign) are unavoidable equivalences (Comon, 1994). If instead \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\), every orthogonal \(R\) gives \(RZ\overset{d}{=}Z\); replacing \(A\) by \(AR^\top\) preserves the observed law, so whitening cannot choose a unique rotation.

    Sparse Coding and Dictionary Learning¤

    Sparse coding permits a noisy, rectangular, even overcomplete dictionary:

    \[ \begin{aligned} z_k&\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim\operatorname{Laplace}(0,1/\lambda),\\ x\mid z,W&\sim\mathcal N(Wz,\sigma^2I). \end{aligned} \]

    For fixed \(W\), MAP inference is the Lasso-shaped problem

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal J(z) &=\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|x-Wz\|_2^2\\ &\phantom{{}={}}+\lambda\|z\|_1,\\ \hat z_{\mathrm{MAP}}&=\arg\min_z\mathcal J(z). \end{aligned} \]

    With

    \[ W= \begin{bmatrix}1&0&1\\0&1&1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad x=\begin{bmatrix}3\\1\end{bmatrix}, \]

    both \(z=(3,1,0)^\top\) and \(z=(2,0,1)^\top\) reconstruct \(x\) exactly, but their \(\ell_1\) norms are \(4\) and \(3\). The MAP objective prefers the latter when the residuals tie. This is estimator behavior under a declared prior—not proof that the selected atoms are unique physical causes.

    Learning \(W\) while integrating \(z\) maximizes a marginal likelihood. Alternating between point estimates of \(z_n\) and updates of \(W\) instead optimizes a joint or profile objective. These procedures can be useful but are not interchangeable. Dictionary-column norms must also be constrained: replacing one column by \(cw_k\) and its coefficient by \(z_k/c\) preserves reconstruction while shrinking the coefficient penalty as \(c\) grows. Modern sparse autoencoders inherit these scaling, overcompleteness, stability, and identification questions even when the encoder makes inference fast (Mairal et al., 2010).

    Nonlinear ICA: Expressivity Removes the Linear Theorem¤

    An unrestricted invertible nonlinear mixing \(x=f(z)\) is not identified from one observational distribution merely because the coordinates of \(z\) are independent. Flexible measure-preserving transformations can produce different independent coordinates with the same observed law. Positive nonlinear-ICA results therefore add information or structure: temporal nonstationarity, multiple environments, paired views, an auxiliary variable, restricted mixing maps, sparsity, or interventions.

    Hyvärinen, Sasaki, and Turner (2019) derive one auxiliary-variable route using generalized contrastive learning. Khemakhem et al. (2020) obtain identification for a conditional, factorized exponential-family prior under their stated assumptions; an ordinary unconditional VAE does not inherit that theorem. More specific results extend particular identification routes to multi-domain linear mixtures with dependent components and study sparse-autoencoder stability through dictionary-learning conditions. These results enlarge the assumption–conclusion map. They do not make arbitrary nonlinear latents, ICA axes, or sparse-autoencoder features uniquely meaningful.

    Independence is a model claim, not a visual impression

    Low sample correlation establishes neither mutual independence nor source recovery. State the allowed equivalence class, test held-out density or independence with uncertainty, rerun estimation, perturb preprocessing and dimension, and validate any semantic interpretation using external variables or interventions.

    Factor and source-model retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: factors, views, and sources
    1. Why are factor-loading matrices not uniquely identified by the observed covariance?
    2. What probabilistic ingredients are absent from an unqualified low-rank matrix factorization?
    3. Which assumptions make the finite ICA unmixing example identifiable beyond permutation and scaling?
    4. Why does nonlinear decoder expressivity weaken ordinary nonlinear-ICA identification?

    Solutions. (1) Rotating latent coordinates and counter-rotating loadings preserves the covariance under an isotropic latent prior. (2) A sampling law, noise model, priors/regularization semantics, and predictive distribution. (3) Independent non-Gaussian sources and an invertible linear mixing model, with the usual unavoidable ambiguities. (4) Arbitrary invertible nonlinear transformations can produce observationally equivalent latent representations without auxiliary structure or restrictions.

    Factor and source-model synthesis¤

    Factor models become generative only after their latent, loading, and noise laws are declared. Their coordinates are generally identified only up to a symmetry. Multiple views, sparsity, temporal structure, or auxiliary variables can reduce ambiguity, but each conclusion inherits the assumptions that supplied it.
