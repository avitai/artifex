    This chapter develops discrete noising chains, the three ELBO term classes, denoising objectives, schedule-induced training measures, conditional and marginal scores, SDEs, and probability-flow ODEs. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    Diffusion models declare a forward corruption process that moves data toward a reference distribution and learn a reverse-time conditional, score, denoiser, or equivalent field. The forward process is usually fixed, but it can be parameterized and learned jointly when the required marginals or posteriors remain tractable. The discrete construction was introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho, Jain & Abbeel (2020) gave the widely used DDPM parameterization. Score-based SDEs, probability-flow ODEs, discrete diffusion, and flow-related formulations share some population objects under stated assumptions, but their finite algorithms need not coincide.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand Gaussian conditioning, Markov chains, KL divergence, conditional expectation, gradients of log densities, and basic ODE/SDE notation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive the closed-form forward marginal and posterior of a Gaussian DDPM;
    2. distinguish conditional perturbation scores from noisy marginal scores;
    3. derive the ELBO terms and explain why the simplified noise MSE is a reweighted objective;
    4. convert population-optimal noise, data, score, and velocity predictions with stated conventions; and
    5. distinguish marginal equivalence of the reverse SDE/probability-flow ODE from equality of finite numerical samples.

    Components and consequences¤

    The family separates a known forward path from a learned reverse object. This often yields supervised denoising-style regression targets at sampled noise levels. It also introduces choices that must be reported: path and schedule, target parameterization, loss weighting, architecture, reverse SDE or ODE, solver, time grid, stochasticity, guidance, and representation. Regression form does not guarantee stable optimization, correct likelihoods, mode coverage, or low sampling cost.

    Constructive interpretation: corrupt, estimate, and reverse¤

    The basic construction has four stages:

    1. Choose a forward path. Define transitions or continuous dynamics that progressively reduce information about a data sample while approaching a tractable reference law.

    2. Construct a training target. From a data sample, noise level, and sampled corruption, form a denoising, noise, data, score, velocity, or transition target under an explicit convention.

    3. Estimate the reverse object. Train a parameterized predictor across noise levels. Population conversion formulas relate several targets, while finite networks and weighted losses can make their learned errors different.

    4. Generate numerically. Draw from the reference law and apply a discrete reverse chain, SDE solver, ODE solver, consistency map, or distilled transition. The output distribution depends on both learned error and numerical approximation.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Forward Diffusion Process¤

    The original DDPM formulation uses a fixed Gaussian Markov chain over \(T\) steps; \(T=1000\) is its historical training discretization, not a family requirement:

    \[ q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{1-\beta_t} \, x_{t-1}, \beta_t \mathbf{I}) \]

    where \(\beta_t \in (0,1)\) controls the variance of noise added at timestep \(t\). The complete forward chain factors as:

    \[ q(x_{1:T} | x_0) = \prod_{t=1}^T q(x_t | x_{t-1}) \]

    Key property: We can sample \(x_t\) at any arbitrary timestep directly without simulating the full chain. Defining \(\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t\) and \(\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{i=1}^t \alpha_i\):

    \[ q(x_t | x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0, (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t) \mathbf{I}) \]

    This can be reparameterized as:

    \[ x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \, \epsilon \quad \text{where } \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I}) \]

    Boundary condition: When \(\bar\alpha_T\) is sufficiently small, \(q(x_T\mid x_0)\) is close to \(\mathcal N(0,I)\) for appropriately scaled data. The residual terminal KL is part of the ELBO; a finite schedule need not make the endpoint exactly independent of \(x_0\).

    The posterior conditioned on the original data is also Gaussian with tractable parameters:

    \[ q(x_{t-1} | x_t, x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0), \tilde{\beta}_t \mathbf{I}) \]

    where:

    \[ \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0) = \frac{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_t}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_0 + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t} (1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_t \]
    \[ \tilde{\beta}_t = \frac{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1}}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \cdot \beta_t \]

    The Reverse Diffusion Process¤

    The reverse process learns to invert the forward diffusion, starting from noise \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\) and progressively denoising to data \(x_0\):

    \[ p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) = \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t, t), \Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)) \]

    The complete generative process:

    \[ p_\theta(x_{0:T}) = p(x_T) \prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) \]

    Related parameterizations:

    Noise prediction: Under MSE, the network estimates the conditional mean of the sampled perturbation noise:

    \[ \hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \]
    \[ \mu_\theta(x_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \right) \]

    Data Prediction: The network directly predicts the clean image:

    \[ \hat{x}_0 = f_\theta(x_t, t) \]

    Score Prediction: The network predicts the gradient of the log probability:

    \[ s_\theta(x_t, t) \approx \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t) \]

    For one sampled pair \((x_0,\epsilon)\), the clean sample and noise are deterministically related given \(x_t\) and \(\bar\alpha_t\). The corresponding identity is for the conditional perturbation kernel:

    \[ \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t\mid x_0) \;=\; -\,\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0 - x_t}{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}. \]

    The score needed by the reverse process is instead the marginal score of \(q_t(x_t)=\int q(x_t\mid x_0)q_{\mathrm{data}}(x_0)\,dx_0\). Fisher's identity gives

    \[ \nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(x_t) = \mathbb E\!\left[\nabla_{x_t}\log q(x_t\mid x_0)\mid x_t\right] = -\frac{\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t]}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}. \]

    Tweedie's formula (Efron, 2011) further connects the score to the posterior mean:

    \[ \mathbb{E}[x_0 \mid x_t] \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}}\bigl(x_t + (1-\bar{\alpha}_t)\,\nabla_{x_t}\log q(x_t)\bigr), \]

    Thus the MSE-optimal noise and \(x_0\) predictors estimate conditional means, and those predictions can be converted affinely. An individual latent noise draw is not determined by \(x_t\) after marginalizing over the unknown data sample.

    Mathematical Equivalence

    Under squared-error training and consistent weighting, the population-optimal noise, clean-data posterior mean, and marginal score contain equivalent information. Finite networks, target weighting, preconditioning, optimization, and approximation error make parameterization a real numerical and statistical design choice—not an identity between individual training targets.

    The ELBO Derivation¤

    The latent-variable bridge places this construction beside mixtures, factor models, VAEs, and state-space models while separating auxiliary noisy states from semantic representation latents.

    The discrete-time DDPM construction is a Markovian hierarchical latent-variable model with a VAE-style variational bound. For a fixed observation \(x_0\), the bound decomposes into three semantically different pieces:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \log p_\theta(x_0) &\geq \underbrace{ \mathbb{E}_{q(x_1\mid x_0)}[\log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_1)] }_{\text{decoder or reconstruction term}} \\ &\quad- \underbrace{ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(x_T\mid x_0)\|p(x_T)) }_{\text{terminal prior-matching term}} \\ &\quad- \underbrace{ \sum_{t=2}^T \mathbb{E}_{q(x_t\mid x_0)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\|p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)) }_{\text{reverse-transition terms}}. \end{aligned} \]

    An outer expectation over the empirical data distribution turns this pointwise bound into a training objective. The first term also requires an explicit observation model: for integer-valued images, a discretized decoder distribution is needed before the bound can be interpreted as bits per dimension. The terminal term contains no learned reverse mean when the reference prior is fixed, but it need not be zero at a finite endpoint.

    For Gaussian posteriors and a fixed isotropic model covariance \(\sigma_t^2I\), the part of an intermediate KL that depends on the model mean is

    \[ L_t^{(\mu)} = \mathbb{E}_{q(x_0,x_t)}\left[\frac{1}{2\sigma_t^2} \|\tilde{\mu}_t(x_t,x_0) - \mu_\theta(x_t,t)\|^2\right] + C_t, \]

    where \(C_t\) is independent of the predicted mean under this fixed-variance assumption. If the model predicts a variance, the log-determinant and variance-ratio terms are not constants and must be retained. Substituting the noise parameterization into the mean term yields a time-weighted noise-regression loss:

    \[ L_t^{(\mu)} = w_t\,\mathbb{E}_{x_0,\epsilon}\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t} \, \epsilon, t)\|^2\right] + C_t, \]

    for a schedule- and variance-dependent coefficient \(w_t\). Ho et al. (2020) instead used the unweighted simplified objective for their sample-quality experiments:

    \[ L_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{t \sim U[1,T], \, x_0, \, \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})}\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\right] \]

    This is easy to estimate, but it is not the exact ELBO with its weights removed harmlessly: it changes the relative importance of noise levels. The original paper found this choice useful for sample quality in its evaluated image settings. Likelihood-oriented training, learned variance, other targets, and other time-sampling laws require their own derivation.

    Variance Schedules¤

    The noise schedule \(\{\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_T\}\) fundamentally affects training and sampling quality:

    Linear Schedule (Ho et al., 2020):

    \[ \beta_t = \beta_1 + (\beta_T - \beta_1) \cdot \frac{t-1}{T-1} \]

    Typically \(\beta_1 = 0.0001\), \(\beta_T = 0.02\). Simple but can add too much noise early.

    Cosine Schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021 — IDDPM):

    \[ \bar{\alpha}_t = \frac{f(t)}{f(0)} \quad \text{where} \quad f(t) = \cos^2\left(\frac{t/T + s}{1+s} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2}\right) \]
    \[ \beta_t = 1 - \frac{\bar{\alpha}_t}{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \]

    with \(s = 0.008\). Nichol and Dhariwal found it improved their evaluated DDPM settings relative to the linear schedule; it is one schedule among several parameterisations used by later diffusion, EDM, and flow-matching systems.

    Schedule Selection

    Match the schedule to the prediction target, loss weighting, preconditioning, and sampler. Cosine schedules avoid destroying information as quickly as the original linear DDPM schedule in the evaluated setup. EDM/Karras noise levels, log-SNR schedules, shifted schedules, and flow-matching time distributions make different design choices; compare them under a fixed compute and sampler protocol.

    A schedule is also a measure over denoising problems¤

    It is useful to separate the path coordinate from the distribution used to sample that coordinate during training. Let \(\lambda\) denote log-SNR, take \(L>0\), and fix endpoints \(\lambda(0)=L\) and \(\lambda(1)=-L\). Two monotone schedules with those endpoints are

    \[ \lambda_A(t)=L(1-2t), \qquad \lambda_B(t)=L(1-2t^2). \]

    If \(t\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), schedule \(A\) induces the uniform density \(p_A(\lambda)=1/(2L)\). Schedule \(B\) induces

    \[ p_B(\lambda)=\left|\frac{dt}{d\lambda}\right| =\frac{1}{4Lt} =\frac{1}{4L\sqrt{(1-\lambda/L)/2}}, \]

    so it presents more high-SNR examples near \(\lambda=L\). The two schedules share endpoints, but uniform clock time does not define the same training measure over denoising tasks.

    Variational Diffusion Models show a narrower invariance: in their continuous-time formulation, after the corresponding change of variable and with the SNR endpoints fixed, the exact VLB is invariant to the shape of the schedule. That statement does not make finite Monte Carlo training, network conditioning, optimizer dynamics, or a discretized sampler schedule-invariant. The schedule can instead be learned or chosen to reduce estimator variance, while the endpoints control the represented likelihood bound.

    Score-Based Perspective¤

    The score function \(\nabla_x \log p(x)\) points toward regions of higher probability density. Score-based models (Song & Ermon, 2019 — NCSN) train a network \(s_\theta(x, t)\) to approximate this gradient field through denoising score matching (Vincent, 2011):

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}} = \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\mathbb{E}_{p(\tilde{x}|x)}\left[\|s_\theta(\tilde{x}, t) - \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \log p(\tilde{x}|x)\|^2\right] \]

    Given the learned score, generation proceeds via Langevin dynamics:

    \[ x_{i+1} = x_i + \delta \nabla_x \log p(x) + \sqrt{2\delta} \, z_i \quad \text{where } z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I}) \]

    The connection to diffusion is conditional-expectation based: the marginal noisy-data score equals the negative posterior mean noise divided by its perturbation scale, not an arbitrary individual noise draw recoverable from \(x_t\).

    Stochastic Differential Equations¤

    The continuous-time formulation (Song et al., 2021 — ICLR Outstanding Paper) generalizes discrete diffusion as an SDE:

    \[ dx = f(x,t)dt + g(t)dw \]

    Variance Preserving (VP) SDE corresponds to DDPM:

    \[ dx = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x \, dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)} \, dw \]

    The reverse-time SDE enables generation when integrated from terminal time toward zero; the displayed \(dt\) follows the reverse-time integration convention:

    \[ dx = \left[f(x,t) - g(t)^2\nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt + g(t) d\bar{w} \]

    There exists an equivalent probability flow ODE:

    \[ dx = \left[f(x,t) - \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2\nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt \]

    With an exact score and exact integration, the probability-flow ODE has the same one-time marginals as the SDE and supports a change-in-log-density calculation through its divergence. Neural score error, stochastic divergence estimation, finite tolerances, and numerical integration make reported likelihood and samples approximate in practice.

    Worked example: one scalar forward and posterior step¤

    One example will now carry all three formulations: the discrete DDPM chain, denoising-score regression, and continuous-time reverse dynamics. Let the data distribution be

    \[ X_0\sim\mathcal N(m_0,v_0)=\mathcal N(1,4), \]

    and choose \(\alpha_1=0.8\) and \(\alpha_2=0.5\). Thus \(\bar\alpha_1=0.8\) and \(\bar\alpha_2=0.4\). Write

    \[ a_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}, \qquad b_t^2=1-\bar\alpha_t, \qquad X_t=a_tX_0+b_t\epsilon, \quad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,1). \]

    Gaussian affine closure gives

    \[ X_1\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.8},3.4), \qquad X_2\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2). \]

    We inspect the noisy value \(x_2=0.5\). When a calculation also needs a realized clean training example, use \(x_0=2\). Keeping the random data law, the observed noisy value, and the realized training pair distinct prevents most of the apparent contradictions below.

    Formulation 1: the conditioned DDPM posterior is not the marginal reverse kernel¤

    For the realized pair \((x_0,x_2)=(2,0.5)\), the tractable forward posterior from the DDPM ELBO is

    \[ q(x_1\mid x_2,x_0) =\mathcal N\!\left(x_1; \frac{\sqrt{0.8}\,0.5}{0.6}x_0 +\frac{\sqrt{0.5}\,0.2}{0.6}x_2, \frac{0.2}{0.6}\,0.5\right), \]

    so that

    \[ q(x_1\mid x_2=0.5,x_0=2) =\mathcal N(1.608563,1/6). \]

    The reverse generator does not know \(x_0\). Its population target is the marginal reverse conditional \(q(x_1\mid x_2)\), obtained by integrating the previous posterior against \(q(x_0\mid x_2)\). In this Gaussian example, \((X_1,X_2)\) is jointly Gaussian with

    \[ \operatorname{Cov}(X_1,X_2)=\sqrt{0.5}\,(3.4), \]

    and the Gaussian conditioning formula yields

    \[ q(x_1\mid x_2=0.5)=\mathcal N(0.749680,0.772727). \]

    These distributions are visibly different. For non-Gaussian data, the marginal reverse conditional is generally a mixture and need not be Gaussian even though every \(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\) is Gaussian. A Gaussian \(p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\) is therefore a modeling approximation to the marginal reverse kernel, not a claim that the clean-conditioned posterior is available at generation time. This distinction is central to the variational construction in DDPM.

    Formulation 2: one conditional score target averages to the marginal score¤

    For the same realized training pair, the conditional perturbation score is

    \[ \nabla_{x_2}\log q(x_2\mid x_0=2) =\frac{\sqrt{0.4}\,2-0.5}{0.6} =1.274852. \]

    The marginal noisy-data score uses the law \(X_2\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2)\) instead:

    \[ s_2(0.5) =\nabla_{x_2}\log q_2(0.5) =-\frac{0.5-\sqrt{0.4}}{2.2} =0.060207. \]

    There is no inconsistency. Denoising score matching averages the conditional target over the clean posterior:

    \[ \mathbb E\!\left[ \nabla_{x_2}\log q(X_2\mid X_0) \mid X_2=x_2 \right] =s_2(x_2). \tag{DIFF.1}\label{eq:diffusion-denoising-score-expectation} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:diffusion-denoising-score-expectation}\) is the bridge from conditional denoising targets to the marginal score. Equivalently, Tweedie's identity and the noise-prediction identity give

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E[X_0\mid X_2=0.5] &=\frac{0.5+0.6\,s_2(0.5)}{\sqrt{0.4}} =0.847687,\\ \mathbb E[\epsilon\mid X_2=0.5] &=-\sqrt{0.6}\,s_2(0.5) =-0.046636. \end{aligned} \]

    The realized pair has \(\epsilon=(0.5-\sqrt{0.4}\,2)/\sqrt{0.6}\approx-0.98750\); it is a training target, not the posterior mean noise determined by \(x_2\). The equality is a population-optimum correspondence under the denoising objective, as established by denoising score matching and used by score-based diffusion—not an equality for every sampled pair (Vincent, 2011; Song et al., 2021).

    Formulation 3: an exact VP-SDE embedding and its two reverse dynamics¤

    Let continuous time be \(\tau\) and consider the constant-rate VP process

    \[ dX_\tau=-\frac12X_\tau\,d\tau+dW_\tau. \]

    At \(\tau_i=-\log\bar\alpha_i\), its transition between consecutive grid points is exactly

    \[ X_{\tau_i}\mid X_{\tau_{i-1}} \sim\mathcal N\!\left( \sqrt{\alpha_i}\,X_{\tau_{i-1}},1-\alpha_i \right). \]

    Thus this particular discrete chain is not merely similar to the SDE: it is the SDE observed at the nonuniform times \(\tau_i\). Matching a single endpoint coefficient \(\bar\alpha_t\), however, would not by itself specify the intermediate path or cross-time coupling.

    For arbitrary \(\tau\),

    \[ p_\tau=\mathcal N(m_\tau,v_\tau), \qquad m_\tau=e^{-\tau/2}, \qquad v_\tau=1+3e^{-\tau}, \]

    and hence

    \[ s_\tau(x)=-\frac{x-m_\tau}{v_\tau}. \]

    At \(\tau_\star=-\log 0.4\approx0.916291\), this recovers \(p_{\tau_\star}=\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2)\). The reverse-SDE drift and probability-flow velocity are, respectively,

    \[ r_\tau(x)=-\frac12x-s_\tau(x), \qquad u_\tau(x)=-\frac12x-\frac12s_\tau(x), \]

    with the reverse SDE integrated from \(\tau_\star\) down to zero. At \(x=0.5\) their values are \(r_{\tau_\star}(0.5)=-0.310207\) and \(u_{\tau_\star}(0.5)=-0.280104\). They are not the same vector field.

    The probability-flow ODE nevertheless preserves the same one-time Gaussian marginals under exact integration. Indeed,

    \[ \mathbb E[u_\tau(X_\tau)]=-\frac12m_\tau=m_\tau', \]

    and its centered linear coefficient is \(-1/2+1/(2v_\tau)\), so

    \[ \frac{d}{d\tau}v_\tau =2\left(-\frac12+\frac{1}{2v_\tau}\right)v_\tau =1-v_\tau. \]

    These are exactly the mean and variance equations of the forward VP marginal. The reverse SDE has the same Fokker--Planck evolution when run backward. Shared marginals do not mean equal paths: conditional on a terminal state, the ODE trajectory is deterministic while the SDE trajectory remains stochastic.

    A one-step counterexample: exact score does not make a coarse solver exact¤

    Start from the exact terminal marginal \(X_{\tau_\star}\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2)\) and use the exact score, but freeze each vector field at \(\tau_\star\) for one step of length \(\tau_\star\). Backward Euler for the probability-flow ODE gives

    \[ X_0^{\mathrm E} =X_{\tau_\star}-\tau_\star u_{\tau_\star}(X_{\tau_\star}) \sim\mathcal N(0.922212,3.436936). \]

    One Euler--Maruyama step for the reverse SDE gives

    \[ X_0^{\mathrm{EM}} =X_{\tau_\star}-\tau_\star r_{\tau_\star}(X_{\tau_\star}) +\sqrt{\tau_\star}Z \sim\mathcal N(0.922212,3.303365), \quad Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1). \]

    Neither equals the target \(\mathcal N(1,4)\), and the two numerical laws differ from each other. This is discretization error with no score or initialization error. Exact Gaussian analyses now quantify initialization, terminal truncation, discretization, and score-approximation errors separately (Pierret & Galerne, 2025); current error bounds likewise retain finite-time, time-discretization, statistical, and score-estimation terms rather than collapsing them into “the diffusion error” (De Bortoli et al., 2026).

    Assumption ledger: what is actually equivalent?¤

    Table DIFF.2. Assumption ledger: what is actually equivalent?
    Relationship Exact statement Assumptions It does not imply
    DDPM forward marginal \(X_t=a_tX_0+b_t\epsilon\) matches the \(t\)-step Gaussian chain. Independent Gaussian increments and the declared \(\alpha_t\). The terminal law is exactly standard normal for finite \(t\).
    Clean-conditioned posterior \(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\) has the displayed Gaussian form. Linear Gaussian forward chain and access to \(x_0\). The generator knows \(x_0\), or this equals \(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\).
    Denoising-to-marginal score The conditional perturbation score averages to \(\nabla\log q_t\). Differentiation may pass through the marginal integral; positive density and integrability where evaluated. An individual sampled noise equals its posterior mean.
    Noise, data, and score predictions Their population-optimal conditional means convert affinely at fixed \(t\). Same corruption path, positive \(a_t,b_t\), squared losses, sufficient function class, and positive weighting. Finite shared networks, optimization, or differently weighted risks behave identically.
    Discrete VP chain and VP SDE The chain equals an SDE skeleton at \(\tau_i=-\log\bar\alpha_i\). The OU/VP interpolation and those grid times are declared. A matched endpoint uniquely determines the path between grid points.
    Reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE Exact continuous dynamics have the same one-time marginals. Exact marginal score, correct terminal law, regularity, and exact integration. Equal trajectories, transition kernels, couplings, or finite-step samples.
    Numerical samplers A consistent solver converges under its stability and regularity conditions as its mesh is refined. Solver-specific order, tolerances, score accuracy, and endpoint control. Equal NFE, equal error constants, or exactness at a coarse step count.

    The practical lesson is to name the level of the claim. Algebraic equivalence applies to exact target conversions under one parameterization convention. Population-optimum correspondence connects denoising targets to the marginal score. Distributional equivalence under assumptions connects the exact reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE. A finite sampler is a separate algorithm whose error must be measured.

    Mathematical Foundation: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Discrete diffusion is simultaneously a hierarchical latent model, a denoising estimator, and—under continuous limits—a score-driven stochastic transport. These are precise correspondences under assumptions, not permission to conflate training targets or finite solvers.

    Retrieval practice: diffusion foundations
    1. When is \(x_T\) exactly standard Gaussian and independent of \(x_0\)?
    2. What does MSE noise prediction estimate at population optimum?
    3. Why can a probability-flow ODE and reverse SDE share marginals without producing identical finite-step sample paths?
    4. Why is the simplified DDPM loss not numerically identical to the full ELBO weighting?

    Solutions. (1) Only under an exact terminal construction or limiting schedule; ordinary finite DDPM schedules make it approximate. (2) \(\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t,t]\). (3) One is deterministic conditional on its initial state and the other stochastic, and numerical discretizations introduce different error. (4) It discards or changes timestep-dependent coefficients derived from Gaussian KL terms.
