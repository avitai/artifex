    This chapter covers latent geometry, inversion and editing, evaluation metrics, training practice, and careful comparisons with neighboring model families. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    Latent Space Properties¤

    Latent Space Properties: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes familiarity with held-out evaluation, sample estimators, and the failure modes in Chapter 3. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. test latent continuity and editability without assuming semantic disentanglement;
    2. distinguish latent recovery, reconstruction, posterior inference, and an editing representation;
    3. choose among per-example optimization, amortized encoders, joint inference, and hybrid inversion;
    4. compute and interpret FID, KID, and precision/recall with their estimator limitations;
    5. separate fidelity, coverage, memorization, conditional alignment, and inversion uncertainty;
    6. design fair GAN–VAE–diffusion comparisons using matched operating points; and
    7. turn loss curves, critic generalization, and sample metrics into a debugging hypothesis.

    Interpolation and Continuity¤

    GAN latent spaces are often tested for the following desirable properties; none follows from adversarial training alone:

    • Local output continuity for small latent perturbations
    • Plausible interpolation along a declared path and latent distribution
    • Sample fidelity and coverage under the actual sampling or truncation rule
    # Interpolation between two points
z1 = sample_latent()
z2 = sample_latent()
alphas = np.linspace(0, 1, 10)
z_interp = [(1-α)*z1 + α*z2 for α in alphas]
images = [generator(z) for z in z_interp]

    Semantic Vector Arithmetic¤

    Some trained generators and chosen latent parameterizations exhibit approximately linear semantic edits:

    \[ z_{smiling\_woman} \approx z_{woman} + (z_{smiling\_man} - z_{man}) \]

    These are empirical constructions, not vector identities. Their validity must be tested for edit success, attribute leakage, identity preservation, realism, and subgroup consistency. Candidate examples include:

    • Face with glasses = face + glasses vector
    • Older face = face + aging vector
    • Different expression = face + expression vector

    Latent Space Structure¤

    Latent structure influences controllability, but none of the following follows from the GAN objective alone:

    • discovered directions may correlate with labeled attributes such as age, expression, or lighting;
    • selected paths may change outputs smoothly;
    • approximately separated controls may exist over a bounded domain.

    Discovering Interpretable Directions

    Methods for finding semantic directions:

    • Linear probes (Shen et al., 2020 — InterFaceGAN): train binary classifiers on \(w\) for each attribute; the classifier normal is the attribute direction.
    • GANSpace (Härkönen et al., 2020): unsupervised PCA of intermediate feature activations; the top components recover interpretable axes.
    • SeFa (Shen & Zhou, 2021): closed-form factorisation of the generator's first weight matrix recovers semantic directions without training.
    • Supervised methods: Use attribute labels to find control vectors via paired-sample regression (StyleGAN-NADA, StyleCLIP).

    GAN Inversion and Inference¤

    Begin with the requested object¤

    Let a trained generator define

    \[ Z\sim p_Z,\qquad X=G_\theta(Z),\qquad Y\mid X\sim p_{\mathrm{obs}}(y\mid X). \]

    \(X\) is the generated object and \(Y\) is what a measurement system reveals. For an ordinary image inversion task, \(Y\) may simply be the target image and the observation map may be the identity. The word inversion is nevertheless used for several different queries:

    Table GAN.6. Begin with the requested object
    Query Desired output Success means What it does not establish
    Latent recovery The original \(z_*\), or its declared equivalence class, when \(x=G_\theta(z_*)\) A synthetic ground-truth code is recovered A non-injective generator need not reveal which preimage produced \(x\)
    Reconstruction or projection One code \(\hat z\) or expanded generator state whose output resembles \(y\) \(G_\theta(\hat z)\) is close under declared distortions The code need not be the original latent, typical under \(p_Z\), editable, or unique
    Posterior inference The conditional law \(P_\theta(dz\mid y)\) or expectations under it Compatible latents receive calibrated relative mass under a declared observation model A point optimizer or deterministic encoder is not a posterior distribution
    Editing representation A code and edit operation that change intended attributes while preserving protected content Edit success, locality, realism, and identity/content retention hold over a declared domain The lowest-reconstruction-error code need not be the most editable

    When \(p_{\mathrm{obs}}(y\mid G_\theta(z))\) is evaluable, the latent posterior has the familiar unnormalized form

    \[ p_\theta(z\mid y) \propto p_Z(z)\,p_{\mathrm{obs}}\!\left(y\mid G_\theta(z)\right). \]

    This does not require an observation-space density for the noiseless pushforward \(G_{\theta\#}p_Z\). It does require an observation model. Without one, a reconstruction loss specifies an optimization criterion, not a probabilistic posterior. For a noiseless deterministic generator, a regular conditional law over preimages may exist even when it has no ordinary Lebesgue density.

    Worked counterexample: perfect reconstruction without latent recovery¤

    Let \(Z\) be uniform on \(\{-1,0,+1\}\) and let

    \[ G(z)=z^2. \]

    Then \(P(X=0)=1/3\) and \(P(X=1)=2/3\). Observing \(X=1\) does not identify the generating latent:

    \[ P(Z=-1\mid X=1)=P(Z=+1\mid X=1)=\frac12. \]

    A deterministic encoder can choose \(E(0)=0\) and \(E(1)=+1\). It reconstructs every generated observation exactly because \(G(E(X))=X\). Yet it fails to recover samples generated from \(Z=-1\), so

    \[ P\!\left(E(G(Z))=Z\right)=\frac23, \]

    which is the best any deterministic encoder can do for this example. Returning \(+1\) for \(X=1\) also discards half of the posterior mass. Reconstruction, recovery, and posterior inference are therefore different even before neural approximation or optimization error enters.

    Now present the same generator with the real-valued target \(y=1/2\). No latent has \(G(z)=1/2\). A squared-error optimizer can return \(0\) or either of \(\pm1\), all with distortion \(1/4\). That is a projection onto the generator range, not evidence that the target came from the generator.

    Injectivity is an assumption, not an effect of inversion

    A deterministic encoder and generator can be mutual inverses almost surely only when their maps and probability laws are compatible with that property. Positive-mass collisions such as \(G(-1)=G(+1)\) rule it out. A theorem proved for an ideal deterministic bidirectional GAN at exact joint matching does not make an arbitrary pretrained generator injective.

    Per-example optimization¤

    A common post-hoc inversion holds the generator fixed and solves

    \[ \hat z = \arg\min_z \left[ \lambda_{\mathrm{data}}d_{\mathrm{data}}\!\left(y,G_\theta(z)\right) +\lambda_{\mathrm{feat}}d_{\mathrm{feat}}\!\left(y,G_\theta(z)\right) +\lambda_{\mathrm{prior}}R(z) \right]. \]

    Pixel, perceptual-feature, identity, segmentation, or frequency losses answer different similarity questions. The optimizer is a MAP procedure only if the complete objective is the negative log of a declared likelihood times prior, up to constants, and the relevant optimum is found. Calling an arbitrary perceptual objective “posterior inference” silently invents a likelihood and uncertainty model.

    A defensible optimization protocol uses several prior draws or encoder initializations, records every objective component, retains distinct near-optimal solutions, and reports steps, restarts, wall-clock, and stopping rules. Selecting only the best-looking restart hides ambiguity and compute.

    for restart k = 1, ..., K:
    initialize z_k from the declared prior or encoder
    optimize the declared data, feature, and prior terms
    retain z_k, every loss component, and the stopping diagnostic
cluster near-optimal outputs and report more than one when they differ materially

    Image2StyleGAN is an influential optimization-based StyleGAN embedding study. Its results are evidence for that architecture, latent parameterization, loss, and data protocol—not a universal inverse for GANs.

    Amortized, joint, and hybrid inference¤

    An amortized encoder learns \(E_\phi(y)\), or parameters of \(q_\phi(z\mid y)\), across a training distribution. It makes repeated inversion fast but introduces representation, generalization, and amortization errors. A hybrid method initializes with an encoder and refines the code, generator features, or selected weights for each target. Extra degrees of freedom can lower distortion while moving away from the states on which latent edits were learned.

    BiGAN (Donahue, Krähenbühl, and Darrell, 2017) and ALI (Dumoulin et al., 2017) instead learn generation and inference jointly. Their discriminator compares the two joint laws

    \[ q_\phi(x,z)=p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x), \qquad p_\theta(x,z)=p_Z(z)p_\theta(x\mid z). \]

    At ideal population optimum with a rich enough discriminator and successful optimization, equality of these joints implies matching marginals and \(q_\phi(z\mid x)=p_\theta(z\mid x)\) almost everywhere on the matched model/data law. This is a population-optimum correspondence, not an algebraic property of every trained network. It also learns a coupling between the data and prior; marginals alone do not choose a unique semantic coupling. ALICE analyzes this non-identifiability and adds conditional-entropy or cycle-style constraints to select more reconstructive couplings.

    A post-hoc encoder for a pretrained GAN and a jointly trained BiGAN answer different engineering questions. Adding an encoder after training does not retroactively give the original GAN likelihood semantics, while retraining a joint model can change both the generator and its learned representation.

    Reconstruction, realism, and editability pull in different directions¤

    Style-based generators expose several candidate inversion spaces: the input prior space, mapped style spaces, per-layer extended codes, intermediate features, residuals, and sometimes tuned generator parameters. Increasing the state dimension usually enlarges the set of reconstructible details, but an edit learned on ordinary prior samples need not remain meaningful in those enlarged regions.

    e4e makes the distortion–perception–editability distinction explicit. Later work continues to test the same boundary with constrained latents, feature states, spatial refinement, and frequency-aware residuals—for example Katsumata et al. (WACV 2024), StyleFeatureEditor (CVPR 2024), and WINE (WACV 2025). These are architecture- and protocol-specific system results. They do not establish that one latent space or loss universally optimizes all three properties.

    Four uncertainty sources¤

    Table GAN.7. Four uncertainty sources
    Source Question Appropriate evidence
    Inverse ambiguity Do several latents explain the same observation? Conditional samples, equivalence classes, or diverse near-optimal solutions
    Observation uncertainty How do noise, missing pixels, or a forward operator change compatibility? An explicit \(p_{\mathrm{obs}}(y\mid G(z))\) and sensitivity to its assumptions
    Computation uncertainty Did initialization or finite optimization select one basin? Multiple restarts, convergence traces, between-run variation, and stronger baselines
    Model uncertainty and misspecification Is the target outside the generator's learned range, or is the generator itself uncertain? Held-out and shifted-domain tests, residual structure, ensembles or parameter uncertainty when declared

    A deterministic encoder returns none of these uncertainties by itself. Multiple optimizer restarts reveal sensitivity and possible modes, but their empirical frequencies are not calibrated posterior probabilities unless the algorithm is designed and validated as a sampler.

    Evaluation protocol for inversion and editing¤

    Table GAN.8. Evaluation protocol for inversion and editing
    Property Minimum report Common category error
    Synthetic latent recovery Prior, generator, latent metric or equivalence relation, recovery distribution, and collision rate Scoring a symmetry-equivalent latent as wrong, or testing only easy prior draws
    Reconstruction distortion Pixel and task-relevant distortion with uncertainty over examples Treating low distortion as proof of realism or correct latent recovery
    Reconstruction realism A declared perceptual or distributional measure plus human inspection protocol where needed Calling a realistic but identity-changing output a faithful reconstruction
    Posterior quality Observation model, calibration or coverage test, posterior predictive checks, and query-specific errors Evaluating only a posterior mean or best reconstruction
    Editing Edit success versus magnitude, protected-content retention, locality, realism, attribute leakage, and subgroup results Reporting hand-selected edits or one direction at one strength
    Ambiguity and robustness Multiple solutions, in-domain and shifted targets, occlusion/corruption tests, and failure rates Hiding failed restarts or excluding hard examples after seeing results
    Compute Encoder and generator evaluations, optimization steps, restarts, memory, precision, hardware, and wall-clock Calling a hybrid method “one pass” because its initialization uses one encoder call

    Compare inversion methods using the same frozen generator, targets, edit operators, edit-strength grid, loss implementations, and compute budget. Distribution-level generator metrics remain necessary but do not replace paired inversion and editing measurements. See Generative Model Evaluation for estimator uncertainty, memorization, and human-evaluation design.

    Misconception check: inversion is not one query
    1. Can \(G(E(x))=x\) prove that \(E(x)\) recovered the latent that generated \(x\)?
    2. When is a reconstruction optimizer a MAP estimator?
    3. Does exact BiGAN joint matching identify a unique semantic coupling from the marginals alone?
    4. Why can a more expressive inversion space improve reconstruction but harm editing?
    5. Do frequencies from ordinary random restarts estimate posterior mode probabilities?

    Solutions. (1) No. A non-injective generator can have several preimages, as the finite square-map example proves. (2) Only when the complete objective is the negative log of a declared likelihood and prior, up to constants, and the relevant optimum is found. (3) No. It makes the learned encoder and generator joints agree, but several couplings can share the same marginals; additional structure selects among them. (4) The extra coordinates can encode target-specific detail outside the states on which the edit rule and prior were learned. (5) No. Restart frequencies depend on initialization, optimizer, geometry, and stopping; they need not carry calibrated probability mass.

    Bridge to evaluation. Once the requested inverse object is explicit, the next section asks how to measure it alongside generator fidelity and coverage. A reconstruction score cannot replace a posterior diagnostic, and neither replaces an editing intervention.

    Evaluation Metrics¤

    Inception Score (IS)¤

    Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016) measures quality and diversity using a pre-trained classifier.

    \[ \text{IS} = \exp\left(\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_g}[D_{KL}(p(y|x) \| p(y))]\right) \]
    • High IS: Generated images are clear (low entropy \(p(y|x)\)) and diverse (high entropy \(p(y)\))
    • Limitations: Doesn't compare to real data; can be gamed

    Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)¤

    FID (Heusel et al., 2017) measures similarity between real and generated distributions in InceptionV3 feature space.

    \[ \text{FID} = \|m_r - m_g\|_2^2 + \operatorname{Tr}\!\left(C_r+C_g-2(C_r^{1/2}C_gC_r^{1/2})^{1/2}\right) \]

    where \(m_r, m_g\) are feature means and \(C_r, C_g\) are covariance matrices.

    • Lower is better: population FID is zero when the fitted feature-space Gaussians have identical means and covariances. Distinct non-Gaussian feature distributions—and certainly distinct image distributions—can share those moments, so FID zero does not by itself prove identical distributions.
    • Unlike IS, it uses a real reference set, but remains feature-, preprocessing-, sample-size-, and implementation-dependent.
    • Widely reported, not sufficient alone: use the same feature extractor and code path, disclose sample counts, and pair it with coverage, memorization, and task-specific measures.

    Precision and Recall¤

    Sajjadi et al. (2018) and Kynkäänniemi et al. (2019) extend FID into separate quality vs. coverage metrics:

    Precision: An estimator-dependent approximation to how much generated feature mass lies near the reference manifold (a fidelity proxy).

    Recall: An estimator-dependent approximation to how much reference feature mass is covered by generated samples (a coverage proxy).

    Trade-off: A particular GAN checkpoint can improve a fidelity proxy while losing coverage—for example through truncation or collapse—but this must be measured rather than assigned to the family universally.

    Human Evaluation¤

    Human evaluation measures judgments that automated metrics may miss, but it is not an assumption-free gold standard. Results depend on prompts, annotator population, instructions, interface, presentation order, quality control, and statistical model:

    • Perceptual quality: Do samples look realistic?
    • Diversity: Is there sufficient variety?
    • Artifacts: Are there visible distortions or patterns?

    Metric Limitations

    • FID: Measures overall distribution quality but doesn't separate precision from recall.
    • IS: Assesses the generated distribution without comparing to real data.
    • PR curves: Separate quality from diversity but require more computation.
    • KID (Bińkowski et al., 2018): unbiased kernel-based alternative to FID, more reliable on small sample sets.
    • CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024): replaces InceptionV3 features with CLIP features and the Fréchet distance with MMD. The paper reports higher correlation with human judgments on its evaluated model and dataset collection; this is protocol evidence, not a universal metric ordering.

    Use multiple complementary measures plus visual inspection.

    Training Best Practices¤

    Development Workflow¤

    One defensible development workflow is:

    1. Start simple: DCGAN on MNIST to verify implementation
    2. Scale up: Move to CIFAR-10, CelebA for complexity
    3. Add stability: Implement WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, R1 GP as needed
    4. Monitor carefully: Track both metrics (FID, IS) and visual quality
    5. Save checkpoints: Training is unstable—save frequently
    6. Iterate: Experiment with architectural variations once baseline works
    7. Transfer learning: Use pretrained models when possible

    Historical starting points, not universal ranges¤

    DCGAN used Adam with learning rate \(2\times10^{-4}\) and \((\beta_1,\beta_2)=(0.5,0.999)\); WGAN used RMSProp plus weight clipping; WGAN-GP used Adam and commonly five critic updates; StyleGAN and modern ResNet recipes use still other settings. Reproduce a named baseline first, then tune player learning rates, optimizer moments, update ratio, regularization strength/frequency, effective batch size, and augmentation jointly. Larger batches can improve some estimators and batch statistics but can also change compute, regularization, and optimization; “larger is better” is not a law.

    Debugging Strategies¤

    Common Issues:

    Table GAN.9. Debugging Strategies
    Symptom Likely Cause Solution
    D logistic loss near 0 Strong separation; could be overfit, support mismatch, or an optimization imbalance Compare train/held-out critic scores and inspect generator gradient norms before intervening
    G loss stuck Saturation, optimizer failure, or a critic shortcut Check logits, ranges, gradients, and loss semantics; then test one targeted change
    Nearly identical outputs Mode collapse or deterministic conditioning/data bug Check latent use and pipeline first; then evaluate an anti-collapse intervention
    NaN losses Numerical, optimizer, mixed-precision, or data-range failure Locate the first nonfinite tensor; inspect loss stabilization and update scale
    Poor perceptual detail Data, generator, critic receptive field, objective, or checkpoint issue Inspect matched reconstructions/nearest neighbors and ablate the suspected bottleneck

    Stability Techniques¤

    R1 Gradient Penalty (Mescheder, Geiger & Nowozin, 2018):

    \[ R_1 = \frac{\gamma}{2} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_r}[\|\nabla_x D(x)\|^2] \]
    • Applied only to real samples. R2 is the analogous zero-centred penalty on generated samples. Either supports the local convergence result for the analyzed games under the paper's assumptions; neither is sufficient for arbitrary neural architectures and optimizers.
    • \(\gamma\) and its effective strength depend on data units, loss reduction, resolution, batch size, and lazy frequency. Report the full convention rather than transporting the number 10 blindly.
    • Lazy regularisation (Karras et al., 2020 — StyleGAN2): evaluate the penalty only on scheduled minibatches and rescale its contribution according to the declared interval. This amortizes the extra gradient computation; the actual time and memory saving depends on the implementation and is not fixed at one half.
    • R1/R2 are zero-centred gradient penalties. Mescheder et al. prove local convergence for the analyzed regularized games under explicit assumptions and give counterexamples for finite-update WGAN/WGAN-GP. Neither result is an architecture-independent promise.

    Spectral Normalization (Miyato et al., 2018):

    Normalise weight matrices by their spectral norm (largest singular value):

    \[ W_{SN} = \frac{W}{\sigma(W)} \]
    • Controls each normalized linear operator; a whole-network Lipschitz bound requires accounting for every layer and branch.
    • The common approximate implementation uses power iteration; its cost and accuracy depend on the operator and update scheme.
    • Works well with the standard non-saturating GAN loss; the basis of SAGAN and BigGAN.

    Label Smoothing (Salimans et al., 2016):

    Replace binary labels with smoothed versions:

    • Real labels: \(0.9\) instead of \(1.0\).
    • Fake labels: \(0.0\) (no smoothing).

    One-sided smoothing was proposed to reduce overconfident real-class predictions. It changes the density-ratio target, and its effect is objective- and data-dependent; evaluate calibration, gradients, fidelity, and coverage rather than assuming it prevents instability.

    Comparison with Other Generative Models¤

    GANs vs. VAEs¤

    Table GAN.10. GANs vs. VAEs
    Aspect GANs VAEs
    Sample Quality Can be sharp under adversarial objectives; fidelity and coverage are protocol-dependent Fixed-variance unimodal decoders can average ambiguity; richer likelihoods, hierarchical models, and adversarial/perceptual codecs can be sharp
    Training Stability Game dynamics can be sensitive to architecture, updates, and regularisation Single-objective optimisation is often easier to diagnose, but posterior collapse and likelihood/scale choices remain difficult
    Likelihood The adversarial objective needs samples, not a data likelihood; a typical deterministic neural sampler has no general tractable output-likelihood interface, although a separately explicit generator can be trained adversarially Defines an explicit latent-variable likelihood and commonly optimizes an ELBO; the ELBO is a lower bound, not the likelihood itself
    Mode Coverage Mode collapse is a known failure, not a universal outcome ELBO training does not guarantee semantic mode coverage; posterior and decoder misspecification matter
    Latent Space Geometry is learned implicitly and must be measured Explicit probabilistic latents support posterior queries, but structure and interpretability are not guaranteed
    Sampling computation Often one generator call, with architecture-, cascade-, renderer-, and codec-dependent cost Often one prior draw plus one decoder call, with architecture- and representation-dependent cost

    GANs vs. Diffusion Models¤

    Table GAN.11. GANs vs. Diffusion Models
    Aspect GANs Diffusion (un-distilled) Diffusion (distilled / ADD-style)
    Sample quality Protocol-dependent; strong on several image and domain-specific tasks Protocol-dependent; strong across many image/video benchmarks Can approach a named teacher at a selected one- to four-step operating point
    Sampling computation Often one direct generator execution, but a generator may contain cascades, renderers, or autoregressive submodules A declared number of solver transitions and denoiser evaluations; guidance branches and predictor–corrector stages can multiply work A declared number of student transitions and network evaluations, sometimes plus teacher-independent decoders or adversarially trained components
    Training Stability Game optimisation needs diagnostics and regularisation Denoising regression is often robust, not universally stable Adds teacher, student, and sometimes adversarial optimisation failure modes
    Mode Coverage Collapse can occur Iterative stochastic samplers often cover diverse modes, but objective and finite sampler can still miss them A student can lose teacher modes; coverage must be measured rather than assumed inherited
    Controllability Latent editing can be effective for suitable models Classifier, classifier-free, and posterior guidance offer broad controls Control retention is method- and protocol-dependent
    Text conditioning Demonstrated at scale, but coverage/alignment depends on model and data (Kang et al., 2023) Widely demonstrated with text encoders and cross-attention Retention of teacher alignment is method- and prompt-distribution-dependent

    Research landscape:

    • Diffusion and flow models with iterative samplers lead many high-quality image/video protocols, but step count, guidance, decoder, and model size vary.
    • Pure GANs remain attractive for some low-latency applications, interactive systems, and latent editing; dominance depends on quality, model size, decoder cost, batching, and hardware.
    • Adversarially distilled diffusion/flow models (for example ADD and DMD2) combine teacher or score signals with adversarial learning to reach few-step operating points. Other few-step methods omit an external discriminator, so “fast sampler” and “GAN” are not synonyms.

    Complementary Use Cases:

    • Consider a GAN when: a validated one-pass generator meets the domain's fidelity, coverage, latency, memory, and editing requirements, or a domain-specific adversarial architecture has unusually strong evidence.
    • Consider diffusion or flow models when: their conditioning, coverage, likelihood/path semantics, or available pretrained ecosystem wins under the target quality–latency budget.
    • Consider adversarial distillation when a pretrained diffusion or flow model must be moved to a measured few-evaluation operating point and the added teacher, discriminator, data, and stability costs are acceptable. Compare against non-adversarial students under the same quality and systems budget.

    On the speed advantage

    A direct GAN often uses one top-level generator call, but that call can contain a cascade, renderer, codec, or sequential subroutine. A distilled student can also use one or a few top-level transitions. Outer steps, branch-equivalent network evaluations, launched calls, auxiliary calls, and end-to-end latency are separate quantities. Compare measured wall-clock, memory, and achieved quality at the same operating point rather than assuming a universal family-level speed multiplier.

    Worked example: FID can miss a distributional error¤

    In one feature dimension, let the real feature distribution be \(r=(-1,+1)\) with equal probability and the generated distribution be \(g=(-\sqrt2,0,+\sqrt2)\) with probabilities \((1/4,1/2,1/4)\). Both have mean zero and variance one, so the population Gaussian-moment FID is zero. The distributions are nevertheless different: the generator puts half its mass at a feature value the real distribution never takes. KID or a suitable two-sample test can detect higher-order differences, while a precision/recall estimator can expose support geometry. The example is deliberately simple: it proves that FID is not an identity test.

    Independent practice and assessment map: representations and evaluation¤

    Begin with the requested inverse or evaluation object. Do not let a good reconstruction, attractive edit, or single distributional score substitute for latent recovery, posterior inference, coverage, or uncertainty.

    P1 — Separate inversion queries and build a multi-axis evaluation¤

    Let \(Z\) be uniform on \(\{-1,0,+1\}\), \(G(z)=z^2\), and first observe \(X\) without noise. Then consider real targets outside the generator range and a continuous image generator evaluated through fixed features.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether exact reconstruction identifies the generating latent, whether the lowest-distortion extended code is necessarily the most editable, whether zero FID proves equal feature laws, and whether random-restart frequencies are posterior probabilities. Name the missing injectivity, representation, metric, or sampling condition in each case.
    2. P1.2 — Finite inversion calculation. Compute \(P(Z\mid X=0)\) and \(P(Z\mid X=1)\), the best deterministic latent-recovery rate, and the reconstructions from an encoder choosing \(E(1)=+1\). For target \(y=1/2\), enumerate all squared-error optima and classify the result as recovery, reconstruction/projection, posterior inference, or editing representation.
    3. P1.3 — Inference-route design. For per-example optimization, an amortized encoder, BiGAN/ALI joint matching, and an encoder-plus-refinement hybrid, specify the learned/optimized law or point, compute cost, uncertainty returned, approximation/generalization failure, and tests for ambiguity. State the conditions under which a reconstruction objective is a MAP objective.
    4. P1.4 — Metric calculation and falsification. Recompute the one-dimensional zero-FID counterexample from its means and variances. Choose a characteristic-kernel statistic and a precision/recall-style estimator that could expose the discrepancy, define finite-sample uncertainty, and add memorization and conditional-alignment tests that none of those feature metrics supplies.
    5. P1.5 — Matched family comparison. Design a GAN–VAE–diffusion comparison at equal served quality and separately at equal training compute. Report likelihood/bounds where defined, fidelity, coverage, inversion/editing, model and decoder calls, guidance, hardware, precision, memory, latency distribution, seeds, and selection rules.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Apply inversion and editing to a scientific microscopy generator with non-injective symmetries and a noisy forward operator. Define latent equivalence classes, observation likelihood, posterior-calibration test, protected biological content, edit intervention, subgroup/shift analysis, and the evidence needed before calling a direction mechanistic.

    Comparison with Other Generative Models: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Latent recovery, reconstruction, posterior inference, editability, and distributional quality are different empirical targets. No single metric covers them together with fidelity, diversity, conditional alignment, memorization, human preference, and deployment cost, so comparisons require a declared query, metric vector, and operating point.
