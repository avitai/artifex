    4. Gradient Estimators for Latent, Discrete, and Multi-Sample Models¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Conditional expectation, importance sampling, stochastic computation graphs, and the score-function and pathwise identities.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive estimator identities: obtain score-function, pathwise, and weak/measure-valued representations with their regularity conditions.
    2. O2 — Preserve a target: verify exact marginalization, Rao--Blackwellization, coupling, and control-variate zero-mean conditions.
    3. O3 — Diagnose surrogate bias: distinguish unbiased estimators from relaxed objectives and custom straight-through derivatives.
    4. O4 — Audit multi-sample learning: separate bound tightness, estimator signal, and generative-parameter versus inference-parameter behavior.
    5. O5 — Trace stochastic graphs: assign local score/pathwise terms, baselines, stopped derivatives, and higher-order corrections.
    6. O6 — Select and validate an estimator: use exact small cases, bias, variance, signal-to-noise, and compute rather than estimator names alone.

    One derivative, several estimator families¤

    For

    \[ J(\phi)=\mathbb E_{Z\sim q_\phi}[f_\phi(Z)], \]

    the likelihood-ratio identity is

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E_{q_\phi} \left[ f_\phi(Z)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(Z) +\nabla_\phi f_\phi(Z) \right]. \]

    If \(Z=T_\phi(\varepsilon)\) for parameter-free noise, the pathwise identity is

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E \left[ \nabla_z f_\phi(Z)\,\nabla_\phi T_\phi(\varepsilon) +\nabla_\phi f_\phi(Z) \right]. \]

    Under their regularity conditions, these are two representations of the same population derivative. Their Monte Carlo variance can differ greatly. Exact marginalization, Rao--Blackwellization, antithetic coupling, common random numbers, and control variates reduce randomness without defining a new objective. A continuous relaxation, straight-through rule, or truncated stochastic graph may instead change the differentiated target.

    The third classical family is a weak or measure-valued derivative. It represents a derivative of the sampling law as a signed measure, often as a scaled difference between two probability laws. For the smallest exact case, \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(q)\) and a function \(f\) that does not otherwise depend on \(q\),

    \[ \frac{d}{dq}\mathbb E_q[f(Z)] =f(1)-f(0). \]

    If \(q=q_\phi\), the parameter derivative is \((f(1)-f(0))\,dq_\phi/d\phi\). This is neither a score sample nor a continuous relaxation: it is an exact signed-measure difference. In larger systems a weak derivative may require paired perturbation laws, coupling, and two or more function evaluations. Those costs and the validity of the signed-measure decomposition are part of the estimator contract. The Monte Carlo gradient survey develops score-function, pathwise, and measure-valued estimators in one common framework.

    Worked Bernoulli estimator: exact, score-function, and baseline¤

    Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(q)\) with \(q=\sigma(\phi)\), and let \(f(0)=0.2\), \(f(1)=1.4\). Exact summation gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} J(\phi)&=0.2+1.2q,\\ \frac{dJ}{d\phi}&=1.2q(1-q). \end{aligned} \]

    At \(\phi=0\), \(q=1/2\) and the exact derivative is \(0.3\). Since \(\partial_\phi\log q_\phi(Z)=Z-q\), a one-sample score estimator is

    \[ \widehat g=f(Z)(Z-q). \]

    It equals \(0.7\) when \(Z=1\) and \(-0.1\) when \(Z=0\), so its expectation is \(0.3\) but it has sampling variance. Subtracting a baseline that does not depend on the sampled value preserves the expectation:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat g_b&=(f(Z)-b)(Z-q),\\ \mathbb E[b(Z-q)]&=0. \end{aligned} \]

    Here \(b=0.8\) makes both outcomes equal \(0.3\), eliminating variance in this tiny problem. In a stochastic computation graph, a learned baseline may depend on parents of \(Z\) but must not receive an unintended derivative through the score-surrogate term. Detachment is part of the estimator definition.

    Exact enumeration is preferable when the support is small. For a large structured latent space, sum out any tractable subset first and sample only the remainder. This is Rao--Blackwellization: it is a mathematical variance reduction, not merely a larger batch.

    Discrete variables: an estimator decision table¤

    Table GRAD.2. Discrete variables: an estimator decision table
    Construction Target semantics Main qualification
    Exact sum or dynamic program exact discrete objective support or graph structure must permit marginalization
    Score function with baseline unbiased under support and integrability assumptions often high variance; baseline cannot depend improperly on the sampled child
    VIMCO or leave-one-out multi-sample control variate unbiased gradient of the declared multi-sample bound control variate and particle dependence must match the derivation
    REBAR or RELAX unbiased discrete gradient using conditional relaxations as control variates requires a coupled relaxation and correct correction terms
    ARM, DisARM, and categorical coupling variants unbiased estimators using augmentation, coupling, or antithetic samples applicability depends on binary/categorical structure and coupling
    Concrete or Gumbel--Softmax pathwise gradient exact gradient of a continuous relaxed objective biased for the original discrete objective at finite temperature
    Straight-through rule custom backward surrogate generally biased and need not be the gradient of any scalar objective
    Weak or measure-valued derivative signed-measure or coupled perturbation representation may require multiple function evaluations and a problem-specific decomposition

    RELAX, DisARM, and coupled categorical estimators show that “unbiased for a discrete latent” still describes several algorithms with different assumptions and variance. More recent work combines numerical straight-through constructions with Rao--Blackwellization and learned control variates (Wang and Bui). These are peer-reviewed estimator results on their stated tasks, not a universal ordering.

    Multi-sample bounds couple objective and estimator¤

    For importance weights

    \[ w_k=\frac{p_\theta(x,z_k)}{q_\phi(z_k\mid x)}, \qquad z_k\sim q_\phi(\cdot\mid x), \]

    the importance-weighted bound is

    \[ \mathcal L_K =\mathbb E \left[ \log\left(\frac1K\sum_{k=1}^K w_k\right) \right]. \]

    Increasing \(K\) changes the objective and the dependence among normalized weights. A tighter bound need not produce a higher-signal inference-network gradient: the signal-to-noise ratio of the usual reparameterized estimator can deteriorate even while the generative-parameter estimate improves (Rainforth et al.).

    Doubly reparameterized gradients analytically transform remaining score terms and yield a lower-variance unbiased inference-network estimator for the supported continuous multi-sample objectives (Tucker et al.). VIMCO uses sample-specific leave-one-out control variates when latents are discrete. Reweighted wake--sleep uses a different wake or sleep update. These methods may share samples and weights while optimizing different population quantities.

    Tighter bound, better estimator, and better model are separate claims

    Bound tightness concerns the scalar population objective. Bias and variance concern a derivative estimator. Generative quality concerns the learned solution and finite optimizer. None follows from either of the others without additional analysis.

    Hierarchies and stochastic computation graphs¤

    For a hierarchy

    \[ q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x) =\prod_{\ell=1}^L q_\phi(z_\ell\mid z_{<\ell},x), \]

    each stochastic node has its own score term and downstream cost. A baseline for one node may condition on variables that precede it but not on an uncorrected realization of that node. Local expectation gradients, critics, and conditional Rao--Blackwellization assign credit to smaller subgraphs. Generalized DReG derivations show that apparently pathwise hierarchical objectives can retain score terms that require separate treatment (Bauer and Mnih).

    Repeated automatic differentiation of an ordinary first-order score surrogate can omit higher-order terms because detachment was chosen to reproduce only the first derivative. DiCE constructs a stochastic objective whose repeated differentiation produces the intended higher-order score estimators (Foerster et al.). This is relevant to meta-inference, learned inner optimizers, and games in which one player differentiates through another player's stochastic update.

    Estimator audit¤

    For every stochastic gradient, record:

    1. the exact population objective and parameter block;
    2. all sampled nodes and their conditional laws;
    3. whether each contribution is pathwise, score-function, marginalized, relaxed, or manually substituted;
    4. the control-variate expectation that is claimed to vanish;
    5. every stopped derivative and whether a correction term restores the target;
    6. particle count, coupling, common-random-number, and resampling scheme;
    7. empirical bias against exact small cases when available; and
    8. variance, signal-to-noise ratio, wall-clock, and final generative metrics.

    Independent practice and assessment map: stochastic gradient estimators¤

    Each task keeps the population target fixed until it explicitly declares a new one. Calculate the exact finite case before comparing Monte Carlo formulas.

    1. P1.1 — Recover the zero-variance baseline. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. In the worked Bernoulli problem at \(q=1/2\), derive the constant baseline minimizing the one-sample score-estimator second moment and verify both sampled outcomes equal the exact derivative.
    2. P1.2 — Compare three exact representations. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. With \(q=\sigma(\phi)=0.3\), \(f(0)=0.2\), and \(f(1)=1.4\), compute \(dJ/d\phi\) by enumeration, by expected score estimator, and by the weak derivative. Confirm all three agree.
    3. P1.3 — Predict before computing: diagnose a Gumbel--Softmax claim. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. A paper replaces a categorical sample by a finite-temperature Gumbel--Softmax variable and calls the result “an unbiased gradient for the discrete objective.” Predict which target is differentiated, then specify the limiting and numerical questions needed to support any discrete-target claim.
    4. P1.4 — Separate IWAE bound and inference signal. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. Increasing particles from \(K=1\) to \(K=64\) tightens a reported bound while the inference-network gradient signal-to-noise falls. Explain why there is no contradiction and name a relevant estimator redesign.
    5. P1.5 — Place baselines in a hierarchy. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. For \(q(z_1\mid x)q(z_2\mid z_1,x)\) and a downstream cost, state what a baseline for the \(z_2\) score term may depend on, what it must not use without correction, and where detachment belongs.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer: design an estimator audit. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. A model has a 20-category latent, a differentiable decoder, and a four-sample bound. Propose exact or sampled estimators for decoder and inference parameters, a control-variate strategy, exact-small-case validation, and matched-compute measurements.

    Table GRAD.16. Outcome-to-task alignment: stochastic gradient estimators

    O1 — Derive estimator identities P1.1, P1.2
    O2 — Preserve a target P1.1, P1.2, P1.5

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The variance-minimizing constant is \(b^*=\mathbb E[f(Z)(Z-q)^2]/\mathbb E[(Z-q)^2]\). At \(q=1/2\), \((Z-q)^2=1/4\) for both outcomes, so \(b^*=[f(0)+f(1)]/2=0.8\). Then \((1.4-0.8)(1/2)=0.3\) and \((0.2-0.8)(-1/2)=0.3\), equal to the exact derivative. Check. Zero variance here relies on the two-point symmetry and does not generalize automatically.
    2. P1.2. Enumeration gives \(J=0.2+1.2q\) and \(dJ/d\phi=1.2q(1-q)=1.2(0.21)=0.252\). The score outcomes are \(1.4(0.7)=0.98\) and \(0.2(-0.3)=-0.06\), whose expectation is \(0.3(0.98)+0.7(-0.06)=0.252\). The weak derivative is \((1.4-0.2)(0.21)=0.252\). Check. The sample formulas differ; the population derivative does not.
    3. P1.3. At finite temperature, reparameterized AD differentiates the expectation of the decoder evaluated on the continuous relaxed variable. It is not generally unbiased for the hard categorical objective. A discrete-target claim needs a proof of the zero-temperature limit and interchange of limit and derivative, plus empirical bias against enumeration, gradient variance, underflow or saturation, and downstream hard-sample evaluation over temperatures. Check. Taking a small temperature is not itself a proof.
    4. P1.4. Larger \(K\) changes \(\mathcal L_K\) and can tighten it while normalized importance weights make the usual inference-network gradient's mean shrink relative to its variance. Generative-parameter and inference-parameter estimators have different signal behavior. DReG is one redesign for supported reparameterizable objectives; VIMCO serves discrete multi-sample settings. Check. Better scalar bounds do not order finite gradient estimators.
    5. P1.5. A \(z_2\) baseline may depend on \(x\), \(z_1\), and other variables upstream of the \(z_2\) draw. It must not use the realized \(z_2\) or a downstream descendant unless a correction preserves zero expectation. In a score surrogate, detach the baseline and sampled-cost multiplier as required by the derivation so AD does not invent extra paths. Check. A separately trained critic can still receive its own regression gradient.
    6. P1.6. Since the latent has only 20 states, first test exact marginalization; the decoder gradient can sum state-weighted differentiable losses exactly, and the inference gradient can differentiate the exact four-sample objective only if its combinatorics remain affordable or otherwise use VIMCO/RELAX-like corrections matched to the discrete law. Validate every sampled estimator on a one-record enumerated fixture, then report bias, variance, SNR by parameter block, coupling and particle count, wall-clock, memory, and held-out likelihood/sample metrics at matched compute. Rubric. A complete design does not use one estimator label for both parameter blocks.

    Chapter 4 synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Gradient estimators are representations or approximations of derivatives, not geometries. Exact summation and Rao--Blackwellization remove avoidable randomness; score and pathwise identities can target the same derivative; control variates preserve a target only when their expected contribution is zero; relaxations and straight-through rules generally change the target; and multi-sample objectives make estimator signal a separate design problem.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why does the Bernoulli baseline leave the expected gradient unchanged?
    2. What does a finite-temperature Gumbel--Softmax gradient differentiate?
    3. Why can increasing the number of IWAE particles hurt inference learning?
    4. When is exact marginalization preferable to a sophisticated estimator?

    Solutions. (1) The parameter score has zero expectation, so a sample-independent baseline contributes zero. (2) The continuous relaxed objective, not generally the original discrete objective. (3) Bound tightness and inference-gradient signal-to-noise are different quantities; the signal can shrink faster than estimator noise. (4) Whenever the needed sum or structured dynamic program is tractable, because it removes Monte Carlo variance without estimator bias.

    Bridge. Once the intended parameter gradient has been estimated, Chapter 5 asks how to transform it with a large statistical metric without confusing curvature approximation with gradient-estimator error.
