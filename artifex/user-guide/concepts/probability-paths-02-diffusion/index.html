    2. Score Diffusion and Probability-Flow ODEs¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and score-based diffusion fundamentals. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive reverse dynamics: obtain the reverse-time score correction while keeping the time convention explicit.
    2. O2 — Derive probability flow: rewrite Fokker--Planck evolution as a deterministic current velocity.
    3. O3 — State the equivalence boundary: identify exactly which marginals the exact reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE share and which path objects they do not.
    4. O4 — Recover a marginal score: use posterior averaging of conditional corruption scores and distinguish sample targets from population optima.
    5. O5 — Audit likelihood and finite error: separate score, endpoint, divergence, solver, and preprocessing errors.
    6. O6 — Transfer the audit: design a controlled ODE-versus-SDE comparison with matched probability target and declared compute.

    Consider a forward diffusion

    \[ dX_t=f_t(X_t)\,dt+g_t\,dW_t \]

    with marginals \(p_t\). Under regularity, its reverse-time SDE has drift

    \[ f_t(x)-g_t^2\nabla_x\log p_t(x), \]

    when integrated from terminal time toward zero with the reverse-time convention. Sign mistakes arise if negative \(dt\) and reverse Brownian notation are suppressed.

    The probability-flow ODE is

    \[ \frac{dX_t}{dt} =f_t(X_t)-\frac12g_t^2\nabla_x\log p_t(X_t). \]

    Score-SDE theory shows that the ODE and SDE have the same one-time marginals when the exact score and regularity assumptions hold. This is distributional/dynamical equivalence under assumptions, not pathwise equality:

    • the ODE is deterministic conditional on its initial state;
    • the reverse SDE injects noise;
    • transition laws and couplings across times differ;
    • finite score error and numerical solvers perturb the equivalence.

    Time orientation without sign ambiguity¤

    There are two safe conventions:

    • retain the original coordinate \(t\) and integrate from \(T\) down to \(0\), so the numerical step \(\Delta t\) is negative; or
    • introduce forward-running reverse time \(\tau=T-t\) and transform every drift, diffusion coefficient, and score argument.

    Do not copy a reverse drift written in the first convention into a solver using the second. A unit test should verify the mean and variance evolution for an analytic Gaussian path before any learned score is involved.

    Conditional versus marginal scores¤

    For a corruption kernel \(q_t(x_t\mid x_0)\),

    \[ \mathbb E[\nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(X_t\mid X_0)\mid X_t=x] =\nabla_x\log p_t(x). \]

    Denoising regression therefore targets the marginal score at population optimum. A particular conditional target is not itself the marginal score before averaging over the clean posterior.

    For a Gaussian corruption \(X_t=\alpha_tX_0+\sigma_t\epsilon\),

    \[ \nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(x_t\mid x_0) =-\frac{x_t-\alpha_tx_0}{\sigma_t^2} =-\frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t}. \]

    An \(x_0\) predictor, noise predictor, score predictor, and velocity predictor can be converted by path-dependent linear formulas when the required coefficients are nonzero. This is algebraic equivalence for the chosen Gaussian path. Loss weighting, endpoint singularities, architecture, optimization, and solver behavior are not made equal by the conversion.

    Likelihood through the ODE¤

    For an ODE flow,

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(X_t)=-\nabla\cdot v_t(X_t). \]

    Integrating divergence can estimate likelihood. Hutchinson trace estimation, adaptive integration, score error, and endpoint dequantization affect the result. An ODE existing in theory does not make likelihood cheap.

    For a sample transported from time \(0\) to \(T\),

    \[ \log p_0(X_0) =\log p_T(X_T)+\int_0^T\nabla\cdot v_t(X_t)\,dt, \]

    with the sign determined by the direction in which the trajectory and density are integrated. In high dimensions, a Hutchinson estimator replaces the trace of \(\nabla v\) by \(\epsilon^\top(\nabla v)\epsilon\). Its random probes add variance; reusing or resampling probes changes estimator dependence; and the ODE state and log-density integral share numerical error.

    Worked Gaussian path: shared marginals, different finite algorithms¤

    Reuse the scalar construction from Diffusion Models Explained:

    \[ X_0\sim\mathcal N(1,4), \qquad dX_t=-\frac12X_t\,dt+dW_t. \]

    Its marginal law and score are

    \[ p_t=\mathcal N\!\left(e^{-t/2},1+3e^{-t}\right), \qquad s_t(x)=-\frac{x-e^{-t/2}}{1+3e^{-t}}. \]

    At \(t_\star=-\log 0.4\), the reverse-SDE drift \(r_t(x)=-x/2-s_t(x)\) and probability-flow velocity \(u_t(x)=-x/2-s_t(x)/2\) are different. Exact integration still gives the same one-time marginals: for the ODE,

    \[ \mathbb E[u_t(X_t)]=-\frac12\mathbb E[X_t], \qquad \frac{d}{dt}\operatorname{Var}(X_t) =2\left(-\frac12+\frac{1}{2\operatorname{Var}(X_t)}\right) \operatorname{Var}(X_t) =1-\operatorname{Var}(X_t). \]

    Those are precisely the mean and variance equations of \(p_t\). But one backward step across the whole interval, even with the exact score and exact starting marginal, gives

    Table PATH.1. Worked Gaussian path: shared marginals, different finite algorithms
    Finite algorithm Resulting law at time zero Equals \(\mathcal N(1,4)\)?
    One Euler step for the probability-flow ODE \(\mathcal N(0.922212,3.436936)\) No
    One Euler--Maruyama step for the reverse SDE \(\mathcal N(0.922212,3.303365)\) No

    The example isolates discretization error: there is no learned-score error and no terminal-initialization error. It also shows why “same continuous marginals” is not “same finite-step sampler.” Exact Gaussian analysis separates initialization, truncation, discretization, and score-approximation errors; 2026 error analysis continues to keep finite-time, schedule, discretization, and statistical terms explicit.

    Do not transfer an equivalence across levels

    A path identity does not certify a solver, and a population score identity does not certify a finite network. Record the forward path, score parameterization, endpoint law, numerical method, mesh or tolerances, and score-evaluation count before comparing two implementations.

    A five-layer diffusion error ledger¤

    Table PATH.7. A five-layer diffusion error ledger
    Layer Example Oracle or diagnostic
    endpoint approximation \(p_T\) replaced by a standard Gaussian at finite \(T\) analytic or high-accuracy terminal discrepancy
    statistical score error \(s_\theta\neq\nabla\log p_t\) held-out denoising risk; synthetic exact score
    temporal truncation avoid singular \(t=0\) by stopping at \(\varepsilon\) sweep \(\varepsilon\) separately from step size
    numerical integration Euler, Heun, adaptive ODE/SDE error convergence against a high-accuracy reference
    likelihood estimation stochastic divergence and dequantization probe count, seeds, estimator SE, preprocessing contract

    Changing guidance or conditioning creates an additional model/path-change layer. It should not be hidden inside the score-estimation row.

    Misconception checks: diffusion and probability flow¤

    • “Same marginals means the ODE is a denoised SDE trajectory.” No. The deterministic and stochastic transition laws differ.
    • “An exact score makes Euler exact.” No. It removes score error, not integration error.
    • “A conditional noise label is the score.” One label is noisy conditional supervision; its posterior mean is the marginal target.
    • “Probability-flow likelihood is exact because change of variables is exact.” Only exact integration with an exact field and exact divergence inherits the continuous identity.

    Independent practice and assessment map: diffusion and probability flow¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: drift ordering. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. In the worked Gaussian path at \(t_\star=-\log0.4\) and \(x=0.5\), predict whether the reverse-SDE drift or probability-flow velocity is more negative. Compute the marginal mean, variance, score, and both drifts.
    2. P1.2 — Average a conditional score. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. Let \(X_0\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(X_t=0.8X_0+0.6\epsilon\). At \(X_t=1\), derive \(\mathbb E[X_0\mid X_t]\), average the conditional corruption score, and verify the marginal score.
    3. P1.3 — Separate stationary dynamics. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For \(dX=-Xdt/2+dW\) initialized at \(\mathcal N(0,1)\), derive the score, reverse drift in the original time coordinate, and probability-flow velocity. State which of the six objects agree.
    4. P1.4 — Close a likelihood calculation. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For \(\dot X_t=2tX_t\) on \([0,1]\), solve the flow, integrate its divergence, and verify the one-dimensional change-of-variables formula between \(X_0\) and \(X_1\).
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose the one-step Gaussian result. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. Using the displayed one-step table, classify the endpoint, score, and discretization errors; compute the absolute mean error and variance error for each method relative to \(\mathcal N(1,4)\).
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to an ODE/SDE benchmark. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design a comparison that can attribute differences to score error, terminal approximation, discretization, stochastic variance, and divergence estimation. Specify analytic and learned-score phases plus compute and uncertainty reporting.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The mean is \(\sqrt{0.4}=0.632456\), variance is \(1+3(0.4)=2.2\), and \(s(0.5)=-(0.5-0.632456)/2.2=0.060207\). Thus the reverse drift is \(-0.25-s=-0.310207\) and the probability-flow velocity is \(-0.25-s/2=-0.280104\); the reverse drift is more negative. Check. Their difference is exactly \(-s/2\).
    2. P1.2. Because \(0.8^2+0.6^2=1\), \(X_t\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(\mathbb E[X_0\mid X_t=1]=0.8\). The averaged conditional score is \(-[1-0.8(0.8)]/0.6^2=-0.36/0.36=-1\), equal to \(\nabla\log\mathcal N(0,1)\) at one. Check. A single \(X_0\) target need not equal this posterior mean.
    3. P1.3. \(s=-x\). The reverse drift written in original \(t\) is \(-x/2-(-x)=x/2\) and the probability-flow velocity is \(-x/2-(-x)/2=0\). Both exact dynamics retain the stationary one-time law; one has reverse stochastic transitions and the other fixed deterministic particles. Their endpoints and marginal path agree, but path law, field, randomness, and finite solver do not. Check. The apparent outward reverse drift is integrated with decreasing \(t\).
    4. P1.4. Integration gives \(X_t=e^{t^2}X_0\), hence \(X_1=eX_0\). The divergence is \(2t\), whose integral is one. Therefore \(\log p_1(X_1)=\log p_0(X_0)-1\), exactly the Jacobian formula for scale \(e\). Check. The density integrates to one after the \(e^{-1}\) factor.
    5. P1.5. Terminal initialization and score are exact by construction, so their errors are zero. Both mean errors are \(1-0.922212=0.077788\). ODE variance error is \(4-3.436936=0.563064\); SDE variance error is \(4-3.303365=0.696635\). These are method-specific one-step discretization errors, not evidence against the continuous marginal theorem. Check. Equal mean errors do not imply equal output laws.
    6. P1.6. First use a Gaussian or mixture path with exact score and high-accuracy reference integration; sweep terminal time, truncation, step size, solver, and random seeds independently. Then freeze a learned score and repeat the solver sweep, followed by model-capacity or data sweeps at a fixed solver. Report endpoint discrepancy, score risk, NFE and solver stages, wall time, memory, stochastic confidence intervals, divergence-probe SE, and hardware. Rubric. Full credit makes each reported gap attributable to one controlled layer.
    Retrieval practice: diffusion and probability flow
    1. What does the exact probability-flow ODE share with the reverse SDE, and what does it not share?
    2. Why does averaging a conditional corruption score given \(X_t=x\) recover the marginal score?
    3. Which extra quantity must be integrated to obtain likelihood along the ODE?
    4. Name two finite-computation errors that can break an exact-score correspondence.

    Solutions. (1) They share one-time marginals under the stated regularity and exact-score assumptions, not sample paths, transition laws, or stochastic couplings. (2) Fisher's identity moves the conditional score through the posterior expectation. (3) The divergence of the probability-flow velocity. (4) Examples include learned-score error, time discretization, adaptive-solver tolerance, stochastic trace error, and endpoint/dequantization error.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Scores convert a known diffusion path into reverse stochastic and deterministic dynamics with shared marginals. The equivalence is exact only before approximation. Chapter 3 shows how flow matching learns a velocity directly from a chosen conditional path.
