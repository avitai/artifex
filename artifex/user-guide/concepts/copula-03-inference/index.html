    3. Estimation, Conditional Queries, and Diagnostics¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–2, maximum likelihood, empirical CDFs, and conditional densities. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Distinguish estimation regimes: compare full likelihood, IFM, and rank pseudo-likelihood by target, assumptions, and optimization coupling.
    2. O2 — Propagate two-stage uncertainty: identify which marginal, copula, resampling, and structure-selection stages must be repeated for the reported claim.
    3. O3 — Derive conditional queries: obtain conditional CDFs and inverse-condition samplers from copula derivatives under the correct observation regime.
    4. O4 — Construct and audit Rosenblatt residuals: check both marginal uniformity and mutual independence while tracking coordinate order.
    5. O5 — Use density-ratio objectives correctly: recover a copula ratio under declared class priors and separate finite classifier calibration from normalization and sampling.
    6. O6 — Transfer inference to incomplete observations: write likelihood contributions for missing, censored, interval, or noisy coordinates without replacing regions by points.

    Three estimation regimes¤

    Let marginal parameters be \(\alpha=(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_d)\) and copula parameters be \(\theta\). For continuous observations \(x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(n)}\), the full log likelihood is

    \[ \ell(\alpha,\theta) =\sum_{r=1}^n\left[ \log c_\theta\!\left(F_{1,\alpha_1}(x_1^{(r)}),\ldots,F_{d,\alpha_d}(x_d^{(r)})\right) +\sum_{j=1}^d\log f_{j,\alpha_j}(x_j^{(r)}) \right]. \]

    Three common workflows make different trade-offs:

    1. Full maximum likelihood jointly estimates \(\alpha\) and \(\theta\). It can be efficient under correct specification but couples optimization and model error.
    2. Inference functions for margins (IFM) fits each marginal first, then fits \(\theta\) to the resulting transforms. It is computationally modular, not identical to joint MLE.
    3. Rank or pseudo-likelihood estimation uses pseudo-observations such as \(\hat u_j^{(r)}=R_j^{(r)}/(n+1)\) and estimates dependence semiparametrically. Dividing by \(n+1\) keeps points away from singular boundaries; other conventions have different finite-sample effects.

    Standard errors must reflect the selected regime. Treating estimated pseudo-observations as known IID uniforms generally understates uncertainty. Bootstrap procedures must refit the stages whose uncertainty is being claimed.

    Conditional laws from copula derivatives¤

    For a continuously differentiable bivariate copula,

    \[ h_{2\mid1}(v\mid u) =\Pr(U_2\le v\mid U_1=u) =\frac{\partial C(u,v)}{\partial u}. \]

    If \(h_{2\mid1}(\cdot\mid u)\) is invertible, conditional simulation uses

    \[ W\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1), \qquad U_2=h_{2\mid1}^{-1}(W\mid u). \]

    For observed-scale conditioning \(X_1=x\), set \(u=F_1(x)\) and finally \(X_2=F_2^{-1}(U_2)\). Conditioning on an interval, atom, censored value, or noisy observation requires integrating the appropriate likelihood; substituting a point derivative can be invalid.

    The Rosenblatt transform¤

    For an ordered continuous vector with evaluable conditional CDFs, define

    \[ Z_1=F_1(X_1), \qquad Z_j=F_{j\mid1:j-1}(X_j\mid X_{1:j-1}), \quad j=2,\ldots,d. \]

    Under the correct joint model and regularity conditions, \(Z_1,\ldots,Z_d\) are independent uniforms. Applying \(\Phi^{-1}\) yields independent standard normals. This gives a multivariate residual diagnostic and a constructive sampler through the inverse triangular map.

    The transform depends on coordinate order. Correct models yield independent uniforms for every valid order, but finite estimates and misspecification can make diagnostics order-sensitive. Testing only the one-dimensional histograms misses residual dependence.

    Density-ratio estimation¤

    On the copula scale, the independent reference density is one. Hence

    \[ c(u)=\frac{p_U(u)}{\prod_j p_{U_j}(u_j)}=p_U(u). \]

    Generate negative examples by independently permuting coordinates or sampling from the product of fitted marginals. With equal class priors, the Bayes-optimal probability \(h^*(u)=\Pr(Y=1\mid u)\) of labeling a point as dependent satisfies

    \[ \boxed{c(u)=\frac{h^*(u)}{1-h^*(u)}.} \]

    This is a population-optimum correspondence. A finite classifier can be miscalibrated, class priors change the ratio formula, negative sampling can leak dependence, and an estimated ratio need not integrate to one or have uniform marginal integrals. Density evaluation and sampling from the ratio are separate computational problems.

    Missingness, censoring, and partial observations¤

    If \(O\) indexes observed coordinates and \(M\) missing coordinates, the observed-data likelihood is

    \[ p(x_O)=\int p(x_O,x_M)\,dx_M. \]

    For a continuous copula model, this becomes a lower-dimensional marginal copula contribution after integrating the missing coordinates. Censoring contributes CDF differences or derivatives according to the observation region. The missingness mechanism remains a separate assumption: integrating unobserved values is valid for the declared observation model, not proof that informative missingness can be ignored.

    Complete worked example: condition an FGM copula¤

    For

    \[ C_\theta(u,v)=uv[1+\theta(1-u)(1-v)], \]

    differentiate with respect to \(u\):

    \[ h_{2\mid1}(v\mid u) =v\left[1+\theta(1-v)(1-2u)\right]. \]

    Take \(\theta=1\) and \(u=0.8\). Then

    \[ h(v\mid0.8)=v[1-0.6(1-v)]=0.4v+0.6v^2. \]

    To obtain the conditional median, solve \(0.4v+0.6v^2=0.5\):

    \[ v=\frac{-0.4+\sqrt{0.16+1.2}}{1.2} \approx0.6385. \]

    The conditional copula-scale median exceeds \(0.5\), consistent with positive association given a high \(u\). If \(X_2\) has exponential marginal \(F_2^{-1}(v)=-\log(1-v)\), the observed-scale median is approximately \(1.018\). The conditional quantile needs both the copula and the marginal.

    One dataset, three fitted objects, four audits¤

    The modular algorithm does not make the uncertainties modular. A confidence interval for the final query must reproduce the fitted stages, row dependence, and selection operations that contribute to that query.

    Independent practice and assessment map: copula inference¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: boundary failure. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 6 min. For ranks \(1,2,3,4\), compare pseudo-observations \(R/(n+1)\) with \(R/n\). Predict which convention can evaluate a singular copula density at the boundary and list both sets.
    2. P1.2 — Compare estimation contracts. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. A team fits parametric marginals once, treats the resulting uniforms as fixed, fits a copula, and reports Hessian standard errors from only the second fit. Name the estimation regime and identify the uncertainty omitted from the reported interval.
    3. P1.3 — Reproduce a conditional quantile. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For the FGM copula with \(\theta=1\) and \(u=0.8\), solve for the conditional median of \(U_2\), then transform it through a unit-exponential marginal.
    4. P1.4 — Compute a Rosenblatt coordinate. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Under the same FGM model, an observation has \((u,v)=(0.8,0.5)\). Compute \((Z_1,Z_2)=(u,h_{2\mid1}(v\mid u))\) and state the two distributional properties a correct residual sample must satisfy.
    5. P1.5 — Correct for classifier priors. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. A density-ratio classifier is trained with dependent examples at prior \(\pi=0.2\) and product-reference examples at prior \(0.8\). At a point it reports \(h(u)=0.8\). Derive and compute \(c(u)=p(u)/q(u)\); contrast it with the equal-prior shortcut.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer an interval likelihood. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 15 min. With \(\theta=1\) and \(U_1=0.8\), the second coordinate is known only to lie in \((0.2,0.6]\). Compute its conditional probability and explain why evaluating a conditional density at \(0.4\) is not the same observation model.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. \(R/(n+1)\) gives \((0.2,0.4,0.6,0.8)\); \(R/n\) gives \((0.25,0.5,0.75,1)\). The latter places the maximum at the boundary, where densities with inverse-CDF or logarithmic terms can be singular or numerically undefined. Check. The \(n+1\) convention is a finite-sample device, not an oracle transform.
    2. P1.2. This is a two-stage IFM workflow if the marginals are parametric and their fitted CDFs feed the copula fit. A second-stage Hessian conditional on \(\widehat\alpha\) omits variability and covariance induced by estimating the marginal parameters. Structure/family selection would add further uncertainty if performed. Rubric. Full credit names the first-stage source rather than merely saying the interval is “too small.”
    3. P1.3. Solving \(0.6v^2+0.4v-0.5=0\) gives \(v=(-0.4+\sqrt{1.36})/1.2\approx0.6385\). The exponential quantile is \(-\log(1-v)\approx1.018\). Check. The other quadratic root lies outside \([0,1]\).
    4. P1.4. \(Z_1=0.8\) and \(Z_2=0.4(0.5)+0.6(0.5)^2=0.35\). Across correctly modeled observations, each residual coordinate must be uniform and the coordinates must be mutually independent, with serial or covariate structure also absent when relevant. Check. One numerical pair cannot validate either distributional property.
    5. P1.5. Bayes' rule gives \(h/(1-h)=[\pi/(1-\pi)]c\), hence \(c=[h/(1-h)][(1-\pi)/\pi]=4\cdot4=16\). The equal-prior shortcut would return four and is wrong by the prior-odds factor. Check. Even the corrected pointwise ratio is not by itself a normalized finite classifier or a sampler.
    6. P1.6. \(h(0.6\mid0.8)=0.24+0.216=0.456\) and \(h(0.2\mid0.8)=0.08+0.024=0.104\), so the interval probability is \(0.352\). A density at \(0.4\) has units per copula-scale length and represents a pointwise derivative, not probability over the reported interval. Check. The endpoint difference respects the actual observation region.

    Chapter 3 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Rank transforms remove marginal scales, not sampling dependence across rows or time.
    • A good copula log likelihood under fitted marginals does not validate those marginals.
    • Marginally uniform Rosenblatt residuals can remain dependent; both properties must be checked.
    • Permuting coordinates creates a product reference only when the resampling scheme breaks the relevant within-row dependence without introducing leakage.
    • Plugging the midpoint of a censored interval into a continuous density is not an interval likelihood.
    Retrieval questions
    1. What does IFM estimate in its two stages?
    2. How is a bivariate conditional CDF obtained from a smooth copula?
    3. What should a correct Rosenblatt transform produce?
    4. Under equal classifier priors, how is the density ratio recovered from the optimal class probability?
    5. Why must a bootstrap refit marginals when reporting uncertainty for a two-stage estimator?

    Solutions. (1) Marginal parameters first, then copula dependence parameters using the fitted transforms. (2) Differentiate the copula CDF with respect to the conditioning coordinate. (3) Independent uniform coordinates, subject to the stated continuity and regularity. (4) \(h^*/(1-h^*)\). (5) Because pseudo-observations and therefore the fitted dependence inherit marginal-estimation variability.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Copula estimation is inseparable from marginal estimation, even when the algorithm is modular. Conditional derivatives and the Rosenblatt transform turn a joint copula into queries and diagnostics. Density-ratio classification supplies another objective, but its population identity does not automatically normalize a finite neural estimate.

    Why Chapter 4 follows. Exact low-dimensional queries become a construction problem in high dimensions; vines expose that construction one conditional pair at a time.
