    Architecture Design¤

    Architecture Design: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. separate the denoising target from the U-Net, DiT, state-space, sparse, or equivariant backbone that estimates it;
    2. trace where time, class, text, geometry, and other conditions enter a network;
    3. compare attention and convolution using actual token counts, memory traffic, active parameters, and hardware;
    4. convert \(\epsilon\)-, \(x_0\)-, score-, and VP \(v\)-predictions without confusing flow-matching velocity; and
    5. audit an architectural benchmark without attributing gains caused by data, codec, objective, guidance, or post-training to the backbone alone.

    The diffusion backbone estimates a declared target from a noisy state, time/noise level, and optional conditions. U-Nets, DiTs, multimodal Transformers, convolutional networks, state-space models, linear-attention networks, graph networks, and equivariant architectures are all viable backbones under different state-space and systems constraints. Architecture is not the probabilistic model family.

    U-Net Backbone with Skip Connections¤

    The U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015) dominates classical diffusion models through its encoder-decoder structure with skip connections; the diffusion-specific adaptation is due to Ho et al. (2020) (DDPM) and refined in Nichol & Dhariwal (2021) (IDDPM) and Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) — ADM:

    Key Components:

    • Contracting path: Progressive downsampling (256→128→64→32→16) while increasing channels (the diagram above; production U-Nets often go one or two levels deeper).
    • Expanding path: Upsampling reconstructs output at original resolution
    • Skip connections: Critical for propagating spatial details lost in bottleneck
    • ResNet blocks: \(\text{output} = \text{input} + F(\text{input}, \text{time\_emb})\)
    • Group normalization: Dividing channels into groups (~32) for stability

    Why U-Net for Diffusion?

    1. Input and output have identical dimensions (essential for iterative refinement)
    2. Skip connections preserve fine details through bottleneck
    3. Multi-scale processing captures both coarse structure and fine texture
    4. No information bottleneck—maintains full spatial information

    Representative users: SD 1.x, SD 2.x, SDXL, Imagen, DALL·E 2's decoder, and ControlNet. These examples demonstrate the U-Net design space; they do not establish a universal parameter ceiling or imply that every large-scale system should use the same backbone.

    Beyond U-Net: Transformer, State-Space, and Hybrid Backbones¤

    Transformer-, state-space-, and hybrid-attention backbones offer different scaling, multimodal-interaction, and high-resolution trade-offs from pure U-Nets. No architecture-independent U-Net plateau at a fixed parameter count has been established. Three surveys or educational reviews collect the architectural lineage: From U-Nets to DiTs (ICLR Blogposts 2026), Efficient Diffusion Models: A Comprehensive Survey (2024), and Efficient Diffusion Models: A Survey (2025).

    Diffusion Transformer (DiT)¤

    DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023 — ICCV) replaces the U-Net entirely with a Vision-Transformer-style stack:

    • Patchification: the noisy latent \(x_t\) is cut into patches (typically 2×2) and linearly projected to tokens.
    • Standard transformer blocks (LayerNorm → MHSA → MLP) operate on the token sequence.
    • adaLN-Zero conditioning (see below) injects the timestep + class-label embedding.
    • Final unpatchify projects the token sequence back to the latent grid.

    Headline finding: scaling DiT compute (Gflops) reliably reduces FID through the evaluated DiT-XL/2 scale (675 M parameters). On ImageNet 256², Table 2 of the paper reports FID 9.62 without classifier-free guidance and FID 2.27 with guidance scale 1.5. The guided number must not be presented as an architectural result without its sampling condition.

    Influence: DiT established a clean transformer diffusion baseline. Public reports for SD3 and related systems show clear lineage, while limited public Sora details support only a broad spacetime-transformer relationship rather than an exact architectural genealogy.

    adaLN, adaLN-Zero, adaLN-single, adaLN-LoRA — Conditioning Variants¤

    How a transformer-style diffusion backbone is conditioned on the timestep (and optionally class label / text vector / pooled prompt) is itself a design axis with surprising consequences:

    • adaLN (Perez et al., 2018 → DiT): replace LayerNorm's affine parameters with \((\gamma, \beta)\) that are learned linear functions of the conditioning embedding. Each transformer block has its own \((\gamma, \beta)\) — much more expressive than feeding the timestep as an extra token.
    • adaLN-Zero (Peebles & Xie, 2023): zero-initialise the residual modulation so that each DiT block begins as an identity map. The DiT paper's controlled ablation improved FID relative to its in-context and cross-attention conditioning variants at matched training compute; this is evidence for that architecture and protocol, not a universal factor-of-two guarantee or a family default.
    • adaLN-single (Chen et al., 2023 — PixArt-α): share one set of adaLN parameters across all blocks instead of per-block, with a small per-block residual. Cuts ~20 % of parameters and still trains stably.
    • adaLN-LoRA (Lu et al., 2024 — FiTv2): factorise the per-block adaLN projection through a low-rank decomposition. Lighter than adaLN, more expressive than adaLN-single.

    Unveiling the Secret of AdaLN-Zero (Wei et al., 2024) gives a careful theoretical analysis of why the zero-init form converges so much faster.

    U-ViT — Long Skip Connections in a Transformer¤

    U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023 — CVPR) processes patch tokens with a transformer and adds long skip connections from early to late blocks. Its ablations find these skips important in the evaluated U-ViT configurations. That result does not establish that convolution is irrelevant, that every transformer needs symmetric skips, or that the same change has a fixed effect in another architecture. The paper reports FID 2.29 on class-conditional ImageNet 256² under its stated sampling protocol.

    PixArt-α, PixArt-Σ — Efficient Text-Conditional DiTs¤

    PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2023) is the first DiT to demonstrate that a small, training-efficient DiT can match Stable Diffusion at text-to-image:

    • Cross-attention to T5 text features after each self-attention block.
    • adaLN-single for cheap timestep conditioning.
    • Three-stage training curriculum (pixel dependency → text alignment → high resolution).
    • Trained for ~675 A100-days — about 12 % of SD 1.5's compute budget — and matches SDXL on aesthetic FID.

    PixArt-Σ (Chen et al., 2024) scales to direct 4K-resolution generation via a weak-to-strong training schedule and a token-compression block that linearises attention over high-resolution patches.

    MMDiT (SD3) — Multimodal Dual-Stream Transformer¤

    MMDiT (Esser et al., 2024 — Stable Diffusion 3) is the architectural innovation behind SD3 / SD3.5 and the closest cousin of FLUX.1:

    • Two parallel token streams — one for text, one for image — each with its own QKV/MLP weights.
    • Joint self-attention across the concatenated streams: every text token can attend to every image token and vice versa, in both directions.
    • adaLN-Zero conditioning on a pooled text/timestep vector.

    The SD3 report shows stronger prompt-following and typography for the complete MMDiT-based system than named baselines. Public ablations do not isolate dual-stream attention from data, captions, codec, objective, scale, and post-training well enough to assign the entire gain to that component. FLUX.1 (Black Forest Labs, 2024) uses related double-stream and single-stream blocks plus rotary positional embeddings; public model documentation is a technical-report source, not an independent architecture ablation.

    Hourglass Diffusion Transformer (HDiT)¤

    HDiT (Crowson et al., 2024 — ICML) reintroduces the U-Net's multi-level hierarchy inside a pure-transformer architecture:

    • Hierarchical encoder–decoder with symmetric token-resolution stages (2× downsample / upsample between stages, like U-Net).
    • Neighborhood Attention at high resolutions (linear in pixel count) and standard global self-attention at the bottleneck.
    • Scales linearly with pixel count rather than quadratically.

    HDiT demonstrates pixel-space 1024² training without a VAE codec, multiscale loss, or self-conditioning. The paper reported a leading FFHQ-1024² result relative to its named baselines at publication; that claim is dataset-, metric-, and protocol-bounded.

    SiT — Scalable Interpolant Transformers¤

    SiT (Ma et al., 2024 — ECCV) keeps DiT's exact architecture but replaces the discrete-time DDPM training objective with the continuous stochastic-interpolants framework — a generalisation of flow matching that decouples interpolant choice, prediction target, and sampler. The paper systematically ablates each axis and shows SiT-XL/2 reaches FID 2.06 on ImageNet 256² and 2.62 on 512², both improvements over DiT-XL/2 at the same parameter count and FLOPs. SiT is the backbone REPA (see below) is built on top of.

    FiT / FiTv2 — Flexible-Resolution DiTs¤

    FiT (Lu et al., 2024 — ICML Spotlight) and FiTv2 (Lu et al., 2024) treat images as variable-length token sequences, not static-resolution grids:

    • 2-D RoPE (Su et al., 2024) instead of learned position embeddings, intended to support evaluation at resolutions and aspect ratios not seen during training.
    • SwiGLU MLPs and Q-K vector normalisation for training stability at scale.
    • Masked MHSA to handle variable-length padding cleanly.
    • FiTv2 adds adaLN-LoRA, a rectified-flow scheduler, and a Logit-Normal sampler; its paper reports improved convergence under its matched training protocol.

    FiT is designed to support multiple resolutions and aspect ratios in one checkpoint. Generalization beyond its training distribution and freedom from cropping or composition artifacts remain empirical.

    Hunyuan-DiT — Multi-Resolution Bilingual Text-to-Image¤

    Hunyuan-DiT (Tencent, 2024) is a 1.5 B-parameter DiT that interleaves PixArt-α-style single-stream blocks (cross-attention to text) with SD3-style double-stream blocks (joint attention). It is engineered for bilingual English and Chinese prompts, with a custom text-encoder pipeline and rotary positional encoding. Claims about downstream adoption require separate public technical evidence.

    Lumina-Next, DiT-Air — Parameter-Efficient Multi-Modal DiTs¤

    • Lumina-Next (Alpha-VLLM, 2024) — a "Next-DiT" backbone applied across image, video, audio, and 3D generation with rectified-flow training; Lumina-Image 2.0 develops the image setting.
    • DiT-Air (Chen et al., 2025) — applies layer-wise parameter sharing to MMDiT and reports a 66 % size reduction at near-equal quality under its evaluated comparison. This is a protocol-specific result, not a permanent family-wide efficiency ranking.
    • E-MMDiT (2025) — pushes MMDiT to a 304 M-parameter lightweight variant via a highly compressive tokenizer plus Alternating Subregion Attention.

    Mixture-of-Experts Diffusion: DiT-MoE, EC-DiT, Switch-DiT¤

    Sparse mixture-of-experts layers replace some dense feed-forward computation with learned routing. In diffusion backbones, routing may depend on token position, noise level, conditioning, and architecture:

    • DiT-MoE (Fei et al., 2024) — reports a 16.5 B-parameter model with shared-expert routing and an expert-balance loss, 3.1 B active parameters per token, and FID 1.80 on ImageNet 512² under its class-conditional protocol.
    • Switch-DiT (Park et al., 2024 — ECCV) — synergises denoising-task experts with a sparse routing layer; different timesteps select different experts.
    • EC-DiT (Apple, 2024) — adaptive expert-choice routing where experts pick which tokens to process, balancing expert load implicitly.

    The DiT-MoE analysis reports specialization by spatial position and timestep under its trained routers rather than a simple class-only partition. This is evidence about those models, not a theorem that MoE is preferable for every diffusion workload.

    State-Space and Linear-Attention Backbones¤

    Quadratic self-attention is the bottleneck at high resolution. Three lines of work replace it:

    • Diffusion Mamba (DiM) (Teng et al., 2024) — Mamba selective state-space layers in place of attention, \(O(N)\) in token count; competitive at 1024² with far less compute than DiT.
    • DiMSUM (Phung et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) — adds wavelet-domain processing to Diffusion Mamba, capturing local structure and long-range frequency relations that pure SSMs miss.
    • U-Shape Mamba (USM) (Ergasti et al., 2025 — CVPRW) — wraps Mamba blocks in a U-shaped hierarchy, halving sequence length per encoder stage.
    • DiG (Zhu et al., 2025 — CVPR) — Diffusion Gated Linear Attention; 1.8× faster than Flash-DiT-XL/2 at 2048² while matching ImageNet-512² FID.
    • EDiT / MM-EDiT (Suresh et al., 2025) — linear compressed attention for image-to-image dependencies, plus standard softmax attention for image–text interactions; the paper reports linear scaling in image sequence length and quality comparisons under its evaluated settings.

    Hybrid linear and softmax attention changes the quality–memory–latency trade-off; any claimed advantage must specify sequence length, kernels, hardware, and quality target. An ICLR 2026 expo talk is ecosystem evidence, not a peer-reviewed diffusion benchmark.

    Dynamic / Sparse / Cached DiTs¤

    A complementary line of work leaves the architecture roughly fixed but skips computation adaptively:

    • DyDiT / DyDiT++ (Zhao et al., 2025) — adjusts compute along both timestep and spatial dimensions; reports a 51% DiT-XL FLOP reduction under its quality protocol.
    • Learning-to-Cache (Ma et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) — caches selected layer activations across denoising steps and reports roughly 40% inference acceleration under named model, hardware, and quality settings.
    • DiffSparse (ICLR 2026) — differentiable layer-wise token sparsity with a learnable router and DP solver; further accelerates DiTs without retraining the base model.
    • TeaCache — reuses selected features across nearby steps; system reports describe acceleration in several integrations, but the factor depends on checkpoint, threshold, hardware, and tolerated quality change.

    Autoregressive and Masked Image Alternatives: VAR and MAR¤

    VAR and MAR use autoregressive or masked prediction over discrete or continuous visual representations. They provide comparison points for image generation, but their tokenizer, ordering, conditioning, guidance, and sampling compute must be matched before attributing a result to the family name.

    • VAR — Visual Autoregressive Modeling (Tian et al., 2024 — NeurIPS Best Paper) — next-scale prediction over multi-resolution VQ codes. Beats DiT on ImageNet-256² (FID 1.73 vs DiT-XL/2's 2.27) while being ~20× faster at inference.
    • MAR — Masked Autoregressive Modeling (Li et al., 2024) — autoregression without vector quantisation; uses a small per-token diffusion head over continuous values. Matches diffusion quality with autoregressive sampling.
    • VAR-Image / VAR-Video follow-ups extend next-scale prediction to higher resolutions and video.

    These are not diffusion models, but they pressure-test the assumption that multi-step denoising is the only path to high-quality image generation. Architecturally, DiT, MAR, and VAR can be closer to each other than to U-Net DDPM, even though their probabilistic objects and sampling procedures remain distinct.

    Backbone decision questions¤

    The following rows are evidence-bearing candidates to test, not architecture prescriptions. A controlled comparison must hold the data, representation, target, conditioning, training compute, sampler, and evaluation protocol fixed.

    Table DIFF.3. Backbone decision questions
    Design goal Candidate family to investigate What the evidence does and does not establish
    Class-conditional image scaling study DiT / SiT + adaptive normalization Their papers provide controlled ImageNet experiments; the result does not transfer automatically to text conditioning, another codec, or another compute regime.
    Large-scale text-to-image, prompt-following critical MMDiT (SD3-style) or FLUX.1-style hybrid Joint image-text processing is one strong design; isolate it from data, caption quality, codec, objective, scale, and post-training before assigning causality.
    High-resolution observed-space generation HDiT and other hierarchical or local-attention designs Avoiding a codec removes codec distortion but increases sequence and decoder-backbone cost; compare at matched output quality and end-to-end latency.
    Variable aspect ratios FiT / FiTv2 and other 2-D positional schemes Test extrapolation, padding or masking, crop policy, and resolution distribution rather than inferring generalization from the positional encoding alone.
    Maximum scale under a fixed active-compute budget DiT-MoE / EC-DiT Conditional sparsity can increase total capacity without activating every parameter; it is not the only possible scaling strategy.
    Long-sequence / high-res efficiency DiG, DiM / DiMSUM, EDiT Linear attention and SSMs change quality, memory, and hardware trade-offs. No architecture-independent token threshold guarantees a win over optimized softmax attention.
    End-to-end inference efficiency A matched backbone with caching, adaptive computation, or distillation Each technique changes a different cost term and may change the model; report NFE, calls, tensor shapes, decoder cost, wall-clock, and quality separately.
    Bilingual (zh/en) text-to-image Hunyuan-DiT Designed and evaluated for Chinese–English text-to-image; verify performance on the target language and prompt distribution.
    Shared multimodal backbone (image + video + audio) Lumina-Next / Lumina-Image 2.0 Single backbone across modalities; rectified-flow training.

    Transformer and convolutional U-Net backbones both remain active design families. Adaptive normalization, positional representation, sparsity, attention type, multi-resolution hierarchy, and single- versus dual-stream multimodal processing are separable axes; a product name or parameter count does not determine which axis caused an observed gain.

    Architecture Design: Time / Noise-Level Conditioning¤

    Denoising behaviour is strongly noise-level dependent: at high noise the estimator must rely more heavily on learned data structure, while at low noise it resolves local residual uncertainty. Most shared-weight denoisers therefore encode the timestep \(t\), noise scale \(\sigma\), or log-SNR \(\lambda = \log(\bar\alpha_t/(1-\bar\alpha_t))\) and inject that information repeatedly through the network. Injection at every block is a common effective design, not a mathematical necessity: a model can expose the noise level through another architecture, separate parameters by level, or—in restricted settings—learn without explicit noise conditioning.

    There are two distinct sub-problems here, often conflated in implementations:

    1. Encoding — turn the scalar \(t\) (or \(\sigma\), \(\lambda\)) into a vector \(\tau \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\tau}\) that the network can consume.
    2. Injection — make \(\tau\) influence every layer's computation.

    This subsection walks through both.

    1. Encoding the Scalar¤

    Sinusoidal Positional Embedding (DDPM Default)¤

    The classical choice, inherited from Transformer position encodings (Vaswani et al., 2017):

    \[ \tau_{2i}(t) = \sin\!\left(\frac{t}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \qquad \tau_{2i+1}(t) = \cos\!\left(\frac{t}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \]

    for \(i = 0, \ldots, d/2 - 1\) and embedding dimension \(d\) (with the dimension chosen by the architecture). The fixed log-spaced frequencies expose multiple scales. Periodic features do not guarantee global injectivity: two clock values can in principle collide, while distinctness on a declared finite training grid can be checked directly. DDPM-derived U-Nets commonly transform such an encoding with an MLP before block-wise injection.

    Random Fourier Features¤

    Tancik et al. (2020 — NeurIPS) analyze how Fourier feature maps change the frequency spectrum that an MLP can fit. A Gaussian random feature map for a scalar noise coordinate has the form

    \[ \tau(t) = \big[\sin(2\pi B t),\ \cos(2\pi B t)\big], \qquad B \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \sigma_B^2\, I_{d/2}\right) \;\text{(fixed at init).} \]

    The bandwidth \(\sigma_B\) controls the sampled frequencies, and the draw is normally fixed after initialization. NCSN++ configurations use this construction for continuous noise conditioning. Do not infer the embedding from the surrounding objective name: the official EDM implementation uses positional embeddings for its DDPM++ backbone and Fourier embeddings for its NCSN++ backbone, while the EDM preconditioner supplies the scalar noise label separately.

    Log-SNR / Log-σ Encoding¤

    A second axis is what scalar to encode. Three common choices:

    • Discrete index \(t \in \{1, \ldots, T\}\) — DDPM's default; simple but tied to a specific schedule.
    • Continuous time \(t \in [0, 1]\) — used by score-based SDEs and rectified flow; portable across schedules.
    • Log-SNR \(\lambda = \log(\bar\alpha_t/(1-\bar\alpha_t))\) (Kingma et al., 2021 — VDM; Salimans & Ho, 2022) — an informative coordinate that records the signal-to-noise ratio directly. Encoding \(\lambda\) removes ambiguity about which SNR a clock value denotes, but it does not make training or sampling invariant to the schedule: the time-sampling law induces a measure over \(\lambda\), endpoints matter, and finite networks and solvers still see different numerical problems.
    • EDM σ-conditioning (Karras et al., 2022) — supply \(c_{\text{noise}}(\sigma)=\tfrac14\log\sigma\) to the backbone's declared noise-embedding module. This scalar convention is part of EDM preconditioning; whether the backbone maps it with positional or Fourier features is a separate architecture choice.

    Representative rectified-flow and flow-matching implementations encode continuous \(t\in[0,1]\); EDM preconditioning exposes a rescaled \(\log\sigma\); DDPM-derived U-Nets often encode a discrete or continuous clock. A reproducible model must state the scalar, its direction and endpoints, its embedding, and its injection mechanism. Numerical stability is an empirical property of the complete parameterization, not a consequence of taking a logarithm alone.

    Learned-Table (Embedding-Lookup) Encoding¤

    If you commit to a fixed discrete schedule of \(T\) timesteps, you can simply learn a \(T \times d\) embedding table:

    \[ \tau(t) = \mathbf{E}[t, :], \qquad \mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d}. \]

    Used in some discrete-time variants and conditional models. It cannot evaluate genuinely unseen or fractional timestep indices, but DDIM subsampling normally selects a subset of the integer timesteps present during training, so a learned table does not by itself prevent skip sampling. Continuous or interpolated noise levels require an encoding that generalizes between trained indices.

    Other Encodings (Briefly)¤
    • Multi-resolution hash encodings (Müller et al., 2022 — Instant-NGP) are popular in NeRFs but have not been broadly adopted for time conditioning in diffusion (the scalar input is too low-dimensional to benefit).
    • Hybrid sinusoidal + learned-bias — sinusoidal frequencies plus a small learned offset table per timestep; sometimes seen in distilled / few-step student models.

    2. Injecting the Embedding¤

    The encoded vector \(\tau(t)\) is typically fed through a 2-layer MLP and then injected into every transformer / U-Net block. The five common patterns:

    FiLM (Feature-wise Linear Modulation) — U-Net Default¤

    Perez et al. (2018):

    \[ \text{output}(x, \tau) = \gamma(\tau) \odot x + \beta(\tau), \]

    with per-channel scale \(\gamma\) and shift \(\beta\) produced from \(\tau\) via a small MLP. This is one important U-Net pattern, especially the scale-and-shift normalization used by ADM-style residual blocks, but it is not the universal or original DDPM mechanism. The original DDPM U-Net projects the sinusoidal time embedding and adds it as a channel-wise bias inside each residual block; many latent-diffusion U-Nets retain additive residual-block conditioning. Later architectures optionally turn the projection into scale/shift modulation. These variants must be distinguished when reproducing a checkpoint.

    adaLN-Zero — Transformer Default¤

    For DiT-style backbones, one common mechanism is adaptive layer normalization, which derives normalization and residual-gating parameters from the conditioning embedding. adaLN-Zero (Peebles & Xie, 2023) zero-initialises the residual modulation so each block begins as an identity map; the paper's ablation improves FID within its DiT setting, while effect size remains architecture- and protocol-specific. Variants include:

    • adaLN-single (Chen et al., 2023 — PixArt-α) — share one \((\gamma, \beta)\) projection across all blocks; ~20 % parameter savings.
    • adaLN-LoRA (Lu et al., 2024 — FiTv2) — factorise the per-block projection through a low-rank decomposition.

    See Beyond U-Net → adaLN, adaLN-Zero, adaLN-single, adaLN-LoRA for the full discussion.

    Concatenation as Extra Channels¤

    The simplest scheme is to tile a time representation spatially and concatenate it to an input or intermediate feature map. It appears in pedagogical and some early conditional architectures, but not as the original DDPM paper's timestep mechanism: DDPM adds a projected sinusoidal embedding inside every residual block. Input-only concatenation conditions the first layer directly and asks deeper layers to propagate that information, which is generally less direct than repeated residual-block injection or adaptive normalization.

    Time Token Prepending (DiT Variants)¤

    Some DiT variants tokenise \(\tau\) as an extra time element and let self-attention spread its information across image tokens. This is a different information path from adaLN-style blockwise modulation: its effect depends on token mixing, depth, initialization, and the rest of the conditioning design.

    Cross-Attention to a Conditioning Sequence¤

    For models where time and other conditions (text, class, pooled CLIP) are tightly coupled, the entire conditioning vector — including \(\tau\) — is fed as the key/value sequence in a cross-attention layer. The MMDiT recipe in SD3 and FLUX.1 adds the timestep embedding to the pooled-text embedding before adaLN, then uses joint self-attention across image and text tokens; this combines the strengths of FiLM-style modulation and full attention-based text-conditioning.

    3. Practical Considerations¤

    Joint timestep + class / text conditioning. One class-conditional design adds projected time and class embeddings before FiLM or adaptive-normalization injection. Text systems may combine time with a pooled text vector for modulation, retain token-level text for cross- or joint attention, or use both. These constructions are not interchangeable: state which encoder output enters which block and through which projection.

    Null condition for classifier-free guidance. CFG requires the network to handle a designated unconditional condition during training. Class models often use a learned null label; text systems may use an empty prompt, a learned null representation, or another declared dropout path. The dropout probability and unconditional encoding are checkpoint-specific, while time conditioning normally remains present.

    EMA of the embedding MLP. The projection is usually small relative to the denoising backbone, although its parameter count depends on the embedding and hidden widths and can readily reach hundreds of thousands. When a recipe maintains an exponential moving average of model parameters, this projection normally belongs to the same parameter tree unless the implementation explicitly excludes it.

    4. Can Noise Conditioning Be Omitted?¤

    Noise conditioning can sometimes be omitted without changing the model family:

    • Sun et al. (2025 — ICML) — Is Noise Conditioning Necessary for Denoising Generative Models? — empirically demonstrates that across a wide range of diffusion-style models, removing time conditioning entirely produces only a small quality gap; the noisy input itself implicitly encodes the noise level for the network to read off.
    • Follow-ups show similar results for graph diffusion (NeurIPS 2025) and disjoint-manifold diffusion (2026).

    These experiments show that the noisy input can carry information about its corruption level in the evaluated settings. They do not establish that conditioning is unnecessary for every data distribution, schedule, inverse problem, or architecture, nor do they rank scalar encoding and injection choices independently of the full protocol.

    Attention Mechanisms¤

    Attention cost grows with token count, head shape, sparsity pattern, precision, and kernel implementation. In DiT-style backbones it can become the principal memory or latency term at long sequences, but a portable percentage cannot be assigned without profiling the complete network. The following patterns make different trade-offs among receptive field, approximation, memory traffic, and hardware fit.

    Self-Attention¤

    Every spatial token attends to every other spatial token via standard scaled-dot-product attention (Vaswani et al., 2017):

    \[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) \;=\; \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V. \]

    In multi-head form, the projection is split into \(h\) heads of dimension \(d_k / h\), attended in parallel, then concatenated and re-projected. Different heads learn to focus on different spatial relationships (long-range structure, local texture, object boundaries).

    Complexity is \(O(N^2 d)\) in the number of tokens \(N\). For a 256² latent at f8 patch-2 (i.e. 32² latent → 16² patches), \(N = 256\) and self-attention is cheap; for a 1024² pixel-space input, \(N \sim 65{,}536\) and full self-attention is intractable. The classical U-Net heuristic: only apply self-attention at the bottleneck and lower-resolution stages (typically 32², 16², 8²), rely on convolutions at higher resolutions. Modern DiTs apply self-attention at every layer — patchification keeps \(N\) small enough — but this then makes attention's \(O(N^2)\) cost the binding constraint at high resolution, motivating most of the variants below.

    Cross-Attention (for Text Conditioning)¤

    Cross-attention is the standard mechanism for text-to-image conditioning in U-Net-era diffusion (Stable Diffusion 1.x / 2.x, ControlNet, IP-Adapter, etc.):

    • Queries \(Q\) come from image / patch features.
    • Keys \(K\) and Values \(V\) come from a text encoder's output (CLIP, T5, OpenCLIP-G, T5-XXL).

    Different image regions thereby attend to different text tokens, giving spatially-localised text control.

    Within one denoising evaluation, the text-side \(K,V\) are shared across image positions, so dense cross-attention has \(O(NMd)\) score-value work for \(N\) image and \(M\) text tokens. Reuse across denoising steps requires an implementation cache and is exact only for text projections that do not change with the timestep or other step-dependent state.

    Cross-attention is also how PixArt-α / -Σ inject text into a DiT, where it sits next to every self-attention block and is conditioned on a frozen T5-XXL (Chen et al., 2023).

    Joint Attention — MMDiT-Style Multimodal¤

    Several large-scale text-to-image systems—including Stable Diffusion 3, FLUX.1, and Hunyuan-DiT—use joint attention rather than a simple cross-attention-only design: text and image tokens participate in a shared attention computation, while implementations may retain modality-specific projections or feed-forward paths. This is an architecture pattern, not a defining property of diffusion.

    This lets every text token attend to every image token and vice versa in a bidirectional operation. See the MMDiT subsection above for the architecture diagram. Joint attention is a plausible contributor to stronger prompt interaction and text rendering, but public comparisons do not isolate it from training data, captioning, scale, codec, objective, and post-training strongly enough to call it the single cause.

    Position Encodings: Sinusoidal → Learned → RoPE → 2-D RoPE¤

    Self-attention is position-agnostic on its own — Q-K dot products are invariant to token reordering. The positional information that distinguishes "top-left patch" from "bottom-right patch" comes from the position encoding. Modern diffusion has converged on two choices:

    • Learned absolute position embeddings (one vector per token slot) — simple but tied to a fixed grid; doesn't generalise to other resolutions.
    • Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) — rotate \(Q\) and \(K\) as a function of token position so the dot product encodes relative offsets under the chosen frequency convention. RoPE and its multidimensional variants are used by several DiT systems, but learned, fixed, relative-bias, and convolutional position mechanisms remain valid alternatives.
    • 2-D RoPE factorises the rotation across \((H, W)\) axes; NaViT-style variable-resolution RoPE (Dehghani et al., 2023) supports arbitrary aspect ratios. FiT / FiTv2 (Lu et al., 2024) and FLUX.1 are built on this.

    Spatial, Temporal, and 3-D Attention in Video Diffusion¤

    Naive dense 3-D attention over \(T\times H\times W\) video tokens has \(O((THW)^2)\) score complexity. Three recurring strategies are:

    1. Factorised space-time attention — interleave 2-D spatial attention blocks (treating \(T\) as a batch dimension) with 1-D temporal attention blocks (treating \(HW\) as a batch dimension). Used in Imagen Video, AnimateDiff, Stable Video Diffusion, and most early video-DiT recipes. \(O(THW \cdot (HW + T))\).
    2. Full 3-D attention with spatio-temporal patchification — Sora-style. Patch the video at \((t, h, w)\) resolution to keep \(N\) tractable, then run joint self-attention across all spatio-temporal tokens. Used by Sora, Wan 2.x, HunyuanVideo, Mochi-1.
    3. Sparse / structured 3-D attention — neighbourhood attention in \((t, h, w)\), axial attention, or learned sparse patterns for efficient long-context video generation (for example, LiteAttention, arXiv:2511.11062, and FrameDiT, arXiv:2603.09721).

    Windowed and Neighborhood Attention (Sub-Quadratic, Sparse)¤

    For pixel-space and very high-resolution latents, full self-attention is replaced by local attention windows:

    • Window Self-Attention (Liu et al., 2021 — Swin) — partition tokens into non-overlapping windows; attend within each window, then shift the windows between layers to mix information.
    • Neighborhood Attention (NA / NAT) (Hassani et al., 2023 — CVPR) — every token attends within a sliding neighborhood. At fixed neighborhood size its score computation is linear in \(N\); boundary handling, dilation, shifting, and positional terms determine the exact equivariance properties. The NATTEN extension provides specialized kernels.
    • Dilated / mixed windows combine multiple receptive-field sizes per layer.

    Linear-Attention Variants (Beyond Softmax)¤

    A small zoo of \(O(N)\) attention alternatives has crossed over from LLMs into diffusion (covered in detail in Beyond U-Net → State-Space and Linear-Attention Backbones):

    • Linear / kernelised attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Performer, Choromanski et al., 2021) — replace softmax with a feature map \(\phi\), yielding \(\text{Att} = \phi(Q)(\phi(K)^\top V)\) in \(O(N d^2)\).
    • Gated Linear Attention — used in DiG (Zhu et al., 2025 — CVPR).
    • Selective state-space (Mamba) layers — used in DiM, DiMSUM, U-Shape Mamba.
    • Linear compressed attention for image-to-image, paired with softmax for image-text — used in EDiT / MM-EDiT (Suresh et al., 2025).
    • Hybrid blocks with a declared mix of linear and softmax attention layers, retaining occasional global mixing while changing the cost of other layers.

    Token Merging, Pruning, and Sparse Attention¤

    Rather than redesign the attention mechanism, several methods reduce the token count:

    • ToMeSD (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023 — CVPRW) — merge similar tokens before each attention block in Stable Diffusion. Up to 60 % token reduction → 2× speedup, 5.6× memory reduction, no retraining, minimal quality loss.
    • ToMA — Token Merge with Attention (2025) — adapts token merging specifically for diffusion transformers.
    • DiffSparse (ICLR 2026) — differentiable layer-wise token sparsity with a learnable router; further accelerates DiTs without retraining.
    • DyDiT / DyDiT++ (Zhao et al., 2024–25) — adaptive computation along both timestep and spatial dimensions; 51 % FLOPs reduction on DiT-XL.

    Implementation: FlashAttention and Friends¤

    Many diffusion stacks compile attention through one of three backend families. They implement the same mathematical softmax-attention operation when used in compatible modes, but are not bitwise drop-in equivalents: floating-point accumulation order, masks, dropout, precision, supported head dimensions, and backward kernels can change numerical results and availability.

    • FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022 — NeurIPS; FlashAttention-2, Dao 2023; FlashAttention-3, Shah et al., 2024) — IO-aware tiled attention that avoids materialising the full \(N \times N\) score matrix in high-bandwidth memory. Report the exact kernel, hardware, precision, sequence shape, and achieved throughput when using performance numbers from a FlashAttention paper.
    • xFormers memory-efficient attention (Meta) — earlier and still widely used in Stable Diffusion deployments.
    • PyTorch SDPA / FlexAttention — vendor-blessed kernels with automatic dispatch to FA-3 / Triton on supported hardware.

    Feature and KV Caching at Inference¤

    Diffusion is iterative, so consecutive denoising steps usually have highly correlated attention activations. Modern fast-inference stacks cache them:

    • DeepCache (Ma et al., 2024 — CVPR) — caches U-Net deep-feature activations across steps; ~2× speedup.
    • Learning-to-Cache (L2C) (Ma et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) — learns which DiT layers are safe to cache between steps.
    • TeaCache — feature caching baked into Lumina-Image 2.0, FLUX, and several open video stacks for ~2× inference speedup.
    • ToCa, FORA, Δ-Cache — methods that cache token-level attention output rather than whole layers.

    Caching, efficient attention, token reduction, quantisation, distillation, and latent representations can compose, but their reported speedups cannot simply be multiplied. They change different terms in the end-to-end cost, can contend for the same memory bandwidth, and may use different checkpoints, precisions, batches, or quality targets. The sampling compute ledger separates those terms instead of converting a stack of method-level ratios into one unbounded speedup claim.

    Table DIFF.5. Attention decision questions

    High-resolution observed space Compare neighborhood, windowed, hierarchical, and full attention at matched quality and memory.
    Long latent sequence Compare linear or state-space mixing, token reduction, sparse attention, and optimized full attention on the target hardware.

    Softmax attention with absolute, relative, or rotary positions is one important baseline. Windowing, factorization, linearization, token merging, caching, and state-space layers trade approximation, receptive field, memory traffic, compiler support, and hardware utilization differently; none is a family-wide default.

    Model Parameterization Choices¤

    The same denoising network can be trained to predict different targets derived from \(x_t\). Given the standard VP-SDE forward \(x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar\alpha_t}\, \epsilon\) (write \(a_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\), \(b_t = \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\), so \(a_t^2 + b_t^2 = 1\)), the common prediction targets are:

    • ε — the additive Gaussian noise (DDPM)
    • x₀ — the clean image (data prediction)
    • v — Salimans & Ho's "velocity" (a rotation of (ε, x₀))
    • score — \(\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t)\) (score matching)
    • F — the EDM "preconditioned" target (Karras et al. 2022)
    • flow-matching velocity — \(u_t(x) = \mathbb{E}[x_1 - x_0 \mid x_t]\) (a different "velocity" from Salimans & Ho's v)

    These objects do not all form one affine equivalence class. For one VP perturbation and an interior noise level, sampled \(\epsilon\), sampled \(x_0\), and VP \(v\) are related by an invertible rotation when \(x_t\) is known. Their population-optimal conditional means are related by the same linear maps. The marginal score is obtained through a conditional expectation, EDM wraps a denoiser in a chosen preconditioner, and an arbitrary flow-matching velocity belongs to a separately declared probability path. The distinctions matter before optimization is even considered.

    Conversion boundaries for prediction targets¤

    Pick any two of \((x_t, x_0, \epsilon)\) and the third is determined:

    \[ x_0 = \frac{x_t - b_t\, \epsilon}{a_t}, \qquad \epsilon = \frac{x_t - a_t\, x_0}{b_t}, \qquad v_t \;\equiv\; a_t\, \epsilon - b_t\, x_0. \]

    For the conditional perturbation kernel, the score is a rescaled negative sampled noise. For the noisy marginal, the unknown clean sample and noise draw have been integrated out, so the corresponding identity uses a conditional expectation:

    \[ \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t\mid x_0) = -\frac{\epsilon}{b_t}, \qquad \nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(x_t) = -\frac{\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t]}{b_t}. \]

    A diagonal sanity check on \(v_t\):

    Table DIFF.6. Conversion boundaries for prediction targets
    Target Limit at high noise (\(a_t \to 0\), \(b_t \to 1\)) Limit at low noise (\(a_t \to 1\), \(b_t \to 0\))
    \(\epsilon\) \(\epsilon\) — strongly expressed in \(x_t\) \(\epsilon\) — unit target but weakly expressed in the nearly clean input
    \(x_0\) \(x_0\) — data-scale (large), signal-starved \(x_0\) — data-scale, well-scaled
    \(v_t\) \(-x_0\) (since \(a_t \to 0\), \(b_t\to1\)) \(\epsilon\) (since \(a_t \to 1\), \(b_t\to0\))
    Conditional \(\nabla \log q(x_t\mid x_0)\) \(-\epsilon\) — finite \(-\epsilon/b_t\) — scale grows as \(b_t\to0\)

    The practical asymmetry is here: ε-prediction uses a unit-variance target at every time, but at low noise that target is only weakly visible in the almost-clean input; \(x_0\)-prediction is easy to interpret at low noise but becomes posterior-mean prediction from an almost uninformative input at high noise. The \(v\) rotation exchanges these endpoint roles smoothly, while EDM preconditioning explicitly controls input, skip, and output scales. Loss weighting still determines which SNR regions dominate optimization.

    ε-Prediction (Noise Prediction) — DDPM Default¤

    Network predicts \(\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \approx \epsilon\):

    \[ L_\epsilon \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\,x_0,\,\epsilon}\!\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\right]. \]
    • Pros: The sampled regression target has unit marginal variance at every timestep, avoiding a target-scale change caused solely by the schedule. It is used by DDPM, Stable Diffusion 1.x, classifier-guided ADM-style recipes, and many public implementations.
    • Cons: At low noise, the injected perturbation is small relative to the data, so recovering its particular realization can be statistically difficult even though the target variance remains one. Converting an ε error to \(x_0\) scales it by \(b_t/a_t\): this vanishes at low noise and grows at high noise, not the reverse. Weighting and preconditioning therefore matter across the whole SNR range.

    x₀-Prediction (Data Prediction)¤

    Network predicts \(\hat x_\theta(x_t, t) \approx x_0\):

    \[ L_{x_0} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\,x_0,\,\epsilon}\!\left[\|x_0 - \hat x_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\right]. \]
    • Pros: The output is directly interpretable in data space, especially at low noise, and supports explicit clipping or dynamic-thresholding operations when the observation convention warrants them. Some consistency and distillation formulations predict a clean endpoint directly; others retain noise, velocity, score, or transition targets.
    • Cons: At high noise, \(x_t\) carries little information about the paired clean sample. Under squared error the population predictor approaches a posterior mean—and eventually an unconditional mean—not a sample from the full data prior. Unconstrained network outputs can also leave a bounded data range, so any clipping rule must be declared as part of the sampler.

    v-Prediction (Velocity)¤

    Salimans & Ho (2022) define a velocity target by parameterizing the unit circle with an angle \(\phi\):

    \[ a_t=\cos\phi(t),\qquad b_t=\sin\phi(t),\qquad v_\phi \equiv \frac{\partial x_\phi}{\partial\phi} =a_t\epsilon-b_tx_0. \]

    Thus VP \(v\) is the tangent with respect to the angular coordinate. For an arbitrary clock \(t\),

    \[ \frac{\partial x_t}{\partial t}=\phi'(t)v_\phi, \]

    so writing \(v=\partial x_t/\partial t\) silently assumes \(\phi'(t)=1\). With the convention that \(t=0\) is clean, \(v\to\epsilon\) at the clean endpoint and \(v\to-x_0\) at the high-noise endpoint. The rotation can balance target scale under normalized-data assumptions, but it does not guarantee equal gradient conditioning at every timestep; the schedule and loss weighting still matter.

    • Pros: Often improves numerical balance under wide-SNR schedules and is especially useful in progressive distillation, where stable conversions between time levels matter.
    • Cons: Conceptually less transparent than ε / \(x_0\); requires care converting back for sampling.
    • Documented uses: Progressive distillation studies and the public Stable Diffusion 2 768-pixel configuration use VP-v parameterization. Imagen instead documents ε-prediction. A rectified-flow target reduces to VP \(v\) only under the corresponding path and time rescaling; a closed system should not be assigned a target without a technical disclosure.

    The conversion back to ε / \(x_0\) from a v-prediction is just inverting the rotation:

    \[ \hat\epsilon = a_t\, v_\theta + b_t\, x_t,\qquad \hat x_0 = a_t\, x_t - b_t\, v_\theta. \]

    Score Prediction (Score Matching)¤

    For a sampled VP perturbation, the conditional denoising-score target is \(-\epsilon/b_t\); its scale grows as \(b_t\to0\). The population MSE optimum is the noisy marginal score \(-\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t]/b_t\). That marginal score need not diverge at the data endpoint when the data law has a smooth, full-dimensional density. Score-based models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021) pair noise conditioning, weighting, and endpoint handling with SDE or probability-flow-ODE samplers; one must not transfer the conditional target's pointwise scale claim to every marginal data score.

    EDM "F-Prediction" with Preconditioning¤

    Karras et al., 2022 — EDM reframe parameterisation choice as a preconditioning problem. In the variance-exploding (VE) parameterisation \(x_t = x_0 + \sigma \epsilon\), they wrap the network \(F_\theta\) with input/output rescalings:

    \[ D_\theta(x, \sigma) \;=\; c_{\mathrm{skip}}(\sigma)\, x \;+\; c_{\mathrm{out}}(\sigma)\, F_\theta\!\big(c_{\mathrm{in}}(\sigma)\, x;\; c_{\mathrm{noise}}(\sigma)\big), \]

    with closed-form \(c_{\mathrm{skip}}, c_{\mathrm{out}}, c_{\mathrm{in}}, c_{\mathrm{noise}}\) designed to normalize input and target scales under the assumed data variance. EDM is a useful design-space decomposition, not proof of one universally optimal parameterization, and its denoiser preconditioning should not be called literal \(\epsilon\)/\(x_0\)/\(v\) equivalence without deriving the path and weights.

    Flow-Matching Velocity (a different "v")¤

    To avoid notation collision: flow-matching velocity (Lipman et al., 2023) is a different object from Salimans & Ho's v-prediction. Flow matching defines a probability path \(p_t\) between data (\(p_0\)) and noise (\(p_1\)) via a conditional trajectory \(\psi_t(x_0, x_1)\), and trains a vector field

    \[ u_\theta(x_t, t) \;\approx\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\partial \psi_t}{\partial t} \;\middle|\; x_t\right]. \]

    For the linear interpolant \(\psi_t = (1-t)x_0 + t\, x_1\) (rectified flow, Liu et al., 2023), the target is simply \(u_t = x_1 - x_0\). For the VP-Gaussian interpolant, the flow-matching velocity coincides with Salimans & Ho's v only after the schedule-dependent rescaling is derived. The Stable Diffusion 3 report combines rectified-flow training with a logit-normal time distribution and evaluates that choice in its own architecture; public FLUX materials describe a flow-matching system. Neither fact makes a flow target and VP-v interchangeable in an unmatched system comparison.

    How the Choice Interacts with Loss Weighting¤

    The three classical losses are not the same loss with a relabelled target: they correspond to identical math only after the right per-timestep weighting. Hang et al. (2023) — Min-SNR make this explicit: for a common Min-SNR-\(\gamma\) schedule, the loss weight depends on the parameterisation:

    \[ w(t) \;=\; \begin{cases} \min\!\big(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\, \gamma\big) & \text{(}x_0\text{-prediction)} \\[2pt] \min\!\big(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\, \gamma\big) \,/\, \mathrm{SNR}(t) & \text{(}\epsilon\text{-prediction)} \\[2pt] \min\!\big(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\, \gamma\big) \,/\, \big(\mathrm{SNR}(t) + 1\big) & \text{(v-prediction)} \end{cases} \]

    where \(\mathrm{SNR}(t) = a_t^2 / b_t^2\). With these weights the three are equivalent in expectation; with the naive unweighted MSE used in the simplified DDPM objective, ε-prediction implicitly upweights low-noise steps and \(x_0\)-prediction implicitly upweights high-noise steps.

    Loss Re-weighting: Logit-Normal, EDM2, and P2-Weighting¤

    The implicit per-step weighting introduced by the parameterisation choice is now actively designed rather than accidental:

    • EDM (Karras et al., 2022) samples \(\sigma\) from a log-normal distribution at training time, focusing capacity on the perceptually-relevant SNR band.
    • EDM2 (Karras et al., 2024) tunes the training-noise distribution as a hyperparameter, uses adaptive reciprocal weighting to balance observed per-noise losses, introduces magnitude-preserving architecture changes, and reconstructs EMA profiles after training. It does not make the noise prior a learned model component.
    • Logit-Normal sampling (Esser et al., 2024 — SD3) is the rectified-flow analogue: \(t \sim \mathrm{LogitNormal}(\mu, s^2)\) keeps mid-range timesteps oversampled.
    • P2 weighting (Choi et al., 2022) downweights very high-SNR denoising terms in the paper's perceptual-prioritized objective. Its benefit is protocol-specific rather than a default for ImageNet pipelines.

    Progressive distillation study VP-v or the study's declared parameterization VP-v supports the paper's angular conversions; other distillation families may predict data, scores, or transitions.
    Continuous-time SDE model score, denoiser, or a convertible population target The sampler needs the declared field and scaling; finite learned errors are parameterization-dependent.
    Reproducing a Karras-line image model EDM denoiser preconditioning Match the paper's data scale, noise distribution, weighting, architecture, sampler, and EMA protocol.
    Flow-matching system Path-specific velocity Declare the conditional path, coupling, time direction, time distribution, weighting, and ODE solver; do not rename it VP-v by analogy.

    Equivalence vs. preference

    Where the stated linear-Gaussian parameterisation is invertible, targets convert through linear changes of variable. Finite-training preference depends on scaling, sampled time distribution, endpoint error amplification, weighting, preconditioning, and downstream solver. Flow velocity and VP \(v\) are different objects except under an explicitly derived path/schedule relation.

    Worked example: convert one VP prediction four ways¤

    Let \(a_t=0.8\), \(b_t=0.6\), \(x_t=1\), and a scalar VP velocity prediction \(\hat v=0.5\). Then

    \[\hat x_0=a_tx_t-b_t\hat v=0.5,\qquad \hat\epsilon=a_t\hat v+b_tx_t=1.\]

    The implied score estimate is \(\hat s=-\hat\epsilon/b_t=-5/3\), and \(a_t\hat x_0+b_t\hat\epsilon=1=x_t\). This catches swapped signs and reversed endpoint interpretations. It does not identify \(\hat v\) with the velocity of an arbitrary rectified-flow path.

    Independent practice and assessment map: architectures and targets¤

    Treat the probabilistic target, representation, conditioning path, backbone, attention implementation, and measured system as separate design objects. Hold the other objects fixed before attributing a result to one of them.

    P1 — Design and falsify a diffusion estimator architecture¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each claim follows from the architecture name alone: a DiT uses a DDPM objective; joint attention caused an entire system's typography gain; linear attention is faster at every sequence length; adaLN-Zero makes explicit time conditioning necessary; a guided FID isolates the backbone; and a 2-D positional representation guarantees resolution extrapolation.     3. P1.3 — Compute before ranking attention. Compare a latent grid of \(64\times64\) patchified with \(2\times2\) patches against a pixel-space \(1024\times1024\) grid patchified with \(4\times4\) patches.     4. P1.4 — Conversion and weighting audit. With \(a_t=0.8\), \(b_t=0.6\), \(x_t=1\), and \(\hat v=0.5\), recover \(\hat x_0\), \(\hat\epsilon\), and the implied marginal-score estimate.     5. P1.5 — Causal benchmark audit. Choose one U-Net-to-DiT, single-stream-to-joint-attention, dense-to-MoE, or softmax-to-linear-attention result from the chapter.     Architecture Design: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Backbone, parameterization, conditioning, representation, and objective form a coupled system. A model called “DiT” may use diffusion, stochastic-interpolant, or flow-matching training; the transformer name alone says nothing about its probability path or solver.

    Retrieval practice: architecture and targets
    1. Why is a guided DiT FID not an unguided architecture result?
    2. What are the VP \(v\) endpoints when \(t=0\) is clean?
    3. Why can joint attention not be credited with all SD3 text-rendering gains?
    4. Which quantities are needed before claiming linear attention is faster than softmax attention?

    Solutions. (1) Guidance changes the sampling distribution and fidelity/coverage operating point. (2) \(v\to\epsilon\) at low noise and \(v\to-x_0\) at high noise. (3) Data, captioning, codec, scale, objective, and post-training co-vary. (4) Sequence length, head dimension, active parameters, memory traffic, kernel/compiler, precision, batch size, hardware, and matched quality.
