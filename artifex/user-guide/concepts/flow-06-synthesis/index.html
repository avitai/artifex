    This chapter consolidates the classical and continuous-time perspectives, routes readers to next steps, and organizes foundational and research references. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    Classical normalizing flows combine a tractable change-of-variables density with direct sampling and inversion. Their speed and optimisation behavior depend on the transformation family; continuous and flow-matching models make different numerical trade-offs.

    Capstone objectives¤

    After this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. reconstruct the entire argument from probability object to evidence claim;
    2. select a flow construction from data support and required queries;
    3. design analytic, numerical, and statistical validation; and
    4. classify a research result without confusing its backbone, objective, sampler, codec, or benchmark.

    Core Principles¤

    • Exact Likelihood

      Tractable classical bijections provide an exact model-density expression; discrete-data bounds, CNF integration, and floating-point evaluation carry additional qualifications

    • Invertible Architecture

      Bijective transformations allow direct sampling and latent inversion; arbitrary conditional inference can still be hard

    • Tractable Jacobians

      Structured Jacobians (triangular, diagonal) reduce complexity from \(O(D^3)\) to \(O(D)\)

    • Stable Training

      Maximum likelihood and vector-field regression avoid a minimax game, but stochastic optimization, conditioning, stiffness, and numerical estimators can still fail

    • Composable Design

      Stack transformations to enlarge expressivity within support, topology, architecture, and finite-capacity constraints

    Flow and Transport Literature¤

    1. Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2023) — simulation-free CNF training; the unifying objective.
    2. Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2023) — straight-line ODEs; powers Stable Diffusion 3 and FLUX.1.
    3. Conditional / OT-CFM (Tong et al., 2024) — minibatch-OT couplings; cleanest practical recipe.
    4. TarFlow (Zhai et al., ICML 2025) — Transformer-based masked autoregressive normalizing flow; strong reported likelihood and image generation with sequential inverse sampling.
    5. Mean Flows (Geng et al., NeurIPS 2025 Oral) — average-velocity training; FID 3.43 at 1 NFE on ImageNet 256² with no distillation.
    6. CFG-Zero* (Fan et al., 2025) — improved CFG specifically for flow matching.
    7. Discrete Flow Matching (Gat et al., 2024; Campbell et al., 2024) — CTMC flows for text, molecules, graphs.
    8. Riemannian / Geometric Flow Matching (Chen & Lipman, 2024) — flows on manifolds; the basis for protein and materials applications.
    9. Flow Map Matching (Boffi et al., 2024) — learns time-to-time maps and exposes identity/semigroup relationships to consistency and average-velocity parameterizations.
    10. Preprint research: Stable Velocity / variance-reduced FM (2026) proposes variance-reduced training and finetuning-free acceleration; the author results remain protocol-bounded.
    11. Peer-reviewed research: Frequency-Aware Flow Matching (Ren et al., CVPR 2026) adds frequency and spatial branches with time-dependent frequency conditioning. The accepted paper reports FID 1.38 on class-conditional ImageNet 256 under its protocol; this is evidence for that architecture, not a family-wide SOTA claim across data, guidance, or compute settings.
    12. Peer-reviewed research: several routes to fewer steps must be distinguished: Decoupled MeanFlow adapts pretrained flow models, improved MeanFlow changes training dynamics, MeanFlow-RAE changes representation and uses distillation, and S-VFM adds variational conditioning. Similar output-step counts do not imply the same supervision or total cost.

    When to Use Normalizing Flows¤

    Best Use Cases:

    • A tractable normalized model density is essential, with the preprocessing and anomaly/model-selection semantics independently validated
    • A measured coupling-flow inverse, autoregressive-flow schedule, or few-step flow map satisfies the task's latency/quality budget
    • A non-adversarial maximum-likelihood objective is preferred and its optimisation diagnostics are acceptable
    • Numerically controlled invertibility or a separate lossless coding construction is needed
    • Mode coverage can be measured under maximum-likelihood training and the chosen architecture; it is not guaranteed by invertibility

    Prefer another or hybrid construction when:

    • the desired support changes dimension or topology and an ordinary bijection would be badly mismatched;
    • compressed or lossy representation learning is central, in which case a codec/latent-variable model should be explicit;
    • exact density is unnecessary and its architectural/numerical cost loses under controlled quality–latency comparison; or
    • a discrete, graph, manifold, or simulator state space calls for a generator native to that geometry rather than forced Euclidean dequantisation.

    Open Research Directions¤

    • One-step generation via rectified flows and distillation
    • Pyramidal structures for video and high-resolution media
    • Hybrid models combining flows with diffusion, transformers
    • Scientific applications in materials, proteins, molecular generation
    • Geometric awareness for data on manifolds

    Cumulative research exercise: design a molecular conformation generator¤

    Assume each example contains atom types, bond graph, and 3D coordinates, and the goal is conditional generation plus calibrated free-energy reweighting.

    1. State space: atom types and bonds are discrete; coordinates live modulo translation and rotation, with chirality and collisions carrying physical meaning.
    2. Candidate construction: use a discrete jump generator for types/edges and an equivariant manifold/Euclidean vector field for coordinates, coupled through a general Markov or multimodal generator. An ordinary \(\mathbb R^D\) flow over padded one-hot vectors is a baseline, not a semantics-preserving default.
    3. Training evidence: specify endpoint coupling, conditional paths, equivariant architecture, energy or data supervision, and whether training samples are equilibrium-distributed.
    4. Sampling evidence: report jump/ODE solver, NFE or events, constraint projection, rejection, temperature, wall-clock, and effective sample size after any reweighting.
    5. Evaluation: separate validity, uniqueness, coverage, geometry, conditional accuracy, energy calibration, and held-out system generalization. A lower vector-field loss does not establish correct thermodynamics.

    This exercise forces the curriculum's central discipline: choose the probability object and state space first, then the objective and numerical algorithm, and only then interpret a benchmark.

    Worked synthesis: a constrained simulator posterior¤

    Suppose a simulator produces an observation \(Y\) from a positive reaction rate \(\lambda>0\) and an efficiency \(\eta\in(0,1)\). The query is the calibrated posterior over \((\lambda,\eta)\) for a new observation, not unconditional data generation. Define \(u_1=\log\lambda\) and \(u_2=\operatorname{logit}\eta\). The density in scientific coordinates is

    \[ q_\phi(\lambda,\eta\mid y) =\frac{q_\phi(\log\lambda,\operatorname{logit}\eta\mid y)} {\lambda\,\eta(1-\eta)}. \]

    A defensible first system makes each layer of that claim explicit.

    1. Coordinates and measure. A conditional spline or autoregressive flow models \(q_\phi(u\mid y)\) with respect to Lebesgue measure in transformed coordinates. The formula above applies the coordinate Jacobian. Omitting it changes the density and any region probability computed in the original units.
    2. Training object. Draw \((\lambda,\eta)\) from the declared prior, run the simulator, and fit \(q_\phi(u\mid y)\) by conditional maximum likelihood on transformed parameters. This is neural posterior estimation under that prior and simulator. Invertibility normalizes the learned approximation; it does not make it the exact simulator posterior.
    3. Architecture decision. Begin with a small conditional spline flow because the posterior is two-dimensional and bounded transforms are explicit. A coupling stack, MAF, or flow-matching posterior is an alternative to compare, not an automatic upgrade. Conditioner capacity and transform family are ablated separately.
    4. Analytic and numerical validation. Test both map directions, compare the analytic log-determinant with automatic differentiation at interior and near-boundary points, evaluate normalization by quadrature for selected observations, and repeat in higher precision. Boundary failures in the logit transform are not repaired by a perfect inner bijector.
    5. Statistical validation. Run simulation-based calibration ranks, prior-predictive and posterior-predictive checks, conditional coverage, and tests at shifted observations. If the simulator likelihood is unavailable, the learned posterior cannot simply be importance-corrected with an exact likelihood ratio; that limitation must remain in the claim.

    The worked decision separates a normalized conditional approximation from an exact posterior, and it makes coordinate, numerical, and statistical evidence independently falsifiable.

    Independent practice and assessment map: cumulative flow synthesis¤

    Complete the decision record for a domain different from the worked simulator posterior and the molecular research exercise. Bind every mathematical, statistical, and deployment claim to an executable or source-backed check.

    P1 — Defend an end-to-end flow system¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Classify each statement as an algebraic identity, assumption-bounded correspondence, empirical comparison, hybrid composition, loose analogy, or false claim: exact classical-flow density implies exact data likelihood; CNF and coupling likelihoods have the same numerical contract; flow matching is diffusion under every path; TarFlow is one-NFE generation; MeanFlow is a consistency model; a latent codec plus flow prior is an ordinary observation-space bijection; and a normalized posterior approximation is calibrated. Name the assumptions or evidence needed to strengthen each claim.
    2. P1.2 — Construction decision. For either rare molecular conformations or irregular climate trajectories, declare support, reference measure, symmetries, conditions, required density/posterior/sampling queries, base law, classical/CNF/flow-matching/discrete/geometric construction, architecture, objective, solver or inverse, and representation/codec. Compare at least two alternatives and give a falsifiable reason for the selection.
    3. P1.3 — Three-layer validation. Derive one analytic density or generator identity for the selected construction; design numerical inverse, Jacobian/divergence, solver, dtype, and reduction oracles; and define statistical normalization, coverage, calibration, constraint, and shift tests. Include a tractable finite or Gaussian case whose correct answer is known independently of the model code.
    4. P1.4 — Research evidence audit. Select one classical-flow result, one flow-matching result, and one few-step result from the chapter. Record publication status, probability object, path, backbone, codec, training/teacher compute, sampler and calls, guidance, data, metric implementation, hardware, uncertainty, and reproduction status. Rewrite each headline as the strongest protocol-bounded statement supported by that record.
    5. P1.5 — Transfer and rerouting. Transfer the system to the other domain and identify every support, measure, symmetry, path/kernel, condition, metric, and baseline that must change. Route each unresolved claim to a derivation, executable toy problem, primary source, controlled experiment, explicit limitation, or rejection; do not preserve a flow label when the probability object has changed.

    Final synthesis¤

    "Flow" names a set of related but nonidentical constructions. Classical normalizing flows normalize through an explicit bijection and Jacobian. CNFs normalize through invertible dynamics and an integrated divergence. Flow matching trains a vector field for a chosen probability path without solving the ODE in the common training estimator. Rectification changes couplings or learned trajectories; flow maps and MeanFlow learn longer transitions; discrete and geometric flows replace Euclidean calculus with state-appropriate generators. The relationships are powerful precisely when their assumptions remain visible.

    Cumulative retrieval practice: the complete flows volume
    1. Reconstruct the log-density of \(x=f_K\circ\cdots\circ f_1(z)\) and state the map direction.
    2. Name four reasons an "exact-likelihood flow" can still yield a non-exact or incomparable reported data likelihood.
    3. Explain the MAF/IAF trade-off without saying either direction is constant-cost.
    4. Derive the population-optimal conditional flow-matching predictor.
    5. Give one example each of algebraic equivalence, assumption-bounded dynamical correspondence, and hybrid composition in this volume.
    6. What must accompany a one-step FID claim before it supports a deployment choice?

    Solutions. (1) In the generative direction \(x=f(z)\), add base log-density and subtract every forward log-absolute-determinant; equivalently add inverse-map log-determinants at the observation. (2) Discrete dequantisation bound, omitted preprocessing Jacobian, stochastic trace estimation, ODE discretisation, finite precision, or a changed base measure. (3) MAF parallelizes conditional-parameter evaluation for density but samples sequentially; IAF reverses the dependency direction so base-to-sample is parallel while observation-to-base is sequential, with arithmetic still scaling with model and dimension. (4) For squared loss it is \(\mathbb E[U_t\mid X_t=x,t]\), which yields the marginal velocity under regularity assumptions. (5) Algebraic: forward versus inverse Jacobian formulas. Dynamical: score-path and velocity-path conversions under a declared Gaussian path. Hybrid: a latent codec plus flow-matching prior. (6) Dataset/split, conditioning, representation, checkpoint, guidance, metric implementation/sample count, total FLOPs and sequential evaluations, codec/post-processing, hardware wall-clock, training data/compute, and reproduction status.

    Source-tracing checklist¤

    For any research claim, locate the primary paper or official technical report and record: exact model/objective; publication status and version; data and split; representation; conditional setting and guidance; architecture and parameters; sampler, NFE, and post-processing; metric implementation and sample count; compute/hardware when available; ablation supporting the causal wording; and independent reproduction status. If one field is absent, state that it was not reported rather than inferring it.
