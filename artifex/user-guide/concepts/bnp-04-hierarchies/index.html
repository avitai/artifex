    4. Hierarchies, Dependence, and Neural Composition¤

    Chapter 4 definition and scope¤

    Hierarchical random measures share atoms and statistical strength across related groups. Dependent random measures let masses or atoms vary with a covariate, time, or known graph. Neural components can parameterize likelihoods or posterior algorithms inside either construction. These are three distinct compositions: hierarchy, stochastic dependence, and function approximation.

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional independence, grouped data, Chapters 1–3, and the distinction between a model and an inference network. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive the HDP generative hierarchy and its conditional shared-atom update.
    2. O2 — Explain why independent diffuse-base DPs do not share atoms whereas HDP children do.
    3. O3 — Distinguish hierarchical sharing from covariate-, time-, or graph-indexed dependence.
    4. O4 — Audit marginal validity, joint dependence, and causal overinterpretation in dependent random measures.
    5. O5 — Classify a neural component as likelihood, random-measure map, amortized posterior, or hyperparameter learner.
    6. O6 — Design a grouped or conditional BNP study with an explicit sharing query and matched baseline.

    Use the decision diagram before borrowing a familiar acronym. “HDP,” “dependent DP,” and “neural BNP” locate different modifications of the model or inference pipeline.

    Why independent group priors fail to share atoms¤

    Suppose group \(j\) has a random mixing measure \(G_j\). If

    \[ G_j\overset{\mathrm{ind}}\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H) \]

    with diffuse \(H\), each \(G_j\) is discrete, but two independent draws almost surely place their atoms at different locations. The groups can have similar prior distributions without sharing exact mixture components.

    The hierarchical Dirichlet process instead draws

    \[ G_0\sim\operatorname{DP}(\gamma,H), \qquad G_j\mid G_0\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,G_0), \]

    followed by

    \[ \theta_{ji}\mid G_j\sim G_j, \qquad x_{ji}\mid\theta_{ji}\sim f(x_{ji}\mid\theta_{ji}). \]

    Because \(G_0\) is discrete almost surely, every \(G_j\) draws its atoms from the same countable global catalog. Group weights differ, while atom locations are shared.

    In this plate diagram, circles denote random variables or measures, the filled \(x_{ji}\) node is observed, and rectangles denote replicated groups and items. An arrow specifies a conditional distribution, not a causal effect. The visual roles follow the plate-notation key introduced in Classical Generative Models.

    The Chinese restaurant franchise view¤

    Integrating group measures yields a nested partition representation. Items sit at group-specific tables; tables select global dishes; tables in different groups may serve the same dish. The auxiliary table variables make the sharing structure computationally convenient, but they are not necessarily scientific entities.

    The HDP is useful for grouped mixtures and shared topic catalogs. It does not by itself impose temporal persistence. An HDP hidden Markov model combines the shared-state measure with transition distributions; “sticky” variants add self-transition bias. The transition law and random-measure hierarchy are separate model decisions.

    Worked shared-atom update¤

    Condition on a two-atom global measure

    \[ G_0=0.6\delta_a+0.4\delta_b \]

    and let a group have \(G_j\sim\operatorname{DP}(4,G_0)\). If that group has observed three assignments to \(a\) and one to \(b\), its posterior weights are

    \[ (G_j(a),G_j(b))\mid\theta_{j,1:4} \sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(2.4+3,1.6+1). \]

    The next group-specific latent assignment chooses \(a\) with probability

    \[ \frac{5.4}{5.4+2.6}=\frac{27}{40}=0.675. \]

    This calculation conditions on \(G_0\). A full HDP posterior also updates the uncertain global masses and atoms using evidence from every group.

    Hierarchical and dependent are not synonyms¤

    A hierarchy expresses conditional sharing through parent random measures. A dependent random measure is a collection \((G_s)_{s\in\mathcal S}\) whose values are statistically coupled across an index such as time, space, treatment, or known graph. Desirable properties depend on the application:

    • each marginal \(G_s\) may or may not be a DP;
    • atoms may be shared, move, appear, or disappear with \(s\);
    • weights may be smooth, Markovian, graph-dependent, or only partially exchangeable;
    • the construction must remain a valid jointly measurable collection of random measures.

    Calling \(s\) a covariate does not make the dependence causal. It models observational variation in distributions unless interventions and structural mechanisms are added separately.

    Current dependent-measure constructions¤

    The following results establish particular constructions, not a universal ranking:

    Table BNP.5. Current dependent-measure constructions
    Construction Evidence and generated structure Boundary of the result
    Graphical Dirichlet process Peer-reviewed research (JMLR 2024): dependent group measures respect a declared DAG over nonexchangeable groups; multiple representations and posterior computation are derived the group DAG is assumed known and its Markov property is not automatically causal
    HDP-Flow Peer-reviewed research (UAI 2025): HDP sharing is composed with flow-based state likelihoods and variational inference for two wearable time-series studies reported state-discovery and transfer results are protocol-specific; a flow likelihood does not make the HDP posterior exact
    Covariate-dependent HDP Peer-reviewed research (JMLR 2026): HDP and dependent-DP ideas are combined with mixed covariates; augmented MCMC is applied to scRNA-seq and calcium-imaging data the data augmentation addresses its normalized-weight computation; it does not establish universal identifiability, scaling, or cluster truth

    These examples show why a current curriculum needs both random-measure semantics and modern likelihood/inference composition. They do not justify replacing established finite-mixture or state-space baselines without a matched experiment.

    Broader normalized random-measure families¤

    Normalizing different CRMs changes the atom-frequency and partition law. The Dirichlet process is the normalized-gamma case; normalized inverse-Gaussian and normalized generalized-gamma processes provide other predictive behavior. Pitman–Yor and broader Poisson–Kingman constructions connect random ranked masses, exchangeable partitions, and species-sampling models.

    The choice matters when rare atoms, unseen species, vocabulary growth, or heavy frequency tails are part of the query. It also changes posterior computation. A closed-form CRP update cannot be assumed for every normalized CRM, and an auxiliary variable introduced for tractability becomes part of the inference algorithm rather than the random-measure definition.

    Neural networks can occupy four different roles¤

    Table BNP.6. Neural networks can occupy four different roles
    Neural role Example What remains BNP
    component likelihood a flow, Transformer, or decoder parameterizes \(f_\psi(x\mid\phi_k)\) the random measure and its induced allocation prior
    covariate-dependent mass/atom map a network maps context to weights, kernels, or atom parameters only if the resulting collection is a valid declared random-measure law
    amortized posterior a set network proposes partitions or features the target posterior under the separately specified BNP model
    empirical-Bayes hyperparameter map a network predicts \(\alpha,H\), or likelihood parameters a conditional/learned prior, whose training data and uncertainty must be reported

    Neural Clustering Processes learn reusable approximate posterior samplers over partitions from simulated labeled datasets. They change the inference interface, not the underlying DP or other clustering prior. Generalization is relative to the distribution of models and datasets used for training.

    A permutation-invariant network is not an exchangeable probability model by itself

    Invariance of a deterministic set encoder means reordering inputs does not change its output. Exchangeability is a property of a normalized joint probability law across every finite sample size. A neural posterior proposal can be invariant while still being biased, incoherent across \(n\), or trained for a model whose prior is not BNP.

    Relationship audit¤

    • Hybrid composition: HDP-Flow combines a random-measure hierarchy with flow likelihoods.
    • Implementation analogy: both random functions and dependent random measures are infinite-dimensional, but their finite-dimensional consistency objects differ.
    • Shared estimator pattern: variational truncation can be used for DP, HDP, and feature models without making their posteriors identical.
    • No causal equivalence: a group DAG or covariate-indexed measure represents dependence unless an SCM and identification assumptions are supplied.

    Independent practice and assessment map: hierarchy and dependence¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. In the worked HDP update, predict whether doubling the group concentration from \(4\) to \(8\) while holding \(G_0\) and the four assignments fixed moves the next-\(a\) probability toward \(0.6\) or toward the empirical \(3/4\). Compute the new probability.
    2. P1.2 — Shared-atom derivation. Prove that two independent DP draws with diffuse \(H\) almost surely do not share an atom, then identify the exact line in the HDP hierarchy that changes that conclusion.
    3. P1.3 — Hierarchy/dependence classifier. For each of three systems—HDP groups, time-indexed smoothly varying weights, and a known-DAG graphical DP—name the index/parent structure, what is shared, the finite consistency requirement, and whether the edges are causal.
    4. P1.4 — Neural-role audit. A set Transformer receives an entire dataset and outputs partition samples, while a flow maps component parameters to an observation density. Classify both networks and list the probability-law and calibration obligations that remain.
    5. P1.5 — Evidence-boundary review. Choose one current dependent-measure row in the evidence table. Rewrite its claim as model, algorithm, data, metric, and validity scope; name one conclusion the cited study cannot establish by design.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Design a three-hospital shared-phenotype model. Decide between exact shared atoms and smoothly changing phenotypes, specify the group/covariate structure, observation and batch likelihoods, new-hospital query, negative-transfer check, matched finite baseline, and external validation needed for a biological identity claim.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. Greater concentration pulls the group measure toward \(G_0\). The updated parameters become \((8(0.6)+3,8(0.4)+1)=(7.8,4.2)\), so the next-\(a\) probability is \(7.8/12=0.65\), between \(0.6\) and the former \(0.675\) and farther from the empirical \(0.75\).
    2. Each independent DP draw has countably many IID locations from diffuse \(H\). Every cross-draw pair coincides with probability zero, and a countable union of zero-probability coincidences is zero. In the HDP, \(G_j\mid G_0\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,G_0)\) uses the same discrete \(G_0\) as every child base, so child atoms must come from its common catalog.
    3. HDP groups are conditionally linked through parent \(G_0\) and share atom locations; restriction to fewer items/groups must agree with the hierarchy. Smooth time-indexed weights require a valid joint process across time and coherent finite time restrictions, not a parent–child hierarchy. A graphical DP encodes a declared group DAG and its Markov law. None of these arrows is causal without an SCM and identification assumptions.
    4. The set Transformer is amortized posterior inference; the flow is the component likelihood. The former must define normalized samples/parameters, respect label symmetry, be calibrated against the target posterior, and report simulator/deployment shift. The latter must define a normalized observation density and its Jacobian/support. Neither changes the BNP prior unless the model explicitly says so.
    5. For the covariate-dependent HDP row, for example, the model couples HDP and dependent-DP ideas over mixed covariates, augmented MCMC handles its normalized weights, and the paper studies scRNA-seq and calcium-imaging applications under its protocols. This supports validity and demonstrated use of that construction; it cannot establish causal dynamics, universal cluster truth, or superiority across all dependent measures and datasets.
    6. If phenotype parameters should be identical across hospitals, use an HDP/hierarchical CRM; if biology changes continuously with covariates, use a validated dependent-measure law and do not claim exact shared atoms. Include hospital and patient covariates, batch/noise terms, held-out-hospital prediction, shrinkage and rare-subgroup calibration, a no-sharing or finite hierarchical baseline, and external markers or interventions not used in fitting before naming biological identities.
    Retrieval questions
    1. Why do independent DPs with diffuse \(H\) fail to share exact atoms?
    2. What feature of \(G_0\) lets HDP groups share a catalog?
    3. Is a dependent DP necessarily hierarchical?
    4. What must be named when a neural network is added to a BNP system?
    Complete solutions
    1. Their independently sampled atom locations coincide with probability zero under diffuse \(H\).
    2. A DP draw \(G_0\) is discrete, and each child DP uses that same discrete measure as its base.
    3. No. Dependence may be induced through covariate-indexed weights, atoms, kernels, Poisson measures, or other joint constructions without a parent–child DP hierarchy.
    4. Whether it parameterizes the likelihood, the random measure, an amortized posterior, or learned hyperparameters—and which probability law remains valid.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Hierarchies share random atoms; dependent measures couple distributions across an index; neural components parameterize a declared part of the model or inference system. These operations compose but do not collapse. Chapter 5 asks how finite algorithms approximate or exactly transition within the resulting posterior.
