    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Autoregressive Models 2 — Architectures

    This chapter establishes the chain-rule factorization, conditional-complexity choices, likelihood objective, ordering assumptions, and masking rules on which every later autoregressive architecture depends. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    For an ordered object \(x_{1:n}\), an autoregressive model specifies normalized conditionals \(p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{<i})\) whose product defines a normalized joint distribution. The chain-rule identity itself is architecture independent. Neural parameterizations include recurrent networks, masked density estimators and convolutions, Transformers, state-space models, and hybrids. Representative milestones include neural language models (Bengio et al., 2003 — JMLR), the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), and large-scale autoregressive language modeling (Radford et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020). The same definition also covers continuous, image, audio, scientific, graph, set, and multimodal representations once their ordering and conditional family are declared.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be comfortable with joint and conditional probability, logarithms, expectations, and categorical distributions. If any of those are unfamiliar, first read Probability and Generative Modeling. The notation \(x_{<i}\) means the ordered prefix \((x_1,\ldots,x_{i-1})\); \(p\) denotes a distribution or density and \(p_\theta\) its parameterized approximation.

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. derive the chain-rule factorization for any chosen ordering;
    2. distinguish exact joint likelihood from marginal, conditional, and MAP inference;
    3. calculate a finite autoregressive joint probability and verify normalization;
    4. distinguish a Markov assumption from a recurrent or masked parameterization;
    5. explain how ordering and masking change the learning problem without changing the probability identity; and
    6. detect target leakage and the common one-position shift error; and
    7. distinguish direct conditional training from joint prefix modeling.

    Consequences of the factorization¤

    The chain-rule construction has several consequences that should be stated separately:

    • Direct likelihood computation - exact model likelihood when each normalized conditional is tractable
    • Simple training - standard maximum likelihood with cross-entropy loss
    • Broad applicability - can model discrete symbols or continuous variables once an ordering, conditional family, and support are specified
    • Flexible expressiveness - from simple next-token prediction to complex long-range dependencies
    • Scalability depends on the parameterization and system - sparse experts, state-space or hybrid backbones, long-context mechanisms, multi-token objectives, and caching change compute without changing the probability identity

    The ordering is part of the model specification. It determines the prefixes presented to each conditional and the sequence used by ancestral sampling. Tractable maximum-likelihood training and exact evaluation of a complete ordered observation do not make arbitrary conditional inference, marginalisation, MAP search, or constrained generation exact.

    Constructive interpretation: building an ordered object¤

    An ancestral sampler implements the factorization as a concrete procedure:

    1. Initialize the order. Draw the first element from the normalized initial conditional \(p_\theta(x_1)\).

    2. Evaluate a prefix conditional. At position \(i\), compute \(p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{<i})\) from the elements already sampled.

    3. Sample and extend. Draw \(x_i\) from that conditional and append it to the prefix. Local conditionals compose into a global joint law through their product.

    4. Terminate or invoke a separate inference procedure. Plain left-to-right ancestral sampling does not revise an emitted element. Infilling, reranking, editing, and blocked sampling require appropriate conditionals, proposals, or search procedures beyond the basic ancestral pass.

    This construction makes complete-data likelihood evaluation parallel across observed positions during teacher-forced training, while ancestral generation remains ordered because each newly sampled value becomes part of the next input.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Chain Rule Factorization¤

    The chain rule of probability is the cornerstone of all autoregressive models. Any joint distribution can be factored as:

    \[ p(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) = p(x_1) \prod_{i=2}^{n} p(x_i \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1}) \tag{AR.1}\label{eq:autoregressive-chain-rule} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:autoregressive-chain-rule}\) is an identity for every ordered joint law; an autoregressive model then parameterizes each conditional \(p(x_i \mid x_{<i})\) with a neural network:

    \[ p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i}) = f_\theta(x_i; x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1}) \]

    where \(\theta\) are learnable parameters and \(x_{<i} = (x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1})\) denotes all previous elements.

    Example - Image with 3 pixels:

    \[ p(x_1, x_2, x_3) = p(x_1) \cdot p(x_2 \mid x_1) \cdot p(x_3 \mid x_1, x_2) \]

    For a 256×256 RGB image with discrete pixel values \(\{0, 1, \ldots, 255\}\):

    \[ p(\text{image}) = \prod_{i=1}^{256 \times 256 \times 3} p(x_i \mid x_{<i}) \]

    The chain rule replaces one flat table with 196,608 conditional modeling problems. This is a useful structural decomposition, but it does not mean the problem now has only 196,608 scalar probabilities: each conditional is a function of a potentially enormous prefix, and its representation and generalization remain the central modeling challenge.

    Direct conditioning and joint prefix modeling are different objectives¤

    Let \(C\) be observed context and \(Y_{1:T}\) the object to generate. A directly conditional autoregressive model specifies

    \[ p_\theta(y_{1:T}\mid c) = \prod_{t=1}^{T}p_\theta(y_t\mid c,y_{<t}). \]

    A joint model with the context first instead specifies

    \[ p_\phi(c,y_{1:T}) =p_\phi(c) \prod_{t=1}^{T}p_\phi(y_t\mid c,y_{<t}). \]

    For that ordering, conditioning the normalized joint on an observed \(c\) cancels the prefix factor \(p_\phi(c)\) and leaves the displayed product over \(Y\). Consequently, one decoder-only architecture can support two different training objectives:

    • applying loss to both context and target tokens fits a joint model of the serialized pair; and
    • masking the context-token losses while exposing those tokens as inputs fits the conditional factors needed for \(p_\theta(y\mid c)\).

    The second objective need not learn the context marginal. This can be desirable when prompts are supplied rather than generated, but it means that its loss is not the joint log-likelihood of the concatenated record. Conversely, a joint objective spends capacity and data on \(p(c)\) even when deployment asks only for \(p(y\mid c)\). Padding, separators, modality tags, and the boundary at which loss begins are therefore parts of the probability specification, not formatting details.

    Worked binary example: the same conditional generator, different joints¤

    Suppose

    \[ p(C=0)=0.75, \qquad p(Y=1\mid C=0)=0.20, \qquad p(Y=1\mid C=1)=0.80. \]

    The resulting joint table is

    Table AR.2. Worked binary example: the same conditional generator, different joints
    \(Y=0\) \(Y=1\) context marginal
    \(C=0\) \(0.60\) \(0.15\) \(0.75\)
    \(C=1\) \(0.05\) \(0.20\) \(0.25\)

    A direct conditional model needs only the two Bernoulli probabilities \(0.20\) and \(0.80\). Replacing the context marginal by \(p'(C=0)=0.5\) while retaining those conditionals leaves every requested generation law \(p'(Y\mid C=c)\) unchanged, but changes all four joint masses. Conditional generation therefore does not identify how often contexts occur.

    Ordering matters to query access as well. An exact reverse factorization \(p(y)p(c\mid y)\) represents the same joint distribution, but obtaining \(p(y\mid c)\) from it generally requires normalizing or otherwise inferring over \(y\); one cannot merely prefix \(c\) to a \(Y\)-first ancestral sampler. Neither factorization is universally easier to learn, and generation order alone does not establish causal direction.

    Prefix Length, Statistical Restriction, and Computation¤

    The argument list of \(p(x_t\mid x_{<t})\) grows with \(t\), but argument count is not the same as intrinsic function complexity. A long prefix may contain much irrelevant information, or it may admit a small sufficient summary. Conversely, a short window can omit the one old variable that determines the next value. The chain rule alone does not decide which case holds.

    The naïve tabular construction exposes the scaling problem. Suppose \(T\) variables share an alphabet of size \(K\). At position \(t\), there are \(K^{t-1}\) prefixes, and a normalized categorical distribution needs \(K-1\) free probabilities at each prefix. Separate unrestricted tables therefore require

    \[ \sum_{t=1}^{T} K^{t-1}(K-1)=K^T-1 \]

    free parameters—the same number as a saturated joint table. For four binary variables, the count is \(1+2+4+8=15\). The chain rule makes normalization and ancestral sampling simple; it does not, by itself, solve statistical estimation. Generalization comes from a justified restriction, parameter sharing, or both.

    Four ways to represent the same prediction interface¤

    For \((X_1,X_2,X_3,X_4)\), compare four constructions. The last two still expose a conditional distribution at every position, but share computation across histories.

    Table AR.3. Four ways to represent the same prediction interface
    Construction Four-variable form What it assumes or shares Known-sequence evaluation Ordinary ancestral state
    Full-prefix tables \(p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)p(x_3\mid x_1,x_2)p(x_4\mid x_1,x_2,x_3)\) No conditional-independence restriction; separate value for every prefix Four table lookups; storage grows as \(2^4-1\) here and \(K^T-1\) generally Store the generated prefix, or enough information to address its table entry
    First-order Markov \(p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)p(x_3\mid x_2)p(x_4\mid x_3)\) \(X_t\perp X_{<t-1}\mid X_{t-1}\); position-specific tables use seven binary parameters, while a tied transition uses three including \(p(x_1)\) Four local lookups; all targets are known during scoring Retain the latest value
    Deterministic recurrent state \(h_t=f_\theta(h_{t-1},x_t)\) and \(p_\theta(x_{t+1}\mid h_t)\) Shares an update and output map; the state is a learned summary rather than a declared finite observation window A general nonlinear recurrence has a length-\(T\) dependency path; some structured recurrences admit parallel scans Retain \(h_t\); its dimension may be fixed even though the represented history is not a fixed-order Markov window
    Direct causal masked network \((\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_T)=F_\theta(x_{1:T})\), with \(\eta_t\) forbidden from using \(x_{t:T}\) Shares one network across positions; a full causal receptive field permits every prefix dependency Teacher forcing exposes all conditional parameters in one masked pass; arithmetic depends on the architecture Retain the prefix or an architecture-specific cache; generation still has \(T\) dependent draws in the basic factorization

    Here \(\eta_t\) denotes logits or other normalized conditional parameters. A causal mask is an information-flow constraint: it removes the present target and future from the input to \(\eta_t\). With a full causal receptive field, it does not assert that any earlier variable is irrelevant. A local convolutional mask, finite attention window, or bounded cache adds a stronger restriction.

    The diagram is an audit of the four prototypes, not a claim that real systems must select exactly one branch. A hybrid can, for example, combine recurrent state with local masked attention; its distributional restrictions are determined by the complete information path.

    Assumption versus architecture¤

    The distinction is easiest to audit by asking whether a choice removes distributions from the model family before any neural-network capacity limit is considered.

    Table AR.4. Assumption versus architecture
    Choice Distributional restriction? What can still go wrong?
    Full chain-rule conditionals No: this is a probability identity Separate tables have exponential storage and sparse context counts
    Order-\(k\) Markov conditionals Yes: histories with the same last \(k\) values must have the same next-value law Bias persists with infinite data when an older value matters
    Fixed-dimensional recurrent state Architecture-dependent: all influence must pass through \(h_t\), but this is not the same as an observed order-\(k\) assumption Finite precision, update design, optimization, and length generalization can prevent the state from retaining the needed property
    Full causal attention or a full-receptive-field mask No additional conditional independence beyond causality, before finite-capacity limits Compute, memory, positional representation, and optimization can still limit effective context use
    Local mask, finite window, or truncated cache Yes at that layer or system boundary unless other paths restore the missing dependency A relevant distant variable becomes structurally unreachable

    Calling every recurrent model “Markov” hides an important level distinction. The augmented computation is first-order in its state, \(h_t=f(h_{t-1},x_t)\), but the observable conditional may depend on the entire prefix through \(h_t\). Likewise, calling a masked network “fully non-Markov” is too strong when its actual receptive field is finite.

    Worked four-bit example: long dependence, one-bit state¤

    Let \(X_1,X_2,X_3\) be independent fair bits and set

    \[ X_4=X_1\mathbin{\oplus}X_2\mathbin{\oplus}X_3, \]

    where \(\oplus\) is exclusive OR. The last conditional is deterministic:

    Table AR.5. Worked four-bit example: long dependence, one-bit state
    Prefix \((x_1x_2x_3)\) 000 001 010 011 100 101 110 111
    \(p(X_4=1\mid x_1,x_2,x_3)\) 0 1 1 0 1 0 0 1

    A first-order Markov model cannot reproduce this joint. Given either value of \(X_3\), the parity of the two independent earlier bits is still fair, so

    \[ p(X_4=1\mid X_3=0)=p(X_4=1\mid X_3=1)=\frac12. \]

    The Markov approximation therefore discards a deterministic relationship even with unlimited data. Yet a recurrent model needs only one bit of state:

    \[ h_0=0,\qquad h_t=h_{t-1}\mathbin{\oplus}X_t\quad(t=1,2,3), \qquad p(X_4=1\mid h_3)=h_3. \]

    A full-prefix table and a full causal masked network can also represent the rule. This counterexample makes both lessons concrete: dependence on every prefix variable need not require a large sufficient state, and a small recent window is not interchangeable with a small recurrent state.

    Evidence boundary: recurrent state is not one capability class

    The one-bit construction proves that some recurrence can represent parity; it does not prove that every gated RNN or state-space layer will learn or even express the same length-generalizing update under its parameter and precision constraints. Sarrof, Veitsman & Hahn (NeurIPS 2024) derive formal-language boundaries for specified modern SSM classes. Mamba-3 (ICLR 2026 Oral) changes the recurrent dynamics and reports different state-tracking and parity behavior under its experimental protocol. These are architecture-specific results, not revisions to the chain rule.

    Data and compute are separate axes¤

    Table AR.6. Data and compute are separate axes
    Axis Full tables Markov window Recurrent state Direct masked network
    Statistical sharing None across distinct prefixes Pools histories with the same suffix; lower variance if the assumption is right, irreducible bias if it is wrong Shares transition and output parameters across positions and histories Shares masked features and output parameters across positions and histories
    Parameter/storage scaling Exponential for a saturated finite-alphabet joint Exponential in order \(k\), not sequence length, when transitions are tied Set by state and network dimensions Set by architecture; dense attention activations and KV caches still grow with context

    Parameter count is not a sample-complexity theorem, and big-\(O\) arithmetic is not a latency measurement. A fair comparison must state the alphabet or output density, context length, parameter sharing, precision, batch size, cache policy, hardware, and matched-quality target. NADE shares computation sequentially across binary conditionals; MADE uses masks to expose all known-data conditionals in a vectorized pass. The next chapter develops the architecture consequences, while the later masked-autoregressive-flow treatment connects the same dependency pattern to invertible transformations.

    Worked example: a normalized three-bit model¤

    Let \(x_1,x_2,x_3\in\{0,1\}\). Specify the Bernoulli probability of 1 at every reachable prefix:

    Table AR.7. Worked example: a normalized three-bit model
    Conditional Probability of 1
    \(p(x_1=1)\) \(0.6\)
    \(p(x_2=1\mid x_1=0)\), \(p(x_2=1\mid x_1=1)\) \(0.2,\ 0.7\)
    \(p(x_3=1\mid 00),p(x_3=1\mid 01),p(x_3=1\mid 10),p(x_3=1\mid 11)\) \(0.1,\ 0.4,\ 0.5,\ 0.9\)

    The probability of the event \((1,1,1)\) is

    \[ p(1,1,1)=0.6\times0.7\times0.9=0.378. \]

    For any prefix, the probabilities of the two next values sum to one. Therefore the full joint is normalized:

    \[ \sum_{x_1,x_2,x_3}p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)p(x_3\mid x_1,x_2) =\sum_{x_1}p(x_1)\sum_{x_2}p(x_2\mid x_1)\sum_{x_3}p(x_3\mid x_1,x_2)=1. \]

    This telescoping argument is the essential reason locally normalized conditionals define a normalized joint. Reordering the variables—for example, \(x_3,x_1,x_2\)—gives another exact factorization of the same true joint. With finite data and a restricted neural architecture, however, different orderings induce different approximation and optimization problems.

    Log-Likelihood and Training¤

    The log-likelihood decomposes additively:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i}) \]

    This makes maximum likelihood training straightforward:

    \[ \max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i})\right] \]

    Equivalently, minimize the negative log-likelihood (cross-entropy):

    \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x_i^{(j)} \mid x_{<i}^{(j)}) \]

    where \(N\) is the dataset size.

    With base-two logarithms and discrete normalized conditionals, the same sum is the sequence's ideal lossless code length under the model. An arithmetic, range, or ANS coder can consume the predicted conditional tables in order; model mismatch adds expected redundancy equal to a forward KL. This interpretation requires a concrete discrete alphabet and coding protocol—the four-direction prefix-code example develops the finite case before extending it to sequences.

    What is exact—and what is not

    For discrete observations with normalized categorical conditionals, teacher-forced training evaluates the exact model log-likelihood as a sum of cross-entropies. For continuous observations, each conditional is a normalized density rather than a vocabulary classification. Exact evaluation of a complete ordered observation does not automatically make missing-data marginalization, arbitrary conditional sampling, constrained decoding, or MAP search tractable.

    Ordering and Masking¤

    Choosing an ordering is crucial. Different orderings lead to different models:

    Text (Natural Sequential Order):

    \[ p(\text{"hello"}) = p(\text{h}) \cdot p(\text{e}|\text{h}) \cdot p(\text{l}|\text{he}) \cdot p(\text{l}|\text{hel}) \cdot p(\text{o}|\text{hell}) \]

    Images (Raster Scan):

    Pixels generated left-to-right, top-to-bottom:

    \[ p(\text{image}) = \prod_{h=1}^{H} \prod_{w=1}^{W} \prod_{c=1}^{C} p(x_{h,w,c} \mid x_{<h}, x_{h,<w}, x_{h,w,<c}) \]

    where \(x_{<h}\) denotes all rows above, \(x_{h,<w}\) denotes pixels to the left in current row, and \(x_{h,w,<c}\) denotes previous channels.

    Masking and shifting ensure the autoregressive property. If the output at index \(i\) predicts \(x_i\), it must not access \(x_i\) or later values. Decoder-only Transformers commonly use a shifted convention instead: the representation at input position \(i\) may include \(x_i\) and its output predicts \(x_{i+1}\). Both conventions are correct when the attention mask, input shift, targets, and sampling loop agree.

    Causal Masking (for sequences):

    # Attention mask preventing position i from attending to positions > i
mask = jnp.tril(jnp.ones((seq_len, seq_len), dtype=jnp.bool_))

    Spatial Masking (for images):

    # PixelCNN mask: pixel (h,w) cannot see (h',w') where h' > h or (h'=h and w' > w)
# Implemented via masked convolutions

    Counterexample: a one-token leak

    Suppose a decoder is trained to predict target \(x_i\) from a representation that is allowed to attend to the unshifted input token \(x_i\). It can learn to copy the answer, producing an impressively low training loss without learning \(p(x_i\mid x_{<i})\). At generation time \(x_i\) is precisely the unknown quantity, so the apparent performance disappears. A causality test should change a suffix token and verify that earlier prediction logits are unchanged.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    An autoregressive model is first a probability factorization, not a particular neural architecture. Local normalization gives a normalized joint and makes the log-likelihood of a complete ordered observation directly evaluable. Direct conditional training and joint prefix modeling can use the same causal architecture while fitting different probability objects. The chain rule does not reduce the parameter count of a saturated joint: useful models gain statistical strength from justified conditional independence or shared computation. A Markov window changes the distribution family; recurrent state and causal masking describe how allowed prefix information is represented unless their capacity or receptive field imposes an additional restriction. The price remains an imposed order: architecture and representation must enforce it, and ordinary ancestral sampling follows it one decision at a time. “Exact likelihood” must never be expanded into a claim of exact arbitrary inference.

    Retrieval practice: foundations
    1. Why does a product of normalized conditionals define a normalized joint?
    2. Does changing the variable order invalidate the chain rule?
    3. In a shifted decoder, which logit predicts token \(x_t\)?
    4. Name two inference tasks that exact joint likelihood does not make exact.
    5. Why do unrestricted tables for \(T\) \(K\)-ary variables require \(K^T-1\) free parameters?
    6. Which of these is a statistical assumption: a first-order Markov window, a recurrent implementation, or a full causal mask?
    7. In the parity example, why can one recurrent bit succeed where a one-observation Markov window fails?
    8. What changes when a decoder-only training loss masks every context token?

    Solutions. (1) Repeatedly summing the last variable eliminates its normalized conditional; continuing outward leaves one. (2) No. Every ordering gives an exact factorization of the true joint, although learned approximations can differ. (3) Under the convention used in this guide, the logit at position \(t-1\) predicts \(x_t\). (4) Examples include marginalizing missing values, arbitrary conditional sampling, MAP sequence search, and constrained decoding. (5) Position \(t\) has \(K^{t-1}\) prefixes and \(K-1\) free categorical probabilities per prefix; the geometric sum is \((K-1)\sum_{t=1}^{T}K^{t-1}=K^T-1\). (6) The first-order Markov window. Recurrence and a full causal mask are parameterizations until a bounded state, local receptive field, or other constraint removes dependencies. (7) The latest observation does not determine earlier parity, but the recurrent state can update and retain precisely that one-bit sufficient statistic. (8) Context remains visible to target predictions, but its own factors leave the objective. The loss fits the declared conditional target factors rather than the complete joint likelihood of context plus target.
