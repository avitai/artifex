    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Representations 1 — Codec Semantics

    A variational autoencoder is a latent-variable probability model \(p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)\) paired with an approximate posterior \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) and trained through a variational bound on the observed-data likelihood. The encoder is an inference model, the decoder is an observation model, and the ELBO couples their training without making them the same probabilistic object.

    • Latent-Variable Model

      Specify a prior and observation model whose latent variable is marginalized to obtain the data law

    • Amortized Posterior Inference

      Use a learned variational distribution to approximate posterior queries and estimate the training bound

    • Prior-Based Generation

      Draw a latent from the declared prior and then sample from the decoder likelihood

    • Pathwise Estimation

      Differentiate Monte Carlo estimates through reparameterizable latent distributions

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), introduced concurrently by Kingma & Welling (2014) and Rezende, Mohamed & Wierstra (2014), combine neural latent-variable models with variational inference. Unlike a deterministic autoencoder, a VAE specifies a prior, observation model, and approximate posterior, enabling prior sampling and uncertainty-aware latent inference. Interpretability is a separate property that requires assumptions and evaluation. For a detailed tutorial by the original authors, see Kingma & Welling (2019).

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand joint, marginal, and conditional probability; expectations; Gaussian distributions; KL divergence; and Jensen's inequality. Review Latent Variables and Inference if marginalization or posterior inference is unfamiliar; its EM-to-amortized-learning bridge shows exactly which classical operation the encoder replaces. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. write the VAE generative model separately from its inference model;
    2. derive the ELBO and its exact posterior-KL gap;
    3. derive the dataset-averaged ELBO as a divergence between model and inference joints;
    4. calculate the ELBO of a one-dimensional Gaussian example;
    5. explain why reconstruction quality, aggregated-posterior matching, and posterior inference are distinct;
    6. explain the semantics of the decoder likelihood, including its observation scale; and
    7. parameterize and evaluate a full-covariance Gaussian posterior without confusing conditional covariance with aggregate independence; and
    8. distinguish a pathwise gradient from the false claim that stochastic sampling has no gradient estimator.

    Consequences of the model–inference split¤

    The combination of a latent generative model and amortized variational inference creates several useful capabilities, each with a separate condition:

    • Interpolation can be meaningful when the decoder behaves smoothly along the chosen path and that path stays in regions supported by the aggregate posterior.
    • Prior samples can be realistic when the learned aggregate posterior is sufficiently well matched to the prior and the decoder likelihood is appropriate.
    • Representations can become useful or controllable, but vanilla ELBO training does not identify human-interpretable factors without additional assumptions, supervision, inductive bias, or evaluation.

    Constructive interpretation: generation, inference, and reconstruction¤

    The model can be understood through four operations that should not be conflated:

    1. Generation. Draw \(z\sim p(z)\) and then draw \(x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z)\). This two-stage law, not the encoder, defines the model distribution.

    2. Inference. Given an observation \(x\), evaluate or sample from \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) as an approximation to the generally intractable posterior \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\).

    3. Reconstruction. Draw or summarize a latent under \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) and evaluate the decoder. This composes approximate inference with the observation model; it is not the same query as unconditional generation.

    4. Representation analysis. Study the geometry and information content of \(z\) under the aggregate posterior. The ELBO alone does not arrange coordinates according to human concepts or guarantee that paths between encoded examples stay in well-supported regions.

    The encoder returns a distribution rather than a single deterministic code. The KL term penalizes a conditional posterior–prior discrepancy but does not eliminate holes, aggregate mismatch, or decoder misspecification. Prior-sample validity and interpolation quality are therefore empirical properties to measure.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Generative Story¤

    VAEs model the data generation process as a two-step procedure:

    1. Sample latent code: \(z \sim p(z)\) from a simple prior distribution (typically standard normal)
    2. Generate data: \(x \sim p_\theta(x|z)\) using a decoder network parameterized by \(\theta\)

    The likelihood depends on \(\theta\), not on \(\phi\). VAE training learns \(\theta\) by maximizing a variational lower bound on the observed-data likelihood and learns \(\phi\) to supply a useful amortized posterior approximation and gradient estimator for that bound.

    Variational Inference: Why We Need Approximation¤

    Variational inference (Jordan et al., 1999; see Blei et al., 2017 for a modern review) replaces an intractable inference problem with a tractable optimisation problem.

    The true posterior \(p_\theta(z|x)\) tells us what latent code likely generated our data. However, computing it requires:

    \[ p_\theta(z|x) = \frac{p_\theta(x|z)p(z)}{p_\theta(x)} = \frac{p_\theta(x|z)p(z)}{\int p_\theta(x|z')p(z') dz'} \]

    The integral in the denominator (the evidence \(p_\theta(x)\)) is generally intractable for a neural nonlinear decoder, even though special conjugate or linear-Gaussian latent models admit exact inference. VAEs learn a tractable approximate posterior \(q_\phi(z|x)\) (the encoder), trading exact per-observation inference for amortized optimization.

    The ELBO: Evidence Lower BOund¤

    The key insight of VAEs is to maximize a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood called the Evidence Lower BOund (ELBO):

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]

    This inequality states that the log-likelihood is always at least as large as the ELBO. The gap between them equals exactly \(D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p_\theta(z|x))\)—when our approximate posterior perfectly matches the true posterior, there's no gap and we achieve the true likelihood.

    Derivation from First Principles¤

    Starting with the log-likelihood and introducing our approximate posterior:

    \[ \begin{align} \log p_\theta(x) &= \log \int p_\theta(x, z) dz \\ &= \log \int p_\theta(x, z) \frac{q_\phi(z|x)}{q_\phi(z|x)} dz \\ &= \log \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)} \left[\frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] \end{align} \]

    Applying Jensen's inequality (since log is concave):

    \[ \log \mathbb{E}[f(z)] \geq \mathbb{E}[\log f(z)] \]

    We get:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)} \left[\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] \]

    Expanding \(p_\theta(x, z) = p_\theta(x|z)p(z)\):

    \[ \begin{align} \text{ELBO} &= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] + \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\log \frac{p(z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \end{align} \]

    From One Observation to Two Joint Distributions¤

    The pointwise ELBO answers a local question: for this \(x\), how well does \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) approximate the model posterior? Training over a data distribution raises a second question: which joint distribution over data and latents is being matched? Keeping these questions separate prevents three common mistakes:

    1. treating a reconstruction as evidence that prior samples are correct;
    2. treating an aggregated-posterior match as evidence that every posterior is accurate; and
    3. calling every failure an inference failure when the model marginal itself is wrong.

    Let \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\) denote the population data law. It may be replaced by a well-defined empirical mass function for a finite discrete example. The model and inference sides are:

    Table VAE.2. From One Observation to Two Joint Distributions
    Object Definition Question it answers
    Data marginal \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\) How often does the data-generating process produce \(x\)?
    Model joint \(p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)\) Which \((x,z)\) pairs does the generator assign mass or density to?
    Model marginal \(p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz\) What distribution results from prior sampling and decoding?
    Model posterior \(p_\theta(z\mid x)=p_\theta(x,z)/p_\theta(x)\) Under the model, which latents explain an observed \(x\)?
    Inference joint \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)=p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)\) What joint law results from drawing real data and encoding it?
    Aggregated posterior \(q_\phi(z)=\int q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\,dx\) What latent distribution results after averaging encodings over data?
    Reverse inference conditional \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x\mid z)=q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)/q_\phi(z)\) Which real-data examples share an inferred latent?

    Solid arrows construct, marginalize, or condition distributions. The dotted arrow compares two latent marginals; it is not a sampling transition. The observed-space and full-joint comparisons are given by the KL chain rules below.

    The inference joint is not a second generative model. Its \(x\) marginal is fixed to the data law, and its conditional \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is an inference approximation. The model joint is the object used for unconditional generation.

    Measure and support conditions

    The joint-KL identities below assume that the relevant distributions admit densities or mass functions under compatible reference measures and that \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(p_\theta(x,z)\). An empirical collection of point masses is singular with respect to an ordinary continuous density. In that setting, optimize the empirical average directly, or state a population, dequantized, or noised data law before interpreting the expression as a literal joint KL. The support-mismatch chapter develops this distinction.

    Deriving the augmented-space identity¤

    Average the pointwise ELBO under \(p_{\mathcal D}\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} \overline{\mathcal L}(\theta,\phi) &=\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)} \left[\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)\right]\\ &=\mathbb E_{q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)} \left[\log\frac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)} +\log p_{\mathcal D}(x)\right]\\ &=-D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\,\|\,p_\theta(x,z) \right)-H(p_{\mathcal D}). \end{aligned} \]

    The data entropy is constant with respect to \(\theta\) and \(\phi\). Thus, under the stated measure conditions, maximizing the dataset-averaged ELBO is equivalent to minimizing a joint KL in the augmented space \((x,z)\). This is an algebraic equivalence, not merely an analogy.

    The KL chain rule exposes two complementary views of that same joint mismatch:

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z)) &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\|p_\theta(x))\\ &\quad+\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)\right), \end{aligned} \]

    and

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z)) &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z))\\ &\quad+\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x\mid z)\|p_\theta(x\mid z) \right). \end{aligned} \]

    The first decomposition separates observed-space model fit from posterior inference fit. The second separates aggregated-posterior matching from conditional decoder fit. The last conditional KL is not identical to the usual reconstruction NLL: it also contains the conditional entropy of \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x\mid z)\), which changes when the encoder changes. InfoVAE uses these joint-distribution relationships to analyze how VAE objectives balance learning and inference.

    Finally, the per-example prior regularizer has its own exact decomposition:

    \[ \mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) =I_{q}(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)). \]

    It charges both information carried about the observation and aggregate mismatch to the prior. Consequently, neither a small per-example KL nor an aggregate match alone establishes that the representation is informative, that the decoder fits the data, or that the approximate posterior is accurate.

    Worked counterexample: perfect reconstruction, wrong prior samples¤

    Let \(X,Z\in\{0,1\}\) and let the data be balanced: \(p_{\mathcal D}(X=0)=p_{\mathcal D}(X=1)=1/2\). Choose a deterministic encoder \(q(Z=x\mid X=x)=1\) and deterministic decoder \(p_\theta(X=z\mid Z=z)=1\). Reconstructions are perfect. Now choose the mismatched prior

    \[ p(Z=0)=0.1, \qquad p(Z=1)=0.9. \]

    The two joints have support on the same diagonal pairs but assign different probabilities:

    \((0,0)\) \(0.5\) \(0.1\)
    \((0,1)\) \(0\) \(0\)
    \((1,0)\) \(0\) \(0\)
    \((1,1)\) \(0.5\) \(0.9\)

    The expected reconstruction log-likelihood is \(0\) nats, its largest possible value for this discrete deterministic decoder. Yet the aggregate posterior is balanced while the prior is not:

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)) &=\frac12\log\frac{0.5}{0.1} +\frac12\log\frac{0.5}{0.9}\\ &\approx0.5108\ \text{nats}. \end{aligned} \]

    The posterior is exact for both observations, so the posterior inference gap is zero. Nevertheless, prior sampling produces \(X=1\) with probability \(0.9\) instead of the data probability \(0.5\). The average regularizer is

    \[ \begin{aligned} R &\equiv\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z))\\ &=\frac12\log 10+\frac12\log\frac{10}{9}\\ &\approx1.2040,\\ I_q(X;Z)&=\log2\approx0.6931,\\ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z))&\approx0.5108,\\ R&=I_q(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)). \end{aligned} \]

    Therefore \(\overline{\mathcal L}\approx-1.2040\). The joint identity gives the same answer:

    \[ \begin{aligned} -H(p_{\mathcal D}) -D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}\|p_\theta) &=-\log2-0.5108\\ &\approx-1.2040. \end{aligned} \]

    This example isolates the failure: reconstruction and posterior inference are both perfect, while the model marginal and prior-sample frequencies are wrong.

    Where approximation and amortization enter¤

    For a fixed \(x\) and model \(\theta\), let \(\mathcal Q\) be the local variational family and let \(\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)\) be its best attainable ELBO. Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x),\\ G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x) &=\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x)+G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x). \end{aligned} \]

    The first term remains when the family cannot express the model posterior. The second remains when the shared encoder does not return the best member of that family for this observation. In a finite experiment, incomplete local or global optimization can contaminate the measured second term, so report optimization separately. This decomposition follows the analysis of Cremer, Li, and Duvenaud; more recent theory gives model-dependent conditions under which amortized and per-instance variational optima can coincide (Margossian and Blei, 2024).

    The augmented-space view contains the realized posterior gap averaged over data, but it does not collapse all errors into inference. Even zero approximation and amortization gaps leave the observed-space model mismatch in the first chain-rule decomposition—as the binary counterexample demonstrates. Conversely, matching \(p_\theta(x)\) does not certify that \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is accurate.

    A diagnostic quartet

    Evaluate four objects separately: reconstruction or conditional log-likelihood under encoded latents; prior samples and the model marginal; aggregate-code mismatch \(q_\phi(z)\) versus \(p(z)\); and posterior inference using tighter bounds, per-instance refinement, exact small cases, or other query-appropriate checks. No one member of the quartet certifies the other three.

    Two Interpretable Terms¤

    The ELBO naturally decomposes into two competing objectives:

    1. Reconstruction term¤

    \[ \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] \]
    • Measures how well we can reconstruct the input from sampled latent codes.
    • Encourages the model to preserve information.
    • Higher is better (less negative).

    2. KL divergence¤

    \[ D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]
    • Measures how close each learned encoding distribution is to the prior.
    • Regularises each approximate posterior relative to the prior; smoothness and semantic structure are possible outcomes, not identities of the KL term.
    • Charges information and mismatch relative to the chosen prior; it does not by itself enforce aggregate-posterior equality or semantic organization.
    • Lower is better (closer to the prior under this term).

    The fundamental trade-off: The reconstruction term rewards information useful to the observation model. The KL term charges rate relative to the prior. Their balance defines a rate–distortion operating point; finite optimisation can produce collapse, aggregate-posterior mismatch, unused dimensions, or irregular decoder geometry rather than an automatically structured and smooth latent space.

    Bits-back interpretation of the ELBO¤

    When \(x\) and the coder state are discrete and both sender and receiver share \(p(z)\), \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\), and \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\), a bits-back code gives the negative ELBO an operational lossless-coding interpretation. The sender uses initial random bits to select \(z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)\), encodes \(x\) under \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\) and \(z\) under \(p(z)\), and the receiver reverses those operations and recovers the sampling information under \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\). The ideal net cost is

    \[ \begin{aligned} -\operatorname{ELBO}_2(x) &=\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\!\left[ -\log_2p_\theta(x,z)\right.\\ &\qquad\left.+\log_2q_\phi(z\mid x) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    This statement does not turn every trained VAE into a compressor. A practical system must discretize the relevant distributions, keep probability tables synchronized, seed the coder with bits, and amortize startup and termination overhead across messages. Posterior approximation error remains part of the rate. The information and coding volume derives the operational accounting and its boundaries; the objective-centered treatment connects it to the ELBO and practical BB-ANS construction.

    Worked example: a scalar ELBO with an exact check¤

    Let the generative model and observation be

    \[ p(z)=\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad p(x\mid z)=\mathcal N(z,1), \qquad x=1. \]

    Choose \(q(z\mid x)=\mathcal N(0.5,0.5)\), where the second argument is the variance. The Gaussian KL is

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p) =\frac12\left(0.5+0.5^2-1-\log0.5\right) \approx0.222. \]

    The quadratic moment is

    \[ \mathbb E_q[(1-z)^2] =(1-0.5)^2+0.5 =0.75. \]

    Therefore

    \[ \mathbb E_q[\log p(x=1\mid z)] =-\frac12\left(\log(2\pi)+0.75\right) \approx-1.294. \]

    Thus \(\mathrm{ELBO}\approx-1.516\). This model has the analytic marginal \(p(x)=\mathcal N(0,2)\), so \(\log p(x=1)\approx-1.516\) as well: the chosen \(q\) is exactly the posterior \(\mathcal N(0.5,0.5)\) and the gap is zero. If we instead choose \(q=\mathcal N(0,1)\), the ELBO becomes strictly lower by \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z\mid x)\|p(z\mid x))\). This finite example makes the bound, its equality condition, and the two different KL terms concrete.

    Architecture Components¤

    Encoder: Variational Posterior \(q_\phi(z|x)\)¤

    The encoder is a neural network that maps inputs to parameters of a probability distribution over latent codes:

    \[ q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(z; \mu_\phi(x), \sigma^2_\phi(x) \mathbf{I}) \]

    For a diagonal Gaussian (most common choice), the encoder outputs:

    • Mean \(\mu_\phi(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d\) - the center of the latent distribution
    • Log-variance \(\log \sigma^2_\phi(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d\) - the spread/uncertainty

    Why output log-variance? Numerical stability. Variance must be positive, and learning \(\log \sigma^2\) allows the network to output any real number while ensuring \(\sigma^2 = \exp(\log \sigma^2) > 0\).

    Beyond a diagonal Gaussian posterior¤

    A diagonal Gaussian makes the coordinates of \(Z\) conditionally independent given one input under \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\). It does not imply that coordinates are independent under the aggregate posterior \(q_\phi(z)\), and it says still less about semantic or causal factors. The diagonal choice is popular because the encoder emits \(O(d)\) values and both sampling and the standard-normal KL are coordinatewise. It is a computational restriction on the inference family, not a discovered property of the data.

    For a full-covariance Gaussian, let the encoder emit a lower-triangular matrix \(L_\phi(x)\) with a positive diagonal, for example \(L_{ii}=\operatorname{softplus}(a_{ii})+\varepsilon\). Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_\phi(x)&:=L_\phi(x)L_\phi(x)^\top,\\ q_\phi(z\mid x) &=\mathcal N\!\left(z;\mu_\phi(x),\Sigma_\phi(x)\right),\\ &z=\mu_\phi(x)+L_\phi(x)\epsilon,\\ &\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I). \end{aligned} \]

    For \(p(z)=\mathcal N(0,I)\), the KL is

    \[ \begin{aligned} &D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)\right)\\ &\quad=\frac12\left[ \|\mu\|_2^2+\|L\|_F^2-d\right.\\[-2pt] &\qquad\left.-2\sum_{i=1}^{d}\log L_{ii} \right]. \end{aligned} \tag{VAE.1}\label{eq:vae-full-covariance-gaussian-kl} \]

    The trace, mean, dimension, and log-determinant contributions are all visible in Equation \(\eqref{eq:vae-full-covariance-gaussian-kl}\). This parameterization needs \(d(d+1)/2\) matrix outputs and \(O(d^2)\) work for a triangular matrix-vector product. A dense Cholesky factorization costs \(O(d^3)\) when the model first constructs an unrestricted covariance matrix; that factorization is unnecessary when the encoder emits \(L\) directly. Either route still has quadratic storage, which can be prohibitive for large latent groups. Low-rank-plus-diagonal covariance, sparse precision matrices, graph-structured factors, and normalizing-flow posteriors trade different dependencies, costs, and density-evaluation requirements.

    Worked two-dimensional posterior. Take

    \[ \mu=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \qquad L=\begin{bmatrix} 1&0\\[2pt] \tfrac12&\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2} \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Then \(LL^\top\) has unit marginal variances and covariance \(1/2\). Because \(\|\mu\|^2=1\), \(\|L\|_F^2=2\), and \(\det(LL^\top)=3/4\),

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|\mathcal N(0,I)) =\frac12\left(1-\log\frac34\right) \approx0.6438\ \text{nats}. \]

    A diagonal posterior cannot represent this conditional correlation. A flow or structured posterior may represent much more than a single dense Gaussian, but greater expressiveness does not by itself remove amortization or optimization error. The inference guide separates those gaps and compares structured variational families.

    Decoder: Likelihood \(p_\theta(x|z)\)¤

    The decoder is a neural network that maps latent codes back to data space:

    \[ p_\theta(x|z) = \mathcal{N}(x; \mu_\theta(z), \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}) \quad \text{or} \quad \text{Bernoulli}(x; f_\theta(z)) \]

    The choice of output distribution depends on your data:

    • Gaussian (continuous): For real-valued images (often simplified to MSE loss with fixed variance)
    • Bernoulli (binary): For binary images or features (use sigmoid + BCE loss)
    • Categorical: For discrete data (use softmax + cross-entropy)

    The Reparameterization Trick¤

    The Problem: Backpropagation Through Sampling¤

    We need gradients of \(\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[f(z)]\) with respect to \(\phi\). Treating a sampled value as a constant and differentiating only \(f(z)\) omits how its distribution depends on \(\phi\). Stochastic gradients are still possible through score-function estimators, but they often have high variance. For reparameterizable distributions, the pathwise estimator exposes a lower-variance differentiable route.

    The Solution: Separate Randomness from Parameters¤

    Instead of sampling \(z\) directly from \(q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x), \sigma^2_\phi(x))\), reparameterize as:

    \[ z = \mu_\phi(x) + \sigma_\phi(x) \odot \epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I}) \]

    where \(\odot\) denotes element-wise multiplication.

    Why this works:

    1. The randomness (\(\epsilon\)) is now independent of our parameters \(\phi\)
    2. Gradients flow through the deterministic operations \(\mu_\phi\) and \(\sigma_\phi\)
    3. The expectation is taken over a parameter-independent noise law.
    4. We can approximate this expectation with Monte Carlo sampling: sample \(\epsilon\), compute gradients, average

    The transformed expectation is

    \[ \mathbb{E}_{p(\epsilon)}[f(g_\phi(\epsilon, x))], \]

    where \(g_\phi\) is deterministic in \(\epsilon\) and \(x\) for fixed parameters.

    This idea — proposed simultaneously by Kingma & Welling, 2014 and Rezende et al., 2014 — enabled practical VAE training and has since become fundamental to probabilistic deep learning.

    Counterexample: deterministic reconstruction is not a VAE likelihood

    If a decoder returns only \(\hat x=f_\theta(z)\) and training minimizes an unscaled MSE without declaring \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\), the code may resemble a VAE but its reported loss is not yet a complete ELBO. Choosing a Gaussian observation variance supplies the missing probabilistic semantics; changing that variance changes the relative rate–distortion trade-off even if the network output is unchanged.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A VAE contains two directional constructions with different roles. The joint \(p_\theta(x,z)\) defines generation; the conditional \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) approximates posterior inference. Pointwise, the ELBO gap is a posterior KL. Across a compatible data law, maximizing the average ELBO minimizes a joint KL between the inference and model joints. Its chain-rule decompositions separate model-marginal fit, posterior inference, aggregated-posterior matching, and decoder-conditional fit. Reparameterization changes the gradient estimator, not the model distribution. Decoder output types and scale parameters are part of the likelihood, not implementation decoration. A diagonal encoder posterior is a conditional factorization choice; it neither proves aggregate independence nor identifies semantic factors.

    Retrieval practice: VAE foundations
    1. Which distribution is used for unconditional generation: \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) or \(p(z)\)?
    2. When is the ELBO equal to \(\log p_\theta(x)\)?
    3. Does a diagonal \(q\) imply that the aggregated latent coordinates are statistically independent over the dataset?
    4. What probabilistic assumption turns fixed-scale MSE into a reconstruction NLL?
    5. Can perfect reconstruction and exact posterior inference guarantee correct prior samples?
    6. What two terms appear when the augmented-space joint KL is decomposed in the observed direction?
    7. If an encoder emits a dense Cholesky factor directly, which costs remain quadratic and which cubic factorization can be avoided?

    Solutions. (1) Draw \(z\) from the prior \(p(z)\), then \(x\) from the decoder likelihood. (2) When \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) equals the exact posterior almost everywhere relevant to the observation. (3) No; conditional diagonal covariance does not imply aggregate independence. (4) An isotropic Gaussian decoder with a fixed, declared observation variance and reduction convention. (5) No. Reconstruction uses encoded latents, whereas generation uses the prior; the model marginal can remain wrong even when the posterior inference gap is zero. (6) Observed-space model mismatch \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\|p_\theta(x))\) and the data-averaged posterior KL. (7) Storing and multiplying the triangular factor remain \(O(d^2)\); the \(O(d^3)\) Cholesky factorization of a separately constructed dense covariance is avoided.

    Loss Function and Training¤

    The VAE loss is derived directly from the negative ELBO:

    \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi; x) = -\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] + D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]

    Loss Function and Training: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the ELBO and Gaussian reparameterization from Chapter 1 plus basic automatic differentiation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. implement a one-sample Monte Carlo negative ELBO with explicit RNG ownership;
    2. derive the diagonal-Gaussian KL and validate its zero case;
    3. keep batch, observation-dimension, and latent-dimension reductions consistent;
    4. diagnose posterior collapse without relying on one arbitrary KL range;
    5. distinguish optimization failure, inference-family limitation, and model misspecification; and
    6. state exactly what ELBO optimization does and does not establish about maximum likelihood.

    Practical Implementation¤

    For Gaussian encoder \(q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2\mathbf{I})\) and standard normal prior \(p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\):

    Negative reconstruction log-likelihood for an isotropic Gaussian decoder \(p_\theta(x\mid z)=\mathcal N(\mu_\theta(z),\sigma_x^2I)\) with fixed, declared \(\sigma_x^2\):

    \[ -\log p_\theta(x\mid z) =\frac{1}{2\sigma_x^2}\|x-\mu_\theta(z)\|_2^2 +\frac{D}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_x^2). \]

    MSE is therefore proportional to this term only after fixing the observation variance and reduction convention. Omitting the scale or switching between sum and mean silently changes the relative weight of reconstruction and KL.

    KL Divergence (closed-form for Gaussians; see Kingma & Welling, 2014, Appendix B):

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{d} (1 + \log \sigma_j^2 - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2) \]

    Total Loss:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{KL}} \]

    Training Algorithm¤

    import jax
import jax.numpy as jnp
from flax import nnx
import optax

# Create optimizer (wrt=nnx.Param required in NNX 0.11.0+)
optimizer = nnx.Optimizer(vae, optax.adam(1e-3), wrt=nnx.Param)
rng_key = jax.random.key(0)

for epoch in range(num_epochs):
    for batch in dataloader:
        rng_key, sample_key = jax.random.split(rng_key)

        def loss_fn(vae):
            # Forward pass
            mu, log_var = vae.encoder(batch)

            # Reparameterization trick
            epsilon = jax.random.normal(sample_key, mu.shape)
            z = mu + jnp.exp(0.5 * log_var) * epsilon

            # Decode
            x_recon = vae.decoder(z)

            # ELBO terms — use a *consistent reduction*: sum over non-batch
            # axes (pixels for recon, latent dims for KL), then mean over the
            # batch axis. Mixing jnp.mean for recon with jnp.sum for KL is the
            # single most common bug in VAE code: it leaves the two terms on
            # wildly different scales and the KL silently dominates.
            obs_variance = 1.0  # fixed Gaussian likelihood; declare/tune explicitly
            squared_error = jnp.mean(
                jnp.sum((x_recon - batch) ** 2, axis=tuple(range(1, batch.ndim)))
            )
            recon_loss = squared_error / (2.0 * obs_variance)
            kl_per_sample = -0.5 * jnp.sum(
                1 + log_var - mu ** 2 - jnp.exp(log_var), axis=-1
            )
            kl_loss = jnp.mean(kl_per_sample)

            return recon_loss + kl_loss

        # Gradient update (NNX 0.11.0+ API)
        loss, grads = nnx.value_and_grad(loss_fn)(vae)
        optimizer.update(vae, grads)

    Key Training Metrics to Monitor¤

    No one scalar is the primary metric for every VAE use. Monitor at least three layers:

    • Optimization. Plot the stochastic training objective, gradient norms, learning-rate schedule, and non-finite updates. A minibatch curve need not decrease monotonically; Monte Carlo noise, minibatch variation, and deliberate schedules can all move it upward temporarily.
    • Probabilistic fit. Report a held-out ELBO with the declared observation likelihood, reduction, preprocessing, and number of latent samples. When density quality matters, add a held-out importance bound or another declared likelihood estimator. A training ELBO is neither exact test likelihood nor evidence of calibrated posterior predictions.
    • Rate and representation. Report total and groupwise KL, active units, aggregate-prior discrepancy, and task-specific probes or interventions. No raw range such as 5–20 nats transfers across latent dimensions, likelihoods, datasets, or objective weights.
    • Generated and reconstructed observations. Evaluate prior samples, posterior reconstructions, conditional samples, and coverage separately. A falling reconstruction NLL need not improve prior generation, and an image similarity score need not improve density estimation.

    “All near zero” is evidence that the approximate posterior carries little information relative to the prior under the chosen parameterization. It is not automatically a problem: if the observation is predictable without \(z\), the population optimum may legitimately ignore it. Diagnose collapse relative to the modeling goal using reconstructions, prior samples, conditional samples, active units, mutual-information estimates with stated estimators, and interventions on decoder capacity.

    What ELBO Optimization Does Not Guarantee¤

    For the separate Fisher geometries of the generative joint, observed marginal, and amortized posterior—and for the pathwise, score-function, and implicit estimators used to train them—see Gradients and Geometry in Generative Modeling.

    For a fixed generative parameter \(\theta\), the evidence identity gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} &\max_{q\in\mathcal Q}\mathcal L(\theta,q;x)\\ &\quad=\log p_\theta(x)\\ &\qquad-\min_{q\in\mathcal Q}\\[-2pt] &\qquad D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl( q(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x) \bigr). \end{aligned} \]

    If the exact posterior belongs to \(\mathcal Q\) and the variational optimization finds it, the gap is zero. Otherwise, even a global maximizer of the restricted ELBO can prefer a different \(\theta\) from a maximum-likelihood solution because model fit and posterior approximability are optimized together. With a neural decoder, amortized encoder, minibatches, and Monte Carlo gradients, the joint problem is also non-convex and stochastic. Convergence of a training curve does not guarantee a local maximum of the ELBO, much less a local maximum-likelihood estimate.

    This boundary is not an objection to variational learning. It identifies what must be measured: held-out model fit, the approximation gap to a stronger per-example family, the amortization gap between shared and locally refined inference, and finite optimization or estimator error. The joint-distribution view in Chapter 1 and the approximate-inference chapter provide the corresponding diagnostic constructions.

    Worked reduction audit¤

    Take a batch of two \(2\times2\) scalar images and a two-dimensional latent. Suppose per-example summed Gaussian reconstruction NLLs (dropping the same fixed constant) are \((4,8)\) and KLs are \((1,3)\). The batch-mean negative ELBO is

    \[ \frac{(4+1)+(8+3)}{2}=8. \]

    If reconstruction error were instead averaged over all eight pixels, it would become \(12/8=1.5\), while leaving KL as a batch mean gives \(2\), producing 3.5. Neither reduction is inherently illegal, but they encode different effective weights. A paper or implementation that calls both simply “reconstruction + KL” is underspecified. State whether each term is summed or averaged over examples, pixels/features, time, and latent groups.

    Unit tests for a VAE loss¤

    Before a large training run, test these small invariants:

    • mu = 0 and log_var = 0 must give zero KL to a standard normal prior.
    • Increasing one mean coordinate from 0 to 1 must add \(1/2\) nat to that example's KL.
    • Two independent Monte Carlo samples should differ when RNG keys differ and repeat when the key is replayed intentionally.
    • Duplicating every example in a batch should leave a batch-mean loss unchanged.
    • Reshaping observation axes without changing values should leave a summed-per-example reconstruction term unchanged.
    • Gradients and parameters should be finite after the optimizer update; finite loss alone is insufficient.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    The negative ELBO is simple to write and easy to change accidentally. Observation scale, reduction axes, Monte Carlo samples, and RNG splitting all alter the estimator or effective objective. Diagnostics must identify whether the model ignores latents, the inference family misses the posterior, or the decoder likelihood fits the data poorly; one scalar ELBO cannot separate those causes. A restricted, amortized, stochastically optimized ELBO is a principled objective, not a guarantee that training reached a maximum-likelihood solution.

    Retrieval practice: VAE training
    1. What KL results from \(q(z\mid x)=\mathcal N(0,I)\) and \(p(z)=\mathcal N(0,I)\)?
    2. Why can mean-pixel reconstruction plus summed-latent KL change with image resolution?
    3. Is a zero KL dimension necessarily a bug?
    4. Why should the sampling key be split outside the differentiated loss closure each step?
    5. Can a global optimum of a restricted ELBO differ from the maximum-likelihood parameter?

    Solutions. (1) Zero. (2) Averaging reconstruction removes its dimensional scaling while summed KL retains latent scaling, changing their relative weight as resolution changes. (3) No; it can be unused at the optimum, though widespread collapse may violate the representation goal. (4) It makes stochastic draws explicit and prevents accidental reuse of identical noise across updates while keeping differentiation over model parameters clear. (5) Yes. When the exact posterior is outside the variational family, the optimized bound subtracts a parameter-dependent approximation gap, so its preferred \(\theta\) need not maximize marginal likelihood.

    Bridge to Chapter 3. When the basic objective fails to yield the desired rate, representation, or reconstruction behavior, variants modify priors, posteriors, bottlenecks, and objective weights. Each modification must be tied to the failure it is meant to address.

    VAE Variants: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the rate–distortion interpretation of the ELBO and the reduction audit from Chapter 2. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. identify exactly which term β-VAE, β-TCVAE, FactorVAE, InfoVAE, MMD-VAE, AAE, CVAE, and VQ-VAE modify;
    2. distinguish conditional factorization, aggregate-posterior independence, and semantic disentanglement;
    3. distinguish a conditional VAE from a joint multimodal latent-variable model;
    4. derive Gaussian product-of-experts updates for any observed-modality subset without counting the prior twice;
    5. write labeled and unlabeled bounds for a semi-supervised VAE;
    6. derive the VQ straight-through/codebook/commitment updates and diagnose codebook underuse;
    7. separate posterior collapse from a valid low-rate optimum;
    8. explain why matching \(q_\phi(z)\) to \(p(z)\) does not determine mutual information or per-example inference; and
    9. select interventions through controlled ablations rather than modality-wide hyperparameter rules.

    β-VAE: Disentangled Representations¤

    β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017) modifies the objective to encourage disentanglement, where individual latent dimensions capture independent factors of variation:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\beta} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]

    Effect of β:

    • β = 1: Standard VAE (no additional emphasis on disentanglement)
    • β > 1: Stronger rate penalty often reduces encoded information and can improve some disentanglement metrics on selected factorized datasets; neither independence nor semantic axes are guaranteed
    • β < 1: Weaker rate penalty often improves reconstruction and latent usage, but moves away from the ordinary ELBO; its effect on semantic structure is empirical

    Use \(\beta=1\) as the ordinary-ELBO baseline, then sweep values on a declared grid against reconstruction, rate, prior sampling, and the downstream representation metric. Values reported for dSprites or a text decoder are historical protocol points, not transferable image/text defaults. Burgess et al. (2018) analyse capacity control and propose annealing a target capacity \(C\); this changes the optimization path and still requires identifiability assumptions.

    Applications:

    • Interpretable representations for analysis and visualization
    • Fairness research only when leakage, downstream harms, and affected-group metrics are explicitly tested; factorization does not remove sensitive information by construction
    • Controllable generation by manipulating specific latent factors

    Beta-TCVAE and FactorVAE: Targeting Total Correlation¤

    The expected per-example KL decomposes into mutual information, aggregate total correlation, and dimension-wise prior mismatch under the stated factorized prior. These components affect rate and representation differently; total correlation targets aggregate dependence, but no one term alone guarantees semantic disentanglement. Two variants emphasize it:

    • FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih, 2018) trains a discriminator to estimate the total correlation \(\mathrm{TC}(z) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\,\|\,\prod_j q_\phi(z_j))\) and adds a \(\gamma\cdot\mathrm{TC}\) penalty on top of the standard ELBO.
    • β-TCVAE (Chen et al., 2018) decomposes the KL term in closed form into mutual information, total correlation, and dimension-wise KL, and re-weights only the TC component:
    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) = \underbrace{I_q(x;z)}_{\text{MI}} + \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|\textstyle\prod_j q_\phi(z_j))}_{\text{TC}} + \underbrace{\sum_j D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z_j)\|p(z_j))}_{\text{dim-wise KL}} \]

    β-TCVAE reported favorable disentanglement–reconstruction trade-offs relative to its evaluated baselines, and introduced the Mutual Information Gap (MIG). The estimator, dataset factors, hyperparameters, and random seeds are part of that empirical result.

    Aggregate-Posterior Objectives: InfoVAE, MMD-VAE, and AAE¤

    The ordinary expected prior KL charges two quantities at once:

    \[ \begin{aligned} &\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)\right)\\ &\quad=I_q(X;Z) +D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)\right). \end{aligned} \]

    An objective may therefore change the amount of information carried about each record without changing how strongly the aggregate code distribution is matched to the prior. InfoVAE makes these controls explicit. Define the population reconstruction, expected conditional KL, and aggregate discrepancy as

    \[ \begin{aligned} R&:=\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)} [\log p_\theta(x\mid z)],\\ K&:=\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)\right),\\ M&:=D\!\left(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)\right). \end{aligned} \]

    One parameterization of the InfoVAE objective is then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L_{\mathrm{InfoVAE}} ={}&R-(1-\alpha)K\\ &-(\alpha+\lambda-1)M. \end{aligned} \]

    Here \(D\) is a chosen aggregate-distribution discrepancy. Setting \(\alpha=0\) and \(\lambda=1\) recovers the ordinary ELBO because the coefficient of \(D\) becomes zero. Other coefficients generally define a different objective, not another bound on \(\log p_\theta(x)\). The population proposition in the InfoVAE paper assumes a strict divergence, \(\alpha<1\), \(\lambda>0\), a fixed bounded mutual information, and sufficiently expressive distributions. It does not guarantee the behavior of a finite discriminator, kernel estimator, neural family, or optimizer.

    MMD-VAE: sample the distribution you claim to match¤

    MMD-VAE replaces the intractable aggregate KL by maximum mean discrepancy. Given \(m\) aggregate-posterior samples \(z_i\sim q_\phi(z)\) and \(m\) independent prior samples \(\tilde z_i\sim p(z)\), an unbiased squared-MMD estimator is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat{\operatorname{MMD}}_{u}^{\,2} ={}&\frac{1}{m(m-1)}\\[-2pt] &\quad\times\sum_{i\ne j}k(z_i,z_j)\\ &+\frac{1}{m(m-1)}\\[-2pt] &\quad\times\sum_{i\ne j}k(\tilde z_i,\tilde z_j)\\ &-\frac{2}{m^2} \sum_{i,j}k(z_i,\tilde z_j). \end{aligned} \]

    To sample \(q_\phi(z)\), first sample a record and then sample \(z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)\). Substituting posterior means \(\mu_\phi(x)\) matches the distribution of deterministic means instead; it is a different target unless \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is itself deterministic. A characteristic kernel makes the population MMD identify equality of the two distributions. Kernel choice, bandwidth, finite-batch estimator, and optimization still determine what signal the model receives. In particular, this unbiased finite-sample estimator can be negative even though population \(\operatorname{MMD}^2\) is nonnegative.

    AAE: an adversarial aggregate-matching game¤

    Adversarial Autoencoders combine a reconstruction objective with a discriminator trained to distinguish samples from \(q_\phi(z)\) and \(p(z)\). Under the original logistic game, an unrestricted population-optimal discriminator corresponds to Jensen–Shannon matching. A finite neural discriminator and alternating optimizer provide only an estimated training signal. An AAE can also use a deterministic encoder, so its name does not imply a Gaussian variational posterior or an ordinary ELBO. The required contract is the encoder distribution, reconstruction likelihood or distortion, adversarial loss, prior sampler, and update schedule.

    Worked finite counterexample: the same aggregate, different information¤

    Let \(X\) and the prior \(Z\) both be uniform on \(\{0,1\}\). Compare two encoders:

    1. Record-preserving: \(q_A(z\mid x)=\mathbf 1[z=x]\).
    2. Record-ignoring: \(q_B(z\mid x)=1/2\) for both values of \(z\) and every \(x\).

    Both have the exact aggregate \(q_A(z)=q_B(z)=p(z)\), so every strict aggregate discrepancy is zero. But

    \[ I_{q_A}(X;Z)=\log 2, \qquad I_{q_B}(X;Z)=0. \]

    Encoder A supports zero reconstruction NLL with the deterministic decoder \(x=z\). Under encoder B, the best decoder for this balanced population remains \(p(x\mid z)=1/2\) and pays \(\log2\) nats per record. Aggregate matching is thus neither a collapse cure nor a semantic-representation guarantee. It must be read together with reconstruction, mutual information, inference accuracy, and the intended downstream query.

    Retrieval practice: aggregate-posterior objectives
    1. Can \(D(q_\phi(z)\|p(z))=0\) prove that \(Z\) contains information about \(X\)?
    2. Why is an MMD computed on posterior means generally not an estimator of MMD between \(q_\phi(z)\) and \(p(z)\)?
    3. When does the InfoVAE parameterization displayed above reduce to the ordinary ELBO?
    4. Does adversarial aggregate matching make an AAE a maximum-likelihood VAE?

    Solutions. (1) No; the two binary encoders above have the same perfectly matched aggregate but mutual information \(\log2\) and zero. (2) The aggregate posterior includes conditional sampling noise; posterior means define a different pushforward distribution unless the encoder is deterministic. (3) At \(\alpha=0\) and \(\lambda=1\); the aggregate-discrepancy coefficient is then zero. (4) No. The reconstruction term, discriminator game, variational family, and finite optimization must be stated; ordinary ELBO and likelihood identities do not follow from the AAE label.

    Conditional VAE (CVAE)¤

    Conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015) incorporate additional information \(y\) (class labels, attributes, text descriptions) to enable controlled generation:

    \[ \begin{align} q_\phi(z|x, y) &= \mathcal{N}(z; \mu_\phi(x, y), \sigma^2_\phi(x, y) \mathbf{I}) \\ p_\theta(x|z, y) &= \mathcal{N}(x; \mu_\theta(z, y), \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}) \end{align} \]

    How conditioning works:

    • Concatenation: Append \(y\) to the input before encoding and to \(z\) before decoding — simple but less expressive than feature-wise modulation.
    • Conditional Batch Normalization (De Vries et al., 2017; Dumoulin et al., 2017): Replace BN's affine parameters with \(y\)-dependent values.
    • FiLM (Feature-wise Linear Modulation) (Perez et al., 2018): Scale and shift each feature channel by \(y\)-dependent \((\gamma, \beta)\). Used pervasively in modern conditional generators.
    • Cross-attention: For sequence/text conditions, attend from feature maps onto an encoded \(y\) — the technique that powers Stable Diffusion's text-to-image conditioning.

    Applications:

    • Class-conditional generation: Generate specific digit classes in MNIST or specific ImageNet classes (Mirza & Osindero, 2014).
    • Attribute manipulation: Change hair color, age, expression in face images.
    • Multimodal conditional decoding: use a learned condition in encoder/decoder distributions. DALL·E 1's autoregressive prior over discrete image tokens and latent-diffusion systems are related hybrid pipelines, not examples of the basic CVAE objective.

    Conditional generation is not joint multimodal modeling¤

    A CVAE normally treats \(y\) as supplied side information and models \(p_\theta(x\mid y)\). It need not define \(p(y)\), so it cannot by itself answer joint-density questions about \((x,y)\) or generate \(y\) unconditionally. A multimodal latent-variable model instead defines a joint law over all modalities. A semi-supervised model goes one step further: the same variable \(y\) is observed in some records and latent in others.

    Table VAE.4. Conditional generation is not joint multimodal modeling
    Goal Probabilistic object What must be available at generation or inference time?
    Controlled generation \(p_\theta(x\mid y)\) the condition \(y\)
    Joint multimodal generation \(p_\theta(x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(M)})\) no modality is necessarily supplied
    Cross-modal generation \(p_\theta(x_T\mid x_S)\) an observed subset \(x_S\); targets \(T\) are generated
    Semi-supervised classification and generation \(p_\theta(x,y)\) \(y\) is observed for labeled records and inferred for unlabeled records

    Calling every one of these systems a “conditional VAE” hides different model, inference, and data contracts.

    Multimodal VAEs and Missing Modalities¤

    Let \(X=(X^{(1)},\ldots,X^{(M)})\) denote \(M\) modalities and let \(S\subseteq\{1,\ldots,M\}\) index the modalities observed for one query. A shared-latent multimodal VAE commonly assumes

    \[ p_\theta(z,x^{(1:M)}) =p(z)\prod_{m=1}^{M}p_{\theta_m}(x^{(m)}\mid z). \]

    The factorization says that the modalities are conditionally independent given the declared latent \(Z\). It does not say that raw modalities are marginally independent, nor does it guarantee that one shared latent captures modality-private variation. When private information matters, the model may need modality-specific latents \(u_m\) in addition to \(z\).

    For observed modalities \(x_S\) and target modalities \(T\subseteq\{1,\ldots,M\}\setminus S\), the model's cross-modal predictive distribution is

    \[ p_\theta(x_T\mid x_S) =\int\left[\prod_{m\in T}p_{\theta_m}(x^{(m)}\mid z)\right] p_\theta(z\mid x_S)\,dz. \]

    This is a distribution, not a single filled-in value. Its spread should retain ambiguity left unresolved by the observed modalities.

    Which ELBO is being optimized?¤

    Let \(q_{\phi,S}(z\mid x_S)\) be an inference distribution that consumes only the observed subset. If a training record contains scored values for a set \(A\) with \(S\subseteq A\), then

    \[ \mathcal B_{S\rightarrow A}(x_A) =\mathbb E_{q_{\phi,S}(z\mid x_S)} \left[\sum_{m\in A}\log p_{\theta_m}(x^{(m)}\mid z)\right] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_{\phi,S}(z\mid x_S)\,\|\,p(z)\right). \]
    • With \(A=S\), this is an ELBO for the observed-subset marginal \(\log p_\theta(x_S)\).
    • With a fully paired record and \(A=\{1,\ldots,M\}\), it is a joint-data ELBO whose proposal is restricted to use \(x_S\). Training several such directions teaches cross-generation.
    • A missing target value cannot contribute its reconstruction likelihood merely because a decoder could predict it; the value is unavailable for scoring.
    • Modality weights \(\lambda_m\) can balance gradients or encode utilities, but arbitrary weights generally define a reweighted objective rather than the ordinary joint-likelihood ELBO. Report them as part of the estimand.

    The MVAE construction uses subset-based training so the encoders are exercised on incomplete inputs. An architecture that can syntactically omit an encoder is not thereby trained for every omission pattern.

    Gaussian likelihood messages: the product update¤

    Suppose the shared latent is \(d\)-dimensional, with \(p(z)=\mathcal N(z;\mu_0,\Lambda_0^{-1})\). Let modality \(m\) contribute the Gaussian-shaped likelihood message

    \[ e_m(z;x^{(m)}) \propto \exp\!\left[-\frac12(z-r_m)^\top\Delta\Lambda_m(z-r_m)\right], \qquad \Delta\Lambda_m\succeq0. \]

    Under the shared-coordinate, conditional-independence, Gaussian-message, and positive-semidefinite-precision assumptions, the subset posterior

    \[ q_S(z)\propto p(z)\prod_{m\in S}e_m(z;x^{(m)}) \]

    has natural parameters

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Lambda_S &=\Lambda_0+\sum_{m\in S}\Delta\Lambda_m,\\ \eta_S &=\Lambda_0\mu_0+ \sum_{m\in S}\Delta\Lambda_m r_m,\\ \Sigma_S&=\Lambda_S^{-1}, &\mu_S&=\Sigma_S\eta_S. \end{aligned} \]

    Omitting a missing modality means omitting its likelihood message. It does not mean inserting a zero observation. With no observed modalities, the result is the prior.

    Worked two-modality Gaussian model¤

    Consider the scalar model

    \[ \begin{aligned} Z&\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\\ X^{(1)}\mid Z=z&\sim\mathcal N(z,1),\\ X^{(2)}\mid Z=z&\sim\mathcal N(z,4). \end{aligned} \]

    Suppose the realized observations are \(x^{(1)}=1\) and \(x^{(2)}=-1\). The prior precision is \(1\). Modality 1 contributes precision \(1\) and natural parameter \(1\); modality 2 contributes precision \(1/4\) and natural parameter \(-1/4\). The four possible observation patterns therefore give:

    Table VAE.5. Worked two-modality Gaussian model
    \(S\) \(\Lambda_S\) \(\mu_S\) \(\Sigma_S\)
    \(\varnothing\) \(1\) \(0\) \(1\)
    \(\{1\}\) \(2\) \(1/2\) \(1/2\)
    \(\{2\}\) \(5/4\) \(-1/5\) \(4/5\)
    \(\{1,2\}\) \(9/4\) \(1/3\) \(4/9\)

    The more precise first modality outweighs the conflicting second modality; the mean is not an unweighted average. Missing-modality prediction preserves both latent and decoder uncertainty:

    \[ \begin{aligned} X^{(2)}\mid X^{(1)}=1 &\sim\mathcal N\!\left(\frac12,\frac92\right),\\ X^{(1)}\mid X^{(2)}=-1 &\sim\mathcal N\!\left(-\frac15,\frac95\right). \end{aligned} \]

    A point imputation would conceal this uncertainty and could make a many-valued cross-modal query appear deterministic.

    Do not count the prior twice¤

    The preceding \(e_m\) are likelihood messages, not normalized unimodal posteriors. For exact single-modality posteriors under the same prior, Bayes' rule gives the identity

    \[ p(z\mid x_S) \propto p(z)^{1-|S|}\prod_{m\in S}p(z\mid x^{(m)}). \]

    If encoders instead output \(q_m(z\mid x^{(m)})\approx p(z\mid x^{(m)})\), the corresponding approximate aggregator is

    \[ \widetilde q_S(z) \propto p(z)^{1-|S|}\prod_{m\in S}q_m(z\mid x^{(m)}). \]

    For Gaussian posterior experts \(q_m=\mathcal N(\mu_m,\Lambda_m^{-1})\), this becomes

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Lambda_S &=\sum_{m\in S}\Lambda_m-(|S|-1)\Lambda_0,\\ \eta_S &=\sum_{m\in S}\Lambda_m\mu_m -(|S|-1)\Lambda_0\mu_0. \end{aligned} \]

    The resulting precision must remain positive definite. In the scalar example, the unimodal posteriors are \(q_1=\mathcal N(1/2,1/2)\) and \(q_2=\mathcal N(-1/5,4/5)\). The corrected rule has precision \(2+5/4-1=9/4\) and mean \(1/3\), agreeing with exact Bayes. The naive product \(p(z)q_1(z)q_2(z)\) has precision \(17/4\) and mean \(3/17\); it is spuriously overconfident because it includes the same prior three times.

    Name the expert parameterization

    A formula of the form \(p(z)\prod_m q_m(z\mid x^{(m)})\) is valid when the learned factors are intended as likelihood-like messages. If each factor is advertised as a complete unimodal posterior, the prior correction above is required. The symbol \(q_m\) alone does not settle which object was learned.

    PoE, MoE, mixtures of products, and learned aggregation¤

    There is no universally superior aggregation rule. Each encodes a different geometry and failure mode.

    Table VAE.6. PoE, MoE, mixtures of products, and learned aggregation
    Aggregator Distributional effect Useful behavior Characteristic risk
    Product of likelihood experts Intersects evidence; Gaussian precision accumulates Confident agreement and modular subset inference One overconfident or contradictory expert can dominate; dependence can be counted as independent evidence
    Mixture of posterior experts Forms a weighted union \(\sum_m\pi_mq_m\) Retains alternative modes and weak experts Does not perform Bayesian evidence accumulation; a sample may follow only one expert
    Mixture of subset products Mixes PoE posteriors over modality subsets Trades conjunction against mode coverage All \(2^M\) subsets are expensive without sampling or structure
    Learned set/dependence-aware aggregator Learns interactions and can be permutation invariant Can represent correlated, heterogeneous evidence Needs mask and dependence coverage; loses the closed-form Bayesian interpretation

    MMVAE studies mixture aggregation, while MoPoE-VAE mixes subset-specific products. Later work makes the assumptions more explicit: CoDE-VAE models dependence among experts, and HELVAE places PoE and MoE inside a broader opinion-pooling view. These are peer-reviewed research results, not proofs that one aggregator wins for every modality, mask distribution, or query.

    The observation mask is part of the problem¤

    Let \(R\in\{0,1\}^M\) record which modalities are observed. Three issues must not be conflated:

    Table VAE.7. The observation mask is part of the problem
    Situation Statistical statement Consequence for a generator
    Designed mask or modality dropout \(R\) is chosen by the training protocol Robustness is learned only for masks and frequencies actually sampled
    Missing at random The mechanism can depend on observed values, but not on missing values after conditioning on what is observed Ignoring the mask mechanism in likelihood/Bayesian inference additionally requires the usual parameter-distinctness conditions
    Missing not at random \(R\) still depends on an unobserved value or latent cause after conditioning on observed data Modeling only \(p(x)\) can give biased conditionals; model or sensitivity-analyze \(p(R\mid x,z)\)

    This is the generative-model consequence of the classical missing-data conditions in Rubin (1976). A zero, empty sequence, mask token, and unavailable sensor are different observations. The model must receive an unambiguous mask, and evaluation must preserve the deployment missingness mechanism rather than randomly deleting modalities by default.

    Missing-modality robustness is trained, not inherited

    Training only \(q(z\mid x^{(1)},x^{(2)})\) does not validate \(q(z\mid x^{(1)})\) or \(q(z\mid x^{(2)})\). Exercise the intended subset encoders or mask-aware set encoder during training, or perform query-adaptive inference against the decoder at test time. Even then, extrapolation to unseen masks or a changed missingness mechanism remains an empirical claim.

    The query-adaptive inference approach is an important alternative: optimize a posterior for the particular observed query using the trained decoder instead of assuming a fixed amortized encoder generalizes to every mask. It trades test-time optimization for less dependence on the encoder's training-mask support.

    Training and evaluation contract¤

    For a model intended to support arbitrary modality subsets:

    1. define the modeled joint, any shared/private latents, and the conditional- independence assumptions;
    2. specify whether each encoder emits a likelihood message, posterior expert, mixture component, or learned feature;
    3. sample training masks from a declared distribution, retaining full and single-modality anchor cases when those queries matter;
    4. score only target values actually present in the training record and report every modality likelihood scale or reweighting coefficient;
    5. separate paired, weakly paired, and unpaired records—marginal datasets alone do not identify their coupling without additional assumptions;
    6. evaluate unconditional joint generation, reconstruction, every required cross-generation direction, conditional diversity, calibration, and performance by mask; and
    7. test contradictory inputs and modality ablations so a strong expert cannot silently erase weaker modalities.

    Multimodal ELBOs can also suffer modality collapse, where optimization serves an easy or high-gradient modality while neglecting another. This is distinct from posterior collapse and should be diagnosed with per-modality likelihoods, gradient contributions, cross-generation, and intervention tests; see Javaloy et al. (2022).

    Semi-Supervised VAE: One Label, Two Observation States¤

    Semi-supervised learning is not just a CVAE with some label vectors replaced by zeros. In the M2 model of Kingma et al. (2014), the label is a random variable in the generative model:

    \[ p_\theta(x,y,z)=p(y)p(z)p_\theta(x\mid y,z). \]

    Labeled observation state. Both \(x\) and \(y\) are observed, while \(z\) is latent.

    Unlabeled observation state. Only \(x\) is observed; inference must represent uncertainty about both \(y\) and \(z\).

    Solid arrows show the generative graph; dotted arrows show variational inference. For a labeled pair \((x,y)\), use \(q_\phi(z\mid x,y)\) and the bound

    \[ \mathcal B_L(x,y) =\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x,y)}[\log p_\theta(x\mid y,z)] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x,y)\,\|\,p(z)\right) +\log p(y). \]

    For an unlabeled \(x\), introduce \(q_\phi(y,z\mid x)=q_\phi(y\mid x)q_\phi(z\mid x,y)\). When \(Y\) is a small discrete label set, marginalize it exactly:

    \[ \mathcal B_U(x) =\sum_y q_\phi(y\mid x)\mathcal B_L(x,y) +H\!\left[q_\phi(y\mid x)\right]. \]

    The entropy term is not an optional confidence bonus; it comes from \(-\mathbb E_q\log q_\phi(y\mid x)\) in the unlabeled ELBO. An auxiliary supervised term \(\alpha\log q_\phi(y\mid x)\) is commonly added on labeled records because the generative bound alone may train the classifier weakly. Its coefficient changes the combined objective and must be reported.

    Two misconception checks follow directly:

    • Replacing an unobserved label by \(\arg\max_y q(y\mid x)\) is hard pseudo- labeling, not exact marginalization; it discards class uncertainty and changes the estimator.
    • If the probability that a label is observed depends on the unobserved class after conditioning on \(x\), the ordinary M2 objective does not repair that selection mechanism. The label-observation process must be modeled or sensitivity-analyzed.

    Vector Quantized VAE (VQ-VAE)¤

    VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) turns each continuous encoder vector into a finite index and its associated embedding. This creates two related but different latent objects:

    • the index \(C_j\in\{1,\ldots,K\}\), which a categorical prior models; and
    • the embedding \(E_{C_j}\in\mathbb R^d\), which the decoder consumes.

    For position \(j\), let \(A_j=E_\phi(x)_j\) be the encoder output and let \(\mathcal C=\{e_1,\ldots,e_K\}\) be the codebook. Squared-Euclidean nearest- neighbour quantization gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} c_j(x) &=\arg\min_{k\in\{1,\ldots,K\}} \lVert A_j-e_k\rVert_2^2,\\ Z_{q,j}(x)&=e_{c_j(x)},\\ q_\phi(C_j=k\mid x)&=\mathbf 1\{k=c_j(x)\}. \end{aligned} \]

    The tie rule, distance metric, feature normalization, and search approximation are part of this encoder. An index is not itself a point in Euclidean space: renaming every index while permuting the codebook leaves the codec unchanged, but can invalidate a separately trained prior that expects the old names.

    A probabilistic statement and an optimization recipe¤

    If every position has a uniform categorical prior \(p(C_j=k)=1/K\) and the posterior above is deterministic, then

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(C_j\mid x)\,\|\,p(C_j)\right) =\log K. \]

    For \(T\) positions with a factorized uniform prior, the KL is \(T\log K\) and does not train the encoder. The reconstruction term is therefore the only data-dependent term in that elementary ELBO. The codebook and commitment terms below are auxiliary optimization terms; they should not be silently described as likelihood or KL terms.

    Define the straight-through decoder input

    \[ Z_{q,j}^{\mathrm{ST}} =A_j+\operatorname{sg}\!\left(e_{c_j}-A_j\right), \]

    where \(\operatorname{sg}\) is identity in the forward pass and has zero derivative. The decoder sees \(e_{c_j}\), while automatic differentiation uses \(\partial Z_{q,j}^{\mathrm{ST}}/\partial A_j=I\) and \(\partial Z_{q,j}^{\mathrm{ST}}/\partial e_{c_j}=0\). A common loss is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal J_{\mathrm{codec}} =&-\log p_\theta\!\left(x\mid Z_q^{\mathrm{ST}}\right)\\ &+\alpha\sum_j \left\lVert\operatorname{sg}(A_j)-e_{c_j}\right\rVert_2^2 +\beta\sum_j \left\lVert A_j-\operatorname{sg}(e_{c_j})\right\rVert_2^2. \end{aligned} \]

    The first line may be a declared negative log likelihood or a reconstruction, perceptual, adversarial, or task loss. Only a normalized observation model makes it a likelihood. The second line contains, respectively, the codebook and commitment losses. Their coefficients depend on reduction axes, feature scale, and the other loss terms; values from one implementation are not universal.

    The dashed arrows are backward/update routes, not additional forward edges. Under this construction, reconstruction gradients update the decoder and are copied to the encoder; they do not update the codebook. The codebook loss or an EMA rule supplies the codebook update, and the commitment loss supplies an additional encoder update. A system that routes gradients differently is a different estimator and must say so.

    EMA codebook updates are online clustering¤

    The codebook-loss gradient pulls a selected vector toward the mean of its assigned encoder outputs. An exponential-moving-average implementation tracks the same sufficient statistics. Let \(n_k^{(t)}\) and \(s_k^{(t)}\) be the current minibatch count and sum for code \(k\):

    \[ n_k^{(t)}=\sum_j\mathbf 1\{c_j=k\}, \qquad s_k^{(t)}=\sum_{j:c_j=k}A_j. \]

    With decay \(\gamma\in[0,1)\), update

    \[ \begin{aligned} N_k^{(t)}&=\gamma N_k^{(t-1)}+(1-\gamma)n_k^{(t)},\\ M_k^{(t)}&=\gamma M_k^{(t-1)}+(1-\gamma)s_k^{(t)},\\ e_k^{(t)}&=\frac{M_k^{(t)}}{N_k^{(t)}}. \end{aligned} \]

    Implementations often smooth the denominator, bias-correct early iterates, or use the opposite convention for the number called “decay.” State the exact recurrence. In distributed training, counts and sums must be aggregated across replicas before every replica applies the same update.

    Worked two-code example. Start with scalar codes \(e_1=0\) and \(e_2=4\), statistics \((N_1,M_1)=(2,0)\) and \((N_2,M_2)=(1,4)\), and decay \(\gamma=1/2\). A minibatch produces encoder values \(0.5,1.5,5.0\). Nearest-code assignment sends the first two values to code 1 and the last to code 2, so

    Table VAE.8. EMA codebook updates are online clustering
    Code \(n_k\) \(s_k\) New \(N_k\) New \(M_k\) New \(e_k\)
    1 \(2\) \(2\) \(2\) \(1\) \(0.5\)
    2 \(1\) \(5\) \(1\) \(4.5\) \(4.5\)

    The update retains history: code 1 does not jump to the current batch mean \(1.0\). If a code receives no assignment, both its count and sum decay; their ratio does not move toward new data. EMA alone therefore does not revive a dead code. Reinitialization, split/merge rules, usage regularization, changed geometry, or a different quantizer must be evaluated as separate interventions.

    The general discrete-gradient problem¤

    VQ uses deterministic nearest-neighbour assignments, but a VAE may instead use a stochastic categorical posterior. Let

    \[ J(\phi)=\mathbb E_{C\sim q_\phi}[F_\phi(C)] =\sum_{c}q_\phi(c)F_\phi(c). \]

    Whenever differentiation and summation may be interchanged,

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E_q\!\left[ \nabla_\phi F_\phi(C) +F_\phi(C)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(C) \right]. \]

    This identity exposes the choices rather than claiming that discrete variables cannot be optimized with gradients.

    Exact finite example. Let three categories have \(q=(0.2,0.5,0.3)\) and losses \(F=(4,1,2)\). Then \(J=1.9\). If \(a\) are the categorical logits, softmax differentiation gives

    \[ \frac{\partial J}{\partial a_k}=q_k(F_k-J), \qquad \nabla_aJ=(0.42,-0.45,0.03). \]

    These exact values are a useful unit-test oracle. Enumeration requires three decoder evaluations here, but a joint state of \(L\) unrestricted categorical variables has \(K^L\) configurations. Dynamic programming or local enumeration can still be exact when the factorization permits it.

    For sampled hard categories, the score-function estimator uses

    \[ \widehat g_{\mathrm{SF}} =\nabla_\phi F_\phi(C) +\bigl(F_\phi(C)-b\bigr)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(C), \qquad C\sim q_\phi. \]

    A baseline \(b\) that does not depend on the sampled category, conditional on the information already observed, leaves the expectation unchanged and can reduce variance. Learned baselines, leave-one-out baselines, Rao–Blackwellization, antithetic coupling, and relaxation-based control variates differ in cost and assumptions. NVIL, VIMCO, REBAR, and RELAX are representative constructions. “Unbiased” describes the named gradient target under its conditions; it does not mean low variance or low finite-compute error.

    Gumbel–Softmax and hard straight-through samples¤

    For categorical probabilities \(\pi_1,\ldots,\pi_K\), draw independent \(U_k\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and set \(G_k=-\log[-\log U_k]\). The Gumbel-max rule

    \[ H=\operatorname{onehot}\!\left( \arg\max_k\{\log\pi_k+G_k\} \right) \]

    is an exact categorical sample. The hard argmax remains nondifferentiable. Gumbel–Softmax and the Concrete distribution replace it by a simplex-valued sample

    \[ Y_{\tau,k} =\frac{\exp[(\log\pi_k+G_k)/\tau]} {\sum_{r=1}^{K}\exp[(\log\pi_r+G_r)/\tau]}, \qquad \tau>0. \]

    The decoder may consume the mixture \(\sum_kY_{\tau,k}e_k\), which permits a pathwise gradient. At finite temperature, however, the decoder is trained on interior simplex points rather than categorical vertices. It therefore optimizes a relaxed objective, not the original hard-discrete expectation. As \(\tau\) decreases, samples concentrate near vertices, while derivatives carry a \(1/\tau\) scale and saturate away from close competitions. Low temperature is not a free route to an unbiased, low-variance gradient.

    A hard-forward, soft-backward construction uses

    \[ Y_{\mathrm{ST}} =\operatorname{sg}(H-Y_\tau)+Y_\tau. \]

    The forward value is \(H\) and the backward Jacobian is that of \(Y_\tau\). This removes the decoder's soft-input mismatch in the forward pass but does not make the gradient unbiased. Likewise, copying an identity derivative through a hard binary threshold is only a surrogate: if \(C\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(\sigma(a))\) and \(F(C)=C\), the true derivative is \(\sigma(a)[1-\sigma(a)]\), whereas an identity straight-through rule returns \(1\). A straight-through estimator can work well without being the derivative of the declared discrete objective.

    Learning the code prior changes the model¤

    After fitting a codec, encode the training set into index sequences \(c(x)\) and fit a prior by minimizing

    \[ \mathcal J_{\mathrm{prior}}(\psi) =-\mathbb E_{x\sim\mathcal D}\log p_\psi(c(x)). \]

    The resulting probabilistic generator is

    \[ p_{\psi,\theta}(x) =\sum_c p_\psi(c)\,p_\theta(x\mid e_c), \]

    when the decoder defines a normalized conditional distribution. With a deterministic renderer it is instead a pushforward sampler unless an observation model is added. An autoregressive, masked, diffusion, flow, or other categorical model can serve as \(p_\psi\); the family name does not follow from the codec.

    Training this prior after freezing the codec does not retroactively turn the first-stage reconstruction-plus-auxiliary loss into the joint ELBO for the learned prior. Joint fine-tuning is possible, but the prior loss reaches the encoder only through an explicitly declared estimator, and a moving tokenizer changes the prior's target data. Checkpoint compatibility therefore includes encoder, codebook, index ordering, latent shape, preprocessing, and decoder.

    Alternatives and current research boundaries¤

    • Finite Scalar Quantization (Peer-reviewed research) constructs an implicit product codebook from fixed scalar levels and removes learned nearest-neighbour code vectors. Its reported robustness does not imply that every factorized code geometry is suitable for every source or downstream prior.
    • ReinMax and Beyond ReinMax (Peer-reviewed research, with the latter accepted by TMLR in 2026) study increasingly accurate or variance-controlled straight-through-family estimators for stochastic discrete latents. Their benchmark results do not make one estimator uniformly best across support size, hierarchy, and compute.
    • VQ-Transplant (ICLR 2026) and IVQ (ICML 2026) are peer-reviewed research on efficient module replacement and structured codebooks, respectively. They address codebook construction and adaptation, not the general discrete-gradient problem.
    • Diffusion-based categorical reparameterization (Gourevitch et al., 2026) is a workshop paper. It belongs in the evidence map as a developing relaxation, not as an established replacement for enumeration, score-function, or Gumbel-based estimators.

    Applications¤

    • DALL·E 1 (Ramesh et al., 2021) uses a transformer prior over discrete image codes. Later DALL·E systems should not be inferred to use the same model from the shared product name.
    • Jukebox (Dhariwal et al., 2020) uses hierarchical VQ representations for raw audio and separate priors over their code streams.
    • VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) combines quantization with perceptual and adversarial codec losses and a transformer prior. This is a hybrid objective, not evidence that quantization itself makes reconstructions sharp.
    • Neural audio codecs use residual or grouped codebooks whose order, bandwidth, and delayed generation patterns become part of the downstream prior's factorization.

    Training Dynamics and Common Challenges¤

    Posterior Collapse¤

    What is it?

    The encoder learns to ignore the input, producing latent codes that are essentially identical to the prior \(q_\phi(z|x) \approx p(z)\). The decoder learns to generate data without using latent information, defeating the purpose of the model.

    Candidate diagnostics:

    • per-group KL and active-unit profiles across data rather than only a total scalar
    • decoder sensitivity to replacing \(z\) with a prior draw or posterior mean
    • estimates of \(I_q(X;Z)\) with estimator and bias limitations stated
    • reconstruction and conditional-generation changes under latent interventions

    Why does it happen?

    Powerful autoregressive decoders (especially in text VAEs) can model \(p(x)\) without needing latent information. The KL term drives encodings toward the prior, and if the decoder doesn't need \(z\), the KL term wins.

    Interventions to test with controlled ablations: no intervention has a universal effectiveness ranking. Its effect depends on the decoder, data, likelihood, latent grouping, optimiser, and evaluation target.

    1. KL Annealing: Start with \(\beta=0\) and ramp toward the intended objective so the encoder can become informative before receiving the full KL pressure.
    2. Linear (Bowman et al., 2015): beta = min(1.0, step / warmup_steps). The warmup fraction is a protocol choice, not a transferable constant.
    3. Cyclical (Fu et al., 2019): repeat the 0→1 ramp. It improved the evaluated NLP settings in the original paper, but it changes the optimisation path and is not guaranteed to dominate a well-tuned monotonic schedule.

    4. Sigmoid / monotonic schedules are also common. Report the complete schedule and compare against training the target objective without annealing.

    5. Free bits (Kingma et al., 2016): make the objective insensitive to reducing a latent group's average KL below a chosen budget \(\lambda_g\):

      \[ \widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_{\mathrm{KL}} =\sum_g\max\!\left( \lambda_g, \mathbb E_{x\sim\mathcal D} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z_g\mid x)\|p(z_g)\right) \right). \]

      A minibatch average usually estimates the data expectation. The floor removes the objective's incentive to shrink an already-small group KL; it does not force KL upward, guarantee \(\lambda_g\) nats of mutual information \(I_q(X;Z_g)\), or guarantee that each coordinate is active. Report the unclamped KL and an information/active-unit diagnostic separately.

    6. δ-VAE (Razavi et al., 2019): replace the standard normal prior with a constrained family (e.g., AR-1 latent dynamics) that has a strict lower bound on KL, eliminating the collapsed solution from the optimisation landscape entirely.

    7. \(\beta\)-VAE with \(\beta<1\): Reducing KL pressure can increase latent usage, but changes the rate–distortion operating point and no longer optimises the ordinary ELBO. Tune \(\beta\) against a declared downstream objective; there is no modality-independent recommended range.

    8. Word dropout (for text): Randomly replace some decoder inputs so the decoder cannot rely exclusively on autoregressive shortcuts. The corruption rate changes the conditional model and must be tuned and reported rather than copied as a universal percentage.

    9. Changing decoder information access: reduce receptive field/capacity, add latent connections, or corrupt teacher-forced inputs. This can force latent use while worsening the observation model, so compare likelihood and downstream utility rather than calling weaker automatically healthier.

    Blurry Reconstructions¤

    Why it happens:

    With fixed-variance Gaussian likelihood and a deterministic mean decoder, squared-error risk is minimized by the conditional mean. When \(z\) leaves unresolved multimodal detail, that mean can average incompatible outcomes. Blur is therefore linked to the observation model, information bottleneck, architecture, and data—not to “VAEs” as an unavoidable family property.

    Solutions:

    1. Perceptual Loss (LPIPS — Zhang et al., 2018): Replace pixel-wise MSE with VGG/AlexNet feature matching.
    2. Significantly improves sharpness while maintaining structure.

    3. Used in DFC-VAE (Hou et al., 2017) and inside the SD-VAE training recipe.

    4. Adversarial Training: Add a discriminator to penalise unrealistic outputs.

    5. VAE-GAN (Larsen et al., 2016) is the canonical recipe.
    6. The Stable Diffusion VAE adds a PatchGAN (Isola et al., 2017) discriminator on top of LPIPS + L1.

    7. Combines reconstruction, KL, and adversarial losses.

    8. Multi-scale SSIM (Wang et al., 2003): Structural-similarity loss across multiple resolutions, better correlated with perceptual quality than MSE.

    9. VQ-VAE / VQGAN (van den Oord et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021): Discrete latents and adversarial training together produce sharper outputs.

    10. Learned Variance: Let the decoder predict per-pixel variance \(\sigma^2_\theta(z)\) instead of using a fixed σ² — recovers a proper Gaussian likelihood and lets the model express its own uncertainty.

    Optimization Challenges¤

    NaN losses:

    • Match the decoder output parameterisation to the observation model: a Bernoulli likelihood uses logits; a bounded continuous or discretised likelihood needs its own valid parameterisation. Pixel scaling alone does not imply a sigmoid decoder.
    • If clipping is justified, clip the norm of the complete gradient PyTree with optax.clip_by_global_norm; coordinate-wise jnp.clip changes gradient direction and is a different intervention.
    • Parameterize either unconstrained log_var directly (with optional clipping) or a positive scale, sigma = nnx.softplus(sigma_raw) + 1e-6, followed by log_var = 2 * jnp.log(sigma). Do not apply softplus to log_var itself: that unnecessarily forces the variance above one.
    • Reduce learning rate if gradients explode

    Loss not decreasing:

    • Verify loss signs: minimize negative ELBO
    • Check that data range/support matches the declared observation distribution and decoder parameterization; there is no universal [0,1] or [-1,1] rule
    • Ensure encoder-decoder dimension matching
    • Monitor gradient norms and non-finite values relative to model size, optimiser, precision, and clipping rule. No universal healthy raw-norm interval transfers across architectures.

    Imbalanced loss terms:

    • Reconstruction loss sums over many pixels; KL sums over few latent dimensions
    • Choose and report a consistent reduction and objective weighting; changing from sums to means or introducing \(\beta\) changes the rate–distortion target

    Worked diagnosis: collapse or legitimate zero rate?¤

    Suppose a conditional VAE predicts \(x\) from a condition \(y\) that already determines \(x\) almost exactly. The decoder can achieve high conditional likelihood without \(z\), and the optimum may have \(q(z\mid x,y)=p(z\mid y)\) with zero conditional rate. Calling this “posterior collapse” describes latent non-use but does not prove optimization failure. If the goal is diverse residual uncertainty given \(y\), evaluate multiple valid \(x\) per condition, conditional coverage, and sensitivity to \(z\). If the data contain no residual variation, forcing positive KL only encodes nuisance information.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    VAE variants change a specific probabilistic or optimization component: objective weights, aggregate-dependence penalties, conditioning, subset inference, label observability, or the latent alphabet. Aggregate-prior matching does not determine per-record information; InfoVAE coefficients, MMD kernels, and adversarial games state different estimator and objective contracts. A conditional model and a joint multimodal model answer different queries. Product aggregation is Bayesian only under its declared message and independence assumptions, while missing-modality robustness depends on the training-mask distribution. None of these variants supplies semantic factors or sharp output by name. Collapse is goal-relative, codebooks have their own utilization failures, and every intervention changes a rate, support, estimator, or decoder assumption that must be reported. For discrete latents, exact marginalization, score-function gradients, continuous relaxations, and straight-through rules target different quantities; codebook optimization and prior learning add further, separate contracts.

(3) The condition or decoder may already explain all predictable variation, leaving no useful residual information for \(z\). (4) Expected posterior-to-prior KL/rate, not semantic entanglement directly. (5) Each unimodal posterior already contains one copy of the prior, so their product needs the correction \(p(z)^{1-|S|}\); multiplying by another prior double-counts it. (6) Its observation noise variance is smaller, so its likelihood message contributes precision \(1\) rather than \(1/4\). (11) No. The relaxed sample approaches a vertex, but derivatives become ill-conditioned and the finite-temperature objective differs from the hard objective. (12) A codec can discard information even if the prior is perfect, while a strong codec can be paired with a poor prior; only separate measurements localize the failure.

    Hierarchical VAEs¤

    Model family and notation¤

    Let \(z_{>\ell}=z_{\ell+1:L}\) denote the latent groups above group \(\ell\), where \(z_L\) is farthest from the observation. A general top-down hierarchical VAE defines

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\theta(x,z_{1:L}) &=p_\theta(z_L)\\ &\quad\times \prod_{\ell=1}^{L-1}p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell})\\ &\quad\times p_\theta(x\mid z_{1:L}). \end{aligned} \]

    The Markov special case uses only \(z_{\ell+1}\) in each conditional and often only \(z_1\) in the observation model. Skip-connected hierarchies allow every lower group and the likelihood to depend on all groups above them. Those are different probabilistic models, even if their neural backbones look similar.

    The word hierarchical also does not guarantee semantic ordering. Spatial resolution, receptive field, prior dependencies, and decoder paths can encourage coarse-to-fine allocation, but the ELBO is invariant to many latent reparameterizations and does not name “global” or “local” factors.

    Top-down and bottom-up are posterior factorizations¤

    A variational family aligned with the generative ordering is

    \[ \begin{aligned} q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x) &=q_\phi(z_L\mid x)\\ &\quad\times \prod_{\ell=1}^{L-1}q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{>\ell}). \end{aligned} \]

    This is usually implemented by a deterministic bottom-up feature pass followed by stochastic top-down sampling. At each group, an inference network combines data-dependent evidence with the conditional prior supplied by already sampled higher groups.

    Ladder VAE gives the bottom-up network a likelihood-message interpretation and combines Gaussian messages with top-down Gaussian priors. Residual posterior parameterizations instead predict corrections to prior parameters. BIVA adds a stochastic bottom-up path as well as the stochastic top-down path (Maaløe et al., 2019).

    The ordering itself is not a theorem that top-down inference is always more accurate. The chain rule also permits

    \[ \begin{aligned} q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x) &=q_\phi(z_1\mid x)\\ &\quad\times \prod_{\ell=2}^{L}q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{<\ell}). \end{aligned} \]

    With unrestricted conditionals, either ordering can represent the exact posterior. Differences arise after architectural restrictions:

    Table VAE.11. Top-down and bottom-up are posterior factorizations
    Inference construction What it assumes or exposes Characteristic limitation
    Mean-field \(\prod_\ell q(z_\ell\mid x)\) no posterior dependence among groups cannot represent explaining-away dependence
    Restricted bottom-up \(q(z_1\mid x)\prod_{\ell>1}q(z_\ell\mid z_{\ell-1})\) information is compressed successively upward need not match the top-down conditional-prior structure
    Full chain-rule bottom-up conditions on all sampled lower groups expressive in principle but can be costly and awkward for per-prior corrections
    Top-down with bottom-up evidence matches the prior ordering and exposes a local posterior-versus-prior comparison remains approximate; higher-group errors propagate downward
    Bidirectional or iteratively refined expands inference computation or variational flexibility adds latency, memory, optimization, or estimator complexity

    The layerwise ELBO¤

    For the matched top-down factorization, the KL chain rule gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal B(x) ={}&\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x)} [\log p_\theta(x\mid z_{1:L})]\\ &-D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_\phi(z_L\mid x)\,\|\,p_\theta(z_L) \right)\\ &-\sum_{\ell=1}^{L-1} \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z_{>\ell}\mid x)} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{>\ell}) \,\|\, p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \right) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    Define the per-record group rate

    \[ R_\ell(x)= \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z_{>\ell}\mid x)} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_\phi(z_\ell\mid x,z_{>\ell}) \,\|\, p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \right) \right], \]

    with the expectation omitted for the top group. Then the total rate is \(\sum_\ell R_\ell(x)\). Each lower-group term must be averaged over its random parents; evaluating it only at posterior means is a different diagnostic.

    Worked two-layer Gaussian hierarchy¤

    Consider the scalar model

    \[ \begin{aligned} Z_2&\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\\ Z_1\mid Z_2=z_2&\sim\mathcal N(z_2,1),\\ X\mid Z_1=z_1&\sim\mathcal N(z_1,1). \end{aligned} \]

    Marginally, \(X\sim\mathcal N(0,3)\). For the observation \(x=3\), exact Gaussian conditioning gives the top-down posterior

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(z_2\mid x=3) &=\mathcal N\!\left(1,\frac23\right),\\ p(z_1\mid x=3,z_2) &=\mathcal N\!\left(\frac{3+z_2}{2},\frac12\right). \end{aligned} \]

    Using these exact conditionals as \(q\), the two group rates are

    \[ \begin{aligned} R_2(3) &=\frac13+\frac12\log\frac32 \approx0.536\ \text{nats},\\ R_1(3) &=\mathbb E_{p(z_2\mid x=3)} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( p(z_1\mid x=3,z_2)\,\|\,p(z_1\mid z_2) \right) \right]\\ &=\frac13+\frac12\log2 \approx0.680\ \text{nats}. \end{aligned} \]

    The lower rate is an expectation over \(Z_2\); it is not the KL obtained by substituting only \(z_2=1\). The posterior marginal is \(Z_1\mid X=3\sim\mathcal N(2,2/3)\), so

    \[ \mathbb E_q[\log p(x=3\mid Z_1)] =-\frac12\log(2\pi)-\frac56. \]

    Subtracting both rates yields

    \[ \mathcal B(3) =-\frac12\log(6\pi)-\frac32 =\log p(x=3). \]

    The equality is expected because \(q\) is exact. The example also shows why a group-rate dashboard is more informative than total KL: both stochastic groups carry posterior information, and their contributions are quantitatively separable.

    What does a group KL measure?¤

    Let \(r(x,z)=p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)\) be the inference joint, and let \(r(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell})\) be its conditional aggregate. Averaging a group rate over data gives the exact decomposition

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)}R_\ell(x) ={}&I_r(X;Z_\ell\mid Z_{>\ell})\\ &+\mathbb E_{r(z_{>\ell})} \!\left[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( r(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \,\|\, p_\theta(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell}) \right) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The first term is conditional information about the observation. The second is conditional aggregate-posterior mismatch. Consequently:

    • a large group KL need not mean that the group conveys much information about \(x\); prior mismatch can dominate;
    • a small group KL means the encoder contributes little data-dependent update beyond higher groups, but does not by itself reveal whether this is an appropriate optimum or an inference/optimization failure; and
    • equal KL across groups is neither required by probability theory nor evidence that the groups learned different semantic scales.

    Record diagnostics per stochastic group and, where practical, per channel or dimension:

    The classic “active unit” statistic often thresholds \(\operatorname{Var}_x\mathbb E_{q(z_j\mid x)}[z_j]\). That is not new conditional evidence at the lower group. Prefer residual posterior-versus-prior statistics or conditional interventions alongside any marginal active-unit count. No threshold such as \(0.01\), \(0.02\), or \(0.1\) is universal across parameterizations or reductions.

    Variational pruning: unused capacity or failed optimization?¤

    A group is pruned in the inference sense when its approximate conditional posterior closely matches its conditional prior for almost all records. Calling that outcome over-pruning requires evidence that the unused capacity harms the declared goal. The distinction is operational:

    Table VAE.13. Variational pruning: unused capacity or failed optimization?
    Observation Legitimate unused capacity Evidence of harmful pruning
    Near-zero group rate remaining groups already model the relevant variation a stronger inference/training procedure activates the group and improves held-out model or task criteria
    Decoder intervention removing or prior-resampling the group has negligible effect the decoder is sensitive to the group, but the encoder cannot infer it reliably
    Model fit held-out likelihood and posterior predictive checks do not improve with the group the model underfits or misses long-range/multiscale structure that the group was introduced to capture
    Representation goal no required information is lost conditional information, interventions, or downstream probes show a missing factor
    Stability the group remains unused across seeds and adequate optimization activation depends strongly on initialization, warm-up, or gradient path

    Yeung et al. (2017) demonstrate that inactive factors can persist even when a VAE underfits, but also show why merely lowering the KL weight or forcing more “active” units is insufficient: extra coordinates may memorize, duplicate information, or worsen prior generation. More active units are not an objective by themselves.

    Autoregressive conditionals and parallel generation¤

    A top-down hierarchy is sequential across any dependent groups, but it can be parallel inside a conditionally factorized group. The actual sampling critical path is determined at several layers:

    Table VAE.15. Autoregressive conditionals and parallel generation
    Component Factorized form Sampling consequence
    Across latent groups \(p(z_L)\prod_{\ell<L}p(z_\ell\mid z_{>\ell})\) at least the dependency depth of the latent DAG
    Within one group \(\prod_jp(z_{\ell j}\mid z_{>\ell})\) coordinates can be drawn in parallel after parents are known
    Autoregressive group prior \(\prod_jp(z_{\ell j}\mid z_{\ell,<j},z_{>\ell})\) sequential over the chosen within-group ordering
    Factorized observation decoder \(\prod_ip(x_i\mid z_{1:L})\) output coordinates can be drawn in parallel given latents
    Autoregressive observation decoder \(\prod_ip(x_i\mid x_{<i},z_{1:L})\) sequential over observations and can reduce pressure to use latents
    Tree or multiscale hierarchy conditionally independent nodes at each depth parallel by level only if the declared graph and implementation permit it

    A sufficiently deep hierarchical latent model can represent an autoregressive distribution through the degenerate construction \(z_i=x_i\), an autoregressive prior over \(z\), and an identity decoder. This is an algebraic inclusion construction, not evidence that an arbitrary trained VAE learned the same factorization or sampling cost. Conversely, grouping conditionally independent details below a shared parent can shorten the critical path relative to a flat autoregressive ordering. Never infer \(O(\log D)\) generation merely from the word “hierarchical”; state the graph depth, within-group dependencies, decoder, batch size, and hardware.

    Architectures and research boundaries¤

    Intended benefits to test:

    • different latent groups can represent information at different spatial or semantic scales, but coarse-to-fine ordering is not guaranteed;
    • conditional priors can allocate rate adaptively across groups;
    • conditionally independent coordinates can reduce the ancestral critical path; and
    • added expressiveness may improve likelihood or reconstructions while increasing posterior-design, numerical-stability, and optimization difficulty.

    Representative deep hierarchies include:

    • Ladder VAE recursively combines bottom-up approximate-likelihood messages with top-down conditional priors.
    • BIVA adds stochastic bottom-up variables and generative skip paths to a bidirectional hierarchy.
    • NVAE uses deep Gaussian groups, residual posterior parameters, depthwise-separable decoder cells, temporary KL balancing, and spectral regularization; each contribution needs its own ablation.
    • Very Deep VAE / VDVAE (Child, 2021) scales stochastic depth and demonstrates fast, protocol-specific image sampling relative to the PixelCNN baselines it tested.
    • HQ-VAE (Takida et al., 2024) gives stacked discrete codebooks a variational treatment; discrete codebook collapse is related to, but not identical with, Gaussian group pruning.

    The design space remains open rather than converging to one hierarchy recipe. An ICLR 2026 study adds training-only spectral constraints to hierarchical learned image compression, explicitly targeting cross-scale frequency allocation (Cong et al., 2026). An ICASSP 2026 study combines amortized initialization with iterative posterior refinement in a transform-separated HVAE for its inverse-problem setting (Penninga & van Sloun, 2026). These peer-reviewed results establish application-specific constructions, not a universal hierarchy, semantic ordering, or speed advantage.

    Sequence VAEs: One Global Code or a Latent Process?¤

    The phrase sequence VAE does not determine where stochastic state lives. Two common constructions answer different questions.

    One latent variable for the whole sequence¤

    A global-code model can use

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\theta(z,x_{1:T}) &=p(z)\prod_{t=1}^{T}\\[-2pt] &\quad p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t},z). \end{aligned} \]

    An offline posterior model may condition on the whole sequence as \(q_\phi(z\mid x_{1:T})\).

    The latent variable can capture sequence-level properties such as speaker, style, topic, or a global plan. The decoder remains autoregressive when its conditional depends on \(x_{<t}\): drawing \(z\) once does not make token generation parallel. A bidirectional sequence encoder is valid for offline posterior inference because the complete observation is known, even though the generative model must sample in causal order. For online inference, the available conditional would instead be \(q(z\mid x_{1:t})\) and changes as evidence arrives.

    A stochastic latent state at each time¤

    A latent-process model can factorize more generally as

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\theta(z_{1:T},x_{1:T}) &=\prod_{t=1}^{T}\left[ p_\theta(z_t\mid z_{<t},x_{<t})\right]\\[-2pt] &\quad\times\left[ p_\theta(x_t\mid z_{\le t},x_{<t})\right]. \end{aligned} \]

    VRNN implements these histories through a recurrent state. A filtering inference model uses only data available through the current time,

    \[ \begin{aligned} &q_\phi^{\mathrm{filter}}(z_{1:T}\mid x_{1:T})\\ &\quad=\prod_{t=1}^{T} q_\phi(z_t\mid z_{<t},x_{\le t}), \end{aligned} \]

    whereas an offline smoother may use future observations,

    \[ \begin{aligned} &q_\phi^{\mathrm{smooth}}(z_{1:T}\mid x_{1:T})\\ &\quad=\prod_{t=1}^{T} q_\phi(z_t\mid z_{<t},x_{1:T}). \end{aligned} \]

    The smoother can reduce posterior uncertainty about an earlier state using later evidence, but it is unavailable for a strictly online decision at time \(t\). The ELBO contains one expected observation term and one conditional KL per time step; those KLs are averaged over inferred histories. Global collapse and per-time collapse are therefore different diagnostics. A model may use a global code while ignoring local innovations, or the reverse.

    The choice is about the desired probability law:

    • Use a global code when one persistent random variable is a defensible account of shared sequence variation.
    • Use a latent process when new stochastic innovations, evolving uncertainty, or state-space queries matter.
    • Combine global and local variables when both are needed, and report their rates and interventions separately.
    • Do not infer superior likelihood, long-range memory, or calibrated forecast uncertainty merely from adding stochastic states. Compare against an autoregressive baseline and classical or neural state-space models under the same observation likelihood and evaluation horizon.

    Worked two-step sequence: future evidence updates a shared cause¤

    Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and let independent noise bits satisfy \(E_t\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(0.1)\). Define \(X_t=Z\mathbin{\mathsf{xor}}E_t\). Marginally, each observation is a fair bit, but the global latent couples the two times:

    \[ \Pr(X_1=X_2) =0.9^2+0.1^2 =0.82. \]

    After observing \(X_1=1\), filtering gives \(\Pr(Z=1\mid X_1=1)=0.9\). After the future observation \(X_2=1\) arrives, smoothing gives

    \[ \Pr(Z=1\mid X_1=1,X_2=1) =\frac{0.9^2}{0.9^2+0.1^2} =\frac{81}{82}\approx0.9878. \]

    If instead each time has an independent fair latent \(Z_t\) and the same local noise channel, \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) are independent fair bits and \(\Pr(X_1=X_2)=1/2\). Matching one-time marginals therefore does not establish the same sequence law. Temporal posterior inference must be checked against joint and predictive queries, not framewise reconstruction alone.

    Structured amortization is a separate design axis¤

    Mean-field, autoregressive, state-space, message-passing, and flow-based posteriors can all be amortized. The architecture should encode the conditional dependencies needed by the query rather than treating a wider encoder as a substitute for structure. Amortized Structural Variational Inference, listed by AISTATS 2026, combines a structured variational family with context-aware amortization and reports lower variational or amortization gaps in its studied graphical and time-series models. Those results are specific to its models, neighborhoods, estimators, and experiments; they do not establish one universally best sequence posterior.

    Retrieval practice: sequence VAEs
    1. Does a global sequence latent remove an autoregressive decoder's token-by-token sampling path?
    2. What information distinguishes filtering from smoothing?
    3. In the worked binary model, why does the second observation change the posterior for the same global \(Z\)?
    4. Can matching every frame marginal prove that two models learned the same sequence distribution?

    Solutions. (1) No. The decoder still conditions on previously generated observations. (2) Filtering uses observations only through the current time; smoothing can use the complete observed sequence to revise earlier states. (3) Both observations share the same cause, so the later bit supplies another likelihood factor for \(Z\). (4) No; the global and independent-local constructions above have identical fair one-frame marginals but equality probabilities 0.82 and 0.5.

    Importance Weighted VAE (IWAE)¤

    Burda et al. (2016) use multiple samples to get tighter bounds on the log-likelihood:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{IWAE}} = \mathbb{E}_{z_{1:K} \sim q} \left[ \log \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \frac{p(x, z_k)}{q(z_k|x)} \right] \]

    Under the standard IWAE construction, the expected bound is non-decreasing in \(K\) and converges toward the log marginal likelihood under suitable integrability conditions. It is strictly tighter only when the added importance samples provide non-degenerate information; equality is possible, including when the proposal is the exact posterior. Larger \(K\) also raises compute and can reduce the signal-to-noise ratio of inference-network gradients even while tightening the bound (Rainforth et al., 2018). Treat \(K\) as an estimator/optimisation trade-off and report it.

    Normalizing Flow VAE¤

    Rezende & Mohamed (2015) replace the Gaussian posterior with flexible distributions via invertible transformations:

    \[ q_\phi(z|x) = q_0(z_0|x) \left|\det \frac{\partial f}{\partial z_0}\right|^{-1} \]

    where \(f\) is an invertible function. Common flow families: Real NVP (Dinh et al., 2017), MAF (Papamakarios et al., 2017), IAF (Kingma et al., 2016) — well-suited as a posterior because sampling is fast — and Glow (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018).

    Benefits:

    • More expressive posterior families within the support, topology, invertibility, and architecture constraints of the chosen flow
    • Potentially better approximation of the true posterior and a tighter ELBO; neither is automatic after finite optimisation
    • downstream generation can improve if posterior fit was the relevant bottleneck; this is not guaranteed

    Trade-off: Increased compute during training; some flows (MAF) are slow to sample, others (IAF) are slow to evaluate density.

    VampPrior and Learned Priors¤

    The standard \(\mathcal{N}(0, I)\) prior can be mismatched with the aggregate posterior \(q_\phi(z)=\int q_\phi(z\mid x)p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)dx\). Then samples from some prior regions may decode poorly, while some encoded regions may receive too little prior mass. VampPrior (Tomczak & Welling, 2017) replaces \(p(z)\) with a mixture \(p_\lambda(z) = \tfrac{1}{K}\sum_{k} q_\phi(z\mid u_k)\) parameterised by \(K\) learned pseudo-inputs \(u_k\). The original work reports improved bounds and samples in its settings; component count, shared encoder coupling, and evaluation cost are part of the comparison. Diffusion priors (Wehenkel & Louppe, 2021; Vahdat et al., 2021 — LSGM) replace a simple prior with an iterative latent generator, changing both training and sampling cost.

    VAEs as Codecs and Latent Components¤

    One influential role is lossy learned compression for a separate latent generator. Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), SDXL (Podell et al., 2024), and documented image/video systems use an autoencoder-like codec followed by a diffusion, flow-matching, or autoregressive prior. Product names do not establish that every codec is a classical VAE; closed reports may omit architecture, loss, data, or latent scaling details.

    The cross-family Representations and Multimodal Generative Systems volume develops the corresponding rate–distortion, discrete-token, temporal-codec, semantic-alignment, and multimodal evaluation questions without assuming every codec is a classical VAE.

    Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs)¤

    The original latent-diffusion work uses a KL-regularized autoencoder with spatial downsampling and combines reconstruction, perceptual, adversarial, and mild KL terms (Rombach et al., 2022). Exact coefficients, latent scaling, channels, and losses are checkpoint-specific and should be read from the implementation/model card rather than elevated to a family constant. The downstream diffusion or flow model—not the simple reference prior alone—is the operational latent generator.

    Diffusion–VAE Hybrids¤

    Beyond the LDM "VAE-then-diffusion" pipeline, several hybrids tie the two more tightly:

    • DiffuseVAE (Pandey et al., 2022) conditions a diffusion model on a VAE reconstruction; speed and quality depend on both stages and the sampler.
    • LSGM (Vahdat et al., 2021) trains a diffusion model as the prior of an NVAE, jointly maximising a single ELBO.
    • Diffusion autoencoders (Preechakul et al., 2022 — DiffAE) combine semantic encodings with conditional diffusion decoding; representation and sample claims are protocol-specific.
    • Generalisation theory (Wang et al., 2025) gives a unified information-theoretic analysis of when VAE encoders + diffusion generators generalise, formalising what the empirical LDM literature has been observing.

    Foundation-Model Tokenizers¤

    A complementary direction uses frozen or aligned vision-foundation encoders and trains a decoder or adapter (ICLR 2026 poster; CVPR 2026). It trades end-to-end codec freedom for inherited features. Whether those features improve conditioning, reconstruction, generation, or transfer depends on the downstream task and protocol; the results do not prove KL is irrelevant.

    Latent Space Properties and Interpretation¤

    Name the latent claim before selecting a metric¤

    The phrase “good latent space” can refer to at least five different objects:

    Table VAE.17. Name the latent claim before selecting a metric
    Latent role Relevant question
    Inference state Does \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) approximate the model posterior required by the query?
    Codec state Which observation details can the encoder–decoder preserve, and at what rate?
    Generative state Can the learned prior place mass where the decoder produces the required data distribution?
    Downstream feature Can a declared adapter recover target \(Y\) from a chosen posterior statistic with finite data?
    Semantic or causal factor Is the coordinate meaning identified under explicit supervision, environment, intervention, or structural assumptions?

    These roles may share one tensor without sharing one guarantee. In particular, the ELBO does not state that a posterior mean is a linearly accessible feature, that a coordinate is a true causal variable, or that an augmentation should be ignored. The representation evaluation matrix separates reconstruction, generative adequacy, target sufficiency, accessibility, invariance/equivariance, disentanglement, identifiability, compression, and causal adequacy.

    Exact reconstruction deserves a precise qualification. If a deterministic decoder satisfies \(X=d(Z)\), then every target generated from \(X\) remains recoverable from \(Z\) by composing its predictor with \(d\). It need not be recoverable by a linear probe or from a small labeled sample. Conversely, a one-bit feature can be sufficient for one target while intentionally discarding most of \(X\). The finite XOR example makes both boundaries explicit.

    Continuity and Interpolation¤

    A common hope is that a VAE learns a useful continuous latent representation, but the following are empirical properties to test, not consequences of the ELBO alone:

    • Local decoder continuity: a continuous neural decoder maps sufficiently small latent perturbations to small output-parameter changes, but perceptual change can still be sharp.
    • Useful interpolation: linear or spherical paths may decode smoothly, yet can traverse low-density or semantically implausible regions.
    • Prior coverage: samples from the prior may decode well when the aggregate posterior and prior are suitably aligned; posterior holes and low-quality prior regions can remain.

    Interpolation therefore diagnoses the learned geometry; it does not prove disentanglement, semantic linearity, topology preservation, or global coverage.

    Testing interpolation:

    # Encode two images
z1 = encoder(x1)[0]  # Take mean, ignore variance
z2 = encoder(x2)[0]

# Interpolate
alphas = jnp.linspace(0, 1, num=10)
z_interp = [(1 - alpha) * z1 + alpha * z2 for alpha in alphas]

# Decode interpolated points
x_interp = [decoder(z) for z in z_interp]

    Disentanglement: Independent Factors of Variation¤

    An idealized axis-aligned disentangled representation assigns declared generative factors to separate coordinates, for example:

    • \(z_1\): Object class (digit identity)
    • \(z_2\): Rotation angle
    • \(z_3\): Stroke width
    • \(z_4\): Position
    • ...

    Axis alignment is a relationship between learned coordinates and named factors. It is stronger than independence of the aggregate posterior. The total correlation

    \[ \operatorname{TC}_q(Z) =D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q(z)\,\middle\|\,\prod_j q(z_j)\right) \]

    vanishes when the learned coordinates are statistically independent, but a measure-preserving transformation can mix the intended factors while retaining independent coordinates. The finite XOR construction above has exactly this property. If the scientific factors are themselves dependent, forcing independence can also misrepresent their structure.

    Achieving disentanglement:

    • Treat β-VAE (\(\beta>1\)) as one rate-control baseline, not an automatic disentangler.
    • Use β-TCVAE or FactorVAE when you want to reweight only the total-correlation component of the KL.
    • Use datasets with known generative factors for factor-based metrics; natural-data evaluation requires operational labels, interventions, or downstream tasks rather than assuming ground truth.
    • Add supervision or weak supervision when semantic identification matters. Locatello et al., 2019 show that unsupervised disentanglement is not identifiable without inductive biases; different equally likely representations can encode the same observations.

    Positive identifiability results are assumption-specific. For example, iVAE uses an observed auxiliary variable and a conditionally factorized latent prior to identify a class of nonlinear latent models up to a restricted equivalence. This does not turn an ordinary unconditional VAE into an identifiable model. Controlled pairs, temporal changes, environments, and interventions can supply other symmetry-breaking information, but their support and mechanism assumptions must be stated.

    Measuring disentanglement:

    • MIG (Mutual Information Gap) — gap between the top-2 latents most informative about each ground-truth factor; introduced with β-TCVAE.
    • SAP (Separated Attribute Predictability) (Kumar et al., 2018) — gap between the top-2 latents that best predict each factor.
    • DCI (Disentanglement, Completeness, Informativeness) (Eastwood & Williams, 2018) — three-metric framework based on a probe regressor's importance matrix.
    • FactorVAE score (Kim & Mnih, 2018) — accuracy of a majority-vote classifier predicting which factor is held fixed in a batch.

    When to Use VAEs¤

    VAEs Excel When:

    • You need explicit latent variables for posterior inference or downstream representations and will validate their structure on the target task
    • A single-objective latent-variable baseline and amortized inference match the task
    • You want both generation and reconstruction capabilities
    • You need a representation whose task utility, calibration, and intervention behavior will be explicitly evaluated
    • The chosen likelihood and latent structure fit the data, regardless of modality

    Example Applications:

    • Medical image anomaly detection via reconstruction error (Zimmerer et al., 2019)
    • Molecular design with controllable chemical properties (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018)
    • Semi-supervised learning with limited labels (Kingma et al., 2014 — M2 model)
    • Data compression and denoising (Ballé et al., 2018)
    • Recommendation systems (Liang et al., 2018 — Mult-VAE)

    When to Use GANs¤

    GANs Excel When:

    • A direct implicit generator and adversarial objective fit the data and latency target
    • You need a fast direct generator; an encoder is optional and can be learned through inversion, an auxiliary encoder, or a bidirectional architecture
    • You can evaluate game dynamics, coverage, and memorization under the target protocol

    Limitations:

    • No inferred latent representation by default; nevertheless, GANs have an explicit input latent space and models such as StyleGAN exhibit useful interpolation and editing structure
    • Training instability (mode collapse, oscillation)
    • No amortized reconstruction path by default; optimization-based inversion or a jointly trained encoder can provide one

    When to Use Diffusion Models¤

    Diffusion Models Excel When:

    • Your main objective is image/video generation rather than amortised latent inference or codec reconstruction. Diffusion and flow systems are relevant comparisons, but quality claims must be bounded by dataset, conditioning, guidance, sampler, and verification date.
    • Computational cost is acceptable.
    • You need excellent mode coverage and diversity (Ho et al., 2020 — DDPM; Song et al., 2021 — score-based).

    Limitations:

    • Iterative sampling cost depends on solver, NFE, distillation, representation, guidance, and hardware.
    • End-to-end cost may exceed or undercut a VAE/GAN pipeline depending on decoder and quality target; measure it.
    • Often paired with a learned autoencoder in latent image/video systems; pixel-space, discrete-token, and other representation choices remain active alternatives.

    Suppose Codec A improves PSNR but a fixed latent diffusion prior trained for equal steps obtains worse conditional fidelity than with Codec B. This is not contradictory. A complete audit reports: (1) reconstruction metrics and bitrate; (2) aggregate latent statistics and prior mismatch; (3) downstream generator quality, coverage, and convergence under matched compute; and (4) encode/decode latency and memory. Only layer 3 answers which codec better serves that generator.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Modern “VAE” systems range from probabilistic latent models to lightly KL-regularized codecs, VQ tokenizers, foundation-encoder adapters, sequence-level codes, latent stochastic processes, and hybrids with diffusion priors or decoders. The label does not identify the objective. A hierarchy or temporal model specifies conditional dependencies and a sampling critical path; it does not guarantee semantic scales, useful activity in every group, long-range memory, or calibrated forecast uncertainty. Layerwise and per-time KL terms combine conditional data information with prior mismatch, so pruning and collapse must be diagnosed through held-out criteria, residual statistics, gradients, and interventions. Importance weighting, flow posteriors, structured amortization, and learned priors address different approximation gaps, while tokenizer research must be evaluated through both reconstruction and downstream generation.

    Evaluation Metrics¤

    VAEs are evaluated on three loosely-orthogonal axes: density-estimation quality, sample / reconstruction fidelity, and representation quality (since the encoder is half the model).

    Density-Estimation Metrics¤

    • Negative ELBO — an upper bound on NLL under the declared variational model. Dividing a summed negative ELBO by \(D\log2\) gives a variational BPD upper bound, not exact \(-\log_2p_\theta(x)/D\) unless the posterior gap is zero. Text perplexity derived from a bound must be labeled accordingly.
    • IWAE bound (Burda et al., 2016) — a tighter expected lower bound under standard conditions. Report \(K\), proposal, estimator, repeated runs, and compute; it is not exact likelihood by default.
    • Active units — count dimensions using a declared activity statistic and threshold. A per-dimension KL threshold such as \(10^{-2}\) is a convention, not an invariant diagnostic; also report the full KL distribution and mutual-information/aggregate-posterior measures when possible.

    Sample / Reconstruction Quality¤

    • Reconstruction error — pixel MSE on a held-out set; the simplest sanity check.
    • rFID — reconstruction FID — FID computed between real images and reconstructions. It is commonly reported for image codecs but inherits feature-Gaussian, sample-count, implementation, and domain limitations.
    • LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) — perceptual distance via VGG features; complements pixel MSE.
    • gFID — generative FID — FID computed on unconditional samples \(x = \mathrm{Decoder}(z)\) with \(z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)\). Distinguishes a good codec from a good generator; a VAE can have low rFID but high gFID if the aggregated posterior is far from the prior.
    • CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024) — kernel discrepancy in a chosen contrastive embedding space; the paper reports human-correlation benefits in selected studies, not a universal perceptual guarantee.

    Representation / Latent-Space Quality¤

    • Linear probing — train a declared linear model on a specified posterior statistic such as \(\mu(x)\), with fixed sample size, regularization, optimization, and shift protocol. This tests finite-sample linear accessibility for that target, not information-theoretic sufficiency or universal semantic quality.
    • Nonlinear and few-shot probes — vary adapter capacity and labeled sample count to separate information presence from accessibility and sample efficiency. Probe selection on the test set invalidates the comparison.
    • MIG / DCI / SAP / FactorVAE score — factor-based metrics for \(\beta\)-VAE / \(\beta\)-TCVAE / FactorVAE evaluations on datasets with declared generative factors (see the Disentanglement section). Use several metrics and seeds; none proves unsupervised identifiability, causal meaning, or downstream benefit by itself.
    • Invariance/equivariance tests — evaluate paired transformations and verify that the target really should remain fixed or transform under the declared action.
    • Intervention and environment tests — required when a latent is claimed to represent a mechanism, causal variable, or counterfactually stable factor. Observational reconstruction and probes are insufficient.

    Use the complete representation evaluation matrix to keep these claims separate from codec and prior-sample evaluation.

    Quantisation, Distillation, and Edge Deployment¤

    • FP16 / BF16 — commonly effective, but not universally lossless. Validate encoder statistics, log-variance, quantization/codebook operations, decoder highlights, and reconstruction metrics against FP32; BF16's exponent range is often safer than FP16 for reductions.
    • INT8 or lower precision can affect encoder statistics, code assignments, and decoder detail differently. Calibrate and evaluate both directions for the exact checkpoint; product support and artifacts change by runtime.
    • TAESD (Berman, 2023) is a small approximate decoder/encoder family used for previews. Parameter count and latency vary by variant, resolution, hardware, and runtime; approximation quality must be separated from the full codec.
    • Mobile/edge conversion requires supported operators, memory accounting, calibration, and device measurements. A successful format conversion is not evidence of acceptable reconstruction or thermals.

    Ethical and Safety Notes¤

    Codec training can memorize or expose data just like other learned components, but reconstruction quality alone does not establish leakage. Audit membership/privacy with declared attacks, preserve data lineage and licensing for codec and prior separately, and evaluate decoded outputs under the deployment's risk model. Safety controls are application- and modality-specific; one classifier is not universally sufficient.

    Worked deployment accounting¤

    A codec with \(16\times\) spatial compression has \(1/256\) as many spatial positions as pixels, but increasing latent channels from 4 to 128 multiplies latent scalar count by 32. The net scalar compression relative to RGB is therefore approximately \(256\times3/128=6\). This does not determine entropy rate, memory layout, or prior FLOPs, but it catches the misleading claim that “16× spatial compression” means 256× less information or compute.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    VAEs combine neural latent-variable models with variational inference. They can learn useful representations, but structure, interpretability, identifiability, and disentanglement require additional assumptions and empirical validation. Understanding VAEs provides essential foundations for modern generative modeling, from continuous latent codecs used by latent diffusion to discrete VQ-style codes used by image and video tokenizers.

    Capstone objectives¤

    After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. specify a latent generative story and observation likelihood;
    2. derive and implement its variational objective;
    3. choose diagonal, structured, or temporal posterior dependencies for a declared query;
    4. diagnose approximation, amortization, and model gaps;
    5. decide whether a proposed “VAE” is a probabilistic generator or codec component; and
    6. design an evidence-bounded experiment through deployment accounting.

    Core Principles:

    • ELBO objective trades expected observation log likelihood against posterior-to-prior rate; “latent structure” is not a guaranteed term
    • Reparameterization trick supplies a pathwise gradient estimator for compatible distributions; other estimators remain possible
    • Probabilistic framework provides an explicit latent-variable model and posterior approximation; whether its latent geometry is smooth, covered, or semantically useful must be measured
    • Variational inference provides principled approximations to intractable posteriors

    Key Variants:

    • β-VAE / β-TCVAE / FactorVAE alter rate and dependence penalties to encourage particular factorisations; disentanglement and interpretability remain assumption- and metric-dependent
    • InfoVAE / MMD-VAE / AAE separate or estimate aggregate-prior matching through different objectives; matching one marginal does not determine per-record information
    • VQ-VAE / VQGAN use discrete latents and expose a code sequence to a separate prior; quality and utilization remain empirical
    • Conditional VAE enables controlled generation with auxiliary information
    • Hierarchical VAEs allocate stochastic information across groups; scale semantics and improvements must be measured
    • Sequence VAEs may use one persistent code, per-time stochastic states, or both; filtering, smoothing, and autoregressive generation remain distinct computations
    • Latent-generator codecs may be KL-regularized autoencoders, VQ systems, or foundation-aligned tokenizers; the codec and downstream prior are distinct components

    Best Practices:

    • Begin with the intended objective and no-annealing baseline; ablate schedules rather than prescribing them by modality
    • Monitor likelihood/reconstruction, total and group KL, active units, prior samples, and downstream utility
    • Match observation distribution, support, scale, and reduction to the data
    • Treat perceptual/adversarial losses as changed objectives with their own fidelity, coverage, and calibration consequences
    • Separate codec, posterior, prior, and full-system evaluations

    Design a latent model for probabilistic weather-field forecasting with calibrated uncertainty, irregular missing observations, physical conservation diagnostics, and fast ensemble sampling.

    Worked solution: one defensible design

    Define a conditional latent state-space model rather than a plain image VAE: \(p(z_{1:T}\mid c)\) for dynamics and \(p(x_t\mid z_t,c)\) for observation sites, with masks included in the likelihood rather than imputed as truth. This is a stochastic-process/latent hybrid; a low reconstruction MSE alone would not validate forecast uncertainty.

Derive its per-record loss and identify every reduction, estimator, and sequential sampling dependency.
    3. P1.3 — Gap and evidence study. Design controls for model misspecification, posterior approximation, amortization, optimization, missingness shift, and decoder/prior composition. Report calibration, held-out bounds, posterior predictive checks, subgroup uncertainty, utility, privacy attacks, latency, memory, and uncertainty over seeds.
    4. P1.4 — Transfer and routing. Recast the system first as a deterministic codec plus an independently trained latent prior and then as an unordered set generator. State which probability, inference, symmetry, evaluation, and deployment claims change; route each implementation question to documentation, a local executable test, or a new scientific experiment.

    Final misconception check¤

    • A Gaussian prior does not guarantee a smooth, semantic, or fully covered latent space.
    • The ELBO is not exact likelihood unless the variational posterior gap vanishes.
    • Optimizing a restricted stochastic ELBO does not guarantee that training reached a local maximum-likelihood solution.
    • Posterior collapse is latent non-use; whether it is a failure depends on the modeling goal.
    • Aggregate-prior matching alone does not establish mutual information, semantic factors, or accurate per-example inference.
    • A discrete codebook avoids one Gaussian-collapse mechanism but can itself be underused.
    • Lower reconstruction error does not guarantee a better downstream generative tokenizer.
    • “VAE” in a product stack may denote a lightly regularized codec rather than an end-to-end variational generator.
    Retrieval practice: cumulative VAE synthesis
    1. Name the model, inference, approximation, and amortization gaps that can affect a VAE result.
    2. Why does optimizing \(\beta<1\) cease to be the ordinary ELBO?
    3. What evidence distinguishes a useful inferred latent from a merely reconstructive one?
    4. Which relationship label describes a codec followed by an independently trained latent diffusion prior?

    Solutions. (1) Model misspecification/data gap, variational-family approximation gap, amortization gap from shared inference, and finite optimization/estimation error. (2) It reweights the posterior-to-prior KL away from the variational identity for \(\log p_\theta(x)\). (3) Downstream probes/interventions, calibration or conditional utility, robustness, and comparison to baselines under held-out data—not reconstruction alone. (4) Hybrid composition.
