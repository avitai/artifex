    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • World Models 1 — Semantics and Queries
    • World Models and Causal Simulators

    Modern generators rarely model raw observations directly. They operate on text tokens, acoustic codes, image/video latents, geometric features, or hierarchical learned codecs. Representation choice decides what information is preserved, what dependencies the generator must learn, and which modalities can share a model.

    This volume connects rate–distortion, discrete and continuous tokenizers, latent diffusion, video codecs, cross-modal conditioning, fusion, AR–diffusion hybrids, synchronization, and modality imbalance. A shared architecture is not assumed to imply one probabilistic model or beneficial cross-task transfer.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–5.
    Research: all chapters, including evidence and synergy evaluation.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to treat a codec as a rate–distortion and support choice; distinguish reconstruction, sufficiency, accessibility, invariance, disentanglement, identifiability, compression, and causal adequacy; compare discrete/continuous/hierarchical representations; separate a discrete index from its embedding and learned prior; write conditional and joint multimodal factorizations; separate padding, corruption, and modality-observation masks; trace causal masks and synchronization; distinguish shared architecture from beneficial transfer; and evaluate codec, generator, alignment, subset inference, and end-to-end system layers separately.

    1. Representation Is Part of the Generative Model¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional probability and basic encoder/decoder models. By the end, you should be able to treat tokenizers, codecs, coordinate systems, and modality transforms as parts of the modeled system; distinguish invertible, lossy, stochastic, and many-to-one representations; derive the induced law on observations; and identify when representation error or support loss dominates a generator comparison.

    Let an encoder produce \(z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)\) and a decoder define \(p_\psi(x\mid z)\). A latent generator \(p_\theta(z)\) induces

    \[ p(x)=\int p_\psi(x\mid z)p_\theta(z)dz. \]

    Every output is filtered through the codec. A perfect latent generator reproduces the encoded-data distribution, not information discarded by \(q_\phi\).

    Rate–distortion view¤

    A codec trades reconstruction distortion \(D\) against representation rate \(R\):

    \[ \min_{q,p}\;D+\beta R. \]

    Distortion may be pixel, waveform, perceptual, adversarial, semantic, or task specific. Rate may refer to measured code length, an entropy-model cross-entropy, a variational KL, or a token-count proxy, but these quantities are not interchangeable. The operational rate–distortion chapter defines each one. There is no universal “best latent”: a representation optimized for perceptual reconstruction may omit text, counts, timing, or scientific quantities important downstream.

    Deterministic and stochastic codecs¤

    A deterministic encoder maps \(x\) to one \(z\). A stochastic encoder retains posterior uncertainty and can represent ambiguous measurements, at the cost of inference and sampling choices. Decoder likelihood semantics still matter: mean-squared reconstruction corresponds to a Gaussian observation model only with specified variance and independence assumptions.

    Representation-induced support¤

    The decoder restricts reachable outputs. A latent flow can have exact density in \(z\) while the decoded distribution is implicit or singular in observation space. A many-to-one decoder means distinct latents can represent the same output; an undercomplete codec can make some outputs unreachable.

    Worked information loss¤

    Let \(x=(a,b)\) with independent bits and encode \(z=a\). A generator can learn \(p(z)\) exactly. If the decoder predicts \(b\) uniformly, it reproduces the joint only because the discarded bit was independent. If instead \(b=a\oplus c\) for hidden context \(c\) unavailable to the decoder, reconstruction and conditional control over \(b\) are impossible regardless of generator scale.

    A representation has no task-free quality score¤

    Let \((X,Y)\sim p\) denote an observation and a declared target, and let the encoder be \(Z\sim q_\phi(z\mid X)\). The same \(Z\) may be excellent for one query and unusable for another. Before choosing a metric, specify:

    1. the source and deployment distributions over \(X\);
    2. the target, conditional query, or family of downstream tasks;
    3. which transformations should leave the answer unchanged and which should transform it predictably;
    4. the allowed decoder or adaptation class, its training sample, and its compute budget;
    5. the rate, latency, or state-space constraint on \(Z\); and
    6. the equivalence class under which a latent-recovery claim counts as identified.

    Without these declarations, “semantic,” “compact,” “invariant,” “linear,” and “disentangled” are descriptions without a testable contract.

    Sufficiency, accessibility, and utility¤

    Under the joint law \(p(x,y)q_\phi(z\mid x)\), \(Z\) is sufficient for predicting \(Y\) from \(X\) when

    \[ Y\perp X\mid Z, \qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad I(Y;X\mid Z)=0. \]

    This is a population property for a named target and distribution. It says that an unrestricted decision rule loses no predictive information by receiving \(Z\) instead of \(X\). A linear probe asks a different question: can one member of a particular function class be estimated from a finite sample, with a declared regularizer and optimization procedure? Poor linear-probe performance does not prove that information is absent, and strong probe performance does not prove robustness under distribution shift.

    If \(X\) can be recovered exactly as \(X=d(Z)\), then every target generated from \(X\) is recoverable from \(Z\) in principle. Exact reconstruction therefore implies information-theoretic sufficiency for such targets, but it does not make them linearly accessible, sample-efficient, invariant, causally valid, or semantically axis-aligned. Approximate reconstruction under one distortion is weaker still: a small average error can hide a rare task-critical feature.

    Invariance is a quotient; equivariance retains a transformation¤

    For a deterministic encoder \(f\) and transformation \(g\), invariance means \(f(gx)=f(x)\). For a stochastic encoder, the corresponding statement is equality of conditional laws, \(q_\phi(\cdot\mid gx)=q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)\). Invariance collapses every declared transformation orbit to one code. That is useful only when the target is constant on those orbits.

    If rotation changes the quantity to be generated or controlled, an invariant code has discarded necessary information. An equivariant code instead obeys \(f(gx)=\rho(g)f(x)\) for a known action \(\rho\) in representation space. It keeps the transformation available while organizing it predictably. The geometric-generative guide develops these group actions for sets, graphs, point clouds, and physical systems.

    Disentanglement and identifiability answer different questions¤

    A disentanglement claim relates coordinates or subspaces of \(Z\) to named generative factors. Statistical independence among coordinates is not enough: independent variables can be transformed into different independent variables that mix the original semantics. Conversely, correlated causal factors need not admit independent coordinates even when their mechanisms are scientifically meaningful.

    Identifiability asks whether the available data distribution and model assumptions determine the latent representation up to a declared equivalence class—perhaps permutation and componentwise reparameterization. If two latent models induce the same observed law but disagree beyond that class, no observational metric can select the intended one. The unsupervised non-identifiability result of Locatello et al. and positive results with an observed auxiliary variable under a conditional factorized prior in Khemakhem et al. illustrate why every positive recovery result must state its assumptions. The classical latent-model chapter derives the narrower linear ICA theorem, its permutation and scaling equivalences, and the scale degeneracy inherited by sparse dictionaries. Sparse autoencoders add an amortized encoder and modern architectures, but overcompleteness and reconstruction do not erase those identification and stability questions; a peer-reviewed analysis of identifiable sparse autoencoders is evidence for particular repaired designs under its definitions, not for arbitrary learned features. Augmentation-paired views can also identify invariant content only under a specified latent view model and equivalence class (von Kügelgen et al.).

    Representation evaluation matrix¤

    The rows below are complementary. Passing one row does not silently pass the others.

    Table REP.1. Representation evaluation matrix
    Claimed property Population question Representative evidence What it does not establish
    Observation fidelity Can \(d(Z)\) recover the declared aspects of \(X\) under a specified likelihood or distortion? held-out likelihood/distortion, residual stratification, perceptual and task-specific errors prior-sample quality, semantic axes, compactness, or easy downstream access
    Generative adequacy Do \(Z\sim p_\theta(z)\) and \(X\sim p_\psi(x\mid Z)\) reproduce the required observation law and conditions? latent NLL where available, decoded fidelity/coverage, conditional calibration, memorization audit recovery of the data-generating latent variables
    Target sufficiency Is \(Y\perp X\mid Z\) for the named \(Y\) and deployment law? proper held-out risk against an \(X\)-based reference, conditional-information bounds when justified sufficiency for another target or environment
    Accessibility and utility Can a declared adapter learn the target from \(Z\) with a stated sample and compute budget? frozen linear/nonlinear probes, few-shot curves, fine-tuning, shift tests information absence after a weak probe, or causal validity
    Invariance/equivariance Does \(Z\) collapse or predictably transform along the declared action, while retaining target information? paired transformations, orbit-wise tests, equivariance residuals, out-of-orbit checks benefit for targets that change under the action
    Disentanglement Do named factors map to declared coordinates or subspaces under a stated equivalence? factor labels, controlled pairs/interventions, several factor metrics, intervention tests unsupervised identification, downstream utility, or causal mechanisms by itself
    Identifiability Does equality of observable laws imply equality of latent structure up to the allowed transformations? a theorem with explicit model, support, auxiliary-variable, temporal, or intervention assumptions finite-sample recovery or successful optimization
    Compression/minimality How much rate or state is needed while preserving the declared distortion or task family? actual bitstream rate, entropy-model cross-entropy, information bounds, rate–utility curves a universal smallest representation independent of the query
    Causal adequacy Does \(Z\) retain identified variables or mechanisms needed for intervention or counterfactual queries? multi-environment/interventional tests, mechanism-shift prediction, sensitivity analysis causal semantics from observational reconstruction or prediction alone

    Metric names do not define the claim

    A reconstruction score depends on its distortion and decoder; a probe depends on its adapter and data budget; a disentanglement score depends on known factors and its invariances; an information estimate depends on its estimator; and an intervention test depends on identification assumptions. Report the entire row contract, not just the scalar.

    Adaptation protocols measure different interfaces¤

    A representation is not evaluated by attaching an unspecified "probe." Let \(r_\ell\) denote the extraction rule at layer \(\ell\), including token selection or pooling and normalization, and let \(\mathcal A\) be the class of adapters allowed to act on \(Z=r_\ell(X)\). A reproducible adaptation protocol declares the layer, preprocessing, adapter class, labeled-sample budget, regularization, optimizer, model-selection data, and evaluation distribution. Changing any of these changes the question.

    The common protocols expose five different interfaces:

    • Frozen linear probe. It asks whether the target is linearly accessible from one fixed extraction under the declared data and optimization budget. A positive result supports linear accessibility and measured sample efficiency, not absence of nonlinear information, full-model adaptability, or robustness to another shift.
    • Frozen nonlinear probe. It asks whether a richer declared adapter can recover the target without changing the encoder. A positive result supports accessibility through that function class, not simple organization or proof that the adapter did not learn most of the task itself.
    • Few-shot adaptation curve. It asks how held-out risk changes with labeled data and update budget. It supports sample-efficiency comparisons over the measured range, not asymptotic sufficiency or extrapolation beyond that range.
    • Full fine-tuning. It asks whether an initialization adapts well when the encoder itself may change. It measures optimization and transfer behavior of the initialized system, not only the quality of the original frozen code.
    • Zero-shot fixed interface. It asks whether a declared prompt, similarity rule, or decoder works without task-specific parameter updates. Failure does not establish information absence or failure under another readout.

    Fine-tuning is especially easy to misname: because it changes \(r_\ell\), its result measures an initialization and learning procedure, not only the frozen code. Fair comparisons hold the labeled data, splits, search budget, adapter capacity, stopping rule, and reported layer fixed. They also report a raw-input or randomly initialized baseline when it answers a meaningful control question.

    The XOR code below anticipates the distinction. \(Z_{\mathrm{mix}}\) preserves the class bit exactly, yet no affine readout separates the two classes. A two-layer nonlinear adapter can implement XOR. Calling the linear result "information loss" would therefore confuse one adapter's limitation with the representation's population information.

    Representational similarity is a diagnostic, not a quality score¤

    Similarity methods compare two activation spaces on the same named examples. They answer a correspondence question, which is different from predicting a target or substituting one latent space inside a generator. For centered activation matrices \(X\in\mathbb R^{n\times p}\) and \(Y\in\mathbb R^{n\times q}\) with nonzero feature variation, linear centered kernel alignment is

    \[ \operatorname{CKA}_{\mathrm{lin}}(X,Y) = \frac{\lVert X^{\mathsf T} Y\rVert_{F}^{2}} {\lVert X^{\mathsf T} X\rVert_F \lVert Y^{\mathsf T} Y\rVert_F}. \]

    Linear CKA is unchanged by isotropic rescaling or an orthogonal change of feature basis. It is not invariant to every invertible linear map. This is a deliberate restriction: a similarity statistic invariant to every invertible linear transform becomes uninformative when the feature dimension is at least the number of examples. CCA-family measures use different invariances and regularization choices; none should be reported without the sample set, centering, layer, pooling, kernel or truncation, and uncertainty across seeds. See the primary analysis of Kornblith et al..

    Worked three-point example: CKA one, fixed decoder failure¤

    Consider the already centered matrices

    \[ X= \begin{bmatrix} -1&0\\ 0&0\\ 1&0 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Now swap its two feature coordinates:

    \[ \begin{aligned} Q= \begin{bmatrix} 0&1\\ 1&0 \end{bmatrix}, \\ Y&=XQ. \end{aligned} \]

    Because \(Q\) is orthogonal, \(\operatorname{CKA}_{\mathrm{lin}}(X,Y)=1\). Indeed, the numerator and the two denominator factors are \(4\), \(2\), and \(2\). Yet a fixed decoder \(d(z)=z_1\) returns \((-1,0,1)\) from \(X\) and \((0,0,0)\) from \(Y\). The spaces become decoder-equivalent only if the interface is changed, for example by composing the decoder with \(Q^{\mathsf T}\).

    Thus high CKA can support a claim about activation geometry while failing to establish any of the following:

    • equal downstream risk under a fixed adapter;
    • plug-compatible generator latents or decoder states;
    • recovery of the same semantic coordinates;
    • equality of likelihoods, samples, or conditional responses; or
    • a causal relation between the two networks.

    The converse also needs care. Low similarity under one metric can reflect an allowed reparameterization while both representations support the same target. Use similarity alongside, not in place of, the query-specific rows in the evaluation matrix.

    Worked finite counterexample: four codes, four different claims¤

    Let \(C\) be a class bit and \(N\) a nuisance bit, independently uniform on \(\{0,1\}\). The observation is \(X=(C,N)\) and the first downstream target is \(Y=C\). Compare four deterministic representations:

    \[ Z_{\mathrm{axis}}=(C,N),\qquad Z_{\mathrm{mix}}=(C\oplus N,N),\qquad Z_{\mathrm{task}}=C,\qquad Z_{\mathrm{zero}}=0. \]
    Table REP.2. Worked finite counterexample: four codes, four different claims
    Representation Entropy Reconstruct all of \(X\)? Sufficient for \(Y=C\)? Invariant to changing \(N\)? Axis-aligned with \((C,N)\)?
    \(Z_{\mathrm{axis}}\) \(2\) bits yes yes no yes
    \(Z_{\mathrm{mix}}\) \(2\) bits yes, since \(C=Z_1\oplus Z_2\) yes, with a nonlinear rule no no
    \(Z_{\mathrm{task}}\) \(1\) bit no; the best guess of \(N\) is correct with probability \(1/2\) yes yes only for the declared target
    \(Z_{\mathrm{zero}}\) \(0\) bits no no; the best class accuracy is \(1/2\) yes no

    Both coordinates of \(Z_{\mathrm{mix}}\) are independent uniform bits, so its total correlation is zero. It also reconstructs perfectly. Nevertheless its first coordinate mixes the two named factors, and \(C\) is XOR—not linearly separable—from \((Z_1,Z_2)\). This one example disproves three tempting implications:

    1. zero total correlation does not imply semantic axis alignment;
    2. exact reconstruction does not imply linear accessibility; and
    3. task sufficiency and nuisance invariance do not imply full reconstruction.

    Changing the target exposes the query dependence. If the target becomes \(Y'=N\), then \(Z_{\mathrm{task}}\) is no longer sufficient. More generally, if two observations share a code but have different deterministic target values, no predictor of that target from the code can be correct on both.

    The example also gives an observational identifiability failure. A generator can sample either \(Z_{\mathrm{axis}}\) and use the identity decoder, or sample \(Z_{\mathrm{mix}}\) and invert the XOR map. Both induce exactly the same uniform law over \(X\). Likelihood of \(X\) cannot decide which latent coordinates have the intended semantics.

    What each training objective supplies¤

    An objective creates pressure toward some rows of the matrix, not a certificate for all of them.

    Table REP.3. What each training objective supplies
    Objective or data signal Direct pressure Additional evidence still required
    Reconstruction or denoising preserve information rewarded by the chosen corruption and distortion prior compatibility, semantic accessibility, and behavior on task-critical residuals
    Latent generative likelihood or ELBO fit an observation law while regularizing a latent/inference model as specified semantic identification, interventions, and downstream adaptation
    Autoregressive or masked prediction retain context useful for the chosen conditional prediction problem whether an internal layer is a sufficient, stable, or compact exported representation
    Contrastive or joint-embedding learning align declared positive views and separate or otherwise avoid collapse generative density/decoder semantics, and whether the view relation preserves every target of interest
    Supervised prediction expose information for the labeled target under the training environments reconstruction, other tasks, and robustness to mechanism or environment shift
    Auxiliary variables, temporal structure, grouped observations, or interventions break specific latent symmetries under a declared generative model checking every assumption and the remaining equivalence class
    Joint generative–semantic training optimize several declared rows simultaneously a Pareto comparison showing which gains persist at matched rate, data, model, and compute

    The objectives guide supplies the statistical contracts behind likelihood, variational, contrastive, and information objectives. The VAE guide applies this map to inferred latent variables. The causal-generative guide explains why interventional or counterfactual semantics require more than an observational representation objective.

    Four evaluation layers¤

    1. Codec reconstruction on held-out real data.
    2. Latent distribution modeling.
    3. Decoded sample quality and coverage.
    4. Conditional/task fidelity for information the representation should preserve.

    Reporting only layer 3 hides whether failure came from the codec or generator.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    The codec defines the state space, support, information bottleneck, and observation model. Representation claims are query-specific: reconstruction, generative adequacy, sufficiency, accessibility, similarity, invariance, disentanglement, identifiability, compression, and causal adequacy require different evidence. A probe measures one declared adapter interface; a similarity index measures one declared geometric correspondence. Neither is a task-free score. Chapter 2 compares discrete, continuous, hierarchical, and temporal representations.

    Retrieval practice: representation claims
    1. If \(X=d(Z)\) exactly, what does this imply about predicting a target generated from \(X\), and what does it not imply about a linear probe?
    2. Why does \(\operatorname{TC}(Z)=0\) fail to prove semantic disentanglement?
    3. When does invariance to a transformation destroy target sufficiency?
    4. Is sufficiency a property of \(Z\) alone?
    5. What must an identifiability claim declare before it is meaningful?
    6. In the finite-bit example, which code is the most compressed sufficient representation for \(Y=C\) among the four shown?
    7. Why does full fine-tuning not measure only the frozen representation?
    8. In the three-point example, why can linear CKA equal one while a fixed decoder fails?

    Solutions. (1) Any predictor from \(X\) can in principle be composed with \(d\) to act on \(Z\); a restricted linear adapter may still be unable to express or estimate that composition. (2) Independence is a distributional property of learned coordinates, while semantic disentanglement relates them to named factors; a measure-preserving mixing can retain independence. (3) When the target differs within a collapsed transformation orbit. (4) No. It is defined relative to a target and joint/deployment law. (5) The observed model, assumptions or auxiliary information, and the equivalence class under which recovery counts. (6) \(Z_{\mathrm{task}}=C\), with one bit. (7) Fine-tuning updates the encoder and therefore measures the initialized system's adaptation path as well as its starting code. (8) CKA treats the orthogonal coordinate swap as identical geometry, while the fixed decoder assigns meaning to one particular coordinate and is not transformed with the representation.

    Bridge to Chapter 2. The query map tells us what a representation must preserve. We next study the discrete, continuous, hierarchical, and temporal state spaces that implement those choices.

    2. Discrete, Continuous, Hierarchical, and Temporal Codecs¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and latent-variable modeling. By the end, you should be able to compare discrete, continuous, hierarchical, residual, and temporal codecs by rate, distortion, prior, and decoder contracts; diagnose codebook collapse and temporal inconsistency; explain how codec choice changes the downstream generative target; and select metrics that expose perceptual and task-relevant information loss.

    Vector quantization¤

    VQ-VAE maps encoder output to the nearest codebook vector:

    \[ k^*=\arg\min_k\|z_e(x)-e_k\|^2, \qquad z_q=e_{k^*}. \]

    The integer \(k^*\) and vector \(e_{k^*}\) have different roles. A categorical generator models indices; a decoder consumes embeddings. The indices have no intrinsic metric—permuting their names and applying the same permutation to the codebook preserves the codec—whereas distances between embeddings affect quantization and decoding. A prior trained before such a permutation is no longer compatible.

    Nearest-neighbour assignment is discontinuous. A common hard VQ recipe copies the decoder gradient to the encoder with a straight-through surrogate, updates selected embeddings with a codebook loss or moving assignment statistics, and uses a commitment loss to keep encoder outputs near their selected codes. These are different update routes. The VAE estimator chapter derives their stop-gradient equations, gives a complete EMA example, and compares them with exact enumeration, score-function estimators, and Gumbel–Softmax.

    Codec, vocabulary, and prior are three interfaces¤

    Let \(c(x)=(c_1,\ldots,c_T)\) be the encoded index sequence. A two-stage system first learns an encoder–codebook–decoder and then fits \(p_\psi(c_1,\ldots,c_T)\) to frozen encoded data. Its behavior must be audited at three interfaces:

    Table REP.4. Codec, vocabulary, and prior are three interfaces
    Interface Representative checks Failure hidden by the next interface
    Observation \(\leftrightarrow\) codec reconstruction likelihood/distortion, task fidelity, support, latency information discarded before generation
    Encoder \(\leftrightarrow\) vocabulary quantization error, code counts, entropy/perplexity, dead-code duration, assignment stability nominal vocabulary that supplies little effective capacity
    Vocabulary \(\leftrightarrow\) prior held-out token NLL, conditioning, sequence length, sampler cost, checkpoint/index compatibility prior mismatch or expensive code dependencies

    A codebook of size \(K\) permits at most \(K\) symbols per position and a fixed- width identifier needs \(\lceil\log_2K\rceil\) bits. Neither is the source rate. If observed code frequencies are \(\widehat p_k\), their marginal empirical entropy is \(-\sum_k\widehat p_k\log_2\widehat p_k\) bits per token; a contextual entropy model can exploit dependencies and assign a different cross-entropy. Actual compression additionally requires synchronized probability tables and a bitstream implementation.

    Discrete indices support categorical autoregressive, masked, diffusion, and other priors. Quantization can shorten or regularize the state sequence, but it also adds discontinuity and reconstruction loss. Code utilization alone does not settle the trade-off: a tokenizer can use all codes to encode nuisance noise, and a low-entropy source can legitimately need only a subset.

    From latent tensor to bitstream¤

    A learned representation becomes a compression system only after its protocol is specified:

    1. an analysis transform maps \(x\) to latent values;
    2. quantization maps those values to discrete symbols \(\hat z\);
    3. an entropy model assigns synchronized probabilities \(m_\psi(\hat z)\);
    4. an entropy coder and decoder turn the symbols into and out of a bitstream;
    5. a synthesis transform reconstructs \(\hat x\).

    The following measurements answer different questions:

    Table REP.5. From latent tensor to bitstream
    Quantity What it measures What it omits
    Latent shape Number of stored or modeled scalars precision and statistical redundancy
    Nominal index rate tokens \(\times\lceil\log_2K\rceil\) nonuniform and contextual predictability
    Empirical entropy frequency uncertainty in a chosen sample ordering, side information, coder overhead
    Entropy-model cross-entropy ideal expected bits under \(m_\psi\) finite coder and file-format overhead
    Actual bitrate serialized bits per pixel, sample, second, or source symbol reconstruction and perceptual quality
    Generator token count state-space size seen by the prior whether any code is serialized at all

    For a small audit, suppose 16 tokens contain symbols with counts \((8,4,2,2)\). A fixed-width 256-entry vocabulary uses a nominal \(16\times8=128\) bits. The empirical entropy of the four used symbols is \(1.75\) bits per token, or 28 ideal bits for this sequence under its empirical frequencies. Neither number is an actual file size: a decoder must know the probability model, and a real stream has termination, finite-precision, and possibly header costs. A contextual autoregressive entropy model can also assign a rate different from the order-free frequency calculation.

    In latent generation, a compact grid may be chosen primarily to reduce prior compute. Calling a \(16\times\) spatial downsampling factor “\(256\times\) compression” confuses positions with information: channels, precision, entropy, and overhead remain. If no entropy-coded bitstream is produced, report latent shape and generator compute rather than a storage compression ratio.

    Finite scalar and factorized quantization¤

    Finite Scalar Quantization replaces learned vector lookup with bounded scalar levels across dimensions. It can improve code utilization and simplicity, but factorization imposes geometry and does not eliminate the rate–distortion trade-off.

    Residual, product, and lookup-free quantization¤

    One code index need not carry the entire quantization budget. In residual vector quantization, the first codebook approximates \(z\), the next quantizes the remaining error, and so on:

    \[ r_0=z,\qquad e_\ell=Q_\ell(r_{\ell-1}),\qquad r_\ell=r_{\ell-1}-e_\ell,\qquad \widehat z=\sum_{\ell=1}^L e_\ell. \]

    With codebook sizes \(K_1,\ldots,K_L\), a fixed-width representation uses \(\sum_\ell\lceil\log_2K_\ell\rceil\) nominal bits per latent position. The actual entropy can be smaller and depends on correlations within and across levels. Dropping later levels supplies a variable-rate hierarchy only if the decoder was trained for those truncations. SoundStream is a canonical residual-quantization codec with structured quantizer dropout; its reported audio results are protocol-specific rather than a theorem that residual quantization dominates a single codebook.

    Product quantization splits coordinates into groups and combines their indices. It reduces lookup cost but assumes that the chosen grouping exposes useful factorization. Lookup-free or binary schemes encode signs/bits without storing an explicit table of all \(2^d\) combinations. MAGVIT-v2 uses lookup-free quantization in a visual tokenizer. Eliminating the explicit table does not eliminate the exponentially large nominal alphabet, entropy modeling, bit imbalance, or decoder distortion.

    Reconstruction and prior learnability are separate objectives¤

    Let \(C=E(X)\) be a discrete code sequence and let \(m_\psi(C)\) be its learned prior. Codec reconstruction evaluates \(D(X,D(C))\); prior learnability is probed by held-out

    \[ \mathbb E[-\log m_\psi(C)]. \]

    Two lossless representations can have identical ideal information but different sequence interfaces. If \(X=(U,V)\) consists of two independent fair bits, a tokenizer may emit two binary tokens \((U,V)\) or one four-way token with index \(2U+V\). Both use two ideal bits and reconstruct exactly. The first needs two generation positions with binary outputs; the second needs one position with a four-class output. With finite networks, masks, vocabularies, and hardware, their optimization and latency need not match even though neither contains more information.

    Tokenizer--prior co-design can add a proxy for downstream predictability, denoising robustness, or generator loss. It changes the representation target and can sacrifice a reconstruction property not captured by that proxy. Latent Denoising Makes Good Visual Tokenizers, an ICLR 2026 paper, reports gains across its six named downstream generators when tokenizer latents are trained to reconstruct after corruption. This is evidence for downstream-aligned tokenizer training under those protocols, not a task-independent definition of a good latent.

    Retrieval practice: quantizer composition
    1. Does an implicit \(2^d\) lookup-free alphabet have zero rate?
    2. When does truncating residual levels define a supported bitrate?
    3. Can equal reconstruction imply equal prior compute?

    Solutions. (1) No; its bits have entropy and must still be generated or coded. (2) When training and evaluation include the same truncation rule and decoder behavior. (3) No; token count, alphabet, dependencies, and architecture can differ.

    Continuous latents¤

    Continuous codecs avoid quantization and work naturally with diffusion or flows. Scale, covariance, and tail behavior affect noise schedules and conditioning. A learned latent need not be Gaussian merely because the generator starts from Gaussian noise.

    Latent Diffusion separates perceptual compression from generative modeling. This reduces spatial compute but makes decoder distortion and latent scaling part of every result.

    Hierarchical and multiscale tokens¤

    Coarse latents capture layout; fine latents capture texture. Pyramid or residual quantizers can allocate rate adaptively. A generator may model scales sequentially, jointly, or with separate experts. Coarse errors propagate, while independent fine-scale decoding can lose global consistency.

    Video and audio¤

    Temporal codecs compress space and time. Causal encoders support streaming but see less future context; bidirectional encoders improve compression but add latency. Temporal stride can erase fast events, lip–audio alignment, physical contacts, or beats.

    Video reconstruction needs temporal metrics in addition to frame quality. Token count scales with duration, resolution, frame rate, and stride; reporting tokens per clip without these quantities is meaningless.

    Semantic versus reconstruction tokens¤

    Understanding favors invariance to nuisance detail; generation needs enough detail to reconstruct. A single representation can compromise both. Dual codebooks, shared indices with separate features, or decoupled encoders trade architectural unity against task specialization.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Discrete tokens simplify categorical modeling; continuous latents simplify geometric transport; hierarchies allocate information across scale; temporal codecs determine synchronization and latency. Chapter 3 explains how modalities interact after representation.

    3. Conditioning, Fusion, and Cross-Modal Alignment¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional generation and representation learning. By the end, you should be able to distinguish early, late, cross-attentive, product, mixture, and shared-latent fusion; state what contrastive alignment identifies and leaves ambiguous; handle missing modalities under an explicit observation law; and design conditional-fidelity, retrieval, and leakage tests for paired and weakly paired data.

    For modalities \(X,Y\), a system may model \(p(x,y)\), \(p(y\mid x)\), two separate marginals, or only aligned embeddings. These are different probabilistic claims.

    This chapter focuses on representation and fusion. The conditioning and steering volume owns the reusable probability semantics for target tilting, score and discrete guidance, hard constraints, candidate selection, and retrieval composition.

    Fusion patterns¤

    • Early fusion: convert modalities to a common token sequence before a shared backbone.
    • Late fusion: encode separately and combine pooled states or predictions.
    • Cross-attention: one stream queries another while retaining modality-specific representations.
    • Joint latent fusion: encode modalities into a shared stochastic latent.
    • Mixture of experts: share routing/backbone components but retain modality experts.

    Early fusion enables dense interaction but can be dominated by the longest modality. Late fusion is modular but can miss fine alignment. Cross-attention cost scales with both sequence lengths and needs masking that matches the generative factorization.

    Conditional and joint factorizations¤

    A text-to-image system estimates \(p(I\mid T)\); an image-caption model estimates \(p(T\mid I)\). A joint AR sequence can define

    \[ p(T,I)=p(T)p(I\mid T) \]

    under one order, but reversing the order defines different conditionals and training exposure. Bidirectional understanding attention cannot be used unchanged for causal generation without leakage.

    The pair law defines a contrastive objective¤

    "Positive" and "negative" are sampling semantics, not intrinsic properties of two objects. Let \(p_+(x,y)\) be the declared positive-pair law and let its \(Y\)-marginal supply negatives. With one positive candidate and \(N-1\) independent marginal candidates, a common InfoNCE convention is

    \[ \begin{aligned} w_{\eta,j}&=\exp s_\eta(X,Y_j),\\ Z_N&=\sum_{j=1}^{N}w_{\eta,j},\\ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{NCE}} &=-\mathbb E\!\left[ \log \frac{w_{\eta,1}}{Z_N} \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The candidate law is

    \[ \begin{aligned} Y_1&\sim p_+(\,\cdot\mid X), \\ Y_{2:N}&\overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}p_+(y). \end{aligned} \]

    Under this construction and the regularity needed by the variational argument,

    \[ I_{p_+}(X;Y) \ge \log N-\mathcal L_{\mathrm{NCE}}. \]

    At the population optimum over a sufficiently expressive score class, the score represents the log density ratio \(\log p_+(y\mid x)-\log p_+(y)\) up to an \(x\)-only offset. This classification interpretation does not make the embeddings a normalized joint generator, a decoder, or a calibrated likelihood model. The bound is at most \(\log N\); the true mutual information is not. Consequently, standard contrastive bounds can severely underestimate large mutual information, and dependent in-batch sampling, multiple positives, hard-negative mining, or a different negative proposal requires a new derivation rather than the same formula by habit. See Poole et al. and Sordoni et al..

    The pair law also chooses an invariance. A marginal "negative" may share the same task-relevant class as the anchor, producing a class collision; a positive pair may differ in a factor that the downstream generator must preserve. Theory linking contrastive loss to downstream classification therefore states a latent class or view model rather than deriving usefulness from the loss name alone (Saunshi et al.).

    Worked two-view example: useful invariance, lost generative detail¤

    Let \(C,N_0,N_1\) be independent uniform bits and construct two views

    \[ X=(C,N_0), \qquad Y=(C,N_1). \]

    The views share exactly the class bit: \(I(X;Y)=H(C)=1\) bit. An encoder returning only \(C\) preserves all common information and is ideal for class retrieval. It is also invariant to the independently redrawn nuisance. But it cannot reconstruct the original \(N_0\), and two marginal candidates with the same \(C\) are false negatives for a class-only task. If \(N_0\) controls pose, timing, or identity required by conditional generation, this positive-pair construction has deliberately removed task-critical information.

    Reconstruction can make the opposite trade: it may preserve every local detail without making class semantics easily accessible. A multimodal system must therefore state whether its alignment loss is intended for retrieval, conditioning, token grounding, reconstruction, generation, or some declared combination—and evaluate each interface separately.

    Synchronization¤

    Audio, video, text, actions, and sensors have different clocks. Alignment may be timestamp-, event-, phoneme-, frame-, or learned-attention based. Resampling can introduce aliasing and apparent lead/lag. A multimodal generator should state whether it models simultaneous samples, causal history, or offline bidirectional context.

    Missing and asynchronous modalities¤

    Missingness may be random, informative, scheduled by an acquisition policy, or caused by sensor and user actions. It creates a query over a subset, not just a shorter tensor. For a modality-presence mask \(R\in\{0,1\}^M\), distinguish:

    Table REP.6. Missing and asynchronous modalities
    Mask Unit Meaning Typical failure if conflated
    Modality mask complete stream whether image, audio, text, or another modality exists a zero-valued sensor is treated as absent
    Padding mask positions within a stream which token/time positions are structural padding padding tokens leak into pooled features or likelihoods
    Corruption mask observed values or spans which available entries were hidden by a training objective artificial masking is mistaken for real acquisition missingness
    Causal mask ordered token pairs which information is permitted by the generative factorization future targets leak into likelihood training
    Loss mask predictions which outputs have ground truth and should be scored generated but unavailable targets contribute fabricated losses

    A mask token, absent stream, imputed value, empty sequence, and genuine zero observation must therefore have different representations. If a modality encoder is skipped, the fusion layer still needs its identity and absence state; otherwise two different observed subsets can map to the same fused input.

    Mask support is a training distribution¤

    Let \(\rho_{\mathrm{train}}(S)\) be the probability that training presents the observed-modality subset \(S\). The learned risk is an average over this mask distribution. Good performance under one sampled deletion rate does not imply good performance for every subset, for a different deletion frequency, or for informative deployment failure.

    A defensible protocol therefore:

    1. lists the deployment queries \(p(x_T\mid x_S)\);
    2. gives required subsets nonzero training probability or supplies a declared test-time inference algorithm;
    3. keeps fully observed and important single-modality anchors when those queries matter;
    4. records which target modalities are actually available for each loss; and
    5. reports results separately for each mask rather than only averaging over a convenient random-mask mixture.

    The VAE chapter derives the corresponding subset ELBO, Gaussian product update, and missingness assumptions. Its central warning applies to other architectures too: an input format that accepts missing streams is not evidence of calibrated inference on those streams.

    Partial pairing does not identify a joint by itself¤

    Suppose one dataset contains only images and another only text. The two marginals \(p(I)\) and \(p(T)\) do not identify their coupling \(p(I,T)\): many joint laws share the same marginals. Paired examples, temporal co-occurrence, identifiers, known mechanisms, optimal-transport assumptions, or other structural information must determine the association. A shared embedding or matching marginal distributions is insufficient evidence that generated pairs are semantically correct.

    Copula Models formalizes this gap as a separate dependence distribution with fixed marginals. That construction can make the missing coupling explicit, but it still needs paired or structural evidence; Sklar's theorem does not select the semantically correct cross-modal copula.

    Missingness and acquisition can be informative¤

    If a sensor is activated only after an alarming earlier measurement, its presence conveys information. If users withhold a modality based on its unseen content, absence depends on a missing value. Random modality dropout does not reproduce either mechanism. Under informative missingness, condition on or model the acquisition process and perform sensitivity analyses; otherwise the learned conditional may answer the randomized-dropout problem instead of the deployment problem.

    For asynchronous streams, presence is time-indexed. A delayed packet is not the same as a permanently missing modality, and future arrival can be unavailable to an online generator even if it appears in an offline record. Report the decision time, permitted history, latency tolerance, and whether inference is filtering or smoothing.

    Evaluation matrix for incomplete inputs¤

    For every required observed subset \(S\), evaluate:

    • reconstruction of modalities in \(S\) separately from generation of targets outside \(S\);
    • conditional quality, diversity, calibration, and coverage for every target direction;
    • consistency when an additional, agreeing modality is added;
    • behavior under contradictory modalities rather than only clean pairs;
    • mask-frequency and missingness-mechanism shift; and
    • performance of each modality so an averaged score cannot hide modality collapse.

    Pointwise imputation error rewards conditional means and can punish valid diversity. Pair it with a proper conditional score or distributional diagnostic that matches the output type.

    Worked causal mask¤

    Suppose text tokens precede image tokens in one AR sequence. Image tokens may attend to all text and previous image tokens; text must not attend to future image tokens during likelihood training. Allowing that attention yields a lower training loss but invalidates the claimed factorization at generation time.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Fusion is meaningful only relative to a joint or conditional query, modality and causal masks, pairing mechanism, observation process, and clock. A contrastive objective inherits its meaning from the positive and negative sampling laws; an InfoNCE bound is neither a decoder nor a normalized generative model. Missing-stream robustness is a property of the training distribution and inference algorithm, not of a mask token alone. Chapter 4 studies shared backbones and AR–diffusion hybrids that combine several objectives and representations.

    Retrieval practice: alignment and pairing
    1. What sampling laws must accompany an InfoNCE loss?
    2. Why does the lower bound \(\log N-\mathcal L_{\mathrm{NCE}}\) become insensitive to sufficiently large mutual information at fixed \(N\)?
    3. In the two-bit view example, which information is shared and which information is deliberately unavailable across views?
    4. Why can unpaired modality marginals fail to identify a joint distribution even when both marginals are fitted perfectly?

    Solutions. (1) The positive joint or conditional law, the negative proposal, the number and dependence of candidates, and any multiple-positive or mining rule. (2) The loss is nonnegative, so this particular lower bound cannot exceed \(\log N\), although the true mutual information can. (3) The class bit \(C\) is shared; the independently redrawn nuisances \(N_0\) and \(N_1\) are not. (4) Many couplings have the same two marginals, so paired observations or a justified structural assumption must select one.

    4. Shared Backbones and AR–Diffusion Hybrids¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes autoregressive and diffusion model mechanics. By the end, you should be able to distinguish shared parameters from shared probability factorizations, map token/patch/latent interfaces in AR–diffusion hybrids, identify training-objective and scheduling conflicts, and compare unified backbones with composed specialists under matched codecs, conditioning, compute, and output laws.

    A shared system may reuse parameters without sharing tokens, objectives, or probability semantics. Separate encoders can feed one backbone; one tokenizer can feed separate heads; a shared sequence can alternate discrete AR and continuous denoising segments.

    Joint objective¤

    A schematic hybrid loss is

    \[ \mathcal L =\lambda_{\mathrm{AR}}\mathcal L_{\mathrm{CE}} +\lambda_{\mathrm{diff}}\mathcal L_{\mathrm{denoise}} +\lambda_{\mathrm{align}}\mathcal L_{\mathrm{align}}. \]

    The terms have different units, gradient scales, data frequencies, and noise. Static weights do not ensure balanced learning. Gradient conflict, task sampling, expert routing, and curriculum order are part of the model.

    Representation alignment is an auxiliary generative objective¤

    A denoising or transport model can train an internal activation against a teacher representation while retaining its original generative target. A generic form first names the aligned student and teacher states,

    \[ \begin{aligned} A_{\theta,\ell} &=P_\ell h_{\theta,\ell}(X_t,t,C),\\ T_\ell &=\operatorname{sg}\!\left(E_\ell(X_0)\right). \end{aligned} \]

    The combined objective is then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L_{\mathrm{total}}(\theta) &=\mathcal L_{\mathrm{gen}}(\theta) +\lambda\,\mathbb E\!\left[ d\!\left(A_{\theta,\ell},T_\ell\right) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    Here \(h_{\theta,\ell}\) is a declared hidden layer, \(P_\ell\) reconciles feature shape or basis, \(E_\ell\) is an external or jointly learned representation, \(\operatorname{sg}\) stops teacher gradients when the teacher is frozen, and \(d\) may compare vectors, directions, relations, or kernels. The protocol must also state which noise levels, tokens, spatial scales, layers, and data augmentations receive the loss. The teacher is commonly removed at inference, so sampling NFE need not increase even though training compute and memory do.

    This construction is an objective component, not a new probability family. It can change optimization and the finite learned solution, but it does not by itself replace the base diffusion, flow, autoregressive, or latent-variable law, nor does it specify a sampler. The base loss remains responsible for the generative query unless the paper proves a stronger relationship.

    Failure case: the teacher collapses a required distinction¤

    Suppose two observations \(x^{(a)}\) and \(x^{(b)}\) require different conditional outputs but \(E_\ell(x^{(a)})=E_\ell(x^{(b)})=e\). Setting both projected hidden states to \(e\) gives zero alignment loss for both examples. The auxiliary term therefore cannot force the generator to distinguish them. A reconstruction, likelihood, denoising, or conditional loss may still retain the missing detail, but that success must be measured rather than attributed to alignment.

    REPA demonstrates this component for selected diffusion-transformer states and a frozen visual encoder under its ImageNet protocol. Later work extends the design to U-Nets and self-supervised spectral targets. These results establish useful system instances, not a theorem that one teacher, layer, or feature geometry is universally optimal.

    AR–diffusion composition¤

    An AR backbone can plan discrete structure or interleaved modality spans while a diffusion/flow head generates continuous blocks. This is hybrid composition, not a proof that AR and diffusion objectives are equivalent. Total inference cost includes planning tokens, repeated continuous-head calls, codec decoding, and guidance.

    Transfusion is a prominent early technical report combining next-token prediction for discrete text with diffusion for continuous image patches. Its architectural result should be labeled Technical report unless an archival version is cited.

    Shared versus decoupled visual encoding¤

    Janus, CVPR 2025, argues for decoupled visual encoding while retaining a unified transformer. TokenFlow uses semantic and pixel-level codebooks aligned through shared indices. These are distinct answers to the semantic–reconstruction tension.

    Interleaved generation¤

    Documents, conversations, demonstrations, and agent traces can alternate text, images, audio, video, and actions. Training requires explicit boundary tokens, ordering, loss masks, and modality-specific decoders. Long high-rate visual/audio spans can overwhelm text tokens unless sampling and loss normalization compensate.

    Shared Capability versus Synergy¤

    A model that performs understanding and generation separately is multifunctional. Synergy requires evidence that understanding improves generation or generation improves understanding under controlled comparisons. Shared parameters can instead cause negative transfer.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Shared systems combine representations, losses, and components at several possible layers. Architectural sharing is not probabilistic or functional synergy. Representation alignment is one auxiliary objective with a declared teacher, layer, projection, distance, and compute cost—not a new generative family or sampler. Chapter 5 gives diagnostics for imbalance, collapse, synchronization, and evaluation leakage.

    Retrieval practice: representation alignment in a generator
    1. Which part of the displayed objective still defines the base generative query?
    2. Why can the teacher often be removed at inference without making training free?
    3. Give one condition under which zero representation-alignment loss fails to preserve a required distinction.

    Solutions. (1) \(\mathcal L_{\mathrm{gen}}\) retains the declared denoising, transport, likelihood, or other family-specific target unless a stronger relationship is derived. (2) The frozen teacher and projection target may be needed only to compute the training loss; teacher inference, activation storage, and the auxiliary backward pass still consume training compute and memory. (3) If the teacher maps two observations requiring different outputs to the same feature, matching that feature cannot distinguish the observations.

    5. Training Dynamics, Modality Imbalance, and Evaluation¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–4 and multi-objective optimization. By the end, you should be able to diagnose gradient, data-volume, token-count, and loss-scale imbalance across modalities; distinguish collapse from legitimate task specialization; audit codec and end-to-end metrics separately; detect identity leakage and contamination; and report modality-specific compute and uncertainty.

    Sources of imbalance¤

    • token-count imbalance from images/video/audio;
    • dataset-size and quality differences;
    • loss-scale and gradient-norm differences;
    • modality-specific optimizer or normalization behavior;
    • easier tasks dominating shared features;
    • missing-pair and selection bias;
    • expensive modalities receiving fewer updates.

    Mitigations include balanced task sampling, per-token/per-example normalization, uncertainty or gradient-based weighting, separate experts, gradient surgery, staged curricula, and replay. Each changes the effective objective and must be ablated.

    Collapse and underuse¤

    A decoder may ignore a conditioning modality; a router may starve experts; a codebook may collapse; a strong text prior may hallucinate over visual evidence. Diagnose with modality ablation, counterfactual swaps, attention/intervention tests, code utilization, conditional mutual information proxies, and performance under conflicting cues.

    Attention weights alone do not prove causal use of a modality.

    Codec metrics¤

    Report measured bitrate when a real bitstream exists, together with entropy-model cross-entropy, token count, code entropy, utilization, PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, reconstruction FID/FVD, and temporal/spectral error as appropriate. State bits per what—pixel, audio sample, second, point, token, or complete object—and whether headers, side information, and model weights are included. Compare at matched resolution, frame rate, bitrate, and decoder compute. Perceptual metrics can reward plausible substitutions that are unacceptable for text, identity, medicine, or science.

    Evaluate the codec and latent generator both separately and end to end. Codec reconstruction gives an upper bound on what a perfect latent prior can reproduce from encoded data; low reconstruction distortion does not show that the prior covers the latent law. Conversely, a strong decoded-sample score can hide information already discarded by the codec. The autoregressive guide connects categorical NLL to sequential coding, while the VAE guide explains when a latent-variable bound has a lossless bits-back interpretation.

    System metrics¤

    Evaluate each modality and direction separately, then interleaved tasks, grounding, synchronization, conditional controllability, and synergy. Use human evaluation where automated metrics miss semantics, with rater protocol and uncertainty.

    Leakage and contamination¤

    Paired web data can contain duplicates, captions generated from the image, benchmark answers, watermarks, or near-identical clips. Cross-modal retrieval and generation can exploit identity leakage rather than alignment. Split at source/entity/time level and audit nearest neighbors across modalities.

    Compute accounting¤

    Include tokenizer and encoder cost, cached versus online embeddings, sequence lengths by modality, active expert parameters, generation NFE, decoder cost, and wall-clock hardware. A shared model can reduce orchestration while increasing total compute.

    Retrieval practice: multimodal training and evaluation
    1. Why can equal loss weights still produce unequal influence across modalities?
    2. What test distinguishes codec loss from generator loss in a latent multimodal system?
    3. How can cross-modal retrieval appear strong because of identity leakage?
    4. Which compute units should be reported when modalities have different tokenizations and encoders?

    Solutions. (1) Gradient norms, batch frequencies, token counts, noise, and attainable loss scales can differ. (2) Evaluate reconstruction/representation ceilings with true latents or codec round trips, then evaluate the generator in the fixed latent space and the composed output system. (3) Near-duplicate items, shared metadata, watermarks, or entity overlap can let the model match identity rather than semantics. (4) Per-modality tokens/examples, encoder/decoder work, shared-backbone work, active parameters, transfers, and end-to-end latency or FLOPs under the deployed mixture.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Multimodal training is a resource-allocation and evidence problem as much as an architecture problem. Balanced loss does not guarantee balanced information use. Chapter 6 audits peer-reviewed research and defines research exercises.

    6. Research Directions¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the complete representation and multimodal framework. By the end, you should be able to formulate a research question at the representation, alignment, generator, or system layer; construct ablations that hold the other layers fixed; evaluate missing-modality, scaling, temporal, and safety behavior; and classify evidence for unified multimodal systems without attributing every result to the shared backbone.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • REPA, ICLR 2025 Oral — Peer-reviewed research for aligning selected diffusion-transformer hidden states to a frozen visual encoder; the reported convergence and generation gains are architecture-, teacher-, data-, objective-, and evaluation-bounded.
    • U-REPA, NeurIPS 2025 — Peer-reviewed research for adapting representation alignment to U-Net stages, spatial resolutions, and relational feature losses; it demonstrates that the DiT recipe does not transfer without architectural choices.
    • Revisiting Spectral Representations in Generative Diffusion Models, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a self-supervised spectral regularizer across reported image and point-cloud protocols; its perturbation-kernel analysis and empirical gains do not make spectral alignment identical to the base score objective in every parameterization.
    • Evaluating the Representation Space of Diffusion Models via Self-Supervised Principles, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a representation-geometry diagnostic of invariance and memorization under the paper's models and data regimes; an internal diagnostic is not a universal sample-quality or privacy certificate.
    • Reconciling Visual Perception and Generation in Diffusion Models, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for joint perception and generation with gradient alignment; joint capability and reported mutual gains remain protocol-specific rather than automatic consequences of parameter sharing.
    • AToken, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a shared 4D image/video/3D tokenizer with continuous and discrete latents; benchmark breadth does not prove one representation is optimal for every domain.
    • Diffusion Transformers with Representation Autoencoders, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for pairing frozen representation encoders with learned decoders and adapting DiT design to the resulting high-dimensional latents; its ImageNet protocol does not establish universal superiority over reconstruction-trained codecs.
    • VQRAE, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for continuous semantic features and discrete generative tokens in one tokenizer; understanding, reconstruction, code utilization, and generation remain separate reported interfaces.
    • BiGain, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a training-free frequency-aware token-compression method under reported diffusion classification and generation protocols; its speed–accuracy–FID balance is not a task-independent sufficiency theorem.
    • UniAR, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a shared discrete visual tokenizer and autoregressive context across understanding/generation; conclusions remain tied to its training and evaluation protocols.
    • Unison, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research benchmark separating isolated capability from understanding–generation synergy; learned-judge agreement is not a substitute for all human evaluation.
    • Self-Adaptive Interleaved Visual Reasoners, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for adaptive planning/refinement in anything-to-image tasks; it is a system result, not a tokenizer theorem.
    • UniMedVL, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for unified medical understanding and generation across reported modalities; clinical utility requires prospective external validation.

    Withdrawn submissions are excluded from peer-reviewed conclusions. Technical reports remain labeled as such even when influential.

    1. Can one representation achieve a Pareto improvement in rate, reconstruction, semantics, and downstream generation rather than moving along a trade-off?
    2. When does shared context produce measurable bidirectional synergy rather than parameter sharing alone?

    4. Can codecs expose calibrated uncertainty about discarded information?
    5. How should multimodal models remain coherent under missing, delayed, or contradictory evidence?
    6. Which evaluation detects that generated media is internally understood by the same model?

    Exercise 1 solution

    If code frequencies are \(p_k\), the order-free empirical entropy is \(H=-\sum_kp_k\log_2p_k\le\log_2K\). A contextual entropy model can exploit ordering and should be evaluated by held-out cross-entropy. A serialized stream additionally includes coder inefficiency, termination, side information, and headers. Low entropy can reveal collapsed or underused codes even when the nominal vocabulary is large, but low rate alone does not establish useful reconstruction.

    Cumulative retrieval: representations and multimodal systems
    1. Why is a tokenizer or codec part of the probability and evaluation contract?
    2. What is the difference between representation alignment and conditional generation fidelity?
    3. Which ablation isolates a shared-backbone claim from a better codec claim?
    4. What evidence supports a claim of robust missing-modality generation?

    Solutions. (1) It defines the modeled alphabet/support, information loss, prior target, and map back to observations. (2) Alignment makes paired or semantic representations comparable; fidelity asks whether the generated conditional output law matches the requested condition. (3) Hold data, codec/tokenizer, objectives, conditioning, compute, and evaluation fixed while replacing only shared versus separate backbone parameters. (4) Train/test masks matching declared observation processes, held-out missingness patterns, calibrated uncertainty, strong unimodal and imputation baselines, leakage checks, and modality-specific plus joint metrics.

    Final synthesis¤

    Representations determine what a generator can know and reproduce. Multimodal systems succeed when codecs, clocks, masks, objectives, and evaluation match the intended joint or conditional queries. Shared capability should describe a demonstrated interaction, not merely shared parameters.
