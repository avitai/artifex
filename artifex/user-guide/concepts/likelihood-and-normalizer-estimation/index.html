    Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Latent Variables and Inference
    • Objectives and Statistical Distances
    • Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics
    • Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods
    • Energy-Based Models (EBMs) Explained

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Evaluating Generative Models

    A generative model can have a tractable joint density while hiding the quantity a reader actually wants behind a sum, integral, or partition function. In a latent model, that quantity may be the observed-data likelihood. In Bayesian model comparison, it may be the evidence. In an energy-based model, it is the partition function required to turn relative energies into probabilities. These are different scientific queries, but their estimators share one mathematical problem: integrate a nonnegative function whose important mass is hard to reach.

    This volume develops that problem as a coherent part of generative modeling. It separates the target integral from its estimator, derives ordinary importance sampling before annealed methods, follows normalizer ratios through AIS and SMC, compares bridge, path, and nested constructions, and ends with diagnostics and a reporting contract. No estimator is called “the likelihood” without its proposal, sample count, transitions, numerical choices, and uncertainty.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2 establish the query and the importance-weight identity. Practitioner: Chapters 1–5 provide method selection and diagnostics. Research: read all chapters, including path-space correctness, heavy-tail failure, bidirectional checks, learned transport, and current complexity results.

    Reader orientation¤

    The recurring notation¤

    Table EST.1. The recurring notation
    Symbol Meaning
    \(\gamma(x)\) evaluable nonnegative unnormalized density or mass
    \(Z=\int\gamma(x)d\mu(x)\) unknown normalizing constant
    \(\pi(x)=\gamma(x)/Z\) normalized target law
    \(q(x)\) normalized proposal that can be sampled and scored
    \(w(x)=\gamma(x)/q(x)\) ordinary importance weight
    \(\gamma_t,Z_t,\pi_t\) unnormalized density, normalizer, and law at bridge index \(t\)
    \(K\) number of independent importance draws or trajectories
    \(N\) number of SMC particles or evaluated data examples, as stated locally

    The base measure and support are part of every definition. Sums replace integrals on finite spaces.

    1. Name the Integral Before Choosing the Estimator¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know joint, marginal, and conditional probability; expectations; latent variables; and normalized versus unnormalized densities. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • distinguish an observed-data marginal, Bayesian evidence, and EBM partition function;
    • express each as \(Z=\int\gamma,d\mu\) without erasing what is integrated out;
    • identify when enumeration, conjugacy, dynamic programming, or a tractable transformation is exact;
    • compute one finite latent likelihood and posterior exactly; and
    • state why a log estimate, a lower bound, and an unbiased estimate of \(Z\) are different objects.

    Four queries with one integral shape¤

    For a latent-variable model,

    \[ p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz \]

    integrates out a per-example latent \(z\) while holding the learned parameter \(\theta\) fixed. For Bayesian model comparison,

    \[ p(D\mid M)=\int p(D\mid\theta,M)p(\theta\mid M)\,d\theta \]

    integrates parameter uncertainty under a declared prior. For an EBM,

    \[ Z_\theta=\int e^{-E_\theta(x)}d\mu(x), \qquad \log p_\theta(x)=-E_\theta(x)-\log Z_\theta, \]

    normalizes a law over possible observations. A conditional or constrained target may introduce yet another normalizer,

    \[ Z(c)=\int p_0(x\mid c)w(x,c)d\mu(x). \]

    The algebraic template is shared; the random variable, measure, scientific interpretation, and reusable computation are not. A per-example latent likelihood cannot be substituted for a parameter-integrated Bayes factor. An EBM partition function may be shared across a test set at fixed \(\theta\), whereas every latent example has its own \(p_\theta(x)\).

    Query register¤

    Table EST.2. Query register
    Query Integrated variable Quantity reused across observations? Typical exact route If intractable
    latent likelihood \(p_\theta(x)\) \(z\) usually no finite sum, conjugacy, low-treewidth messages VI bound, IS, SMC, specialized quadrature
    Bayesian evidence \(p(D\mid M)\) \(\theta\) and possibly latents one value per dataset/model conjugate integration bridge/path/nested sampling, AIS/SMC
    EBM partition \(Z_\theta\) possible data \(x\) yes at fixed model finite enumeration, tractable circuit AIS/SMC, bridge/path estimators
    conditional normalizer \(Z(c)\) generated output \(x\) one value per condition finite legal set or dynamic program IS/SMC, specialized constrained inference

    Worked finite latent model¤

    Let \(z\in\{0,1,2\}\) have prior

    \[ p(z)=\left(\frac12,\frac13,\frac16\right), \]

    and let

    \[ p(x=1\mid z)=\left(\frac15,\frac12,\frac45\right). \]

    The three joint masses for \(x=1\) are

    \[ \gamma(z)=p(x=1,z) =\left(\frac1{10},\frac16,\frac2{15}\right). \]

    Exact summation gives

    \[ p(x=1)=\sum_z\gamma(z) =\frac3{30}+\frac5{30}+\frac4{30} =\frac25. \]

    The exact posterior is therefore

    \[ p(z\mid x=1) =\frac{\gamma(z)}{2/5} =\left(\frac14,\frac5{12},\frac13\right). \]

    This example exposes three quantities that later estimators must keep separate:

    1. each joint term \(\gamma(z)\) is directly scoreable;
    2. the marginal \(Z=p(x=1)=2/5\) is a sum over all latent states; and
    3. the posterior divides each joint term by that marginal.

    Enumeration is exact here because there are only three states. Replacing \(z\) by a high-dimensional sequence may make the same sum exponential unless conditional independence supports dynamic programming.

    Exactness is structural¤

    Before using Monte Carlo, check for reusable exact computation:

    • finite enumeration when the state space is genuinely small;
    • conjugate integration when prior and likelihood close algebraically;
    • variable elimination or sum-product on low-treewidth graphs;
    • forward recursions in hidden Markov models;
    • determinant identities in linear-Gaussian models;
    • normalized autoregressive products, tractable circuits, or bijective flows.

    “Neural” does not imply intractable and “classical” does not imply tractable. The relevant question is whether the requested sum, integral, determinant, or Jacobian can be evaluated at the required scale.

    Three nonlinear operations that change the claim¤

    Suppose \(\widehat Z\) is an unbiased nonnegative estimator of \(Z\). Then generally:

    \[ \mathbb E[\widehat Z]=Z, \qquad \mathbb E[\log\widehat Z]\le\log Z, \qquad \mathbb E[1/\widehat Z]\ne 1/Z. \]

    The second relation is Jensen's inequality; the third has no universal direction without stronger conditions. Unbiasedness does not survive arbitrary nonlinear transformations. A variational lower bound, an unbiased estimator of likelihood, and an estimator of log likelihood therefore require different reporting language.

    Misconception check

    A cheap joint density does not imply a cheap marginal. An unbiased likelihood estimate does not imply an unbiased log-likelihood estimate. Exact inference does not make a misspecified model scientifically correct.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Likelihoods, evidences, and partition functions can share an integral form while answering different questions. Exactness comes from exploitable structure. Chapter 2 replaces exact enumeration by a proposal distribution and derives exactly what importance weights preserve.

    Retrieval practice: identify the query
    1. In a VAE evaluated at fixed parameters, which variable is integrated out in \(p_\theta(x)\)?
    2. In an EBM, is \(Z_\theta\) example-specific at fixed \(\theta\)?
    3. If \(\widehat Z\) is unbiased, is \(\log\widehat Z\) unbiased for \(\log Z\)?

    Solutions. (1) The latent variable \(z\). (2) No; it normalizes the model over the observation space and is shared at fixed \(\theta\). (3) Generally no; Jensen gives a downward expectation bias for a non-degenerate positive estimator.

    2. Importance Sampling: Correctness Comes from the Ratio¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand Chapter 1 and expectations under a discrete or continuous law. By the end, you should be able to:

    • derive ordinary and self-normalized importance estimators;
    • state the support and moment conditions separately;
    • compute the variance of a finite estimator;
    • derive the connection between relative variance and \(\chi^2\) divergence;
    • interpret ESS and Pareto-tail diagnostics without treating them as proofs; and
    • distinguish likelihood estimation from a multi-sample log bound.

    The change-of-measure identity¤

    Let \(q\) be normalized, sampleable, and evaluable. If \(q(x)>0\) wherever \(\gamma(x)>0\), then

    \[ Z=\int\gamma(x)d\mu(x) =\int q(x)\frac{\gamma(x)}{q(x)}d\mu(x) =\mathbb E_q[w(X)], \]

    where \(w(x)=\gamma(x)/q(x)\). Independent draws \(X_k\sim q\) give

    \[ \widehat Z_K=\frac1K\sum_{k=1}^{K}w(X_k). \]

    When \(\mathbb E_q[|w|]<\infty\), this sample mean is unbiased for \(Z\). A finite variance and a conventional central limit theorem additionally require \(\mathbb E_q[w^2]<\infty\). Support coverage, finite mean, and finite variance are three separate requirements.

    For a normalized target expectation \(\mathbb E_\pi[f(X)]\), the self-normalized estimator is

    \[ \widehat\mu_K =\frac{\sum_k w_k f(X_k)}{\sum_k w_k} =\sum_k\widetilde w_k f(X_k), \qquad \widetilde w_k=\frac{w_k}{\sum_jw_j}. \]

    It is generally biased at finite \(K\) because it is a ratio of random sums, but it is consistent under suitable moment and support conditions.

    Worked importance estimate for the finite latent model¤

    Return to \(\gamma=(1/10,1/6,2/15)\) and choose

    \[ q=\left(\frac12,\frac14,\frac14\right). \]

    The importance weights are

    \[ w=\frac{\gamma}{q} =\left(\frac15,\frac23,\frac8{15}\right). \]

    Their expectation closes the exact likelihood:

    \[ \mathbb E_q[w] =\frac12\frac15+\frac14\frac23+\frac14\frac8{15} =\frac25. \]

    For four realized proposal draws \((0,1,1,2)\),

    \[ \widehat Z_4 =\frac14\left(\frac15+\frac23+\frac23+\frac8{15}\right) =\frac{31}{60}. \]

    The result is not equal to \(2/5\) in this realization; unbiasedness is an expectation over repeated random samples. A self-normalized estimate of \(p(z=2\mid x=1)\) from the same draws is

    \[ \widehat p(z=2\mid x=1)=\frac{8/15}{31/15}=\frac8{31}, \]

    whereas the exact posterior probability is \(1/3\).

    The single-draw variance is checkable:

    \[ \operatorname{Var}_q(w) =\left[\frac12\left(\frac15\right)^2 +\frac14\left(\frac23\right)^2 +\frac14\left(\frac8{15}\right)^2\right] -\left(\frac25\right)^2 =\frac{19}{450}. \]

    Thus \(\operatorname{Var}(\widehat Z_K)=19/(450K)\) for independent draws.

    Proposal quality as a divergence¤

    Write \(\pi=\gamma/Z\). Then

    \[ \frac{\operatorname{Var}(\widehat Z_K)}{Z^2} =\frac1K\left(\int\frac{\pi(x)^2}{q(x)}d\mu(x)-1\right) =\frac1K\chi^2(\pi\|q). \]

    The zero-variance proposal is \(q=\pi\), but evaluating it requires the unknown \(Z\). Practical proposal design tries to approximate the target while remaining sampleable, scoreable, and sufficiently heavy-tailed. Reverse-KL variational quality alone does not guarantee small \(\chi^2(\pi\|q)\); a proposal can cover one mode sharply while missing another mode that dominates importance variance.

    Effective sample size is a diagnostic¤

    The common weight ESS is

    \[ \widehat{ESS} =\frac{(\sum_k w_k)^2}{\sum_k w_k^2} =\frac1{\sum_k\widetilde w_k^2}. \]

    It ranges from \(1\) to \(K\), but it is a concentration summary, not a guarantee that unseen target regions were covered. If every draw misses a mode, the observed weights can look uniform and ESS can look excellent. For correlated trajectories, a weight-only ESS also omits autocorrelation.

    Pareto-smoothed importance sampling fits the upper weight tail and reports a tail-shape diagnostic \(\widehat k\), stabilized estimates, and Monte Carlo error. Population tail regimes help interpretation: \(k<1/2\) corresponds to finite variance in the idealized generalized-Pareto tail, while \(k\ge1\) threatens even existence of the mean. Finite-sample thresholds depend on sample size and estimator. Smoothing extreme weights changes the finite estimator; it cannot repair missing support.

    Importance-weighted log objectives¤

    For latent \(z_k\sim q(z\mid x)\),

    \[ \mathcal L_K(x) =\mathbb E\left[ \log\frac1K\sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{p_\theta(x,z_k)}{q(z_k\mid x)} \right] \le\log p_\theta(x). \]

    This is the IWAE bound under its standard construction. The inner arithmetic mean is an unbiased estimator of \(p_\theta(x)\); taking its logarithm creates the lower bound in expectation. Increasing \(K\) tightens the bound under standard conditions, but it changes compute and can worsen inference-network gradient signal without specialized estimators. Report \(K\), proposal, gradient estimator, and whether the result is a bound, a raw likelihood estimate, or a log of one finite estimate.

    Multiple proposals and variance reduction¤

    Mixture importance sampling can combine proposals that cover different modes. Rao–Blackwellization analytically averages a tractable conditional variable. Control variates use known zero-mean functions. Quasi-Monte Carlo can improve integration rates under smooth low-effective-dimensional structure. Each method has its own assumptions; clipping or truncating weights trades variance for bias and must be declared.

    Failure case: a proposal with the wrong support

    If \(q(x)=0\) on a set where \(\gamma(x)>0\), the ratio is undefined there and no number of samples from \(q\) will discover the missing mass. Weight normalization, ESS, clipping, and PSIS do not restore validity.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Importance sampling is correct because a density ratio changes measure, not because proposal samples resemble target samples. Variance depends on overlap in the target-to-proposal direction. Chapter 3 replaces one difficult correction by a sequence of smaller corrections.

    Retrieval practice: weights and claims
    1. What condition is required before a variance calculation matters?
    2. Is the self-normalized estimator unbiased at finite \(K\)?
    3. Can a high observed ESS certify that no target mode was missed?

    Solutions. (1) Proposal support must cover the unnormalized target support. (2) Generally no; it is a random ratio, though it can be consistent. (3) No; ESS only summarizes observed weights.

    3. Annealed Importance Sampling and Sequential Monte Carlo¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand importance weights, Markov kernels, invariant distributions, and effective sample size. By the end, you should be able to:

    • define a bridge from a tractable reference to an unnormalized target;
    • derive the forward AIS weight and its normalizer-ratio direction;
    • explain why invariant transitions need not mix exactly for AIS correctness but still control variance;
    • derive the general SMC incremental weight with forward and backward kernels;
    • separate AIS, SMC, parallel tempering, and diffusion sampling; and
    • diagnose path, schedule, transition, resampling, and particle errors.

    A path turns one ratio into many¤

    Choose unnormalized densities \(\gamma_0,\ldots,\gamma_T\) with known \(Z_0\) and desired \(Z_T\). A geometric path is

    \[ \gamma_t(x) =\gamma_0(x)^{1-\beta_t}\gamma_T(x)^{\beta_t}, \qquad 0=\beta_0<\cdots<\beta_T=1. \]

    Other paths can be valid and much better. Every intermediate distribution must be normalizable, adjacent laws must overlap, and the path must preserve the target support needed by the weight identity.

    Forward AIS convention¤

    This book uses the following order:

    1. draw \(X_0\sim\pi_0\) exactly;
    2. for \(t=1,\ldots,T\), update \(W\leftarrow W\,\gamma_t(X_{t-1})/\gamma_{t-1}(X_{t-1})\);
    3. draw \(X_t\sim K_t(X_{t-1},\cdot)\), where \(K_t\) leaves \(\pi_t\) invariant; and
    4. return \(W\), whose expectation is \(Z_T/Z_0\) under the AIS path construction.

    Average independent trajectory weights before multiplying by \(Z_0\):

    \[ \widehat Z_T =Z_0\frac1K\sum_{k=1}^{K}W^{(k)}. \]

    Neal's AIS construction does not require every \(K_t\) transition to produce an independent equilibrium draw. Invariance supplies the path-space importance identity. Poor movement still makes weights variable and can leave finite runs dominated by rare trajectories. “No exact equilibration required” is therefore not “mixing is irrelevant.”

    Worked two-state AIS path¤

    Let

    \[ \gamma_0=(1,1),\quad Z_0=2, \qquad \gamma_1=(1,3),\quad Z_1=4. \]

    The desired ratio is \(Z_1/Z_0=2\). Insert \(\beta=1/2\):

    \[ \gamma_{1/2}=(1,\sqrt3), \qquad Z_{1/2}=1+\sqrt3. \]

    Suppose the intermediate transition draws exactly from \(\pi_{1/2}\). The first factor is \((1,\sqrt3)\) at \(X_0\sim(1/2,1/2)\), so

    \[ \mathbb E[W_1]=\frac{1+\sqrt3}{2}. \]

    The second factor is again \((1,\sqrt3)\), now averaged under \(\pi_{1/2}\):

    \[ \mathbb E[W_2] =\frac{1+3}{1+\sqrt3} =\frac4{1+\sqrt3}. \]

    Here the exact transition makes the factors independent, so

    \[ \mathbb E[W_1W_2] =\frac{1+\sqrt3}{2}\frac4{1+\sqrt3}=2. \]

    The intermediate normalizer cancels. With imperfect invariant moves, the same AIS expectation identity can hold while factor dependence increases variance.

    General SMC sampler weight¤

    SMC maintains a population of weighted particles. Let \(K_t(x_{t-1},x_t)\) be the forward proposal and \(L_{t-1}(x_t,x_{t-1})\) a declared backward kernel. On the extended path, the incremental weight is

    \[ G_t(x_{t-1},x_t) =\frac{ \gamma_t(x_t)L_{t-1}(x_t,x_{t-1}) }{ \gamma_{t-1}(x_{t-1})K_t(x_{t-1},x_t) }. \tag{EST.1}\label{eq:smc-sampler-incremental-weight} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:smc-sampler-incremental-weight}\) states the direction explicitly. Specialized backward kernels can simplify it. In the reweight-then-move construction used for AIS, an invariant reversal yields the familiar \(\gamma_t(X_{t-1})/\gamma_{t-1}(X_{t-1})\) factor.

    SMC alternates:

    1. reweight particles for the next target;
    2. resample when declared, using an unbiased resampling scheme;
    3. move descendants with target-compatible kernels; and
    4. accumulate the estimated normalizer ratio.

    When resampling occurs at every stage under the standard proper-weighting construction,

    \[ \widehat{\frac{Z_T}{Z_0}} =\prod_{t=1}^{T} \left(\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^{N}G_t^{(i)}\right). \]

    The normalizer estimator can be unbiased under standard SMC assumptions even though a normalized finite-particle expectation is generally biased. Del Moral, Doucet, and Jasra give the general sampler construction.

    This volume owns the normalizer query and uncertainty comparison. The complete Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods foundation owns the reusable particle algorithm: Feynman–Kac semantics, resampling offspring laws, genealogical diagnostics, proposals, smoothing, PMCMC, static targets, twisting, and learned particle systems. Keeping those owners distinct prevents an unbiased \(\widehat Z\) theorem from being transferred to a normalized particle expectation, selected path, or stopped-gradient learning objective.

    Resampling solves one problem and creates another¤

    Resampling reallocates particles toward high-weight regions and prevents all future compute from staying on negligible paths. It also duplicates ancestors, adds variance, and can collapse early path diversity. Multinomial, residual, stratified, and systematic resampling have different variance and dependence properties. Report the scheme and trigger.

    Adaptive temperatures often choose the next \(\beta_t\) to maintain a target conditional ESS. This can concentrate bridge points where distributions change rapidly. It does not guarantee global mode discovery, and adaptation must remain part of the estimator definition and proof rather than being added informally.

    Do not collapse four algorithms¤

    Table EST.3. Do not collapse four algorithms
    Method Population Main output Correctness mechanism Characteristic failure
    AIS independent weighted paths normalizer ratio and weighted endpoint estimates path-space importance weight plus invariant kernels rare dominant trajectories
    SMC sampler interacting particles normalizer ratio and particle approximation proper incremental weights, resampling, mutation weight and genealogical degeneracy
    parallel tempering persistent chains at several temperatures target samples invariant within-temperature moves and MH swaps poor round trips and mode isolation
    diffusion/transport sampler learned reverse or transport dynamics approximate samples, sometimes density under extra machinery learned dynamics and numerical solver model, discretization, and path error

    Intermediate distributions alone do not make these methods equivalent.

    The sampling foundation derives the parallel-tempering product target and swap ratio and distinguishes it from simulated annealing. This chapter remains the canonical owner of AIS weight identities and of SMC normalizer uncertainty; the particle-method foundation owns the general SMC computation and its non-normalizer queries.

    Path and transition design¤

    Geometric annealing can cross phase transitions or regions of poor overlap. More bridge points reduce individual changes but cost more transitions. Better MCMC kernels improve movement but may require gradients and tuning. Learned invertible transports can push particles between temperatures while Jacobians and subsequent weights preserve the declared estimator. Annealed Flow Transport and CRAFT are specific peer-reviewed constructions, not permission to omit correction terms from an arbitrary learned map.

    Peer-reviewed research (ICLR 2026). Guo, Tao, and Chen give a non-asymptotic complexity analysis for normalizing-constant estimation under stated smoothness, path-action, and oracle assumptions, and analyze a reverse-diffusion-based construction. The result sharpens how path geometry enters complexity; it does not establish a dimension-free or universal solution for multimodal neural targets.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    AIS and SMC estimate ratios by extending importance sampling to paths. Direction, initialization, kernels, resampling, and schedules are part of the estimator. Chapter 4 develops alternatives that use samples from both endpoints, integrate derivatives along a path, or transform a likelihood integral into prior-mass coordinates.

    Retrieval practice: path correctness
    1. Does AIS require every intermediate transition to return an independent equilibrium draw?
    2. Why can poor mixing still matter?
    3. What extra object appears in the general SMC incremental weight besides the forward kernel?

    Solutions. (1) No; the standard construction requires appropriate invariant kernels and path-space weighting. (2) It can make weights extremely variable and finite estimates unreliable. (3) A compatible backward kernel on the extended path.

    4. Bridge, Path, Nested, and Bidirectional Estimation¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand normalized expectations, MCMC output, and Chapter 3 paths. By the end, you should be able to:

    • derive the bridge-sampling identity;
    • derive thermodynamic integration from \(d\log Z_\beta/d\beta\);
    • state the nested-sampling prior-mass transformation and its computational bottleneck;
    • explain why the harmonic-mean estimator is dangerous;
    • state the exact-endpoint requirement behind bidirectional stochastic bounds; and
    • select a method by available samples and operations rather than by name recognition.

    Bridge sampling uses both endpoint laws¤

    Let \(\pi_i=\gamma_i/Z_i\) for \(i\in\{0,1\}\). For any positive bridge function \(h\) with finite nonzero expectations,

    \[ \frac{Z_1}{Z_0} =\frac{ \mathbb E_{\pi_0}[h(X)\gamma_1(X)] }{ \mathbb E_{\pi_1}[h(X)\gamma_0(X)] }. \]

    Both expectations contain the same integral \(\int h\gamma_0\gamma_1d\mu\), divided by different normalizers. Empirical bridge sampling replaces them with averages from endpoint samples. Its variance depends on endpoint overlap, dependence in the samples, and bridge choice. The asymptotically optimal bridge depends on the unknown ratio and is commonly solved by fixed-point iteration; “optimal” is relative to the assumed endpoint samples and asymptotic criterion.

    Worked two-state bridge¤

    Use \(\gamma_0=(1,1)\) and \(\gamma_1=(1,3)\). With \(h=1\),

    \[ \mathbb E_{\pi_0}[\gamma_1(X)] =\frac12(1+3)=2, \]

    while \(\gamma_0(X)=1\) everywhere, so

    \[ \mathbb E_{\pi_1}[\gamma_0(X)]=1. \]

    The population bridge ratio is \(2\), exactly \(Z_1/Z_0\). Finite endpoint samples need not reproduce these expectations. If the endpoint laws scarcely overlap, the numerator and denominator can both be estimated from rare events and become unstable.

    Meng and Wong's bridge framework and the broader path-sampling treatment of Gelman and Meng place importance, bridge, and path estimators in a common ratio-estimation view.

    Thermodynamic integration and path sampling¤

    Let \(\gamma_\beta\) be differentiable in \(\beta\), with fixed support and valid differentiation under the integral. Then

    \[ \frac{d}{d\beta}\log Z_\beta =\mathbb E_{\pi_\beta} \left[\frac{\partial}{\partial\beta}\log\gamma_\beta(X)\right]. \]

    Integrating gives

    \[ \log\frac{Z_1}{Z_0} =\int_0^1 \mathbb E_{\pi_\beta} \left[\partial_\beta\log\gamma_\beta(X)\right]d\beta. \]

    For a geometric path, the integrand is

    \[ \mathbb E_{\pi_\beta} [\log\gamma_1(X)-\log\gamma_0(X)]. \]

    This moves the logarithm outside the Monte Carlo average but introduces numerical quadrature and intermediate-expectation errors.

    For the two-state path \(\gamma_\beta=(1,3^\beta)\),

    \[ \mathbb E_{\pi_\beta}[\log3\,\mathbb 1\{X=1\}] =\log3\frac{3^\beta}{1+3^\beta}. \]

    Because this is \(d\log(1+3^\beta)/d\beta\),

    \[ \int_0^1\log3\frac{3^\beta}{1+3^\beta}d\beta =\log4-\log2=\log2. \]

    In real problems, discretizing \(\beta\) creates quadrature bias and MCMC creates sampling error. Replicates at the same grid do not diagnose missed curvature between grid points.

    Nested sampling changes coordinates¤

    For Bayesian evidence

    \[ Z=\int L(\theta)\pi(\theta)d\theta, \]

    define remaining prior mass above likelihood level \(\lambda\) by

    \[ X(\lambda)=\Pr_{\theta\sim\pi}[L(\theta)>\lambda]. \]

    Under regularity and a generalized inverse \(L(X)\),

    \[ Z=\int_0^1L(X)dX. \]

    Nested sampling maintains live points, repeatedly removes the lowest-likelihood point, and replaces it with a draw constrained to a higher likelihood contour. With \(N\) ideal independent live points, the shrinkage ratio has a \(\operatorname{Beta}(N,1)\) law. The hard operation is not sorting likelihoods; it is drawing correctly from a possibly disconnected constrained prior. Skilling's formulation supplies both an evidence estimate and posterior weights, but uncertainty depends on shrinkage assumptions and constrained-sampling quality.

    Posterior-ordinate and harmonic identities¤

    Chib-style estimators rearrange Bayes' rule at a chosen parameter value:

    \[ p(D)=\frac{p(D\mid\theta^*)p(\theta^*)}{p(\theta^*\mid D)}. \]

    They can be effective when the posterior ordinate is evaluable from well-mixed output, but label symmetries and multimodality require careful treatment.

    The harmonic-mean identity,

    \[ \frac1{p(D)} =\mathbb E_{p(\theta\mid D)}\left[\frac1{p(D\mid\theta)}\right], \]

    is algebraically correct under its assumptions and often statistically disastrous. Rare posterior draws with tiny likelihood produce enormous reciprocal values; variance can be infinite. A number produced by the estimator is not evidence that its moments exist.

    Bidirectional checks have an exact-endpoint requirement¤

    Forward AIS gives stochastic lower bounds on \(\log Z\) after taking expected logs. Reverse constructions can provide the opposite direction only under specific initialization. Bidirectional Monte Carlo obtains stochastic upper bounds for simulated-data settings when the reverse run starts from an exact target or posterior draw. An approximate sample from a long chain does not silently satisfy that condition. RAISE answers a different question about an approximation and must not be advertised as a generic upper bracket for the original model.

    Method selection by available operations¤

    Table EST.4. Method selection by available operations
    Available operation Natural candidates Main question to audit
    IID scoreable proposal draws ordinary or multiple importance sampling Does the proposal cover target tails and modes?
    exact reference plus invariant target-path kernels AIS Do schedule and transitions control path-weight variance?
    particle population plus mutation/resampling SMC sampler Are incremental weights and backward kernels correct?
    samples from both normalized endpoints bridge sampling Do endpoint samples overlap and mix?
    samples across many temperatures thermodynamic integration Is the temperature grid dense where the integrand bends?
    reliable likelihood-constrained prior sampling nested sampling Does the constrained sampler cover disconnected contours?
    exact target/posterior sample in a simulated problem bidirectional Monte Carlo Is the endpoint exact rather than approximately mixed?

    No row promises superiority. A method whose required operation is invalid is not rescued by more nominal samples.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Bridge methods compare endpoint expectations, path methods integrate local derivatives, and nested sampling integrates over prior mass. Each relocates difficulty rather than removing it. Chapter 5 turns their failure modes into an uncertainty and diagnostic protocol.

    Retrieval practice: choose the missing assumption
    1. What makes nested sampling difficult on a multimodal posterior?
    2. What two numerical errors enter thermodynamic integration beyond model error?
    3. Can an approximate endpoint sample justify a bidirectional upper bound requiring an exact sample?

    Solutions. (1) Correct sampling from successively constrained, possibly disconnected prior regions. (2) Monte Carlo error in intermediate expectations and quadrature error over the path. (3) No.

    5. Bias, Variance, Diagnostics, and Numerical Stability¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know sampling variance, Jensen's inequality, and the estimator constructions in Chapters 2–4. By the end, you should be able to:

    • separate model, estimator, transition, path, numerical, and dataset error;
    • quantify the log bias in a finite example;
    • design independent-replicate and paired-comparison uncertainty analyses;
    • use weight, chain, path, and resampling diagnostics together;
    • implement log-domain accumulation; and
    • validate an estimator against a ladder of tractable targets.

    An error budget, not one error bar¤

    An exact estimate of the wrong model does not fix model error. Many particles do not fix a misdirected ratio. Multiple test examples reduce dataset uncertainty but not a shared partition-function error. A useful report names each layer separately.

    Worked log-bias calculation¤

    For the Chapter 2 single-draw weight distribution,

    \[ w\in\left\{\frac15,\frac23,\frac8{15}\right\} \quad\text{with probabilities}\quad \left(\frac12,\frac14,\frac14\right). \]

    We already know \(\mathbb E[w]=2/5\). The expected single-sample log estimate is

    \[ \mathbb E[\log w] =\frac12\log\frac15 +\frac14\log\frac23 +\frac14\log\frac8{15} \approx-1.06324. \]

    The true log likelihood is

    \[ \log\frac25\approx-0.91629. \]

    Thus the expected one-draw log gap is about \(0.14695\) nats. A particular \(\log\widehat Z\) may lie above \(\log Z\); Jensen constrains the expectation, not every realization.

    For a concentrated estimator, the delta method gives the leading approximation

    \[ \mathbb E[\log\widehat Z]-\log Z \approx-\frac{\operatorname{Var}(\widehat Z)}{2Z^2}. \]

    This approximation is unreliable for heavy-tailed or highly skewed estimates—the settings where it is most tempting to use it casually.

    Average in probability space¤

    Given independent positive estimates \(\widehat Z^{(1)},\ldots,\widehat Z^{(R)}\), combine them by

    \[ \overline Z=\frac1R\sum_{r=1}^{R}\widehat Z^{(r)}, \]

    then take \(\log\overline Z\) if a log-scale summary is required. Averaging log estimates targets \(\mathbb E[\log\widehat Z]\), not \(\log\mathbb E[\widehat Z]\). Numerically, compute

    \[ \log\overline Z =\operatorname{logsumexp}(\ell_1,\ldots,\ell_R)-\log R, \qquad \ell_r=\log\widehat Z^{(r)}. \]

    Independence must be real: particles within one resampling genealogy, temperatures in one path, and autocorrelated MCMC states are not independent replicates.

    Diagnostics by estimator family¤

    Table EST.5. Diagnostics by estimator family
    Estimator Minimum diagnostics What they cannot certify
    IS/IWAE log-weight histogram, maximum normalized weight, ESS, \(\widehat k\), repeats unseen support or modes
    AIS independent log-weight distributions, forward repeats, schedule refinements, transition diagnostics exact target mixing from one stable mean
    SMC ESS trajectory, resampling times/scheme, unique ancestors, move acceptance and mixing, particle scaling unbiased normalized expectations at finite \(N\)
    bridge sampling overlap measures, endpoint chain diagnostics, split-chain repetitions validity when either endpoint chain misses a mode
    thermodynamic integration integrand versus temperature, grid refinement, chain diagnostics at each region curvature between an inadequately chosen grid
    nested sampling live-point scaling, repeated runs, insertion/rank tests where appropriate, constrained-sampler diagnostics correct disconnected-contour exploration by nominal live-point count alone

    For independent raw importance estimates with finite variance, a standard error of the mean is meaningful. With heavy tails, empirical variance may be unstable and a Gaussian interval misleading. Bootstrap resampling cannot reveal a mode absent from the observed sample. Use known tractable targets, proposal stress tests, and increasing-compute stability together.

    Dataset and estimator uncertainty are different¤

    Suppose held-out NLL averages \(n\) examples and each \(\log p_\theta(x_i)\) is itself estimated. There are at least two sources of variation:

    1. which held-out examples were drawn; and
    2. Monte Carlo randomness conditional on each example.

    Use repeated estimator runs for a subset or all examples to estimate the second component. Use example-level resampling or a standard error across independent examples for the first. If a single estimated \(\log Z_\theta\) is shared across every EBM example, its error is correlated across the entire test set and must not be divided by \(\sqrt n\) as though it were independent per example.

    For model differences on the same examples, paired per-example differences reduce dataset variance. If two estimates share random numbers, record the coupling; it can reduce or increase variance and changes the uncertainty analysis.

    Numerical stability is part of correctness¤

    Accumulate products as sums of log factors. Normalize log weights with log-sum-exp. Track whether energies and log densities use nats or bits. Use sufficient precision for long products, determinants, and energy differences. A finite returned value is not proof of stability: overflow may be hidden by clipping, and underflowed weights may silently become zero.

    For AIS,

    \[ \log W =\sum_{t=1}^{T} [\log\gamma_t(X_{t-1})-\log\gamma_{t-1}(X_{t-1})]. \]

    For test likelihoods, report whether a common normalizer estimate was reused and whether its random seed or uncertainty was propagated.

    Validation ladder¤

    Before trusting a new estimator on a large generator:

    1. test a finite state space where \(Z\) is enumerated;
    2. test a Gaussian integral with analytic \(Z\);
    3. add separated modes with known mixture weights;
    4. vary dimension, tail thickness, and energy offsets;
    5. reverse the bridge when exact endpoint samples are available;
    6. refine particles, schedules, transitions, and numerical precision independently; and
    7. compare at least two estimator families whose failure modes differ.

    Passing the first two checks finds direction and implementation errors. The later checks expose overlap, mixing, and scaling failures.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Estimator uncertainty is layered and often correlated. Log transforms, self-normalization, resampling, quadrature, and shared normalizers change the error analysis. Chapter 6 turns these distinctions into a publication-ready reporting and research protocol.

    Retrieval practice: diagnose the error bar
    1. Why can an example-level standard error understate EBM test-NLL uncertainty?
    2. Should independent likelihood estimates be averaged before or after taking logs when estimating \(Z\)?
    3. Can a bootstrap discover a completely missed mode?

    Solutions. (1) A shared partition-function estimate induces correlated error across all examples. (2) Average the raw positive estimates, using log-sum-exp for stability, then take the log. (3) No; it only resamples observed information.

    6. Evaluation, Reporting, and Current Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand all previous chapters and the distinction between density fit and sample quality. By the end, you should be able to:

    • write a reproducible estimator specification;
    • separate model likelihood, bound, estimator, and numerical report;
    • compare model likelihoods without hiding prior or protocol dependence;
    • audit learned proposals and transports without granting unearned correctness;
    • interpret current research claims at their actual evidence maturity; and
    • design a cumulative likelihood-estimation study.

    The reporting contract¤

    Every likelihood or normalizer result should declare the following fields.

    Table EST.6. The reporting contract
    Field Required content
    probabilistic object latent marginal, Bayesian evidence, partition function, conditional normalizer, or ratio
    model semantics support, base measure, preprocessing/dequantization, conditioning, and parameter checkpoint
    estimator exact sum, ELBO/IWAE, IS, AIS, SMC, bridge, path, nested, or another named construction
    direction and reference \(Z_T/Z_0\) or inverse; \(\gamma_0\), known \(Z_0\), and target \(\gamma_T\)
    proposal/path proposal density, temperatures, bridge function, constrained prior, or transport
    finite computation draws, particles, intermediate laws, transitions per stage, resampling, chains, and total evaluations
    numerical protocol dtype, log-domain operations, solver/quadrature tolerances, and determinant/divergence approximation
    diagnostics weights, ESS, tail, mixing, ancestry, overlap, grid, and replicate checks as applicable
    uncertainty independent repeats, estimator SE/interval, dataset SE/interval, and correlation or pairing
    evidence status exact, bound, unbiased estimate of \(Z\), consistent ratio, approximation, or unverified empirical result
    compute wall time, device, parallelism, peak memory, energy evaluations, gradients, and comparable budget

    “AIS likelihood: −84.2” is not a reproducible claim. It omits whether the number is per example or dimension, how \(Z_0\) was defined, which direction was run, how schedules and chains behaved, and how much uncertainty remains.

    Family-specific consequences¤

    Latent-variable models. State whether the report is an ELBO, IWAE bound, raw importance estimate, SMC likelihood estimate, or log of a finite estimate. Per-example proposal failure can vary across the test set.

    Energy-based models. Report \(-E_\theta(x)\) and \(-\log\widehat Z_\theta\) as separate terms. The same normalizer estimate is shared across examples at fixed \(\theta\). Energy shifts change \(Z\) oppositely and must leave normalized likelihood invariant.

    State-space models. Particle-filter likelihood estimators depend on proposal, resampling, and particle count. Path degeneracy affects smoothing even when the likelihood estimate appears stable.

    Flows and probability-flow ODEs. A tractable change of variables can eliminate Monte Carlo normalization, but numerical inversion, divergence estimation, and ODE tolerance can still make likelihood approximate. Do not call those errors partition-function uncertainty.

    Autoregressive models and circuits. Exact local normalization yields exact joint scoring under the declared representation, but tokenization, quantization, missing-data marginalization, or conditional constraints can introduce new sums.

    Point processes and random finite sets. A tractable conditional intensity still requires its finite-window compensator, while Gibbs configuration models may have a parameter-dependent global normalizer and DPPs may have tractable finite determinants. The complete family volume keeps temporal integration, spatial pseudolikelihood, and configuration normalization as separate estimator contracts.

    Model comparison needs a declared scientific question¤

    A Bayes factor

    \[ B_{10}=\frac{p(D\mid M_1)}{p(D\mid M_0)} \]

    depends on both likelihoods and parameter priors. Diffuse-prior sensitivity is part of the method, not estimator noise. Predictive log score, conditional marginal likelihood, and Bayes factors answer related but different questions. Report prior sensitivity and estimator uncertainty before interpreting evidence categories.

    For two generators evaluated on the same held-out examples, report paired log-likelihood differences when possible. A statistically resolved density difference may still be irrelevant to perceptual quality, utility, safety, or sampling cost; the evaluation volume keeps those axes separate.

    Worked audit of the running example¤

    A complete minimal report for the Chapter 2 estimator would read:

    We estimated the latent marginal \(p(x=1)=\sum_{z=0}^{2}p(x=1,z)\) on a three-state discrete model. The proposal was \(q=(1/2,1/4,1/4)\), whose support covers all joint-positive states. Ordinary independent importance sampling used \(K\) draws and weights \(p(x=1,z)/q(z)\). Exact enumeration gives \(Z=2/5\) and single-draw variance \(19/450\), so the implementation was checked against both. The displayed four-draw realization \((0,1,1,2)\) gives \(31/60\); it is an estimate of \(Z\), not an exact value or a bound. Log-scale summaries average independent raw estimates with log-sum-exp before taking a logarithm.

    This short report contains the query, proposal, support, estimator, sample count symbol, exact reference, variance, realization, and log convention. A large experiment adds actual \(K\), seeds, compute, diagnostics, and intervals.

    Learned transports retain an estimator contract¤

    Normalizing flows, diffusion transports, and neural proposals can reduce mismatch between adjacent targets. They do not make weights optional unless the learned map itself defines an exact normalized model whose likelihood is the requested quantity. When a transport is used inside AIS or SMC, report:

    • training data or particles used to fit it;
    • whether fitting and evaluation reuse randomness;
    • Jacobian and transition terms;
    • subsequent importance or MH correction;
    • failure under mode omission; and
    • amortization cost versus per-target savings.

    NETS is a peer-reviewed 2025 non-equilibrium transport sampler connected to work identities and normalizer estimation. Normalizing-flow regression studies evidence estimation with expensive offline likelihood evaluations. These methods expand the design space; comparisons remain target-, compute-, and protocol-specific.

    Current research questions¤

    1. Path geometry. Which path minimizes weight variance or action under available transitions, and can it be learned without invalidating evaluation?
    2. Multimodal guarantees. How can mode discovery be certified when diagnostics see only visited regions?
    3. High-dimensional complexity. Which smoothness, isoperimetry, transport, or warm-start assumptions yield non-asymptotic relative-error guarantees?
    4. Adaptive validity. Which learned schedules, proposals, and resampling rules preserve unbiased normalizer estimates under data reuse?
    5. Correlated computation. How should accelerators, shared random numbers, and population interactions enter reliable uncertainty estimates?
    6. Generative evaluation. When does improved held-out likelihood predict the downstream claim, and when must it remain only one coordinate of an evaluation vector?

    The ICLR 2026 complexity result cited in Chapter 3 is evidence that path action can enter explicit bounds under strong assumptions. It should motivate assumption-aware analysis, not a claim that one path is universally optimal.

    Cumulative study design¤

    For a new unnormalized generator:

    1. define support, base measure, energy, and the exact quantity to estimate;
    2. find a tractable reference with known normalizer and support coverage;
    3. validate directions and constants on finite and Gaussian cases;
    4. compare at least AIS/SMC or another path method with an estimator having a different failure mode;
    5. scale schedules, transitions, particles, and independent repeats separately;
    6. record weight, mixing, path, ancestry, and numerical diagnostics;
    7. propagate shared normalizer uncertainty into test likelihoods;
    8. report density results beside sample, calibration, compute, and task metrics; and
    9. bound every conclusion by the actual target and protocol.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Normalizer estimation is not a bookkeeping detail. It is an inference problem with support, path, transition, numerical, and uncertainty contracts. A publication-quality result identifies the probabilistic object first, derives the estimator direction, exposes finite computation, and reports what remains uncertain.

    Retrieval practice: final audit
    1. What must be reported before an AIS number can be interpreted?
    2. Does a learned transport remove the need for correction when used only as a proposal?
    3. Why should an EBM normalizer error not be divided by the square root of the test-set size?

    Solutions. (1) At minimum the target/reference, direction, path, schedules, kernels, trajectories, diagnostics, uncertainty, numerical protocol, and compute. (2) No; the declared importance, SMC, or MH correction supplies correctness unless the map itself defines the requested exact model. (3) The same estimated \(\log Z_\theta\) is shared across all examples and hence induces correlated error.
