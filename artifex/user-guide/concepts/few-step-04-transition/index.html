    4. Transition Matching and Continuous Autoregression¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional generation, transition kernels, and exposure bias in autoregressive systems. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Specify a transition model: declare the outer state, context, conditional kernel, supervision process, and internal sampler.
    2. O2 — Separate deterministic and stochastic transitions: identify when a Dirac map cannot represent the required coarse conditional law.
    3. O3 — Audit continuous autoregression: factorize blocks while keeping causal generation order distinct from causal inference.
    4. O4 — Bound kernel error: propagate uniform total-variation errors through a finite chain and state the conditioning assumptions.
    5. O5 — Diagnose exposure and history shift: separate teacher-forced, partial-history, full-history, and free-running distributions.
    6. O6 — Audit nested compute: count backbone, head, internal-solver, codec, and end-to-end costs for a new transition architecture.

    Transition Matching, NeurIPS 2025, models a finite sequence of continuous-state transitions rather than an infinitesimal ODE/SDE. An outer backbone represents the current state and schedule; an internal generative head samples an expressive transition kernel.

    Schematically,

    \[ X_{k+1}\sim K_{\theta,k}(\cdot\mid X_k,C_k). \]

    The transition can be stochastic and can use supervision paths that are not continuous. Flow/diffusion-like refinement and continuous autoregressive generation become choices of state, context, and transition order.

    A complete specification must say whether \(X_k\) is the current canvas, only the new block, a latent plus mask, or an augmented history. If the head samples from

    \[ X_{k+1}=G_{\theta,k}(X_k,C_k,Z_k), \qquad Z_k\sim p_Z, \]

    then \(G\) induces \(K\) by pushing forward \(p_Z\). The map \(G\) and kernel \(K\) are not interchangeable: different latent parameterizations can induce the same kernel, and fixing \(Z_k\) turns a stochastic transition into one coupled sample, not its distribution.

    The supervision process can be discontinuous or non-Markov in the visible state. A learned transition is Markov only relative to the state supplied to it. Full history can be made Markov by augmenting the state with that history, at the cost of a growing context and a different transition space.

    Difference Transition Matching¤

    Difference Transition Matching learns coarse refinements between states. Unlike one Euler step, its kernel can model residual uncertainty after conditioning on the current state. More expressive transitions can reduce outer steps, but the head has its own NFE, sampling randomness, and compute.

    The official NeurIPS record describes DTM, partially causal ARTM, and fully causal FHTM under controlled architecture/data settings. Treat the reported quality as evidence for those implementations and protocols. “TM generalizes flow matching” is an architectural/probabilistic containment statement: a deterministic or narrow head can recover special cases. It is not a theorem that every trained TM dominates a flow model in likelihood, latency, or quality.

    Continuous autoregression¤

    A continuous autoregressive model factorizes generation by blocks or positions but models each conditional continuous value with a generator or transition distribution:

    \[ p(x)=\prod_{i=1}^mp(x_{B_i}\mid x_{B_{<i}}). \]

    The outer factorization is causally ordered; each conditional may be flow-, diffusion-, or transition-based. It should not be confused with causal inference—the word causal here refers to the attention/generation order.

    Transition Matching studies partially causal ARTM and full-history FHTM variants. Full history exposes all earlier generated states; partial variants trade context and parallelism. Exact likelihood is not automatic merely because the outer order is autoregressive: the continuous conditional kernel may remain implicit.

    Likewise, “fully causal” refers to the information available to each generated block or state. It does not establish an intervention semantics. The factor \(p(x_{B_i}\mid x_{B_{<i}})\) is observational unless the model and data include a structural causal interpretation.

    Three histories that must not be conflated¤

    Table FEW.15. Three histories that must not be conflated
    History Used when Primary risk
    clean teacher prefix conventional teacher-forced training exposure shift at deployment
    corrupted/scheduled prefix robustness training objective bias and schedule dependence
    generated full history free-running training or deployment compounding errors and high compute

    A partial-history model can be faster or more parallel but loses conditional information. A full-history model can represent richer dependencies but makes the transition conditional on an expanding object. Compare both at matched backbone/head compute and with identical block order.

    Worked block model¤

    Generate an image latent in four blocks. A pure block AR model samples each \(p(x_{B_i}\mid x_{B_{<i}})\) once. A transition model may maintain a full canvas and update the next block through several internal stochastic refinements. Outer steps equal four; total neural calls can exceed four. Quality comparisons must separate block count, head calls, and backbone calls.

    If each of four outer steps uses one expensive backbone evaluation and three cheap head evaluations, the system uses four backbone calls and twelve head calls, not “four NFEs” without qualification. If a codec runs once at the end, there are seventeen named model calls. FLOPs and latency still depend on tensor shapes, caching, branch fusion, and hardware.

    Exposure and error propagation¤

    Teacher-forced continuous conditionals see clean histories during training, while generation supplies model histories. Full-history architectures can exploit richer context but also propagate earlier errors. Scheduled sampling does not generally provide a consistent fix and can bias the learned conditional objective.

    Kernel error propagation in total variation¤

    Let \(P_i\) be reference kernels and \(Q_i\) learned kernels. If

    \[ \sup_x\operatorname{TV}(P_i(x,\cdot),Q_i(x,\cdot))\leq\varepsilon_i, \]

    then Markov-kernel contraction and a telescoping replacement give

    \[ \operatorname{TV}(\mu P_1\cdots P_m, \mu Q_1\cdots Q_m) \leq\sum_{i=1}^m\varepsilon_i. \]

    The uniform supremum matters: an average error measured only under clean teacher prefixes does not control states reached by \(Q_1\cdots Q_{i-1}\). Tighter bounds can exploit contraction/mixing, while adversarial history shift can make the simple sum loose or unattainable from available measurements.

    Worked binary two-block propagation¤

    The reference emits \(X_1\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and sets \(X_2=X_1\). A learned model emits \(Q(X_1=1)=0.6\) and then copies its generated \(X_1\) correctly. The conditional second kernel has zero error for every prefix, yet the final joint law differs:

    \[ P(0,0)=P(1,1)=0.5, \qquad Q(0,0)=0.4,\quad Q(1,1)=0.6. \]

    Their joint TV distance is \(0.1\), exactly the first-step error. Now suppose the second learned kernel flips its input with probability \(0.05\). Its uniform conditional error is \(0.05\), so the two-step bound is \(0.15\). Direct calculation gives masses \((0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1)=(0.38,0.02,0.03,0.57)\) and TV \(0.12\). The bound is valid but not necessarily tight because some errors redistribute rather than align.

    Retrieval practice: transition matching
    1. What is matched by a finite-transition objective that is not matched by an infinitesimal velocity loss alone?
    2. Why is continuous autoregression still autoregressive?
    3. How can a small per-block error become a large sequence error?
    4. Which distribution shift separates teacher-forced training from free-running generation?

    Solutions. (1) The conditional map or kernel over a nonzero time interval. (2) Blocks are generated conditionally in an ordered factorization even if each block uses continuous dynamics. (3) Later conditionals consume earlier generated rather than true blocks, so errors alter subsequent input distributions and compound. (4) Training conditions on ground-truth prefixes while deployment conditions on model-generated prefixes.

    Misconception checks: transition models¤

    • “A stochastic head is free because the backbone is reused.” Its internal calls, memory traffic, and random sampling still contribute to cost.
    • “Autoregressive factorization gives tractable likelihood.” Each continuous conditional must itself have a tractable normalized density.
    • “Full history is non-Markov.” It is non-Markov only relative to a state that omits history; augmentation restores Markov form.
    • “Causal generation order discovers causal effects.” Ordering is not an intervention model.

    Independent practice and assessment map: transition kernels and order¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: error persistence. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. In the binary two-block example with a perfect copying second kernel and biased first marginal, predict whether the second stage removes, preserves, or amplifies the joint TV error, then compute it.
    2. P1.2 — Derive the kernel bound. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 14 min. Prove the two-kernel TV bound by inserting \(\mu Q_1P_2\) and using contraction; state how induction gives the \(m\)-step result.
    3. P1.3 — Audit nested compute. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For four backbone calls, three head calls per backbone, and one codec, report all named calls and explain why neither four nor sixteen alone is a complete cost report.
    4. P1.4 — Diagnose exposure shift. Type: design · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 14 min. Specify a \(2\times2\) evaluation crossing clean/generated prefixes with one-step/full-rollout metrics.
    5. P1.5 — Classify a full-history model. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Define an augmented state that makes an ordered full-history generator Markov, and state the memory and transition-space costs.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to multimodal generation. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Design a transition that generates image regions conditioned on text and prior regions; declare its stochastic head, causal order, likelihood status, and a dependence diagnostic.

    Feedback contract: transition kernels and order¤

    Table FEW.16. Feedback contract: transition kernels and order — table 1
    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 The perfect copy preserves the \(0.1\) first-stage error in the joint law.
    P1.2 A telescoping intermediate and kernel contraction yield \(\varepsilon_1+\varepsilon_2\) under uniform conditional bounds.
    P1.3 Four backbone, twelve head, and one codec call are reported with separate FLOPs/latency qualifiers.
    P1.4 The matrix exposes teacher-prefix performance, generated-state conditional performance, and final sequence error separately.
    P1.5 State augmentation is correct and the growing history/context cost is not hidden.
    P1.6 Text is conditioning rather than an intervention; region dependence and head randomness are explicitly tested.

    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup

    Table FEW.18. Outcome-to-task alignment: transition kernels and order
    Learning objective Independently assessed by
    O1 — Specify a transition model P1.3, P1.6
    O2 — Separate deterministic and stochastic transitions P1.6
    O3 — Audit continuous autoregression P1.5, P1.6

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The second transition copies without error, so \(Q\) places masses \(0.4\) and \(0.6\) on \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\) instead of \(0.5\) each. TV is \(\tfrac12(|0.4-0.5|+|0.6-0.5|)=0.1\). The error is preserved. Check. A perfect conditional kernel cannot correct a wrong input marginal unless it is deliberately mixing or corrective.
    2. P1.2. Insert \(\mu Q_1P_2\). Applying the same kernel \(P_2\) contracts TV, so the first term is at most \(\varepsilon_1\). Integrating the uniform \(P_2\) versus \(Q_2\) bound under \(\mu Q_1\) makes the second at most \(\varepsilon_2\). Replacing one kernel at a time proves the sum by induction. Check. An average teacher-state conditional error cannot replace the supremum without a state-shift argument.
    3. P1.3. The program makes four expensive backbone calls, twelve head calls, and one codec call: seventeen named calls. “Four” hides the head and codec; “sixteen neural calls” treats expensive and cheap networks as equal. Report calls by component, FLOPs, shapes, memory, and wall time. Check. Cache reuse belongs in the measured systems record.
    4. P1.4. Evaluate next-step conditional error on clean prefixes and on prefixes sampled from the model. Then evaluate complete rollouts initialized from a clean prefix and fully free-running from the start. The clean/one-step cell measures teacher-forced fit; the generated/full cell measures deployed compounding. Rubric. One aggregate sequence metric cannot localize exposure shift.
    5. P1.5. Use \(S_i=(i,X_{B_1},\ldots,X_{B_{i-1}})\). Then \(S_{i+1}\) depends only on \(S_i\) and a new block sample, so the augmented process is Markov. Its state dimension/context length grows, attention and storage cost increase, and kernels live on step-dependent prefix spaces. Check. Markovization changes representation cost, not the observable factorization.
    6. P1.6. Let the outer order select image regions and let an internal flow head sample each new latent region from text, position, and the generated prefix. The conditionals may be implicit, so exact likelihood is not promised. Evaluate a cross-region attribute or boundary-continuity statistic under full rollouts and compare it with a model that conditions only on text. Check. Text control is observational conditioning unless an intervention model is supplied.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Transition Matching expands finite steps into learned stochastic kernels and connects global refinement with continuous autoregressive orders. Its expressivity makes “step count” especially ambiguous. Chapter 5 classifies methods that compress a pretrained teacher rather than learn long transitions from scratch.
