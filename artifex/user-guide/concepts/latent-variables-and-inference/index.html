    Latent Variables and Inference¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Classical Generative Models and Graphical Structure

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Bayesian Nonparametrics 1 — Random Measures

    Latent variables express unobserved causes, compressed representations, mixture assignments, trajectories, or nuisance variation. They also create integrals and posteriors that are often intractable. This chapter develops the inference toolkit shared by VAEs, state-space models, diffusion ELBOs, Bayesian neural models, and probabilistic programs.

    Inference begins with a target and a query, not with an algorithm name. Computing an evidence, estimating one posterior expectation, drawing posterior states, finding a mode, and filtering an online sequence are different computational problems. A method that is suitable for one may not even return the object required by another.

    Prerequisite Refresh¤

    You should be able to marginalize a joint distribution, apply Bayes' rule, take an expectation, and interpret KL divergence. Review Probability and Generative Modeling if any of those operations are unfamiliar; review Bayesian Learning and Model Assessment for posterior, predictive, and evidence queries. The examples below use the distinction between a model \(p_\theta(x,z)\) and an inference distribution \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) throughout:

    • \(\theta\) changes what data the model assigns probability to;
    • \(\phi\) changes how the hidden state is inferred for an observation;
    • a training objective may update either or both;
    • a good inference algorithm does not rescue a misspecified generative model, and a good model does not make every posterior query easy.

    One Model, Two Directions¤

    The most important picture in this chapter has two directions that must not be merged. The model explains how a hidden state could generate an observation. An inference method starts from the observation and reasons about compatible hidden states.

    The dashed reverse arrow is deliberately not part of the generative graph. It may represent Bayes' rule, enumeration, message passing, an encoder, importance weighting, or a Markov chain. The rest of the chapter asks what each route returns and which error it introduces.

    • Model

      The joint law \(p_\theta(x,z)\) and its assumptions.

    • Query

      Evidence, a marginal, an expectation, samples, a mode, a filter, or a smoother.

    • Algorithm

      Exact elimination, optimization, weighted particles, or Markov transitions.

    • Error contract

      Approximation, optimization, Monte Carlo, mixing, numerical, and model error remain separate.

    Learning Objectives¤

    • derive the pointwise ELBO and its dataset-level joint-KL decomposition;
    • derive a mean-field coordinate update and explain its local-convergence boundary;
    • map evidence, marginal, expectation, sampling, MAP, filtering, and smoothing queries to suitable exact or approximate methods;
    • distinguish exactness, conjugacy, variational approximation, Monte Carlo error, and amortization as separate properties;
    • compare exact inference, VI, importance sampling, MCMC, and SMC by their finite-compute error and asymptotic contract;
    • derive how exact EM, variational EM, and amortized stochastic variational learning reuse the same lower-bound identity;
    • distinguish a flow posterior, a direct flow proposal, and a flow-preconditioned target by the operation that supplies correctness;
    • distinguish coordinate-ascent, message-passing, black-box, automatic-differentiation, amortized, and semi-amortized VI;
    • explain reparameterized and score-function gradient estimators and the transformations used by ADVI;
    • understand importance sampling, IWAE, SMC, and posterior collapse;
    • describe what a probabilistic-programming inference engine separates from a model.

    1. Marginal Likelihood and Posterior¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes joint, marginal, and conditional probability. By the end, you should be able to distinguish the latent joint law, observed marginal, posterior, MAP point, and posterior predictive; compute exact finite conditioning; apply quadrature and Laplace approximations with their assumptions; and choose an inference query before comparing estimator accuracy or cost.

    For

    \[ p_\theta(x,z)=p_\theta(x\mid z)p_\theta(z), \]

    the evidence and posterior are

    \[ p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz, \qquad p_\theta(z\mid x)=\frac{p_\theta(x,z)}{p_\theta(x)}. \]

    Even when the joint is cheap, the evidence integral may be high-dimensional and the posterior multimodal. A tractable inference method must approximate a query, exploit structure, or change the training objective.

    When inference is exact¤

    Approximate inference is not the definition of a latent-variable model. Exact inference remains possible when algebra or graphical structure keeps the required sum or integral tractable:

    • finite mixtures permit exact responsibility calculations once their component densities are known;
    • conjugate exponential-family pairs permit analytic posterior updates;
    • hidden Markov models use forward–backward dynamic programming;
    • linear Gaussian state-space models use Kalman filtering and smoothing;
    • trees and low-treewidth factor graphs admit exact message passing.

    The boundary is computational, not philosophical. A discrete model with \(K^m\) joint latent configurations may still allow \(O(mK^2)\) inference because conditional independences let messages reuse partial sums. Conversely, one continuous scalar latent can have an analytically intractable posterior if a nonlinear likelihood destroys conjugacy.

    Local and global latent variables¤

    In a dataset \(x_{1:N}\), a global variable \(\beta\) influences many observations while local variables \(z_n\) explain individual observations:

    \[ p(\beta,z_{1:N},x_{1:N}) =p(\beta)\prod_{n=1}^{N}p(z_n\mid\beta)p(x_n\mid z_n,\beta). \]

    The corresponding plate makes the scope visible. The global random variable \(\beta\) sits outside the item plate because every observation informs it; each \(z_n\) and \(x_n\) is replicated inside the plate.

    Topic proportions, cluster assignments, and per-example VAE codes are local; mixture parameters, population-level rates, or a shared physical constant are global. This distinction controls minibatching: local terms subsample naturally, whereas a global posterior must aggregate evidence across the dataset.

    “Local” describes scope, not automatic independence. In an HMM, every state \(z_t\) is local to a time index but adjacent states remain coupled. A spatial latent can be local to one graph node while its prior connects it to neighboring nodes. A group-level latent can sit between a population variable and per-observation latents. The factorization above is one important independent-local pattern, not the definition of locality. Before using minibatches or factorized variational updates, inspect which factors cross item, time, group, or graph boundaries.

    Counterexample: one latent per record does not imply a product posterior

    Suppose \(z_{1:T}\) is a Markov chain and each observation depends only on \(z_t\). The likelihood factors locally, but conditioning on all observations couples neighboring states through the transition factors. In general,

    \[ p(z_{1:T}\mid x_{1:T})\ne\prod_{t=1}^{T}p(z_t\mid x_t). \]

    A mean-field product is an approximation; forward–backward messages, a Kalman smoother, structured VI, particles, or trajectory MCMC preserve more of the posterior dependence.

    One Model, Five Inference Regimes¤

    The next table is easiest to read as a set of independent switches rather than a ranking. For example, a learned proposal can be amortized, its weighted correction can be Monte Carlo, and part of the model can still be conjugate.

    The phrases exact, conjugate, variational, Monte Carlo, and amortized answer different questions. They are not five rungs of one accuracy ladder.

    Table INFER.1. One Model, Five Inference Regimes
    Property What it says When it can be exact What can still fail
    Exact inference The returned sum, integral, marginal, or draw has the declared target under the model, up to numerical arithmetic Enumeration, variable elimination, dynamic programming, analytic conditioning, or exact direct sampling Cost can be exponential; finite precision and implementation errors remain; exact inference in a misspecified model is still inference in that model
    Conjugate inference Prior and likelihood combine into a posterior in a known family through sufficient statistics For the variables and conditioning pattern covered by the conjugate pair A larger graph can couple variables, an unknown hyperparameter can require another integral, and nonconjugate factors can break closure
    Variational inference A tractable \(q\) is chosen by optimizing a divergence or bound Only when the family contains the target, the relevant optimum is found, and required expectations are evaluated correctly Approximation, amortization, optimization, and gradient-estimation errors can remain
    Monte Carlo inference Random samples or weighted particles estimate a query Some direct samplers are exact; many estimators are asymptotically consistent under support, ergodicity, and moment conditions A finite run has sampling error; normalized ratios may be biased; chains may not mix; proposals may miss support
    Amortized inference Computation is shared across a population of related observations or targets A learned map can in principle reproduce an exact solution on a restricted problem, but the word amortized supplies no such guarantee Distribution shift and an amortization gap; the shared map may optimize average rather than instance-level performance

    Conjugacy is thus one mechanism for exact inference, not a synonym for exactness. Monte Carlo describes how an integral is approximated, while amortization describes whether work is reused. A particle filter with a learned proposal is both Monte Carlo and amortized. A finite-state forward algorithm can be exact and reused without being a learned inference network. A VAE encoder is amortized and variational, but generally neither conjugate nor exact.

    EM, Variational EM, and a VAE Share One Identity¤

    For local latents \(z_{1:N}\) and point parameters \(\theta\), define

    \[ \mathcal F(\theta,q_{1:N}) =\sum_{n=1}^{N} \mathbb E_{q_n(z_n)} \left[ \log p_\theta(x_n,z_n)-\log q_n(z_n) \right]. \]

    Then

    \[ \sum_{n=1}^{N}\log p_\theta(x_n) =\mathcal F(\theta,q_{1:N}) +\sum_{n=1}^{N} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_n(z_n)\,\|\,p_\theta(z_n\mid x_n)\right). \]

    The same identity supports three learning procedures:

    1. Exact EM. At iteration \(s\), the E-step sets \(q_n(z_n)=p_{\theta^{(s)}}(z_n\mid x_n)\), so the bound touches the likelihood at \(\theta^{(s)}\). The M-step increases \(\mathcal F\) with \(q\) fixed. This proves likelihood monotonicity under the usual exact or generalized-M-step conditions; it does not prove convergence to the global maximum.
    2. Variational EM. When the exact posterior is unavailable, each \(q_n\) is restricted to a family \(\mathcal Q\). The E-step improves rather than necessarily closes the bound. The optimized quantity may remain strictly below the observed-data likelihood.
    3. Amortized stochastic variational learning. Replace the per-item parameters by a shared map \(q_\phi(z\mid x_n)\) and estimate the dataset sum with minibatches. A VAE combines this amortized inference model with a neural \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\) and pathwise or other gradient estimators (Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes).

    This bridge is structural, not a claim that all three algorithms have the same fixed points. Restricting \(q\), sharing \(\phi\) across observations, using stochastic gradients, and changing the M-step each introduce different approximation or optimization behavior.

    Counterexample: exact responsibilities do not make EM Bayesian

    In a finite mixture, the E-step can enumerate every assignment exactly conditional on the current component parameters. Standard EM still returns point estimates of those parameters, carries no posterior over their uncertainty, may stop at a local optimum, and cannot resolve an observational non-identifiability. Exactness of one conditional query does not transfer to every learning claim.

    The Same Boundary Across Generative Structures¤

    Table INFER.2. The Same Boundary Across Generative Structures
    Model Latents and graph Inference available because… Boundary after increasing flexibility
    Finite mixture independent categorical \(z_n\) given parameters each responsibility sums over \(K\) states Bayesian parameter uncertainty couples items; rich component models may make their likelihoods or parameters hard to infer
    Factor analysis Gaussian \(z_n\) with a linear-Gaussian decoder Gaussian marginalization and conditioning close analytically a nonlinear decoder usually makes \(p_\theta(x)\) and \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\) intractable
    Topic model document proportions plus token assignments Dirichlet–categorical conjugacy permits useful collapsed or coordinate computations assignments remain coupled through document proportions; large-vocabulary posterior inference is not one independent conjugate update
    HMM or linear state-space model a latent Markov chain with local emissions dynamic programming, or linear-Gaussian closure, reuses messages nonlinear or non-Gaussian factors require particles, approximations, or structured variational families
    VAE or hierarchical neural latent model local neural latent hierarchy a learned \(q_\phi\) makes repeated approximate inference fast the ELBO can retain model, family, amortization, optimization, and estimator gaps
    Discrete-time diffusion noisy states form an auxiliary Markov hierarchy the chosen forward process gives analytic training conditionals and a variational decomposition the learned reverse kernels and simplified denoising loss do not make the exact data marginal or arbitrary posterior query free
    Latent world model controlled latent dynamics and observation/reward factors filters, smoothers, particles, or structured inference networks exploit temporal factorization an encoder state is not automatically a calibrated belief, a physical state, or a causally identified mechanism

    The diffusion row needs particular care. A DDPM can be written as a hierarchical latent-variable model and trained with a variational bound (Ho, Jain, and Abbeel, 2020). Its intermediate noisy variables are auxiliary states in that construction; they need not be semantic representation latents, and the common simplified denoising objective changes the weighting of the variational terms. This is a precise formulation-level correspondence, not the statement that every diffusion method is a VAE.

    Temporal models make the reuse of classical structure especially visible. Structured inference networks retain state-space dependencies while replacing analytic messages by a learned variational approximation (Krishnan, Shalit, and Sontag, 2017). Particle methods can instead preserve weighted-sampling corrections while learning proposals or parallelizing parts of smoothing; Brady, Nüsken, and Li (2026) give one peer-reviewed deep-state-space construction. These methods change the inference implementation and finite-compute trade-offs, not the model/inference distinction.

    The dedicated Filtering and Smoothing for State-Space Generators volume specializes this chapter's target–query–representation contract to online Bayes recursions, exact linear-Gaussian closure, backward trajectories, nonlinear projection, ensembles, mixtures, robust updates, learned filters, and state-space diagnostics.

    Retrieval practice: classify the inference claim
    1. Is a conjugate update always the posterior of an entire graphical model?
    2. Does an amortized proposal make importance sampling approximate in a new way?
    3. Which change turns exact EM into variational EM?
    4. Why is a diffusion hierarchy not automatically a semantic representation hierarchy?

    Solutions. (1) No. It is exact only for the variables and conditioning structure to which conjugate closure applies; other graph integrations may remain. (2) The proposal is approximate, but importance weighting still supplies the correction when support and integrability conditions hold. Amortization changes efficiency and proposal error, not the identity of the target. (3) Restricting the E-step distribution to a family that may not contain the exact posterior. (4) The intermediate noisy states are auxiliary variables chosen for a corruption/reverse construction; semantic content and identifiability require separate evidence.

    Query-First Inference Map¤

    Let the conditioned target be

    \[ \begin{aligned} \gamma(z)&=p_\theta(x,z),\\ Z&=p_\theta(x),\\ \pi(z)&=p_\theta(z\mid x)=\frac{\gamma(z)}{Z}. \end{aligned} \]

    The same unnormalized function \(\gamma\) supports several queries, but their outputs differ. Before choosing an algorithm, write down the exact mathematical object that the downstream task consumes.

    Notice that a mode is not a sample, a posterior sample is not an evidence estimate, and a variational distribution is not the target merely because it was optimized against it. This diagram is the navigation key for the methods that follow.

    The five-field inference contract¤

    An inference claim is incomplete until it declares five fields:

    1. Target: the conditioned law or unnormalized measure, including support, reference measure, and fixed model parameters.
    2. Query: a normalizer, marginal, expectation, event probability, sample, optimum, filter, smoother, or decision.
    3. Returned representation: a number, factor table, parametric density, weighted particles, correlated chain, transport map, or learned predictor.
    4. Guarantee: exact under stated algebra, a deterministic approximation, an unbiased estimator, a consistent limit, an invariant transition, a calibrated region, or no formal guarantee beyond empirical fit.
    5. Finite-compute validation: the reduced exact case, repeated-run uncertainty, convergence or weight diagnostic, calibration experiment, and downstream query check used to test the claim.

    The algorithm name is not a sixth substitute field. “We used MCMC” does not state the kernel, initialization, run length, or query estimator; “we used VI” does not state the family, divergence, optimizer, or calibration behavior.

    Table INFER.3. The five-field inference contract
    Query Required output Exploit exact structure first Approximate routes when exact computation fails Important limitation
    Evidence or normalizer \(Z=p(x)\) One nonnegative integral or sum, often its logarithm Enumeration, variable elimination, conjugacy, forward algorithm, tractable circuit Ordinary importance sampling, AIS, SMC, nested or bridge estimators; VI supplies a bound Posterior MCMC ratios cancel \(Z\), so an ordinary posterior chain does not estimate it
    Posterior marginal \(p(z_j\mid x)\) A normalized probability or density Enumeration, sum-product, junction tree, conjugate update, Kalman smoothing VI, weighted particles, MCMC frequency or Rao–Blackwellized average, SMC A MAP state is not a marginal and can be unrepresentative
    Expectation \(\mathbb E_\pi[f(Z)]\) A query-specific integral Exact summation, analytic moments, dynamic programming Ordinary or self-normalized importance sampling, MCMC averages, SMC, expectation under a variational approximation Accuracy for one \(f\) does not certify tails, modes, or another \(f\)
    Posterior draws \(Z\sim\pi\) Unweighted states with a declared dependence contract Direct conditional sampling, exact ancestral sampling, rejection sampling MCMC; importance resampling or SMC gives finite-particle approximations VI samples come from \(q\), weighted IS proposals are not target draws, and MCMC draws are correlated
    MAP or constrained optimum \(\arg\max_z\gamma(z)\) Max-product, Viterbi, exhaustive search Continuous or combinatorial optimization, annealing A mode discards posterior mass and depends on parameterization for continuous densities
    Online filtering \(p(z_t\mid x_{1:t})\) A recursively updated current-state belief Forward recursion, finite-state HMM, Kalman filter Particle filter, assumed-density filter, amortized filter Filtering cannot use future observations
    Offline smoothing \(p(z_t\mid x_{1:T})\) A past-state belief conditioned on the full sequence Forward–backward, Kalman smoother Particle smoothing, SMC, structured VI, MCMC over trajectories Path degeneracy can damage early-state estimates even when current filtering is adequate

    The table gives starting points, not a method ranking. “Exact” means exact for the declared query under the declared arithmetic and model representation. A probabilistic circuit may answer a marginal exactly but make MAP hard without determinism; a low-treewidth graph may support exact marginals but still have too many states for explicit enumeration. Structure is a computational resource, and tractability is query-specific.

    Continue first to Graphical Models and Structured Probability for directed and undirected law semantics, separation, normalization, CRFs, mixed graphs, and latent projections. Then use Graphical Inference and Message Passing for the common derivation behind enumeration, variable elimination, tree messages, junction trees, loopy BP, and compiled circuits. This volume remains the owner of the broader posterior-algorithm taxonomy, variational gaps, importance weighting, MCMC, and sequential inference.

    A decision sequence¤

    1. Declare the target. State the joint or unnormalized density, conditioning event, support, and reference measure.
    2. Declare the query. Distinguish a normalizer, marginal, expectation, sample, optimum, filter, or smoother.
    3. Search for exact eliminations. Use finite support, conjugacy, conditional independence, linear-Gaussian structure, or a tractable circuit before approximating.
    4. Choose an approximation whose output matches the query. Do not substitute a variational mode for calibrated tail mass or a posterior chain for an evidence estimator.
    5. Budget every error. Separate representation or family error, finite-sample error, initialization and mixing, numerical error, optimization error, and model misspecification.
    6. Validate the query itself. Compare with exact inference on a reduced model and test the particular expectations, events, or decisions used downstream.

    Bias, variance, consistency, and reuse¤

    These words need an estimator and a target. VI returns an optimized distributional approximation; calling “VI” biased without naming a posterior functional is incomplete. Likewise, an MCMC transition can have the correct invariant law while a finite average remains initialization-biased.

    A Finite Atlas: Exact Conditioning, MAP, Quadrature, and Laplace¤

    One posterior can expose the different objects returned by four common routes. Use the rate parameterization

    \[ \begin{aligned} \lambda&\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(\alpha_0=2,\beta_0=1),\\ y_i\mid\lambda&\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda). \end{aligned} \]

    and observe \(y=(0,1,4)\). The likelihood kernel in the unknown rate is \(\lambda^{\sum_i y_i}e^{-N\lambda}\). It matches the Gamma prior kernel, so conjugacy—not merely assigning familiar family names—gives

    \[ \lambda\mid y\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(\alpha=7,\beta=4). \]

    The exact posterior mean is \(7/4=1.75\), its variance is \(7/16=0.4375\), and its mode in the \(\lambda\) coordinate is \((7-1)/4=1.5\). These are three different summaries of the same law.

    MAP depends on the coordinate used for a continuous density¤

    Let \(\phi=\log\lambda\). Densities transform with the Jacobian:

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\Phi(\phi\mid y) &=p_\Lambda(e^\phi\mid y)e^\phi,\\ &\propto \exp(7\phi-4e^\phi). \end{aligned} \]

    The mode of this density satisfies \(7-4e^\phi=0\), hence \(e^{\phi_{\mathrm{MAP}}}=7/4=1.75\). Mapping the \(\lambda\)-density mode gives \(\log(1.5)\) instead. Nothing about the posterior measure changed; the reference element used to define density height changed. Continuous MAP is therefore not a coordinate-free representative of posterior mass.

    The mode also differs from marginal decisions. In the finite joint law

    Table INFER.5. MAP depends on the coordinate used for a continuous density
    \(z_2=0\) \(z_2=1\)
    \(z_1=0\) \(0.30\) \(0.29\)
    \(z_1=1\) \(0.00\) \(0.41\)

    the joint MAP is \((1,1)\), while the marginal modes are \(z_1=0\) and \(z_2=1\); their pair \((0,1)\) is not the joint MAP. A global configuration, coordinate-wise marginal modes, posterior means, and Bayes decisions answer different queries.

    Grid quadrature is a numerical integral, not a density family¤

    On a one-dimensional bounded interval, midpoint quadrature makes the approximation visible. For spacing \(h\), nodes \(\lambda_j=(j+\tfrac12)h\), and unnormalized posterior kernel \(g(\lambda)=\lambda^6e^{-4\lambda}\),

    \[ \widehat{\mathbb E}[f(\Lambda)\mid y] =\frac{\sum_j h\,g(\lambda_j)f(\lambda_j)} {\sum_j h\,g(\lambda_j)}. \]

    With \(h=0.5\) on \([0,5]\), this gives an approximate posterior mean \(1.74866\). The exact Gamma tail probability beyond \(5\) is about \(2.55\times10^{-4}\); both discretization and truncation belong in the error account. Refining a grid is useful in one or two dimensions and invaluable as a test oracle, but its cost grows exponentially with dimension unless structure or adaptive integration changes the calculation.

    Laplace approximation is local and coordinate-sensitive¤

    Laplace approximation expands the log density around a mode \(\hat z\) and uses the inverse negative Hessian:

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(z\mid x)&\approx\mathcal N(z;\hat z,\Sigma_{\mathrm L}),\\ \Sigma_{\mathrm L} &=[-\nabla_z^2\log p(\hat z\mid x)]^{-1}. \end{aligned} \]

    In the \(\lambda\) coordinate, the Gamma log kernel is \(6\log\lambda-4\lambda\). At \(\hat\lambda=1.5\), its negative curvature is \(8/3\), so

    \[ q_\Lambda(\lambda)=\mathcal N(1.5,3/8). \]

    This Gaussian assigns about \(0.715\%\) probability to the impossible region \(\lambda<0\). In the log coordinate, the mode is \(\hat\phi=\log(1.75)\) and the negative curvature is \(7\), giving

    \[ q_\Phi(\phi)=\mathcal N(\log1.75,1/7). \]

    Transforming \(q_\Phi\) back produces a positive lognormal approximation with mean about \(1.8796\), not the Gaussian above and not the exact mean. Approximating and transforming do not commute. Laplace can be effective near a dominant regular mode with concentrated mass, but skewness, boundaries, multiple modes, weak identification, singular curvature, and poor coordinates require explicit diagnosis. Modern finite-sample error bounds remain assumption-dependent rather than turning Laplace into a universal asymptotic slogan (Kasprzak, Giordano, and Broderick, 2025).

    2. ELBO Derivation¤

    Chapter 2 chapter prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the query and target distinctions from Chapter 1 and familiarity with KL divergence. By the end, you should be able to derive the ELBO, separate its functional from a finite estimator, decompose model and inference mismatch, diagnose approximation and amortization gaps, and distinguish a flow used as a variational posterior from the same map used as a corrected proposal or preconditioner.

    What problem is the ELBO solving?

    The evidence contains a hard log integral. The ELBO introduces a normalized distribution \(q(z)\), rewrites that log evidence as a computable expectation plus a nonnegative posterior KL, and then optimizes the computable part. The identity is exact; the approximation enters through the chosen \(q\) and how well it is optimized.

    Introduce any normalized \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) whose support covers the relevant posterior:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\log\int q_\phi(z\mid x) \frac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}dz. \]

    Jensen's inequality gives

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) \ge \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)} \left[\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)\right] \equiv\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ELBO}}. \]

    Adding and subtracting the posterior log density yields the exact gap:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ELBO}} +D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)\bigr). \]

    Therefore the ELBO is tight exactly when \(q_\phi\) equals the true posterior almost everywhere. Rewriting the joint gives the familiar VAE form:

    \[ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{ELBO}} =\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)). \]

    Bound, objective, and estimator are different objects¤

    Three layers are easy to conflate:

    1. The ELBO is a deterministic functional of \(p_\theta\) and \(q_\phi\).
    2. The optimization objective may be the ELBO, a weighted modification such as a \(\beta\)-objective, or a multi-sample bound.
    3. The estimator is the finite-sample computation used to estimate that objective and its gradient.

    Changing a minibatch size or number of latent samples changes estimator variance; it does not change the one-sample ELBO itself. Multiplying the KL term by \(\beta\ne1\) changes the objective and generally means it is no longer the ordinary evidence lower bound for the original model.

    Dataset-level decomposition¤

    Define the empirical aggregate posterior \(q_\phi(z)=\frac1N\sum_n q_\phi(z\mid x_n)\). Averaging the per-example KL gives

    \[ \mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) =I_q(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)). \]

    The KL budget therefore pays both for information about the individual observation and for mismatch between the aggregate code distribution and the prior. This identity is central to rate–distortion analysis: reducing the KL indiscriminately can improve prior matching while erasing information needed for reconstruction.

    Augmented-space view: model fit and inference fit are separate¤

    For a data law \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\), define the inference joint \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)=p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)\). When this joint and \(p_\theta(x,z)\) have compatible reference measures and the required support, the average ELBO satisfies

    \[ \overline{\mathcal L}(\theta,\phi) =-H(p_{\mathcal D}) -D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z) \right). \]

    Applying the KL chain rule in the observed direction gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z)) &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\|p_\theta(x))\\ &\quad+\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)). \end{aligned} \]

    The first term is an observed-space model mismatch. The second is the average realized inference gap. Better inference can reduce the second term for a fixed model, but it cannot make an incorrect \(p_\theta(x)\) correct. Likewise, a flexible model can fit \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\) while its amortized posterior remains inaccurate. The full derivation, graphical map, and a finite case with perfect reconstruction but incorrect prior samples appear in VAEs in augmented space.

    An empirical average is not automatically a continuous-distribution KL

    If \(p_{\mathcal D}\) is represented by point masses while \(p_\theta\) is a continuous density, the two are singular. The empirical ELBO average is still a valid training computation, but the displayed joint KL should be interpreted only after specifying a compatible population, dequantized, or noised data law. This is the same reference-measure issue that appears in continuous maximum likelihood.

    3. Three Sources of Inference Error¤

    Let \(\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)\) be the highest pointwise ELBO attainable in a declared local family \(\mathcal Q\). The posterior inference gap decomposes as

    \[ \begin{aligned} G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x),\\ G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x) &=\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x)+G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x). \end{aligned} \]

    This idealized split, studied directly by Cremer, Li, and Duvenaud, assumes the best local value and trained amortized value are available. A practical error ledger should therefore keep four entries:

    1. Model mismatch: \(p_\theta(x)\) does not represent the data law. This is outside the posterior inference gap.
    2. Approximation gap: the chosen variational family cannot represent the model posterior.
    3. Amortization gap: a shared inference map fails to return the best member of that family for a particular observation. Whether it can close this gap depends on model structure and the function's inputs, not only network width (Margossian and Blei, 2024).
    4. Estimation and optimization error: finite samples, noisy gradients, numerical error, or incomplete optimization miss the intended objective or its best amortized parameters.

    A more expressive posterior can reduce the approximation gap while increasing optimization difficulty. Per-example refinement can reduce amortization error at additional inference cost.

    Variational families and the geometry of reverse KL¤

    A mean-field approximation assumes

    \[ q(z)=\prod_{j=1}^{d}q_j(z_j). \]

    It is computationally attractive but cannot represent posterior dependence. Full-covariance Gaussians restore linear dependence at \(O(d^2)\) storage and often \(O(d^3)\) factorization cost. Structured mean-field retains selected graphical dependencies. Normalizing-flow posteriors transform a tractable base density through invertible maps, while hierarchical and auxiliary-variable approximations introduce additional latent layers.

    Standard variational inference minimizes \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)\) because that is the gap in the ELBO. Reverse KL heavily penalizes placing mass where the posterior has very low density, but it does not directly penalize missing a separated mode. In a bimodal posterior, a unimodal \(q\) may concentrate on one mode rather than average across the low-density valley. This is a property of the direction and the restricted family—not a theorem that every reverse-KL optimization always selects exactly one mode.

    Counterexample: a small ELBO gap is query-dependent evidence

    If two posterior approximations have similar ELBOs, they can still differ substantially on a rare-event probability or tail expectation. The ELBO controls a KL divergence, not every downstream functional uniformly. Validate the posterior against the queries you will actually use.

    From the functional optimum to coordinate-ascent VI¤

    Mean field is more than a covariance restriction: it creates a reusable coordinate update. Hold every factor except \(q_j\) fixed and write \(q_{-j}(z_{-j})=\prod_{k\ne j}q_k(z_k)\). The terms of the ELBO that depend on \(q_j\) are

    \[ \mathcal L(q_j) =\int q_j(z_j) \left( \mathbb E_{q_{-j}}[\log p(x,z)] -\log q_j(z_j) \right)dz_j +\text{constant}. \]

    Normalizing the exponential of the expected log joint gives the functional optimum

    \[ \boxed{ q_j^*(z_j) \propto \exp\!\left\{ \mathbb E_{q_{-j}}[\log p(x,z)] \right\}. } \]

    This is coordinate-ascent variational inference (CAVI). In a conditionally conjugate exponential-family model, the expectation often reduces to an update of natural parameters or sufficient statistics. The same local algebra can be organized as variational message passing. An exact coordinate maximization cannot decrease the ELBO, but that statement does not imply a unique fixed point, a global optimum, or an accurate posterior. Parallel stale-coordinate updates, damping, approximate expectations, and finite numerical solves define different algorithms and need their own convergence analysis. The classical CAVI and stochastic-VI constructions are developed in Hoffman et al., and the local message organization in Winn and Bishop.

    Worked finite example: a factorized optimum can break a real symmetry¤

    Let \(z_1,z_2\in\{-1,+1\}\) and

    \[ p(z_1,z_2) =\frac{1}{4\cosh J} \exp(Jz_1z_2), \qquad J=1.6. \]

    The exact law is unchanged when both signs flip. Consequently \(\mathbb E_p[Z_1]=\mathbb E_p[Z_2]=0\), while

    \[ \Pr_p(Z_1=Z_2) =\frac{e^J}{e^J+e^{-J}} \approx0.96083, \qquad \mathbb E_p[Z_1Z_2]=\tanh J\approx0.92167. \]

    Parameterize a product approximation by \(m_j=\mathbb E_q[Z_j]\). The coordinate update above becomes

    \[ m_1\leftarrow\tanh(Jm_2), \qquad m_2\leftarrow\tanh(Jm_1). \]

    Starting at \(m_1=m_2=0\) stays at the symmetric fixed point, which represents neither positive correlation nor either high-probability diagonal pair. Starting with a small common positive or negative perturbation converges to \(m_1=m_2\approx\pm0.89064\). Those asymmetric solutions have a higher ELBO than the zero solution, yet invent nonzero singleton means even though the exact marginals are balanced. They also approximate the correlation by \(m_1m_2\approx0.79325\), below the exact \(0.92167\).

    The optimizer did not create a numerical accident. Reverse KL plus an independence restriction prefers one coherent diagonal region over a product law that spreads mass onto the low-probability off-diagonal states. Different initializations expose different fixed points; exact coordinate updates do not remove family error.

    ADVI transforms the target as well as the sample¤

    Suppose a model parameter \(\theta\) lives in a constrained space and a differentiable bijection \(T:\mathbb R^d\to\Theta\) maps an unconstrained \(\zeta\) to \(\theta=T(\zeta)\). The transformed joint density is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \log p_T(x,\zeta) &=\log p(x,T(\zeta))\\ &\quad+\log\left|\det J_T(\zeta)\right|. \end{aligned} \]

    ADVI chooses a tractable \(q_\lambda(\zeta)\)—commonly a diagonal or full-rank Gaussian—samples \(\zeta=\mu+L\epsilon\) with \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\), and optimizes

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L(\lambda) &=\mathbb E_{q_\lambda(\zeta)}\!\left[ \log p_T(x,\zeta)\right.\\ &\qquad\left.-\log q_\lambda(\zeta) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The Jacobian term is part of the transformed target, not an optional regularizer. Invertibility is required here because ADVI changes coordinates and evaluates the resulting density. A generic pathwise derivative only needs a differentiable parameter-to-sample construction under its regularity conditions; it does not require every sampling map to be invertible.

    Automatic differentiation removes hand-derived gradients, not posterior geometry. A diagonal Gaussian cannot encode dependence; a full-rank Gaussian uses quadratic covariance storage and a factorization whose dense cost can be cubic in dimension; neither represents separated modes. Centered versus noncentered model parameterizations can also make the same posterior much easier or harder to optimize. These boundaries are explicit in the original ADVI formulation; mixture extensions change the family and estimator rather than making Gaussian ADVI universally multimodal (Morningstar et al.).

    Randomness design is a separate axis from the guide family. Fresh iid draws, deterministic low-discrepancy points, independently randomized QMC scrambles, or a fixed sample-average objective can all estimate or optimize related variational quantities while carrying different uncertainty and convergence contracts. Sampling and Continuous-Time Mathematics derives those distinctions. In particular, deterministic ADVI optimizes a fixed finite Monte Carlo objective rather than changing the posterior family, and ICLR 2026 quasi-Monte Carlo latent-variable models use randomized numerical integration for specialized very low-dimensional latent marginals rather than introducing a general replacement for posterior inference.

    4. Gradient Estimators¤

    Chapter 4 chapter prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Assume the expectation and ELBO notation from Chapter 2. By the end, you should be able to derive pathwise and score-function estimators, explain how BBVI and ADVI instantiate them, identify when exact enumeration or Rao–Blackwellization is preferable, and state separately whether an objective estimator and its gradient estimator are biased. The exhaustive estimator treatment, including discrete relaxations, antithetic couplings, multi-sample gradients, and stochastic computation graphs, lives in Gradients and Geometry; this chapter keeps the inference-facing derivation beside the ELBO.

    Pathwise reparameterization¤

    For

    \[ J(\phi)=\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}[f_\phi(z)], \qquad z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon),\quad \epsilon\sim s(\epsilon), \]

    differentiate the fixed-noise computation under the required domination and smoothness conditions:

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E_s\!\left[ \nabla_z f_\phi(z)\big|_{z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon)} \nabla_\phi g_\phi(x,\epsilon) +(\partial_\phi f_\phi)(z)\big|_{z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon)} \right]. \]

    Here \(\partial_\phi f_\phi\) differentiates only the explicit dependence of \(f_\phi\) on \(\phi\), holding its argument \(z\) fixed. The first term accounts for the dependence carried through the sampled state \(z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon)\); using a total derivative in the second term would count that path twice.

    For a diagonal Gaussian, \(z=\mu+\sigma\odot\epsilon\). This often has lower variance than a score-function estimator because it uses local derivatives, but there is no universal ordering: discontinuities, parameter-dependent support, heavy tails, or an ill-conditioned transform can invalidate the identity or make it unstable.

    Score-function estimator¤

    Without a convenient pathwise construction, differentiate the density:

    \[ \nabla_\phi\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z)}[f_\phi(z)] =\mathbb E_q\!\left[ f_\phi(z)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(z) +\nabla_\phi f_\phi(z) \right]. \]

    For the ELBO, the score-function term simplifies because \(\mathbb E_q[\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi]=0\):

    \[ \nabla_\phi\mathcal L =\mathbb E_{q_\phi}\!\left[ \bigl(\log p(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z)\bigr) \nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(z) \right], \]

    when \(p\) does not share \(\phi\) and the usual support/interchange conditions hold. This likelihood-ratio construction underlies black-box VI. A constant baseline \(b\) can replace the parenthesized learning signal by that signal minus \(b\), because the expected parameter score is zero. Structured baselines must satisfy the corresponding conditional zero-mean argument; an arbitrary function of a sampled child can bias the result.

    BBVI is “black box” with respect to model-specific conjugate algebra, not with respect to computation. It can still suffer severe gradient variance, guide misspecification, support mismatch, and poor local optima. ADVI instead combines valid unconstraining transforms with pathwise Gaussian samples and automatic differentiation. The two labels identify estimator pipelines, not posterior accuracy guarantees.

    Discrete relaxations such as Gumbel–Softmax trade temperature-dependent bias for pathwise differentiation of a relaxed objective; straight-through rules substitute a backward derivative and are generally biased. Exact marginalization is preferable when feasible, while score estimators with control variates remain available for the original discrete objective.

    Rao–Blackwellization and enumeration¤

    Before reaching for a relaxed categorical variable, ask whether part of the latent state can be summed out. If \(z=(z_a,z_b)\) and \(z_a\) has small finite support, compute

    \[ \mathbb E_{q(z_b)}\left[\sum_{z_a}q(z_a\mid z_b)f(z_a,z_b)\right] \]

    instead of sampling both variables. This conditional expectation is a Rao–Blackwellized estimator and cannot have higher variance than the original estimator under the usual square-integrability conditions. Exact enumeration also avoids the temperature bias of continuous relaxations.

    Biased and unbiased gradients¤

    An unbiased objective estimator need not produce an unbiased gradient after nonlinear operations such as self-normalization, clipping, or taking a logarithm. Conversely, a deliberately biased estimator can be useful if its mean-squared error is lower. A rigorous report should name the target objective, state whether the estimator and gradient are unbiased, and measure variance or effective sample size where possible.

    The distinction remains active in current work. For example, importance-weighted VI without pathwise reparameterization (Preprint, 2026) analyzes score-function estimators for multi-sample bounds and proposes an estimator under its stated conditions. Until peer review and broader reproduction, it is evidence that the discrete/nonreparameterizable case is still technically open—not evidence that one estimator dominates VIMCO, marginalization, or model-specific alternatives.

    5. Importance Sampling and IWAE¤

    Chapter 5 chapter prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes normalized and unnormalized target notation plus basic Monte Carlo expectations. By the end, you should be able to distinguish ordinary and self-normalized importance estimators, explain the IWAE bound, derive a particle-filter incremental weight, separate normalizer unbiasedness from normalized-expectation bias, and diagnose support, weight, and path degeneracy.

    This section explains how importance estimators answer latent-posterior and per-example evidence queries. For the complete cross-family derivation, including ordinary importance variance, AIS/SMC normalizer ratios, bridge and path methods, heavy-tail diagnostics, and reporting, read Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants.

    With proposal \(q(z\mid x)\),

    \[ p(x)=\mathbb E_q\left[\frac{p(x,z)}{q(z\mid x)}\right]. \]

    A normalizing flow can make this proposal expressive, directly sampleable, and exactly scoreable. The ratio—not flow tractability by itself—changes an expectation under \(q\) into an estimator of a target integral. The three-interface map states the corresponding VI, importance, and MCMC contracts.

    The self-normalized weights are

    \[ w_k=\frac{p(x,z_k)}{q(z_k\mid x)}, \qquad \tilde w_k=\frac{w_k}{\sum_j w_j}. \]

    The IWAE bound (Burda et al., 2016) is

    \[ \mathcal L_K =\mathbb E\left[\log\frac1K\sum_{k=1}^{K}w_k\right], \]

    which is nondecreasing in \(K\) under standard conditions and approaches the log evidence. A tighter bound does not automatically give a better amortized posterior; inference-network gradient signal can degrade without specialized estimators.

    The raw importance estimator \(\hat p_K(x)=K^{-1}\sum_k w_k\) is unbiased for \(p(x)\) when the proposal covers the target support and the weights are integrable. Its logarithm is biased downward by Jensen's inequality; the expectation of that logarithm is the IWAE bound.

    Weight diagnostics¤

    For normalized weights \(\tilde w_k\), a common finite-sample diagnostic is

    \[ \operatorname{ESS}=\frac{1}{\sum_{k=1}^{K}\tilde w_k^2}, \]

    which ranges from \(1\) to \(K\). It is a heuristic summary, not proof of accurate tail estimation. Report the distribution of log weights, the largest normalized weight, and stability across repeated runs. Pareto-smoothed importance sampling adds a tail-shape diagnostic, but its interpretation depends on the target and proposal and does not turn a support mismatch into a valid estimator.

    Multi-sample objectives require care about which parameters receive which gradient. VIMCO supplies leave-one-out control variates for discrete latent variables; doubly reparameterized gradients reduce inference-network signal-to-noise problems for reparameterizable IWAE objectives. These methods change the estimator, not the probabilistic model.

    The same weights support different learning objectives¤

    When \(q_\phi\) does not share generative parameters \(\theta\), the formal generative-parameter derivative of the IWAE objective has the normalized-weight form

    \[ \nabla_\theta\mathcal L_K =\mathbb E\!\left[ \sum_{k=1}^{K}\tilde w_k \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x,z_k) \right]. \]

    The inference-parameter derivative is not obtained merely by replacing \(\theta\) with \(\phi\): \(\phi\) changes both the samples and proposal densities, and the normalized weights couple the particles. This is why a larger \(K\) can improve the scalar bound while weakening a conventional inference-network gradient, and why VIMCO, doubly reparameterized gradients, and reweighted wake–sleep are distinct estimators or objectives rather than aliases.

    Other bounds integrate information across a path. The thermodynamic variational objective, for example, discretizes a path-sampling identity between the proposal and posterior and yields a family of lower bounds under its construction. Rényi and alpha-divergence objectives make other changes. None is “a tighter ELBO” without declaring the path or divergence, discretization, particle law, parameter block, and gradient estimator. A tighter scalar evidence bound also need not optimize the posterior approximation needed by a particular downstream query.

    Wake–sleep separates model learning from recognition learning¤

    The historical wake–sleep algorithm introduced separate top-down generative and bottom-up recognition networks (Hinton et al., 1995). Its two phases answer different distributional questions:

    • in a wake phase, observed \(x\) and inferred \(z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)\) train the generative model; without a valid correction, using an approximate posterior makes this a biased substitute for the exact likelihood gradient;
    • in a sleep phase, \((z,x)\sim p_\theta(z,x)\) trains the recognition network by minimizing expected \(-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)\), equivalently the inclusive posterior KL averaged over the model's own \(p_\theta(x)\), up to terms independent of \(\phi\).

    The sleep objective encourages coverage of model-posterior states, but its input distribution is the current model rather than the data. If the model misses a real-data region, excellent sleep-phase inference there is irrelevant.

    Reweighted wake–sleep uses importance particles to improve the wake updates and supplies a wake inference-network update aimed at an inclusive-KL target under the data law. Its normalized importance estimates remain finite-\(K\) approximations whose support and weight behavior must be diagnosed. Thus ELBO training, IWAE, sleep training, and reweighted wake updates may share samples while following different population objectives.

    6. Sequential Monte Carlo¤

    SMC propagates particles through a sequence of intermediate distributions using proposal, weighting, and resampling steps. It estimates normalizing constants and represents multimodal posteriors more flexibly than one parametric \(q\). Particle degeneracy, path degeneracy, resampling variance, and proposal quality govern performance.

    The complete Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods foundation derives the generic Feynman–Kac construction, particle filters, resampling, proposal adaptation, smoothing, PMCMC, static-target samplers, learned twists, and particle-guided generators. The normalizer-estimation volume specializes that construction to evidence and ratio uncertainty. This section keeps the compact latent-inference interface needed to compare SMC objectives with variational and wake–sleep objectives.

    For a state-space model with proposal \(q_t(z_t\mid z_{t-1},x_{1:t})\), the incremental weight is

    \[ \omega_t^{(k)}= \frac{p(x_t\mid z_t^{(k)})p(z_t^{(k)}\mid z_{t-1}^{a_k})} {q_t(z_t^{(k)}\mid z_{t-1}^{a_k},x_{1:t})}, \]

    where \(a_k\) denotes the selected ancestor after resampling. The marginal-likelihood estimator accumulates average incremental weights. Resampling combats weight degeneracy but duplicates ancestral paths, which can make smoothing over early states poor. More particles reduce Monte Carlo error but do not repair a proposal that misses relevant support.

    The importance-weight ESS summarizes current normalized weights; it is not the autocorrelation-based MCMC ESS, the CESS used to choose a tempering step, or a measure of unique ancestry. These distinctions and a complete finite-state calculation appear in particle filtering, resampling, and degeneracy.

    SMC, AIS, and diffusion paths¤

    Annealed importance sampling constructs intermediate densities

    \[ \pi_t(z)\propto p_0(z)^{1-\lambda_t}p_1(z)^{\lambda_t} \]

    and applies invariant Markov transitions between importance updates. The schedule, transition mixing, and number of intermediate distributions jointly determine variance. A diffusion or transport path also introduces intermediate distributions, but shared notation does not make the algorithms equivalent: diffusion training typically learns reverse dynamics, whereas AIS estimates a normalizing-constant ratio using weighted trajectories.

    Sequential latent models use particle filters online and particle smoothers offline. Annealed importance sampling uses a temperature/noise path between tractable and target distributions and is important for EBM partition-function estimation.

(3) A sequence of intermediate targets with incremental weighting, resampling, and often invariant rejuvenation moves.     7. Posterior Collapse¤

    Chapter 7 chapter prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the variational gaps and weighted-sampling interfaces developed earlier. By the end, you should be able to diagnose unused latents, separate a probabilistic program from its inference engine, choose among iterative, amortized, and hybrid workloads, and state the simulator-relative scope of a simulation-based posterior or likelihood surrogate.

    If a powerful decoder can model \(p(x)\) without using \(z\), the optimum may satisfy

    \[ q_\phi(z\mid x)\approx p(z), \qquad I_q(X;Z)\approx0. \]

    The KL term becomes small, but that alone is not a bug: it reports that the latent is unused under the chosen objective/model. Remedies—free bits, KL schedules, weaker decoders, hierarchical latents, discrete bottlenecks, mutual-information terms—change optimization pressure or model capacity and must be evaluated against likelihood and downstream goals.

    Diagnosing collapse rather than naming it¤

    Track several quantities together:

    • per-dimension and per-example KL under a clearly stated reduction;
    • active units, with the variance threshold explicitly defined;
    • reconstruction or predictive performance after shuffling or ablating \(z\);
    • mutual-information estimates, with estimator limitations;
    • samples from both \(q(z\mid x)\) and the prior \(p(z)\);
    • decoder sensitivity \(\|\partial f_\theta(z)/\partial z\|\) around relevant codes.

    A low KL with acceptable likelihood can be the correct optimum for redundant latents. A high KL does not prove useful semantics: the code may memorize nuisance details. The test is whether the latent supports the intended probabilistic and downstream queries.

    8. Probabilistic Programming¤

    A probabilistic program separates:

    • a generative program declaring random choices and observations;
    • an inference algorithm such as MCMC, SMC, variational inference, or enumeration;
    • query-specific conditioning.

    The same model can be paired with different inference engines when their assumptions match. Probabilistic programming is therefore a composition framework rather than a single generative-model family.

    Automatic inference does not remove modeling responsibility. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo requires differentiable continuous states; enumeration requires bounded discrete structure; variational guides must cover posterior support; and inference compilation amortizes over a training distribution that may differ from deployment queries. The program determines the target distribution, while the inference backend determines how—or whether—that target is approximated. The MCMC foundation supplies the HMC, warmup, diagnostics, pseudo-marginal, and reversible-jump contracts reused by probabilistic-programming backends.

    The same composition principle is especially visible for random measures. The Bayesian Nonparametric Models volume compares collapsed, slice, sequential, truncated variational, and amortized algorithms while keeping the infinite-dimensional prior distinct from the finite computation.

    9. Iterative, Amortized, and Hybrid Inference¤

    Iterative inference optimizes or samples separately for each observation. It adapts to a new posterior but can be slow. Amortized inference learns a map \(x\mapsto q_\phi(z\mid x)\), paying an up-front training cost for fast repeated queries. Hybrid inference initializes with an encoder and then refines by gradient optimization, MCMC, SMC, or learned iterative updates.

    The choice depends on workload:

    Table INFER.9. 9. Iterative, Amortized, and Hybrid Inference

    One high-stakes posterior Iterative MCMC/SMC or optimization Compute and convergence assessment

    Simulator with no tractable likelihood Simulation-based inference Coverage of simulated training distribution

    The wake–sleep derivation illustrates that the model and inference network need not use the same divergence direction or input law. Sharing samples or alternating updates does not create a single objective automatically.

    Semi-amortized VI makes the hybrid explicit: an inference network predicts initial local variational parameters, then a declared number of instance-level optimization steps refines them. If training differentiates through the refinement, the truncation, optimizer state, stopped derivatives, and memory budget become part of the outer objective. If deployment runs more or fewer steps, the returned inference algorithm changes. The original construction demonstrated this pattern for VAEs (Kim et al.); it can reduce an amortization gap without closing family or model gaps.

    Hybrid workflows can also use amortized inference as a fast first pass and reserve MCMC for observations that fail calibration or convergence diagnostics. This is not an automatic exactness upgrade: the diagnostic, escalation rule, MCMC kernel, and final query estimator all remain part of the contract. Li et al. (2026) develop one such amortized Bayesian workflow for repeated datasets.

    10. Simulation-Based Inference¤

    When a simulator samples \(x\sim p(x\mid\theta)\) but cannot evaluate its likelihood, one can learn:

    • a posterior approximation \(q_\phi(\theta\mid x)\);
    • a likelihood surrogate \(q_\phi(x\mid\theta)\);
    • or a likelihood-to-evidence ratio through classification.

    Sequential methods concentrate simulations near observations of interest. Calibration must be checked under the simulation-based joint, for example with simulation-based calibration rank statistics. Accurate inference relative to the simulator does not imply that the simulator faithfully represents reality; model discrepancy remains a separate error source.

    Worked Example: Exact ELBO Gap for a Discrete Latent¤

    Worked Example: Exact ELBO Gap for a Discrete Latent: Chapter prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter reuses every preceding inference contract. By the end, you should be able to compare enumeration, VI, importance sampling, and MCMC without changing the target; expose a variational family failure independently of optimization; and design validation that separates implementation error, approximation error, finite-compute error, and model misspecification.

    One finite model lets us compare algorithms without changing the target between sections. Let \(Z,X\in\{0,1\}\) and

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(Z=0)&=p(Z=1)=\tfrac12,\\ p(X=1\mid Z=0)&=\tfrac15,\\ p(X=1\mid Z=1)&=\tfrac45. \end{aligned} \]

    We observe \(X=1\) and ask two queries: the evidence \(p(X=1)\) and the posterior mean \(\mathbb E[Z\mid X=1]\). Because \(Z\) is binary, the mean is also the posterior probability of state one.

    Enumeration: the exact baseline¤

    Table INFER.10. Enumeration: the exact baseline
    \(z\) \(p(z)\) \(p(X=1\mid z)\) \(p(X=1,z)\) \(p(z\mid X=1)\)
    0 \(1/2\) \(1/5\) \(1/10\) \(1/5\)
    1 \(1/2\) \(4/5\) \(2/5\) \(4/5\)

    Summing the joint column gives

    \[ p(X=1)=\frac1{10}+\frac25=\frac12, \qquad \mathbb E[Z\mid X=1]=\frac45. \]

    Enumeration is exact here because there are only two states. With \(d\) unconstrained binary latents, a naive table has \(2^d\) rows; conditional independence may permit variable elimination without materializing that table.

    Variational inference: optimize a distribution¤

    Let \(q_r(Z=1)=r\). The ELBO for the observed event is

    \[ \mathcal L(r) =(1-r)\log\frac{1/10}{1-r} +r\log\frac{2/5}{r}. \]

    Its derivative is

    \[ \frac{d\mathcal L}{dr} =\log\frac{4(1-r)}{r}, \]

    so the optimum is \(r^*=4/5\). Here VI recovers the exact posterior because the Bernoulli variational family contains it and the one-dimensional optimum is found. That is a property of this example, not a general VI guarantee.

    For the deliberately suboptimal value \(r=0.6\),

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L(0.6)&\approx-0.79780,\\ \log p(X=1)&\approx-0.69315,\\ \log p(X=1)-\mathcal L(0.6)&\approx0.10465. \end{aligned} \]

    The final value equals \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\operatorname{Bern}(0.6)\|\operatorname{Bern}(0.8))\) up to rounding. This corrects a common category error: the ELBO gap measures the fitted \(q\) against the posterior, whereas Monte Carlo variance would measure randomness in an estimator of the ELBO or its gradient.

    Importance sampling: estimate with weighted proposals¤

    Use the prior as the proposal, \(q(z)=p(z)=1/2\). The importance weight is

    \[ w(z)=\frac{p(X=1,z)}{q(z)}=p(X=1\mid z), \]

    so \(w(0)=1/5\) and \(w(1)=4/5\). Suppose one reproducible four-draw run gives

    \[ (z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4)=(0,0,0,1). \]

    The ordinary evidence estimate and self-normalized posterior-mean estimate are

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat Z_4 &=\frac14\sum_{k=1}^4w(z_k) \\ &=\frac7{20}=0.35,\\[4pt] \widehat \mu_{\mathrm{SNIS}} &=\frac{\sum_kw(z_k)z_k}{\sum_kw(z_k)} \\ &=\frac47\approx0.5714. \end{aligned} \]

    Neither realized value is exact. Across repeated proposal draws, \(\widehat Z_4\) is unbiased for \(1/2\). The self-normalized ratio is generally biased at finite \(K\) but consistent under support and integrability conditions. A rare state with a large weight can dominate both estimates; the proposal count alone does not measure accuracy.

    MCMC: construct an invariant transition¤

    Now target the normalized posterior \(\pi=(1/5,4/5)\) and propose switching the binary state. Metropolis–Hastings always accepts \(0\to1\) and accepts \(1\to0\) with probability \(1/4\), giving

    \[ K= \begin{bmatrix} 0&1\\ 1/4&3/4 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \pi K=\pi. \]

    Starting deterministically at state zero, the successive state distributions are

    \[ \begin{aligned} (1,0)&\to(0,1)\\ &\to(0.25,0.75)\\ &\to(0.1875,0.8125)\\ &\to\cdots\to(0.2,0.8). \end{aligned} \]

    The chain is irreducible and aperiodic, so its empirical state-one frequency converges to \(4/5\). A finite run is correlated and depends on initialization. The acceptance ratio cancels \(p(X=1)\), which is why this posterior chain estimates posterior expectations but does not by itself recover the evidence.

    What changed—and what did not¤

    All four methods used the same joint probabilities. Enumeration returned both queries exactly. VI returned a distribution and happened to be exact at its optimum. Importance sampling returned independent weighted proposals, an unbiased evidence estimator, and a biased finite-sample self-normalized expectation. MCMC returned a correlated path with the desired invariant posterior but no ordinary evidence estimate. Algorithm choice follows the query, model structure, and acceptable error—not a universal accuracy ordering.

    Worked Example: Why Mean Field Misses Dependence¤

    Consider a two-dimensional Gaussian posterior

    \[ p(z\mid x)=\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1&\rho\\\rho&1\end{bmatrix} \right),\qquad |\rho|<1. \]

    A diagonal Gaussian \(q(z)=\mathcal N(0,\operatorname{diag}(s_1^2,s_2^2))\) cannot reproduce the tilted elliptical contours when \(\rho\ne0\). Minimizing \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)\) gives \(s_1^2=s_2^2=1-\rho^2\), so the approximation contracts both marginal variances. For \(\rho=0.9\), each variance becomes \(0.19\), even though the true marginal variance is \(1\). The approximation avoids the target's low-density directions by becoming overconfident.

    This example separates two issues:

    1. optimization can find the best diagonal Gaussian exactly;
    2. the answer is still poor because the family forbids correlation.

    A full-covariance Gaussian closes this approximation gap for this target. A nonlinear or multimodal posterior may require flows, mixtures, particles, or MCMC instead.

    Validating Approximate Inference¤

    No scalar diagnostic certifies an approximate posterior. Validation should move from cases with known answers to representative targets, then test calibration and the actual downstream queries. Each layer answers a different question.

    Layer 1: algebraic and implementation oracles¤

    Start with a model small enough to enumerate or condition analytically. Compare normalized probabilities, log normalizers, moments, quantiles, gradients, and samples—not merely the final training loss. The binary example above catches swapped weight normalizations and MCMC transition errors; the Gamma–Poisson example catches rate-versus-scale conventions, missing Jacobians, invalid support, and MAP/mean conflation.

    Property tests can then generate random small instances. For a finite model, check that marginals normalize and agree with brute-force elimination. For a conjugate model, compare posterior sufficient statistics across many simulated datasets. For a sampler, test stationary expectations on targets with known moments and include deliberately difficult initial states.

    Layer 2: reference targets without pretending they are exact truth¤

    Representative posterior suites expose geometry absent from toy cases. posteriordb, for example, packages model-data targets with high-quality reference draws for algorithm development and benchmarking. Such draws are numerical references, not symbolic ground truth. Their chain diagnostics, Monte Carlo uncertainty, parameterization, and provenance remain part of the benchmark.

    For every target, compare the objects the method claims to return:

    • marginal means, scales, quantiles, and tail events with uncertainty on both the candidate and reference estimates;
    • joint dependence, not only coordinate-wise summaries;
    • log density or normalizer estimates when the algorithm claims them;
    • divergences or classifier tests only after stating their estimator error and dimension sensitivity;
    • failures, divergences, invalid values, and initialization sensitivity; and
    • wall-clock, target evaluations, gradients, memory, parallelism, warm-up, and hardware under a matched budget.

    A method can match means while missing tails, or match one target while failing another geometry. Aggregate ranks therefore need per-target failure reports rather than a winner-takes-all average.

    Layer 3: prior-predictive simulation-based calibration¤

    Simulation-based calibration (SBC) tests self-consistency of a model implementation and posterior algorithm over data generated by the declared model (Talts et al.). For a scalar continuous parameter:

    1. draw \(\tilde\theta\sim p(\theta)\);
    2. draw \(\tilde x\sim p(x\mid\tilde\theta)\);
    3. obtain \(M\) posterior draws \(\theta^{(1:M)}\) using the algorithm under test; and
    4. record the rank
    \[ R=\sum_{m=1}^{M}\mathbf 1\!\left[\theta^{(m)}<\tilde\theta\right]. \]

    Under exact posterior sampling, continuity, and the exchangeability conditions of the experiment, \(R\) is discrete uniform on \(\{0,\ldots,M\}\). Ties, autocorrelation, and finite replicated datasets require the corresponding randomized or adjusted procedure. Rank histograms should be accompanied by uncertainty bands and several scientifically meaningful scalar summaries or projections; one coordinate can be calibrated while a joint feature is wrong.

    SBC validates inference under the model's prior-predictive experiment. It can detect an inconsistent model implementation, biased computation, or posterior approximation over that experiment. It does not show that the prior predictive law describes real data, identify which of model code and inference code caused a failure, or prove accuracy for every individual observation. Opposing conditional errors can also cancel in an aggregate rank histogram.

    Layer 4: validation near the observed-data posterior¤

    Once data are observed, the relevant posterior may occupy a small and difficult part of the prior support. Posterior SBC focuses the self-consistency experiment on the posterior region associated with the observed data. Prior SBC remains valuable for an algorithm or model intended across many possible datasets; posterior SBC asks the more local question for a particular analysis. Neither replaces ordinary convergence diagnostics, because repeated refits can inherit the same numerical and implementation failures.

    For amortized generative posterior estimators, coverage tests can work from samples even when a normalized posterior density is unavailable. TARP constructs random-point coverage tests intended to detect multivariate posterior error. Conformal procedures can instead wrap candidate amortized approximations with finite-sample coverage statements: CANVI supplies marginal coverage for its constructed regions under its exchangeability and calibration setup. Marginal coverage is not conditional coverage for every \(x\), and a calibrated region is not proof that the underlying density, posterior dependence, or all expectations are correct.

    Layer 5: query-specific and model-specific checks¤

    The final test must mirror use. If a posterior controls a safety decision, estimate the relevant tail probability and decision regret. If it initializes a generator, compare prior and inferred latent regions. If it supports planning, propagate uncertainty through the planner and test policy sensitivity. If it supplies a scientific interval, audit coverage, width, and the consequences of model discrepancy.

    Posterior predictive checks probe whether model-generated observables reproduce selected aspects of data; they do not isolate inference error and do not prove the model true. Sensitivity to priors, likelihood choices, simulator discrepancy, and missing mechanisms belongs beside inference validation. A calibrated algorithm applied to a misspecified simulator is still calibrated to the simulator's law, not automatically to reality.

    A minimum inference validation report

    State the five-field inference contract; exact or reference targets; parameterization and support transformations; seeds, initializations, warm-up, and stopping; estimator uncertainty; chain, weight, or optimization diagnostics; calibration experiment and its conditioning distribution; downstream queries; model checks; compute and hardware; and every observed failure. PSIS and variational SBC diagnostics can add useful evidence for VI, but a favorable diagnostic remains evidence about its declared test rather than a universal posterior certificate.

    Practical Diagnostics Checklist¤

    Before trusting an approximate posterior:

    1. write the target, query, returned representation, guarantee, and finite validation protocol;
    2. verify support: every target region relevant to the query must be reachable by the proposal, guide, or transition;
    3. compare against exact inference on a reduced model or synthetic case;
    4. compare with independently produced reference draws on representative targets and retain their numerical uncertainty;
    5. run multiple seeds or chains and report Monte Carlo or optimization uncertainty;
    6. inspect importance weights, tail diagnostics, ESS, autocorrelation, divergences, and mixing as appropriate;
    7. use prior SBC for broad self-consistency and posterior SBC or local stress tests when one observed-data region matters;
    8. test the actual expectations, tail events, decisions, or generated objects consumed downstream;
    9. test posterior predictive behavior and model sensitivity, not only the optimized bound; and
    10. distinguish implementation, approximation, finite-compute, and model failures.

    Misconception Checks¤

    A tighter lower bound always gives better representations.

    No. A tighter evidence bound can improve density estimation while producing gradients or latent representations that are less useful for another goal. State the target query and evaluate it directly.

    The reparameterization trick makes discrete variables differentiable without approximation.

    Not generally. Continuous relaxations introduce temperature-dependent bias; score-function estimators remain unbiased but can have high variance; enumeration is exact when feasible.

    More particles fix a bad proposal.

    Only if the proposal covers the important target support and the required weight moments behave well. A missing mode has zero chance of appearing regardless of a finite particle count.

    Closed-form CAVI updates guarantee the correct posterior.

    No. They optimize one coordinate inside a declared restricted family. The finite Ising example has exact closed-form updates, multiple fixed points, invented singleton means, and missing dependence.

    ADVI makes a probabilistic program inference-free.

    No. It automates transformations, a default guide, differentiation, and optimization. Posterior geometry, discrete structure, parameterization, local optima, numerical behavior, and validation remain.

    Summary and Next Step¤

    Inference is a map from a conditioned target and a declared query to a computational result. Exactness, conjugacy, variational approximation, Monte Carlo, and amortization lie on separate axes: a method can combine several, and no label transfers guarantees to a different query. EM, variational EM, CAVI, VMP, SVI, BBVI, ADVI, and VAE training reuse related lower-bound structure while automating different computations and imposing different family, gradient, and data-access assumptions. Exact structure should be used before approximation; VI, importance sampling, wake–sleep, MCMC, and SMC return or optimize different objects and carry different finite-compute and asymptotic contracts. Their errors come from family restriction, amortization, support mismatch, sampling, initialization, mixing, optimization, and model misspecification. The augmented-space identity makes the boundary explicit: posterior inference error is one term in joint fit, not a substitute for validating the model marginal.
