    Graphical Inference and Message Passing¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Classical Generative Models and Graphical Structure
    • Latent Variables and Inference
    • Objectives and Statistical Distances
    • Graphical Models and Structured Probability

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Filtering 1 — Bayesian Recursions and Information Sets
    • Filtering and Smoothing for State-Space Generators

    A factorized generative model describes a global law through local functions. Graphical Models and Structured Probability establishes the directed, undirected, conditional, and mixed-graph semantics that determine those functions. Inference asks whether that local structure can answer a global query without enumerating every complete configuration. Sometimes it can: variable elimination rearranges sums and products, tree messages cache the resulting partial computations, and junction trees extend the same idea to graphs with cycles. Sometimes it cannot: intermediate factors become exponentially large, or a deliberately approximate message algorithm converges to beliefs that are not the exact marginals.

    This volume follows that computational thread from a three-variable table to current learned inference. The same finite model is solved by enumeration, variable elimination, sum-product messages, and differentiation of its log normalizer. A constrained posterior separates marginal modes, max-marginals, and a globally coherent MAP state. Gaussian messages then expose sparse elimination in information form, while a separate loopy example demonstrates why convergence and accuracy are different claims. The final chapter places compiled inference, randomized optimization, differentiable solvers, belief-propagation neural networks, graph neural networks, and amortized encoders at their correct abstraction layers.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–3 build factor semantics and exact tree inference from one complete example.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–5 add ordering, numerical stability, junction-tree reuse, loopy diagnostics, and approximation selection.
    Research: read all six chapters, including decision-specific message algebras, Gaussian-message convergence, free-energy boundaries, randomized optimization, compilation, implicit differentiation, and current learned-message evidence.

    Evidence convention

    Algebraic identities and mature algorithms are labeled Established. Archival results are Peer-reviewed research. Work without an archival acceptance is Preprint or workshop paper, even when its experiments are promising. A neural method is not described as exact merely because its architecture resembles belief propagation.

    1. From a Generative Factorization to an Inference Query¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know joint, marginal, and conditional probability and the distinction between normalized and unnormalized models. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    • translate a product of factors into a bipartite factor graph;
    • distinguish a directed generative graph, factor graph, plate diagram, computation graph, and message-passing neural network;
    • name the exact query before selecting an inference algorithm;
    • enumerate one finite model to obtain its normalizer and marginals; and
    • explain why small local factors do not guarantee cheap global inference.

    The probabilistic object comes before the graph¤

    Let each factor \(f_a\) depend only on the subtuple \(x_a\). The nonnegative function

    \[ \gamma(x)=\prod_{a\in\mathcal F}f_a(x_a) \]

    defines a probability law only when

    \[ 0<Z=\sum_x\gamma(x)<\infty, \qquad p(x)=\frac{\gamma(x)}{Z}. \]

    A factor graph is a bipartite representation of this product: variable nodes connect to factor nodes whose scopes contain them. It records which arguments each local function uses. It does not, by itself, normalize the product, select an inference algorithm, or assert that the graph is sparse enough for tractable exact computation.

    Directed and undirected generative models both admit factor-graph representations. A Bayesian network contributes one normalized conditional factor per node. A Markov random field contributes nonnegative potentials and usually a global partition function. After evidence is fixed, either representation becomes a product of factors over the remaining unknown variables.

    The running three-variable model¤

    Let \(A,B,C\in\{0,1\}\). Define unary factors

    \[ u_A=(2,1), \qquad u_C=(1,4), \]

    and pair factors, with rows indexing the left variable and columns the right variable,

    \[ f_{AB}= \begin{bmatrix} 3&1\\ 1&3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad f_{BC}= \begin{bmatrix} 2&1\\ 1&2 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    The unnormalized joint mass is

    \[ \gamma(a,b,c)=u_A(a)f_{AB}(a,b)f_{BC}(b,c)u_C(c). \]

    Enumeration is the reference computation against which later algorithms can be checked. Its cost grows as \(2^n\) for \(n\) unrestricted binary variables. Factorization becomes useful only when distributivity avoids repeating most of that work.

    One model, several queries¤

    Table MSG.3. One model, several queries
    Query Mathematical object What an answer must contain
    normalizer or evidence \(Z=\sum_x\gamma(x)\) one nonnegative scalar and its numerical accuracy
    marginal \(p(x_Q)=Z^{-1}\sum_{x_{\setminus Q}}\gamma(x)\) a normalized law over the query variables
    conditional \(p(x_Q\mid x_E=e)\) evidence support, a marginal numerator, and nonzero evidence probability
    MPE \(\arg\max_x\gamma(x)\) one or all globally maximizing complete assignments
    marginal MAP \(\arg\max_{x_Q}\sum_{x_R}\gamma(x_Q,x_R,e)\) the order of sum and max operations; they generally do not commute
    exact sample \(X\sim p\) a procedure whose output law is \(p\), not only a high-scoring assignment

    Evidence can be represented by an indicator factor, such as \(I(B=1)\), or by restricting the relevant factor tables. A posterior is not created merely by shading a node in a diagram; conditioning changes the function being summed or normalized.

    Plate notation shows repetition, not computation¤

    Plate notation is valuable when a generative story repeats local variables. In a latent model with global parameter \(\theta\) and \(N\) exchangeable observations,

    \[ p(\theta,z_{1:N},x_{1:N}) =p(\theta)\prod_{n=1}^{N}p(z_n\mid\theta)p(x_n\mid z_n,\theta). \]

    The plate compresses repeated model structure. It does not say whether the posterior over \(\theta\) is conjugate, whether the \(z_n\) can be eliminated independently after conditioning on \(\theta\), or whether an amortized encoder will be used. A factor graph derived after fixing observations makes those inference dependencies explicit.

    Five graphs that answer different questions¤

    Table MSG.4. Five graphs that answer different questions
    Representation Nodes and edges mean What it does not imply
    directed graphical model local normalized conditionals and conditional-independence structure cheap arbitrary posterior inference
    factor graph variables and local factors in one product acyclicity, normalization, or convergence of loopy messages
    plate diagram repeated variables and shared parameters an executable elimination or message schedule
    computation graph primitive numerical operations for one implementation a probabilistic interpretation of every intermediate value
    GNN or MPNN graph learned state exchange under an architecture calibrated beliefs or exact inference in a declared probability law

    This volume assumes that the factor scopes to be queried are already given. Selecting those scopes from repeated multivariate observations is a different model-selection problem developed in Learning Dependency Graphs from Data. Learning a graph and answering a marginal on the fitted graph can be composed, but neither operation performs the other.

    Counterexample: tiny factors can create a hard sum

    A grid of binary variables may use only pairwise \(2\times2\) factors, yet eliminating variables across the grid creates factors whose scopes grow with the grid width. Local table size and global inference complexity are different quantities.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    The product \(\gamma=\prod_a f_a\) defines the object to be queried; a factor graph exposes its local scopes. Enumeration supplies an exact baseline but scales exponentially. Chapter 2 uses distributivity to eliminate variables without repeatedly evaluating every complete configuration.

    Retrieval practice: model, graph, and query
    1. Does drawing a factor graph prove that its product is normalized?
    2. What does a plate communicate that a factor graph need not?
    3. In the running example, what are \(Z\) and \(p(B=1)\)?
    4. Why must marginal MAP state the order of summation and maximization?

    Solutions. (1) No; normalization requires a finite positive \(Z\) or a construction that proves \(Z=1\). (2) Repetition and parameter sharing across indexed variables. (3) \(Z=87\) and \(p(B=1)=45/87=15/29\). (4) Sum and max generally do not commute, so changing their order changes the query.

    Bridge. The running model is small enough to enumerate, but its factorization already contains a cheaper calculation. Continue continuously or open Chapter 2.

    2. Variable Elimination and the Cost of Intermediate Factors¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the factor and query semantics from Chapter 1. By the end, you should be able to:

    • derive variable elimination from distributivity;
    • execute an elimination step and track its new factor scope;
    • construct the induced graph and identify fill-in edges;
    • distinguish order-specific induced width from graph treewidth; and
    • state time and memory cost using variable cardinalities rather than node count alone.

    Distributivity is the algorithm¤

    Suppose the query retains variables \(Q\) and eliminates \(R\). For one variable \(X_i\in R\), collect every current factor whose scope contains \(X_i\):

    \[ h_i(x_{S_i\setminus\{i\}}) =\sum_{x_i}\prod_{a:\,i\in a}f_a(x_a), \qquad S_i=\bigcup_{a:\,i\in a}a. \]

    Replace those factors by \(h_i\) and continue. Factors that do not contain \(X_i\) remain outside the sum. This is ordinary algebra, but the order of reassociation determines the size of every intermediate table.

    factors = initial factors with evidence incorporated
for variable in elimination_order:
    bucket = every factor whose scope contains variable
    product_factor = multiply(bucket)
    new_factor = sum_out(product_factor, variable)
    factors = (factors without bucket) plus new_factor
return multiply(factors)  # normalize if the query requires a probability

    The pseudocode is exact for finite tables. A robust implementation also tracks named axes, base measures, sparse or deterministic structure, log scaling, and the difference between summing and maximizing.

    Worked example: eliminate the leaves, retain \(B\)¤

    For the running chain, eliminate \(A\) first:

    \[ r_{A\to B}(b)=\sum_a u_A(a)f_{AB}(a,b). \]

    The two entries are

    \[ r_{A\to B}(0)=2(3)+1(1)=7, \qquad r_{A\to B}(1)=2(1)+1(3)=5. \]

    Eliminating \(C\) gives

    \[ r_{C\to B}(0)=2(1)+1(4)=6, \qquad r_{C\to B}(1)=1(1)+2(4)=9. \]

    The unnormalized marginal over \(B\) is their pointwise product,

    \[ \widetilde p(B)=(7,5)\odot(6,9)=(42,45). \]

    Its sum is \(87\), recovering the enumerated normalizer, and normalization gives \((14/29,15/29)\). Elimination did not approximate the table; it evaluated the same sum with reusable partial results.

    Scope growth, fill-in, and induced width¤

    When eliminating a variable, all its uneliminated neighbors become arguments of the new factor. Graphically, connect those neighbors into a clique before removing the variable. Newly required edges are fill-in edges.

    Consider a four-cycle \(A-B-C-D-A\). Eliminating \(A\) combines the factors on \((A,B)\) and \((D,A)\) and creates a new factor on \((B,D)\), adding the chord \(B-D\). The largest factor created by a complete order determines that order's induced width: largest induced clique size minus one.

    The treewidth of a graph is the minimum induced width over all elimination orders. It is a property of the graph after the relevant transformations, whereas induced width belongs to a particular order. A tree has treewidth one; a cycle with at least four vertices has treewidth two; a complete graph on \(n\) vertices has treewidth \(n-1\).

    For at most \(K\) states per variable and width \(w\), a common coarse bound is

    \[ \text{time}=O(nK^{w+1}), \qquad \text{peak table memory}=O(K^{w+1}). \]

    With heterogeneous cardinalities, the cost of a factor on scope \(S\) is proportional to \(\prod_{i\in S}|\mathcal X_i|\), not \(K^{|S|}\). Sparse support, determinism, context-specific independence, algebraic decision diagrams, low-rank structure, or Gaussian linear algebra may reduce actual cost. Treewidth is therefore a central structural control, not a complete description of every representation.

    Ordering is a combinatorial problem¤

    Finding an order of minimum width is NP-hard. Practical systems use heuristics such as:

    • min-degree: eliminate a variable with the fewest current neighbors;
    • min-fill: eliminate a variable that introduces the fewest fill-in edges;
    • weighted min-fill: weight a prospective clique by state cardinalities;
    • nested dissection: recursively separate sparse spatial graphs; or
    • domain-specific orders that preserve repeated or temporal structure.

    Report the chosen order or heuristic, induced width, maximum factor entries, and whether deterministic or sparse compression was used. Reporting only the number of variables hides the main cost driver.

    Exact does not mean cheap, and neural does not mean approximate¤

    Exactness concerns the returned query. Cost concerns the representation and order. A large low-treewidth tabular model can support exact inference; a small dense model can be expensive. A neural network may parameterize a local factor while exact elimination remains possible, or it may parameterize an amortized posterior that is approximate even when the original graph is a tree.

    For a Gaussian graphical model, elimination corresponds to Schur complements and sparse matrix factorization. The relevant width controls fill-in in the Cholesky factor. Exact Gaussian algebra avoids enumerating continuous states, but poor conditioning, floating-point error, and dense fill remain practical concerns.

    Counterexample: the parameter count is not the elimination cost

    One compact network may evaluate a high-order factor with few learned parameters. Eliminating a neighboring variable can still require representing that factor over exponentially many discrete configurations. Parameter sharing does not shrink an exact table unless the inference algorithm exploits its functional structure.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Variable elimination is distributivity organized as an algorithm. Every eliminated variable produces a factor over its remaining neighbors, so order determines induced width and memory. Chapter 3 interprets the same intermediate factors as directional messages and reuses them to obtain every marginal on a tree.

    Retrieval practice: elimination and width
    1. What arguments remain in the factor created when eliminating \(X_i\)?
    2. Why did eliminating \(A\) and \(C\) in the running chain cost less than enumeration?
    3. What is the difference between induced width and treewidth?
    4. Why can two variables with the same graph degree have different elimination costs?

    Solutions. (1) The union of scopes of factors containing \(X_i\), minus \(X_i\). (2) Each leaf sum was computed once and reused for both values of \(B\). (3) Induced width is for one order; treewidth is the minimum over orders. (4) Their state cardinalities and the cardinalities or structure of their neighbors may differ.

    Bridge. An elimination factor summarizes everything on one side of a graph boundary. On a tree, that summary can be cached as a message in both directions. Continue continuously or open Chapter 3.

    3. Sum-Product on Trees¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be able to multiply and marginalize finite factors and follow the elimination calculation in Chapter 2. By the end, you should be able to:

    • derive variable-to-factor and factor-to-variable messages;
    • explain why the recipient's incoming message is excluded;
    • compute exact node and factor marginals on an acyclic factor graph;
    • separate sum-product, max-product, and sampling queries; and
    • implement normalization and log-domain updates without changing the target.

    Two local update rules¤

    For a factor graph, let \(N(i)\) be the factors adjacent to variable \(X_i\) and \(N(a)\) the variables in factor \(f_a\). A variable-to-factor message multiplies information arriving from every other neighboring factor:

    \[ m_{i\to a}(x_i) =\prod_{b\in N(i)\setminus\{a\}}m_{b\to i}(x_i). \]

    A factor-to-variable message incorporates the factor and sums out its other arguments:

    \[ m_{a\to i}(x_i) =\sum_{x_{a\setminus i}}f_a(x_a) \prod_{j\in N(a)\setminus\{i\}}m_{j\to a}(x_j). \]

    The recipient is excluded because its message already summarizes evidence from that side of the cut. Multiplying it back into the outgoing message would immediately double-count the same subtree.

    Once both directions of every edge have been computed on a tree,

    \[ b_i(x_i)\propto\prod_{a\in N(i)}m_{a\to i}(x_i), \]

    and

    \[ b_a(x_a)\propto f_a(x_a) \prod_{i\in N(a)}m_{i\to a}(x_i). \]

    After normalization, these beliefs are the exact node and factor marginals. This is the sum-product algorithm on an acyclic factor graph.

    Why two passes are enough¤

    Remove any edge from a tree. The graph splits into two components. A message across that edge is the partial sum of every factor on the sender's component, expressed as a function of the boundary variable. A leaf-to-root pass computes all summaries toward an arbitrary root; a root-to-leaf pass computes the opposite summaries. Every complete configuration contributes exactly once.

    An implementation may use a postorder traversal followed by a preorder traversal, or any schedule that sends a message only after all prerequisite messages are ready. For a static tree, each directed edge message is needed once. Parallel synchronous updates reach all nodes after a number of rounds controlled by the tree diameter.

    Worked example: recover all three marginals¤

    The leaf messages into \(B\) from Chapter 2 are

    \[ r_{A\to B}=(7,5), \qquad r_{C\to B}=(6,9). \]

    Their product gives

    \[ b_B\propto(42,45). \]

    To send the right-hand evidence through \(B\) toward \(A\),

    \[ r_{B\to A}(a)=\sum_b f_{AB}(a,b)r_{C\to B}(b) =(27,33). \]

    Multiplying by \(u_A\) yields

    \[ b_A\propto(2,1)\odot(27,33)=(54,33). \]

    Similarly,

    \[ r_{B\to C}(c)=\sum_b r_{A\to B}(b)f_{BC}(b,c) =(19,17), \]

    so

    \[ b_C\propto(1,4)\odot(19,17)=(19,68). \]

    Every belief sums to the same \(Z=87\) before normalization, matching enumeration. The messages are precisely cached elimination factors, now reused for several queries.

    Message scale and the normalizer¤

    Messages can be multiplied by arbitrary positive constants without changing normalized beliefs. Normalizing every message prevents underflow, but discarding all scale factors loses the normalizer. To recover \(Z\) or a sequence likelihood, store the normalization constants under a consistent convention or evaluate one calibrated root expression before normalizing it.

    For positive factors, log-domain updates replace products by sums and factor marginalization by logsumexp:

    \[ \log m_{a\to i}(x_i) =\operatorname{logsumexp}_{x_{a\setminus i}} \left[ \log f_a(x_a)+ \sum_{j\in N(a)\setminus\{i\}}\log m_{j\to a}(x_j) \right]. \]

    Zero factors represent impossible configurations and become \(-\infty\) in log space. Code must define how an all-impossible message is reported rather than silently producing NaN after normalization.

    Chains, hidden Markov models, and state-space inference¤

    Forward-backward in a hidden Markov model is sum-product on a chain. A forward message combines the filtered state with a transition and new emission; a backward message summarizes later observations. Their product gives a smoothed marginal. Viterbi replaces summation by maximization and stores backpointers. Forward-filtering backward-sampling uses calibrated conditionals to draw a complete posterior path.

    These computations answer different queries:

    Table MSG.5. Chains, hidden Markov models, and state-space inference
    Operation Replaced algebra Result
    sum-product sum and product exact marginals and normalizer on a tree
    max-product / max-sum max and product / addition in log space max-marginals; global MPE requires compatible traceback
    backward sampling normalized calibrated factors exact posterior sample on a tree
    coordinatewise marginal mode argmax of each exact marginal minimum per-node 0–1 loss; need not be a legal or globally most probable joint state

    Sum and max cannot be interchanged indiscriminately. Marginal MAP, which sums nuisance variables and maximizes query variables, needs an order compatible with those operations and is generally harder than either all-sum marginalization or all-max MPE.

    Posterior modes answer different decision problems¤

    The distinction is visible in a four-row posterior. Let three binary variables obey an even-parity constraint, so only \(000\), \(011\), \(101\), and \(110\) are legal, with probabilities

    Table MSG.6. Posterior modes answer different decision problems
    State \(000\) \(011\) \(101\) \(110\)
    Posterior probability \(0.27\) \(0.18\) \(0.30\) \(0.25\)

    The unique globally most probable explanation is

    \[ x^{\mathrm{MPE}}=101. \]

    The singleton posterior probabilities of state \(1\) are

    \[ p(X_1=1)=0.55, \qquad p(X_2=1)=0.43, \qquad p(X_3=1)=0.48. \]

    Choosing the most probable value independently at each coordinate therefore returns \(100\), which has posterior probability zero because it violates the parity factor. That coordinatewise rule is still a valid Bayes action when the loss is Hamming loss: each bit is scored separately. MPE instead minimizes posterior expected exact-match \(0\)–\(1\) loss, where the entire assignment must be correct.

    Max-product computes max-marginals. In this example their values are

    \[ \begin{aligned} \zeta_1(0),\zeta_1(1)&=(0.27,0.30),\\ \zeta_2(0),\zeta_2(1)&=(0.30,0.25),\\ \zeta_3(0),\zeta_3(1)&=(0.27,0.30). \end{aligned} \]

    Their unique local maximizers stitch together as \(101\), the MPE. With tied max-marginals, independently breaking ties can again create an incompatible assignment; a tree traceback or another globally consistent reconstruction is required.

    Marginal MAP introduces a third decision. For query variables \(X_Q\) and nuisance variables \(X_R\),

    \[ x_Q^*=\arg\max_{x_Q}\sum_{x_R}p(x_Q,x_R\mid e). \]

    It maximizes posterior mass after eliminating \(X_R\); it need not equal the restriction of a full MPE assignment. The elimination schedule must respect which variables are summed and which are maximized because those operations do not generally commute. Thus “run max-product” is incomplete until the query, loss, tie rule, and reconstruction procedure have been named.

    Exactness contract for tree messages¤

    The standard exact guarantee needs:

    1. an acyclic factor graph after incorporating evidence;
    2. factors representing the declared nonnegative finite sum or compatible integral;
    3. every required message computed from the correct incoming messages;
    4. exact local sum/product arithmetic, apart from stated floating-point error; and
    5. a query supported by the chosen algebra.

    A graph that merely looks tree-like, a truncated number of rounds, pruned states, low-rank message compression, quadrature, or particle messages changes the guarantee. Those may be excellent approximations, but they must be named.

    Misconception check

    Exact singleton marginals do not determine the joint law, and choosing the most probable state of every singleton can violate a global constraint. Use factor marginals, a traceback procedure, or posterior sampling when dependencies matter.

    4. Junction Trees and Reusable Exact Inference¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes variable elimination, induced width, and exact tree messages. By the end, you should be able to:

    • construct the clique scopes induced by an elimination order;
    • state the running-intersection property;
    • derive a clique-to-clique separator message;
    • explain how junction-tree calibration reuses work across many marginals; and
    • connect elimination to compiled arithmetic and probabilistic circuits without equating their representation costs.

    Turn a loopy graph into a tree of clusters¤

    The junction-tree algorithm performs exact message passing on clusters. For a discrete undirected graph, a standard construction is:

    1. choose an elimination order and add its fill-in edges, producing a chordal graph;
    2. identify the maximal cliques of that chordal graph;
    3. connect cliques into a tree whose edge weights favor large intersections; and
    4. require the running-intersection property: all cliques containing any variable \(X_i\) form a connected subtree.

    For a directed Bayesian network, first incorporate evidence and convert the relevant factorization to undirected scopes. Graphically this includes connecting co-parents when their child conditional is treated as one factor; a practical implementation can work directly from factor scopes instead of relying on a hand-drawn moralization step.

    Let adjacent clique nodes have scopes \(C_i\) and \(C_j\) and separator \(S_{ij}=C_i\cap C_j\). Assign every original factor to one clique containing its scope and multiply assigned factors into clique potential \(\psi_i\). The message is

    \[ m_{i\to j}(x_{S_{ij}}) =\sum_{x_{C_i\setminus S_{ij}}} \psi_i(x_{C_i}) \prod_{k\in N(i)\setminus\{j\}} m_{k\to i}(x_{S_{ki}}). \]

    This is sum-product again: the “variable” transmitted across an edge is now a separator tuple. After a collect and distribute pass, neighboring clique beliefs agree on their separator marginals. Any node or factor marginal contained in a clique can then be extracted locally.

    Worked example: triangulate a four-cycle¤

    Suppose a model has pair factors on the cycle \(A-B-C-D-A\). Eliminating \(A\) first adds the chord \(B-D\). One valid pair of maximal cliques is

    \[ C_1=\{A,B,D\}, \qquad C_2=\{B,C,D\}, \]

    with separator

    \[ S_{12}=\{B,D\}. \]

    Assign \(f_{AB}\) and \(f_{DA}\) to \(C_1\), and \(f_{BC}\) and \(f_{CD}\) to \(C_2\). The left-to-right message is

    \[ m_{1\to2}(b,d)=\sum_a f_{AB}(a,b)f_{DA}(d,a). \]

    The right-to-left message similarly sums out \(c\). Calibrated clique beliefs can answer \(p(A,B)\) from \(C_1\), \(p(B,C)\) from \(C_2\), and \(p(B,D)\) from either clique. For binary variables, each clique table has \(2^3\) entries. Exactness was recovered by clustering, but its cost rose from pairwise tables to width-two clique tables.

    Variable elimination versus a junction tree¤

    Variable elimination is efficient for one target marginal and one evidence pattern when intermediate factors need not be reused. A calibrated junction tree stores messages or clique beliefs so that many compatible marginals can be read without repeating the complete elimination. Changing evidence may permit local repropagation; changing factor scopes or the elimination order may require recompilation.

    Table MSG.7. Variable elimination versus a junction tree
    Representation Main reusable object Strength Cost boundary
    one VE run intermediate factors for one order/query simple exact contraction repeated queries may repeat work
    junction tree calibrated clique and separator messages all contained marginals after calibration clique tables exponential in width
    arithmetic circuit DAG of sums and products repeated evaluation and differentiation under changing evidence/parameters compiled DAG may be large
    probabilistic circuit a distribution represented directly by constrained sums/products selected exact queries linear in circuit size compact valid representation is not guaranteed to exist or be easy to learn

    Elimination is compilation¤

    An elimination order creates an expression made of local additions and multiplications. Keeping that expression as a DAG rather than immediately evaluating it yields an arithmetic circuit. Shared subexpressions can be reused for new evidence, parameter values, derivatives, and related marginals. This is algorithmic amortization by compilation, not learned amortized inference.

    Probabilistic circuits make the compiled representation the model interface. Smoothness and decomposability support exact marginal evaluation by construction; determinism enables additional maximization queries; structured decomposability controls compatibility between circuits. These properties exploit more than a graph's coarse treewidth, including context-specific independence and logical support. The cost statement is linear in the compiled circuit size, which itself can be exponential in the original model.

    Query-aware exactness¤

    “The model has a junction tree” is incomplete because every finite graph can be triangulated. Ask instead:

    • What is the induced width under the actual order?
    • Which variable cardinalities determine clique entries?
    • Are the factors dense, sparse, deterministic, low rank, Gaussian, or symbolically integrable?
    • Is one query required, all marginals, repeated evidence, MAP, or sampling?
    • What compilation and calibration memory is retained?

    The tree decomposition can also guide hybrid algorithms: exact messages within tractable clusters, sampling across cutsets, variational approximations for selected factors, or neural proposals for costly local integrals. Each hybrid must identify which operations remain exact.

    Counterexample: a tree of huge cliques is not tractable

    The junction graph is always a tree after construction, but a single clique may contain most variables. Tree-shaped control flow does not overcome the exponential state table inside that clique.

    5. Loopy Belief Propagation and Variational Messages¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know the exact tree updates and why a tree edge separates two components. By the end, you should be able to:

    • run the sum-product updates iteratively on a graph with cycles;
    • separate convergence, fixed-point validity, and marginal accuracy;
    • relate interior belief-propagation fixed points to stationary points of the Bethe free energy under the required conditions;
    • derive scalar Gaussian messages in information form and state their tree and loopy guarantees;
    • use damping and residual diagnostics without treating them as correctness proofs; and
    • distinguish loopy BP, generalized BP, variational message passing, expectation propagation, and amortized inference.

    Reusing tree equations on a loopy graph¤

    Loopy belief propagation applies the Chapter 3 update equations repeatedly even though an edge no longer separates independent subtrees. Information can return around a cycle in a later iteration, so the tree proof no longer applies. One must answer three separate questions:

    1. Convergence: do the messages approach a fixed point under the declared schedule and damping?
    2. Validity: do the resulting beliefs normalize and satisfy the local marginal-consistency constraints?
    3. Accuracy: how close are those beliefs or the estimated normalizer to the exact target query?

    A converged run can be inaccurate. A nonconverged run can occasionally yield a useful early iterate. Locally consistent beliefs need not correspond to any global joint distribution on a loopy graph.

    Worked counterexample: convergence without exact marginals¤

    Let \(A,B,C\in\{0,1\}\) form a triangle. Give each edge the same compatibility factor

    \[ f(x_i,x_j)= \begin{bmatrix} 3&1\\ 1&3 \end{bmatrix}, \]

    and use unary factors \(u_A=(2,1)\) and \(u_B=u_C=(1,1)\). Exact enumeration gives \(Z=108\) and

    \[ p(A)=\left(\frac23,\frac13\right), \qquad p(B)=p(C)=\left(\frac{11}{18},\frac7{18}\right) \approx(0.61111,0.38889). \]

    Starting all directed messages uniformly and applying normalized synchronous sum-product updates converges for this instance. Its fixed-point beliefs are approximately

    \[ b_A\approx(0.70691,0.29309), \qquad b_B=b_C\approx(0.63794,0.36206). \]

    The messages converged, the beliefs are normalized, and the answer is still wrong. This finite example rejects the common inference “the residual is small, therefore the posterior is accurate.”

    Bethe free energy: a precise but bounded connection¤

    For normalized candidate factor beliefs \(b_a(x_a)\) and node beliefs \(b_i(x_i)\) satisfying local marginal consistency, the Bethe free energy can be written

    \[ F_{\mathrm B}(b) =\sum_a\sum_{x_a}b_a(x_a) \log\frac{b_a(x_a)}{f_a(x_a)} +\sum_i(1-d_i)\sum_{x_i}b_i(x_i)\log b_i(x_i), \]

    where \(d_i\) is the factor-graph degree of variable \(i\). On a tree, the Bethe entropy decomposition is exact and minimizing this objective recovers the exact marginals and log normalizer. On a loopy graph, it is an approximation.

    Under positivity, interiority, and regularity conditions, loopy-BP fixed points correspond to stationary points of the constrained Bethe free energy (Yedidia, Freeman, and Weiss, 2005). This does not guarantee that an iteration converges, that the stationary point is unique or globally optimal, or that its beliefs are accurate.

    Scheduling, damping, and numerical practice¤

    Common stabilizers include:

    • normalize messages or subtract a log-message offset after every update;
    • update serially, in residual-priority order, or by a graph coloring instead of fully synchronously;
    • damp a normalized update \(\widehat m\) using
    \[ m^{(t+1)}=(1-\lambda)m^{(t)}+\lambda\widehat m^{(t+1)}, \qquad 0<\lambda\le1; \]
    • detect oscillation and track both maximum message residual and belief change;
    • set an explicit iteration and wall-clock budget; and
    • compare with exact enumeration on small induced subgraphs or with an independent approximate method.

    Convex damping in probability space and interpolation in log space are different algorithms. State which one is used. Damping changes the update dynamics, not the target factor graph, and supplies no universal convergence or accuracy guarantee.

    What can be guaranteed in special cases?¤

    Guarantees depend on model class. Weak interactions can make the update a contraction. For Gaussian graphical models, walk-summability is a useful sufficient condition for convergence; when Gaussian BP converges under the standard valid-model conditions, its means can be exact while variance estimates need not be (Malioutov, Johnson, and Willsky, 2006). A statement proved for Gaussian means must not be transferred to categorical marginals, MAP, or normalizer estimates.

    Generalized belief propagation clusters short loops into regions, trading larger messages for a richer free-energy approximation. At the limit of a valid exact junction tree, clustering recovers exact inference. Intermediate region graphs need carefully chosen counting numbers and consistency constraints; “uses bigger clusters” is not a complete guarantee.

    Gaussian messages are sparse elimination in information form¤

    A Gaussian Markov random field can be written

    \[ p(x)\propto \exp\!\left(-\frac12x^\top Jx+h^\top x\right), \qquad J\succ0. \]

    Zeros in the precision matrix \(J\) encode missing pairwise edges. For scalar nodes, write the pair interaction as \(\exp(-J_{ij}x_ix_j)\). A Gaussian message from \(i\) to \(j\) has canonical form

    \[ m_{i\to j}(x_j) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac12P_{i\to j}x_j^2+q_{i\to j}x_j\right). \]

    Combining the unary term at \(i\) with every incoming message except the one from \(j\) gives the cavity parameters

    \[ P_{i\setminus j}=J_{ii}+\sum_{k\in N(i)\setminus\{j\}}P_{k\to i}, \qquad q_{i\setminus j}=h_i+\sum_{k\in N(i)\setminus\{j\}}q_{k\to i}. \]

    When \(P_{i\setminus j}>0\), integrating out \(x_i\) is a one-dimensional Schur complement:

    \[ \boxed{ P_{i\to j}=-\frac{J_{ij}^2}{P_{i\setminus j}}, \qquad q_{i\to j}=-\frac{J_{ij}q_{i\setminus j}}{P_{i\setminus j}} } \]

    The negative message precision is not a negative posterior variance. A message is an unnormalized partial integral and need not be a normalizable density by itself. The combined belief must have positive precision

    \[ P_i=J_{ii}+\sum_{k\in N(i)}P_{k\to i}, \qquad q_i=h_i+\sum_{k\in N(i)}q_{k\to i}, \]

    and then \(\mathbb E[X_i]=q_i/P_i\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=1/P_i\).

    Consider the chain

    with

    \[ J= \begin{bmatrix} 2&-1&0\\ -1&3&-1\\ 0&-1&2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad h= \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Both leaf-to-center messages have

    \[ (P_{1\to2},q_{1\to2}) =(P_{3\to2},q_{3\to2}) =\left(-\frac12,\frac12\right). \]

    Thus \(P_2=2\) and \(q_2=1\), giving mean \(1/2\) and variance \(1/2\). The message from node \(2\) to node \(1\), after absorbing node \(3\), is

    \[ (P_{2\to1},q_{2\to1}) =\left(-\frac25,\frac15\right), \]

    so node \(1\) has precision \(8/5\), information \(6/5\), mean \(3/4\), and variance \(5/8\). Direct linear algebra confirms

    \[ J^{-1}h= \begin{bmatrix}3/4\\1/2\\3/4\end{bmatrix}, \qquad J^{-1}=\frac18 \begin{bmatrix} 5&2&1\\ 2&4&2\\ 1&2&5 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    On a Gaussian tree these messages are exact sparse elimination. On a loopy Gaussian graph, convergence is a separate question. Walk-summability is one useful sufficient condition; when standard Gaussian BP converges under its valid-model conditions, the means can be exact while the marginal variances remain approximate (Malioutov, Johnson, and Willsky, 2006). Block messages, reparameterization, damping, and alternative splittings change the convergence analysis.

    Do not transfer that theorem to a nonlinear or non-Gaussian factor graph merely because every local message has been projected to a Gaussian. Deep Gaussian factor graphs (Peer-reviewed research, ICML 2024) learn within a Gaussian-message construction; Gaussian Ensemble Belief Propagation (Peer-reviewed research, ICLR 2025) uses ensemble and low-rank local approximations for black-box factors. A 2026 preprint derives Gaussianization with graph, factor, uncertainty, and asymptotic assumptions. None of these results makes an arbitrary locally Gaussianized loopy solver exact.

    Expectation propagation replaces one site through a tilted law¤

    Expectation propagation (EP) uses a different approximation object from mean-field VI. Write a target and tractable approximation as

    \[ p(z)=\frac{1}{Z}f_0(z)\prod_{n=1}^{N}f_n(z), \qquad q(z)=\frac{1}{Z_q}f_0(z)\prod_{n=1}^{N}t_n(z), \]

    where each tractable site \(t_n\) approximates the effect of \(f_n\). One EP update performs four distinct operations:

    \[ \begin{aligned} q_{\setminus n}(z) &\propto \frac{q(z)}{t_n(z)} &&\text{remove the old site},\\ \widetilde p_n(z) &\propto f_n(z)q_{\setminus n}(z) &&\text{form the tilted law},\\ q^{\mathrm{proj}}_n &=\arg\min_{r\in\mathcal Q} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\widetilde p_n\|r) &&\text{project by moment matching},\\ t_n^{\mathrm{new}}(z) &\propto\frac{q^{\mathrm{proj}}_n(z)}{q_{\setminus n}(z)} &&\text{replace the site}. \end{aligned} \]

    If \(\mathcal Q\) is a regular exponential family and the tilted moments exist, the forward-KL projection matches its sufficient-statistic expectations. The factor \(f_n\) itself need not belong to that family; the required tilted integrals may still need quadrature, analytic identities, or another approximation. Minka's corrected UAI paper and thesis give the foundational construction.

    This local forward-KL projection is not the global reverse-KL objective of ordinary ELBO VI. Repeated site replacement may converge to a fixed point of an EP energy under the applicable formulation, but EP can oscillate, produce improper cavity or site parameters, or converge to an inaccurate approximation. Damping a site in natural-parameter space can stabilize the iteration without proving accuracy. Assumed-density filtering is the one-pass relative that folds each factor into the current approximation without later removing and revising its site.

    Power EP changes the amount of a site removed and the factor power inserted; limits connect to variational constructions under particular alpha-divergence conventions. Alpha symbols and divergence directions differ across papers, so an implementation must state its cavity, tilted law, energy, and limiting convention rather than claim that EP “is forward KL.” Stochastic EP compresses many tied sites into a global approximation to reduce memory (Li, Hernández-Lobato, and Turner); black-box alpha methods define another scalable objective (Hernández-Lobato et al.). These are related algorithms, not interchangeable names.

    6. Compilation, Differentiation, and Learned Inference¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes exact and approximate message passing and basic automatic differentiation. By the end, you should be able to:

    • derive marginals from derivatives of a log partition function;
    • distinguish compiled exact reuse from learned amortization;
    • explain why integrated nested Laplace approximation is specialized to a latent Gaussian model with sparse structure and low-dimensional hyperparameters;
    • identify what remains exact when messages are unrolled and differentiated;
    • distinguish exact configuration-level Gumbel-Max sampling, approximate low-dimensional perturbations, and exact Gaussian perturb-and-solve;
    • separate probabilistic BP from a general GNN or MPNN; and

    Differentiate a correct normalizer to obtain moments¤

    Write an exponential-family factorization as

    \[ p_\eta(x)=\exp\{\eta^\top T(x)-A(\eta)\}, \qquad A(\eta)=\log Z(\eta). \]

    Whenever differentiation and summation or integration may be exchanged,

    \[ \nabla_\eta A(\eta)=\mathbb E_{p_\eta}[T(X)]. \]

    For a tabular factor entry parameterized by its log value, this becomes

    \[ \frac{\partial\log Z}{\partial\log f_a(x_a)} =p_\eta(X_a=x_a). \]

    Thus an exact differentiable program for \(\log Z\) can return all represented factor marginals in one reverse-mode pass. The gradient is exact only to the extent that the forward normalizer computation and numerical differentiation are exact.

    Worked example: differentiate the running chain¤

    In the running model, scale \(u_A(0)\) by \(e^\theta\). Differentiating at \(\theta=0\) gives

    \[ \left.\frac{\partial\log Z(\theta)}{\partial\theta}\right|_{\theta=0} =p(A=0)=\frac{54}{87}=\frac{18}{29}. \]

    Likewise, differentiating with respect to \(\log f_{AB}(0,0)\) returns

    \[ p(A=0,B=0)=\frac{12+24}{87}=\frac{12}{29}. \]

    These values agree with enumeration and calibrated messages. Automatic differentiation changed how the marginals were extracted, not the probability law or the sum being computed.

    Randomized optimization: when a MAP oracle becomes a sampler¤

    Optimization returns a high-scoring state; sampling must reproduce an entire law. Random perturbations connect the two only under a declared construction. For a finite configuration space \(\mathcal X\) with unnormalized log mass \(a(x)\), draw one independent standard Gumbel variable \(G_x\) for every complete configuration. Then

    \[ X^*=\arg\max_{x\in\mathcal X}\{a(x)+G_x\} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \Pr(X^*=x)=\frac{e^{a(x)}}{\sum_{x'}e^{a(x')}}. \]

    For weights \((1,2,5)\), the returned configuration therefore has probabilities \((1/8,2/8,5/8)\). This is an exact sample, not a temperature heuristic. The difficulty is computational: a structured state with exponentially many configurations appears to require exponentially many independent perturbations and a global argmax.

    Low-dimensional perturb-and-MAP replaces that field with noise on unary or local factors so a structured MAP solver can be reused. It is generally a different output law. For example, if an equality constraint leaves only \(00\) and \(11\), full-configuration Gumbel-Max compares one difference of two Gumbels, which is logistic with variance \(\pi^2/3\). Independently perturbing the two unary factors compares a sum of two independent logistic differences, whose variance is \(2\pi^2/3\). The selection law therefore cannot agree for all score gaps. Factorwise perturbation can be useful, but exactness needs a separate theorem, correction, or special factorization.

    This boundary explains the difference between exact Gumbel-process search such as A* sampling, low-dimensional methods with exact or approximate regimes (Hazan, Maji, and Jaakkola, 2013), and perturb-and-max-product for discrete EBMs, which is presented as approximate sampling. A continuous relaxation of the argmax supplies a differentiable surrogate; it does not preserve the discrete sample law automatically.

    Gaussian quadratic models provide an important exact local construction. If

    \[ p(x)\propto \prod_{\ell=1}^{L} \mathcal N(F_\ell x;\mu_\ell,\Sigma_\ell), \]

    define

    \[ J=\sum_\ell F_\ell^\top\Sigma_\ell^{-1}F_\ell, \qquad h=\sum_\ell F_\ell^\top\Sigma_\ell^{-1}\mu_\ell. \]

    Draw \(\widetilde\mu_\ell\sim \mathcal N(\mu_\ell,\Sigma_\ell)\) independently and solve

    \[ \widetilde x =J^{-1}\sum_\ell F_\ell^\top\Sigma_\ell^{-1}\widetilde\mu_\ell. \]

    Then \(\mathbb E[\widetilde x]=J^{-1}h\) and \(\operatorname{Cov}(\widetilde x)=J^{-1}\), so the solve returns an exact Gaussian sample when \(J\) is nonsingular and the factor perturbations and solve are exact. This Gaussian local-perturbation construction can exploit sparse or matrix-free solvers. Finite solver tolerance changes the sample unless its error is controlled or corrected.

    Keep a randomized-optimization audit with:

    1. the target score and temperature;
    2. the unit being perturbed—configuration, factor, variable, or continuous field;
    3. the noise law and dependence structure;
    4. whether the argmax or linear solve is exact, bounded, relaxed, or truncated;
    5. the theorem or correction connecting returned states to the target law; and
    6. small-state frequency, moment, constraint, and independence checks.

    Gumbel noise can also be used as latent randomness in a learned or distilled generator. For example, Gumbel Distillation for Parallel Text Generation (Preprint, 2026) trains a parallel model using a Gumbel-coupled autoregressive teacher. That is a distillation construction with empirical claims, not evidence that arbitrary local perturb-and-MAP samples the teacher exactly. The few-step and distillation volume provides the corresponding teacher–student evaluation contract.

    Differentiating through messages¤

    Unrolling \(L\) iterations creates a feed-forward computation graph. Gradients can train factor parameters, damping, schedules, or downstream objectives. On a tree with a dependency-respecting complete schedule, the exact result needs finitely many messages; differentiating that exact computation is principled. On a loopy graph, differentiating \(L\) iterations optimizes the behavior of that truncated approximate solver.

    Implicit differentiation treats a converged fixed point \(m^*=F_\theta(m^*)\) as a constraint. It requires a sufficiently regular fixed point and solving a linear system involving \(I-\partial F/\partial m\). Nonconvergence, nonuniqueness, singular Jacobians, or switching between fixed points can invalidate a naive gradient. “No need to store every iterate” is a memory statement, not a correctness theorem.

    Integrated nested Laplace approximation: structure-specific deterministic inference¤

    Some graphical models expose a more specialized deterministic route. A latent Gaussian model has a latent field \(x\) whose conditional prior is Gaussian with sparse precision \(Q(\theta)\), a comparatively low-dimensional hyperparameter \(\theta\), and observations that are conditionally independent given components or linear projections of \(x\):

    Define the conditionally factorized observation likelihood

    \[ L(y\mid x,\theta) =\prod_{i=1}^{n}p(y_i\mid\eta_i(x),\theta). \]

    Then the joint model is

    \[ p(y,x,\theta)=p(\theta)p(x\mid\theta)L(y\mid x,\theta), \]

    with sparse latent Gaussian law

    \[ p(x\mid\theta) =\mathcal N\!\left(x;m(\theta),Q(\theta)^{-1}\right). \]

    The posterior is generally non-Gaussian when the observation laws are, for example, Bernoulli or Poisson. Integrated nested Laplace approximation (INLA) targets marginal posteriors without constructing an MCMC chain. Its two integrations make the name literal:

    \[ \widetilde p(x_i\mid y) =\int \widetilde p(x_i\mid\theta,y) \widetilde p(\theta\mid y)\,d\theta. \]

    With quadrature points \(\theta_k\) and area weights \(\Delta_k\), write

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widetilde p(x_i\mid y) &\approx\sum_k w_k\widetilde p(x_i\mid\theta_k,y),\\ w_k&=\widetilde p(\theta_k\mid y)\Delta_k. \end{aligned} \]

    First, a Gaussian approximation to \(p(x\mid\theta,y)\) around its conditional mode supplies a Laplace approximation to \(p(\theta\mid y)\). Second, Gaussian, simplified-Laplace, or full-Laplace approximations estimate \(p(x_i\mid\theta,y)\) at selected \(\theta_k\). Numerical integration over those points yields the marginal. Sparse Cholesky operations exploit the GMRF precision structure; the low dimension of \(\theta\) makes its numerical integration plausible.

    This is not a theorem that every graphical model has fast, globally accurate Laplace inference. The contract can fail or degrade when the latent field is not conditionally Gaussian, the precision lacks useful sparsity, the hyperparameter dimension is too large for numerical integration, relevant posterior mass is strongly multimodal, or local quadratic/skewness corrections are inadequate. A mode-finding routine also finds a stationary point or local mode under its numerical conditions—not an unconditional global maximum. Check approximation variants against exact small cases, importance sampling, or well-diagnosed MCMC whenever the scientific conclusion is sensitive to tails or joint rather than marginal behavior.

    The original INLA construction (Established / peer-reviewed) explicitly assumes a latent Gaussian field, uses sparsity and few hyperparameters for speed, and studies Gaussian, simplified-Laplace, and Laplace marginal approximations. It therefore belongs beside graphical compilation and differentiation as a structure-aware inference method, not as a universal alternative to Monte Carlo.

    Probabilistic messages, neural messages, and architecture analogies¤

    Keep four designs separate:

    1. Learned factors, exact messages. A network outputs local factor values; a tree or tractable circuit still computes an exact query for the resulting model.
    2. Parameterized BP corrections. Learned operators adjust damping or messages while preserving some BP invariances or constraints.
    3. Unrolled approximate inference. A fixed number of recognizable BP, mean-field, EP, or optimization steps is trained end to end.
    4. General MPNN/GNN. Hidden vectors are exchanged and aggregated on a graph, but they need not be log probabilities, normalized beliefs, or messages for any factorized law.

    Belief Propagation Neural Networks (Peer-reviewed research, NeurIPS 2020) parameterize operators on factor graphs and study retained BP properties. Factor Graph Neural Networks (Peer-reviewed research, NeurIPS 2020) show that a factor-graph neural architecture can represent max-product BP. Neither result makes every trained GNN a calibrated posterior solver.

    Current generative connections¤

    Message structure continues to enter generative modeling in several distinct ways:

    • a score, energy, or decoder can be parameterized by an equivariant MPNN while sampling is performed by diffusion, a flow, MCMC, or autoregression;
    • exact or approximate messages can infer latent variables inside a structured generator;
    • a learned message system can amortize inference across related factor graphs; and
    • graph structure itself can be optimized for a downstream iterative decoder.

    Factored Score Matching on Graphical Models (Workshop paper / preprint, 2026) studies exact score computation by messages on trees and contractive approximations on loopy Markov random fields, then uses the structure in a score-based generator. Its stated graph and contraction assumptions bound the theory; the reported molecular results do not establish a general superiority of factorized score networks.

    Factor Graph Optimization for Belief Propagation Decoding (Preprint / conference submission, 2026 record) differentiates through a representation of BP to optimize code graphs. It is evidence for learned graph-and-algorithm co-design in a particular decoding setting, not an established generic inference guarantee.

    These examples belong in this chapter because they connect a model factorization, inference computation, or generative score to learned message structure. A generic graph architecture with no declared generative object or inference query belongs in a graph-learning text instead.

    Evaluation contract for learned inference¤

    Use instances small enough for exact enumeration or junction-tree answers before scaling. Then report:

    • the model/query distribution used for training and testing, including graph sizes and out-of-distribution structures;
    • whether ground truth is exact, bounded, Monte Carlo estimated, or unavailable;
    • marginal error, calibration, normalizer error, MAP objective gap, or sample discrepancy as appropriate;
    • constraint violations and local/global consistency, not only task accuracy;
    • iterations, messages, active parameters, peak memory, wall-clock hardware, and compilation/training cost;
    • sensitivity to initialization, factor scale, graph diameter, degree, loops, and evidence strength; and
    • baselines matched by target query and compute, including exact methods wherever feasible.

    A solver trained only to predict the correct MAP label may produce excellent decisions and poor posterior probabilities. Conversely, small marginal KL does not prove accurate rare-event probabilities, joint samples, or marginal MAP. Match the metric to the exposed query.

    Method selection¤

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Exact inference can be cached, compiled, differentiated, and—in special randomized constructions—reused for sampling. Approximate inference can be specialized to latent Gaussian structure, randomized through lower-dimensional perturbations, unrolled, implicitly differentiated, or amortized. Neural message passing becomes probabilistic inference only when its target, semantics, and guarantees are stated. Across all variants, model structure, query, algorithm, numerical implementation, and empirical evaluation remain separate layers.
