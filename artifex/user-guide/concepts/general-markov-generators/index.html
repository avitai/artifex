    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Few-Step Generation 1 — Finite Transitions
    • Transitions and Few-Step Generative Models

    An infinitesimal generator describes how the expectation of every suitable test function changes under a continuous-time Markov process. Deterministic flows, diffusions, finite-state CTMCs, and continuous jump processes use different-looking equations, but all fit this operator view.

    The operator language is genuinely unifying. It is also easy to overextend: two generators may share an endpoint, a forward equation, or even a population training objective while requiring different admissibility assumptions and simulators. This volume keeps those layers separate.

    The motivating problem is concrete. A generative model proposes local moves---a velocity, a covariance, a token replacement, a birth event, or several of these at once. What has to be checked before those local moves define a valid global random process with the advertised marginals? A neural network can output a matrix or differential expression without producing a conservative Markov semigroup. Conversely, two valid processes can agree on every one-time marginal and still disagree on histories, conditional predictions, and finite-step samples. The book therefore moves through four contracts in order:

    1. the operator contract: state space, test functions, domain, and boundary;
    2. the process contract: existence, uniqueness in law, positivity, and non-explosion;
    3. the learning contract: which conditional target and test-function class identify which coefficients;
    4. the finite-algorithm contract: simulator, mesh or event rule, tolerances, constraints, and measured error.

    Book-length routes into the subject¤

    No single reference spans operator semigroups, martingale problems, jump simulation, and contemporary generator matching at the level needed here. The following routes were checked against publisher, author, proceedings, or journal pages and should be used complementarily:

    • Semigroup route: Engel and Nagel's One-Parameter Semigroups for Linear Evolution Equations develops strongly continuous semigroups, generators, resolvents, perturbations, and approximation. It explains why the domain belongs to an operator, but it is not a generative-modeling manual.
    • Markov-process route: Ethier and Kurtz's Markov Processes: Characterization and Convergence supplies the semigroup and martingale-problem language behind process existence, characterization, and convergence. Chapters 1--2 translate that language into finite checks before returning to neural coefficients.
    • Diffusion and jump route: Øksendal's Stochastic Differential Equations is an accessible route through Itô calculus and diffusion generators, while Applebaum's Lévy Processes and Stochastic Calculus covers nonlocal and compensated jump generators. A finite-state CTMC is the simplest jump process, not the definition of all jump processes.
    • Event-simulation route: Gillespie's exact stochastic simulation paper derives event-time sampling for reaction CTMCs; his tau-leaping paper deliberately trades exact event resolution for speed. Chapter 5 uses their difference to keep a continuous-time generator separate from its deployed simulator.
    • Modern learning route: Generator Matching, published at ICLR 2025, develops conditional-to-marginal generator learning, superposition, and multimodal constructions. The present volume adds explicit theorem assumptions, counterexamples, finite oracles, and assessments that prevent operator-level unification from becoming an algorithm-level claim.

    Translate every borrowed claim into the four contracts above. A theorem about an operator domain is not a simulator guarantee; an exact population regression identity is not a finite-sample rate estimate; and a sample metric is not a proof that a learned matrix generates a Markov process.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to compute an infinitesimal generator on test functions, recover forward/backward equations in finite and continuous state spaces, distinguish diffusion, jump, and deterministic components, state admissibility/non-explosion assumptions, and audit when generator matching or superposition preserves a target process rather than only an objective pattern.

    1. Semigroups, Generators, and Forward Equations¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional probability and elementary linear algebra. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate operator levels: distinguish a transition kernel, semigroup, infinitesimal generator, adjoint, and finite simulator.
    2. O2 — Derive dual evolution: obtain backward observable evolution and forward distribution evolution under an explicit row/column convention.
    3. O3 — Compute a finite semigroup: exponentiate a two-state rate matrix and verify stochasticity, stationarity, and limiting behavior.
    4. O4 — Audit a candidate generator: test constants, positivity, the positive maximum principle, domain, boundary, and non-explosion.
    5. O5 — Use weak characterization: convert the generator into Dynkin's formula and a martingale diagnostic.
    6. O6 — Transfer the contract: specify the missing operator and process data for a neural generator on a new state space.

    Markov transition operators¤

    For a time-homogeneous Markov process \((X_t)\), define the transition semigroup on test functions \(\varphi\) by

    \[ (P_t\varphi)(x)=\mathbb E[\varphi(X_t)\mid X_0=x]. \]

    It satisfies \(P_0=I\) and \(P_{s+t}=P_sP_t\). The infinitesimal generator is

    \[ (\mathcal L\varphi)(x) =\lim_{h\downarrow0}\frac{P_h\varphi(x)-\varphi(x)}{h}, \]

    for functions in the generator's domain. Equivalently,

    \[ \mathbb E[\varphi(X_{t+h})-\varphi(X_t)\mid X_t=x] =h\mathcal L_t\varphi(x)+o(h). \]

    Time-inhomogeneous generative processes use \(\mathcal L_t\). The generator is generally an unbounded operator, so its domain and boundary conditions are part of the model—not optional functional-analysis decoration.

    For a Feller process on a locally compact state space, \(P_t\) is usually studied as a strongly continuous positive contraction semigroup on \(C_0(\mathcal X)\):

    \[ \|P_t\varphi\|_\infty\leq\|\varphi\|_\infty, \qquad \lim_{t\downarrow0}\|P_t\varphi-\varphi\|_\infty=0. \]

    Positivity means \(\varphi\geq0\Rightarrow P_t\varphi\geq0\); conservativity means \(P_t\mathbf1=\mathbf1\) when the constant function belongs to the space or an appropriate extension. These are probabilistic statements. A generic linear operator can generate a perfectly respectable evolution equation without generating probability-preserving Markov transitions.

    The domain

    \[ D(\mathcal L)=\left\{\varphi:\lim_{h\downarrow0} \frac{P_h\varphi-\varphi}{h}\ \text{exists in the chosen norm}\right\} \]

    is dense in the usual semigroup setting but generally not the whole function space. A convenient smaller core can determine the closure of the generator. In learning papers, smooth compactly supported functions are often used as a formal test class; on a bounded domain, manifold, or configuration space, that choice may miss exactly the boundary or topology conditions that distinguish processes.

    Backward and forward equations¤

    The generator acts on observables in the backward equation. Its adjoint acts on distributions:

    \[ \partial_t p_t=\mathcal L_t^*p_t. \]

    This is the master conservation statement. For deterministic flows it becomes the continuity equation; for diffusions, Fokker–Planck; for CTMCs, the chemical/master equation.

    The adjoint depends on the base measure and boundary conditions. Reflecting, absorbing, periodic, and open boundaries produce different processes even when the interior differential expression looks identical.

    The two directions can be remembered through the scalar pairing

    \[ \langle p_t,\varphi\rangle=\int\varphi(x)p_t(dx). \]

    If differentiation and adjunction are justified, then

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\langle p_t,\varphi\rangle =\langle p_t,\mathcal L_t\varphi\rangle =\langle\mathcal L_t^*p_t,\varphi\rangle. \]

    The middle expression is the weak forward equation. It remains meaningful when \(p_t\) has no smooth density, which is why generators transfer naturally to discrete, manifold, and mixed state spaces.

    Generator versus transition kernel¤

    A transition kernel \(K_{s,t}(x,dy)\) describes finite-time movement. A generator describes only the infinitesimal limit. Recovering a unique process from an operator requires well-posedness of the associated martingale problem or evolution equation. Neural coefficients that explode, violate positivity, or fail regularity can make the intended process non-unique or non-existent.

    For time-homogeneous finite chains there is no domain subtlety: a rate matrix \(Q\) has nonnegative off-diagonal entries and zero row sums, and \(P_t=e^{tQ}\) is row-stochastic. In infinite dimensions, a useful necessary diagnostic is the positive maximum principle. If a real test function in the domain attains a nonnegative global maximum at \(x\), a conservative Feller generator satisfies

    \[ (\mathcal L\varphi)(x)\leq0. \]

    At a maximum, a valid infinitesimal evolution cannot immediately increase the already largest observable value. This condition alone is not a universal existence theorem, but violating it is a decisive failure. The semigroup generation theorems add closedness, range/resolvent, density, and growth conditions; the martingale-problem route instead proves existence and uniqueness of a path law for the proposed local characteristics.

    Worked two-state CTMC¤

    For states \(0,1\) with rates \(a:0\to1\) and \(b:1\to0\),

    \[ Q=\begin{pmatrix}-a&a\\b&-b\end{pmatrix}. \]

    For a function \(\varphi\),

    \[ (\mathcal L\varphi)(0)=a[\varphi(1)-\varphi(0)], \]

    and analogously at state 1. With row-vector probabilities,

    \[ \frac{dp_t}{dt}=p_tQ. \]

    The stationary law is \((b/(a+b),a/(a+b))\). Negative off-diagonal “rates” would break the Markov interpretation even if a neural operator still returned numbers.

    The matrix exponential makes the finite-time meaning explicit. With \(r=a+b\),

    \[ e^{tQ}= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{b}{r}+\frac{a}{r}e^{-rt}& \frac{a}{r}(1-e^{-rt})\\ \frac{b}{r}(1-e^{-rt})& \frac{a}{r}+\frac{b}{r}e^{-rt} \end{pmatrix}. \]

    Every row sums to one, every entry is nonnegative for \(a,b\geq0\), \(P_0=I\), and both rows converge to the stationary law when \(a,b>0\). These four checks form a small exact oracle for rate-matrix code. Euler's approximation \(I+hQ\) is stochastic only when \(h\leq1/\max(a,b)\); the continuous generator can be valid while a coarse discrete update is not.

    Worked boundary-domain contrast¤

    The formal interior expression for one-dimensional Brownian motion is \(\mathcal L\varphi=\tfrac12\varphi''\) on \((0,1)\). It does not specify what happens at \(0\) and \(1\).

    • Reflecting Brownian motion uses a Neumann-type domain with \(\varphi'(0)=\varphi'(1)=0\) and preserves total probability inside the interval.
    • Brownian motion killed at the boundary uses a Dirichlet-type domain with \(\varphi(0)=\varphi(1)=0\) for its sub-Markov semigroup; surviving mass decreases when paths hit the boundary.
    • Periodic Brownian motion identifies the endpoints and instead lives on a circle, with periodic function and derivative conditions.

    The displayed differential expression is identical in the interior. The state space, domain, and conservation law are not. Clipping an unconstrained Euler step to \([0,1]\) defines yet another finite algorithm and is not automatically a simulation of reflection.

    Martingale characterization¤

    For suitable \(\varphi\),

    \[ M_t^\varphi=\varphi(X_t)-\varphi(X_0) -\int_0^t\mathcal L_s\varphi(X_s)ds \]

    is a martingale. This weak characterization is valuable on manifolds, graphs, and mixed spaces where coordinate SDE notation can be awkward.

    Taking expectations gives Dynkin's formula,

    \[ \mathbb E[\varphi(X_t)]-\mathbb E[\varphi(X_0)] =\mathbb E\int_0^t\mathcal L_s\varphi(X_s)\,ds. \]

    It suggests a data diagnostic: for several bounded test functions, estimate the left side from trajectory endpoints and the integral from recorded states. A zero empirical residual does not prove the entire martingale problem---the test class and sampling error matter---but systematic residuals falsify the proposed generator or simulator.

    Misconception checks: operators and processes¤

    • “The generator is a small transition matrix.” In a finite CTMC, \(I+hQ\) is only a first-order approximation and can become non-stochastic.
    • “The differential formula determines the process.” Boundary conditions, domain, and well-posedness complete the specification.
    • “The forward equation proves a unique path law.” It describes one-time law evolution; uniqueness of that equation and uniqueness of the martingale problem require assumptions.
    • “A constant-preserving neural operator is valid.” Mapping constants to zero is necessary for conservativity, but not sufficient for positivity, existence, or non-explosion.

    2. P1.2 — Audit an invalid neural rate matrix. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min.     Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    The generator specifies infinitesimal changes of observables, while its adjoint evolves distributions. Validity requires a domain, boundary behavior, positivity, and a well-posed process. Chapter 2 instantiates the operator for flows, diffusions, jumps, and hybrids.

    Does a neural matrix define a CTMC generator?

    Only if off-diagonal entries are nonnegative, rows sum to zero under the chosen convention, and the resulting process is non-explosive.

    2. Flows, Diffusions, Jumps, and Piecewise-Deterministic Processes¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and basic ODE/SDE notation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Read path behavior from an operator: associate first-order, second-order, and nonlocal terms with flows, diffusions, and jumps.
    2. O2 — Derive family-specific forward equations: adjoint drift, state-dependent covariance, and jump-rate terms without suppressing the base measure.
    3. O3 — Construct hybrid processes: write jump-diffusion and PDMP generators with valid clocks and post-event kernels.
    4. O4 — Diagnose explosion and integrability: distinguish finite activity, infinite activity, compensation, and accumulating event times.
    5. O5 — Respect geometry: choose test functions, tangent coefficients, allowed moves, and boundary behavior for the actual state space.
    6. O6 — Transfer by mechanism: choose a family from the path regularity and events a new generative task must represent.

    Deterministic flow¤

    For \(dX_t=b_t(X_t)dt\),

    \[ \mathcal L_t\varphi(x)=b_t(x)^T\nabla\varphi(x). \]

    The adjoint equation is \(\partial_tp=-\nabla\cdot(bp)\). With a sufficiently regular unique ODE, randomness comes only from the initial state; trajectories do not branch after conditioning on \(X_0\).

    For coordinate tests \(\varphi_i(x)=x_i\), the generator returns the drift \(\mathcal L\varphi_i=b_i\). For quadratic tests, it also exposes how a cloud deforms. A first-order generator satisfies the derivation rule \(\mathcal L(\varphi\psi)=\varphi\mathcal L\psi+\psi\mathcal L\varphi\); diffusion adds a nonzero carré-du-champ term. This gives an operator-level way to distinguish deterministic transport from stochastic local variation.

    Diffusion¤

    For

    \[ dX_t=b_t(X_t)dt+\sigma_t(X_t)dW_t, \qquad a=\sigma\sigma^T, \]

    Itô's formula gives

    \[ \mathcal L_t\varphi =b^T\nabla\varphi+ \frac12\operatorname{Tr}(a\nabla^2\varphi). \]

    The second-order term encodes local covariance. Two matrices \(\sigma\) with the same \(a\) generate the same diffusion law under appropriate well-posedness, although their noise-coordinate implementations differ.

    With respect to Lebesgue measure, the forward equation for state-dependent \(a\) is

    \[ \partial_t p =-\sum_i\partial_i(b_i p) +\frac12\sum_{i,j}\partial_i\partial_j(a_{ij}p). \]

    Moving \(a\) outside the derivatives is valid only under additional spatial constancy. Positive semidefiniteness of \(a\) is necessary, but a matrix square root, coefficient regularity, boundary behavior, and a well-posed SDE or martingale problem are still needed. Degenerate diffusion may be entirely appropriate---for example, noise restricted to tangent directions---but it changes which smoothing and uniqueness arguments apply.

    Pure jump process¤

    For finite-activity jumps with rate kernel \(q_t(x,dy)\),

    \[ \mathcal L_t\varphi(x) =\int[\varphi(y)-\varphi(x)]q_t(x,dy). \]

    The total rate \(\lambda_t(x)=q_t(x,\mathcal X\setminus\{x\})\) controls waiting time; the normalized kernel selects the destination. Infinite-activity Lévy processes require a compensated integral and additional integrability conditions.

    In Euclidean coordinates a common compensated form is

    \[ \mathcal L\varphi(x)=b(x)^T\nabla\varphi(x) +\frac12\operatorname{Tr}(a(x)\nabla^2\varphi(x)) +\int\!\left[\varphi(x+z)-\varphi(x) -\mathbf1_{\{\|z\|<1\}}z^T\nabla\varphi(x)\right]\nu(x,dz). \]

    The subtraction makes the small-jump integral finite under the corresponding Lévy integrability condition. Changing the truncation convention shifts the reported drift, so “same jump measure” does not by itself mean two code paths use the same drift parameterization. Courrège-type representation results explain why drift, covariance, killing, and jump terms arise for rich Feller generators satisfying the positive maximum principle; they do not license arbitrary neural choices for each term.

    For finite categorical states this reduces to a rate matrix. Discrete diffusion and masking CTMCs are structured jump processes, not Euclidean diffusions with merely discrete tensors.

    For a finite-state law \(p_t(x)\) under the row-rate convention,

    \[ \partial_t p_t(x) =\sum_{y\ne x}p_t(y)q_t(y,x) -p_t(x)\sum_{y\ne x}q_t(x,y). \]

    The first term is incoming probability flux and the second is outgoing flux. This gain-minus-loss form is the safest way to recover transposes in an implementation.

    Jump diffusion and PDMP¤

    Adding valid components produces

    \[ \mathcal L=\mathcal L_{\mathrm{drift}} +\mathcal L_{\mathrm{diffusion}} +\mathcal L_{\mathrm{jump}}. \]

    A piecewise-deterministic Markov process follows an ODE between random events. Its generator combines \(b^T\nabla\varphi\) with a jump term. This is useful when continuous geometry evolves smoothly but discrete topology, object count, or event type changes abruptly.

    Writing the event rate and post-event kernel separately makes the PDMP contract visible:

    \[ \mathcal L\varphi(x) =b(x)^T\nabla\varphi(x) +\lambda(x)\int[\varphi(y)-\varphi(x)]K(x,dy). \]

    Between events, solve the ODE. Along that trajectory, integrate the hazard until it reaches an exponential threshold, then sample the reset from \(K\). Assuming a constant exponential holding time while \(\lambda(X_t)\) changes along the flow simulates a different process. Davis's PDMP construction formalizes this deterministic-motion/random-event interface.

    Worked birth process¤

    For count \(N_t\in\mathbb N\) with birth rate \(\lambda(n)\),

    \[ (\mathcal L\varphi)(n)=\lambda(n)[\varphi(n+1)-\varphi(n)]. \]

    If \(\lambda(n)=\lambda\) is constant, \(N_t-N_0\) is Poisson with mean \(\lambda t\). If rates grow too quickly, infinitely many jumps can occur in finite time; non-explosion must be checked.

    The rate-growth warning has an exact pure-birth test. Starting at \(1\), the holding times are independent exponentials \(E_n\) with mean \(1/\lambda(n)\). For \(\lambda(n)=n\), the sum of means diverges and the usual pure-birth process does not explode. For \(\lambda(n)=n^2\), \(\sum_n1/n^2<\infty\) and the total time to infinitely many births is finite almost surely. A finite rate at every individual state therefore does not imply non-explosion.

    On a Riemannian manifold, Brownian motion uses the Laplace–Beltrami operator, not the ambient Euclidean Laplacian. On graphs, allowed edges constrain jump rates. On sets with variable cardinality, birth/death moves change dimension. “Use the same generator network” is an implementation analogy unless the operator respects the state space.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    First-order, second-order, and nonlocal terms correspond to deterministic drift, diffusion, and jumps. Their sum can model hybrid evolution, but every component must be a valid operator on the same state space. Chapter 3 explains how conditional generators can be marginalized into a learnable generative process.

    3. Conditional-to-Marginal Generator Matching¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional expectation and the operator families from Chapters 1–2. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive weak marginalization: integrate conditional weak forward equations and recover a posterior-averaged marginal generator.
    2. O2 — State the projection boundary: distinguish one-time marginal agreement from transition-law, path-law, latent-variable, and uniqueness claims.
    3. O3 — Match constrained coefficients: average drift, covariance, rates, or jump kernels in the space in which the generator is linear.
    4. O4 — Audit the loss: explain population Bregman regression, finite-data estimation, parameterization, and optimization as separate layers.
    5. O5 — Diagnose identifiability and singularity: choose a separating test class and quantify endpoint truncation for a collapsing conditional path.
    6. O6 — Transfer the construction: design and validate a conditional generator for a new discrete, continuous, or mixed target.

    Generator Matching, ICLR 2025, generalizes the conditional-flow-matching pattern to arbitrary Markov processes. Introduce a target variable \(Z\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}\) and a conditional path \(p_t(x\mid Z)\) that ends at or concentrates on \(Z\). Let \(\mathcal L_t^Z\) generate that conditional path.

    Under regularity, the marginal generator satisfies, for suitable \(\varphi\),

    \[ (\mathcal L_t\varphi)(x) =\mathbb E[(\mathcal L_t^Z\varphi)(x)\mid X_t=x]. \]

    The derivation is a tower-property argument in weak form, not a pointwise manipulation of densities. If each conditional path obeys

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\mathbb E[\varphi(X_t)\mid Z=z] =\mathbb E[(\mathcal L_t^z\varphi)(X_t)\mid Z=z], \]

    then, when differentiation and integration can be interchanged,

    \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt}\mathbb E[\varphi(X_t)] &=\mathbb E[(\mathcal L_t^Z\varphi)(X_t)]\\ &=\mathbb E\!\left[ \mathbb E[(\mathcal L_t^Z\varphi)(X_t)\mid X_t] \right]\\ &=\mathbb E[(\mathcal L_t\varphi)(X_t)]. \end{aligned} \]

    Thus the posterior-averaged operator solves the desired weak marginal equation. The proof still needs measurable conditional versions, a common test-function domain or justified core, integrability, and existence of a process generated by the averaged operator. Equality on a small empirical test set is evidence for a restricted moment identity, not automatic equality of generators.

    The posterior over \(Z\) is conceptual; training samples \((Z,X_t)\) directly from the conditional construction. Proper regression or matching losses can recover marginal coefficients at population optimum.

    More precisely, the conditional mixture need not retain its original transition law after \(Z\) is marginalized. The formula constructs a Markovian projection whose one-time marginals follow the desired path. It does not claim equality of multi-time couplings or sample paths with the latent conditional mixture.

    This limitation is classical rather than specific to neural models. Gyöngy's mimicking theorem and Brunick and Shreve's extension construct SDEs whose coefficients are conditional expectations and whose fixed-time laws match an Itô process. Brunick and Shreve explicitly separate existence from uniqueness: under their weak conditions the mimicking SDE need not have a unique solution, while additional regularity can yield uniqueness in law and a strong Markov process.

    Markovian projection claim ladder¤

    Accordingly, the safe claim ladder is:

    1. the conditional weak identities hold;
    2. posterior-averaged coefficients are defined on a suitable domain;
    3. a process with those coefficients exists;
    4. its one-time marginals solve the intended weak forward equation;
    5. uniqueness identifies one Markov law;
    6. only a separate argument could identify the original multi-time law.

    A hidden-volatility example makes the last step visible. Let a latent variable \(Z\) choose \(\sigma_Z\in\{1,2\}\) once and set \(X_t=\sigma_ZW_t\). A local diffusion with covariance \(a(t,x)=\mathbb E[\sigma_Z^2\mid X_t=x]\) can mimic every fixed-time marginal. In the latent process, observing past quadratic variation reveals the persistent regime \(Z\); the Markovian projection keeps only the current state. The processes therefore need not agree on future conditionals given a history.

    Special cases¤

    • For deterministic drifts, the identity reduces to conditional flow matching: \(b_t(x)=\mathbb E[b_t^Z(x)\mid X_t=x]\).
    • For fixed diffusion coefficient, drift or score matching recovers the marginal reverse dynamics.
    • For CTMCs, marginal transition rates are conditional averages of valid target-conditioned rates.
    • For jump kernels, matching may predict total intensity and destination distribution separately.

    Average the coefficient in which the generator is linear. For an Itô diffusion, that means \(b\) and \(a=\sigma\sigma^T\), not an arbitrary square root \(\sigma\). In general,

    \[ \mathbb E[\sigma_Z\mid X_t=x] \mathbb E[\sigma_Z\mid X_t=x]^T \ne \mathbb E[\sigma_Z\sigma_Z^T\mid X_t=x]. \]

    For finite CTMCs, conditional averaging preserves nonnegative off-diagonal rates and zero row sums provided the rates are integrable. For jump kernels, it preserves nonnegative measures, but finite activity and non-explosion still require bounds or Lyapunov arguments. A neural output parameterized in logits, square roots, or normalized destinations must be transformed into the linear generator coefficient before applying the conditional-expectation claim.

    For a generator that is linear in a constrained coefficient field, the Generator Matching result uses Bregman divergences: the conditional and inaccessible marginal losses differ by a parameter-independent constant and therefore have the same gradient. This is a population-optimum correspondence. It does not make all coefficient parameterizations, losses, or simulators identical.

    Squared loss gives the most familiar special case. Let \(Y\) be a sampled conditional coefficient target, \(m(x)=\mathbb E[Y\mid X=x]\), and \(f(X)\) the network output. Then

    \[ \mathbb E\|Y-f(X)\|^2 =\mathbb E\|Y-m(X)\|^2 +\mathbb E\|m(X)-f(X)\|^2. \]

    The first term is irreducible conditional variance and does not depend on the network. This identity explains the population minimizer; it does not remove Monte Carlo variance, make a misspecified network contain \(m\), guarantee global optimization, or turn a coefficient-space average into an exact finite transition. For rates or simplex-valued destinations, the loss and parameterization must keep the predicted object in its valid convex set.

    Worked categorical conditional process¤

    Let \(Z\in\{1,2,3\}\) be a data category. A conditional process starts from a mask \(m\) and jumps once to \(Z\) with hazard \(h(t)\). Given \(X_t=m\), the marginal destination rate to category \(j\) is proportional to

    \[ h(t)\,p(Z=j\mid X_t=m). \]

    The network learns a posterior over clean categories from corrupted states. Once a category has been revealed in this absorbing construction, its outgoing rates are zero. A model allowing revisions defines a different conditional generator and path.

    Suppose the data prior is \(\pi=(0.2,0.3,0.5)\) and the mask survival probability is \(S(t)=\exp[-\int_0^th(s)ds]\). Because survival is independent of \(Z\) in this simple construction,

    \[ p(Z=j\mid X_t=m)=\pi_j. \]

    At a time where \(h(t)=4\), the rates from the mask to categories \(1,2,3\) are \((0.8,1.2,2.0)\), with total exit rate \(4\). Over a short interval \(\Delta t\), the reveal probability is \(4\Delta t+o(\Delta t)\) and the conditional destination probabilities are again \(\pi\). If corruption survival depends on the category or on auxiliary observations, the posterior is no longer the prior; training then estimates that nontrivial denoising posterior.

    Test functions and identifiability¤

    Matching \(\mathcal L\varphi\) for too small a test-function class may not identify the entire generator. Coordinate functions can identify drift but not necessarily diffusion; quadratic functions reveal covariance; indicator functions characterize finite-state rates. Objective design must match the operator components being learned.

    For a diffusion, \(\varphi_i(x)=x_i\) gives \(b_i\). Applying the generator to \(\varphi_{ij}(x)=x_ix_j\) gives

    \[ \mathcal L\varphi_{ij}=b_ix_j+b_jx_i+a_{ij}. \]

    Once drift is known, quadratic tests identify covariance. For a finite state space, using every state indicator recovers every row of \(Q\). For a general jump kernel, finitely many moments usually do not determine the destination measure. Universal kernels, characteristic functions, or a declared separating algebra can enlarge the test class, but finite estimation then introduces its own conditioning and sample-complexity problem.

    Endpoint singularities¤

    Conditional paths that collapse to point masses can have coefficients or scores that diverge near endpoints. Time weighting, truncation, schedule design, and parameterization affect variance. Exact endpoint recovery in continuous theory does not justify evaluating a singular neural field at \(t=0\) or \(1\).

    For the absorbing mask with survival \(S(t)=1-t\), the required hazard is \(h(t)=1/(1-t)\). It diverges at \(t=1\), yet its integrated survival law is simple. Stopping at \(1-\varepsilon\) leaves mask probability \(\varepsilon\); clipping the hazard to \(H\) changes the survival to the exponential of the clipped cumulative hazard. These are quantifiable path changes, not neutral numerical safeguards.

    Misconception checks: matching and projection¤

    • “Posterior averaging recovers the latent process.” It recovers a marginal generator under assumptions; persistent latent history can be lost.
    • “Average diffusion square roots.” The generator is linear in covariance, not in a chosen noise factor.
    • “Same population minimizer means same training run.” Finite data, parameterization, optimization, and regularization remain different.
    • “A few moment tests identify a jump kernel.” They identify only the moments those tests expose unless the class is separating.

Averaging logits instead of rates does not directly implement the generator identity.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Generator Matching lifts conditional-to-marginal regression from velocities to Markov operators. The abstraction is powerful because each operator class retains its own validity constraints. Chapter 4 uses operator addition to combine processes and modalities.

    Does one loss work unchanged for every generator?

    No. The conditional-expectation principle is shared, but drifts, covariances, rates, and jump kernels live in different constrained spaces and require suitable losses and parameterizations.

    4. Superposition and Multimodal State Spaces¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes product probability spaces and generator notation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Distinguish four constructions: separate generator sums, convex same-path combinations, random switching, and mixtures of whole path laws.
    2. O2 — Verify valid superposition: check common state space/domain, nonnegative clocks, covariance addition, conservativity, and non-explosion.
    3. O3 — Preserve a marginal path deliberately: use adjoint linearity and identify when an added mechanism changes only paths versus marginal time.
    4. O4 — Couple product spaces: encode cross-modal dependence through joint coefficients, shared events, and state-transforming jumps.
    5. O5 — Quantify splitting error: relate noncommuting generators to Lie and Strang approximations and distinguish local error from invalid transitions.
    6. O6 — Transfer composition safely: design an auditable hybrid generator for a new multimodal or variable-structure task.

    Adding generators¤

    If \(\mathcal L_1\) and \(\mathcal L_2\) are valid generators on a common domain, then under suitable well-posedness

    \[ \mathcal L=\alpha_1\mathcal L_1+\alpha_2\mathcal L_2, \qquad \alpha_i\ge0, \]

    represents superposed infinitesimal mechanisms. Nonnegative scaling changes clocks or strengths. Negative coefficients generally destroy Markov positivity and are not “subtractive generation.”

    “Under suitable well-posedness” matters. For finite CTMCs, nonnegative sums of rate matrices are again rate matrices. For regular SDE components, drifts and covariances can be combined and the resulting SDE or martingale problem checked. For unbounded operators, a common dense domain is only the starting point: closure and perturbation conditions are needed to know that the formal sum generates a semigroup. A simulator that alternates two individually valid mechanisms does not repair an ill-posed continuous sum.

    Validity of the sum is not the same as preservation of a chosen probability path. If \(\mathcal L_1\) and \(\mathcal L_2\) each generate the same \(p_t\), then the convex combination

    \[ \mathcal L_t=\lambda_t\mathcal L_{1,t}+(1-\lambda_t)\mathcal L_{2,t}, \qquad 0\le\lambda_t\le1, \]

    also generates that path because the weak forward equation is linear. An arbitrary positive sum usually changes the marginal evolution unless its extra component has zero adjoint action on \(p_t\).

    If each component individually satisfies \(\mathcal L_{i,t}^*p_t=\partial_tp_t\), their unnormalized sum satisfies \((\mathcal L_{1,t}+\mathcal L_{2,t})^*p_t=2\partial_tp_t\): it generally doubles the marginal clock rather than preserving the same scheduled path. A convex combination retains the derivative. By contrast, if every component is \(p\)-stationary, so \(\mathcal L_i^*p=0\), any nonnegative sum remains \(p\)-stationary. These two cases are often conflated.

    For independent jump mechanisms, rates add. For independent diffusion noises, covariance matrices add, not arbitrary signed noise matrices. Drift fields add directly.

    For two independent jump clocks with exit rates \(\lambda_1(x)\) and \(\lambda_2(x)\), the next event time is exponential with total rate \(\lambda_1+\lambda_2\); conditional on an event, mechanism \(i\) is selected with probability \(\lambda_i/(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)\). This competing-clock construction realizes the sum exactly when the rates are constant between events or the corresponding integrated-hazard procedure is used. It is not a mixture that selects one mechanism once per sample.

    Product state spaces¤

    Let \(X=(C,Y)\) contain discrete category \(C\) and continuous value \(Y\). A generator may combine:

    \[ \mathcal L\varphi(c,y) =b(c,y)^T\nabla_y\varphi +\frac12\operatorname{Tr}(a(c,y)\nabla_y^2\varphi) +\sum_{c'\ne c}q(c,c';y) [\varphi(c',T_{c\to c'}y)-\varphi(c,y)]. \]

    The jump can transform continuous coordinates through \(T\). This models a label switch, bond edit, birth/death event, or modality transition without pretending all variables share a Euclidean representation.

    Multimodal coupling¤

    Independent modality generators produce a product distribution and cannot learn cross-modal alignment. Coupling enters through coefficients conditioned on the joint state, shared events, or joint conditional targets. A text-token jump rate can depend on an image latent; an image drift can depend on text state.

    Missing modalities require a defined state and inference procedure, not a zero-filled tensor by default. Observed-only, imputed, masked, and nonexistent variables have different semantics.

    If the joint generator separates as \(\mathcal L=\mathcal L_C+\mathcal L_Y\), with the \(C\) coefficients independent of \(Y\) and the \(Y\) coefficients independent of \(C\), then an initially factorized law stays factorized. Joint training data alone do not create alignment in such dynamics. Dependence requires at least one of:

    • coefficients conditioned on the other component;
    • a joint event that changes several components;
    • a shared latent variable retained in the state;
    • a conditional target whose posterior couples the coefficient regressions.

    Appending an observed/missing indicator is often necessary but not sufficient: the reference distribution and transition rules for that indicator must also be declared.

    Worked superposition¤

    Suppose a molecule has continuous coordinates \(R\) and discrete bonds \(B\). Coordinate diffusion alone cannot change graph topology; bond jumps alone cannot relax geometry. A hybrid generator uses

    \[ \mathcal L=\mathcal L_{\mathrm{coordinate\ diffusion}} +\mathcal L_{\mathrm{bond\ jumps}}. \]

    If bond rates depend on invariant geometric features and coordinate dynamics are equivariant, the joint process can respect rigid motions. Alternating two simulators approximates the superposition but introduces operator-splitting error because the components need not commute.

    Worked noncommuting two-state split¤

    Let

    \[ Q_1=\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad Q_2=\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\2&-2\end{pmatrix}. \]

    \(Q_1\) activates \(0\to1\) and \(Q_2\) activates \(1\to0\). Both and their sum are valid rate matrices, but

    \[ [Q_1,Q_2]=Q_1Q_2-Q_2Q_1 =\begin{pmatrix}2&-2\\2&-2\end{pmatrix}\ne0. \]

    Lie splitting uses \(e^{hQ_1}e^{hQ_2}\) or the reverse order and has a leading local discrepancy proportional to \(h^2[Q_1,Q_2]\). Strang splitting \(e^{hQ_1/2}e^{hQ_2}e^{hQ_1/2}\) cancels that second-order term and has local error of order \(h^3\) under the required regularity. Each factor is stochastic, so these particular finite products remain valid transition matrices even while they approximate the wrong finite-time law. Higher-order compositions with negative substeps may lose Markov positivity.

    Composition versus mixture¤

    Adding generators makes mechanisms act within the same path. Mixing path laws first samples which entire process to run. These are not the same:

    \[ e^{t(\mathcal L_1+\mathcal L_2)} \ne \pi e^{t\mathcal L_1}+(1-\pi)e^{t\mathcal L_2} \]

    in general. Operator splitting, random switching, and mixture-of-experts each define different processes.

    5. Time Reversal, Simulation, and Error Budgets¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes forward equations, scores or density ratios, and numerical simulation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Reverse with an explicit clock: derive diffusion and CTMC reverse coefficients while keeping density support, transposes, and time convention visible.
    2. O2 — Select a family-specific simulator: distinguish exact event time, uniformization, tau-leaping, ODE, SDE, PDMP, and splitting procedures.
    3. O3 — Verify exact finite oracles: compare small-state simulation with a matrix exponential, moment law, or analytically integrated hazard.
    4. O4 — Diagnose approximation failure: identify negativity, missed events, boundary bias, stiffness, support singularity, and explosion.
    5. O5 — Build a metric-consistent error budget: separate reference, learning, truncation, numerical, and Monte Carlo terms without double counting.
    6. O6 — Transfer to deployment: specify an adaptive simulator and convergence report for a new learned generator.

    Reverse dynamics¤

    Generation often learns a forward corruption and simulates its time reversal. For diffusions, reverse drift uses the marginal score together with any state-dependent covariance-divergence correction.

    For a forward Itô diffusion with covariance \(a_t(x)\), define reverse clock \(s=T-t\). Under the regularity and density assumptions of reverse-diffusion results such as Anderson's reverse-time model, one common component convention is

    \[ \bar b_{s,i}(x) =-b_{T-s,i}(x) +\frac{1}{p_{T-s}(x)} \sum_j\partial_j\!\left[a_{T-s,ij}(x)p_{T-s}(x)\right]. \]

    For spatially constant \(a\), the correction becomes \(a\nabla\log p\). In the machine-learning convention that keeps the original time variable and integrates with negative \(dt\), the same dynamics is often written with forward drift \(b-a\nabla\log p\). Copying one formula while using the other clock flips signs. State-dependent covariance also contributes the divergence of \(a\), not only a score term.

    For a finite-state CTMC with forward rates \(q_t(x,y)\) and positive marginals,

    \[ q_t^{\mathrm{rev}}(x,y) =q_t(y,x)\frac{p_t(y)}{p_t(x)}, \qquad x\ne y, \]

    with time arguments interpreted under the reverse clock. Zero-probability states and support changes require care. The ratio plays a discrete role analogous to a score.

    The identity can be remembered as instantaneous flux reversal:

    \[ p_t(x)q_t^{\mathrm{rev}}(x,y)=p_t(y)q_t(y,x). \]

    This is not an assertion that the forward chain is stationary or satisfies detailed balance. The marginal \(p_t\) changes with time, and the reverse rate is itself time-dependent.

    This is the convention used by the continuous-time discrete denoising framework: \(q_t(x,y)\) denotes the forward rate from current state \(x\) to destination \(y\). Transposing matrix conventions without transposing the formula is a common implementation error.

    For deterministic invertible flows, reversal negates time direction along the flow. Jump processes can lose information through many-to-one events, so reverse simulation may require learned birth or reconstruction moves.

    Worked reverse of an absorbing two-state corruption¤

    Start in state \(0\) and jump \(0\to1\) at rate \(\lambda\), with state \(1\) absorbing. Then

    \[ p_t(0)=e^{-\lambda t},\qquad p_t(1)=1-e^{-\lambda t}. \]

    The only nonzero reverse move at original time \(t>0\) is

    \[ q_t^{\mathrm{rev}}(1,0) =\lambda\frac{e^{-\lambda t}}{1-e^{-\lambda t}}. \]

    At \(\lambda=2\) and \(t=(\log2)/2\), both states have probability \(1/2\) and the reverse rate is \(2\). As \(t\downarrow0\), state \(1\) becomes rare and the conditional reverse rate diverges. Truncating away from the endpoint or changing the corruption schedule changes the remaining endpoint error and must be reported.

    For a time-homogeneous finite CTMC in state \(x\), exact next-event simulation draws \(\tau\sim\operatorname{Exp}(\lambda(x))\) and then selects \(y\) with probability \(q(x,y)/\lambda(x)\). This is the Gillespie construction for reaction networks. If rates depend explicitly on time or change continuously along a PDMP flow, replace the constant exponential clock by an integrated hazard or a valid thinning bound.

    Uniformization supplies another exact finite-state oracle. Choose \(\Lambda\geq\max_x[-Q_{xx}]\) and define the stochastic matrix \(R=I+Q/\Lambda\). Over time \(t\), draw \(N\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\Lambda t)\) and apply \(N\) transitions from \(R\), including self-transitions. Then

    \[ e^{tQ}=e^{-\Lambda t}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\Lambda t)^n}{n!}R^n. \]

    Uniformization is exact for the declared homogeneous \(Q\); truncating the Poisson sum or approximating time-varying rates introduces error. For the two-state \(Q\) with \(a=2,b=1\), \(\Lambda=2\) gives \(R=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\1/2&1/2\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\).

    Tau-leaping instead freezes rates over an interval and samples multiple event counts, following Gillespie's approximate acceleration. In a death process starting from one object with frozen mean count one, subtracting \(K\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(1)\) produces a negative population whenever \(K\geq2\), with probability \(1-2/e\approx0.264\). Binomial, adaptive, or hybrid schemes can address this failure, but each changes the finite algorithm and must be named.

    For SDEs, strong error couples numerical and exact trajectories using the same noise, whereas weak error compares expectations of test functions. Generative sample quality is usually a weak/distributional question, but strong path accuracy matters for conditional trajectories and coupled estimators. Adaptive ODE/SDE solvers also make compute random or sample dependent; report accepted/rejected steps and neural-function evaluations rather than only a nominal step count.

    Error decomposition¤

    Endpoint error can arise from:

    1. path/reference mismatch;
    2. finite data and conditional-target variance;
    3. function approximation and optimization;
    4. invalid coefficient constraints;
    5. time truncation near singular endpoints;
    6. simulator discretization or event approximation;
    7. Monte Carlo variance and insufficient mixing;
    8. guidance or conditioning applied after training.

    Reporting only sample quality collapses these causes. Low-dimensional systems with known transition laws can isolate them.

    When all terms use the same integral probability metric over a declared bounded test class \(\mathcal F\), a triangle inequality can organize the budget:

    \[ d_{\mathcal F}(\widehat p_h,p_{\mathrm{data}}) \leq d_{\mathcal F}(\widehat p_h,p_{\widehat L}) +d_{\mathcal F}(p_{\widehat L},p_L) +d_{\mathcal F}(p_L,p_{\mathrm{ref}}) +d_{\mathcal F}(p_{\mathrm{ref}},p_{\mathrm{data}}). \]

    The terms can represent Monte Carlo/numerical, learned-generator, reference- path/truncation, and model-target discrepancies. Adding FID, total variation, and a moment error into one number has no such justification.

    Worked finite error ledger¤

    For one bounded observable, suppose independent controlled studies bound the absolute expectation discrepancies by \(0.02\) for reference-path mismatch, \(0.04\) for learned coefficients relative to the exact reference generator, \(0.01\) for endpoint truncation, and \(0.03\) for the simulator plus Monte Carlo estimate. The triangle bound is \(0.10\). It is an upper bound, not a causal decomposition: errors can cancel, shared evaluation samples correlate estimates, and a term measured under a different observable class cannot be inserted without a comparison theorem.

    Stability and explosion¤

    Large drifts, diffusion coefficients, or jump rates can cause numerical instability or explosion. Clipping changes the generator. Adaptive solvers can hide stiffness through large NFE. Rate caps alter waiting-time laws. Stabilization should therefore be reported as part of the model, not a neutral implementation detail.

    A robust simulator audit varies at least three axes independently:

    1. refine time mesh, event tolerance, thinning bound, or Poisson truncation;
    2. remove or vary coefficient clipping, rate caps, and boundary projection;
    3. increase the state-space truncation or maximum-event budget.

    Convergence under only the first axis can hide bias from the other two. Record invalid proposals, projected states, rejected steps, maximum rates, event-count tails, and termination causes per sample.

    Misconception checks: reversal and finite algorithms¤

    • “Reverse rates are just transposed forward rates.” Marginal density ratios are required except in special stationary reversible cases.
    • “Gillespie is exact for any neural rate.” Exactness assumes the declared rates and clock are evaluated correctly; time-varying hazards require integration or valid thinning.
    • “Tau-leaping is a faster Gillespie implementation.” It approximates multiple events and can violate state constraints.
    • “An adaptive solver removes discretization error.” It controls an estimator under tolerances and error heuristics; it does not make the finite law identical to the continuous process.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. \(e^{-2t}=1/2\), so \(q_t^{\mathrm{rev}}(1,0)=2(1/2)/(1/2)=2\). It is neither above nor below. As \(t\downarrow0\), \(p_t(1)\downarrow0\) while reverse flux must return its rare mass, making the conditional rate diverge. Check. The reverse probability flux remains \(p_t(1)q^{\mathrm{rev}}=2e^{-2t}\).
    2. P1.2. The correction is \(p^{-1}\sum_j\partial_j(a_{ij}p)=\sum_j\partial_ja_{ij}+\sum_ja_{ij}\partial_j\log p\). Thus reverse-clock drift is \(-b+\nabla\cdot a+a\nabla\log p\). With original \(t\) integrated backward, the written coefficient becomes \(b-\nabla\cdot a-a\nabla\log p\) multiplying negative \(dt\). Check. For spatially constant \(a\), the divergence term vanishes.
    3. P1.3. \(R=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&1\\1/2&1/2\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\) has nonnegative entries and unit row sums. The Poisson mixture is \((1-\Lambda t)I+\Lambda tR+O(t^2)=I+t\Lambda(R-I)+O(t^2)=I+tQ+O(t^2)\). Check. Its derivative at zero is exactly the generator.
    4. P1.4. \(P(K\geq2)=1-P(K=0)-P(K=1)=1-2/e\approx0.2642\). Shorten/adapt the leap, use a binomial constraint-aware leap, or switch critical low-count reactions to exact events. Check. Clipping negative counts is another algorithm and generally biases the law.
    5. P1.5. The stated bound is \(0.02+0.04+0.01+0.03=0.10\). A FID difference cannot replace or be added to a bounded-test expectation error without a theorem relating the metrics. Check. A ledger is meaningful only when every term bounds the same final discrepancy.
    6. P1.6. Declare the reverse or generation-time drift, rate, reset kernel, and support; integrate the ODE and cumulative hazard or use a proven thinning upper bound; record tolerances, bound violations, proposals, accepted events, NFE, and wall time; verify reset states and Lyapunov/non-explosion conditions; and refine ODE tolerance, hazard bound, event cap, and state truncation independently. Rubric. Refining the ODE alone is not a full PDMP convergence study.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Each generator class has its own reversal formula and simulator. Continuous-time unification ends before finite numerical equivalence begins. Chapter 6 provides the comparison matrix, evidence audit, and research exercises.

    6. Comparison, Evidence, and Research Directions¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the full operator and simulation framework. By the end, you should be able to:

    2. O2 — Build a validation ladder: test operator validity, infinitesimal action, marginal evolution, transition/path behavior, and finite deployment separately.
    3. O3 — Compare compute fairly: match physical horizon and report NFE, stages, proposals, accepted events, latency, memory, and hardware.

    5. O5 — Design decisive benchmarks: use exact finite chains, solvable diffusions, and controlled hybrids to localize error sources.
    6. O6 — Transfer the review protocol: write a publication-ready claim, evidence, and reproducibility audit for a new general-generator method.

    Evaluation contract¤

    State the process class, state space, base measure, generator domain, boundary conditions, endpoint path, and whether the learned coefficients guarantee validity. Report objective weighting, time sampling, simulator, NFE/event count, tolerances, wall time, memory, hardware, invalid-event handling, and endpoint metrics. For mixed generators, include ablations and splitting order.

    A minimum decisive benchmark suite¤

    1. Two-state CTMC: exact matrix exponential, reverse rates, uniformization, and event frequencies expose sign, transpose, and clock bugs.
    2. Ornstein--Uhlenbeck diffusion: exact Gaussian moments and transitions expose score, reverse-time, and weak-solver error.
    3. State-dependent one-dimensional diffusion: checks covariance-divergence terms hidden by constant-noise formulas.
    4. Pure birth/death chain: exposes non-explosion assumptions, boundary at zero, tau-leap negativity, and state truncation.
    5. Noncommuting finite generators: distinguishes exact sum dynamics from Lie/Strang splitting and path-law mixtures.
    6. Coupled discrete/continuous toy system: tests resets, shared events, equivariance/invariants, and multimodal dependence before large models hide failures.

    For every benchmark, separate an exact-coefficient simulator test from a learned-coefficient test. Otherwise an accurate network can be blamed for a bad solver, or an exact simulator can conceal that the learning target was wrong.

    Worked end-to-end two-state validation¤

    Take the true rates \((a,b)=(2,1)\) and horizon \(t=\log2/3\). Chapter 1 gives

    \[ P_t=\begin{pmatrix}2/3&1/3\\1/6&5/6\end{pmatrix}. \]

    Suppose a learned generator has \((\widehat a,\widehat b)=(2.1,0.9)\). Its total rate is still three, so at the same horizon

    \[ \widehat P_t =\begin{pmatrix}0.65&0.35\\0.15&0.85\end{pmatrix}. \]

    The maximum row-wise total-variation error is \(1/60\approx0.0167\). This is the coefficient/model error under exact exponentiation. Next simulate the true \(Q\) and \(\widehat Q\) separately with exact events or uniformization; confidence intervals around their empirical transition frequencies quantify Monte Carlo error. Finally run the deployed approximate sampler and compare it with \(\widehat P_t\); that difference isolates numerical error. Comparing only the deployed samples with \(P_t\) would merge all three.

    Misconception checks: evidence and evaluation¤

    • “A broad theorem validates a particular network.” The coefficients must satisfy the theorem's hypotheses and the implementation must realize them.
    • “A better endpoint metric proves a better generator.” Different paths and even invalid intermediate dynamics can share endpoint samples.
    • “One hundred steps is compute matched.” A step can contain different neural evaluations, event proposals, linear solves, or rejected stages.
    • “Recent means stronger evidence.” Venue, assumptions, reproducibility, and relevance determine support; date alone does not.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Derive \(\mathcal L\) and \(\mathcal L^*\) for a one-dimensional drift–diffusion.
    2. Recover the two-state CTMC transition matrix \(e^{tQ}\) and compare it with tau-leaping.
    3. Show that coordinate test functions identify drift but not diffusion covariance.
    4. Superpose a birth process and deterministic decay, then simulate by a joint event/ODE scheme.
    5. Derive the reversed rates of a three-state CTMC from known \(p_t\).
    6. Compare \(e^{h(L_1+L_2)}\) with Lie and Strang splitting and measure noncommutation error.
    Exercise 3 solution

    For \(\varphi(x)=x_i\), the Hessian vanishes, so \(\mathcal L\varphi=b_i\). Quadratic tests \(\varphi(x)=x_ix_j\) introduce Hessian terms and expose the covariance \(a\).

    Final synthesis¤

    Infinitesimal generators provide the broadest rigorous bridge among flows, diffusions, and jumps. The abstraction succeeds when it preserves each operator's admissibility, reversal, and simulation requirements. It fails when “same generator framework” is shortened to “same model or cost.”
