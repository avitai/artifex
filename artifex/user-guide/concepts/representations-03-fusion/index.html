    3. Conditioning, Fusion, and Cross-Modal Alignment¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional generation and representation learning. By the end, you should be able to distinguish early, late, cross-attentive, product, mixture, and shared-latent fusion; state what contrastive alignment identifies and leaves ambiguous; handle missing modalities under an explicit observation law; and design conditional-fidelity, retrieval, and leakage tests for paired and weakly paired data.

    For modalities \(X,Y\), a system may model \(p(x,y)\), \(p(y\mid x)\), two separate marginals, or only aligned embeddings. These are different probabilistic claims.

    This chapter focuses on representation and fusion. The conditioning and steering volume owns the reusable probability semantics for target tilting, score and discrete guidance, hard constraints, candidate selection, and retrieval composition.

    Fusion patterns¤

    • Early fusion: convert modalities to a common token sequence before a shared backbone.
    • Late fusion: encode separately and combine pooled states or predictions.
    • Cross-attention: one stream queries another while retaining modality-specific representations.
    • Joint latent fusion: encode modalities into a shared stochastic latent.
    • Mixture of experts: share routing/backbone components but retain modality experts.

    Early fusion enables dense interaction but can be dominated by the longest modality. Late fusion is modular but can miss fine alignment. Cross-attention cost scales with both sequence lengths and needs masking that matches the generative factorization.

    Conditional and joint factorizations¤

    A text-to-image system estimates \(p(I\mid T)\); an image-caption model estimates \(p(T\mid I)\). A joint AR sequence can define

    \[ p(T,I)=p(T)p(I\mid T) \]

    under one order, but reversing the order defines different conditionals and training exposure. Bidirectional understanding attention cannot be used unchanged for causal generation without leakage.

    The pair law defines a contrastive objective¤

    "Positive" and "negative" are sampling semantics, not intrinsic properties of two objects. Let \(p_+(x,y)\) be the declared positive-pair law and let its \(Y\)-marginal supply negatives. With one positive candidate and \(N-1\) independent marginal candidates, a common InfoNCE convention is

    \[ \begin{aligned} w_{\eta,j}&=\exp s_\eta(X,Y_j),\\ Z_N&=\sum_{j=1}^{N}w_{\eta,j},\\ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{NCE}} &=-\mathbb E\!\left[ \log \frac{w_{\eta,1}}{Z_N} \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The candidate law is

    \[ \begin{aligned} Y_1&\sim p_+(\,\cdot\mid X), \\ Y_{2:N}&\overset{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}p_+(y). \end{aligned} \]

    Under this construction and the regularity needed by the variational argument,

    \[ I_{p_+}(X;Y) \ge \log N-\mathcal L_{\mathrm{NCE}}. \]

    At the population optimum over a sufficiently expressive score class, the score represents the log density ratio \(\log p_+(y\mid x)-\log p_+(y)\) up to an \(x\)-only offset. This classification interpretation does not make the embeddings a normalized joint generator, a decoder, or a calibrated likelihood model. The bound is at most \(\log N\); the true mutual information is not. Consequently, standard contrastive bounds can severely underestimate large mutual information, and dependent in-batch sampling, multiple positives, hard-negative mining, or a different negative proposal requires a new derivation rather than the same formula by habit. See Poole et al. and Sordoni et al..

    The pair law also chooses an invariance. A marginal "negative" may share the same task-relevant class as the anchor, producing a class collision; a positive pair may differ in a factor that the downstream generator must preserve. Theory linking contrastive loss to downstream classification therefore states a latent class or view model rather than deriving usefulness from the loss name alone (Saunshi et al.).

    Worked two-view example: useful invariance, lost generative detail¤

    Let \(C,N_0,N_1\) be independent uniform bits and construct two views

    \[ X=(C,N_0), \qquad Y=(C,N_1). \]

    The views share exactly the class bit: \(I(X;Y)=H(C)=1\) bit. An encoder returning only \(C\) preserves all common information and is ideal for class retrieval. It is also invariant to the independently redrawn nuisance. But it cannot reconstruct the original \(N_0\), and two marginal candidates with the same \(C\) are false negatives for a class-only task. If \(N_0\) controls pose, timing, or identity required by conditional generation, this positive-pair construction has deliberately removed task-critical information.

    Reconstruction can make the opposite trade: it may preserve every local detail without making class semantics easily accessible. A multimodal system must therefore state whether its alignment loss is intended for retrieval, conditioning, token grounding, reconstruction, generation, or some declared combination—and evaluate each interface separately.

    Synchronization¤

    Audio, video, text, actions, and sensors have different clocks. Alignment may be timestamp-, event-, phoneme-, frame-, or learned-attention based. Resampling can introduce aliasing and apparent lead/lag. A multimodal generator should state whether it models simultaneous samples, causal history, or offline bidirectional context.

    Missing and asynchronous modalities¤

    Missingness may be random, informative, scheduled by an acquisition policy, or caused by sensor and user actions. It creates a query over a subset, not just a shorter tensor. For a modality-presence mask \(R\in\{0,1\}^M\), distinguish:

    Table REP.6. Missing and asynchronous modalities
    Mask Unit Meaning Typical failure if conflated
    Modality mask complete stream whether image, audio, text, or another modality exists a zero-valued sensor is treated as absent
    Padding mask positions within a stream which token/time positions are structural padding padding tokens leak into pooled features or likelihoods
    Corruption mask observed values or spans which available entries were hidden by a training objective artificial masking is mistaken for real acquisition missingness
    Causal mask ordered token pairs which information is permitted by the generative factorization future targets leak into likelihood training
    Loss mask predictions which outputs have ground truth and should be scored generated but unavailable targets contribute fabricated losses

    A mask token, absent stream, imputed value, empty sequence, and genuine zero observation must therefore have different representations. If a modality encoder is skipped, the fusion layer still needs its identity and absence state; otherwise two different observed subsets can map to the same fused input.

    Mask support is a training distribution¤

    Let \(\rho_{\mathrm{train}}(S)\) be the probability that training presents the observed-modality subset \(S\). The learned risk is an average over this mask distribution. Good performance under one sampled deletion rate does not imply good performance for every subset, for a different deletion frequency, or for informative deployment failure.

    A defensible protocol therefore:

    1. lists the deployment queries \(p(x_T\mid x_S)\);
    2. gives required subsets nonzero training probability or supplies a declared test-time inference algorithm;
    3. keeps fully observed and important single-modality anchors when those queries matter;
    4. records which target modalities are actually available for each loss; and
    5. reports results separately for each mask rather than only averaging over a convenient random-mask mixture.

    The VAE chapter derives the corresponding subset ELBO, Gaussian product update, and missingness assumptions. Its central warning applies to other architectures too: an input format that accepts missing streams is not evidence of calibrated inference on those streams.

    Partial pairing does not identify a joint by itself¤

    Suppose one dataset contains only images and another only text. The two marginals \(p(I)\) and \(p(T)\) do not identify their coupling \(p(I,T)\): many joint laws share the same marginals. Paired examples, temporal co-occurrence, identifiers, known mechanisms, optimal-transport assumptions, or other structural information must determine the association. A shared embedding or matching marginal distributions is insufficient evidence that generated pairs are semantically correct.

    Copula Models formalizes this gap as a separate dependence distribution with fixed marginals. That construction can make the missing coupling explicit, but it still needs paired or structural evidence; Sklar's theorem does not select the semantically correct cross-modal copula.

    Missingness and acquisition can be informative¤

    If a sensor is activated only after an alarming earlier measurement, its presence conveys information. If users withhold a modality based on its unseen content, absence depends on a missing value. Random modality dropout does not reproduce either mechanism. Under informative missingness, condition on or model the acquisition process and perform sensitivity analyses; otherwise the learned conditional may answer the randomized-dropout problem instead of the deployment problem.

    For asynchronous streams, presence is time-indexed. A delayed packet is not the same as a permanently missing modality, and future arrival can be unavailable to an online generator even if it appears in an offline record. Report the decision time, permitted history, latency tolerance, and whether inference is filtering or smoothing.

    Evaluation matrix for incomplete inputs¤

    For every required observed subset \(S\), evaluate:

    • reconstruction of modalities in \(S\) separately from generation of targets outside \(S\);
    • conditional quality, diversity, calibration, and coverage for every target direction;
    • consistency when an additional, agreeing modality is added;
    • behavior under contradictory modalities rather than only clean pairs;
    • mask-frequency and missingness-mechanism shift; and
    • performance of each modality so an averaged score cannot hide modality collapse.

    Pointwise imputation error rewards conditional means and can punish valid diversity. Pair it with a proper conditional score or distributional diagnostic that matches the output type.

    Worked causal mask¤

    Suppose text tokens precede image tokens in one AR sequence. Image tokens may attend to all text and previous image tokens; text must not attend to future image tokens during likelihood training. Allowing that attention yields a lower training loss but invalidates the claimed factorization at generation time.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Fusion is meaningful only relative to a joint or conditional query, modality and causal masks, pairing mechanism, observation process, and clock. A contrastive objective inherits its meaning from the positive and negative sampling laws; an InfoNCE bound is neither a decoder nor a normalized generative model. Missing-stream robustness is a property of the training distribution and inference algorithm, not of a mask token alone. Chapter 4 studies shared backbones and AR–diffusion hybrids that combine several objectives and representations.

    Retrieval practice: alignment and pairing
    1. What sampling laws must accompany an InfoNCE loss?
    2. Why does the lower bound \(\log N-\mathcal L_{\mathrm{NCE}}\) become insensitive to sufficiently large mutual information at fixed \(N\)?
    3. In the two-bit view example, which information is shared and which information is deliberately unavailable across views?
    4. Why can unpaired modality marginals fail to identify a joint distribution even when both marginals are fitted perfectly?

    Solutions. (1) The positive joint or conditional law, the negative proposal, the number and dependence of candidates, and any multiple-positive or mining rule. (2) The loss is nonnegative, so this particular lower bound cannot exceed \(\log N\), although the true mutual information can. (3) The class bit \(C\) is shared; the independently redrawn nuisances \(N_0\) and \(N_1\) are not. (4) Many couplings have the same two marginals, so paired observations or a justified structural assumption must select one.
