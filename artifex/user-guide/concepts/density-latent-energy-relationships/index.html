    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Probability Paths 1 — Couplings and Conservation
    • Probability Paths and Stochastic Transport

    Generative-model families often describe the same distribution through different interfaces: normalized conditionals, an invertible transformation, a latent marginal, an unnormalized energy, or a score field. These representations are mathematically connected, but the connections do not make their likelihoods, inference algorithms, or finite-compute behavior interchangeable.

    This volume builds a conversion map with the curriculum's six relationship labels: algebraic equivalence, distributional equivalence under assumptions, population-optimum correspondence, shared estimator, implementation analogy, and hybrid composition. Every arrow states what is preserved and what becomes intractable.

    The motivating question is deliberately concrete: if two systems represent the same probability law, why can one answer a likelihood query immediately while another needs integration, optimization, or a long Markov chain? The answer is that a probability law and an interface to that law are different objects. This book keeps them separate and then reconnects them with proofs, finite examples, visual maps, and query-specific failure tests.

    When the comparison concerns derivatives or update geometry rather than the represented density interface, use Gradients and Geometry in Generative Modeling. It distinguishes parameter, state, game, implicit, functional, natural, and search gradients and separates Fisher geometry from transport-based pullbacks.

    How to read this book

    Read continuously to follow the conversion argument.
    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2 after the core-family books.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–4 and the composition rules in Chapter 6.
    Research: all chapters, especially integrability, posterior identities, and finite-compute boundaries.

    Book-length routes into the subject¤

    No single book treats density factorization, latent-variable inference, energies, scores, and transport at equal depth. The following complementary routes were selected from publisher or author-maintained sources, not from secondary recommendation lists:

    • Modern unification route: Murphy's Probabilistic Machine Learning: Advanced Topics connects deep generative models, latent variables, energy-based learning, Monte Carlo, variational inference, and flows in one modern notation. It is the closest broad companion, but this volume makes representation-versus-query costs more explicit.
    • Graphical-model and free-energy route: Wainwright and Jordan's Graphical Models, Exponential Families, and Variational Inference develops log-partition functions, marginal polytopes, conjugate duality, and variational free energies. Use it when the phrase “marginalization hides a normalizer” needs a convex-analytic foundation.
    • Graduate latent-variable route: Bishop's Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning builds mixture, latent-variable, graphical-model, and approximate-inference ideas with extensive diagrams and exercises. Barber's Bayesian Reasoning and Machine Learning offers a more hands-on bridge with worked algorithms and exercises.
    • Energy route: LeCun and collaborators' A Tutorial on Energy-Based Learning explains why an energy is a compatibility interface rather than automatically a normalized likelihood. Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville's structured probabilistic-model chapter connects this view to graphical models and free energy.
    • Coding and inference route: MacKay's Information Theory, Inference, and Learning Algorithms motivates normalization through messages, codes, exact small examples, and approximate inference. It is especially useful before Chapter 2's claim that normalized probabilities are an operational resource, not cosmetic notation.

    Each source uses its own conventions. Translate it into this volume's invariant before transferring a result: state space, base measure, support, normalized law, interface, query, relationship label, exact assumptions, and finite algorithm.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to convert among normalized density, conditional factorization, latent marginal, energy, score, and transport interfaces while stating support and base measure; derive which conversions preserve a law and which require intractable sums, integrals, or partition functions; test score-field integrability; and classify every AR–EBM–VAE–diffusion–flow connection with one of the six relationship labels.

    Do not collapse representation into algorithm

    “There exists an energy, score, factorization, or transport for this law” is an existence statement. It does not imply that the object can be evaluated, learned, normalized, marginalized, or sampled efficiently.

    1. One Distribution, Several Mathematical Interfaces¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes elementary probability; gradients are needed only for the continuous-score paragraphs. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate law from interface: distinguish normalized density, conditional, latent joint, energy, score, and sampler interfaces.
    2. O2 — State measure and support: identify the base measure, support, and zero-density states before comparing numerical densities or energies.
    3. O3 — Convert a finite law: move exactly among probability masses, energies, energy differences, and neighboring probability ratios.
    4. O4 — Diagnose gauge information: determine what additive energy shifts, scores, and ratios preserve or discard.
    5. O5 — Match queries to interfaces: decide whether evaluation, normalization, marginalization, coding, or sampling is directly available.
    6. O6 — Transfer the contract: audit a new continuous, discrete, or lower-dimensional generator without silently assuming a density exists.

    Let \(p(x)\) be a probability law on support \(\mathcal X\). Depending on the model, we may access it through:

    • a normalized density or mass \(p(x)\);
    • normalized conditionals \(p(x_i\mid x_{<i})\);
    • a latent joint \(p(x,z)\) whose marginal is \(p(x)=\int p(x,z)dz\);
    • an energy \(E(x)\) with \(p(x)=e^{-E(x)}/Z\);
    • a score \(s(x)=\nabla_x\log p(x)\) for continuous \(x\);
    • samples or a transport map without a convenient pointwise density.

    These are interfaces, not mutually exclusive ontologies. Whenever \(p(x)>0\), define

    \[ E_p(x)=-\log p(x). \]

    This is an exact normalized energy with \(Z=1\). Conversely, an arbitrary energy defines a probability density only when

    \[ 0<Z=\int_{\mathcal X}e^{-E(x)}d\mu(x)<\infty. \]

    Computing \(Z\), marginals, or samples may still be intractable.

    When the requested query is the missing marginal or partition function, use Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants for exact baselines, importance estimators, AIS/SMC, bridge and path methods, diagnostics, and reporting.

    Gauge freedom¤

    For any constant \(c\),

    \[ \tilde E(x)=E(x)+c \]

    defines the same normalized distribution because \(\tilde Z=e^{-c}Z\). Energies and log densities are identifiable only up to an additive constant when training observes ratios or scores.

    Gauge freedom does not include scaling. Replacing \(E\) by \(\beta E\) changes relative probabilities unless \(\beta=1\) or the energy is constant. Nor can a finite gauge shift repair an impossible state: if \(p(x)=0\), the normalized energy \(-\log p(x)\) is \(+\infty\). Treating this value as merely “a large logit” changes the support.

    Base measure and support¤

    A density is defined relative to a base measure. Discrete token masses, Euclidean densities, and manifold densities cannot be compared by ignoring that measure. A deterministic lower-dimensional generator embedded in \(\mathbb R^d\) may have no Lebesgue density at all. Adding observation noise, using a manifold measure, or working implicitly changes the probabilistic object.

    The score inherits this dependence. If \(p_\mu=dP/d\mu\), then the notation \(s_\mu=\nabla\log p_\mu\) is safest: changing coordinates or reference measure changes the density and contributes a Jacobian or Radon–Nikodym term. A score is therefore not a coordinate-free arrow unless a geometry and reference volume are supplied.

    Worked finite-state conversion¤

    Let \(\mathcal X=\{a,b,c\}\) and energies be \(E(a)=0\), \(E(b)=\log2\), \(E(c)=\log4\). Then

    \[ e^{-E}=(1,1/2,1/4),\qquad Z=7/4, \]

    and

    \[ p=(4/7,2/7,1/7). \]

    Adding 10 to every energy changes neither probability nor ratios. Multiplying all energies by 2 does change the distribution; it is an inverse-temperature transformation, not gauge freedom.

    There is no ordinary derivative on this three-state set. The useful discrete analog is a declared ratio along an allowed edge. Along \(a\leftrightarrow b\) and \(b\leftrightarrow c\),

    \[ \frac{p(b)}{p(a)}=e^{-[E(b)-E(a)]}=\frac12, \qquad \frac{p(c)}{p(b)}=\frac12. \]

    Multiplying ratios along the path gives \(p(c)/p(a)=1/4\). On a graph with cycles, valid exact ratios must multiply to one around every closed cycle. This cycle condition is the discrete counterpart of zero circulation for a smooth score field.

    Queries determine practical tractability¤

    Two representations of the same ideal \(p\) may offer different queries:

    Table DLE.1. Queries determine practical tractability
    Interface Usually direct Usually difficult
    AR conditionals exact joint log probability, ancestral sampling parallel sampling, arbitrary marginalization
    Tractable flow exact density and inversion topology/support mismatch, constrained marginalization
    VAE joint prior sampling, joint density, amortized posterior exact data marginal and posterior
    EBM relative energy, products/constraints partition function and mixing
    Score model local log-density gradient, iterative sampling normalization and exact likelihood
    Implicit generator sampling pointwise density and posterior inference

    An identity between represented distributions does not transfer a tractable algorithm from one interface to another.

    A four-question interface audit¤

    Before accepting a claimed conversion, answer in this order:

    1. Object: is the claim about a probability measure, a density relative to \(\mu\), a joint law, a conditional law, an energy, a score, or samples?
    2. Domain: what is the support, and is it discrete, Euclidean, manifold- valued, mixed, or lower-dimensional?
    3. Information: which constants, component masses, or latent variables are discarded by the conversion?
    4. Query: what sum, integral, derivative, inverse, or Markov-chain limit is required to answer the user's actual question?

    This sequence is intentionally query-first. It prevents a mathematically true identity from being presented as a computationally free algorithm.

    Independent practice and assessment map: law and interface objects¤

    Attempt each task before opening the hints. Keep the law, its representation, and the requested query in separate lines of your solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: finite conversion. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For masses proportional to \((4,2,1)\) on \((a,b,c)\), predict which state has the smallest energy. Normalize the law, choose gauge \(E(a)=0\), and compute both adjacent probability ratios.
    2. P1.2 — Diagnose a zero-mass state. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 8 min. Replace the third weight by zero. State \(E(c)\), \(p(c)/p(b)\), whether a finite gauge shift can restore positive mass, and why an ordinary continuous score at \(c\) is not defined by this table.
    3. P1.3 — Compare densities after a coordinate change. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Let \(X\) be uniform on \([0,1]\) and \(Y=2X\). Derive the density of \(Y\), compare the interior scores, and explain why unequal density values do not mean unequal probability laws under the bijection.
    4. P1.4 — Match query to interface. Type: compare · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. For an exact AR model and an exact score model of the same continuous law, classify direct joint-density evaluation, ancestral generation, a local log-density derivative, and absolute lossless code length as direct or additional-computation queries for each interface.
    5. P1.5 — Audit a singular generator. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. A generator maps scalar \(Z\) to \((Z,Z^2)\) in \(\mathbb R^2\). Decide whether its output has a two-dimensional Lebesgue density, name a valid alternative reference object, and state what adding isotropic Gaussian observation noise changes.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a discrete graph. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. Design an interface contract for a molecular-state generator whose states are graph nodes and whose model returns directed neighbor ratios. Specify support, reference measure, reciprocal and cycle checks, how component masses could be recovered, and one query the ratio interface does not answer by itself.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Normalized densities, energies, and scores can describe the same law, but normalization, support, and query access determine what can actually be computed. Chapter 2 studies representations where normalization is built into the factorization or transformation.

    Does every neural energy define a probability distribution?

    No. The exponential must be integrable with a finite, nonzero partition function on the stated support and base measure.

    2. Normalized Constructions: Autoregression, Flows, and Circuits¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 plus elementary change of variables and conditional factorization. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Prove local-to-global normalization: normalize autoregressive products by iterated summation or integration.
    2. O2 — Expose the reverse-conversion cost: derive energy-to-AR suffix partition recursions with their boundary condition.
    3. O3 — Transform densities and scores: apply an invertible flow's Jacobian rule without losing its reference measure.
    4. O4 — Audit circuit tractability: state the leaf, weight, smoothness, and decomposability conditions behind exact circuit queries.
    5. O5 — Connect probability to coding: determine when normalized conditionals support an operational lossless code.
    6. O6 — Transfer normalization proofs: locate where normalization lives in a new structured or hybrid generator.

    Autoregressive normalization¤

    The chain rule writes

    \[ p(x)=\prod_{i=1}^{d}p(x_i\mid x_{<i}). \]

    If every conditional is normalized, summing or integrating in reverse order proves the joint is normalized. Its energy is

    \[ E_{\mathrm{AR}}(x) =-\sum_{i=1}^{d}\log p(x_i\mid x_{<i}), \]

    with known partition \(Z=1\). Thus an AR model is an EBM by algebraic equivalence, but a specially normalized one.

    The reverse conversion is not free. For a discrete energy over complete sequences, define suffix partition functions

    \[ Z_i(x_{<i})= \sum_{x_i,\ldots,x_d}e^{-E(x_1,\ldots,x_d)}. \]

    Close the recursion with

    \[ Z_{d+1}(x_{\le d})=e^{-E(x_{1:d})}. \]

    Then

    \[ p(x_i\mid x_{<i})= \frac{Z_{i+1}(x_{\le i})}{Z_i(x_{<i})}. \]

    These prefix-dependent sums can be exponentially expensive. Saying “every EBM is autoregressive by the chain rule” is distributionally true after normalization, but hides the hard inference problem.

    Worked binary energy¤

    For \(x_1,x_2\in\{0,1\}\), let

    \[ E(x_1,x_2)=-Jx_1x_2-h_1x_1-h_2x_2. \]

    The first AR conditional requires

    \[ p(x_1)= \frac{\sum_{x_2}e^{-E(x_1,x_2)}} {\sum_{x'_1,x_2}e^{-E(x'_1,x_2)}}. \]

    For two bits this is trivial; for a dense \(d\)-bit energy, each early conditional sums over exponentially many suffixes unless structure enables dynamic programming or approximation.

    For example, choose \(J=\log2\) and \(h_1=h_2=0\). The unnormalized weights of \((00,01,10,11)\) are \((1,1,1,2)\), so \(Z=5\),

    \[ p(x_1=1)=\frac35, \quad p(x_2=1\mid x_1=0)=\frac12, \quad p(x_2=1\mid x_1=1)=\frac23. \]

    Multiplying the first marginal by the appropriate second conditional recovers all four joint probabilities. The example is small enough to enumerate; the suffix sum is the hidden operation that scales poorly.

    Normalizing flows¤

    Let \(z\sim q(z)\) and \(x=f_\theta(z)\) be a bijection. Then

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\log q(f_\theta^{-1}(x)) +\log\left|\det J_{f_\theta^{-1}}(x)\right|. \]

    The induced normalized energy is

    \[ E_\theta(x)= -\log q(f_\theta^{-1}(x)) -\log\left|\det J_{f_\theta^{-1}}(x)\right|. \]

    Normalization follows by change of variables. An arbitrary EBM can be represented by a sufficiently expressive transport under regularity assumptions, but learning that transport from energy evaluations or samples is a separate computational problem.

    Differentiating the same identity also gives the transformed score. With \(g=f^{-1}\),

    \[ s_X(x)=J_g(x)^\top s_Z(g(x)) +\nabla_x\log|\det J_g(x)|. \]

    The second term is essential: a flow changes local volume as well as moving points. The JMLR review of normalizing flows develops the density and architectural consequences in depth.

    Probabilistic circuits¤

    Normalized sum/product circuits preserve normalization recursively when their leaf distributions are normalized, sum-node weights are nonnegative and sum to one, sum children are smooth (share scope), and product children are decomposable (have disjoint scopes). Smoothness and decomposability support linear-time exact marginal evaluation for the corresponding circuit class; additional determinism conditions enable further queries such as efficient MAP in important subclasses. Taking negative log turns a circuit into an energy, but the tractable circuit structure is obscured by a monolithic energy network.

    In log space, product nodes add log values while sum nodes compute log-sum-exp. This resembles energy composition, but circuit tractability depends on scopes and structural properties—not merely on using those operations.

    Coding and normalization¤

    An exact normalized discrete model supports arithmetic coding with expected code length near \(-\log_2p(x)\). An unnormalized EBM cannot supply those probabilities without \(Z\) and the relevant conditionals. Score equality up to a constant is therefore insufficient for lossless coding.

    This gives normalization an operational test. If a system claims exact code lengths but cannot provide compatible normalized next-symbol probabilities, either another normalization mechanism is present or the claim is incomplete.

    Joint normalization versus marginal tractability¤

    A latent-variable model defines

    \[ p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z), \qquad p_\theta(x)=\int p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)dz. \]

    The joint can be normalized and easy to sample while the marginal likelihood and posterior

    \[ p_\theta(z\mid x)=\frac{p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)}{p_\theta(x)} \]

    remain intractable. The observed-space energy is

    \[ E_X(x)=-\log\int e^{-E_{XZ}(x,z)}dz, \]

    a negative log-sum-exp or free energy over latent states.

    The identity assumes a common dominating measure and a finite, positive integral at the observed \(x\). If latent support depends on \(x\), differentiating the marginal can introduce boundary terms; the posterior identities below are not licensed merely by writing an integral sign.

    Variational inference changes the objective, not the model identity¤

    For any \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\),

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\mathcal L(x;\theta,\phi) +D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)), \]

    where

    \[ \mathcal L =\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)]. \]

    A VAE is not “an approximate distribution” merely because it uses an ELBO: its generative joint is exact as specified, while likelihood learning and posterior inference use approximations.

    The distinction is operational. Sampling \(z\sim p(z)\) and then \(x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z)\) is exact sampling from the specified joint even when \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is poor. Conversely, a tight ELBO on observed data does not make every posterior functional or out-of-distribution sample accurate.

    Fisher identity for the observed score¤

    Under differentiation/integration regularity,

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_\theta(x) =\mathbb E_{p_\theta(z\mid x)} [\nabla_x\log p_\theta(x,z)]. \]

    The marginal score is a posterior average of joint scores. Replacing the true posterior with an amortized approximation yields a biased score unless the approximation is exact.

    Differentiating once more gives the observed-information or missing-information identity

    \[ \nabla_x^2\log p_\theta(x) =\mathbb E[\nabla_x^2\log p_\theta(x,z)\mid x] +\operatorname{Cov}(\nabla_x\log p_\theta(x,z)\mid x). \]

    The positive-semidefinite covariance term shows why the curvature of a marginal is not merely the posterior mean of complete-data curvature. This is the \(x\)-derivative analog of the missing-information decomposition associated with Louis's observed-information identity.

    Worked Gaussian latent model¤

    Let \(z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(x\mid z\sim\mathcal N(z,\sigma^2)\). Then

    \[ x\sim\mathcal N(0,1+\sigma^2), \qquad s(x)=-\frac{x}{1+\sigma^2}. \]

    The posterior is Gaussian with

    \[ z\mid x\sim\mathcal N\left( \frac{x}{1+\sigma^2},\frac{\sigma^2}{1+\sigma^2} \right). \]

    Averaging the joint derivative

    \[ \nabla_x\log p(x,z)=-\frac{x-z}{\sigma^2} \]

    under that posterior gives exactly \(-x/(1+\sigma^2)\).

    The Hessian identity also checks. The complete-data curvature is \(-1/\sigma^2\), while the posterior variance of the complete-data score is \(1/[\sigma^2(1+\sigma^2)]\); their sum is \(-1/(1+\sigma^2)\), the marginal Gaussian curvature.

    Gaussian corruption, Tweedie's identity, and denoising¤

    Let \(Y=X+\tau\epsilon\) with \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\) and noisy marginal \(p_\tau(y)\). Applying Fisher's identity to the latent clean variable gives

    \[ \nabla_y\log p_\tau(y) =\frac{\mathbb E[X\mid Y=y]-y}{\tau^2}, \qquad \mathbb E[X\mid Y=y] =y+\tau^2\nabla_y\log p_\tau(y). \]

    This is Tweedie's identity in the Gaussian location model. It explains why a denoiser and a noisy-marginal score encode one another at population optimum. It does not say that the sample-level conditional target \(\nabla_y\log p(y\mid x)=(x-y)/\tau^2\) equals the marginal score for each training pair. Efron's treatment of Tweedie's formula provides the empirical-Bayes perspective, while Vincent's denoising score-matching analysis establishes the score-matching connection.

    Diffusion as a hierarchical latent model¤

    A finite-step diffusion generative model defines

    \[ p_\theta(x_{0:T}) =p(x_T)\prod_{t=1}^{T}p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t). \]

    The intermediate states \(x_{1:T}\) are latent for the observed datum \(x_0\). Its marginal is

    \[ p_\theta(x_0)=\int p_\theta(x_{0:T})dx_{1:T}, \]

    and the forward noising chain commonly supplies the variational distribution used for the ELBO. This is an algebraic latent-variable formulation of a particular discrete diffusion model. It does not make every VAE training algorithm or latent representation equivalent to diffusion.

    The original denoising diffusion probabilistic-model construction makes this variational path explicit. Later score/SDE formulations reorganize the same broad ingredients around time-indexed marginals and reverse dynamics; the relationship label must be assigned to the exact formulation being compared.

    In continuous time, the score \(\nabla_x\log p_t(x)\) belongs to the noisy marginal at time \(t\). It is not generally the conditional corruption score for one clean \(x_0\), although denoising-score objectives have matching population optima under the stated expectation identity.

    Learned codecs and nested latent systems¤

    Latent diffusion first maps data to a learned representation \(z_0\) and then runs diffusion in that space. There are two latent layers: codec uncertainty/distortion and diffusion path variables. Likelihood, support, and counterfactual claims must account for the decoder and representation, not only the latent sampler.

    4. Energy–Score Duality and Integrability¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes multivariable gradients and elementary line integrals; score-matching objectives are introduced rather than assumed. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive energy–score duality: obtain \(s=-\nabla E\) on the interior of positive support under a fixed coordinate system and base measure.
    2. O2 — Test score validity: separate local cross-partial, global topology, boundary, disconnected-support, and normalizability conditions.
    3. O3 — Transform score objects: account for coordinate Jacobians, reference measures, manifolds, and discrete neighbor ratios.
    4. O4 — Compare score estimators: distinguish explicit, denoising, sliced, ratio-based, and noise-contrastive objectives and their population targets.
    5. O5 — Audit finite samplers: separate invariant-law results from discretization bias, burn-in, autocorrelation, and multimodal mixing.
    6. O6 — Transfer conditional composition: decide when guidance or a sum of learned fields corresponds to a normalizable target.

    For a differentiable positive density

    \[ p(x)=\frac{e^{-E(x)}}{Z}, \]

    the score is

    \[ s(x)=\nabla_x\log p(x)=-\nabla_xE(x), \]

    because \(Z\) is constant with respect to \(x\). This is algebraic equivalence. The score discards the additive energy constant and the partition function.

    The equation is an interior statement. At a hard support boundary, where the density vanishes, \(\log p\) may diverge and a classical score may not exist. Distributional derivatives, reflecting diffusions, or manifold gradients can be appropriate, but each defines a different mathematical contract.

    Scores determine density only under conditions¤

    On a connected domain, an exact score determines log density up to a constant. But an arbitrary learned vector field \(v(x)\) is a score only if it is integrable. On a simply connected domain with sufficient smoothness, a necessary and sufficient local condition is symmetry of cross-partials:

    \[ \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_j} =\frac{\partial v_j}{\partial x_i}. \]

    Topology and boundary behavior also matter. Neural score estimators need not be exactly conservative at finite capacity, even if their population target is a true score.

    Three further checks prevent false reconstruction claims:

    • Disconnected support: integration recovers one additive log-density constant per connected component. The field alone cannot determine relative component masses.
    • Nontrivial topology: zero local curl does not guarantee zero circulation around holes; all closed-loop periods must be compatible.
    • Normalizability: even an exact gradient such as \(v(x)=2x\) integrates to \(\log\tilde p(x)=x^2+C\), whose exponential is not integrable on \(\mathbb R\). Conservativity is necessary but not sufficient for a probability density on an unbounded domain.

    Worked non-integrable field¤

    Consider \(v(x,y)=(-y,x)\). Its cross-partials are

    \[ \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial y}=-1, \qquad \frac{\partial v_2}{\partial x}=1. \]

    They disagree, so no scalar log density on \(\mathbb R^2\) has this score. Integrating around the unit circle gives nonzero circulation. A sampler driven by this field may rotate probability mass rather than ascend any log density.

    Coordinates, reference measures, manifolds, and discrete states¤

    For a differentiable coordinate change \(y=f(x)\) with inverse \(g\), the score of the transformed density relative to Euclidean volume is

    \[ s_Y(y)=J_g(y)^\top s_X(g(y)) +\nabla_y\log|\det J_g(y)|. \]

    Thus score vectors do not transform by the first term alone. Changing the base measure similarly adds the derivative of the log Radon–Nikodym factor. On a Riemannian manifold, the intrinsic score is a Riemannian gradient relative to a declared volume measure; embedding-space gradients can contain irrelevant normal components. Riemannian score-based generative modeling is a primary example of making this geometry explicit.

    For discrete \(x\), \(\nabla_x\log p(x)\) is not canonically defined. A graph of allowed moves can instead carry ratios

    \[ r(x,y)=\frac{p(y)}{p(x)}=e^{-[E(y)-E(x)]}. \]

    Reciprocity and cycle consistency replace continuous zero-curl checks. Recent discrete diffusion modeling by data-ratio estimation develops a ratio-based objective for discrete state spaces; it should not be described as an ordinary Euclidean score without this translation.

    Score matching¤

    Explicit score matching minimizes

    \[ \mathbb E_{p_{\mathrm{data}}} \left[\frac12\|s_\theta(x)\|^2+ \nabla\cdot s_\theta(x)\right] \]

    under boundary assumptions, avoiding the model partition function. Denoising score matching perturbs data and regresses a noisy conditional score; after expectation, its population minimizer is the noisy marginal score.

    This is a population-optimum correspondence. Finite noise schedules, weighting, parameterization, architecture, and optimization alter estimator behavior.

    Langevin sampling¤

    Using the score in unadjusted Langevin dynamics,

    \[ x_{k+1}=x_k+\frac{\eta}{2}s(x_k)+\sqrt{\eta}\,\epsilon_k, \qquad \epsilon_k\sim\mathcal N(0,I), \]

    targets \(p\) only in an ideal limiting sense under regularity and ergodicity; finite step size causes bias unless corrected, and multimodal mixing can be slow. Knowing a score locally is not the same as having an efficient global sampler.

    Even the one-dimensional standard Gaussian exposes the finite-step boundary. With \(s(x)=-x\), unadjusted Langevin becomes

    \[ x_{k+1}=(1-\eta/2)x_k+\sqrt\eta\,\epsilon_k. \]

    For \(0<\eta<4\), its stationary variance is \(1/(1-\eta/4)\) rather than one. The bias vanishes only as \(\eta\to0\); a Metropolis correction can restore the target invariant law but changes cost and rejection behavior.

    Conditional scores and exponential tilting¤

    Bayes' rule gives

    \[ \nabla_x\log p(x\mid y) =\nabla_x\log p(x)+\nabla_x\log p(y\mid x), \]

    because the evidence is constant in \(x\). Thus classifier guidance adds a likelihood score. More generally, tilting a base density by reward \(r(x)\),

    \[ p_\beta(x)\propto p_0(x)e^{\beta r(x)}, \]

    adds \(\beta\nabla r(x)\) to the score where differentiable. Approximate guidance fields, guidance scaling, and time-dependent noisy classifiers introduce modeling error; the identity alone does not validate arbitrary guidance.

    The identity also inherits support. A tilt of \(p_0\) cannot create probability where \(p_0=0\), and a conditional score is meaningful only where \(p(x,y)>0\) and the required derivatives exist. Adding two approximate fields can violate conservativity even when each was trained toward a valid population score.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Energy gradients are scores, but scores erase normalization and require integrability to correspond to a density. Score matching and denoising recover true scores at population optima under assumptions; finite samplers add another approximation layer. Chapter 5 uses these facts to classify exact and inexact cross-family statements.

    Can the score recover log likelihood?

    It can recover log-density differences by path integration when the field is exact and the domain is connected. Absolute normalization still requires a constant, and non-conservative estimation error makes the integral path-dependent.

    Assumption-dependent correspondences¤

    • A generic energy becomes AR conditionals only after suffix marginalization. This is a distributional equivalence with potentially intractable computation.
    • A score field determines a density up to normalization only when it is integrable and normalizable.
    • Denoising conditional-score regression and marginal-score regression share a population optimum under the denoising identity, not the same sample-level target.
    • Probability-flow ODEs and reverse SDEs can share time marginals under regularity and exact scores; their paths and finite solvers differ.
    • A sufficiently expressive transport may map a base law to an EBM target, but learning it is not implied by existence.

    AR–EBM correspondences without overclaiming¤

    An AR model supplies a globally normalized energy. An EBM supplies conditionals through ratios of prefix/suffix partition functions. They are two representations of the same normalized law after those quantities exist, but their inductive biases and accessible computations differ.

    Sequence-level energy reweighting of an AR reference,

    \[ p_R(x)\propto p_{\mathrm{AR}}(x)e^{R(x)}, \]

    is a product-of-experts or exponential tilt. Sampling it with rejection, MCMC, sequential importance sampling, or policy optimization is an additional algorithm. Adding \(R\) to token logits greedily is generally not exact sequence-level sampling.

    Worked density ratios and discriminators¤

    For normalized \(p\) and \(q\),

    \[ \log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}=E_q(x)-E_p(x). \]

    An optimal binary discriminator estimates a transformed density ratio at population optimum. This connects adversarial learning to energy differences, but a finite discriminator need not be an integrable log-density model, and the ratio alone needs a reference density to recover either distribution.

    With class priors \(\pi_p\) and \(\pi_q\), the population-optimal discriminator is

    \[ D^*(x)=\frac{\pi_p p(x)}{\pi_p p(x)+\pi_q q(x)}, \qquad \log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} =\operatorname{logit}D^*(x)+\log\frac{\pi_q}{\pi_p}. \]

    For equal priors the prior correction vanishes. Recovering \(p\) still requires a normalized reference \(q\) whose support covers \(p\); a ratio on regions never seen from either class is not identified by the classification experiment.

    Mixture scores require posterior responsibilities¤

    For a differentiable mixture \(p_{\mathrm{mix}}=\sum_k\pi_kp_k\), its score is

    \[ s_{\mathrm{mix}}(x) =\sum_k r_k(x)s_k(x), \qquad r_k(x)=\frac{\pi_kp_k(x)}{\sum_j\pi_jp_j(x)}. \]

    The weights are posterior component responsibilities and depend on \(x\). Therefore mixture scores are not plain sums of component scores. By contrast, a normalized product of experts has score \(\sum_k\alpha_ks_k\) wherever all factors are positive and differentiable. Confusing union-like mixture composition with intersection-like product composition is a common source of invalid guidance formulas.

    Energy–flow–score triangle¤

    The three interfaces solve different subproblems:

    • an energy evaluates an unnormalized target;
    • a score supplies a local direction field;
    • a flow supplies a global transport and possibly a normalized density.

    Algorithms that distill an energy or score into a flow are hybrid compositions. Success depends on proposal coverage, trajectory learning, and target evaluation; the mathematical existence of all three objects does not give a free conversion algorithm.

    A flow is global: it assigns a coupled map from base samples to target samples. An energy is pointwise up to normalization, and a score is local and loses component constants. Moving from a score to a flow therefore requires solving a dynamical or transport problem; moving from a flow to a score additionally requires a differentiable inverse and tractable Jacobian. Those operations are the content of an algorithm, not consequences of drawing a triangle.

    Product, mixture, and tilt¤

    Products of normalized experts obey

    \[ p_{\Pi}(x)\propto\prod_k p_k(x)^{\alpha_k}, \qquad E_{\Pi}(x)=\sum_k\alpha_kE_k(x), \]

    for nonnegative weights when the product is normalizable. Products emphasize intersection. The new partition function is generally unknown even if each expert was normalized.

    More precisely, the product exists as a probability law only when

    \[ 0<Z_\Pi=\int\prod_kp_k(x)^{\alpha_k}d\mu(x)<\infty. \]

    Disjoint hard supports make \(Z_\Pi=0\); heavy tails combined with negative weights can make the integral diverge, which is one reason this chapter keeps the default \(\alpha_k\ge0\). Even with positive finite \(Z_\Pi\), a sampler may rarely reach the overlap region.

    A mixture

    \[ p_{\mathrm{mix}}(x)=\sum_k\pi_kp_k(x) \]

    has energy

    \[ E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x)=-\log\sum_k\pi_ke^{-E_k(x)} \]

    when the component energies are normalized negative log densities. Mixtures emphasize union and retain a latent component variable.

    If instead \(E_k\) are unnormalized with unknown \(Z_k\), then the apparent mixture weights inside log-sum-exp are not the probability mixture weights: each component contributes its own unknown scale. Normalize components or carry those constants explicitly before interpreting \(\pi_k\).

    An exponential tilt \(p_R\propto p_0e^{R}\) combines a reference generator with reward or constraint. It exists only when \(0<\mathbb E_{p_0}[e^R]<\infty\) and cannot create mass outside the support of \(p_0\). All three constructions require support and normalization checks, but only a product requires overlap among distinct expert supports.

    Worked Gaussian product and mixture¤

    The product of \(\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(\mathcal N(2,1)\) is proportional to \(\mathcal N(1,1/2)\). Precisions add, and the mean is the precision-weighted average. This is an exact product calculation, but the overlap integral is a new constant.

    For a mixture, the geometry is different. At \(x=0\), the model \(0.75\mathcal N(-1,1)+0.25\mathcal N(1,1)\) has responsibilities \((0.75,0.25)\) because the two component densities happen to agree there. Its score is \(0.75(-1)+0.25(1)=-1/2\), not the unweighted sum zero. Product scores add; mixture scores average with posterior responsibilities.

    Classifiers as joint energies¤

    Given logits \(f_y(x)\), a joint energy model may define

    \[ p(x,y)\propto e^{f_y(x)}, \qquad E(x)=-\log\sum_y e^{f_y(x)}. \]

    The usual conditional softmax \(p(y\mid x)\) cancels the input partition and can be well trained while the implied \(p(x)\) is poor. Discriminative calibration does not establish generative calibration.

    Indeed, finite logits always define a conditional softmax over a finite label set, but a joint density exists only if

    \[ 0<\int\sum_y e^{f_y(x)}d\mu(x)<\infty. \]

    For example, \(f_0(x)=0\) and \(f_1(x)=x\) define a valid binary conditional at every real \(x\), while \(\int_{\mathbb R}(1+e^x)dx=\infty\). There is no normalized joint with Lebesgue base measure from those logits. A data marginal or explicit input energy must supply the missing generative structure.

    1. P1.1. Precisions add to two, so variance is \(1/2\) and mean is \((0+2)/2=1\). The overlap integral is the \(\mathcal N(0,2)\) density of the mean difference two: \(Z_\Pi=(2\sqrt\pi)^{-1}e^{-1}\). Thus \(p_1p_2=Z_\Pi\mathcal N(1,1/2)\). Check. A product of normalized densities need not integrate to one.
    2. P1.2. The density ratio of the mean-\(2\) component to the mean-\(-2\) component is \(e^4\). Responsibilities are \(r_+=e^4/(1+e^4)\) and \(r_-=1/(1+e^4)\). Component scores are \(1\) and \(-3\), hence \(s(1)=(e^4-3)/(e^4+1)\approx0.9281\). Check. The score points toward the dominant right component but retains a correction from the left one.
    3. P1.3. The indicator products vanish everywhere, so \(Z_\Pi=0\) and no normalized product exists. Each uniform separately integrates to one. Convolution or replacement by Gaussian-tailed experts changes support and therefore changes the model; it is not an algebraic identity. Check. Composition failure does not imply component failure.
    4. P1.4. The conditional is \(p(y=1\mid x)=e^x/(1+e^x)\), valid for every finite \(x\). But \(\int_{\mathbb R}(1+e^x)dx=\infty\), so the logits do not define the proposed joint energy.     Final synthesis¤

    Every normalized model has an energy; every smooth positive density has a score; every latent joint induces an observed free energy; and suitable transports can represent many target laws. What distinguishes families is where normalization, inference, and sampling complexity live. A useful unification preserves those costs rather than erasing them.
