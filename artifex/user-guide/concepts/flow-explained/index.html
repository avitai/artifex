    A classical normalizing flow pushes a tractable base law through an invertible map and evaluates the resulting density with change of variables. Continuous normalizing flows and flow-matching models instead parameterize time-dependent vector fields; they share transport language with classical flows but have different likelihood and numerical contracts.

    • Exact Likelihood

      Evaluate a classical flow's modeled continuous density exactly in its declared coordinates; data likelihood and numerical evaluation retain explicit qualifications

    • Bijective Transformations

      Invertible mappings support direct latent encoding and decoding through forward and inverse passes; this is not the same as exact arbitrary conditional or Bayesian inference

    • Expressivity Under Structural Constraints

      Transform simple base distributions into complex target distributions through learned compositions

    • Map-Dependent Sampling Cost

      Coupling maps, autoregressive inverses, and numerical ODE trajectories expose different parallelism, NFE, memory, and latency profiles

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    Early neural normalizing-flow constructions include NICE and the variational-inference formulation of Rezende & Mohamed (2015). They define distributions using invertible transformations with tractable Jacobian determinants and provide an exact symbolic expression for the model density; reported data likelihood may still include dequantization bounds, preprocessing Jacobians, finite precision, or numerical integration. Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023) trains a time-dependent vector field without requiring the same structured-Jacobian architecture. Its likelihood, when requested, is obtained through continuous-time dynamics and divergence computation rather than the cheap finite composition used by coupling flows.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be comfortable with densities, conditional probability, multivariable derivatives, determinants, and the distinction between a random variable and one of its coordinates. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. derive change of variables in both map directions without changing the Jacobian sign;
    2. explain which assumptions make a transformation a diffeomorphism almost everywhere;
    3. distinguish an exact model-density expression from a discrete-data likelihood or a floating-point computation;
    4. diagnose support and topology mismatches; and
    5. calculate a complete scalar and two-dimensional flow likelihood.

    Consequences of map structure¤

    Classical flows couple density evaluation and sampling through the same bijection. The consequences depend on the map architecture:

    • Evaluate the model density with an exact change-of-variables formula when the inverse and Jacobian determinant are tractable
    • Generate samples through fast inverse transformations
    • Encode and decode latents directly without an amortised inverse; arbitrary marginal, conditional, or posterior queries can remain intractable
    • Train by maximum likelihood without an adversarial game; optimisation stability remains architecture-, preprocessing-, and precision-dependent

    Two flavours of flow

    The flow-matching family trades the structured-Jacobian requirement for architectural freedom: a DiT, U-Net, or another network regresses a time-dependent vector field. Likelihood evaluation then requires solving an ODE and integrating or estimating its divergence, so it is numerical rather than the cheap exact formula of a coupling flow. This document covers both the discrete-step classical-flow architectures and the continuous-time / flow-matching family.

    Evidence and family boundaries¤

    The volume covers flow matching, rectified flow, MeanFlow, discrete flow matching, and Riemannian or equivariant variants. Rectified-flow objectives are used in systems such as SD3 and FLUX.1; MeanFlow reports one-evaluation ImageNet generation under its class-conditional protocol (Geng et al., NeurIPS 2025); and discrete flow-matching papers evaluate language and structured-state generation. These are method- and protocol-specific results, not a family-wide ordering. Their assumptions and costs are developed in Conditional and Average-Velocity Flow Objectives.

    Constructive interpretation: transport and volume change¤

    For a classical flow \(x=f(z)\), generation and density evaluation use opposite directions of the same map:

    1. Choose a base law. Select \(p_Z\) with tractable sampling and density evaluation on a declared support.

    2. Generate through the forward map. Draw \(z\sim p_Z\) and compute \(x=f(z)\). A composition of invertible layers preserves a one-to-one correspondence within its modeled support.

    3. Evaluate through the inverse map. Compute \(z=f^{-1}(x)\) and use the Jacobian determinant to account for local volume expansion or contraction.

    4. Audit representation and numerics. Dequantization, preprocessing, map direction, determinant evaluation, support, topology, conditioning, and floating-point error remain part of the likelihood claim.

    Flow matching retains the transport view but learns a velocity field rather than a finite bijective composition. Generation then solves an ODE or related dynamics, and density evaluation—when required—adds numerical divergence integration.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Change of Variables Formula¤

    The change of variables formula serves as the cornerstone of all normalizing flow architectures. Given a random variable \(\mathbf{z}\) with known density \(p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z})\) and an invertible transformation \(\mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z})\), the density of \(\mathbf{x}\) becomes:

    \[ p_{\mathcal{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = p_{\mathcal{Z}}(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) \left| \det \frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \right| \]

    Or equivalently in log space:

    \[ \log p_{\mathcal{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}) + \log \left| \det \frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \right| \]

    where \(\mathbf{z} = f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})\).

    Geometric Intuition

    The Jacobian determinant \(\left| \det \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}} \right|\) quantifies the relative change in volume of an infinitesimal neighborhood under transformation \(f\). When the transformation expands a region (\(|\det J| > 1\)), the probability density must decrease proportionally to conserve total probability mass. Conversely, contraction (\(|\det J| < 1\)) concentrates probability, increasing density.

    For \(D\)-dimensional vectors, the Jacobian matrix \(J_f(\mathbf{z})\) is the \(D \times D\) matrix of partial derivatives \([\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial z_j}]\). Computing a general determinant requires \(O(D^3)\) operations, which becomes intractable for high-dimensional data like 256×256 RGB images with \(D = 196{,}608\) dimensions.

    The entire field of normalizing flows revolves around designing transformations with structured Jacobians—triangular, diagonal, or block-structured matrices where determinants reduce to \(O(D)\) computations.

    Composing Multiple Transformations¤

    A single invertible transformation typically provides limited modeling capacity. The power of flows emerges through composition: stacking \(K\) transformations:

    \[ \mathbf{x} = f_K \circ f_{K-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{z}) \]

    The log-likelihood decomposes additively:

    \[ \log p_{\mathcal{X}}(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}_0) - \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log \left| \det \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial \mathbf{z}_{k-1}} \right| \]

    where \(\mathbf{z}_0 = \mathbf{z}\) and \(\mathbf{z}_k = f_k(\mathbf{z}_{k-1})\) for \(k=1,\ldots,K\).

    Additive Structure in Log-Space

    The chain rule for Jacobians states \(\det J_{f_2 \circ f_1}(\mathbf{u}) = \det J_{f_2}(f_1(\mathbf{u})) \cdot \det J_{f_1}(\mathbf{u})\), so log-determinants simply add: \(\log|\det J_{\text{total}}| = \sum_k \log|\det J_k|\). This ensures numerical stability and makes total computational cost \(O(KD)\) when each layer has \(O(D)\) Jacobian computation.

    Three Requirements for Flow Layers¤

    For a transformation \(f\) to be a valid flow layer, it must satisfy:

    1. Invertibility: \(f\) must be bijective (one-to-one and onto)
    2. Efficient Jacobian: \(\log \left| \det \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}} \right|\) must be tractable to compute
    3. Efficient Inverse: \(f^{-1}\) must be computable efficiently (for sampling)

    Different flow architectures make different trade-offs among these requirements.

    The list is intentionally computational as well as mathematical. A bijection with an intractable inverse is a valid transformation theorem but a poor sampler. A bijection with a dense \(D\times D\) Jacobian may define a model but defeats the purpose of a scalable normalizing-flow layer. Conversely, flow matching does not require each neural-network evaluation to be invertible: invertibility, when it holds, belongs to the integrated ODE flow under regularity and uniqueness assumptions.

    Support, topology, and almost-everywhere qualifications¤

    Suppose the base density is positive everywhere on connected \(\mathbb R^D\) and \(f\) is a continuous bijection with a continuous inverse. Then the image support is connected. Such a map can assign very little mass to a bridge between two modes, but it cannot turn the support into two truly disconnected components. The same homeomorphism logic prevents an ordinary fixed-dimensional flow from exactly mapping Euclidean space onto a lower-dimensional manifold.

    This does not mean flows cannot model multimodal data. It means the approximation may retain low-density connections, become ill-conditioned near a topological obstruction, or require a changed construction: mixtures, surjective or injective flows, augmented dimensions, manifold-valued maps, stochastic dynamics, or discrete state-space generators. Density formulas also hold only where the needed derivatives and inverse exist; modern layers are often piecewise differentiable, so the correct statement is "almost everywhere" with respect to the reference measure.

    What changes when the transformation is not bijective?¤

    The ordinary change-of-variables formula applies to a dimension-preserving bijection. Three common extensions change the statistical contract rather than merely relaxing an implementation detail.

    Surjective transformations. If several latent states map to the same observation, an observation density must aggregate all preimages. In a finite example, let

    \[ p_Z(1)=0.2,\qquad p_Z(2)=0.3,\qquad p_Z(3)=0.5, \]

    and let \(f(1)=f(2)=a\), \(f(3)=b\). Then \(p_X(a)=0.5\) and \(p_X(b)=0.5\). Choosing one convenient inverse for \(a\) would return either \(0.2\) or \(0.3\) and is wrong. In continuous models, summation becomes integration over a fibre or an auxiliary reverse distribution. SurVAE Flows organize bijective, surjective, and stochastic transformations in one modular framework; particular surjections can retain exact likelihood terms, whereas others yield a variational bound.

    Injective transformations. If \(f:\mathbb R^d\to\mathbb R^D\) with \(d<D\), the image distribution is generally singular with respect to \(D\)-dimensional Lebesgue measure. When \(f\) is a regular injective chart, its density with respect to \(d\)-dimensional volume on the image uses the metric factor

    \[ p_{\mathcal M}(f(z)) =\frac{p_Z(z)}{\sqrt{\det\!\left(J_f(z)^T J_f(z)\right)}}. \]

    This is not an ordinary ambient-space likelihood. Chart coverage, self-intersection, observation noise, and manifold dimension must be declared.

    Augmented transformations. A model can append an auxiliary \(U\) and learn a bijection for the joint \((X,U)\). Exact joint density \(p(x,u)\) does not by itself give exact observed density because

    \[ p(x)=\int p(x,u)\,du. \]

    The integral may be analytic, estimated, or variationally bounded. Augmentation can ease topology and optimization while adding auxiliary dimension, inference, and compute.

    These constructions fill genuine architecture and representation gaps, but they do not turn every non-bijective decoder into a classical normalizing flow. The conditioning and constraints volume separately explains feasible maps, projection, and conditional-law preservation.

    Retrieval practice: topology-changing flow components
    1. Why is one inverse branch insufficient for a surjection?
    2. With respect to which measure is an injective manifold density defined?
    3. Does exact \(p(x,u)\) imply tractable exact \(p(x)\)?

    Solutions. (1) Probability from every preimage contributes to the observation. (2) The appropriate lower-dimensional volume measure on the image, not ordinary ambient Lebesgue measure. (3) No; the auxiliary must still be marginalized.

    Base Distribution¤

    Efficient sampling usually starts from a simple reference law \(p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z})\):

    Standard Gaussian (most common):

    \[ p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{z}; \mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{D/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{z}\|^2\right) \]

    Uniform (less common):

    \[ p_{\mathcal{Z}}(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{U}(\mathbf{z}; -a, a) = \frac{1}{(2a)^D} \mathbb{1}_{[-a,a]^D}(\mathbf{z}) \]

    The base is part of the model, not a harmless placeholder. A bounded base passed through a continuous map has bounded image if the map extends continuously to the compact closure; a full-support Gaussian avoids that particular restriction but still inherits the topology constraints above. A learned or mixture base can improve fit while changing sampling, density evaluation, and identifiability.

    Worked example: one affine map, both Jacobian directions¤

    Let \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(X=f(Z)=2Z+3\). For the observation \(x=5\), the inverse is \(z=(5-3)/2=1\) and \(\lvert dz/dx\rvert=1/2\). Therefore

    \[ \log p_X(5)=\log p_Z(1)-\log 2 =-\frac12-\frac12\log(2\pi)-\log 2. \]

    Using the forward Jacobian gives the identical answer only if its sign is reversed:

    \[ \log p_X(f(z))=\log p_Z(z)-\log\lvert df/dz\rvert. \]

    In two dimensions, \(f(z)=Az+b\) gives \(\log p_X(x)=\log p_Z(A^{-1}(x-b))-\log|\det A|\). If \(A=\operatorname{diag}(2,1/2)\), the transformation changes shape but preserves volume because \(|\det A|=1\); individual scale changes do not imply a nonzero total log-determinant.

    Counterexample: invertible does not mean universally expressive

    No homeomorphism from connected \(\mathbb R\) has an image equal to \((-2,-1)\cup(1,2)\). A one-dimensional continuous normalizing flow can approximate a two-component target by leaving a very low-density bridge, but it cannot exactly reproduce that disconnected support while retaining the stated assumptions.

    Independent practice and assessment map: flow foundations¤

    Declare the probability space, map direction, support, reference measure, and numerical representation before using the change-of-variables formula. Show the Jacobian direction and sign behind every density equality.

    P1 — Reconstruct a normalized flow without hiding its assumptions¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: an invertible neural network has a tractable model density; a zero log-determinant means the transformation is the identity; a bijection from a full-support Gaussian can exactly produce disconnected support; exact \(p(x,u)\) makes \(p(x)\) tractable; direct inversion computes any partial-observation posterior; and an exact continuous density is an exact uint8 probability mass. For every false statement, name the missing map, measure, marginalization, topology, or data-semantics condition.
    2. P1.2 — Direction and composition ledger. Let \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\), \(f_1(z)=3z-2\), and \(f_2(u)=\exp(u)\). For the observation \(x=e^1\), recover every intermediate state and compute \(\log p_X(x)\) once with forward Jacobians and once with the inverse-map Jacobian. Identify the support of \(X\) and explain why reversing only the determinant sign without reversing the evaluated states is invalid.
    3. P1.3 — Two-dimensional likelihood and oracle. For \(X=AZ+b\) with \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,I_2)\), \(A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\0&1/2\end{bmatrix}\), and \(b=(1,-1)^\top\), compute the density at the image of \(z=(1,2)^\top\). Derive the forward and inverse Jacobians, verify the determinant product, and design a finite-difference or automatic-differentiation oracle that would catch a batched sign or reduction bug.
    4. P1.4 — Change the transformation contract.     A classical flow is a normalized density construction because a base law, a map direction, and a volume correction are specified together. Exactness attaches to that mathematical construction. Data discretisation, preprocessing, finite precision, and numerical ODE integration are additional layers whose errors must be reported rather than hidden behind the word exact.

    Design primitives: maps before architectures¤

    A flow layer must answer three computational questions:

    1. Can we evaluate \(x=f(u)\)?
    2. Can we recover \(u=f^{-1}(x)\) in the direction the application needs?
    3. Can we compute \(\log|\det J_f(u)|\) or its inverse-map counterpart with a declared error model?

    These questions are independent. A map can have a cheap determinant but an expensive inverse, or an analytic inverse but a dense determinant. Named architectures are best understood as different ways of allocating those costs.

    Structured affine maps¤

    The simplest multivariate bijection is

    \[ x=Au+b, \]

    where \(A\in\mathbb R^{D\times D}\) is nonsingular. Its inverse is \(u=A^{-1}(x-b)\) and its log-volume change is \(\log|\det A|\). This already separates statistical expressivity from computational structure: an unrestricted dense \(A\) can mix every coordinate, but repeatedly factorizing it costs \(O(D^3)\) in general.

    Table FLOW.2. Structured affine maps
    Structure of \(A\) Forward map Inverse map \(\log\lvert\det A\rvert\) What is restricted?
    dense \(O(D^2)\) \(O(D^3)\) factorization, then \(O(D^2)\) per solve \(O(D^3)\) factorization no linear mixing restriction beyond nonsingularity
    triangular \(O(D^2)\) \(O(D^2)\) triangular solve \(O(D)\) from the diagonal dependency order is triangular
    diagonal \(O(D)\) \(O(D)\) \(O(D)\) no coordinate mixing
    permutation \(O(D)\) \(O(D)\) \(0\) in log absolute value reorders coordinates without reshaping density

    The dense row assumes no reusable factorization. If \(A\) is fixed across many evaluations, a cached factorization changes the amortized solve cost; during training, an unconstrained \(A\) changes and its factorization or parameterization remains part of the computation. A cheap log-determinant therefore does not imply a cheap transformation, and conversely.

    Finite worked example. Let

    \[ A=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\1&3\end{bmatrix},\qquad b=\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix},\qquad u=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Then \(x=Au+b=(3,6)^\top\) and \(\det A=6\). Solving the triangular system \(A u=x-b\) recovers \((1,2)^\top\). If \(u\) has density \(p_U\), the output density at this paired point is

    \[ \log p_X(3,6)=\log p_U(1,2)-\log 6. \]

    The minus sign is not a convention to memorize separately: \(A\) expands a unit area by a factor of six, so density per unit output area falls by that factor.

    Monotone scalar maps¤

    An elementwise transform

    \[ x_i=h_i(u_i;\theta_i) \]

    has a diagonal Jacobian and

    \[ \log|\det J_f(u)|=\sum_{i=1}^{D}\log|h_i'(u_i;\theta_i)|. \]

    A continuous scalar map is invertible on its declared domain if it is strictly monotone and maps that domain onto the declared codomain. Positivity of the derivative is a convenient sufficient construction, but it does not by itself state the range or the numerical cost of inversion.

    Table FLOW.3. Monotone scalar maps
    Scalar construction How monotonicity is obtained Forward/inverse behavior Main limitation
    affine \(h(u)=e^a u+b\) positive scale both analytic one layer only shifts and rescales
    positive-weight monotone network monotone activations and constrained paths forward analytic; inverse commonly uses root finding weight constraints can limit the convenient parameterization
    integrated positive derivative \(h(u)=b+\int_0^u [\operatorname{softplus}(g(s))+\epsilon],ds\) forward uses quadrature; inverse nests root finding around integration numerical quadrature and root tolerances enter the density computation
    sum-of-squares polynomial integrate a nonnegative polynomial derivative, with strictness handled explicitly polynomial forward and derivative; inverse generally uses root finding polynomial degree and tails constrain practical behavior
    monotone rational-quadratic spline positive bin widths, heights, and knot derivatives analytic within the selected bin, including a quadratic inverse requires tail and boundary conventions plus bin lookup

    Monotone neural transformations, sum-of-squares flows, and rational-quadratic splines supply increasingly flexible scalar maps (Huang et al., 2018; Jaini et al., 2019; Wehenkel & Louppe, 2019; Durkan et al., 2019). Applied independently, however, scalar maps cannot create dependence between initially independent coordinates. Coupling and autoregressive layers add conditioners that make each scalar map depend on other coordinates while preserving a structured Jacobian.

    This ladder prevents a common category error. A spline is a scalar transformation; a masked autoregressive layer is a dependency structure; a stack plus base distribution is a normalized model; and the same stack can instead parameterize a variational posterior. None of these labels alone determines the full system.

    1. NICE: Pioneering Coupling Layers¤

    NICE (Dinh, Krueger & Bengio, 2014) — Non-linear Independent Components Estimation — introduced additive coupling layers that made normalizing flows practical for high-dimensional data.

    Coupling Layer Mechanism:

    Given input \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\), partition into \((\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2)\):

    \[ \begin{align} \mathbf{y}_1 &= \mathbf{x}_1 \\ \mathbf{y}_2 &= \mathbf{x}_2 + m(\mathbf{x}_1) \end{align} \]

    where \(m\) can be any arbitrary function (typically a neural network).

    Key Properties:

    • Volume-preserving: \(\log|\det(\mathbf{J})| = 0\) (determinant is exactly 1)
    • Efficient inverse: \(\mathbf{x}_1 = \mathbf{y}_1\), \(\mathbf{x}_2 = \mathbf{y}_2 - m(\mathbf{y}_1)\)
    • No Jacobian computation: The triangular structure makes the determinant trivial
    • Flexible coupling function: evaluating the triangular determinant does not require the internal Jacobian of \(m\), but the runtime and memory of the layer still depend on the size and architecture of \(m\)

    When to Use NICE:

    • Fast forward and inverse computations required
    • Volume-preserving transformations are acceptable
    • Starting point for understanding coupling layers
    • Lower-dimensional problems (hundreds of dimensions)

    2. RealNVP: Adding Scale for Expressiveness¤

    RealNVP (Dinh, Sohl-Dickstein & Bengio, 2017 — ICLR) — Real-valued Non-Volume Preserving — extends NICE with affine coupling layers:

    \[ \begin{align} \mathbf{y}_1 &= \mathbf{x}_1 \\ \mathbf{y}_2 &= \mathbf{x}_2 \odot \exp(s(\mathbf{x}_1)) + t(\mathbf{x}_1) \end{align} \]

    where \(s(\cdot)\) and \(t(\cdot)\) are neural networks outputting scale and translation, and \(\odot\) denotes element-wise multiplication.

    Key Properties:

    • Tractable Jacobian: \(\log|\det(\mathbf{J})| = \sum_i s_i(\mathbf{x}_1)\)
    • Efficient inverse: the triangular inverse remains analytic.
    \[ \begin{align} \mathbf{x}_1 &= \mathbf{y}_1 \\ \mathbf{x}_2 &= (\mathbf{y}_2 - t(\mathbf{y}_1)) \odot \exp(-s(\mathbf{y}_1)) \end{align} \]
    • Alternating masks: Alternate which dimensions are transformed across layers
    • No determinant of the internal scale/translation Jacobians is needed: gradients still flow through \(s\) and \(t\) into all of their parameters, but the block-triangular coupling Jacobian makes the log-determinant depend only on the emitted scale values. More capable conditioners can therefore be used without computing their full Jacobian determinant, although their ordinary evaluation cost still matters.

    Multi-Scale Architecture:

    RealNVP introduced hierarchical structure that revolutionized flow-based modeling:

    1. Squeeze operation: Reshape \(s \times s \times c\) tensors into \(\frac{s}{2} \times \frac{s}{2} \times 4c\)
    2. Factor out: After several coupling layers, factor out half the channels to the prior
    3. Continue processing: Transform remaining channels at higher resolution

    This reduces repeated computation on factored-out variables and supports higher-dimensional image experiments, while the exact memory and runtime depend on the number of scales, channels, conditioners, and implementation.

    When to Use RealNVP:

    • Need both fast sampling and density estimation
    • Working with continuous data, especially images
    • Image generation tasks at moderate to high resolution
    • Moderate-dimensional data (hundreds to thousands of dimensions)

    3. Glow: Learnable Permutations¤

    Glow (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018 — NeurIPS) extends RealNVP with three key block-level innovations. Artifex retains these Glow blocks as a single-scale image flow baseline rather than the paper's squeeze/split multi-scale hierarchy.

    Glow Block Architecture:

    Each flow step combines three layers:

    1. ActNorm (Activation Normalization):

    Per-channel affine transformation with trainable parameters:

    \[ y_{i,j,c} = s_c \cdot x_{i,j,c} + b_c \]
    • Data-dependent initialization: normalize first minibatch to zero mean, unit variance
    • Enables training with batch size 1 (critical for high-resolution images)
    • \(\log|\det J| = H \cdot W \cdot \sum_c \log|s_c|\) for \(H \times W\) spatial dimensions

    2. Invertible 1×1 Convolution:

    Learned linear mixing of channels using invertible matrix \(\mathbf{W}\):

    \[ \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{W} \mathbf{x} \]
    • Replaces fixed permutations with learned channel mixing
    • LU decomposition: \(\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{P} \cdot \mathbf{L} \cdot (\mathbf{U} + \text{diag}(\mathbf{s}))\)
    • Determinant: \(\log|\det \mathbf{W}| = \sum_i \log|s_i|\) (reduced to \(O(c)\))
    • The learned mixing was an empirical component of the paper's complete Glow system; an isolated likelihood gain is protocol- and ablation-dependent

    3. Affine Coupling Layer:

    Similar to RealNVP but with the above improvements.

    When to Use Glow in Artifex:

    • Need a fixed-size image flow with learnable channel mixing
    • Want an analytic model-density expression with image-shaped forward and inverse transforms
    • Need ActNorm plus invertible 1×1 convolutions as a stronger image baseline
    • Want a deeper single-scale alternative to RealNVP on image tensors

    Artifex Runtime Note

    The retained implementation is configured through image_shape and blocks_per_scale. If you need the original paper's multi-scale squeeze/split hierarchy, you will need a different runtime.

    4. MAF: Masked Autoregressive Flow¤

    MAF (Papamakarios, Pavlakou & Murray, 2017 — NeurIPS) turns a continuous autoregressive density into an invertible triangular transformation. Let \(x\) denote data and \(z\) a standard-normal base vector. One layer declares

    \[ p(x_i\mid x_{<i}) =\mathcal N\!\left(x_i;\mu_i(x_{<i}),\exp(2\alpha_i(x_{<i}))\right), \]

    which has the data-to-base map

    \[ z_i=(x_i-\mu_i(x_{<i}))\exp[-\alpha_i(x_{<i})]. \]

    The map is triangular: \(z_i\) depends only on \(x_{\le i}\). Its Jacobian is generally not diagonal, because \(\mu_i\) and \(\alpha_i\) can depend on earlier \(x_j\); only its diagonal is needed for the determinant:

    \[ \frac{\partial z_i}{\partial x_i}=\exp[-\alpha_i(x_{<i})], \qquad \log\left|\det\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right| =-\sum_{i=1}^{D}\alpha_i(x_{<i}). \]

    The conditional family must make the scalar inverse explicit. A single Gaussian conditional is unimodal, while stacking autoregressive layers with permutations can create substantially richer joints. Mixture conditionals are another route to flexibility, but a generic mixture CDF need not provide the analytic bijection required by a classical flow.

    Why MADE makes density evaluation parallel¤

    The functions \(\mu_i\) and \(\alpha_i\) are commonly produced by a MADE network (Germain et al., ICML 2015). For a complete observed \(x\), a mask guarantees that output pair \((\mu_i,\alpha_i)\) can see \(x_{<i}\) but not \(x_{\ge i}\). All output pairs can therefore be computed by one feed-forward network evaluation. The binary MADE construction and four-variable mask audit derives the mask inequalities and multiplies the masks to verify every permitted path.

    For the order \((1,2,3)\), the resulting conditional dependency graph is:

    The graph describes conditioner dependencies, not the full computational graph of a particular hidden network. In particular, \(x_i\) enters the scalar transformation for \(z_i\) directly but is forbidden from the network that emits its own \((\mu_i,\alpha_i)\).

    Given arbitrary observed \(x\), the sequence of operations is:

    1. evaluate the masked conditioner once to obtain every \((\mu_i,\alpha_i)\);
    2. compute every \(z_i\) elementwise in parallel;
    3. add the base log-density and the triangular log-determinant.

    Sampling reverses the map:

    \[ x_i=\mu_i(x_{<i})+z_i\exp[\alpha_i(x_{<i})]. \]

    Now \(x_{<i}\) must exist before the parameters for coordinate \(i\) are valid. A basic sampler therefore exposes one coordinate at a time and may require \(D\) masked-network evaluations per layer. Specialized caching can reduce repeated arithmetic, but it cannot remove the declared dependency chain without changing the model or using an approximate parallel inversion.

    When to use MAF:

    • Density estimation is the primary goal
    • Sampling speed is less critical
    • Tabular or low-to-moderate dimensional data
    • Need highly expressive transformations
    • All dimensions should interact

    5. IAF: Inverse Autoregressive Flow¤

    IAF (Kingma et al., 2016 — NeurIPS) uses the same kind of masked conditioner but attaches its dependencies to the base vector. With \(z\sim p_0\) already available, define

    \[ x_i=\mu_i(z_{<i})+z_i\exp[\alpha_i(z_{<i})]. \]

    All conditioner inputs \(z\) are known before the layer runs. A MADE-style network can emit all parameter pairs in one evaluation, after which every \(x_i\) is computed elementwise. The same pass supplies the diagonal Jacobian terms needed to evaluate the density of this generated sample. This is why IAF is useful in amortized variational inference: the algorithm needs samples from the approximate posterior and their densities.

    The inverse problem is different. Given an arbitrary external \(x\), recovering \(z_1\) makes \(z_1\) available to compute the parameters for \(z_2\), and so on. Arbitrary-data density evaluation is therefore sequential in the basic IAF construction.

    Table FLOW.5. 5. IAF: Inverse Autoregressive Flow

    Generate a sample and its density all \(z\) \(z\to x\) one masked evaluation per layer; coordinate arithmetic is parallel
    Score arbitrary external data all \(x\), no \(z\) prefix \(x\to z\) sequential in the basic construction

    Calling one map “forward” is unsafe unless the author first declares whether forward means base-to-data, model execution, or density evaluation. This book always names the coordinates and direction explicitly.

    When to use IAF:

    • Fast sampling is the primary goal
    • Density is needed mainly for samples produced by the same transformation
    • Variational inference (amortized inference in VAEs)
    • Settings where the base-to-sample direction must be parallel within each layer, after measuring complete latency

    A MADE density estimator is not automatically a flow

    Binary MADE defines normalized Bernoulli conditionals but is not a bijection between equal-dimensional continuous spaces. A continuous MADE with arbitrary mixture conditionals can define a tractable autoregressive density without supplying an analytic inverse. MAF and IAF require scalar transformations whose inverse and Jacobian diagonal are available in the needed direction. The dependency mask is reusable; the probabilistic object is not interchangeable.

    6. Neural Spline Flows¤

    Neural Spline Flows (Durkan, Bekasov, Murray & Papamakarios, 2019 — NeurIPS) use monotonic rational-quadratic splines to create highly expressive yet tractable transformations.

    Rational-Quadratic Spline Transform:

    Each spline maps interval \([-B, B]\) to itself using \(K\) rational-quadratic segments, parameterized by:

    • \(K+1\) knot positions \(\{(x^{(k)}, y^{(k)})\}\)
    • \(K+1\) derivative values \(\{\delta^{(k)}\}\)

    Within segment \(k\), the transformation applies a ratio of quadratic polynomials.

    Key Properties:

    • Strict monotonicity: positive bin widths, heights, and derivatives make each scalar spline invertible on its declared interval, with the boundary/tail rule completing the global map
    • Smooth derivatives: No discontinuities (unlike piecewise-linear)
    • Closed-form operations: Forward evaluation, analytic inverse (quadratic equation), closed-form derivative
    • Piecewise-monotone expressivity: increasing the number of bins can approximate richer scalar monotone transforms, but the required count depends on tail bounds, conditioner capacity, data, and optimisation; 8 or 16 bins are experimental settings, not universal sufficiency results

    Compared to alternatives:

    • vs Affine: a spline emits widths, heights, and derivatives for several bins rather than only a shift and scale; the exact parameter count depends on bin count and boundary convention
    • vs Neural Autoregressive Flows: No iterative root-finding needed
    • vs Flow++: No bisection algorithms required
    • vs Piecewise-linear: Smooth derivatives improve optimization

    Results:

    • Established historical result: the Neural Spline Flows paper reports competitive log likelihood on tabular and image-density benchmarks under its architectures and preprocessing. Numbers such as CIFAR-10 BPD are meaningful only with the dequantisation, split, parameter count, and evaluation convention attached.

    When to Use Neural Spline Flows:

    • Maximum expressiveness with tractability
    • Density estimation on complex distributions
    • Want fewer parameters than Glow
    • Need smooth, differentiable transformations

    7. Residual transformations: determinant and inverse design¤

    A residual transformation has the form

    \[ f(u)=u+F(u). \]

    The identity skip connection does not make an arbitrary residual block invertible. There are at least two distinct ways to make residual transformations useful in flows, and they solve different problems:

    1. give \(J_F\) a low-rank factorization so its determinant is cheap, then establish invertibility for the particular parameterization; or
    2. make \(F\) contractive so invertibility follows globally, then estimate the generally dense log-determinant.

    Low-rank route: the determinant lemma reduces dimension, not assumptions¤

    Let

    \[ F(u)=Vh(Wu+b), \]

    where \(W\in\mathbb R^{M\times D}\), \(V\in\mathbb R^{D\times M}\), \(M\ll D\), and \(h\) acts elementwise. With \(D_h=\operatorname{diag}(h'(Wu+b))\),

    \[ J_f(u)=I_D+V D_h W. \]

    The matrix determinant lemma gives

    \[ \det(I_D+V D_h W)=\det(I_M+D_h W V). \tag{FLOW.1}\label{eq:flow-low-rank-residual-determinant} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:flow-low-rank-residual-determinant}\) replaces a \(D\times D\) determinant with an \(M\times M\) determinant. This can greatly reduce determinant cost when \(M\ll D\). It does not prove that the determinant is nonzero everywhere, supply an analytic inverse, or guarantee that numerical inversion is stable.

    The one-unit case \(M=1\) is the planar flow introduced for variational inference by Rezende & Mohamed (2015):

    \[ f(u)=u+v\tanh(w^\top u+b), \]

    with

    \[ \det J_f(u)=1+\bigl(1-\tanh^2(w^\top u+b)\bigr)w^\top v. \]

    For this activation, \(w^\top v>-1\) is a convenient sufficient condition for strict monotonicity along the affected direction and hence invertibility. The condition is not implied by low rank; it must be enforced, for example by reparameterizing the component of \(v\) parallel to \(w\). Sylvester flows replace the single-unit bottleneck by a structured multi-unit residual block while retaining a reduced determinant (van den Berg et al., UAI 2018).

    Worked two-dimensional planar map. Choose

    \[ w=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix},\qquad v=\begin{bmatrix}1/2\\1\end{bmatrix},\qquad b=0. \]

    Then

    \[ f(u_1,u_2)= \left(u_1+\tfrac12\tanh u_1,\;u_2+\tanh u_1\right), \]

    and

    \[ \det J_f(u)=1+\tfrac12\operatorname{sech}^2(u_1)>0. \]

    At \(u=(0,1)\), the map returns \(x=(0,1)\) but locally expands area by \(3/2\), so the paired output log-density subtracts \(\log(3/2)\). To invert a general \(x\), first solve the strictly increasing scalar equation \(x_1=u_1+\tfrac12\tanh u_1\), then set \(u_2=x_2-\tanh u_1\). The determinant is analytic even though the inverse usually is not.

    Counterexample: rank one is not an inverse certificate

    In one dimension, \(f(u)=u-2\tanh u\) is an identity plus a rank-one residual, yet \(f'(0)=-1\) and \(f'\) changes sign. The map is not globally one-to-one. The determinant lemma evaluates a local volume factor efficiently; it does not turn an arbitrary bottleneck residual network into a bijection.

    Table FLOW.7. Low-rank route: the determinant lemma reduces dimension, not assumptions
    Residual route What makes the determinant tractable? What makes the map invertible? Typical inverse
    planar/Sylvester-style low rank determinant lemma reduces \(D\) dimensions to bottleneck dimension \(M\) case-specific parameter constraints analytic only in special cases; otherwise structured numerical solve
    contractive residual flow convergent log-determinant power series, often with trace estimation a global Lipschitz constant below one Banach fixed-point iteration

    The comparison is architectural, not chronological: neither route is a universally superior replacement for the other. Low rank limits the determinant computation but can bottleneck interactions; contraction permits a dense residual Jacobian but constrains Lipschitz behavior and introduces iterative numerical work.

    Contractive route: a global inverse with estimated volume¤

    Invertible residual networks and residual flows (Behrmann et al., ICML 2019; Chen et al., NeurIPS 2019) instead constrain \(g:\mathbb R^D\to\mathbb R^D\) in

    \[ f(u)=u+g(u) \]

    to have a global Lipschitz constant \(L<1\). Given \(x\), define \(T_x(u)=x-g(u)\). This map is also an \(L\)-contraction, so Banach's fixed-point theorem gives a unique solution of

    \[ u_{k+1}=x-g(u_k),\qquad u_*=x-g(u_*),\qquad f(u_*)=x. \]

    For any initialization, the error decreases geometrically; one useful a posteriori bound is

    \[ \lVert u_k-u_*\rVert \leq \frac{L^k}{1-L}\lVert u_1-u_0\rVert. \]

    In a multilayer residual network, controlling each weight operator and using activations with known Lipschitz constants can bound the product for the complete residual branch. Spectral normalization estimates and rescales operator norms, but a finite power-iteration estimate is not an exact certificate unless its approximation error is controlled. Moreover, blindly forcing every matrix to norm \(c\) is different from projecting only matrices whose norm exceeds a bound.

    The residual Jacobian is generally dense. Under the contraction condition, the matrix log series converges:

    \[ \log\det(I+J_g) =\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{k+1}}{k}\operatorname{tr}(J_g^k). \]

    For a probe \(v\) with \(\mathbb E[vv^\top]=I\),

    \[ \mathbb E[v^\top J_g^k v]=\operatorname{tr}(J_g^k), \]

    so automatic differentiation can estimate each trace without materializing the full Jacobian. Truncating the infinite series gives a biased log-determinant approximation. Randomized Russian-roulette truncation can remove that truncation bias when the survival probabilities and summability conditions justify exchanging the random sum and expectation; finite expected work and finite variance are additional properties to check, not automatic consequences of the name.

    Sufficient is not necessary

    Contractivity is a convenient global certificate, not a characterization of every invertible residual map. For example, \(f(u)=2u\) is invertible even though its residual branch \(g(u)=u\) has Lipschitz constant one. Conversely, a small determinant-estimation error does not certify that an unconstrained residual map is globally one-to-one.

    Use this route when dense coordinate interaction is worth iterative inversion and stochastic or truncated density computation. Report the norm constraint, power-iteration accuracy, inverse stopping rule, trace-probe distribution and count, series truncation rule, and resulting numerical error.

    8. Transformer autoregressive flows: TarFlow and successors¤

    TarFlow (Zhai et al., ICML 2025) applies masked autoregressive flow transformations to image patches with Transformer conditioners and alternates the autoregressive direction across blocks. It is a classical likelihood-based normalizing flow, not a flow-matching ODE. The distinction matters operationally:

    • training and density evaluation can obtain all conditional parameters for a block in a masked parallel evaluation;
    • inverse sampling must reveal autoregressive patches or groups sequentially;
    • Gaussian noise augmentation and post-training denoising in the reported image system change how raw likelihood and perceptual sample quality should be interpreted; and
    • "one inverse call" in an API does not mean one parallel neural evaluation.

    STARFlow (Gu et al., 2025 preprint) scales the TarFlow idea in an autoencoder latent space with a deep–shallow design and guidance. It retains a normalizing-flow objective in its continuous modeled space, while the external codec introduces representation loss and a separate data-space semantics. FARMER (Zheng et al., CVPR 2026) is a 2026 autoregressive-flow-over-pixels system; its accepted result is evidence for that complete architecture and protocol, not proof that all Transformer flows dominate other families.

    Worked example: the RealNVP determinant does not differentiate the conditioner¤

    Take \(x=(x_1,x_2)\) and

    \[ y_1=x_1,\qquad y_2=x_2 e^{s(x_1)}+t(x_1). \]

    Its Jacobian is

    \[ J=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0\\ x_2e^{s(x_1)}s'(x_1)+t'(x_1) & e^{s(x_1)} \end{bmatrix}, \]

    so \(\log|\det J|=s(x_1)\). The derivatives \(s'\) and \(t'\) are present in the lower-left block but disappear from the triangular determinant. During optimization, however, \(s(x_1)\) and \(t(x_1)\) still depend on their neural-network parameters, so ordinary backpropagation absolutely does update both conditioners. "No conditioner Jacobian determinant" does not mean "no conditioner gradient."

    If \(s(x_1)=\log 2\) and \(t(x_1)=x_1\) at \(x=(1,3)\), then \(y=(1,7)\) and the forward log-determinant is \(\log 2\). The inverse returns \(x_2=(7-1)/2=3\) and contributes \(-\log2\).

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Architecture design allocates three different costs: map evaluation, inversion, and log-volume computation. Structured affine and scalar maps make the primitive costs visible. Coupling flows buy parallel forward and inverse maps with partial updates; MAF and IAF exchange which direction is sequential; splines improve scalar expressivity; low-rank residual flows reduce determinant dimension but need a separate inverse argument; contractive residual flows obtain that argument while accepting iterative inversion and log-determinant estimation. Transformer flows scale autoregressive conditioners but do not erase their dependency order.

    Training Normalizing Flows: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes a declared data representation and a flow whose forward/inverse conventions have been tested. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive maximum likelihood through a composed inverse map;
    2. use one change-of-variables interface as a model, variational posterior, or Monte Carlo proposal without confusing their objectives;
    3. distinguish conditional density, joint modeling, neural likelihood, and neural posterior estimation;
    4. distinguish continuous density, discrete probability mass, uniform dequantisation, and variational dequantisation;

    Maximum Likelihood Objective¤

    Flow training optimizes the straightforward objective of maximum likelihood:

    \[ \max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log p_\theta(x)] \]

    Equivalently, minimize negative log-likelihood:

    \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}\left[\log p_u(f^{-1}_\theta(x)) + \log \left| \det \frac{\partial f^{-1}_\theta}{\partial x} \right|\right] \]

    This simplicity contrasts sharply with:

    • GANs: Adversarial minimax optimization with mode collapse risks
    • VAEs: ELBO with reconstruction-regularization trade-off
    • Diffusion: Multi-step denoising with noise schedule design

    Training diagnostics

    Gradients flow through the composition by backpropagation, but likelihood need not improve monotonically under minibatch stochastic optimisation. Conditioner saturation, extreme log-scales, ill-conditioned Jacobians, preprocessing, precision, and optimiser settings can all destabilise training. Report the optimiser and schedule, then monitor held-out likelihood, per-layer log-determinants, gradient norms, and inverse error.

    The population objective has a useful statistical interpretation. If \(p_{\text{data}}\) has a density with respect to the same base measure,

    \[ -\mathbb E_{p_{\text{data}}}\log p_\theta(X) =H(p_{\text{data}})+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\|p_\theta). \]

    Thus population maximum likelihood minimizes the forward KL up to a constant. Finite data, misspecification, regularisation, early stopping, preprocessing, and imperfect optimization remain. Forward-KL training penalizes assigning too little density to observed regions, but it does not prove semantic mode coverage for an arbitrary finite model.

    One bijection, three probabilistic roles¤

    A normalizing flow is first a distribution-transform interface. Its role is determined by which random variable is transformed, which context is held fixed, which objective is optimized, and whether a later estimator or Markov transition corrects the learned distribution. Treating “flow model,” “flow posterior,” and “flow proposal” as synonyms hides those choices.

    Fix a context \(c\)—possibly absent—and let

    \[ U\sim r_\lambda(u\mid c),\qquad Y=f_\phi(U;c),\qquad U=g_\phi(Y;c)=f_\phi^{-1}(Y;c). \]

    Assume that, for this fixed \(c\), \(f_\phi\) is a differentiable bijection on compatible supports. The pushforward density is

    \[ q_{\phi,\lambda}(y\mid c) =r_\lambda(g_\phi(y;c)\mid c) \left|\det J_{g_\phi}(y;c)\right|, \]

    or, at a generated point \(y=f_\phi(u;c)\),

    \[ \log q_{\phi,\lambda}(y\mid c) =\log r_\lambda(u\mid c)-\log\left|\det J_{f_\phi}(u;c)\right|. \]

    These are the same change-of-variables identity. What changes across the three roles is the meaning of \(Y\) and the computation surrounding its density.

    The diagram has one mathematical center and three different exits: likelihood defines the learned-model role, an inference objective defines the posterior-approximation role, and a weight or invariant kernel supplies the proposal role's correction.

    Conditional, joint, and simulator-based uses¤

    Conditioning does not require transforming the context. If \(c\) is fixed and \(x=f_c(u)\) is bijective in \(u\), then change of variables defines \(p_\theta(x\mid c)\). This is a normalized conditional density over \(x\) for every declared context, but it is not yet a joint model of \((X,C)\). A joint requires a law for the context, for example

    \[ p_\theta(x,c)=p_\psi(c)\,p_\theta(x\mid c). \]

    Alternatively, a normalized flow density \(p_\theta(x)\) and a normalized predictive head \(p_\psi(c\mid x)\) define \(p_{\theta,\psi}(x,c)=p_\theta(x)p_\psi(c\mid x)\). Hybrid invertible-feature models use this second factorization to share a representation between generative and predictive terms (Nalisnick et al., ICML 2019). The joint is mathematically normalized when both factors are; that fact does not guarantee calibrated classification, useful out-of-distribution ranking, or a good marginal density.

    Flows also serve simulation-based inference when a simulator can draw

    \[ \theta\sim p(\theta),\qquad x\sim p(x\mid\theta) \]

    but cannot evaluate \(p(x\mid\theta)\). Paired simulations support at least two different learned objects:

    • neural likelihood: fit \(q_\phi(x\mid\theta)\), then combine it with the prior and an inference algorithm for \(q_\phi(x_{\mathrm{obs}}\mid\theta)p(\theta)\);
    • neural posterior: fit \(q_\phi(\theta\mid x)\) and draw parameters directly at \(x_{\mathrm{obs}}\).

    Sequential Neural Likelihood uses an autoregressive flow for the first route (Papamakarios et al., AISTATS 2019). Flow-matching posterior estimation uses a conditional continuous flow for the second (Wildberger et al., NeurIPS 2023). These are approximations learned under a simulator, prior, proposal, and simulation budget. Neither invertibility nor low training loss proves posterior calibration under simulator misspecification or a shifted observation; simulation-based calibration, coverage, posterior predictive checks, and sensitivity to the proposal remain necessary.

    A proposal distribution and a transport-preconditioned target are different¤

    There are two related ways to put a learned bijection inside Monte Carlo:

    Direct proposal. Draw \(z=f(u)\) from \(q_\phi\) and form, for an unnormalized target \(\gamma(z)\),

    \[ w(z)=\frac{\gamma(z)}{q_\phi(z)}. \]

    Ordinary or self-normalized importance estimators use these weights; independent Metropolis–Hastings puts the same proposal ratio inside an acceptance probability. Support coverage and moment conditions still matter.

    Transport preconditioner. Pull the target back to base coordinates,

    \[ \gamma_U(u)=\gamma(f_\phi(u))\left|\det J_{f_\phi}(u)\right|, \]

    Run an invariant MCMC kernel targeting \(\gamma_U\), and map retained states forward. Here the Jacobian has a plus sign in \(\log\gamma_U\) because the target density is being pulled back to \(u\); it has a minus sign in \(\log q(f(u))\) because a base density is being pushed forward to \(z\). NeuTra HMC uses this construction. More recent neural-transport parallel tempering likewise keeps an asymptotically valid classical transition around learned transports (Zhang et al., ICLR 2026).

    The flow need not equal the target for the second construction to be valid. A better map may improve geometry and mixing; exact target invariance comes from evaluating the pulled-back target and using a valid transition. Conversely, sending base samples through an approximate map without correction produces samples from \(q_\phi\), not exact samples from the target.

    Worked scalar map: the formula stays fixed while the question changes¤

    Let \(U\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and use the affine bijection

    \[ f_{\mu,\sigma}(u)=\mu+\sigma u,\qquad g_{\mu,\sigma}(y)=\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma},qquad \sigma>0. \]

    Then \(Y\sim\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)\) and

    \[ \log q(y) =-\frac12\left(\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2 -\frac12\log(2\pi)-\log\sigma. \]
    • As a model, set \(y=x\), fit \(\mu,\sigma\) with data log-likelihoods, and generate with \(x=\mu+\sigma u\).
    • As a posterior, let an encoder output \(\mu_\phi(x_{\mathrm{obs}})\) and \(\sigma_\phi(x_{\mathrm{obs}})\), then estimate \(\log p_\theta(x_{\mathrm{obs}},z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x_{\mathrm{obs}})\) at \(z=\mu_\phi+\sigma_\phi u\).
    • As a proposal, draw the same way but evaluate \(\log\gamma(z)-\log q_\phi(z)\) for a weight or acceptance ratio.
    • As an MCMC transport, evaluate \(\log\gamma(\mu+\sigma u)+\log\sigma\) in \(u\)-space. The extra \(+\log\sigma\) is the pullback volume correction.

    “The same map” here means the same mathematical interface, not that one parameter pair should serve every role. The model, posterior, and proposal generally learn different parameters because they solve different distribution-matching problems.

    Failure diagnostic: exact density, unusable coverage¤

    Tractable scoring is a computational property; posterior or proposal quality is a statistical property. Consider the normalized target

    \[ \pi(z)=\tfrac12\mathcal N(z;-a,s^2)+\tfrac12\mathcal N(z;a,s^2) \]

    and the affine-flow approximation \(q(z)=\mathcal N(z;-a,s^2)\). This \(q\) has an exact normalized density, exact samples, full real-line support, and a closed-form inverse. Yet it follows only the left component closely.

    Let \(r(z)=\mathcal N(z;a,s^2)/\mathcal N(z;-a,s^2)\). For importance weights \(w=\pi/q=\tfrac12(1+r)\),

    \[ \mathbb E_q[r]=1, \qquad \mathbb E_q[r^2]=\exp\!\left(\frac{4a^2}{s^2}\right), \qquad \mathbb E_q[w^2]=\frac{3+\exp(4a^2/s^2)}{4}. \]

    Therefore, as the number of independent proposals grows, the usual importance-weight ESS fraction converges to

    \[ \frac{\operatorname{ESS}}{K} \longrightarrow \frac{(\mathbb E_q w)^2}{\mathbb E_q[w^2]} =\frac{4}{3+\exp(4a^2/s^2)}. \]

    At \(a=2\) and \(s=1\), this fraction is about \(4.50\times10^{-7}\). Symmetry gives \(\Pr_\pi(Z>0)=0.5\), whereas \(\Pr_q(Z>0)=1-\Phi(2)\approx0.0228\). The weights are formally valid, but their variance makes the proposal practically ineffective; uncorrected variational samples also give the wrong right-mode probability. An inverse round-trip test and a density-normalization test would both pass.

    Tractable density does not certify an inference method

    Ask three separate questions: Can the density be evaluated? Does the approximation cover the posterior features needed by the query? Does a valid estimator or transition correct the remaining mismatch? A “yes” to the first supplies neither of the other two.

    The query-first implications and Monte Carlo error contracts are developed in Latent Variables and Inference.

    Critical Preprocessing Steps¤

    Proper preprocessing proves essential for successful training:

    1. Dequantization:

    Discrete data (e.g., uint8 images) create delta peaks in continuous space, allowing flows to assign arbitrarily high likelihood to exact discrete values while ignoring intermediate regions.

    # Uniform dequantization
rng, noise_key = jax.random.split(rng)
x_float = x.astype(jnp.float32)
u = jax.random.uniform(noise_key, x.shape, dtype=jnp.float32)
x_dequantized = (x_float + u) / 256.0

# Variational dequantization: q_phi(u | x) must produce u in [0, 1).
u, log_q_u = dequantizer.sample_and_log_prob(x_float, rngs=rngs)
x_dequantized = (x_float + u) / 256.0

    For variational dequantization, the training bound also includes the \(-\log q_\phi(u\mid x)\) correction; generating an unconstrained value called noise is not sufficient.

    For one discrete observation \(x\in\{0,\ldots,255\}^D\), define \(y=(x+u)/256\) with \(u\in[0,1)^D\). The induced discrete mass is

    \[ P_\theta(X=x)=\int_{[0,1)^D}p_Y\!\left(\frac{x+u}{256}\right)256^{-D}\,du. \]

    Uniform dequantisation uses Jensen's inequality to optimize a lower bound on \(\log P_\theta(X=x)\). A learned \(q_\phi(u\mid x)\) produces the tighter variational bound

    \[ \mathbb E_{q_\phi}\!\left[ \log p_Y\!\left(\frac{x+u}{256}\right)-D\log256-\log q_\phi(u\mid x) \right]. \]

    The \(-D\log256\) term is the rescaling Jacobian. Different papers sometimes absorb constants into reported bits/dimension, which is why the exact convention must accompany a number.

    2. Optional logit transform:

    Maps bounded \([0,1]\) data to unbounded \((-\infty, +\infty)\) space matching Gaussian priors:

    # Add small constant for numerical stability
alpha = 0.05
x = alpha + (1 - 2*alpha) * x

# Apply logit transform
x_logit = jnp.log(x) - jnp.log1p(-x)

    Match preprocessing to density semantics

    Dequantisation or a discrete likelihood construction is required before claiming a continuous-density likelihood for discrete observations. A logit transform is optional: bounded-support flows, alternative base distributions, or architectures with an explicit support transform may not need this particular map. Every applied transform and its log-Jacobian must be included consistently in likelihood evaluation.

    Numerical Stability Techniques¤

    1. Log-Space Computation:

    For likelihood code, prefer a signed log-determinant or a structured diagonal sum over forming a determinant and taking its logarithm; the latter loses sign information and is usually less stable:

    # WRONG
det_J = jnp.linalg.det(jacobian)
log_det = jnp.log(det_J)  # Overflow!

# CORRECT
sign, log_det = jnp.linalg.slogdet(jacobian)
# Or for triangular Jacobians:
log_det = jnp.sum(jnp.log(jnp.abs(jnp.diagonal(jacobian))))

    2. Gradient Clipping:

    Prevents exploding gradients in deep architectures (10-15+ layers):

    tx = optax.chain(
    optax.clip_by_global_norm(1.0),
    optax.adam(learning_rate=1e-3),
)

    3. Normalization Layers:

    Batch normalization or ActNorm stabilizes intermediate representations:

    # ActNorm: data-dependent initialization
scale, bias = compute_initial_stats(first_batch)
# Then treat as learnable parameters

    4. Learning Rate Schedules:

    Warmup plus decay is one reasonable experiment, not a flow theorem. Tune the optimizer and schedule against held-out likelihood and numerical diagnostics:

    # Warmup: 2-10 epochs linear increase
# Main training: polynomial decay from 1e-3 to 1e-4

    2. Per-layer log-determinants: Monitor for sudden spikes (numerical issues)

    Common Pitfalls and Solutions¤

    • Missing Preprocessing

      Symptom: Immediate divergence or NaN losses

      Solution: Use a declared dequantisation/discrete-likelihood scheme. Apply a logit transform only when it matches the chosen support construction, and include its Jacobian.

    • Numerical Instability

      Symptom: Sudden spikes in log-determinants or NaN gradients

      Solution: Use log-space computation, gradient clipping, monitor per-layer statistics

    • Poor Invertibility

      Symptom: round-trip error is materially larger than a dtype-, scale-, and depth-matched reference

      Solution: isolate each bijector, compare both map directions, inspect scale parameterization and conditioning, reduce depth if needed, and test higher precision

    • Slow Convergence

      Symptom: Likelihood plateaus early

      Solution: first compare train/validation curves and preprocessing; then ablate capacity, transformation family, optimizer, and regularisation rather than assuming a larger model is the remedy

    Worked example: one uint8 value and the missing constant¤

    Suppose \(x=127\), draw \(u=0.25\), and evaluate a density on \(y=(127.25)/256\). If the continuous model reports \(\log p_Y(y)=-0.4\), the one-sample uniform-dequantisation contribution to the discrete log-mass lower bound is

    \[ -0.4-\log256. \]

    Reporting only \(-0.4\) silently changes the base measure. In \(D\) dimensions the omitted constant is \(D\log256\), or exactly 8 bits per dimension. A subsequent logit transform contributes another observation-dependent log-Jacobian; it cannot be discarded as a dataset-wide constant.

    Counterexample: exact density, wrong evaluation

    A flow can evaluate its modeled continuous density exactly while a benchmark implementation forgets the logit Jacobian. The code still returns deterministic numbers and round trips perfectly, yet the reported data likelihood is wrong. Invertibility tests and likelihood-accounting tests therefore catch different bugs.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    The change-of-variables identity does not determine how a flow is used. A learned model defines its own output density; a variational flow enlarges an approximate-posterior family; and a proposal becomes target-correct only through valid weights or an invariant transition. A conditional density needs another factor to become a joint law, while a simulator-trained likelihood or posterior remains an approximation tied to its prior, proposal, and simulation regime. Maximum likelihood is simple only after the probability space is declared. Dequantisation turns a discrete probability into a bound; preprocessing changes density through its Jacobian; floating-point inversion and estimated log-determinants add numerical error. A credible experiment reports the probabilistic role, objective, correction mechanism, and all three numerical layers.

    Continuous Normalizing Flows (Neural ODEs)¤

    Continuous flows (Chen et al., 2018 — NeurIPS Best Paper; Grathwohl et al., 2019 — FFJORD) parameterize the derivative of the hidden state:

    \[ \frac{d\mathbf{z}_t}{dt} = f(\mathbf{z}_t, t, \theta) \]

    The output \(\mathbf{z}_1\) at time \(t=1\) given initial condition \(\mathbf{z}_0\) at \(t=0\) is computed using ODE solvers.

    Key Innovation (FFJORD):

    The change in log-density follows:

    \[ \frac{d \log p(\mathbf{z}_t)}{dt} = -\text{Tr}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}_t}\right) \]

    Hutchinson's trace estimator makes this tractable:

    \[ \text{Tr}(A) \approx \mathbf{v}^T A \mathbf{v} \quad \text{where } \mathbf{v} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \]

    With a zero-mean probe satisfying \(\mathbb E[vv^\top]=I\), \(v^\top A v\) is an unbiased estimator of \(\operatorname{Tr}(A)\). Automatic differentiation can evaluate it without materializing the full Jacobian. The cost is roughly that of additional vector–Jacobian/Jacobian–vector products through the network, not universally \(O(D)\): network architecture, batching, probe count, and autodiff mode determine actual time and memory, while stochastic variance determines density-estimation error.

    Advantages:

    • Fewer layer-level determinant constraints: the vector field need not be assembled from coupling layers, although it must satisfy the regularity and numerical requirements of the ODE.
    • Topology is still constrained: a standard deterministic ODE flow is a continuous bijection and cannot change connectedness or create a truly sharp discontinuous boundary from a full-support connected base. Augmented dimensions, stochastic dynamics, mixtures, or non-invertible constructions are needed to escape this limitation.
    • Adjoint methods trade memory for recomputation: continuous-adjoint differentiation need not store every solver state, but checkpointing, solver internals, parameters, and numerical mismatch between forward and backward trajectories prevent a universal \(O(1)\) end-to-end memory claim

    Challenges:

    • Unpredictable cost: Number of function evaluations adapts to complexity
    • Stiff dynamics: Can struggle with certain distributions
    • Slower than discrete flows: Requires ODE integration

    When to Use:

    • When a flexible time-dependent vector field is worth numerical integration and divergence-estimation cost
    • Physics and scientific systems where continuous dynamics are a useful inductive bias
    • Density estimation or transport on compatible continuous supports, with augmentation, mixtures, stochasticity, or manifold constructions considered when topology differs

    Flow Matching: Simulation-Free Training¤

    Flow Matching (Lipman, Chen, Ben-Hamu, Nickel & Le, 2023 — ICLR) introduced a paradigm shift for training continuous normalizing flows without ODE simulation.

    Key Idea:

    Rather than integrating forward dynamics during training (as in Neural ODEs), perform regression on the vector field of fixed conditional probability paths.

    Training Procedure:

    1. Given samples \(\mathbf{x}_0 \sim p_0\) and \(\mathbf{x}_1 \sim p_1\)
    2. Define interpolant: \(\mathbf{x}_t = (1-t)\mathbf{x}_0 + t\mathbf{x}_1\)
    3. Train neural network \(\mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\) to match conditional vector field:
    \[ \min_\theta \mathbb{E}_{t, \mathbf{x}_0, \mathbf{x}_1}\left[\|\mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) - (\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_0)\|^2\right] \]

    This common estimator is L2 regression requiring no ODE trajectory simulation inside each training example.

    That statement is about the common training estimator, not the entire system. Sampling still integrates an ODE unless a flow-map or one-step objective is learned, and likelihood evaluation still integrates the divergence. The straight-line target \(x_1-x_0\) is a conditional velocity for a sampled endpoint pair. At an intermediate location \(x\), many pairs may cross, so the population MSE solution is

    \[ v^*(x,t)=\mathbb E[X_1-X_0\mid X_t=x,t]. \]

    Under the usual integrability and regularity assumptions, this conditional expectation is the marginal velocity that satisfies the continuity equation for the induced marginal path. It generally is not the velocity of any particular training pair.

    Inference:

    Integrate the learned field using standard ODE solvers:

    \[ \frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}, t), \quad t \in [0, 1] \]

    Optimal Transport Flow Matching:

    Uses minibatch optimal transport to couple noise and data samples before interpolation. It often reduces conditional displacement/crossing diagnostics in reported settings, but minibatch OT is an approximation and no fixed NFE reduction follows automatically.

    Results:

    • The original paper reports favorable ImageNet likelihood and sample-quality comparisons under its architecture and protocol
    • Later work extends conditional paths and couplings to Riemannian manifolds, discrete data, video, scientific systems, and multimodal state spaces
    • No result establishes a protocol-independent ordering over diffusion, flow matching, or simulation-trained CNFs

    When to Use:

    • Training continuous flows efficiently
    • Want simulation-free gradients
    • Need strong likelihood estimates
    • See Conditional Flow Matching and Optimal-Transport Couplings for OT-CFM, stochastic interpolants, and production considerations.

    Rectified Flows: Learning Straight Trajectories¤

    Rectified Flow (Liu, Gong & Liu, 2023 — ICLR) learns ODEs following straight-line paths connecting source and target distributions.

    Training:

    Given coupling between noise samples \(\mathbf{u} \sim p_0\) and data samples \(\mathbf{x} \sim p_1\):

    1. Linearly interpolate: \(\mathbf{x}_t = (1-t)\mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{x}\)
    2. Learn velocity field: \(\frac{d\mathbf{x}_t}{dt} = \mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\)

    Reflow Process (Key Innovation):

    1. Train initial model
    2. Generate paired samples: \((\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{x}_{\text{gen}})\) where \(\mathbf{x}_{\text{gen}} = \text{model}(\mathbf{u})\)
    3. Retrain on these pairs

    This iterative rectification progressively straightens trajectories.

    Benefits:

    • Provably non-increasing convex transport costs
    • Potential few-step generation: straighter learned trajectories can reduce discretisation error for a fixed solver, but endpoint error and field error remain
    • Reflow is an additional training procedure: the useful number of rounds is empirical and protocol-dependent

    Applications:

    • Stable Diffusion 3: its technical report uses a rectified-flow formulation with an MMDiT backbone and reports complete-system comparisons
    • InstaFlow: reports a distilled/reflowed one-step image system under its hardware and benchmark protocol
    • Few-step generation is a research and deployment target, not a consequence of drawing straight conditional interpolants alone

    When to Use:

    • Experiments targeting few-step or one-step generation with an explicit quality budget.
    • Latency-sensitive applications after end-to-end hardware measurement.
    • Reflow or distillation studies where teacher cost and student supervision are reported.
    • MeanFlow is a distinct one-step-from-scratch objective covered in Mean Flows. TarFlow is also discussed there for conceptual comparison, but it is an autoregressive normalizing flow with sequential inverse sampling—not a MeanFlow-style one-step map.

    Continuous-Time Flows and Foundational Flow Matching: Discrete Flow Matching¤

    Discrete Flow Matching (Gat et al., 2024 — NeurIPS Spotlight; Campbell et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) extends flows to discrete data (text, molecules, code) using Continuous-Time Markov Chains (CTMC).

    Problem:

    Traditional flows designed for continuous data. Dequantization workarounds prove inadequate for inherently discrete data like language.

    Solution:

    Use a CTMC with a time-dependent generator \(Q_t\). For \(x'\ne x\), \(Q_t(x,x')\ge 0\) and each row sums to zero. The forward equation is

    \[ \frac{dp_t(x)}{dt}=\sum_{x'}p_t(x')Q_t(x',x). \]

    Training:

    Choose a conditional probability path and a conditional generator, then train a parameterized generator or equivalent denoising distribution so its population optimum yields the corresponding marginal dynamics. The exact loss may be cross-entropy, rate matching, or a generator-matching objective; a generic Euclidean squared norm over an unconstrained rate matrix is not the definition and can violate non-negativity or zero-row-sum constraints.

    Evidence boundary:

    • Gat et al. and Campbell et al. report text, code, molecule, and protein experiments under different state spaces and transition parameterizations.
    • Dirichlet/Fisher–Rao constructions instead evolve continuous simplex-valued objects and should not be conflated with a finite-state CTMC merely because both target categorical data.
    • Benchmark numbers require the checkpoint, decoding budget, task harness, and baseline protocol; they are not properties of "discrete flow matching" in the abstract.

    When to Use:

    • Text generation (alternative to autoregressive)
    • Molecular generation with discrete atom types
    • Code generation
    • Any discrete structured data

    Geometric Flows: Riemannian Manifolds¤

    Riemannian Flow Matching (Chen & Lipman, 2024) extends flows to non-Euclidean geometries, critical for data on manifolds.

    Applications:

    • Molecular conformations: SE(3) equivariant flows
    • Protein structures: SO(3) rotations and translations
    • Robotic configurations: Configuration space manifolds
    • Materials: crystal generation with periodic, permutation, and lattice constraints

    Key Idea:

    Replace Euclidean straight-line interpolants with geodesics on the manifold:

    \[ \frac{d\mathbf{x}_t}{dt} = \mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) \quad \text{on manifold } \mathcal{M} \]

    When to Use:

    • Data naturally lives on manifolds
    • Symmetries and geometric constraints are important
    • Protein design, molecular generation, materials discovery
    • 3D geometry and robotics applications

    Conditional, Rectified, and Average-Velocity Flow Objectives¤

    The transitions and few-step guide derives exact flow-map and average-velocity identities, then distinguishes their training approximations and NFE accounting.

    Classical work made flows tractable through structured Jacobians; continuous normalizing flows replaced layer stacks with ODEs; flow-matching research develops conditional paths, couplings, flow maps, few-step objectives, discrete generators, and scientific applications. Publication counts from search queries are unstable and taxonomy-dependent, so this section tracks named primary results rather than using paper volume as evidence of scientific importance.

    Conditional Flow Matching and Optimal-Transport Couplings¤

    Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) / Independent CFM (I-CFM) (Tong et al., 2024 — TMLR) generalises Lipman et al.'s flow matching to arbitrary noise–data couplings, and introduces:

    • OT-CFM — uses minibatch optimal-transport couplings instead of independent pairs; the paper reports straighter/easier transports in its settings, but a minibatch assignment is not the population OT coupling and does not guarantee a particular NFE reduction.
    • SB-CFM — a Schrödinger-bridge variant that combines OT with stochastic dynamics.

    These objectives are implemented in the authors' open-source TorchCFM library and are widely used research baselines; adoption is not itself a correctness or performance guarantee.

    Stochastic Interpolants (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Albergo et al., 2024) generalise flow matching with stochastic couplings and bridge ODEs and SDEs in a single framework—the framework that underlies SiT, Multitask Stochastic Interpolants (2026), and related shared objectives.

    Generator Matching (Holderrieth et al., 2024) formulates a broader conditional-to-marginal generator-learning framework covering deterministic flows, diffusions, and jump processes under construction-specific assumptions. "Subsumes" here is a mathematical relationship, not a claim that the same network, loss implementation, or finite-step sampler is interchangeable across state spaces.

    Mean Flows: One-Step Generation Without Distillation¤

    Mean Flows (Geng, Deng, Bai, Kolter & He, 2025 — NeurIPS Oral) replace flow matching's instantaneous velocity field with the average velocity along a trajectory:

    \[ u^{\text{mean}}_\theta(x_t, s, t) \;\approx\; \frac{1}{t - s}\!\int_s^t v(x_\tau, \tau)\, d\tau. \]

    Training uses a closed-form identity between average and instantaneous velocities, requiring no pre-training, no distillation, and no curriculum. The single-network model achieves:

    • FID 3.43 at 1 NFE on ImageNet 256² — trained from scratch (vs. flow-matching teachers needing 25–50 NFE for matching quality).

    Follow-ups include AlphaFlow (ICLR 2026) — an analysis and generalised objective — Improved Mean Flows (CVPR 2026) — a velocity-reparameterized objective with guidance as explicit conditioning variables — and Pixel Mean Flows (2026 preprint) for one-step pixel-space generation. These results demonstrate strong protocol-specific one-step systems. One network evaluation is not "near-zero" total cost: backbone FLOPs, guidance branches, codec decoding, memory traffic, and hardware remain.

    Classifier-Free Guidance for Flow Matching¤

    Standard CFG (Ho & Salimans, 2022) was designed for diffusion models; the 2025 wave adapts it specifically to flow matching:

    • CFG-Zero* (Fan et al., 2025) — observes that early-step velocity estimates undershoot in flow ODEs, and introduces (a) an optimised guidance scale correcting velocity inaccuracies, plus (b) zero-init, which zeroes the first few ODE-solver steps. Consistently outperforms standard CFG on Lumina-Next, SD3, FLUX.1, and Wan-2.1.
    • Flow-Matching Guidance Theory (2024–25) — derives guidance techniques for flow-matching dynamics beyond a direct reuse of diffusion CFG; scope depends on the conditional path and field parameterization.

    Discrete Flow Matching at Scale¤

    Discrete flow matching has matured rapidly:

    • DFM (Gat et al., 2024 — NeurIPS Spotlight) and Multiflow (Campbell et al., 2024) — CTMC-based flows for categorical data; foundation for the modern discrete-flow literature.
    • Fisher Flow (Davis et al., 2024) — a geometric discrete flow that interprets categorical distributions as points on a statistical manifold with the Fisher–Rao metric.
    • DeFoG (Ho et al., ICML 2025) — applies DFM to graph generation and reports benchmark improvements under its molecular-graph protocols.
    • Discrete Flow Matching as AR Post-Training (2025) — studies DFM post-training for autoregressive language models; improvements belong to its models, data, decoding, and evaluation setup.

    These connect to the broader diffusion language model family (LLaDA, Mercury, Dream — see the Diffusion explainer). Some DFM and masked discrete-diffusion constructions admit a common CTMC description after their state space, forward path, and parameterization are aligned; the names alone do not make their finite samplers identical.

    For DeFoG and other graph-valued flows, Random Graphs and Relational Generators supplies the missing object-level contract: node identity, relabeling, graph size, exact constraints, and graph-distribution evaluation.

    For flows used as dependence models, Copula Models distinguishes cube support from the stronger requirement of exactly uniform output marginals. A generic normalized flow on \([0,1]^d\) is not automatically a copula.

    Variance-Reduced Training and Inference-Time Scaling¤

    One line of work attacks the variance of flow-matching gradient estimators and the inference compute budget:

    • Stable Velocity (2026) — a variance-perspective on flow matching that reveals a two-regime structure; introduces StableVM for unbiased variance-reduced training, VA-REPA for selective auxiliary supervision, and StableVS for finetuning-free acceleration.
    • Inference-Time Compute Scaling for Flow Matching (2025) — reports inference-time search/scaling experiments for FM models in protein design and folding; extra compute changes the generation-and-selection system rather than the base sampler alone.
    • An Error Analysis of Flow Matching (NeurIPS 2024) — analyzes how estimation and numerical errors propagate in flow-matching CNFs under stated assumptions.
    • Local Flow Matching (NeurIPS 2024) and Wasserstein Flow Matching (NeurIPS 2024) — generative modelling over families of distributions and over Wasserstein space respectively.

    Flow Map Matching and Consistency Bridges¤

    Flow Map Matching (Boffi et al., 2024) learns a flow map \(\Phi_{s\leftarrow t}(x_t)\) between times on an underlying process. Exact flow maps obey identity and semigroup constraints, \(\Phi_{t\leftarrow t}=I\) and \(\Phi_{r\leftarrow s}\circ\Phi_{s\leftarrow t}=\Phi_{r\leftarrow t}\). Consistency and average-velocity models can be related to particular endpoints or parameterizations, but their finite neural objectives and errors are not interchangeable merely because the ideal maps coincide.

    Worked example: crossing conditional paths and the marginal velocity¤

    Let the source and target both put probability \(1/2\) on \(-1\) and \(+1\), but pair endpoints independently. At \(t=1/2\), the pairs \((-1,+1)\) and \((+1,-1)\) both pass through \(x=0\) with conditional velocities \(+2\) and \(-2\). Squared-error regression at \((x,t)=(0,1/2)\) returns their conditional mean, \(v^*(0,1/2)=0\).

    The network has not recovered either particle's velocity; it has recovered the velocity appropriate to the marginal probability path. With the identity coupling \(X_1=X_0\), every path is stationary and the velocity is zero everywhere. Both couplings have the same endpoint marginals, yet they define different training targets and intermediate paths. Endpoint distributions alone do not determine a flow-matching algorithm.

    Independent practice and assessment map: continuous flows¤

    Keep the conditional path, marginal dynamics, vector-field estimator, density estimator, numerical solver, long-transition model, and non-Euclidean generator distinct. Continuous-time identities do not certify finite computations.

    P1 — Audit a continuous or flow-matching generator end to end¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each claim is necessarily true: Hutchinson estimation makes a CNF density exact; an adaptive solver tolerance is a proof of exact integration; an adjoint method uses constant end-to-end memory; simulation-free training makes sampling simulation-free; straight conditional paths give straight marginal trajectories; common endpoint marginals identify one coupling; one MeanFlow evaluation is an exact ODE solve; and a Euclidean velocity field is a valid CTMC or tangent field. For every false claim, name the remaining estimator, solver, conditioning, or state-space requirement.
    2. P1.2 — Instantaneous change of variables and numerical density. For the affine ODE \(\dot x_t=a(t)x_t+b(t)\) in two dimensions, solve the state and log-density equations analytically for a simple chosen \(a,b\). Derive the divergence formula and estimate its trace with Gaussian and Rademacher probes. Compare exact density with one fixed-step solve and adaptive solves at two tolerances, recording accepted/rejected steps and NFE, then add a stochastic single-probe estimate. Separate discretization bias, tolerance diagnostics, trace variance, and floating-point error.
    3. P1.3 — Conditional-to-marginal regression. Enumerate all four endpoint pairs for independent couplings of \(X_0,X_1\in\{-1,+1\}\). At \(t=1/2\), calculate the conditional velocities and their conditional expectations at every reachable \(X_t\). Repeat with identity and swap couplings. State the integrability/regularity assumptions needed for the population regressor to satisfy the marginal continuity equation.
    4. P1.4 — Long-transition taxonomy. For ordinary flow matching, reflow, a distilled one-step student, flow-map matching, consistency, MeanFlow trained from scratch, and Decoupled MeanFlow, declare the learned target, time arguments, teacher or initialization, identity/semigroup condition, sampling call graph, and error source. Give a counterexample to inferring equal supervision or total cost from equal output-step count.
    5. P1.5 — Replace Euclidean dynamics correctly. Construct a three-state CTMC generator with nonnegative off-diagonal rates and zero row sums, calculate one infinitesimal mass update, and compare it with a simplex-valued Fisher–Rao path. Separately specify a tangent velocity and reference volume for a circle or rotation group. Identify why neither construction is an ordinary Euclidean diffeomorphism over one-hot vectors.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Design a coupled discrete–geometric flow for protein sequence and backbone or atom types and molecular coordinates. Declare endpoint coupling, conditional path or jump generator, equivariance/gauge, marginal target, training estimator, ODE/jump integrator, NFE/events, constraint handling, likelihood semantics, and a small state-space or harmonic test that can falsify normalization and endpoint claims.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Continuous flows move tractability from layer determinants to differential equations. Flow matching avoids trajectory simulation in the common training estimator, not at sampling or likelihood time. Couplings determine conditional paths; regression marginalizes crossings; solvers approximate learned dynamics; flow-map and MeanFlow objectives learn longer transitions with different supervision. Discrete and geometric variants require their own generators, measures, and integrators.

    2. What is the population MSE predictor for a conditional flow target?
    3. Does a straight conditional interpolant imply a straight marginal ODE trajectory?
    4. Why are MeanFlow, Decoupled MeanFlow, and a distilled one-step flow not one method?
    5. What must a finite-state CTMC generator satisfy?

    Flows vs Diffusion: Speed vs Quality with Converging Trajectories¤

    Historical Trade-off:

    • Diffusion: historically strong perceptual results with iterative samplers ranging from very few to many evaluations
    • Classical flows: direct invertible sampling, with coupling and autoregressive architectures having different sequential depth; early image systems often trailed perceptual generators under their protocols

    Additional objectives modify the trade-off:

    1. Rectified flows (Liu et al., 2023) — straight paths enabling few-step generation; powers SD3 and FLUX.1.
    2. Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023) — simulation-free vector-field regression for chosen paths, with precise correspondences to some diffusion probability paths.
    3. TarFlow (Zhai et al., ICML 2025) — a Transformer-based masked autoregressive normalizing flow with strong reported likelihood and sample-quality results. Its inverse generates patches sequentially; it is neither flow matching nor a one-NFE parallel sampler.
    4. Mean Flows (Geng et al., NeurIPS 2025 Oral) — FID 3.43 at 1 NFE on ImageNet 256² with no distillation, no curriculum.
    5. CFG-Zero* (Fan et al., 2025) — flow-specific guidance closing the conditional-generation gap with diffusion.

    Sample Quality (Flow Matching / TarFlow / Mean Flows)¤

    Modern flow-matching DiTs are evaluated on the same metrics as diffusion models:

    • FID (Heusel et al., 2017) — Fréchet Inception Distance.
    • CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024) — CLIP-feature MMD; better correlated with human perception.
    • CLIPScore — alignment for text-conditional generation.
    • Inception Score (IS) — legacy class-conditional metric.

    Reference results from Conditional, Rectified, and Average-Velocity Flow Objectives must retain their sampler context: TarFlow reports diffusion-comparable image quality from a Transformer MAF but samples autoregressively; MeanFlow reports FID 3.43 at one network evaluation on ImageNet 256² in its evaluated class-conditional setting. Cross-paper numbers are not rankings unless data, conditioning, guidance, compute, and metric implementations match.

    Test Quantities Specific to Flows¤

    • Round-trip error — test both map directions over representative and adversarial scales with dtype-aware absolute/relative tolerances; passing this test does not validate the log-determinant.
    • Log-determinant oracle — on small dimensions, compare the analytic result with slogdet(jacfwd(f)(x)) or finite differences. Test signs and batch reductions separately.
    • Per-layer log-determinants and condition estimates — shifts, heavy tails, or non-finite values can precede training failure, but thresholds are model-specific.
    • CNF density consistency — tighten solver tolerances, vary trace probes, and compare forward/reverse integration on a low-dimensional analytic system.
    • Path geometry — report kinetic energy, curvature/discretisation diagnostics, and endpoint quality together. Constant speed alone does not prove a correct transport.

    Memory and Quantisation¤

    • Classical flows need the activations required by their ordinary backward pass and whatever statistics compute the structured log-determinant; they do not generally materialize or store a full Jacobian. Mixed precision safety depends on scales, inverses, LU/triangular operations, and accumulation dtype.
    • Post-training integer quantisation can break inverse consistency or bias log-determinants, but it is not categorically impossible. Compare density, round trips, condition numbers, and downstream decisions with a higher-precision reference.
    • Flow-matching DiTs often use diffusion-style mixed precision and quantization, but no format is universally safe. Validate attention, normalization, time conditioning, vector-field error, codec error, and complete trajectories.

    Common Production Issues¤

    • Drift on out-of-distribution inputs — classical flows assign well-defined likelihoods to OOD inputs but those likelihoods can be higher than in-distribution ones (Nalisnick et al., 2019) — a counter-intuitive failure mode that complicates OOD detection from \(\log p_\theta\) alone.
    • Numerical reversibility — long bijector stacks and ODE solves accumulate error. Choose tolerances in the modeled space from dtype, scale, and downstream sensitivity; there is no universal pixel-space threshold.
    • Adaptive ODE solvers can raise NFE on stiff trajectories. Fixed-step Euler or higher-order Runge–Kutta/multistep methods offer predictable budgets, but solver suitability depends on the vector field and time parameterization; a diffusion solver name is not automatically valid for every flow ODE.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    Classical normalizing flows combine a tractable change-of-variables density with direct sampling and inversion. Their speed and optimisation behavior depend on the transformation family; continuous and flow-matching models make different numerical trade-offs.

    Core Principles¤

    • Exact Likelihood

      Tractable classical bijections provide an exact model-density expression; discrete-data bounds, CNF integration, and floating-point evaluation carry additional qualifications

    • Invertible Architecture

      Bijective transformations allow direct sampling and latent inversion; arbitrary conditional inference can still be hard

    • Tractable Jacobians

      Structured Jacobians (triangular, diagonal) reduce complexity from \(O(D^3)\) to \(O(D)\)

    • Stable Training

      Maximum likelihood and vector-field regression avoid a minimax game, but stochastic optimization, conditioning, stiffness, and numerical estimators can still fail

    • Composable Design

      Stack transformations to enlarge expressivity within support, topology, architecture, and finite-capacity constraints

    When to Use Normalizing Flows¤

    Best Use Cases:

    • A tractable normalized model density is essential, with the preprocessing and anomaly/model-selection semantics independently validated
    • A measured coupling-flow inverse, autoregressive-flow schedule, or few-step flow map satisfies the task's latency/quality budget
    • A non-adversarial maximum-likelihood objective is preferred and its optimisation diagnostics are acceptable
    • Numerically controlled invertibility or a separate lossless coding construction is needed
    • Mode coverage can be measured under maximum-likelihood training and the chosen architecture; it is not guaranteed by invertibility

    Prefer another or hybrid construction when:

    • the desired support changes dimension or topology and an ordinary bijection would be badly mismatched;
    • compressed or lossy representation learning is central, in which case a codec/latent-variable model should be explicit;
    • exact density is unnecessary and its architectural/numerical cost loses under controlled quality–latency comparison; or
    • a discrete, graph, manifold, or simulator state space calls for a generator native to that geometry rather than forced Euclidean dequantisation.

    Cumulative research exercise: design a molecular conformation generator¤

    Assume each example contains atom types, bond graph, and 3D coordinates, and the goal is conditional generation plus calibrated free-energy reweighting.

    1. State space: atom types and bonds are discrete; coordinates live modulo translation and rotation, with chirality and collisions carrying physical meaning.
    2. Candidate construction: use a discrete jump generator for types/edges and an equivariant manifold/Euclidean vector field for coordinates, coupled through a general Markov or multimodal generator. An ordinary \(\mathbb R^D\) flow over padded one-hot vectors is a baseline, not a semantics-preserving default.
    3. Training evidence: specify endpoint coupling, conditional paths, equivariant architecture, energy or data supervision, and whether training samples are equilibrium-distributed.
    4. Sampling evidence: report jump/ODE solver, NFE or events, constraint projection, rejection, temperature, wall-clock, and effective sample size after any reweighting.
    5. Evaluation: separate validity, uniqueness, coverage, geometry, conditional accuracy, energy calibration, and held-out system generalization. A lower vector-field loss does not establish correct thermodynamics.

    This exercise forces the curriculum's central discipline: choose the probability object and state space first, then the objective and numerical algorithm, and only then interpret a benchmark.

    Worked synthesis: a constrained simulator posterior¤

    Suppose a simulator produces an observation \(Y\) from a positive reaction rate \(\lambda>0\) and an efficiency \(\eta\in(0,1)\). The query is the calibrated posterior over \((\lambda,\eta)\) for a new observation, not unconditional data generation. Define \(u_1=\log\lambda\) and \(u_2=\operatorname{logit}\eta\). The density in scientific coordinates is

    \[ q_\phi(\lambda,\eta\mid y) =\frac{q_\phi(\log\lambda,\operatorname{logit}\eta\mid y)} {\lambda\,\eta(1-\eta)}. \]

    A defensible first system makes each layer of that claim explicit.

    1. Coordinates and measure. A conditional spline or autoregressive flow models \(q_\phi(u\mid y)\) with respect to Lebesgue measure in transformed coordinates. The formula above applies the coordinate Jacobian. Omitting it changes the density and any region probability computed in the original units.
    2. Training object. Draw \((\lambda,\eta)\) from the declared prior, run the simulator, and fit \(q_\phi(u\mid y)\) by conditional maximum likelihood on transformed parameters. This is neural posterior estimation under that prior and simulator. Invertibility normalizes the learned approximation; it does not make it the exact simulator posterior.
    3. Architecture decision. Begin with a small conditional spline flow because the posterior is two-dimensional and bounded transforms are explicit. A coupling stack, MAF, or flow-matching posterior is an alternative to compare, not an automatic upgrade. Conditioner capacity and transform family are ablated separately.
    4. Analytic and numerical validation. Test both map directions, compare the analytic log-determinant with automatic differentiation at interior and near-boundary points, evaluate normalization by quadrature for selected observations, and repeat in higher precision. Boundary failures in the logit transform are not repaired by a perfect inner bijector.
    5. Statistical validation. Run simulation-based calibration ranks, prior-predictive and posterior-predictive checks, conditional coverage, and tests at shifted observations. If the simulator likelihood is unavailable, the learned posterior cannot simply be importance-corrected with an exact likelihood ratio; that limitation must remain in the claim.

    The worked decision separates a normalized conditional approximation from an exact posterior, and it makes coordinate, numerical, and statistical evidence independently falsifiable.

    Final synthesis¤

    "Flow" names a set of related but nonidentical constructions. Classical normalizing flows normalize through an explicit bijection and Jacobian. CNFs normalize through invertible dynamics and an integrated divergence. Flow matching trains a vector field for a chosen probability path without solving the ODE in the common training estimator. Rectification changes couplings or learned trajectories; flow maps and MeanFlow learn longer transitions; discrete and geometric flows replace Euclidean calculus with state-appropriate generators. The relationships are powerful precisely when their assumptions remain visible.

    Solutions. (1) In the generative direction \(x=f(z)\), add base log-density and subtract every forward log-absolute-determinant; equivalently add inverse-map log-determinants at the observation. (3) MAF parallelizes conditional-parameter evaluation for density but samples sequentially; IAF reverses the dependency direction so base-to-sample is parallel while observation-to-base is sequential, with arithmetic still scaling with model and dimension. Dynamical: score-path and velocity-path conversions under a declared Gaussian path. Hybrid: a latent codec plus flow-matching prior. (6) Dataset/split, conditioning, representation, checkpoint, guidance, metric implementation/sample count, total FLOPs and sequential evaluations, codec/post-processing, hardware wall-clock, training data/compute, and reproduction status.
