    Data Governance, Privacy, and Provenance¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Evaluating Generative Models
    • Representations and Multimodal Generative Systems

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Compute Accounting and Controlled Comparisons

    Generative systems inherit the rights, errors, duplication, private information, synthetic artifacts, and security weaknesses of their data pipeline. Data governance is therefore part of model design and scientific validity, not a paperwork step after training.

    This volume covers dataset lineage, consent, leakage, benchmark contamination, memorization, extraction and membership attacks, differential privacy, provenance standards, watermark limits, licensing, deletion/unlearning, incident response, and the supporting research evidence. It provides engineering and research guidance, not legal advice; obligations depend on jurisdiction, facts, licenses, and deployment.

    Legal and policy scope

    Regulatory summaries are verification pointers, not a substitute for counsel. Record the jurisdiction and verification date, and re-check official sources before release.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2 for lineage, splits, and contamination.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–5 plus the release checklist.
    Research: all chapters, including privacy threat models, DP, unlearning, provenance limits, and evidence appraisal.

    1. Data Lineage and Lifecycle¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes a basic model-development lifecycle. By the end, you should be able to trace data from acquisition through transformation, training, evaluation, release, and retirement; distinguish dataset, record, source, and derived-artifact identities; construct a lineage manifest with accountable versions; and identify governance obligations that survive deduplication or format conversion.

    A dataset is a versioned process¤

    A dataset release is not just a directory. It is the result of collection, filtering, normalization, annotation, deduplication, splitting, and packaging:

    Reproducibility needs immutable source snapshots or content hashes, acquisition time, query/crawl configuration, code version, filter statistics, annotation provenance, split assignment, license/consent fields, removals, and parent/derived relationships.

    Unit of provenance¤

    Track rights and provenance at the smallest usable asset level—not only the dataset name. A collection can mix public-domain, permissively licensed, contract-restricted, personal, synthetic, and unknown-origin records. Batch-level labels erase exceptions.

    A useful record includes:

    Table GOV.1. Unit of provenance
    Field Example meaning
    source URI/identifier where the asset was obtained
    acquisition timestamp which mutable version was seen
    cryptographic hash exact-content identity
    perceptual/semantic fingerprint near-duplicate family
    creator/rightsholder fields asserted attribution, not verified ownership by default
    license/terms snapshot text/version and applicable scope
    consent/purpose allowed collection/use context
    transformations crop, caption, translation, codec, synthetic augmentation
    lineage parents inputs used to derive the asset
    removal/restriction status tombstone and downstream propagation

    Data documentation¤

    Datasheets for Datasets motivates structured documentation of composition, collection, preprocessing, uses, distribution, and maintenance. A generative-data card should additionally report duplication, synthetic-content detection, personal/sensitive attributes, opt-out/removal process, benchmark overlap, modality alignment, caption origin, and known generator contamination.

    Consent, purpose, and minimization¤

    Availability is not consent, and consent for one use may not cover model training or redistribution. Collect only fields needed for the declared purpose, limit retention, isolate high-risk data, and preserve a path for correction/removal. “Public on the web” describes access, not privacy expectation, license, ethical suitability, or lawful basis.

    Derived and synthetic data¤

    Synthetic data retains lineage to source models, prompts, retrieval context, reference media, filters, and licenses. It can reproduce private/copyrighted material, amplify bias, or leak benchmark answers. Label generator checkpoint, sampling parameters, date, postprocessing, and source/reference inputs.

    Training recursively on generated outputs can reduce diversity or homogenize models depending on mixture and process. Recursive cross-model training, ICLR 2026, is Peer-reviewed research evidence in its studied regimes—not a theorem that all synthetic data is harmful or beneficial.

    Worked lineage failure¤

    A video clip is cropped from a licensed film, auto-captioned by a model, translated, and placed in a “synthetic captions” dataset. Calling the row synthetic hides that the visual asset and caption semantics derive from protected source material and another model. The lineage graph must preserve every parent and transformation.

    Retrieval question: why is a dataset-level license insufficient?

    Collections can contain mixed or conflicting asset rights and exceptions. Compliance, attribution, deletion, and redistribution decisions often require record-level lineage.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Governance begins with a versioned lineage graph. Chapter 2 shows how duplication and cross-split overlap can invalidate both privacy and evaluation claims.

    2. Leakage, Contamination, and Deduplication¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and standard train/evaluation splits. By the end, you should be able to distinguish exact, near-duplicate, semantic, temporal, and entity leakage; select split units that match the generalization claim; explain false-positive and false-negative costs in deduplication; and design contamination audits spanning pretraining, post-training, retrieval, benchmarks, and generated evaluation data.

    Different overlap problems¤

    • Exact duplicate: identical bytes or normalized text.
    • Near duplicate: resized/cropped/re-encoded image, paraphrase, template variant, clip overlap.
    • Semantic duplicate: same fact/problem/scene with different surface form.
    • Entity leakage: the same person, patient, molecule scaffold, repository, or video appears across splits.
    • Temporal leakage: future information enters training or preprocessing.
    • Benchmark contamination: test questions, solutions, labels, or derived discussions enter training.
    • Evaluator leakage: the judge/embedding model was trained on the benchmark or generator outputs.

    Each needs different detection. Hashing catches exact identity; MinHash/suffix arrays catch long text overlap; perceptual hashes and learned embeddings catch media similarity; entity/time/source-group splits address shared causes.

    Duplicates reweight the objective¤

    Maximum likelihood on a multiset uses empirical probability

    \[ \hat p_D(x)=\frac{count_D(x)}{|D|}. \]

    Repeating one record \(k\) times multiplies its contribution. Duplicates therefore change the intended distribution, gradient frequency, memorization risk, and train/test independence. They are not merely wasted storage.

    Deduplicating language-model data found reduced verbatim emission and more reliable validation in its study. Kandpal et al. connect duplication to extraction risk. Deduplication mitigates one risk factor; unique rare records can still be memorized.

    Split before or after augmentation?¤

    Group original entities/sources first, then derive augmentations within their assigned split. Randomly splitting crops, frames, translations, or paraphrases lets sibling derivatives cross the boundary. For time series, split chronologically before windows; overlapping windows can share almost all content.

    Benchmark contamination audit¤

    Maintain a registry of test assets, normalized variants, canaries, publication dates, and likely web derivatives. Audit:

    1. exact/n-gram overlap;
    2. paraphrased questions and answer rationales;
    3. source documents from which tests were derived;
    4. code repositories and unit tests;
    5. benchmark discussion pages and model-generated solutions;
    6. post-training/RL and retrieval corpora, not only pretraining.

    Contamination detection has false positives and false negatives. Report thresholds, examples, coverage, and an uncontaminated/private or newly collected confirmation set.

    Data leakage through preprocessing¤

    Fitting a tokenizer, normalizer, feature selector, codec, imputer, or dedup index on all data can leak evaluation distribution. Unsupervised does not mean harmless. Fit learned preprocessing on training data unless the protocol explicitly studies transductive access.

    Worked video split¤

    One 10-minute video is cut into overlapping 5-second clips every second, then randomly split. Adjacent train/test clips share 80% of frames. A generator can retrieve background/actors and appear to generalize. Assign the entire source video—or higher-level event/person—to one split before clipping.

    Contamination-aware reporting¤

    Report clean and flagged subsets, not only one corrected score. State whether contaminated examples were removed before any model choice. If discovered after tuning, a new confirmatory set is preferable to retroactively redefining the same test.

    Retrieval question: why can semantic deduplication harm data?

    A threshold can collapse legitimate rare variants, minority dialects, or distinct scientific cases. Audit deletion rates by subgroup/source and preserve a reversible mapping rather than assuming every nearby embedding is redundant.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Overlap changes training weights, privacy, and independence. Chapter 3 formalizes the privacy threats that remain even after careful deduplication.

    3. Memorization, Privacy Attacks, and Differential Privacy¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes probability, optimization, and the lineage vocabulary. By the end, you should be able to distinguish memorization from extractability and privacy harm; formulate membership, attribute, and extraction threat models; interpret differential-privacy parameters under composition and unit choice; and design red-team and utility evaluations without treating a failed attack as proof of privacy.

    Memorization is not one quantity¤

    Distinguish:

    • eidetic/verbatim memorization: reproducing a training sequence or image;
    • exposure: ranking a planted canary unusually highly;
    • membership leakage: revealing whether a record was in training;
    • attribute/property inference: revealing sensitive attributes or aggregate dataset properties;
    • training-data extraction: recovering content via queries/optimization;
    • general factual retention: learning information that may also be derivable elsewhere.

    Generalization and memorization can coexist. The privacy question is whether an adversary learns information about protected records beyond the allowed output.

    Threat model first¤

    Specify adversary goal, candidate knowledge, model access (samples, probabilities/logits, gradients, weights), query budget, auxiliary data, and success metric. A white-box attack and a black-box prompt-only attack support different conclusions.

    Membership inference asks whether a target record was in training. LLM extraction and diffusion extraction demonstrate concrete recovery attacks in studied models. Their attack yields and models should not be universalized, but “a generator never stores examples” is empirically false.

    Differential privacy¤

    A randomized training mechanism \(M\) is \((\epsilon,\delta)\)-differentially private if for neighboring datasets \(D,D'\) differing in one protected unit and any event \(S\),

    \[ P(M(D)\in S)\le e^\epsilon P(M(D')\in S)+\delta. \]

    The neighboring unit—record, person, user, patient, document—must match the privacy claim. Smaller \(\epsilon,\delta\) generally mean stronger protection, but interpretation requires composition, sampling assumptions, and threat scope.

    DP-SGD clips each per-unit gradient to norm \(C\), aggregates, and adds Gaussian noise. A privacy accountant composes steps. Clipping batches instead of per-example/user does not provide the same guarantee. Hyperparameter selection, preprocessing, checkpoints, and repeated releases can consume privacy budget if data dependent.

    What DP does and does not do¤

    DP bounds how much one unit changes the released model distribution. It does not:

    • make the data lawful, licensed, unbiased, or accurate;
    • hide population facts shared by many people;
    • protect non-private pretraining or external retrieval stores;
    • guarantee zero memorization for any finite \(\epsilon,\delta\);
    • automatically protect groups when adjacency is individual-record based.

    Empirical attacks are useful audits but failure to attack is not a proof of privacy. A correctly scoped DP guarantee is stronger but may reduce utility and increase compute.

    Privacy engineering layers¤

    Use minimization and access control before training; deduplicate and redact high-risk identifiers; isolate secrets from general corpora; consider DP at the correct unit; restrict output/logit access; rate-limit and monitor extraction; filter outputs carefully; and maintain incident/removal paths. Filters are defense in depth, not removal from weights.

    Worked user-level adjacency¤

    If one user contributes 1,000 messages, record-level DP treats removing one message as neighboring. A user-level deletion changes all 1,000, so the claimed bound may compose poorly or not apply. Clip/aggregate per user and account with user-level sampling when the promise is “protect any user's participation.”

    Retrieval question: why does a failed extraction audit not prove privacy?

    It only shows that one adversary, budget, candidate set, and attack failed. Stronger attacks or auxiliary information may succeed; formal privacy requires a correctly scoped guarantee.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Privacy requires a threat model and, where promised, a formal unit-level guarantee. Chapter 4 turns from hidden training data to the provenance of generated outputs.

    4. Output Provenance, Watermarks, and Authenticity¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes cryptographic-signature and detection concepts at an introductory level. By the end, you should be able to distinguish signed provenance, embedded watermarks, statistical detectors, and content authenticity; enumerate their trust and robustness assumptions; construct an appropriate metadata record; and test editing, laundering, false-attribution, privacy, and accessibility failure modes.

    Provenance versus detection¤

    Provenance records an asset's asserted history. Detection infers whether content was generated or manipulated from the content itself. They fail differently:

    • provenance may be absent, stripped, forged by a compromised signer, or contain misleading assertions;
    • detectors drift across generators, codecs, edits, languages, and adversaries and can create false accusations.

    Use both as signals, never as an infallible oracle.

    C2PA Content Credentials¤

    The current C2PA 2.4 specification defines signed manifests, assertions, ingredient relationships, hard/soft bindings, validation, and trust lists. Cryptographic validation can show that a signed assertion is bound to content and has not been altered under the trust model.

    It does not prove the assertion is truthful, the signer is benevolent, the depicted event occurred, or unsigned content is synthetic. C2PA's own principles separate verifiability from value judgments. Preserve credentials through transformations, expose validation state and signer, and do not collapse “valid signature” into “true content.”

    Hard and soft bindings¤

    A cryptographic hash is a hard binding to exact bytes/defined asset regions and breaks under changes unless a new derived manifest is added. Perceptual fingerprints and invisible watermarks can provide soft recovery after transformations but permit collisions and adversarial removal/transfer. Record which binding established the match and its confidence.

    Watermark threat model¤

    Watermarks may be embedded in pixels/audio, latent features, or token sampling. Evaluate:

    1. detectability at a fixed false-positive rate;
    2. robustness to crop, compression, paraphrase, translation, re-recording, regeneration;
    3. quality/utility cost;
    4. key compromise and spoofing;
    5. collusion/model extraction;
    6. accessibility and privacy harms;
    7. detectability after mixed human/AI editing.

    Statistical watermark detection is a hypothesis test, not certain attribution. Publish thresholds and confidence, and provide appeal/review paths for consequential uses.

    Lineage graph for generated media¤

    Record model/version, organization/system signer, prompt disclosure according to privacy policy, random seed where appropriate, input/reference asset credentials, retrieval sources, editing actions, filters, and export transformations. Avoid embedding private prompts or identities by default; provenance itself can leak sensitive workflow information.

    Worked signature misconception¤

    An editor signs a manifest asserting “camera original” for a generated image. Signature validation succeeds because the manifest is intact and the signer key is trusted. The content claim is still false. Provenance authenticates the assertion's origin/integrity, not reality.

    Retrieval question: what does a valid Content Credential establish?

    Under its trust model, it establishes integrity/binding and signer-associated assertions. The consumer must still judge assertion truth, signer trust, missing history, and context.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Provenance supplies tamper-evident history, not automated truth. Chapter 5 addresses whether assets may be collected, transformed, trained on, redistributed, or generated under their rights and obligations.

    5. Licensing, Data Rights, and Release Governance¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the lifecycle and provenance chapters but not legal expertise. By the end, you should be able to maintain a jurisdiction- and use- specific rights ledger without presenting it as universal legal advice; distinguish source licenses, contractual permissions, privacy/biometric duties, and downstream release terms; define escalation and opt-out workflows; and make model/data release decisions from documented risk rather than dataset labels.

    Separate rights and actions¤

    For every asset/model identify the proposed action:

    • access/crawl;
    • copy/store;
    • transform/annotate;
    • train or fine-tune;
    • distribute dataset or embeddings;
    • release weights;
    • generate and commercialize outputs;
    • provide attribution or source summary;
    • honor removal/opt-out/reservation.

    A permission for one action does not imply all others. Dataset license, website terms, copyright, database rights, privacy/data-protection law, publicity/personality rights, contracts, and research ethics can overlap.

    Public, open, and permissive are different¤

    “Publicly accessible” says nothing about license. “Open data” should name an actual license and version. A code license may not cover bundled media/data; a dataset license may not cover individual assets or personal data. Record provenance and license text at acquisition because web terms can change.

    License compatibility matters when combining sources or redistributing derivatives. Attribution, notice, share-alike, noncommercial, no-derivatives, field-of-use, and database provisions need asset- and jurisdiction-specific review. Do not invent a universal “AI training permitted” label from incomplete metadata.

    Current regulatory pointers¤

    As verified 2026-07-11:

    • The EU AI Act includes obligations for providers of general-purpose AI models concerning an EU-copyright-compliance policy and a sufficiently detailed public summary of training content, with scope/exceptions defined in the Regulation. Check current Commission/AI Office implementation materials for applicability.
    • The European Commission describes GDPR rights including access, correction, restriction, and a non-absolute right to erasure. Translating a valid request into datasets, checkpoints, adapters, caches, logs, and retraining/unlearning requires a documented process.
    • The U.S. Copyright Office's AI study published a May 2025 pre-publication Part 3 on generative-AI training and notes a final version is forthcoming. It is analysis, not a substitute for current statutes, cases, contracts, or counsel.

    Do not copy these summaries into a release decision without checking dates and facts.

    Governance gates¤

    Before training/release require named owners for data, privacy, security, legal, model behavior, and incident response. Record unresolved sources and risk acceptance. Block release when provenance is unknown beyond policy, deletion requests cannot propagate, evaluation data is contaminated, required notices are absent, or licenses conflict with distribution.

    Transparency without new harm¤

    Publish source categories, major datasets, collection periods, filters, rights policy, synthetic proportions, opt-out/removal process, and limitations at the level required and safe. Do not publish personal identifiers, exploitable extraction recipes for live victims, trade secrets, or security-sensitive exact filters merely to appear transparent.

    Worked mixed-license collection¤

    A corpus combines CC BY media, noncommercial assets, public-domain works, scraped unknown-license images, and private user uploads. Labeling the bundle “research only” does not harmonize rights. Partition by permitted use, preserve attribution, exclude unresolved/high-risk data, and obtain review before training or redistribution.

    Retrieval question: why does deleting a source file not finish a removal request?

    Derived copies may exist in shards, caches, embeddings, captions, checkpoints, adapters, synthetic derivatives, backups, and downstream distributions. The applicable obligation and feasible remedy require lineage and legal/process review.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Rights attach to sources, people, actions, and jurisdictions. Chapter 6 integrates removal/unlearning, audits, incidents, and research evidence into an operational maintenance loop.

    6. Unlearning, Audits, Incidents, and Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes Chapters 1–5 and basic security incident response. By the end, you should be able to choose among source deletion, retrieval removal, filtering, targeted updates, approximate unlearning, checkpoint replacement, and retraining; specify an unlearning reference and acceptance test; plan recurring audits; and execute an incident record that preserves evidence, affected-scope analysis, communication, remediation, and follow-up evaluation.

    Removal hierarchy¤

    Possible responses include source deletion for future runs, retrieval/index deletion, output blocking, targeted fine-tuning/editing, approximate machine unlearning, adapter/checkpoint replacement, or full retraining. These have different guarantees. “Unlearned” must state the target, access model, reference retraining procedure, and acceptance tests.

    Evaluate at least:

    1. forgetting on direct and paraphrased/extraction attacks;
    2. retain-set utility;
    3. neighboring/related knowledge damage;
    4. ability to use the forgotten fact when supplied in context;
    5. privacy/membership risk;
    6. robustness after fine-tuning or model merging;
    7. comparison with retraining without the data.

    Context-aware unlearning, ICML 2026, is Peer-reviewed research evidence that methods can erase parametric access while inadvertently damaging contextual use, and proposes a mitigation in its experiments. It does not establish exact deletion from all model behavior.

    Audit schedule¤

    At dataset freeze, pretraining checkpoints, pre-release, and post-incident, run:

    • lineage/license completeness and unresolved-source counts;
    • exact/near/semantic duplication and benchmark contamination;
    • PII/sensitive-data scans with manual validation;
    • extraction, membership, and canary audits under declared budgets;
    • subgroup representation and harm analysis;
    • synthetic-content/source-model proportions;
    • provenance credential validation/stripping tests;
    • removal propagation and disaster-recovery exercises.

    Incident response¤

    Preserve evidence, restrict access, identify affected data/models/outputs through lineage, assess severity and legal notification duties, deploy containment, notify appropriate owners/users, remediate, validate, and publish a bounded postmortem where safe. Do not destroy logs needed for investigation; apply retention/access policy.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • Natural Identifiers for Privacy and Data Audits, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for auditing with seeded natural identifier strings and for clarifying held-out-data requirements in dataset inference; canaries/identifiers change the audit setting.
    • Train Once, Answer All, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for multiplexing controlled pretraining experiments on contamination, poisoning, memorization, and related questions, with interaction testing in its setup.
    • Recursive Cross-Model Training, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for studying benefits and homogenization when models ingest other models' outputs under reported mixtures.
    • Context-Aware Unlearning, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for the distinction between parametric forgetting and contextual utility.
    • ZeroUnlearn, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for targeted few-shot knowledge remapping in reported models; editing success is not a formal privacy or exact-deletion guarantee.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Build a record-level lineage manifest and propagate one deletion through derived assets.
    2. Compare exact, MinHash, perceptual, and embedding deduplication; audit subgroup deletion rates.
    3. Plant user-level canaries and compare exposure, membership, and extraction metrics.
    4. Implement record- versus user-level DP accounting on a toy corpus.
    5. Sign and validate a C2PA 2.4 derived-asset chain, then test stripping and a false assertion.
    6. Evaluate an unlearning method against retraining, paraphrases, extraction, contextual use, and model merging.
    7. Create an incident tabletop exercise from leaked private output to checkpoint replacement.
    Exercise 5 solution criterion

    A successful exercise distinguishes three outcomes: cryptographic validation of the signed manifest, continuity of the ingredient/edit chain, and independent truth assessment of assertions. Stripping should produce unknown/absent provenance—not a verdict that the asset is fake.

    Cumulative retrieval: governance and provenance
    1. Why must a lineage graph preserve source identity after records are transformed or deduplicated?
    2. What threat-model statement is required before interpreting a privacy attack or defense?
    3. Which provenance mechanism can prove who signed a claim without proving that the depicted event is true?
    4. What evidence is needed before calling a model update “unlearning”?

    Solutions. (1) Rights, consent, quality, deletion, and incident obligations attach to sources and transformations, not only final bytes. (2) Adversary access, target unit, auxiliary knowledge, success metric, baseline, and population. (3) A cryptographically signed provenance record; it authenticates the signer and metadata, not real-world truth. (4) A declared removal target and access model, a reference such as retraining without the target, retained-utility tests, attack/query evaluations, uncertainty, and limitations on the guarantee.

    Final synthesis¤

    Trustworthy generative modeling requires traceable sources, independent splits, explicit privacy units, attack-aware testing, formal guarantees where promised, tamper-evident but non-oracular provenance, asset-level rights, removal paths, and maintained audits. Governance is a living system because datasets, laws, standards, attacks, and downstream distributions change.
