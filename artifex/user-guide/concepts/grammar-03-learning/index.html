    3. Estimation, Neural Parameterization, and Grammar Induction¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes likelihood, EM, and variational inference. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Estimate observed-tree rules: derive locally normalized maximum- likelihood or smoothed rule probabilities from declared counts.
    2. O2 — Perform soft latent-tree learning: compute posterior expected counts and distinguish inside–outside EM from hard-parse training.
    3. O3 — Audit neural parameterization: explain which rule contractions stay exact and which expressivity or complexity choices a neural score introduces.
    4. O4 — Locate approximation: separate exact conditional tree sums from the approximate continuous-latent integral in compound grammars.
    5. O5 — Diagnose identifiability: test symbol duplication, collapse, annotation dependence, and the mismatch between likelihood and tree quality.
    6. O6 — Design and transfer an evaluation: match density, structure, symbol use, and compute evidence to a newly learned grammar formalism.

    Observed derivations: count and normalize¤

    If every training tree is observed, the PCFG log likelihood is

    \[ \ell(\theta)=\sum_r c_r\log\theta_r, \]

    where \(c_r\) is the total rule count. Subject to local normalization, maximum likelihood gives

    \[ \widehat\theta_{A\to\beta} =\frac{c_{A\to\beta}} {\sum_{r:\operatorname{lhs}(r)=A}c_r}. \]

    Dirichlet priors provide smoothing. Rule-count estimation is simple because the tree exposes every local choice. It does not determine whether the grammar structure, nonterminal inventory, or observation encoding is appropriate.

    Hidden derivations: inside–outside EM¤

    With yields but no trees, the log likelihood contains a log of a sum over derivations. EM alternates:

    1. E-step: use inside–outside to compute posterior expected rule counts;
    2. M-step: normalize those expected counts for each left-hand side.

    EM does not decrease the data likelihood when its E- and M-steps are exact, but it can converge to local optima or saddle points. Unused rules can remain dead; large latent symbol sets can duplicate one another; and a better likelihood can correspond to a less linguistically meaningful tree inventory.

    Complete worked example: one ambiguous E-step¤

    Consider

    \[ \begin{array}{lll} S\to AB & (0.5), & S\to BA\quad(0.5),\\ A\to a & (0.9), & A\to b\quad(0.1),\\ B\to a & (0.2), & B\to b\quad(0.8). \end{array} \]

    For the observed string ab, the two tree probabilities are

    \[ p(t_{AB},\texttt{ab})=0.5(0.9)(0.8)=0.36, \]

    and

    \[ p(t_{BA},\texttt{ab})=0.5(0.2)(0.1)=0.01. \]

    Therefore \(p(\texttt{ab})=0.37\) and

    \[ p(t_{AB}\mid\texttt{ab})=\frac{36}{37}, \qquad p(t_{BA}\mid\texttt{ab})=\frac1{37}. \]

    The E-step assigns fractional expected counts, not a hard parse. For example, the expected count of \(S\to AB\) is \(36/37\). A hard-EM update that assigns it count one discards posterior uncertainty and optimizes a different objective.

    Neural PCFGs¤

    A neural PCFG can embed symbols and score rules before normalizing them:

    \[ \theta_{A\to BC} =\frac{\exp s_\phi(A,B,C)} {\sum_{B',C'}\exp s_\phi(A,B',C')}. \]

    The chart still enforces context-free composition and exactly marginalizes trees for a fixed set of rule probabilities. The neural network shares statistical strength and changes the parameterization; it does not turn the inside algorithm into an approximation.

    Low-rank tensor factorization can reduce the symbol-dependent cost of binary rule contractions. Tensor-decomposed neural PCFGs demonstrate this trade-off. Rank is both a computational and expressivity choice, so a speed claim must state symbol count, rank, sentence length, and implementation.

    Global latents and conditional tractability¤

    A compound PCFG introduces a sentence-level latent \(z\):

    \[ z\sim p(z), \qquad t\mid z\sim\operatorname{PCFG}(\theta(z)), \qquad x=\operatorname{yield}(t). \]

    For fixed \(z\), the tree sum remains an exact inside computation. Marginally,

    \[ p(x)=\int p(z)\sum_{t:\operatorname{yield}(t)=x}p(t\mid z)\,dz, \]

    and the integral generally needs variational or Monte Carlo approximation. Compound PCFGs use amortized variational inference for \(z\) while collapsing the discrete trees conditionally. “Exact tree marginalization” must not be inflated into “exact model inference.”

    Grammar induction and identifiability¤

    Grammar induction learns hidden categories and structures from strings or weak supervision. Several obstacles recur:

    • renaming latent symbols leaves the observed law unchanged;
    • splitting one category into duplicate categories can preserve or nearly preserve the yield distribution;
    • different grammars can define the same language or yield probabilities;
    • likelihood can favor structural explanations that disagree with a chosen linguistic annotation scheme; and
    • neural parameterizations may underuse or collapse latent symbols.

    The 2025 EMNLP analysis of probability-distribution collapse in compact neural grammars is evidence about particular parameterizations and multilingual parsing protocols, not a theorem that all neural PCFGs collapse.

    Report held-out likelihood and structural measures separately. Unlabeled span F1 depends on a treebank convention and does not establish recovery of a unique true grammar.

    Beyond context-free rules¤

    Lexicalized PCFGs, tree-substitution grammars, adaptor grammars, synchronous grammars, tree-adjoining grammars, hyperedge-replacement grammars, and attribute grammars weaken different independence assumptions or change the generated object. More expressivity can increase inference cost or require approximation. The formalism, probability normalization, and tractable queries must be named; “grammar-based” is too broad to predict any of them.

    Independent practice and assessment map: grammar learning¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: the supervised estimate. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 6 min. Observed trees use \(S\to AB\) six times and \(S\to BA\) twice. Predict which rule receives larger maximum-likelihood probability, then compute both estimates.
    2. P1.2 — Compute a soft E-step. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. In the worked ab grammar, compute the expected counts for both root rules and for \(A\to a\), \(A\to b\), \(B\to a\), and \(B\to b\) from the two posterior tree masses.
    3. P1.3 — Complete one M-step. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Treat the single ab observation and the expected counts from P1.2 as the whole corpus. Normalize each left-hand side and report the updated six rule probabilities. Contrast the result with hard EM.
    4. P1.4 — Locate exactness. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For a compound PCFG with continuous \(z\), classify each operation as exact, approximate, or assumption-dependent: neural score evaluation at fixed \(z\), rule softmax, inside tree sum at fixed \(z\), integration over \(z\), amortized posterior inference, and pruning a chart.
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose non-identifiability. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Split a latent nonterminal \(A\) into \(A_1,A_2\) with identical emissions and parent weights that sum to the old \(A\) weight. Explain why the observed yield law can stay unchanged and name two diagnostics that reveal the unused distinction.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Design an evaluation for a neural molecular graph grammar learned without derivation annotations. Separate held-out density or bound, invariant validity, latent-rule use, structural stability, compute, and downstream property evidence.

    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 Counts are normalized only against alternatives with the same left-hand side.
    P1.2 Fractional rule counts follow posterior tree membership rather than a best-tree assignment.
    P1.3 Every left-hand-side distribution sums to one and the difference from hard EM is explicit.
    P1.4 Conditional discrete marginalization is not promoted to exact continuous-latent inference.
    P1.5 An observationally equivalent category split and diagnostics for symbol use or instability are both supplied.
    P1.6 The study reports probabilistic fit, formal invariants, learned structure, resources, and domain utility as separate evidence.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The \(AB\) rule is more frequent. The estimates are \(\widehat\theta_{S\to AB}=6/8=0.75\) and \(\widehat\theta_{S\to BA}=2/8=0.25\). Check. No emission counts enter this root-rule denominator.
    2. P1.2. The expected root counts are \(E[c_{S\to AB}]=36/37\) and \(E[c_{S\to BA}]=1/37\). In the \(AB\) tree, \(A\) emits a and \(B\) emits b; in the \(BA\) tree, \(B\) emits a and \(A\) emits b. Thus \(E[c_{A\to a}]=E[c_{B\to b}]=36/37\) and \(E[c_{A\to b}]=E[c_{B\to a}]=1/37\). Check. Each nonterminal's expected outgoing counts sum to one.
    3. P1.3. Normalization gives root probabilities \((36/37,1/37)\), \(A\) emissions \((36/37,1/37)\) for (a,b), and \(B\) emissions \((1/37,36/37)\) for (a,b). Hard EM would select the \(AB\) tree and assign zeros and ones instead, discarding the \(1/37\) alternative mass. Check. All three probability pairs sum to one.
    4. P1.4. Score evaluation and the rule softmax are exact numerical maps for a fixed \(z\); the inside sum is exact for the declared finite context-free chart at that \(z\). The integral over \(z\) and amortized posterior are generally approximate. Pruning is assumption-dependent: it is exact only for provably zero states and otherwise changes the sum. Check. “Exact tree marginalization” describes one conditional operation, not the whole posterior.
    5. P1.5. If every occurrence formerly assigned to \(A\) is represented by indistinguishable \(A_1\) or \(A_2\) alternatives whose incoming masses add to the original and whose outgoing laws coincide, summing the duplicate latent labels recovers the same yield probabilities. Occupancy/entropy per symbol and stability across restarts or perturbations can expose collapse or arbitrary swapping; synthetic rule recovery can add evidence when truth is known. Check. Better likelihood alone cannot identify which duplicate label is meaningful.
    6. P1.6. A defensible study reports a held-out graph likelihood or named variational bound; exact valence and rewrite-invariant success; rule occupancy, entropy, and restart stability; recovery on synthetic known grammars or a justified structural proxy; parameters, grammar size, chart/search costs, wall time, memory, and hardware; and separately calibrated molecular properties, novelty, synthesizability, and shift tests. Rubric. Full credit keeps every layer separate and states when the density itself is only bounded or estimated.

    Chapter 3 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Supervised counts do not learn structure. They estimate probabilities for a declared grammar and observed trees.
    • EM monotonicity is not global optimality. Initialization and grammar topology can dominate the result.
    • Neural scores are not probabilities until normalized under the declared rule set. Masking rules changes that set.
    • Parsing accuracy is not density estimation. A model can improve one while worsening the other.
    Retrieval question: what remains exact in a compound PCFG?

    Conditional on the global latent and its resulting rule probabilities, the sum over context-free trees is exact. The posterior and marginal integral over the continuous latent are generally approximate.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Observed trees reduce learning to rule counts; hidden trees require posterior expected counts; neural and global-latent parameterizations share information but do not erase the grammar's inference boundaries. Chapter 4 changes the observation map from a terminal frontier to execution of a generated program.
