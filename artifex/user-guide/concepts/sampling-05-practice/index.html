    Worked Example: ULA for a Standard Gaussian¤

    Sampling capstone learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the complete sampling volume. By the end, you should be able to compute ULA bias and a two-state Metropolis transition, select family-specific diagnostics, use primary references to verify validity conditions, answer the retrieval set with feedback, and route a sampling failure to target/support, proposal/kernel, adaptation, geometry, discretization, or Monte Carlo estimation.

    For \(E(x)=x^2/2\), ULA is

    \[ x_{k+1}=(1-\eta)x_k+\sqrt{2\eta}\,\xi_k. \]

    This AR(1) chain has stationary variance

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{2\eta}{1-(1-\eta)^2} =\frac{1}{1-\eta/2}, \]

    which exceeds the target variance one for finite \(\eta\). The example exposes ULA discretization bias exactly. MALA's accept/reject correction restores the exact target stationary distribution, at the cost of rejected proposals.

    Stability also requires \(|1-\eta|<1\), hence \(0<\eta<2\). A step size can therefore produce a stable but biased chain, an extremely correlated chain, or an unstable process. “The samples look Gaussian” is not enough: compare variance, autocorrelation, and initialization sensitivity.

    Worked Example: A Metropolis Move on a Two-State Target¤

    Reuse the posterior from the binary latent-model example: \(\pi(0)=0.2\), \(\pi(1)=0.8\). Propose switching states with probability one. From state \(0\), the move to state \(1\) is always accepted. From state \(1\), the reverse acceptance probability is

    \[ \alpha(1,0)=\min(1,0.2/0.8)=0.25. \]

    The transition matrix is

    \[ K=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\0.25&0.75\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Checking invariance,

    \[ (0.2,0.8)K=(0.2,0.8). \]

    Starting at state zero gives the finite-time laws

    \[ \begin{aligned} (1,0)&\to(0,1)\\ &\to(0.25,0.75)\\ &\to(0.1875,0.8125)\\ &\to\cdots. \end{aligned} \]

    The more probable state has more rejections because proposed departures move toward lower target density. Those self-transitions are not wasted bookkeeping; they are required for the correct stationary law. The chain estimates posterior occupancy as time grows, while its acceptance ratio has discarded the evidence \(p(X=1)\).

    Sampler Diagnostics by Family¤

    Table SAMP.6. Sampler Diagnostics by Family
    Sampler Useful evidence Misleading shortcut
    Importance/AIS log-weight distribution, ESS, repeated estimates, tail diagnostics number of proposals alone
    random-walk or independence MH rank plots, \(\widehat R\), bulk/tail ESS, MCSE, mode occupancy, acceptance, jump and cost statistics one target acceptance rate
    Gibbs, blocked, or collapsed MCMC sweep definition, rank plots, \(\widehat R\), ESS/MCSE per query and cost, block sensitivity acceptance is one, therefore mixing is good
    MALA/HMC/NUTS rank plots, \(\widehat R\), bulk/tail ESS, MCSE, divergences, tree depth, acceptance, gradients and wall-clock high acceptance or zero divergences alone
    ULA/SGLD step-size sensitivity, moment bias, multiple initializations, task observables “acceptance rate” or monotone energy
    pseudo-marginal MH likelihood-estimator variance, state stickiness, ESS/MCSE, target evaluations unbiased likelihood estimate implies efficient chain
    reversible-jump MCMC model occupancy, cross-model round trips, within-model ESS, move-specific acceptance and Jacobian tests pooled acceptance across move types
    parallel tempering cold-chain diagnostics, replica round trips, mode occupancy, swap rates by pair, all-replica cost one high adjacent swap rate
    ODE/CNF tolerance/grid convergence, forward–inverse consistency, NFE, divergence-estimator variance nominal solver order alone
    Reverse SDE/diffusion schedule and solver ablations, seed diversity, endpoint metrics, score error proxies one attractive sample grid
    Distilled/few-step map teacher comparison at equal conditioning, NFE and wall clock, coverage and artifacts calling one NFE exact integration

    No scalar diagnostic certifies a high-dimensional sampler. Choose observables that test the scientific claim: mode occupancy, conserved quantities, calibration, structural validity, tail risk, or downstream utility.

    This table diagnoses the computational sampler. It does not replace uncertainty over datasets, training runs, evaluators, or human raters. Those experimental levels are developed in Evaluating Generative Models and Reproducibility and Generative-Model Research Methods.

    Primary and Archival Reading Trail¤

    • Hammersley and Morton, A New Monte Carlo Technique: Antithetic Variates — the classical paired-variable variance construction.
    • Buchholz, Wenzel, and Mandt, Quasi-Monte Carlo Variational Inference and Liu and Owen, Quasi-Monte Carlo Quasi-Newton in Variational Bayes — deterministic and randomized low-discrepancy designs for variational objectives under stated regularity.
    • Giordano, Ingram, and Broderick, Black Box Variational Inference with a Deterministic Objective — fixed-randomness sample-average approximation and its expressivity boundary.
    • Fujisawa and Sato, Multilevel Monte Carlo Variational Inference — a multilevel gradient-variance construction for VI.
    • Levin, Peres, and Wilmer, Markov Chains and Mixing Times — finite-chain invariant laws, reversibility, convergence, coupling, spectral analysis, and quantitative mixing.
    • Geyer, Practical Markov Chain Monte Carlo and Flegal and Jones, Batch Means and Spectral Variance Estimators in Markov Chain Monte Carlo — dependence-aware Monte Carlo error and consistent long-run-variance estimation under stated conditions.
    • Metropolis et al., Equation of State Calculations by Fast Computing Machines and Hastings, Monte Carlo Sampling Methods Using Markov Chains and Their Applications — the accept/reject construction and its general asymmetric-proposal form.
    • Roberts and Rosenthal, Coupling and Ergodicity of Adaptive Markov Chain Monte Carlo Algorithms — explicit conditions and counterexamples for adaptive-kernel convergence.
    • Andrieu and Roberts, The Pseudo-Marginal Approach for Efficient Monte Carlo Computations — exact marginal targeting through a nonnegative unbiased likelihood estimator on an augmented state.
    • Neal, Slice Sampling — slice augmentation and one- and multidimensional transition constructions.
    • Roberts and Tweedie, Exponential Convergence of Langevin Distributions and Their Discrete Approximations — invariant and convergence boundaries for Langevin diffusions and discretizations.
    • Duane et al., Hybrid Monte Carlo, Girolami and Calderhead, Riemann Manifold Langevin and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo Methods, and Hoffman and Gelman, The No-U-Turn Sampler — Hamiltonian augmentation, position-dependent geometry, and dynamic trajectory length.
    • Vehtari et al., Rank-Normalization, Folding, and Localization — rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\), folded diagnostics, bulk/tail efficiency, and MCSE.
    • Welling and Teh, Bayesian Learning via Stochastic Gradient Langevin Dynamics and Chen, Fox, and Guestrin, Stochastic Gradient Hamiltonian Monte Carlo — minibatch-gradient Langevin and kinetic constructions, with distinct noise and discretization assumptions.
    • Green, Reversible Jump Markov Chain Monte Carlo Computation and Bayesian Model Determination — dimension matching, auxiliary variables, and Jacobian-corrected structure moves.

    Solutions
    1. ULA directly applies an Euler update; it is not wrapped in a Metropolis accept/reject rule.
    2. No. It establishes invariance/reversibility, not a useful finite-time convergence rate.
    3. The finite-step discretization bias of the Langevin proposal, assuming exact target evaluation.
    4. They can share one-time marginals under the theorem's assumptions; trajectories, transition randomness, and numerical errors differ.
    5. Euler discretization changes the invariant distribution at nonzero step size even when the resulting AR process is stable.
    6. Field error is error in the learned continuous dynamics; discretization error comes from numerically approximating those dynamics.
    7. No. Very short or tiny-step trajectories can accept almost always while moving inefficiently or missing modes.
    8. They were sampled from the proposal and then assigned random normalized weights. Resampling only creates a finite empirical approximation and introduces duplication and dependence.
    9. Under a standard properly weighted construction, the product of average incremental weights can be an unbiased estimator of the normalizing-constant ratio.
    10. Yes. Every finite transition matrix has at least one invariant probability law. Reducible and periodic examples may have one or many; those properties control uniqueness and convergence rather than finite-state existence itself.
    11. The chain is irreducible and has a unique invariant law, but periodic oscillation prevents its time-\(t\) law from converging from a point mass. Path averages can still converge.
    12. No. Summing the detailed-balance identities proves invariance directly. Additional conditions are needed for uniqueness, convergence, or rate statements, not for that implication.
    13. Time homogeneity describes reuse of a kernel; stationarity describes a process law, normally requiring invariant initialization. One does not imply the other.
    14. It fixes the sample-count exponent under iid finite-variance assumptions. The observable variance, rare-event probability, draw cost, and proposal or sampler difficulty can all worsen with dimension.
    15. It substitutes an estimated variance into an asymptotic central-limit approximation. Small samples, heavy tails, dependence, rare events, or adaptive stopping can spoil its coverage.
    16. \(Z/M\), where \(Z=\int\gamma\). The value is \(1/M\) only when the enveloped target is already normalized.
    17. No. Gaussian symmetry preserves both marginals, but variance improves only when the two transformed observables have negative covariance.
    18. The variance of a paired difference or comparison. Reusing randomness need not reduce either marginal estimator's variance.
    19. Its points are deterministic and deliberately dependent. Use a deterministic error bound when its assumptions apply, or independent valid randomizations for repeat-based uncertainty.
    20. It establishes convergence for the finite random objective conditional on the fixed draws, not for the population expectation. Fresh draws or increasing sample size test the latter gap.
    21. Adjacent levels must be coupled so their differences have small variance, and sample allocation must balance correction variance against cost.
    22. No. RQMC is an estimator or numerical integration design. The probabilistic family is still defined by the latent-variable model and its distributions.
    23. It is computed from the long-run variance of a declared observable. Different observables can have different autocorrelation structures, and no finite ESS proves that an unvisited mode was explored.
    24. Yes. Negative autocorrelation can make a path average less variable than an iid average with the same number of states, so this variance-equivalence convention can give \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}>N\). The number is still observable- and estimator-specific.
    25. Mixing time is a worst-case distance-to-target property of a specified kernel and tolerance. Warmup may combine initialization movement and adaptation, and discarding it supplies no worst-case distance bound.
    26. No. A local proposal may be irreducible through multi-step paths. The resulting transition graph must reach target-relevant regions under the conditions of the convergence theorem.
    27. Different components can connect different parts of the state space. Their random mixture can create multi-step paths absent from any component used alone.
    28. Diminishing adaptation and a stability condition such as containment. A separate valid workflow is to adapt during warmup and freeze the retained-sampling kernel.
    29. It targets an augmented state containing the likelihood-estimator randomness. Nonnegative unbiasedness makes the desired likelihood appear after marginalization; estimator variance still controls efficiency.
    30. Acceptance one proves that a full-conditional draw needs no MH rejection. Strong dependence can make successive conditional draws move only a tiny distance through the joint target.
    31. Simultaneously drawing dependent coordinates from conditionals evaluated at stale values generally does not equal a composition of valid full-conditional kernels. Conditional independence or another derived parallel kernel is required.
    32. Collapsing changes the chain by analytically removing a variable from its target. Rao–Blackwellization can leave the chain unchanged and replace a sampled quantity in an estimator by its conditional expectation.
    33. Slice sampling is augmentation with one or more variables defining a joint law whose marginal is the target. Coordinate, directional, elliptical, and other multivariate implementations organize the slice differently.
    34. \(p\sim\mathcal N(0,M)\), where \(M\) is the mass matrix and momentum covariance; the kinetic energy is \(p^\top M^{-1}p/2\).
    35. No. A numerical trajectory normally has some finite energy error and is accepted probabilistically. A divergence flags unusually large or unstable error under an implementation's criterion.
    36. NUTS dynamically chooses trajectory extent through a U-turn construction. It still needs step-size and mass-matrix adaptation, a maximum depth, a candidate-selection rule, and diagnostics.
    37. The transformation changes the density relative to the unconstrained base measure. Omitting \(\log|\det J_T|\) targets the wrong distribution in unconstrained coordinates.
    38. Bulk ESS measures information about central rank-based summaries, tail ESS targets tail events or quantiles, and divergences expose unstable numerical integration in local HMC geometry. None subsumes the others.
    39. Minibatch noise is not automatically calibrated thermal noise, and a fixed discretization has stationary bias without an exact correction. It changes both the cost and the target-error contract.
    40. It converts density under one dimension-matched augmented coordinate system to density under the other. Without it, the cross-model acceptance ratio compares incompatible volume elements.
    41. Simulated annealing is a changing-target optimizer, parallel tempering is MCMC on a product of tempered laws, and AIS is a weighted path estimator for normalizers and expectations.
    42. Replica round trips, mode transport across temperatures, cold-chain occupancy, and swap diagnostics across every adjacent pair, reported with the cost of all replicas.

    Summary and Next Step¤

    Sampling quality depends on the target, query, estimator, randomness design, invariance, kernel composition, dependence, mixing, parameterization, discretization, and finite computation. MH correction, Gibbs conditionals, augmentation, HMC geometry, stochastic-gradient scaling, reversible-jump dimension matching, and tempering solve different transition problems. Warmup is not a mixing theorem, acceptance is not exploration, and no diagnostic certifies a missed mode. Ordinary Monte Carlo error, Markov-chain MCSE, model error, and numerical bias answer different questions. ODE and SDE views explain flows and diffusion, while MCMC explains EBMs, posterior inference, and structure uncertainty. Continue with Optimization and Learning Dynamics to separate derivatives, stochastic estimators, optimizer geometry, latent-variable coordinates, search distributions, and transport objectives. Then use the Canonical Notation before comparing papers and begin the Autoregressive Models complete guide.
