    7. Implicit Generators, Adversarial Games, and Bilevel Gradients¤

    Chapter 7 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Pushforward measures, GAN games, score diffusion, and flow matching.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Detect singular endpoint laws: test domination and local KL regularity before invoking a classical Fisher.
    2. O2 — Declare alternative geometry: separate smoothing, Wasserstein, Sinkhorn, learned-feature, and latent/data-manifold constructions.
    3. O3 — Type adversarial updates: distinguish a discriminator conditional Fisher, a generator pullback, and a coupled game preconditioner.
    4. O4 — Differentiate bilevel systems: compare simultaneous/alternating fields, finite unrolling, stopped responses, and implicit best-response derivatives.
    5. O5 — Identify a path-model law: name the endpoint, path, marginal, transition, surrogate conditional, coupling, and time distribution involved.
    6. O6 — Transfer with boundaries: choose a geometry for an implicit or time-varying generator and state exactly what its projection forgets.

    A deterministic generator can be statistically singular¤

    Let \(Z\) have a continuous density and let a generator map it into a higher dimensional ambient space. Its pushforward may live on a lower-dimensional manifold and have no ambient Lebesgue density. Classical Fisher formulas based on \(\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x)\) then need not exist.

    Worked rotating-line generator¤

    Let \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and

    \[ G_\theta(Z)=(Z,\theta Z)\in\mathbb R^2. \]

    For each \(\theta\), the generated law lies on a line. Distinct nonparallel lines intersect only at the origin, which has probability zero, so their measures are mutually singular. The KL divergence between the endpoint laws is infinite, not locally quadratic in \(\theta\).

    Adding observation noise,

    \[ X=G_\theta(Z)+\varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I), \]

    produces a full-rank Gaussian law and a well-defined Fisher. But the geometry now depends on \(\sigma\) and belongs to a different, noise-smoothed generative model. “Add noise and compute Fisher” is a modeling choice, not a harmless numerical trick.

    GANs contain a game, not one canonical Fisher¤

    A discriminator can define a normalized Bernoulli conditional \(p_\phi(y\mid x)\) and therefore a conditional Fisher for \(\phi\). That matrix is not the Fisher of the generator pushforward. A generator-side pullback can instead be built from a declared discrepancy on generated measures, but the choice—KL after smoothing, Wasserstein, Sinkhorn, an MMD, or a learned feature metric—changes the geometry.

    Even if both players use positive-definite preconditioners, the coupled update remains a game vector field. Natural preconditioning does not by itself remove the rotational, non-normal, or support-mismatch behavior derived in the GAN optimization chapter.

    Sinkhorn Natural Gradient pulls back a Sinkhorn-divergence geometry through an implicit generator and studies an empirical information matrix. It is a peer-reviewed construction for that declared transport discrepancy. It is not the classical Fisher--Rao natural gradient under another implementation name.

    Game, unrolled, and implicit gradients answer different questions¤

    For a two-player loss \(V(\theta,\phi)\), simultaneous descent--ascent uses the field

    \[ G(\theta,\phi) =\begin{bmatrix} \nabla_\theta V(\theta,\phi)\\ -\nabla_\phi V(\theta,\phi) \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Alternating updates evaluate its blocks at different states. Extragradient evaluates a look-ahead field. Optimistic and negative-momentum variants combine current and previous fields. These are finite game algorithms, not estimators of one scalar gradient that merely differ in variance. Even simple nonconvex-- nonconcave games can converge, cycle, or diverge under different schemes (Grimmer et al.).

    A bilevel formulation instead defines an inner solution

    \[ \phi^*(\theta)\in\arg\min_\phi L_{\mathrm{in}}(\theta,\phi) \]

    and differentiates an outer objective \(J(\theta,\phi^*(\theta))\). If a smooth isolated inner stationary point has nonsingular \(H_{\phi\phi}=\nabla^2_{\phi\phi}L_{\mathrm{in}}\), implicit differentiation gives

    \[ \frac{d\phi^*}{d\theta} =-H_{\phi\phi}^{-1}H_{\phi\theta}, \]

    and the total outer derivative includes the response term

    \[ \frac{dJ}{d\theta} =\partial_\theta J -H_{\phi\theta}^\top H_{\phi\phi}^{-\top} \partial_\phi J. \]

    Differentiating through \(K\) explicit inner updates produces the gradient of the \(K\)-step unrolled system, not automatically the exact best-response objective. Stopping the gradient through the inner state drops the response term entirely. Unrolled GANs demonstrate a specific finite unrolling construction; they do not prove that more unrolling monotonically improves every adversarial game.

    Diffusion and flow models: which time-indexed law?¤

    A diffusion or flow system exposes several possible geometric objects:

    • the endpoint generated law \(p_\theta(x_0)\);
    • a path law over trajectories;
    • corrupted marginals \(p_{\theta,t}(x_t)\);
    • reverse transition conditionals;
    • denoising, velocity, or noise-prediction surrogate conditionals; and
    • a distribution over time and conditioning variables used by the training objective.

    The generalized Gauss--Newton matrix of a weighted denoising regression loss is well defined. Calling it the Fisher of the final generated distribution requires an additional equivalence proof. Changing timestep weights changes that training geometry even if the population-optimal predictor remains convertible.

    Likewise, a flow-matching regression metric measures errors in a vector field under a selected coupling and time distribution. It is not automatically the Fisher--Rao metric of the terminal density or the \(W_2\) metric of the probability path.

    Projection onto a tractable statistical manifold¤

    A different strategy is to project an evolving generative law onto a tractable statistical family and move that projection geometrically. Liu, Wang, and Wang develop such a construction for time-varying generators using an exponential- family manifold and particle approximations (peer-reviewed study). This is peer-reviewed research with declared projections and estimators. The projection retains selected statistics, not necessarily the complete law, so its natural-gradient motion is a population-optimum correspondence rather than an algebraic identity with arbitrary diffusion or flow training.

    Four geometries that must not be conflated¤

    Table GRAD.3. Four geometries that must not be conflated
    Geometry Object compared Typical local tensor Generative use
    Fisher--Rao nearby dominated probability laws score covariance likelihoods and variational families
    Wasserstein probability laws over a metric state space transport/continuity-equation structure mass-moving paths and implicit generators
    Sinkhorn pullback parametrized laws under a regularized OT divergence divergence Hessian/pullback empirical implicit-generator optimization
    Latent/data-manifold metric individual codes or samples decoder-Jacobian or ambient metric pullback interpolation and geometric sampling

    The last row is about distances between points. The first three are about distances or divergences between distributions. A Riemannian decoder manifold does not, by itself, define a natural gradient for model parameters.

    Independent practice and assessment map: singular laws and game derivatives¤

    These tasks require two declarations before any matrix is computed: which law or discrepancy supplies geometry, and which game or bilevel derivative is being taken.

    1. P1.1 — Prove singularity in the rotating-line model. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. Show that two nonparallel generated lines intersect on a set of probability zero and explain why neither ambient log density nor a finite local KL expansion exists.
    2. P1.2 — Predict before computing: audit noise smoothing. Type: predict · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Add \(\varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I)\) to the rotating-line generator. Predict what happens as \(\sigma\downarrow0\) and list the model components and diagnostics that must mention \(\sigma\).
    3. P1.3 — Classify four adversarial updates. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. Distinguish simultaneous descent--ascent, alternating updates, \(K\)-step discriminator unrolling, and implicit best-response differentiation by the derivative target each implements.
    4. P1.4 — Compute stopped, unrolled, and exact bilevel derivatives. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Let \(L_{\mathrm{in}}(\theta,\phi)=\tfrac12(\phi-\theta)^2\) and \(J(\theta,\phi)=\tfrac12(\phi-1)^2\). At \(\theta=0\), start \(\phi_0=2\) and take one inner gradient step with \(\alpha=1/4\). Compute the stopped outer derivative, one-step unrolled derivative, and exact best-response derivative.
    5. P1.5 — Select a diffusion geometry object. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A method computes the GGN of a denoising loss and calls it “the Fisher of the diffusion model.” List the endpoint, path, marginal, transition, and surrogate laws that could instead be meant, and state what proof would be needed for the name.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a molecular implicit generator. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. A generator maps latent noise to 3D molecular conformations modulo rigid motion. Choose one law-level geometry and one state-manifold geometry, define their roles, specify a generator pullback or game derivative, and state what each construction forgets.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. For slopes \(\theta\ne\theta'\), \((z,\theta z)=(z',\theta'z')\) implies \(z=z'\) and \((\theta-\theta')z=0\), hence \(z=0\). A continuous normal latent assigns the origin probability zero, so each law concentrates on a set to which the other assigns zero mass. There is no ambient Lebesgue density and the KL between the two endpoint measures is infinite rather than quadratic. Check. A latent density does not become an ambient endpoint density through a dimension-increasing deterministic map.
    2. P1.2. For every \(\sigma>0\), convolution produces a full-rank Gaussian; as \(\sigma\) shrinks, directions normal to the line become increasingly concentrated and Fisher conditioning can deteriorate toward the singular limit. The observation model, likelihood, claimed geometry, training data corruption, evaluation distribution, and any comparison must state \(\sigma\). Sweep it while reporting spectrum, predicted/measured divergence, and clean versus smoothed sample metrics. Check. The regularized model is not the original deterministic pushforward.
    3. P1.3. Simultaneous updates evaluate both signed partials at the same state; alternating updates evaluate the second block after the first has moved; \(K\)-step unrolling differentiates an outer loss through exactly \(K\) opponent updates; implicit differentiation targets a smooth isolated best response satisfying its stationary system. Check. These can disagree even with identical instantaneous losses and preconditioners.
    4. P1.4. Stopping through \(\phi\) gives \(\partial_\theta J=0\). One step gives \(\phi_1=2-(1/4)(2-0)=1.5\) and \(d\phi_1/d\theta=1/4\), so \(dJ_1/d\theta=(\phi_1-1)(1/4)=1/8\). The exact response is \(\phi^*=\theta\), hence \(dJ(\theta,\phi^*)/d\theta=\theta-1=-1\) at zero. Check. The three values \(0,1/8,-1\) answer stopped, finite-unrolled, and exact-response questions.
    5. P1.5. Possible objects include endpoint \(p_\theta(x_0)\), full trajectory law, noisy marginal \(p_{\theta,t}(x_t)\), reverse transition \(p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\), and the denoising/noise/velocity surrogate conditional under sampled \((t,x_t)\). Calling the regression GGN an endpoint Fisher requires a theorem equating its local quadratic form with endpoint KL under the same parameter family and weighting. Check. Predictor convertibility at the population optimum is insufficient by itself.
    6. P1.6. One defensible design uses a rigid-motion-invariant Wasserstein or Sinkhorn discrepancy on conformation laws, pulled back through generator parameters, plus the quotient Riemannian metric on each conformation for tangent forces and integration. The law metric compares distributions; the state metric enforces molecular-coordinate symmetry and constraints. The answer must declare couplings, atom correspondence, chirality, energy/validity constraints, and whether summary features erase rare modes. If adversarial learning is used, choose simultaneous, unrolled, or implicit response semantics separately. Rubric. Two geometries acting on different objects are not merged into one tensor.

    Chapter 7 synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Implicit endpoint laws may be singular, adversarial systems contain multiple statistical objects, and path-model training introduces time-indexed surrogate laws. A meaningful natural-gradient claim begins by choosing one of them and ends by checking the finite approximation against that choice.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why does the rotating-line generator lack a regular endpoint Fisher?
    2. Is a discriminator Fisher a generator Fisher?
    3. Why is the Hessian of a denoising loss not automatically endpoint-law Fisher information?

    Solutions. (1) Nearby parameters produce mutually singular measures on different lines, so KL is not locally quadratic. (2) No; it belongs to the discriminator conditional unless a separate theorem connects them. (3) The regression law, time weighting, and endpoint generated law are different probabilistic objects.
