    4. Learning, Rank Adaptation, and Continuous Variables¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes exact MPS contractions and basic maximum likelihood. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive a Born NLL gradient;
    2. compare global-gradient and DMRG-style sweep optimization;
    3. diagnose zero support and numerical scaling;
    4. select ranks without test leakage; and
    5. construct continuous densities with feature maps and Gram contractions.

    Maximum likelihood and its negative phase¤

    For a Born model and empirical data \(x^{(1:n)}\),

    \[ \mathcal L(\theta) =-\frac1n\sum_{m=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x^{(m)}) =-\frac2n\sum_m\log|\Psi_\theta(x^{(m)})| +\log Z_\theta. \]

    For a real parameter \(\theta_j\) away from amplitude zeros,

    \[ \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\theta_j} =-\frac2n\sum_m \operatorname{Re} \frac{\partial_{\theta_j}\Psi(x^{(m)})}{\Psi(x^{(m)})} +\partial_{\theta_j}\log Z. \]

    The data term raises observed amplitudes; the normalizer term redistributes mass over the whole support. Exact MPS environments compute both. If an observed configuration has \(\Psi=0\), its NLL is infinite and the ratio is singular. Initialization and smoothing must address support, not hide the failure behind an arbitrary finite log floor.

    For a normalized canonical parameterization, \(Z=1\) on the constraint manifold. The normalizer cannot simply be dropped while taking an unconstrained Euclidean gradient that leaves that manifold.

    Global gradient training¤

    Automatic differentiation through stabilized contractions updates every core simultaneously. It integrates readily with minibatches, shared neural feature maps, and modern optimizers. Its main hazards are:

    • products whose scale grows or decays exponentially with sequence length;
    • redundant gauge directions and ill-conditioned cores;
    • dense doubled-network memory for Born contractions;
    • rank fixed before training; and
    • unconstrained steps that violate nonnegativity, stochasticity, isometry, or canonical form.

    Environment rescaling with accumulated log factors, periodic canonicalization, and manifold-aware parameterizations are principled controls. A rejected ICLR 2026 submission on probabilistic tensor-network stabilization remains Preprint evidence; its reported scaling results must not be presented as an accepted benchmark.

    Alternating and DMRG-style sweeps¤

    Sweep algorithms place an orthogonality center at one or two adjacent cores, optimize that local block using environments for the rest, and move the center through the chain. A two-site update can reshape the optimized block and split it by SVD:

    \[ M=U\Sigma V^\dagger. \]

    Retaining singular values above a declared rank or discarded-mass criterion adapts the bond dimension. This can discover where capacity is needed, but the criterion is local and the overall objective remains nonconvex. Sweeps do not guarantee a global likelihood optimum.

    Han et al. (2018) introduced MPS Born-machine learning and direct sampling using quantum-many-body optimization ideas. Historical DMRG terminology describes the optimizer and canonical algebra; it does not make the trained probability model a physical ground state.

    Complete worked example: what rank one cannot learn¤

    For the two-bit target from Chapter 1, the maximum-likelihood independent model matches the uniform marginals, hence assigns \(q_{ij}=1/4\). Its population NLL is

    \[ -\sum_{ij}\mathcal P_{ij}\log\tfrac14=\log4\approx1.38629. \]

    The entropy of the true table is

    \[ H(\mathcal P) =-2(0.4\log0.4)-2(0.1\log0.1) \approx1.19355. \]

    The irreducible rank-one excess is

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathcal P\|q) =\log4-H(\mathcal P) \approx0.19274\ \text{nats}. \]

    A rank-two MPS represents the table exactly, so it can close this approximation gap in the population model. Finite-sample validation is still needed: a larger rank can reduce training NLL while worsening held-out NLL.

    Rank and topology selection¤

    Select order, topology, ranks, regularization, and truncation thresholds on a validation protocol fixed before the test evaluation. Useful diagnostics include:

    • held-out NLL as a function of maximum and effective rank;
    • singular-value spectra at every bond;
    • discarded norm or likelihood change after truncation;
    • contraction FLOPs, peak intermediate memory, and sample latency;
    • sensitivity to variable permutations and random restarts; and
    • calibration of requested marginals and conditionals.

    Rank is a structural hyperparameter, not a post hoc explanation. Searching many orders and reporting only the best test result leaks selection information.

    Continuous variables through feature maps¤

    Let \(\phi_i(x_i)\in\mathbb C^{K_i}\) be a feature map and define

    \[ A_i(x_i)=\sum_{s=1}^{K_i}\phi_{i,s}(x_i)A_i[s], \qquad \Psi(x)=A_1(x_1)\cdots A_d(x_d). \]

    The density is \(p(x)=|\Psi(x)|^2/Z\) with respect to the declared product base measure. Define Gram matrices

    \[ G_i[s,t] =\int \phi_{i,s}(x)\overline{\phi_{i,t}(x)}\,d\mu_i(x). \]

    Normalization and marginalization insert \(G_i\) at integrated sites. If features are orthonormal, \(G_i=I\) and the discrete Born contractions carry over. If not, ignoring the Gram matrix changes the density.

    Tensor-train density estimation uses functional tensor trains for exact partition, marginal, CDF, and sampling queries under its basis and optimization assumptions. Meiburg et al. (2025) develop a continuous-valued Born MPS with feature maps, canonical sampling, and universal approximation results for the stated smooth-density class. Universality permits rank and feature dimension to grow; it is not a fixed-budget performance claim.

    Mixed data and learned features¤

    Discrete sites use one-hot or learned categorical features; continuous sites use basis functions or neural feature maps. Learned features can improve compression but alter the Gram integrals. Exact normalization survives only if those integrals remain tractable or are constrained to a known isometry.

    An encoder followed by a tensor density is a composed latent system. Its likelihood on observed data is not automatically the latent tensor likelihood; the observation map, Jacobian or decoder, and information loss must be declared.

    Chapter 4 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Canonical \(Z=1\) does not license an unconstrained update. The optimization route must preserve or restore the canonical constraint.
    • DMRG is not a global optimizer. Sweeps can stall, depend on order, and prune a bond needed later.
    • Rank growth is not free. It raises contraction time and may overfit.
    • Continuous features define the measure. Basis functions, support, Gram matrices, boundary behavior, and sampling inversion belong in the model.
    • Universal approximation is asymptotic. It does not identify the rank, data, or compute required for one benchmark.

    Independent practice and assessment map: tensor learning¤

    Keep the constrained probability model, sampled objective, optimizer geometry, rank-selection protocol, and continuous reference measure in one reproducible learning contract.

    P1 — Derive and diagnose a tensor-network training study¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each change preserves the same learning problem: dropping \(\log Z\) after an unconstrained core update; flooring every zero amplitude inside the log; replacing a two-site sweep by global Adam at the same update count; selecting order on the test set; increasing rank whenever training NLL falls; treating nonorthogonal feature maps as orthonormal; and reporting latent-space likelihood after a lossy encoder as observed-space likelihood. For each change, name the altered objective, support, geometry, selection protocol, or measure.
    2. P1.2 — Born NLL derivative. Let \(\Psi_\theta(0)=\theta\), \(\Psi_\theta(1)=1\), and let the empirical dataset contain only state 0. Derive \(\mathcal L(\theta)=-2\log|\theta|+\log(\theta^2+1)\) and its derivative for \(\theta\ne0\). Evaluate both at \(\theta=2\), separate the data and normalizer terms, and show how omitting \(\partial_\theta\log Z\) changes the update and the represented normalized law.
    3. P1.3 — Support and constrained-update audit. Initialize a finite Born MPS with zero amplitude on one observed configuration and trace the NLL and gradient failure. Compare three explicit remedies—support-preserving parameterization, declared smoothing of the target/model, and arbitrary log flooring—by the probability model each optimizes. Then specify a canonical-manifold update/retraction test that verifies norm, likelihood, and gauge-invariant probabilities after every step.
    4. P1.4 — Global versus sweep protocol. For a two-site block with singular values \((4,1,0.1)\), compute retained rank and discarded squared mass when the smallest value is truncated. Design a matched comparison between global-gradient and two-site sweep training that fixes data order, initialization budget, likelihood evaluations, wall-clock/compute budget, rank ceiling, stopping rule, and restarts. Explain why a local SVD criterion and lower training NLL do not establish globally optimal rank.
    5. P1.5 — Continuous Gram calculation. On \([0,1]\) with Lebesgue measure, take \(\phi_1(x)=1\) and \(\phi_2(x)=x\). Derive \(G=\begin{bmatrix}1&1/2\\1/2&1/3\end{bmatrix}\). Choose a one-site coefficient vector, compute \(Z=c^\dagger Gc\), compare it with the incorrect \(c^\dagger c\), and normalize the resulting density. State support, units, boundary behavior, and the inversion or sampling method required for a complete generative model.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Design a mixed discrete/continuous tensor density for clinical measurements, molecular coordinates, or physical sensor fields. Declare base measures and units, categorical and continuous feature maps, Gram integrals, missingness, topology/order, rank adaptation, optimizer/sweep schedule, numerical controls, validation-only selection, and exact small-instance tests. Identify every encoder, transform, or approximation that prevents latent tensor NLL from being reported as exact observed-space likelihood.

    Retrieval question: where does the normalizer gradient go in a canonical Born MPS?

    Solution. It vanishes only for parameter variations that remain on the normalized canonical manifold. A generic optimizer step in raw core space changes the norm, so either the full \(\partial\log Z\) term is retained or the parameterization/optimization explicitly enforces the constraint and retraction.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Learning balances data amplitudes against a globally contracted normalizer. Canonicalization, environment scaling, sweeps, and rank adaptation control numerics and capacity but do not remove nonconvexity. Feature maps extend the same algebra to continuous and mixed variables when their base measures and Gram contractions are explicit. Chapter 5 places these models relative to HMMs, circuits, autoregression, graphical models, and quantum substrates.
