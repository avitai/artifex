    6. Comparison Protocol and Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the complete probability-path vocabulary. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Type every relationship claim: label agreement at endpoint, coupling, marginal-path, trajectory-law, objective, field, or finite-algorithm level.
    2. O2 — Build a complete error ledger: separate endpoint, statistical, optimization, truncation, discretization, stochastic, and systems effects.
    3. O3 — Match cost honestly: report network evaluations, solver stages, guidance calls, stochastic corrections, wall time, memory, and hardware.
    4. O4 — Design isolating ablations: change coupling, path, target, network, or solver one at a time and state what cannot be isolated.
    5. O5 — Calibrate evidence maturity: distinguish classical theorem, peer-reviewed scoped result, preprint, implementation report, and conjecture.
    6. O6 — Transfer to a reproducible study: write a falsifiable claim with frozen protocol, uncertainty, failure criteria, and artifact checklist.

    Relationship audit¤

    Table PATH.2. Relationship audit
    Claim Correct label Required assumptions
    reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE share \(p_t\) distributional/dynamical equivalence exact score, regularity, exact integration
    conditional and marginal flow objectives have same minimizer population-optimum correspondence correct sampling/path and squared regression
    velocity/noise/data/score targets convert linearly algebraic equivalence same Gaussian path, nonsingular coefficients
    SB approaches OT as noise vanishes limiting distributional relationship reference scaling and convergence conditions
    diffusion and FM code both call a U-Net/DiT implementation analogy no probabilistic equivalence implied
    diffusion prior plus flow distillation hybrid composition separately trained components and error budget

    The ladder is not a universal logical chain: two objectives can share a population optimum without having the same trajectory law, and two different fields can produce the same marginal path. Its purpose is diagnostic. Name the object on each side of an equality before deciding what follows.

    Evaluation checklist¤

    Report endpoint distributions, coupling, intermediate path, state space, training target, and exact sampler. Measure vector/score error where synthetic truth exists, endpoint discrepancy, path energy/action, likelihood where defined, NFE, rejected steps, wall clock, memory, hardware, and sensitivity to tolerances. For stochastic solvers, include variance and random-number cost; for deterministic solvers, include inversion and Jacobian costs where relevant.

    Never compare “one step” without saying whether a step is a network evaluation, solver stage, predictor–corrector pair, or full distilled map.

    Error ledger and attribution order¤

    Use the following order because later layers can mask earlier ones:

    Table PATH.20. Error ledger and attribution order
    Layer Question Isolation strategy
    target law Are source, target, conditioning, and preprocessing equal? hash datasets/config; analytic endpoints where possible
    coupling and path Are paired endpoints and \(p_t\) equal? synthetic path oracle; change one construction at a time
    target estimator Does supervision average to the claimed field? conditional-to-marginal calculation; held-out oracle
    statistical/optimization How close is the learned field? capacity, data, seed, and convergence sweeps
    continuous truncation Is a singular or infinite endpoint omitted? separate horizon or \(\varepsilon\) sweep
    finite solver Does refinement approach the continuous reference? order/tolerance study; positivity and constraint audits
    stochastic estimator What variance comes from paths, traces, or minibatches? replicate seeds and report estimator SE
    systems What do equal NFE and equal wall time actually buy? stages, guidance calls, precision, memory, hardware

    When a metric \(d\) obeys a triangle inequality, a bound such as \(d(p_{\text{out}},p_*)\le e_{\text{endpoint}}+e_{\text{field}}+e_{\text{solver}}\) is an upper bound, not an additive causal decomposition. Interactions must be measured with ablations.

    Falsifiable claim template¤

    Under [endpoint data and preprocessing], [coupling/path], [conditioning], and [training budget], method A improves [named metric] over [tuned baseline] by [effect and interval] at [NFE, solver stages, wall time, memory, hardware]. The claim fails if [registered criterion] occurs and does not assert equality or superiority outside [state space, solver, and evidence boundary].

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • Tilt Matching, ICML 2026 spotlight, derives a stochastic-interpolant velocity update for tilted targets with a lower-variance objective in its setting—Peer-reviewed research.
    • Count Bridges, ICLR 2026, extends bridge/flow ideas to count-valued transcriptomic modeling and deconvolution—Peer-reviewed research with domain- and observation-model-specific evidence.
    • Minibatch OT in Discrete Flow Matching, ICML 2026, develops a discrete transport objective and perplexity-bound estimation—Peer-reviewed research; discrete stochastic paths do not inherit continuous rectification unchanged.
    • Manifold Flow Matching, ICML 2026, supplies intrinsic-structure sample-complexity analysis for a particular model class—Peer-reviewed research.

    Withdrawn, merely submitted, and workshop-only bridge/interpolant papers are excluded from this peer-reviewed tier.

    The tier label answers only what review process the cited result passed. It does not expand a theorem beyond its assumptions or turn one benchmark into generic superiority. Classical results and established textbooks remain the foundation; 2026 papers are scoped checkpoints at the frontier.

    Worked cost audit: equal nominal NFE, unequal execution¤

    Suppose Euler uses 20 steps and one field call per step, while Heun uses 10 steps and two stages per step. Without guidance, both use 20 network evaluations. With classifier-free guidance implemented as separate conditional and unconditional calls, both use 40 calls. They still need not have equal wall time: Heun performs two state stages, implementations may fuse guidance differently, and precision, batching, memory traffic, rejection, or stochastic correctors can differ. The correct report includes calls, stages, wall time, memory, hardware, and output error.

    Independent practice and assessment map: comparison and evidence¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before classifying: seven claims. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. Classify these statements by the strongest object actually shared: same source/target; same OT endpoint plan; same \(p_t\); same transition kernels; same population minimizer; same exact velocity; byte-identical finite outputs under fixed seeds.
    2. P1.2 — Normalize compute units. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Method A uses 20 Euler steps with one network call each. Method B uses 10 Heun steps with two calls each. Both use classifier-free guidance requiring conditional and unconditional calls. Compute nominal network calls and explain why wall time may still differ.
    3. P1.3 — Interpret an error bound. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Suppose a metric bound gives endpoint error \(0.05\), field term \(0.08\), and solver term \(0.03\). Compute the bound and explain why it neither proves observed error \(0.16\) nor assigns causal percentages.
    4. P1.4 — Design an isolating matrix. Type: design · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 14 min. Build the minimum controlled experiments needed to estimate the main effects of two couplings and two conditional paths before changing solver. State what a four-cell design still cannot identify without replication or interaction analysis.
    5. P1.5 — Calibrate a frontier statement. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. Rewrite “manifold flow matching escapes the curse of dimensionality” into a claim supported by the listed ICML 2026 source, and list two transfers the source does not establish.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a registered benchmark. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 24 min. Draft a preregistration comparing reverse SDE, probability-flow ODE, flow matching, and a bridge on an analytic Gaussian-mixture path and one real dataset. Include hypotheses, oracles, ablations, compute, uncertainty, failure rules, and release artifacts.

    Feedback contract: comparison and evidence¤

    Table PATH.21. Feedback contract: comparison and evidence — table 1
    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 Each statement is assigned to a precise shared object without downward transfer.
    P1.2 Both methods have 40 guided network calls, while stage overhead and systems effects remain explicit.
    P1.3 The upper bound \(0.16\) is separated from realized error and causal attribution.
    P1.4 A \(2\times2\) coupling/path design is frozen before solver changes; interaction limits are named.
    P1.5 The claim is restricted to the paper's model class, assumptions, and proved quantities.
    P1.6 Every headline comparison has an oracle, control, cost unit, interval, failure rule, and artifact.
    Graduated hints for P1
    Table PATH.22. Feedback contract: comparison and evidence — table 2
    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup
    P1.1 Move from the first row of the six-object contract to the last. Byte equality is a finite-algorithm claim, not merely distributional equality.
    P1.2 Heun has two stages and CFG doubles each stage. Count network calls before comparing wall time.
    P1.3 Apply the triangle bound once. A bound can be loose and its terms can interact.
    P1.4 Cross coupling and path as two factors. Replicate seeds within all four cells and preserve identical training budgets.
    P1.5 Read the subject and quantifiers in the source claim. Say “specified flow-matching transformers under the paper's manifold assumptions.”
    P1.6 Separate analytic verification from real-data evaluation. Freeze metrics, solver sweeps, tuning budget, seeds, exclusions, and stopping rules in advance.

    Outcome-to-task alignment: comparison and evidence¤

    Table PATH.23. Outcome-to-task alignment: comparison and evidence
    Learning objective Independently assessed by
    O1 — Type every relationship claim P1.1, P1.5
    O2 — Build a complete error ledger P1.3, P1.6
    O3 — Match cost honestly P1.2, P1.6

    O5 — Calibrate evidence maturity P1.5, P1.6
    O6 — Transfer to a reproducible study P1.6
    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The statements respectively share endpoint laws, endpoint coupling, marginal path, trajectory/transition law, population optimum, exact field, and finite algorithm/output. Each stronger statement may imply weaker distributional consequences under assumptions, but the reverse transfers do not follow. Check. “Same \(p_t\)” stops before transition-law equality.
    2. P1.2. A has \(20\times1\times2=40\) network calls. B has \(10\times2\times2=40\). Wall time can differ because Heun stages have state-update overhead and memory traffic, batching may differ, adaptive or fused implementations change utilization, and nominal calls may use different networks or precision. Check. Equal NFE is necessary here for call matching but insufficient for systems matching.
    3. P1.3. The bound is \(0.05+0.08+0.03=0.16\). It says realized error is at most \(0.16\) under the bound's assumptions; cancellations or looseness may make it smaller. The three numbers are bound terms, not experimentally identified causal shares, and changing one layer can alter another. Check. Report the metric and assumptions with the bound.
    4. P1.4. Train the four cells \((C_1,P_1),(C_1,P_2),(C_2,P_1),(C_2,P_2)\) with fixed endpoints, architecture, optimizer, update count, time sampling, seeds, and evaluation solver. Replicated seeds permit main-effect and coupling-by-path interaction estimates. Without replication, random training variation is confounded; without interaction analysis, averaged main effects can hide opposite cell behavior. Check. Solver changes belong in a later crossed study on frozen checkpoints.
    5. P1.5. A calibrated statement is: “For the specified flow-matching transformer and manifold/path assumptions, the ICML 2026 paper derives approximation, estimation, and distribution bounds whose complexity depends on intrinsic structure.” It does not establish that every architecture adapts to unknown manifolds or that every real dataset and finite solver avoids ambient-dimensional cost. Check. Benchmark evidence also remains tied to the reported protocol.
    6. P1.6. Register an analytic phase with exact Gaussian-mixture marginals, scores, velocities, bridge/reference, and high-accuracy samplers, followed by a real-data phase with frozen preprocessing and metrics. Cross path/coupling and model family, sweep solver/tolerance on frozen fields, match calls/stages/tuning/hardware, and report seeds, intervals, failures, endpoint/field/solver/system metrics. Predeclare exclusion and stopping rules and release configs, checkpoints, seeds, logs, environment, exact-oracle code, and evaluation outputs. Rubric. Conclusions must separate theorem verification, synthetic approximation, and real-data empirical ranking.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Construct two couplings of identical Gaussian endpoints and plot their intermediate variances.
    2. Derive the probability-flow ODE from the Fokker–Planck equation.
    3. Simulate an exact Gaussian score with Euler solvers and separate discretization from model error.
    4. Compare independent and OT minibatch couplings by conditional-velocity variance.
    5. Solve a finite-state Schrödinger bridge under two reference chains and compare path laws.
    6. Take a published “diffusion equals flow” statement and classify each equality by object: endpoint, marginal path, vector parameterization, or finite sampler.
    Exercise 1 solution

    With \(X_1=X_0\), variance remains one. With \(X_1=-X_0\), \(X_t=(1-2t)X_0\) and variance is \((1-2t)^2\), collapsing at the midpoint. Both couplings have standard-normal endpoints.

    Cumulative retrieval: probability paths
    1. List the six levels at which two path-based generators can agree or disagree.
    2. Why is equal NFE not necessarily equal compute?
    3. Which ablation isolates coupling choice from path-family choice?
    4. What evidence would support same finite algorithm rather than only same continuous-time marginals?

    Solutions. (1) Endpoints, couplings, marginal paths, particle trajectories or transition laws, training objectives, and finite algorithms. (2) Function evaluations can differ in network size, precision, batching, guidance calls, adaptivity, and auxiliary computation. (3) Hold endpoints, conditional path family, model, optimizer, and budget fixed while changing only the coupling. (4) A theorem or controlled implementation comparison covering discretization, initialization, randomness, score approximation, and matching outputs at the declared tolerance and cost.

    Final synthesis¤

    Probability-path frameworks connect equations and training targets. They do not erase coupling choice, stochasticity, reference dynamics, state-space geometry, or numerical error. The safest comparison names the exact object that is shared and tests the finite algorithm that is actually deployed. The Optimal Transport chapter supplies the Monge, Kantorovich, Wasserstein, entropic, and dynamic-transport contracts used here.

    Continue to the general Markov generators volume, which treats deterministic flows, diffusions, jump processes, and discrete CTMCs through their infinitesimal generators.

    That continuation is available in General Markov Generators, including the conditional-to-marginal matching theorem and the distinction between same-path superposition and arbitrary operator addition.
