    3. Trajectory and Subtrajectory Balance¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–2, log probabilities, and importance-sampling support intuition. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. derive trajectory balance from compatible flow ratios and prove reward proportionality by summing normalized backward paths;
    2. explain how the backward kernel allocates one object's reward among histories without changing the exact terminal target;
    3. derive the log-squared TB residual, learned-normalizer scale test, and positive-reward support requirement;
    4. derive SubTB on arbitrary partial trajectories and distinguish its weighting/normalization conventions from its zero-residual identities;
    5. compare DB, TB, and SubTB credit-assignment and gradient bias–variance behavior without turning empirical observations into universal theorems; and
    6. design on-policy, exploratory, replay, or offline training with explicit coverage and identifiability limits.

    Trajectory balance imposes one constraint on every complete trajectory \(\tau\) ending at \(x\):

    \[ Z_\theta\prod_{t=0}^{n-1}P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) =R(x)\prod_{t=0}^{n-1}P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \tag{GFN.1}\label{eq:gflownet-trajectory-balance} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:gflownet-trajectory-balance}\) compares the complete forward and backward trajectory probabilities after accounting for the source normalizer and terminal reward. The standard log-squared loss is

    \[ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{TB}}(\tau)= \left[ \log Z_\theta +\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}\log P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) -\log R(x) -\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}\log P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}) \right]^2. \]

    At zero loss for all relevant trajectories under the theorem's assumptions, the forward terminal distribution is proportional to reward and \(Z_\theta\) equals the partition function.

    The phrase “all relevant trajectories” is essential. A neural training run observes a finite sample and optimizes a nonconvex empirical objective. Zero residual on a minibatch is neither zero residual on the graph nor proof that all positive-reward terminals have been reached.

    Why the Backward Product Appears¤

    The forward product is trajectory probability. Multiplying terminal reward by the backward trajectory probability assigns the object's reward among its construction histories. Summing the TB identity over all backward paths to \(x\) gives \(ZP_F(x)=R(x)\) because backward path probabilities sum to one.

    More explicitly, define

    \[ P_F(\tau)=\prod_tP_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t), \qquad P_B(\tau\mid x)=\prod_tP_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \]

    Then exact TB says \(P_F(\tau)=R(x)P_B(\tau\mid x)/Z\). Summing over \(\tau\to x\) gives

    \[ P_F(x)=\frac{R(x)}Z \underbrace{\sum_{\tau\to x}P_B(\tau\mid x)}_{1}. \]

    The backward kernel therefore supplies a normalized allocation of object reward over histories. It can be fixed—for example, uniform over valid parents—or learned. A learned \(P_B\) may reduce variance or represent asymmetric construction histories, but it also introduces another model whose normalization, support, and compensation effects require diagnosis.

    Worked TB Calculation¤

    In the diamond graph, take \(Z=4\), \(P_F(a\mid s_0)=1/4\), \(P_F(x\mid a)=1\), and \(P_B(a\mid x)=1/3\). For trajectory \(s_0\to a\to x\),

    \[ 4\left(\frac14\right)(1) =3\left(\frac13\right)(1)=1. \]

    For the path through \(b\), \(P_F(b\mid s_0)=1/2\) and \(P_B(b\mid x)=2/3\), so both sides equal 2. TB does not demand equal probability for paths to the same object; it demands agreement with the backward allocation.

    Learning the Normalizer¤

    TB often treats \(\log Z_\theta\) as a free scalar, or as a context-dependent network output in conditional models. It receives gradients on every complete trajectory. If all rewards are multiplied by a constant \(c\), the correct policy is unchanged while the correct normalizer becomes \(cZ\). This scale test is a useful implementation invariant: terminal frequencies should remain stable and learned \(\log Z\) should shift by \(\log c\).

    Subtrajectory Balance¤

    For any partial trajectory \(s_m\to\cdots\to s_n\),

    \[ F(s_m)\prod_{t=m}^{n-1}P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) =F(s_n)\prod_{t=m}^{n-1}P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \]

    Length-one subtrajectories recover detailed balance; full source-to-terminal subtrajectories recover trajectory balance. SubTB(\(\lambda\)) weights multiple subtrajectory lengths, trading local bootstrapping against long-range credit.

    One generic log residual for \(s_m\to\cdots\to s_n\) is

    \[ \delta_{m:n}= \log F_\theta(s_m) +\sum_{t=m}^{n-1}\log P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) -\log F_\theta(s_n) -\sum_{t=m}^{n-1}\log P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \]

    A SubTB objective averages \(\delta_{m:n}^2\) with declared weights, often depending on length through a hyperparameter \(\lambda\). Papers and libraries use different normalization conventions—per trajectory, per subtrajectory, or per length—so writing only “SubTB(\(\lambda\))” is not enough to reproduce a result.

    In the original SubTB(\(\lambda\)) convention for one length-\(T\) episode, each nonempty subtrajectory \(i{:}j\) receives unnormalized weight \(\lambda^{j-i}\) and the weighted losses are divided by the sum of those weights. Under that convention, \(\lambda\to0^+\) retains the averaged length-one DB terms, \(lambda=1\) weights all subtrajectories uniformly, and \(\lambda\to\infty\) retains the full-trajectory TB term. Batch-global versus per-episode normalization is a further declared choice. These limits describe that estimator convention; they do not say finite-\(\lambda\) gradients equal a convex combination of already-optimized DB and TB models.

    At exact satisfaction:

    • length-one SubTB constraints are DB constraints;
    • every-subtrajectory balance implies full TB by choosing \(m=0,n=T\);
    • full TB alone fixes terminal path ratios but need not identify a unique intermediate state-flow parameterization.

    Constraint length changes where credit enters and which auxiliary quantities are learned. It does not change the exact terminal target when the respective global constraints and support assumptions are satisfied.

    Bias–Variance and Optimization¤

    Long constraints communicate terminal reward directly but combine many log probabilities and can have high variance. Local constraints provide dense updates but bootstrap through inaccurate state flows. Subtrajectory objectives interpolate. These are empirical/optimization trade-offs; all exact balance families target the same reward-proportional terminal law when their constraints are satisfied.

    Trajectory length can also change numerical scale. A sum of 100 log probabilities has different variance and gradient geometry from a sum of 5. Normalizing the residual by length changes the optimization objective and may change which trajectories dominate; it should be reported, not silently treated as algebraically equivalent TB. PowerFlow proposes a specific length-aware TB construction for autoregressive language-model distribution matching. That is peer-reviewed research evidence for one formulation, not a universal fix for length bias.

    Zero Rewards¤

    TB contains \(\log R(x)\), so zero reward is undefined. Restricting support to positive-reward terminals, using an explicit failure sink, or adding \(\epsilon\) are different modeling choices. Adding \(\epsilon\) changes \(P(x)\) by giving formerly impossible objects nonzero mass.

    Off-Policy Training¤

    Trajectories may come from exploratory or replay behavior rather than the current \(P_F\). Balance losses can be evaluated off-policy, but sampling distribution affects which constraints receive updates and the variance/bias of stochastic optimization. Full support over important transitions is essential.

    TB's pointwise residual can be computed on an off-policy trajectory without the ordinary policy-gradient requirement that the trajectory came from \(P_F\). That does not mean arbitrary logged data identify the global solution. If logged prefixes omit actions, multiple forward policies may fit every observed SubTB equation while allocating different mass to unsupported branches. Conservative GFlowNets for offline recommendation, accepted at ICML 2026, formalizes such non-identifiability for a dataset-induced token-prefix DAG and distinguishes flow overestimation, forward leakage, and backward compensation. Its correction is domain/formulation-specific; the general lesson is to state what support the data constrain.

    Importance weights can change which expectation is estimated, but cannot manufacture information about transitions of zero behavior probability. Large ratios can also make variance unacceptable. Replay contents, behavior policy, priority rule, and any correction weights are part of the algorithm specification.

    This distinction is easier to maintain with the gradient contract: the gradient of a sampled balance residual, a policy gradient of expected terminal reward, and an importance-corrected off-policy estimator have different objectives or averaging laws even when they reuse the same trajectories.

    Divergence Views¤

    Balance losses are squared log-ratio objectives, not automatically a named \(f\)-divergence between terminal distributions. Under specified forward/backward trajectory measures, one can derive variational or divergence relationships. On Divergence Measures for Training GFlowNets extends the hierarchical variational-inference view and studies high-variance stochastic divergence gradients with control variates. Its results compare estimators and finite tasks; they do not make TB literally reverse KL in every parameterization.

    Chapter 3 retrieval Questions¤

    1. Why does the backward path sum equal one?
    2. What happens to \(Z\) and \(P_F(x)\) when every reward is multiplied by 10?
    3. Why can off-policy evaluation of a residual still fail to identify the target?
    Complete solutions
    1. Starting at terminal \(x\), a normalized backward kernel defines a probability distribution over parent choices until the unique source; the probabilities of all complete reverse paths sum to one.
    2. \(Z\) becomes \(10Z\), while the normalized terminal law is unchanged.
    3. Logged data may have zero support on some states or edges, leaving their balance equations unconstrained and permitting multiple globally different solutions.

    Chapter 3 Synthesis¤

    Trajectory balance matches complete forward and backward path probabilities to reward; subtrajectory balance applies the same conservation identity at every scale. Chapter 4 interprets \(Z\), conditionals, uncertainty, and Bayesian inference.

    Does a low average TB loss prove the terminal distribution is correct?

    No. Unsampled modes or trajectories may have large violations, and squared log residuals can hide where probability mass is wrong. Evaluate the terminal distribution on tractable test environments and mode/partition diagnostics.

    Independent practice and assessment map: path balance¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. An exact TB model is retrained after every terminal reward is multiplied by 5. Predict the changes to \(P_F\), \(P_B\), \(log Z\), and every TB residual if only \(log Z\) is shifted appropriately; verify algebraically.
    2. P1.2 — Backward allocation proof. Terminal \(x\) has reward 6 and three construction paths. Choose backward path probabilities \((1/2,1/3,1/6)\), derive the required forward path probabilities when \(Z=12\), and sum both sides to recover \(P_F(x)\).
    3. P1.3 — TB residual calculation. For one trajectory, let \(Z=10\), \(P_F(\tau)=0.06\), \(R(x)=2\), and \(P_B(\tau\mid x)=0.20\). Compute the unsquared log residual, its loss, the multiplicative mismatch, and the value of \(P_F(\tau)\) that would make this path exact while holding the other quantities fixed.
    4. P1.4 — SubTB weighting. A length-three episode has six nonempty subtrajectories. Under original weights \(\lambda^{j-i}\) with \(\lambda=2\), compute total weight assigned to lengths 1, 2, and 3 and the normalized weight of each group. Then state the \(lambda\to0^+\) and \(lambda\to\infty\) limits.
    5. P1.5 — Credit-assignment audit. On a length-100 chain with only terminal reward, compare the information route, learned auxiliary quantities, and plausible gradient variance for DB, TB, and SubTB. Identify which statements are algebraic and which remain empirical optimization hypotheses.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. A logged token-prefix dataset never contains one valid source action. Construct two forward policies that agree on every logged transition and observed SubTB residual but allocate different mass after the missing action. State what importance weighting can and cannot repair and propose a support-aware evaluation.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. Multiplying \(R\) by 5 leaves the normalized target and therefore a compatible \(P_F\) unchanged; \(P_B\) may remain unchanged as well. Set \(Z'=5Z\), so \(log Z'=log Z+log5\). Both sides of TB multiply by 5 and every log residual remains zero. Shifting neither the policy nor \(log Z\) would instead introduce residual \(-\log5\).
    2. Exact TB requires \(P_F(\tau_i)=R(x)P_B(\tau_i\mid x)/Z\). The three probabilities are \(1/4,1/6,1/12\). Their sum is \(1/2\), equal to \(R(x)/Z=6/12\). The unequal path law is intentional; the normalized backward allocation sums the object's one reward mass rather than replicating it.
    3. The ratio is \(ZP_F/(RP_B)=10(0.06)/(2(0.20))=1.5\), so the unsquared residual is \(\log1.5\approx0.4055\) and the loss is about \(0.1644\). Exactness requires \(P_F=RP_B/Z=0.04\). The factor 1.5 is a path-equation mismatch, not by itself a terminal total-variation distance.
    4. There are three length-one spans, two length-two spans, and one length-three span. Their group weights are \(3(2)=6\), \(2(4)=8\), and \(1(8)=8\), totaling 22; normalized group weights are \(3/11,4/11,4/11\). As \(\lambda\to0^+\), normalized mass remains on the length-one DB terms; as \(\lambda\to\infty\), it remains on the unique full TB term.
    5. DB sends information through adjacent state-flow bootstraps and learns \(F(s)\) plus both kernels; its residuals are local and terminal information may propagate slowly. TB connects source and terminal in every sampled full path and learns \(Z\) plus the kernels; a 100-term log sum can be noisy. SubTB also learns state flows and uses selected spans to trade these effects. The identities defining their zero-residual solutions are algebraic. Claims about faster convergence, lower variance, or better generalization depend on parameterization, sampling, and task and are empirical.
    6. Keep all logged-edge probabilities and flows fixed. Policy A assigns zero probability to the unseen source action; policy B diverts positive source mass to it and chooses any continuation on the unsupported branch, with compensating unconstrained flows/backward terms. No logged equation distinguishes them. Importance ratios can reweight events with positive behavior probability but cannot reveal a zero-probability action. Evaluate supported and unsupported mass separately, enumerate a small induced DAG, report behavior coverage, and add on-policy or externally justified exploration before claiming global identification.
