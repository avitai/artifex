    4. Parameter and Structure Learning¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand mixture-model EM, maximum likelihood, softmax parameterization, and validation-based model selection. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive sum-edge EM updates from posterior circuit flows;
    2. compare EM, gradient, and minibatch updates without calling any of them universally stable;
    3. separate parameter count from effective circuit capacity and structure;
    4. explain recursive, random/regular, pruning/growing, and distillation-based structure learning; and
    5. audit distributed or tensorized scaling claims independently from probabilistic tractability.

    Parameter Learning with Fixed Structure¤

    For normalized sum weights, maximum likelihood can use projected/softmax gradient optimization or EM. In EM, latent branch choices receive posterior responsibilities in the E-step; the M-step normalizes expected edge counts:

    \[ w_{nc}^{\mathrm{new}} =\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\gamma_{i,nc}} {\sum_{c'}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\gamma_{i,nc'}}. \]

    Leaf parameters update from responsibility-weighted sufficient statistics. Dirichlet or other priors add pseudo-counts and regularization. Deterministic circuits can make branch assignments hard; nondeterministic circuits usually require soft responsibilities.

    The update follows from maximizing the expected complete-trace log likelihood. If \(N_{nc}=\sum_i\gamma_{i,nc}\) is the expected count for edge \((n,c)\), the constrained problem

    \[ \max_{w_{n\cdot}}\sum_c N_{nc}\log w_{nc} \quad\text{subject to}\quad w_{nc}\ge0,\ \sum_c w_{nc}=1 \]

    has solution \(w_{nc}=N_{nc}/\sum_{c'}N_{nc'}\). With a Dirichlet prior, pseudo-count conventions must be stated. If a sum node receives zero total flow, the maximum-likelihood update is undefined; retaining old weights, smoothing, pruning, or reinitializing are different algorithmic choices.

    Gradient learning often parameterizes \(w_{nc}=\operatorname{softmax}(a_n)_c\). It optimizes the observed-data log likelihood directly and composes naturally with neural leaves, but minibatch gradients can have high variance and redundant latent states can create flat or symmetric directions. EM's monotonic full-batch likelihood property under exact E/M steps does not transfer automatically to generalized, stochastic, truncated, or regularized EM.

    Identifiability, redundancy, and regularization¤

    Permuting latent sum children leaves the represented distribution unchanged. Duplicate children create continua of equivalent weights. Very narrow continuous leaves can produce pathological likelihood behavior, and unused subcircuits receive almost no posterior flow. These are model/optimization facts, not inference errors.

    Regularization choices target different failures:

    • Dirichlet pseudo-counts discourage exactly zero mixture weights;
    • entropy penalties can encourage or discourage diffuse routing;
    • leaf priors or variance floors control degenerate local densities;
    • pruning removes persistently low-flow edges;
    • diversity or distillation terms may break redundant latent symmetry.

    Each changes the learned objective. Report it rather than describing the result simply as maximum likelihood.

    A fixed-structure full-batch EM iteration is:

    zero expected edge counts and leaf sufficient statistics
for observation x:
    run upward values and downward flows
    accumulate normalized posterior edge flows
    accumulate responsibility-weighted leaf statistics
normalize outgoing expected counts at every sum node
fit each leaf from its weighted sufficient statistics

    The exact messages can be parallelized over observations, but the global normalization and parameter synchronization still have systems costs.

    Peer-reviewed research: Liu, Shao, and Van den Broeck (AISTATS 2026 Oral) reinterpret full-batch PC EM as optimizing a first-order approximation regularized by a KL penalty on the distributional update. Their anemone minibatch method adaptively scales each parameter update by its contribution to batch likelihood, addressing the paper's diagnosis that ordinary minibatch EM/gradient updates can overfit the current minibatch. The reported language, image, and DNA improvements concern fixed circuit families and optimizer comparisons; they do not solve structure learning, initialization, or every hybrid continuous-latent circuit.

    Structure Learning¤

    Structure determines both expressivity and available queries. Common strategies include:

    • recursively split data into clusters for sum nodes and variables into approximately independent groups for product nodes;
    • start from a random or region-graph structure and optimize parameters;
    • grow, prune, or locally restructure a circuit under a validation objective;
    • compile from a graphical model, logical theory, or vtree;
    • distill latent assignments or representations from a less tractable teacher.

    LearnSPN-style recursive learning is efficient but greedy. SoftLearn replaces hard routing during structure induction with soft clustering and analyzes the relation between learning and inference semantics. Sparse PCs via pruning and growing show that nominally large dense structures can waste capacity and that learned sparsity matters.

    These strategies optimize different objects. Recursive partitioning makes irreversible local decisions. A fixed random region graph delegates structure to a prior and spends learning on parameters. Pruning/growing edits a superstructure. Distillation injects teacher-chosen latent organization. Comparing them requires matching parameter/edge budgets, leaf families, preprocessing, optimization steps, validation searches, and query requirements.

    Regular Structures and Einsum Networks¤

    Einsum Networks organize large numbers of circuit operations into dense tensor contractions. The probabilistic semantics still come from sum/product scopes; einsum is an efficient implementation strategy, not a new density family. Regular region graphs improve accelerator utilization but impose a structural prior on how variables interact.

    Peer-reviewed research: Scaling Probabilistic Circuits via Data Partitioning introduces federated circuits for horizontal, vertical, and hybrid data partitions. Its UAI 2025 experiments address distributed learning and classification/density tasks. They do not imply that arbitrary advanced queries are communication-free, that privacy follows without a threat model, or that the learned global circuit has the same size as a centrally optimized alternative.

    Worked example: a small EM update¤

    Return to the two-component circuit from Chapter 3. For observation \((X=1,Y=1)\), component likelihoods are \(0.56\) and \(0.02\). The posterior responsibility of component A is

    \[ \gamma_A= \frac{0.6(0.56)}{0.6(0.56)+0.4(0.02)} =\frac{0.336}{0.344}\approx0.977. \]

    One point should not drive a reliable update, but it shows the mechanism: exact upward evaluation supplies soft latent counts; normalized counts update mixture weights; weighted leaf statistics update distributions.

    With a second observation, sum the two posterior responsibilities before normalizing. Updating the root weight separately after each example is online/stochastic EM, not the displayed full-batch M-step; its order, step-size, and regularization affect the result.

    Generalization and Overfitting¤

    Exact training likelihood does not prevent overfitting. Circuits can memorize through narrow leaves, excessive mixtures, or near-deterministic partitions. Use held-out log likelihood, calibration, missing-data queries, sample quality, and circuit size. Structure search on a validation set introduces its own multiple-comparison bias.

    Independent practice and assessment map: circuit learning¤

    Keep the represented distribution, latent parameterization, optimizer, structure search, and execution system in separate columns. A likelihood change does not by itself identify which layer caused it.

    P1 — Audit a fixed- and learned-structure training pipeline¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each claim follows: full-batch exact EM never decreases observed likelihood; the same guarantee holds for minibatch, truncated, regularized, or generalized EM; softmax weights remove latent-state non-identifiability; a tensorized circuit has a different probabilistic semantics; exact training likelihood prevents overfitting; and a distributed circuit automatically provides privacy. State the exact assumptions or missing experiment for every answer.
    2. P1.2 — Complete one EM iteration. A two-child sum starts with equal weights. For three observations, the fixed child likelihood pairs are \((0.72,0.08)\), \((0.18,0.42)\), and \((0.10,0.50)\). Compute every posterior responsibility, expected edge count, and normalized M-step weight. Evaluate the observed log likelihood before and after the update, and explain which conclusion would cease to follow if the responsibilities were stale or minibatch-scaled.
    3. P1.3 — Zero-flow and optimizer contract. Construct a sum node that receives zero total posterior flow. Compare retaining its weights, adding declared Dirichlet pseudo-counts, pruning, and reinitializing; write the objective or model change made by each. Then compare projected, softmax-gradient, full-batch EM, stochastic EM, and an adaptive minibatch method using matched initialization, data order, updates, precision, and stopping rules.
    4. P1.4 — Capacity and identifiability falsification. Duplicate one child of a normalized mixture and derive the continuum of weights that leaves the represented distribution unchanged. Add a narrow continuous leaf and show how its likelihood can become pathological. Design separate diagnostics for parameter count, active edges, posterior flow, leaf scale, effective capacity, held-out likelihood, and calibration; do not attribute a flat direction to inference failure.
    5. P1.5 — Controlled structure-learning comparison. Compare recursive data/variable splitting, a fixed random region graph, pruning/growing, and teacher distillation under matched edge/parameter budgets, leaf family, preprocessing, optimizer steps, and validation searches. Record irreversible decisions and search degrees of freedom, then design a nested-validation or held-out protocol that exposes adaptive overfitting.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Transfer the pipeline from one centralized tabular dataset to either horizontally partitioned sensor records or vertically partitioned clinical variables. Declare the global probability object, local data access, synchronization, threat model, communication bytes, compilation time, peak memory, and supported queries. Separate a systems speedup from privacy, statistical equivalence, and preserved tractability.

    Chapter 4 Synthesis¤

    Fixed-structure learning resembles mixture-model EM or differentiable maximum likelihood. Structure learning chooses the independence and mixture decompositions that make exact inference possible. Tensorization and distributed partitioning change systems behavior, while the circuit properties still determine queries. Chapter 5 studies how neural and flow components enlarge that language without silently losing guarantees.

    Retrieval practice: learning circuits
    1. What quantity supplies an EM edge count?
    2. When does EM guarantee non-decreasing observed likelihood?
    3. Does GPU-friendly tensorization create tractability?
    4. Why is a structure learner's validation score optimistically biased after a large search?
    5. What does the 2026 anemone result not solve?

    Solutions. (1) The sum of posterior edge-flow responsibilities over observations. (2) Under the standard full-batch assumptions with an exact E-step and an exact or improving M-step; stochastic/generalized/regularized variants need separate analysis. (3) No. It accelerates a circuit whose scopes already satisfy the properties. (4) The same validation set selected among many structures/hyperparameters, creating multiple-comparison/adaptive-overfitting bias. (5) It addresses fixed-structure parameter optimization, not structure discovery, initialization, all leaf families, or universal generalization.

    Bridge. Learning exposes where tractability can constrain fit. Chapter 5 examines hybrids and expressivity results that change the fit–tractability trade-off without transferring exactness to arbitrary encoders or decoders. Continue continuously or open Chapter 5.
