    Worked Example: MMD for Two Point Masses¤

    Objective-selection capstone learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the discrepancy, support, transport, and information chapters. By the end, you should be able to compute MMD on a finite example, convert a calibrated classifier into a density-ratio estimate and generator update, choose an objective from available information and support geometry, and use the checklist to separate the population discrepancy from its estimator, optimization, and deployed sampling procedure.

    Let \(p=\delta_0\), \(q=\delta_a\), and use the RBF kernel \(k(x,y)=\exp(-(x-y)^2/(2\sigma^2))\). Then

    \[ \operatorname{MMD}^2(p,q) =2-2\exp\left(-\frac{a^2}{2\sigma^2}\right). \]

    For \(|a|\gg\sigma\), MMD approaches \(2\) and its gradient with respect to \(a\) becomes small; for \(\sigma\) too large, distinct points look similar. Multi-scale kernels mitigate but do not remove representation and bandwidth choices.

    Worked Example: From a Classifier to a Ratio, Value, and Generator Step¤

    Let the data law be \(p=(0.75,0.25)\) and let the model family be \(q_\theta=(\theta,1-\theta)\) on outcomes \((a,b)\). Begin at \(\theta=0.5\) and construct a balanced real-versus-generated classification problem. Its joint class masses are

    Table OBJ.4. Worked Example: From a Classifier to a Ratio, Value, and Generator Step
    Outcome real class, \(\tfrac12p(x)\) generated class, \(\tfrac12q_{0.5}(x)\)
    \(a\) \(0.375\) \(0.25\)
    \(b\) \(0.125\) \(0.25\)

    Dividing each real-class mass by the total mass at that outcome gives the Bayes classifier:

    \[ D^*(a)=\frac{0.75}{0.75+0.5}=0.6, \qquad D^*(b)=\frac{0.25}{0.25+0.5}=\frac13. \]

    Its odds recover the density ratios:

    \[ \frac{D^*(a)}{1-D^*(a)}=1.5=\frac{p_{\mathrm{data}}(a)}{p_g(a)}, \]
    \[ \frac{D^*(b)}{1-D^*(b)}=0.5=\frac{p_{\mathrm{data}}(b)}{p_g(b)}. \]

    With unequal class priors, these raw odds would also contain the prior odds; the correction \((1-\pi)/\pi\) is essential. The classifier is therefore not merely labeling individual samples. At its population optimum, with declared priors, it represents a pointwise relation between the two laws.

    Now keep the equal-prior convention used by the original GAN value, which omits factors of \(1/2\). Substitution gives

    \[ V(D^*,q_{0.5})=-1.318650 =-\log4+2D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p\|q_{0.5}), \]

    so \(D_{\mathrm{JS}}(p\|q_{0.5})=0.033822\) nats. This is the optimized population value for the current \(q\); it is not yet a prescription for a finite generator update.

    Frozen-Discriminator Losses Are Different Functions¤

    To see why, freeze the two classifier outputs and compare two generator losses. The minimax generator term is

    \[ L_{\mathrm{MM}}(\theta) =\theta\log(1-0.6)+(1-\theta)\log(1-1/3), \]

    whereas the non-saturating loss is

    \[ L_{\mathrm{NS}}(\theta) =-\theta\log0.6-(1-\theta)\log(1/3). \]

    At \(\theta=0.5\) their values and derivatives are

    \[ \begin{array}{c|c|c} &L(0.5)&dL/d\theta\\ \hline L_{\mathrm{MM}}&-0.660878&\log0.6=-0.510826\\ L_{\mathrm{NS}}&0.804719&\log(5/9)=-0.587787. \end{array} \]

    Gradient descent increases \(\theta\) in both frozen-classifier calculations, toward the target value \(0.75\), but the objectives and gradients are not equal. Reoptimizing \(D\) after \(\theta\) changes produces yet another function of \(\theta\). Equating a frozen-discriminator derivative with the derivative of the optimized value requires the relevant envelope-theorem assumptions and the same underlying objective; it cannot be assumed for the non-saturating replacement.

    Finally, a finite classifier can memorize sample identity, and alternating neural updates need not track \(D^*_{q_\theta}\). Held-out discrimination, regularization, generator-side coverage metrics, and game dynamics therefore remain necessary even though every population number in this example is exact.

    Primary and Archival Reading Trail¤

    • Csiszár, Information-Type Measures of Difference of Probability Distributions and Indirect Observations — measure-level divergence constructions and information monotonicity.
    • Sriperumbudur et al., On the Empirical Estimation of Integral Probability Metrics — function-class assumptions and empirical estimation for IPMs.
    • Gretton et al., A Kernel Two-Sample Test — characteristic-kernel MMD, biased and unbiased estimators, null calibration, power, and linear-time tests.
    • Nowozin, Cseke, and Tomioka, f-GAN: Training Generative Neural Samplers Using Variational Divergence Minimization — convex-dual variational bounds and their adversarial implementation.

    Objective Selection Checklist¤

    Before selecting or naming an objective, answer:

    1. What population distributional quantity does it identify, if any?
    2. Does the claim require an optimal critic, score, posterior, or coupling?
    3. Which density/support/reference-measure assumptions are required?
    4. Is the implemented estimator biased, and how does variance scale?
    5. What representation or kernel defines perceptual similarity?
    6. Which finite-sample failure is invisible to the loss?
    7. Which sampler is used at evaluation, and does training control its error?
    8. Are multiple loss terms being presented as one named divergence without proof?

    Retrieval Check¤

    1. When does the original GAN objective equal a JS-divergence expression?
    2. What property of \(\mathcal F\) makes Wasserstein-1 different from an arbitrary critic IPM?
    3. Why can denoising regression learn a marginal score from conditional targets?
    4. Does a characteristic kernel make every finite-sample MMD estimate reliable?
    5. Why may an unbiased MMD U-statistic be negative?
    6. When does conditional target regression learn a marginal vector or score field?
    7. Why is an OT coupling useful in flow matching without making every flow an optimal transport?
    8. Why does Gaussian smoothing not necessarily destroy population identifiability?
    9. Which two errors control the decoded \(W_1\) bound for a two-stage generator?
    10. Why does population identification not guarantee a useful generator update?
    11. Why is a tokenizer's token count not its operational bitrate?
    12. Why does the VAE variational rate upper-bound rather than generally equal \(I_q(X;Z)\)?
    13. What quantity does a bits-back construction recover from a naive latent two-part code?
    14. Why can the non-saturating GAN loss share the distribution-matching fixed point without being the derivative of the optimized JS value?
    15. Why must an \(f\)-divergence define what happens where \(q=0\)?
    16. Which of \(H\) and \(H^2\) is guaranteed to be a metric?
    17. Why is an MMD value computed on a validation minibatch not automatically a level-\(\alpha\) two-sample test?
    18. What must be stated before comparing two reported chi-squared divergences?
    Solutions
    1. After optimizing the discriminator to its population optimum for the current generator.
    2. The critic class is the set of 1-Lipschitz functions.
    3. The squared-error population optimum is the conditional expectation of the conditional target given the noisy state.
    4. No. It gives population identifiability; finite-sample power and gradients still depend on kernel, representation, and sample size.
    5. Finite-sample estimator noise can cross zero even though the population squared discrepancy is nonnegative.
    6. At the population squared-error optimum, under the construction's conditional-expectation identity and weighting assumptions.
    7. It can reduce path curvature and regression variance, but the learned path need not solve the minimum-action OT problem.
    8. Gaussian convolution has a nowhere-zero characteristic function, so equality of the convolved laws implies equality of the original laws. Bandwidth still changes finite-resolution estimation and optimization.
    9. Observation-space reconstruction error and latent-law mismatch, with the latter amplified by the decoder's Lipschitz constant.
    10. A finite estimator may have high variance or low test power, and a restricted or underoptimized witness may provide a poor parameter-space gradient even when the population optimum is unique.
    11. Token count omits symbol probabilities, contextual redundancy, precision, side information, entropy-coder overhead, and even whether the tokens are serialized.
    12. It equals \(I_q(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z)\|p(z))\); prior mismatch supplies a nonnegative extra term.
    13. It recovers information associated with the posterior sampling choice, ideally \(-\log_2q(z\mid x)\) bits, leaving the negative ELBO as the net expected rate.
    14. It replaces the generator half of the minimax objective while holding the current discriminator fixed. Sharing an ideal fixed point does not make its finite-step gradient equal to the derivative obtained after optimizing the discriminator in the original minimax value.
    15. Singular mass can lie where \(P\) is positive and \(Q\) is zero. The extended-value convention determines whether and how that mass contributes; for forward KL it makes the divergence infinite.
    16. \(H\) is a metric. Its square is an \(f\)-divergence but generally fails the triangle inequality.
    17. A hypothesis test additionally needs a declared statistic, null calibration, significance threshold, and treatment of kernel selection and repeated or adaptive inspection.
    18. The orientation of \(P\) and \(Q\), the normalization factor, the common dominating measure or support convention, and the treatment of zero denominator density.

    Summary and Next Step¤

    Objectives expose different information through different estimators. Divergence names apply only when their auxiliary optima and assumptions hold. Support mismatch can be addressed by smoothing, a support-compatible discrepancy, or a learned state space, but each route changes a different object and carries different identification and optimization conditions. Rate–distortion analysis likewise requires separating transmitted bits, model cross-entropy, variational KL, reconstruction cost, perception, and task utility. Continue with Graphical Models and Structured Probability to see how factorization and normalization determine which likelihood, composite, and conditional objectives are available; use Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics to study how a learned distribution or field becomes actual samples; and use Optimization and Learning Dynamics to audit stochastic gradients, games, latent bounds, and transport algorithms separately from their population objectives.
