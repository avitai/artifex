    A diffusion generative model defines a probability path that corrupts data toward a tractable reference law and learns information needed to simulate a reverse-time process or associated deterministic transport. The forward path, prediction target, population-optimal field, reverse dynamics, numerical solver, guidance rule, representation, and deployed system are separate parts of the specification.

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    Diffusion models declare a forward corruption process that moves data toward a reference distribution and learn a reverse-time conditional, score, denoiser, or equivalent field. The forward process is usually fixed, but it can be parameterized and learned jointly when the required marginals or posteriors remain tractable. The discrete construction was introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho, Jain & Abbeel (2020) gave the widely used DDPM parameterization. Score-based SDEs, probability-flow ODEs, discrete diffusion, and flow-related formulations share some population objects under stated assumptions, but their finite algorithms need not coincide.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand Gaussian conditioning, Markov chains, KL divergence, conditional expectation, gradients of log densities, and basic ODE/SDE notation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive the closed-form forward marginal and posterior of a Gaussian DDPM;
    2. distinguish conditional perturbation scores from noisy marginal scores;
    3. derive the ELBO terms and explain why the simplified noise MSE is a reweighted objective;
    4. convert population-optimal noise, data, score, and velocity predictions with stated conventions; and
    5. distinguish marginal equivalence of the reverse SDE/probability-flow ODE from equality of finite numerical samples.

    Components and consequences¤

    The family separates a known forward path from a learned reverse object. This often yields supervised denoising-style regression targets at sampled noise levels. It also introduces choices that must be reported: path and schedule, target parameterization, loss weighting, architecture, reverse SDE or ODE, solver, time grid, stochasticity, guidance, and representation. Regression form does not guarantee stable optimization, correct likelihoods, mode coverage, or low sampling cost.

    Constructive interpretation: corrupt, estimate, and reverse¤

    The basic construction has four stages:

    1. Choose a forward path. Define transitions or continuous dynamics that progressively reduce information about a data sample while approaching a tractable reference law.

    2. Construct a training target. From a data sample, noise level, and sampled corruption, form a denoising, noise, data, score, velocity, or transition target under an explicit convention.

    3. Estimate the reverse object. Train a parameterized predictor across noise levels. Population conversion formulas relate several targets, while finite networks and weighted losses can make their learned errors different.

    4. Generate numerically. Draw from the reference law and apply a discrete reverse chain, SDE solver, ODE solver, consistency map, or distilled transition. The output distribution depends on both learned error and numerical approximation.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Forward Diffusion Process¤

    The original DDPM formulation uses a fixed Gaussian Markov chain over \(T\) steps; \(T=1000\) is its historical training discretization, not a family requirement:

    \[ q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{1-\beta_t} \, x_{t-1}, \beta_t \mathbf{I}) \]

    where \(\beta_t \in (0,1)\) controls the variance of noise added at timestep \(t\). The complete forward chain factors as:

    \[ q(x_{1:T} | x_0) = \prod_{t=1}^T q(x_t | x_{t-1}) \]

    Key property: We can sample \(x_t\) at any arbitrary timestep directly without simulating the full chain. Defining \(\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t\) and \(\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{i=1}^t \alpha_i\):

    \[ q(x_t | x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0, (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t) \mathbf{I}) \]

    This can be reparameterized as:

    \[ x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \, \epsilon \quad \text{where } \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I}) \]

    Boundary condition: When \(\bar\alpha_T\) is sufficiently small, \(q(x_T\mid x_0)\) is close to \(\mathcal N(0,I)\) for appropriately scaled data. The residual terminal KL is part of the ELBO; a finite schedule need not make the endpoint exactly independent of \(x_0\).

    The posterior conditioned on the original data is also Gaussian with tractable parameters:

    \[ q(x_{t-1} | x_t, x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0), \tilde{\beta}_t \mathbf{I}) \]

    where:

    \[ \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0) = \frac{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_t}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_0 + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t} (1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} x_t \]
    \[ \tilde{\beta}_t = \frac{1 - \bar{\alpha}_{t-1}}{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t} \cdot \beta_t \]

    The Reverse Diffusion Process¤

    The reverse process learns to invert the forward diffusion, starting from noise \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\) and progressively denoising to data \(x_0\):

    \[ p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) = \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t, t), \Sigma_\theta(x_t, t)) \]

    The complete generative process:

    \[ p_\theta(x_{0:T}) = p(x_T) \prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) \]

    Related parameterizations:

    Noise prediction: Under MSE, the network estimates the conditional mean of the sampled perturbation noise:

    \[ \hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \]
    \[ \mu_\theta(x_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \right) \]

    Data Prediction: The network directly predicts the clean image:

    \[ \hat{x}_0 = f_\theta(x_t, t) \]

    Score Prediction: The network predicts the gradient of the log probability:

    \[ s_\theta(x_t, t) \approx \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t) \]

    For one sampled pair \((x_0,\epsilon)\), the clean sample and noise are deterministically related given \(x_t\) and \(\bar\alpha_t\). The corresponding identity is for the conditional perturbation kernel:

    \[ \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t\mid x_0) \;=\; -\,\frac{\epsilon}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\, x_0 - x_t}{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}. \]

    The score needed by the reverse process is instead the marginal score of \(q_t(x_t)=\int q(x_t\mid x_0)q_{\mathrm{data}}(x_0)\,dx_0\). Fisher's identity gives

    \[ \nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(x_t) = \mathbb E\!\left[\nabla_{x_t}\log q(x_t\mid x_0)\mid x_t\right] = -\frac{\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t]}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}. \]

    Tweedie's formula (Efron, 2011) further connects the score to the posterior mean:

    \[ \mathbb{E}[x_0 \mid x_t] \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}}\bigl(x_t + (1-\bar{\alpha}_t)\,\nabla_{x_t}\log q(x_t)\bigr), \]

    Thus the MSE-optimal noise and \(x_0\) predictors estimate conditional means, and those predictions can be converted affinely. An individual latent noise draw is not determined by \(x_t\) after marginalizing over the unknown data sample.

    Mathematical Equivalence

    Under squared-error training and consistent weighting, the population-optimal noise, clean-data posterior mean, and marginal score contain equivalent information. Finite networks, target weighting, preconditioning, optimization, and approximation error make parameterization a real numerical and statistical design choice—not an identity between individual training targets.

    The ELBO Derivation¤

    The latent-variable bridge places this construction beside mixtures, factor models, VAEs, and state-space models while separating auxiliary noisy states from semantic representation latents.

    The discrete-time DDPM construction is a Markovian hierarchical latent-variable model with a VAE-style variational bound. For a fixed observation \(x_0\), the bound decomposes into three semantically different pieces:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \log p_\theta(x_0) &\geq \underbrace{ \mathbb{E}_{q(x_1\mid x_0)}[\log p_\theta(x_0\mid x_1)] }_{\text{decoder or reconstruction term}} \\ &\quad- \underbrace{ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(x_T\mid x_0)\|p(x_T)) }_{\text{terminal prior-matching term}} \\ &\quad- \underbrace{ \sum_{t=2}^T \mathbb{E}_{q(x_t\mid x_0)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\|p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)) }_{\text{reverse-transition terms}}. \end{aligned} \]

    An outer expectation over the empirical data distribution turns this pointwise bound into a training objective. The first term also requires an explicit observation model: for integer-valued images, a discretized decoder distribution is needed before the bound can be interpreted as bits per dimension. The terminal term contains no learned reverse mean when the reference prior is fixed, but it need not be zero at a finite endpoint.

    For Gaussian posteriors and a fixed isotropic model covariance \(\sigma_t^2I\), the part of an intermediate KL that depends on the model mean is

    \[ L_t^{(\mu)} = \mathbb{E}_{q(x_0,x_t)}\left[\frac{1}{2\sigma_t^2} \|\tilde{\mu}_t(x_t,x_0) - \mu_\theta(x_t,t)\|^2\right] + C_t, \]

    where \(C_t\) is independent of the predicted mean under this fixed-variance assumption. If the model predicts a variance, the log-determinant and variance-ratio terms are not constants and must be retained. Substituting the noise parameterization into the mean term yields a time-weighted noise-regression loss:

    \[ L_t^{(\mu)} = w_t\,\mathbb{E}_{x_0,\epsilon}\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t} \, \epsilon, t)\|^2\right] + C_t, \]

    for a schedule- and variance-dependent coefficient \(w_t\). Ho et al. (2020) instead used the unweighted simplified objective for their sample-quality experiments:

    \[ L_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{t \sim U[1,T], \, x_0, \, \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})}\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\right] \]

    This is easy to estimate, but it is not the exact ELBO with its weights removed harmlessly: it changes the relative importance of noise levels. The original paper found this choice useful for sample quality in its evaluated image settings. Likelihood-oriented training, learned variance, other targets, and other time-sampling laws require their own derivation.

    Variance Schedules¤

    The noise schedule \(\{\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_T\}\) fundamentally affects training and sampling quality:

    Linear Schedule (Ho et al., 2020):

    \[ \beta_t = \beta_1 + (\beta_T - \beta_1) \cdot \frac{t-1}{T-1} \]

    Typically \(\beta_1 = 0.0001\), \(\beta_T = 0.02\). Simple but can add too much noise early.

    Cosine Schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021 — IDDPM):

    \[ \bar{\alpha}_t = \frac{f(t)}{f(0)} \quad \text{where} \quad f(t) = \cos^2\left(\frac{t/T + s}{1+s} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2}\right) \]
    \[ \beta_t = 1 - \frac{\bar{\alpha}_t}{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \]

    with \(s = 0.008\). Nichol and Dhariwal found it improved their evaluated DDPM settings relative to the linear schedule; it is one schedule among several parameterisations used by later diffusion, EDM, and flow-matching systems.

    Schedule Selection

    Match the schedule to the prediction target, loss weighting, preconditioning, and sampler. Cosine schedules avoid destroying information as quickly as the original linear DDPM schedule in the evaluated setup. EDM/Karras noise levels, log-SNR schedules, shifted schedules, and flow-matching time distributions make different design choices; compare them under a fixed compute and sampler protocol.

    A schedule is also a measure over denoising problems¤

    It is useful to separate the path coordinate from the distribution used to sample that coordinate during training. Let \(\lambda\) denote log-SNR, take \(L>0\), and fix endpoints \(\lambda(0)=L\) and \(\lambda(1)=-L\). Two monotone schedules with those endpoints are

    \[ \lambda_A(t)=L(1-2t), \qquad \lambda_B(t)=L(1-2t^2). \]

    If \(t\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), schedule \(A\) induces the uniform density \(p_A(\lambda)=1/(2L)\). Schedule \(B\) induces

    \[ p_B(\lambda)=\left|\frac{dt}{d\lambda}\right| =\frac{1}{4Lt} =\frac{1}{4L\sqrt{(1-\lambda/L)/2}}, \]

    so it presents more high-SNR examples near \(\lambda=L\). The two schedules share endpoints, but uniform clock time does not define the same training measure over denoising tasks.

    Variational Diffusion Models show a narrower invariance: in their continuous-time formulation, after the corresponding change of variable and with the SNR endpoints fixed, the exact VLB is invariant to the shape of the schedule. That statement does not make finite Monte Carlo training, network conditioning, optimizer dynamics, or a discretized sampler schedule-invariant. The schedule can instead be learned or chosen to reduce estimator variance, while the endpoints control the represented likelihood bound.

    Score-Based Perspective¤

    The score function \(\nabla_x \log p(x)\) points toward regions of higher probability density. Score-based models (Song & Ermon, 2019 — NCSN) train a network \(s_\theta(x, t)\) to approximate this gradient field through denoising score matching (Vincent, 2011):

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{DSM}} = \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\mathbb{E}_{p(\tilde{x}|x)}\left[\|s_\theta(\tilde{x}, t) - \nabla_{\tilde{x}} \log p(\tilde{x}|x)\|^2\right] \]

    Given the learned score, generation proceeds via Langevin dynamics:

    \[ x_{i+1} = x_i + \delta \nabla_x \log p(x) + \sqrt{2\delta} \, z_i \quad \text{where } z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I}) \]

    The connection to diffusion is conditional-expectation based: the marginal noisy-data score equals the negative posterior mean noise divided by its perturbation scale, not an arbitrary individual noise draw recoverable from \(x_t\).

    Stochastic Differential Equations¤

    The continuous-time formulation (Song et al., 2021 — ICLR Outstanding Paper) generalizes discrete diffusion as an SDE:

    \[ dx = f(x,t)dt + g(t)dw \]

    Variance Preserving (VP) SDE corresponds to DDPM:

    \[ dx = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x \, dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)} \, dw \]

    The reverse-time SDE enables generation when integrated from terminal time toward zero; the displayed \(dt\) follows the reverse-time integration convention:

    \[ dx = \left[f(x,t) - g(t)^2\nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt + g(t) d\bar{w} \]

    There exists an equivalent probability flow ODE:

    \[ dx = \left[f(x,t) - \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2\nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right] dt \]

    With an exact score and exact integration, the probability-flow ODE has the same one-time marginals as the SDE and supports a change-in-log-density calculation through its divergence. Neural score error, stochastic divergence estimation, finite tolerances, and numerical integration make reported likelihood and samples approximate in practice.

    Worked example: one scalar forward and posterior step¤

    One example will now carry all three formulations: the discrete DDPM chain, denoising-score regression, and continuous-time reverse dynamics. Let the data distribution be

    \[ X_0\sim\mathcal N(m_0,v_0)=\mathcal N(1,4), \]

    and choose \(\alpha_1=0.8\) and \(\alpha_2=0.5\). Thus \(\bar\alpha_1=0.8\) and \(\bar\alpha_2=0.4\). Write

    \[ a_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}, \qquad b_t^2=1-\bar\alpha_t, \qquad X_t=a_tX_0+b_t\epsilon, \quad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,1). \]

    Gaussian affine closure gives

    \[ X_1\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.8},3.4), \qquad X_2\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2). \]

    We inspect the noisy value \(x_2=0.5\). When a calculation also needs a realized clean training example, use \(x_0=2\). Keeping the random data law, the observed noisy value, and the realized training pair distinct prevents most of the apparent contradictions below.

    Formulation 1: the conditioned DDPM posterior is not the marginal reverse kernel¤

    For the realized pair \((x_0,x_2)=(2,0.5)\), the tractable forward posterior from the DDPM ELBO is

    \[ q(x_1\mid x_2,x_0) =\mathcal N\!\left(x_1; \frac{\sqrt{0.8}\,0.5}{0.6}x_0 +\frac{\sqrt{0.5}\,0.2}{0.6}x_2, \frac{0.2}{0.6}\,0.5\right), \]

    so that

    \[ q(x_1\mid x_2=0.5,x_0=2) =\mathcal N(1.608563,1/6). \]

    The reverse generator does not know \(x_0\). Its population target is the marginal reverse conditional \(q(x_1\mid x_2)\), obtained by integrating the previous posterior against \(q(x_0\mid x_2)\). In this Gaussian example, \((X_1,X_2)\) is jointly Gaussian with

    \[ \operatorname{Cov}(X_1,X_2)=\sqrt{0.5}\,(3.4), \]

    and the Gaussian conditioning formula yields

    \[ q(x_1\mid x_2=0.5)=\mathcal N(0.749680,0.772727). \]

    These distributions are visibly different. For non-Gaussian data, the marginal reverse conditional is generally a mixture and need not be Gaussian even though every \(q(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,x_0)\) is Gaussian. A Gaussian \(p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\) is therefore a modeling approximation to the marginal reverse kernel, not a claim that the clean-conditioned posterior is available at generation time. This distinction is central to the variational construction in DDPM.

    Formulation 2: one conditional score target averages to the marginal score¤

    For the same realized training pair, the conditional perturbation score is

    \[ \nabla_{x_2}\log q(x_2\mid x_0=2) =\frac{\sqrt{0.4}\,2-0.5}{0.6} =1.274852. \]

    The marginal noisy-data score uses the law \(X_2\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2)\) instead:

    \[ s_2(0.5) =\nabla_{x_2}\log q_2(0.5) =-\frac{0.5-\sqrt{0.4}}{2.2} =0.060207. \]

    There is no inconsistency. Denoising score matching averages the conditional target over the clean posterior:

    \[ \mathbb E\!\left[ \nabla_{x_2}\log q(X_2\mid X_0) \mid X_2=x_2 \right] =s_2(x_2). \tag{DIFF.1}\label{eq:diffusion-denoising-score-expectation} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:diffusion-denoising-score-expectation}\) is the bridge from conditional denoising targets to the marginal score. Equivalently, Tweedie's identity and the noise-prediction identity give

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E[X_0\mid X_2=0.5] &=\frac{0.5+0.6\,s_2(0.5)}{\sqrt{0.4}} =0.847687,\\ \mathbb E[\epsilon\mid X_2=0.5] &=-\sqrt{0.6}\,s_2(0.5) =-0.046636. \end{aligned} \]

    The realized pair has \(\epsilon=(0.5-\sqrt{0.4}\,2)/\sqrt{0.6}\approx-0.98750\); it is a training target, not the posterior mean noise determined by \(x_2\). The equality is a population-optimum correspondence under the denoising objective, as established by denoising score matching and used by score-based diffusion—not an equality for every sampled pair (Vincent, 2011; Song et al., 2021).

    Formulation 3: an exact VP-SDE embedding and its two reverse dynamics¤

    Let continuous time be \(\tau\) and consider the constant-rate VP process

    \[ dX_\tau=-\frac12X_\tau\,d\tau+dW_\tau. \]

    At \(\tau_i=-\log\bar\alpha_i\), its transition between consecutive grid points is exactly

    \[ X_{\tau_i}\mid X_{\tau_{i-1}} \sim\mathcal N\!\left( \sqrt{\alpha_i}\,X_{\tau_{i-1}},1-\alpha_i \right). \]

    Thus this particular discrete chain is not merely similar to the SDE: it is the SDE observed at the nonuniform times \(\tau_i\). Matching a single endpoint coefficient \(\bar\alpha_t\), however, would not by itself specify the intermediate path or cross-time coupling.

    For arbitrary \(\tau\),

    \[ p_\tau=\mathcal N(m_\tau,v_\tau), \qquad m_\tau=e^{-\tau/2}, \qquad v_\tau=1+3e^{-\tau}, \]

    and hence

    \[ s_\tau(x)=-\frac{x-m_\tau}{v_\tau}. \]

    At \(\tau_\star=-\log 0.4\approx0.916291\), this recovers \(p_{\tau_\star}=\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2)\). The reverse-SDE drift and probability-flow velocity are, respectively,

    \[ r_\tau(x)=-\frac12x-s_\tau(x), \qquad u_\tau(x)=-\frac12x-\frac12s_\tau(x), \]

    with the reverse SDE integrated from \(\tau_\star\) down to zero. At \(x=0.5\) their values are \(r_{\tau_\star}(0.5)=-0.310207\) and \(u_{\tau_\star}(0.5)=-0.280104\). They are not the same vector field.

    The probability-flow ODE nevertheless preserves the same one-time Gaussian marginals under exact integration. Indeed,

    \[ \mathbb E[u_\tau(X_\tau)]=-\frac12m_\tau=m_\tau', \]

    and its centered linear coefficient is \(-1/2+1/(2v_\tau)\), so

    \[ \frac{d}{d\tau}v_\tau =2\left(-\frac12+\frac{1}{2v_\tau}\right)v_\tau =1-v_\tau. \]

    These are exactly the mean and variance equations of the forward VP marginal. The reverse SDE has the same Fokker--Planck evolution when run backward. Shared marginals do not mean equal paths: conditional on a terminal state, the ODE trajectory is deterministic while the SDE trajectory remains stochastic.

    A one-step counterexample: exact score does not make a coarse solver exact¤

    Start from the exact terminal marginal \(X_{\tau_\star}\sim\mathcal N(\sqrt{0.4},2.2)\) and use the exact score, but freeze each vector field at \(\tau_\star\) for one step of length \(\tau_\star\). Backward Euler for the probability-flow ODE gives

    \[ X_0^{\mathrm E} =X_{\tau_\star}-\tau_\star u_{\tau_\star}(X_{\tau_\star}) \sim\mathcal N(0.922212,3.436936). \]

    One Euler--Maruyama step for the reverse SDE gives

    \[ X_0^{\mathrm{EM}} =X_{\tau_\star}-\tau_\star r_{\tau_\star}(X_{\tau_\star}) +\sqrt{\tau_\star}Z \sim\mathcal N(0.922212,3.303365), \quad Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1). \]

    Neither equals the target \(\mathcal N(1,4)\), and the two numerical laws differ from each other. This is discretization error with no score or initialization error. Exact Gaussian analyses now quantify initialization, terminal truncation, discretization, and score-approximation errors separately (Pierret & Galerne, 2025); current error bounds likewise retain finite-time, time-discretization, statistical, and score-estimation terms rather than collapsing them into “the diffusion error” (De Bortoli et al., 2026).

Population-optimum correspondence connects denoising targets to the marginal score. Distributional equivalence under assumptions connects the exact reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE. A finite sampler is a separate algorithm whose error must be measured.

    Mathematical Foundation: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Discrete diffusion is simultaneously a hierarchical latent model, a denoising estimator, and—under continuous limits—a score-driven stochastic transport. These are precise correspondences under assumptions, not permission to conflate training targets or finite solvers.

    Retrieval practice: diffusion foundations
    1. When is \(x_T\) exactly standard Gaussian and independent of \(x_0\)?
    2. What does MSE noise prediction estimate at population optimum?
    3. Why can a probability-flow ODE and reverse SDE share marginals without producing identical finite-step sample paths?
    4. Why is the simplified DDPM loss not numerically identical to the full ELBO weighting?

    Solutions. (1) Only under an exact terminal construction or limiting schedule; ordinary finite DDPM schedules make it approximate. (2) \(\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t,t]\). (3) One is deterministic conditional on its initial state and the other stochastic, and numerical discretizations introduce different error. (4) It discards or changes timestep-dependent coefficients derived from Gaussian KL terms.

    Architecture Design¤

    Architecture Design: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. separate the denoising target from the U-Net, DiT, state-space, sparse, or equivariant backbone that estimates it;
    2. trace where time, class, text, geometry, and other conditions enter a network;
    3. compare attention and convolution using actual token counts, memory traffic, active parameters, and hardware;
    4. convert \(\epsilon\)-, \(x_0\)-, score-, and VP \(v\)-predictions without confusing flow-matching velocity; and
    5. audit an architectural benchmark without attributing gains caused by data, codec, objective, guidance, or post-training to the backbone alone.

    The diffusion backbone estimates a declared target from a noisy state, time/noise level, and optional conditions. U-Nets, DiTs, multimodal Transformers, convolutional networks, state-space models, linear-attention networks, graph networks, and equivariant architectures are all viable backbones under different state-space and systems constraints. Architecture is not the probabilistic model family.

    U-Net Backbone with Skip Connections¤

    The U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015) dominates classical diffusion models through its encoder-decoder structure with skip connections; the diffusion-specific adaptation is due to Ho et al. (2020) (DDPM) and refined in Nichol & Dhariwal (2021) (IDDPM) and Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) — ADM:

    Key Components:

    • Contracting path: Progressive downsampling (256→128→64→32→16) while increasing channels (the diagram above; production U-Nets often go one or two levels deeper).
    • Expanding path: Upsampling reconstructs output at original resolution
    • Skip connections: Critical for propagating spatial details lost in bottleneck
    • ResNet blocks: \(\text{output} = \text{input} + F(\text{input}, \text{time\_emb})\)
    • Group normalization: Dividing channels into groups (~32) for stability

    Why U-Net for Diffusion?

    1. Input and output have identical dimensions (essential for iterative refinement)
    2. Skip connections preserve fine details through bottleneck
    3. Multi-scale processing captures both coarse structure and fine texture
    4. No information bottleneck—maintains full spatial information

    Representative users: SD 1.x, SD 2.x, SDXL, Imagen, DALL·E 2's decoder, and ControlNet. These examples demonstrate the U-Net design space; they do not establish a universal parameter ceiling or imply that every large-scale system should use the same backbone.

    Beyond U-Net: Transformer, State-Space, and Hybrid Backbones¤

    Transformer-, state-space-, and hybrid-attention backbones offer different scaling, multimodal-interaction, and high-resolution trade-offs from pure U-Nets. No architecture-independent U-Net plateau at a fixed parameter count has been established. Three surveys or educational reviews collect the architectural lineage: From U-Nets to DiTs (ICLR Blogposts 2026), Efficient Diffusion Models: A Comprehensive Survey (2024), and Efficient Diffusion Models: A Survey (2025).

    Diffusion Transformer (DiT)¤

    DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023 — ICCV) replaces the U-Net entirely with a Vision-Transformer-style stack:

    • Patchification: the noisy latent \(x_t\) is cut into patches (typically 2×2) and linearly projected to tokens.
    • Standard transformer blocks (LayerNorm → MHSA → MLP) operate on the token sequence.
    • adaLN-Zero conditioning (see below) injects the timestep + class-label embedding.
    • Final unpatchify projects the token sequence back to the latent grid.

    Headline finding: scaling DiT compute (Gflops) reliably reduces FID through the evaluated DiT-XL/2 scale (675 M parameters). On ImageNet 256², Table 2 of the paper reports FID 9.62 without classifier-free guidance and FID 2.27 with guidance scale 1.5. The guided number must not be presented as an architectural result without its sampling condition.

    Influence: DiT established a clean transformer diffusion baseline. Public reports for SD3 and related systems show clear lineage, while limited public Sora details support only a broad spacetime-transformer relationship rather than an exact architectural genealogy.

    adaLN, adaLN-Zero, adaLN-single, adaLN-LoRA — Conditioning Variants¤

    How a transformer-style diffusion backbone is conditioned on the timestep (and optionally class label / text vector / pooled prompt) is itself a design axis with surprising consequences:

    • adaLN (Perez et al., 2018 → DiT): replace LayerNorm's affine parameters with \((\gamma, \beta)\) that are learned linear functions of the conditioning embedding. Each transformer block has its own \((\gamma, \beta)\) — much more expressive than feeding the timestep as an extra token.
    • adaLN-Zero (Peebles & Xie, 2023): zero-initialise the residual modulation so that each DiT block begins as an identity map. The DiT paper's controlled ablation improved FID relative to its in-context and cross-attention conditioning variants at matched training compute; this is evidence for that architecture and protocol, not a universal factor-of-two guarantee or a family default.
    • adaLN-single (Chen et al., 2023 — PixArt-α): share one set of adaLN parameters across all blocks instead of per-block, with a small per-block residual. Cuts ~20 % of parameters and still trains stably.
    • adaLN-LoRA (Lu et al., 2024 — FiTv2): factorise the per-block adaLN projection through a low-rank decomposition. Lighter than adaLN, more expressive than adaLN-single.

    Unveiling the Secret of AdaLN-Zero (Wei et al., 2024) gives a careful theoretical analysis of why the zero-init form converges so much faster.

    U-ViT — Long Skip Connections in a Transformer¤

    U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023 — CVPR) processes patch tokens with a transformer and adds long skip connections from early to late blocks. Its ablations find these skips important in the evaluated U-ViT configurations. That result does not establish that convolution is irrelevant, that every transformer needs symmetric skips, or that the same change has a fixed effect in another architecture. The paper reports FID 2.29 on class-conditional ImageNet 256² under its stated sampling protocol.

    PixArt-α, PixArt-Σ — Efficient Text-Conditional DiTs¤

    PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2023) is the first DiT to demonstrate that a small, training-efficient DiT can match Stable Diffusion at text-to-image:

    • Cross-attention to T5 text features after each self-attention block.
    • adaLN-single for cheap timestep conditioning.
    • Three-stage training curriculum (pixel dependency → text alignment → high resolution).
    • Trained for ~675 A100-days — about 12 % of SD 1.5's compute budget — and matches SDXL on aesthetic FID.

    PixArt-Σ (Chen et al., 2024) scales to direct 4K-resolution generation via a weak-to-strong training schedule and a token-compression block that linearises attention over high-resolution patches.

    MMDiT (SD3) — Multimodal Dual-Stream Transformer¤

    MMDiT (Esser et al., 2024 — Stable Diffusion 3) is the architectural innovation behind SD3 / SD3.5 and the closest cousin of FLUX.1:

    • Two parallel token streams — one for text, one for image — each with its own QKV/MLP weights.
    • Joint self-attention across the concatenated streams: every text token can attend to every image token and vice versa, in both directions.
    • adaLN-Zero conditioning on a pooled text/timestep vector.

    The SD3 report shows stronger prompt-following and typography for the complete MMDiT-based system than named baselines. Public ablations do not isolate dual-stream attention from data, captions, codec, objective, scale, and post-training well enough to assign the entire gain to that component. FLUX.1 (Black Forest Labs, 2024) uses related double-stream and single-stream blocks plus rotary positional embeddings; public model documentation is a technical-report source, not an independent architecture ablation.

    Hourglass Diffusion Transformer (HDiT)¤

    HDiT (Crowson et al., 2024 — ICML) reintroduces the U-Net's multi-level hierarchy inside a pure-transformer architecture:

    • Hierarchical encoder–decoder with symmetric token-resolution stages (2× downsample / upsample between stages, like U-Net).
    • Neighborhood Attention at high resolutions (linear in pixel count) and standard global self-attention at the bottleneck.
    • Scales linearly with pixel count rather than quadratically.

    HDiT demonstrates pixel-space 1024² training without a VAE codec, multiscale loss, or self-conditioning. The paper reported a leading FFHQ-1024² result relative to its named baselines at publication; that claim is dataset-, metric-, and protocol-bounded.

    SiT — Scalable Interpolant Transformers¤

    SiT (Ma et al., 2024 — ECCV) keeps DiT's exact architecture but replaces the discrete-time DDPM training objective with the continuous stochastic-interpolants framework — a generalisation of flow matching that decouples interpolant choice, prediction target, and sampler. The paper systematically ablates each axis and shows SiT-XL/2 reaches FID 2.06 on ImageNet 256² and 2.62 on 512², both improvements over DiT-XL/2 at the same parameter count and FLOPs. SiT is the backbone REPA (see below) is built on top of.

    FiT / FiTv2 — Flexible-Resolution DiTs¤

    FiT (Lu et al., 2024 — ICML Spotlight) and FiTv2 (Lu et al., 2024) treat images as variable-length token sequences, not static-resolution grids:

    • 2-D RoPE (Su et al., 2024) instead of learned position embeddings, intended to support evaluation at resolutions and aspect ratios not seen during training.
    • SwiGLU MLPs and Q-K vector normalisation for training stability at scale.
    • Masked MHSA to handle variable-length padding cleanly.
    • FiTv2 adds adaLN-LoRA, a rectified-flow scheduler, and a Logit-Normal sampler; its paper reports improved convergence under its matched training protocol.

    FiT is designed to support multiple resolutions and aspect ratios in one checkpoint. Generalization beyond its training distribution and freedom from cropping or composition artifacts remain empirical.

    Hunyuan-DiT — Multi-Resolution Bilingual Text-to-Image¤

    Hunyuan-DiT (Tencent, 2024) is a 1.5 B-parameter DiT that interleaves PixArt-α-style single-stream blocks (cross-attention to text) with SD3-style double-stream blocks (joint attention). It is engineered for bilingual English and Chinese prompts, with a custom text-encoder pipeline and rotary positional encoding. Claims about downstream adoption require separate public technical evidence.

    Lumina-Next, DiT-Air — Parameter-Efficient Multi-Modal DiTs¤

    • Lumina-Next (Alpha-VLLM, 2024) — a "Next-DiT" backbone applied across image, video, audio, and 3D generation with rectified-flow training; Lumina-Image 2.0 develops the image setting.
    • DiT-Air (Chen et al., 2025) — applies layer-wise parameter sharing to MMDiT and reports a 66 % size reduction at near-equal quality under its evaluated comparison. This is a protocol-specific result, not a permanent family-wide efficiency ranking.
    • E-MMDiT (2025) — pushes MMDiT to a 304 M-parameter lightweight variant via a highly compressive tokenizer plus Alternating Subregion Attention.

    Mixture-of-Experts Diffusion: DiT-MoE, EC-DiT, Switch-DiT¤

    Sparse mixture-of-experts layers replace some dense feed-forward computation with learned routing. In diffusion backbones, routing may depend on token position, noise level, conditioning, and architecture:

    • DiT-MoE (Fei et al., 2024) — reports a 16.5 B-parameter model with shared-expert routing and an expert-balance loss, 3.1 B active parameters per token, and FID 1.80 on ImageNet 512² under its class-conditional protocol.
    • Switch-DiT (Park et al., 2024 — ECCV) — synergises denoising-task experts with a sparse routing layer; different timesteps select different experts.
    • EC-DiT (Apple, 2024) — adaptive expert-choice routing where experts pick which tokens to process, balancing expert load implicitly.

    The DiT-MoE analysis reports specialization by spatial position and timestep under its trained routers rather than a simple class-only partition. This is evidence about those models, not a theorem that MoE is preferable for every diffusion workload.

    State-Space and Linear-Attention Backbones¤

    Quadratic self-attention is the bottleneck at high resolution. Three lines of work replace it:

    • Diffusion Mamba (DiM) (Teng et al., 2024) — Mamba selective state-space layers in place of attention, \(O(N)\) in token count; competitive at 1024² with far less compute than DiT.
    • DiMSUM (Phung et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) — adds wavelet-domain processing to Diffusion Mamba, capturing local structure and long-range frequency relations that pure SSMs miss.
    • U-Shape Mamba (USM) (Ergasti et al., 2025 — CVPRW) — wraps Mamba blocks in a U-shaped hierarchy, halving sequence length per encoder stage.
    • DiG (Zhu et al., 2025 — CVPR) — Diffusion Gated Linear Attention; 1.8× faster than Flash-DiT-XL/2 at 2048² while matching ImageNet-512² FID.
    • EDiT / MM-EDiT (Suresh et al., 2025) — linear compressed attention for image-to-image dependencies, plus standard softmax attention for image–text interactions; the paper reports linear scaling in image sequence length and quality comparisons under its evaluated settings.

    Hybrid linear and softmax attention changes the quality–memory–latency trade-off; any claimed advantage must specify sequence length, kernels, hardware, and quality target. An ICLR 2026 expo talk is ecosystem evidence, not a peer-reviewed diffusion benchmark.

    Dynamic / Sparse / Cached DiTs¤

    A complementary line of work leaves the architecture roughly fixed but skips computation adaptively:

    • DyDiT / DyDiT++ (Zhao et al., 2025) — adjusts compute along both timestep and spatial dimensions; reports a 51% DiT-XL FLOP reduction under its quality protocol.
    • Learning-to-Cache (Ma et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) — caches selected layer activations across denoising steps and reports roughly 40% inference acceleration under named model, hardware, and quality settings.
    • DiffSparse (ICLR 2026) — differentiable layer-wise token sparsity with a learnable router and DP solver; further accelerates DiTs without retraining the base model.
    • TeaCache — reuses selected features across nearby steps; system reports describe acceleration in several integrations, but the factor depends on checkpoint, threshold, hardware, and tolerated quality change.

    Autoregressive and Masked Image Alternatives: VAR and MAR¤

    VAR and MAR use autoregressive or masked prediction over discrete or continuous visual representations. They provide comparison points for image generation, but their tokenizer, ordering, conditioning, guidance, and sampling compute must be matched before attributing a result to the family name.

    • VAR — Visual Autoregressive Modeling (Tian et al., 2024 — NeurIPS Best Paper) — next-scale prediction over multi-resolution VQ codes. Beats DiT on ImageNet-256² (FID 1.73 vs DiT-XL/2's 2.27) while being ~20× faster at inference.
    • MAR — Masked Autoregressive Modeling (Li et al., 2024) — autoregression without vector quantisation; uses a small per-token diffusion head over continuous values. Matches diffusion quality with autoregressive sampling.
    • VAR-Image / VAR-Video follow-ups extend next-scale prediction to higher resolutions and video.

    These are not diffusion models, but they pressure-test the assumption that multi-step denoising is the only path to high-quality image generation. Architecturally, DiT, MAR, and VAR can be closer to each other than to U-Net DDPM, even though their probabilistic objects and sampling procedures remain distinct.

    Architecture Design: Time / Noise-Level Conditioning¤

    Denoising behaviour is strongly noise-level dependent: at high noise the estimator must rely more heavily on learned data structure, while at low noise it resolves local residual uncertainty. Most shared-weight denoisers therefore encode the timestep \(t\), noise scale \(\sigma\), or log-SNR \(\lambda = \log(\bar\alpha_t/(1-\bar\alpha_t))\) and inject that information repeatedly through the network. Injection at every block is a common effective design, not a mathematical necessity: a model can expose the noise level through another architecture, separate parameters by level, or—in restricted settings—learn without explicit noise conditioning.

    There are two distinct sub-problems here, often conflated in implementations:

    1. Encoding — turn the scalar \(t\) (or \(\sigma\), \(\lambda\)) into a vector \(\tau \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\tau}\) that the network can consume.
    2. Injection — make \(\tau\) influence every layer's computation.

    This subsection walks through both.

    1. Encoding the Scalar¤

    Sinusoidal Positional Embedding (DDPM Default)¤

    The classical choice, inherited from Transformer position encodings (Vaswani et al., 2017):

    \[ \tau_{2i}(t) = \sin\!\left(\frac{t}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \qquad \tau_{2i+1}(t) = \cos\!\left(\frac{t}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \]

    for \(i = 0, \ldots, d/2 - 1\) and embedding dimension \(d\) (with the dimension chosen by the architecture). The fixed log-spaced frequencies expose multiple scales. Periodic features do not guarantee global injectivity: two clock values can in principle collide, while distinctness on a declared finite training grid can be checked directly. DDPM-derived U-Nets commonly transform such an encoding with an MLP before block-wise injection.

    Random Fourier Features¤

    Tancik et al. (2020 — NeurIPS) analyze how Fourier feature maps change the frequency spectrum that an MLP can fit. A Gaussian random feature map for a scalar noise coordinate has the form

    \[ \tau(t) = \big[\sin(2\pi B t),\ \cos(2\pi B t)\big], \qquad B \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \sigma_B^2\, I_{d/2}\right) \;\text{(fixed at init).} \]

    The bandwidth \(\sigma_B\) controls the sampled frequencies, and the draw is normally fixed after initialization. NCSN++ configurations use this construction for continuous noise conditioning. Do not infer the embedding from the surrounding objective name: the official EDM implementation uses positional embeddings for its DDPM++ backbone and Fourier embeddings for its NCSN++ backbone, while the EDM preconditioner supplies the scalar noise label separately.

    Log-SNR / Log-σ Encoding¤

    A second axis is what scalar to encode. Three common choices:

    • Discrete index \(t \in \{1, \ldots, T\}\) — DDPM's default; simple but tied to a specific schedule.
    • Continuous time \(t \in [0, 1]\) — used by score-based SDEs and rectified flow; portable across schedules.
    • Log-SNR \(\lambda = \log(\bar\alpha_t/(1-\bar\alpha_t))\) (Kingma et al., 2021 — VDM; Salimans & Ho, 2022) — an informative coordinate that records the signal-to-noise ratio directly. Encoding \(\lambda\) removes ambiguity about which SNR a clock value denotes, but it does not make training or sampling invariant to the schedule: the time-sampling law induces a measure over \(\lambda\), endpoints matter, and finite networks and solvers still see different numerical problems.
    • EDM σ-conditioning (Karras et al., 2022) — supply \(c_{\text{noise}}(\sigma)=\tfrac14\log\sigma\) to the backbone's declared noise-embedding module. This scalar convention is part of EDM preconditioning; whether the backbone maps it with positional or Fourier features is a separate architecture choice.

    Representative rectified-flow and flow-matching implementations encode continuous \(t\in[0,1]\); EDM preconditioning exposes a rescaled \(\log\sigma\); DDPM-derived U-Nets often encode a discrete or continuous clock. A reproducible model must state the scalar, its direction and endpoints, its embedding, and its injection mechanism. Numerical stability is an empirical property of the complete parameterization, not a consequence of taking a logarithm alone.

    Learned-Table (Embedding-Lookup) Encoding¤

    If you commit to a fixed discrete schedule of \(T\) timesteps, you can simply learn a \(T \times d\) embedding table:

    \[ \tau(t) = \mathbf{E}[t, :], \qquad \mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d}. \]

    Used in some discrete-time variants and conditional models. It cannot evaluate genuinely unseen or fractional timestep indices, but DDIM subsampling normally selects a subset of the integer timesteps present during training, so a learned table does not by itself prevent skip sampling. Continuous or interpolated noise levels require an encoding that generalizes between trained indices.

    Other Encodings (Briefly)¤
    • Multi-resolution hash encodings (Müller et al., 2022 — Instant-NGP) are popular in NeRFs but have not been broadly adopted for time conditioning in diffusion (the scalar input is too low-dimensional to benefit).
    • Hybrid sinusoidal + learned-bias — sinusoidal frequencies plus a small learned offset table per timestep; sometimes seen in distilled / few-step student models.

    2. Injecting the Embedding¤

    The encoded vector \(\tau(t)\) is typically fed through a 2-layer MLP and then injected into every transformer / U-Net block. The five common patterns:

    FiLM (Feature-wise Linear Modulation) — U-Net Default¤

    Perez et al. (2018):

    \[ \text{output}(x, \tau) = \gamma(\tau) \odot x + \beta(\tau), \]

    with per-channel scale \(\gamma\) and shift \(\beta\) produced from \(\tau\) via a small MLP. This is one important U-Net pattern, especially the scale-and-shift normalization used by ADM-style residual blocks, but it is not the universal or original DDPM mechanism. The original DDPM U-Net projects the sinusoidal time embedding and adds it as a channel-wise bias inside each residual block; many latent-diffusion U-Nets retain additive residual-block conditioning. Later architectures optionally turn the projection into scale/shift modulation. These variants must be distinguished when reproducing a checkpoint.

    adaLN-Zero — Transformer Default¤

    For DiT-style backbones, one common mechanism is adaptive layer normalization, which derives normalization and residual-gating parameters from the conditioning embedding. adaLN-Zero (Peebles & Xie, 2023) zero-initialises the residual modulation so each block begins as an identity map; the paper's ablation improves FID within its DiT setting, while effect size remains architecture- and protocol-specific. Variants include:

    • adaLN-single (Chen et al., 2023 — PixArt-α) — share one \((\gamma, \beta)\) projection across all blocks; ~20 % parameter savings.
    • adaLN-LoRA (Lu et al., 2024 — FiTv2) — factorise the per-block projection through a low-rank decomposition.

    See Beyond U-Net → adaLN, adaLN-Zero, adaLN-single, adaLN-LoRA for the full discussion.

    Concatenation as Extra Channels¤

    The simplest scheme is to tile a time representation spatially and concatenate it to an input or intermediate feature map. It appears in pedagogical and some early conditional architectures, but not as the original DDPM paper's timestep mechanism: DDPM adds a projected sinusoidal embedding inside every residual block. Input-only concatenation conditions the first layer directly and asks deeper layers to propagate that information, which is generally less direct than repeated residual-block injection or adaptive normalization.

    Time Token Prepending (DiT Variants)¤

    Some DiT variants tokenise \(\tau\) as an extra time element and let self-attention spread its information across image tokens. This is a different information path from adaLN-style blockwise modulation: its effect depends on token mixing, depth, initialization, and the rest of the conditioning design.

    Cross-Attention to a Conditioning Sequence¤

    For models where time and other conditions (text, class, pooled CLIP) are tightly coupled, the entire conditioning vector — including \(\tau\) — is fed as the key/value sequence in a cross-attention layer. The MMDiT recipe in SD3 and FLUX.1 adds the timestep embedding to the pooled-text embedding before adaLN, then uses joint self-attention across image and text tokens; this combines the strengths of FiLM-style modulation and full attention-based text-conditioning.

    3. Practical Considerations¤

    Joint timestep + class / text conditioning. One class-conditional design adds projected time and class embeddings before FiLM or adaptive-normalization injection. Text systems may combine time with a pooled text vector for modulation, retain token-level text for cross- or joint attention, or use both. These constructions are not interchangeable: state which encoder output enters which block and through which projection.

    Null condition for classifier-free guidance. CFG requires the network to handle a designated unconditional condition during training. Class models often use a learned null label; text systems may use an empty prompt, a learned null representation, or another declared dropout path. The dropout probability and unconditional encoding are checkpoint-specific, while time conditioning normally remains present.

    EMA of the embedding MLP. The projection is usually small relative to the denoising backbone, although its parameter count depends on the embedding and hidden widths and can readily reach hundreds of thousands. When a recipe maintains an exponential moving average of model parameters, this projection normally belongs to the same parameter tree unless the implementation explicitly excludes it.

    4. Can Noise Conditioning Be Omitted?¤

    Noise conditioning can sometimes be omitted without changing the model family:

    • Sun et al. (2025 — ICML) — Is Noise Conditioning Necessary for Denoising Generative Models? — empirically demonstrates that across a wide range of diffusion-style models, removing time conditioning entirely produces only a small quality gap; the noisy input itself implicitly encodes the noise level for the network to read off.
    • Follow-ups show similar results for graph diffusion (NeurIPS 2025) and disjoint-manifold diffusion (2026).

    These experiments show that the noisy input can carry information about its corruption level in the evaluated settings. They do not establish that conditioning is unnecessary for every data distribution, schedule, inverse problem, or architecture, nor do they rank scalar encoding and injection choices independently of the full protocol.

    Attention Mechanisms¤

    Attention cost grows with token count, head shape, sparsity pattern, precision, and kernel implementation. In DiT-style backbones it can become the principal memory or latency term at long sequences, but a portable percentage cannot be assigned without profiling the complete network. The following patterns make different trade-offs among receptive field, approximation, memory traffic, and hardware fit.

    Self-Attention¤

    Every spatial token attends to every other spatial token via standard scaled-dot-product attention (Vaswani et al., 2017):

    \[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) \;=\; \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V. \]

    In multi-head form, the projection is split into \(h\) heads of dimension \(d_k / h\), attended in parallel, then concatenated and re-projected. Different heads learn to focus on different spatial relationships (long-range structure, local texture, object boundaries).

    Complexity is \(O(N^2 d)\) in the number of tokens \(N\). For a 256² latent at f8 patch-2 (i.e. 32² latent → 16² patches), \(N = 256\) and self-attention is cheap; for a 1024² pixel-space input, \(N \sim 65{,}536\) and full self-attention is intractable. The classical U-Net heuristic: only apply self-attention at the bottleneck and lower-resolution stages (typically 32², 16², 8²), rely on convolutions at higher resolutions. Modern DiTs apply self-attention at every layer — patchification keeps \(N\) small enough — but this then makes attention's \(O(N^2)\) cost the binding constraint at high resolution, motivating most of the variants below.

    Cross-Attention (for Text Conditioning)¤

    Cross-attention is the standard mechanism for text-to-image conditioning in U-Net-era diffusion (Stable Diffusion 1.x / 2.x, ControlNet, IP-Adapter, etc.):

    • Queries \(Q\) come from image / patch features.
    • Keys \(K\) and Values \(V\) come from a text encoder's output (CLIP, T5, OpenCLIP-G, T5-XXL).

    Different image regions thereby attend to different text tokens, giving spatially-localised text control.

    Within one denoising evaluation, the text-side \(K,V\) are shared across image positions, so dense cross-attention has \(O(NMd)\) score-value work for \(N\) image and \(M\) text tokens. Reuse across denoising steps requires an implementation cache and is exact only for text projections that do not change with the timestep or other step-dependent state.

    Cross-attention is also how PixArt-α / -Σ inject text into a DiT, where it sits next to every self-attention block and is conditioned on a frozen T5-XXL (Chen et al., 2023).

    Joint Attention — MMDiT-Style Multimodal¤

    Several large-scale text-to-image systems—including Stable Diffusion 3, FLUX.1, and Hunyuan-DiT—use joint attention rather than a simple cross-attention-only design: text and image tokens participate in a shared attention computation, while implementations may retain modality-specific projections or feed-forward paths. This is an architecture pattern, not a defining property of diffusion.

    This lets every text token attend to every image token and vice versa in a bidirectional operation. See the MMDiT subsection above for the architecture diagram. Joint attention is a plausible contributor to stronger prompt interaction and text rendering, but public comparisons do not isolate it from training data, captioning, scale, codec, objective, and post-training strongly enough to call it the single cause.

    Position Encodings: Sinusoidal → Learned → RoPE → 2-D RoPE¤

    Self-attention is position-agnostic on its own — Q-K dot products are invariant to token reordering. The positional information that distinguishes "top-left patch" from "bottom-right patch" comes from the position encoding. Modern diffusion has converged on two choices:

    • Learned absolute position embeddings (one vector per token slot) — simple but tied to a fixed grid; doesn't generalise to other resolutions.
    • Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) — rotate \(Q\) and \(K\) as a function of token position so the dot product encodes relative offsets under the chosen frequency convention. RoPE and its multidimensional variants are used by several DiT systems, but learned, fixed, relative-bias, and convolutional position mechanisms remain valid alternatives.
    • 2-D RoPE factorises the rotation across \((H, W)\) axes; NaViT-style variable-resolution RoPE (Dehghani et al., 2023) supports arbitrary aspect ratios. FiT / FiTv2 (Lu et al., 2024) and FLUX.1 are built on this.

    Spatial, Temporal, and 3-D Attention in Video Diffusion¤

    Naive dense 3-D attention over \(T\times H\times W\) video tokens has \(O((THW)^2)\) score complexity. Three recurring strategies are:

    1. Factorised space-time attention — interleave 2-D spatial attention blocks (treating \(T\) as a batch dimension) with 1-D temporal attention blocks (treating \(HW\) as a batch dimension). Used in Imagen Video, AnimateDiff, Stable Video Diffusion, and most early video-DiT recipes. \(O(THW \cdot (HW + T))\).
    2. Full 3-D attention with spatio-temporal patchification — Sora-style. Patch the video at \((t, h, w)\) resolution to keep \(N\) tractable, then run joint self-attention across all spatio-temporal tokens. Used by Sora, Wan 2.x, HunyuanVideo, Mochi-1.
    3. Sparse / structured 3-D attention — neighbourhood attention in \((t, h, w)\), axial attention, or learned sparse patterns for efficient long-context video generation (for example, LiteAttention, arXiv:2511.11062, and FrameDiT, arXiv:2603.09721).

    Windowed and Neighborhood Attention (Sub-Quadratic, Sparse)¤

    For pixel-space and very high-resolution latents, full self-attention is replaced by local attention windows:

    • Window Self-Attention (Liu et al., 2021 — Swin) — partition tokens into non-overlapping windows; attend within each window, then shift the windows between layers to mix information.
    • Neighborhood Attention (NA / NAT) (Hassani et al., 2023 — CVPR) — every token attends within a sliding neighborhood. At fixed neighborhood size its score computation is linear in \(N\); boundary handling, dilation, shifting, and positional terms determine the exact equivariance properties. The NATTEN extension provides specialized kernels.
    • Dilated / mixed windows combine multiple receptive-field sizes per layer.

    Linear-Attention Variants (Beyond Softmax)¤

    A small zoo of \(O(N)\) attention alternatives has crossed over from LLMs into diffusion (covered in detail in Beyond U-Net → State-Space and Linear-Attention Backbones):

    • Linear / kernelised attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Performer, Choromanski et al., 2021) — replace softmax with a feature map \(\phi\), yielding \(\text{Att} = \phi(Q)(\phi(K)^\top V)\) in \(O(N d^2)\).
    • Gated Linear Attention — used in DiG (Zhu et al., 2025 — CVPR).
    • Selective state-space (Mamba) layers — used in DiM, DiMSUM, U-Shape Mamba.
    • Linear compressed attention for image-to-image, paired with softmax for image-text — used in EDiT / MM-EDiT (Suresh et al., 2025).
    • Hybrid blocks with a declared mix of linear and softmax attention layers, retaining occasional global mixing while changing the cost of other layers.

    Token Merging, Pruning, and Sparse Attention¤

    Rather than redesign the attention mechanism, several methods reduce the token count:

    • ToMeSD (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023 — CVPRW) — merge similar tokens before each attention block in Stable Diffusion. Up to 60 % token reduction → 2× speedup, 5.6× memory reduction, no retraining, minimal quality loss.
    • ToMA — Token Merge with Attention (2025) — adapts token merging specifically for diffusion transformers.
    • DiffSparse (ICLR 2026) — differentiable layer-wise token sparsity with a learnable router; further accelerates DiTs without retraining.
    • DyDiT / DyDiT++ (Zhao et al., 2024–25) — adaptive computation along both timestep and spatial dimensions; 51 % FLOPs reduction on DiT-XL.

    Implementation: FlashAttention and Friends¤

    Many diffusion stacks compile attention through one of three backend families. They implement the same mathematical softmax-attention operation when used in compatible modes, but are not bitwise drop-in equivalents: floating-point accumulation order, masks, dropout, precision, supported head dimensions, and backward kernels can change numerical results and availability.

    • FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022 — NeurIPS; FlashAttention-2, Dao 2023; FlashAttention-3, Shah et al., 2024) — IO-aware tiled attention that avoids materialising the full \(N \times N\) score matrix in high-bandwidth memory. Report the exact kernel, hardware, precision, sequence shape, and achieved throughput when using performance numbers from a FlashAttention paper.
    • xFormers memory-efficient attention (Meta) — earlier and still widely used in Stable Diffusion deployments.
    • PyTorch SDPA / FlexAttention — vendor-blessed kernels with automatic dispatch to FA-3 / Triton on supported hardware.

    Feature and KV Caching at Inference¤

    Diffusion is iterative, so consecutive denoising steps usually have highly correlated attention activations. Modern fast-inference stacks cache them:

    • DeepCache (Ma et al., 2024 — CVPR) — caches U-Net deep-feature activations across steps; ~2× speedup.
    • Learning-to-Cache (L2C) (Ma et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) — learns which DiT layers are safe to cache between steps.
    • TeaCache — feature caching baked into Lumina-Image 2.0, FLUX, and several open video stacks for ~2× inference speedup.
    • ToCa, FORA, Δ-Cache — methods that cache token-level attention output rather than whole layers.

    Caching, efficient attention, token reduction, quantisation, distillation, and latent representations can compose, but their reported speedups cannot simply be multiplied. They change different terms in the end-to-end cost, can contend for the same memory bandwidth, and may use different checkpoints, precisions, batches, or quality targets. The sampling compute ledger separates those terms instead of converting a stack of method-level ratios into one unbounded speedup claim.

    Attention decision questions¤

    Model Parameterization Choices¤

    The same denoising network can be trained to predict different targets derived from \(x_t\). Given the standard VP-SDE forward \(x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar\alpha_t}\, \epsilon\) (write \(a_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\), \(b_t = \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\), so \(a_t^2 + b_t^2 = 1\)), the common prediction targets are:

    • ε — the additive Gaussian noise (DDPM)
    • x₀ — the clean image (data prediction)
    • v — Salimans & Ho's "velocity" (a rotation of (ε, x₀))
    • score — \(\nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t)\) (score matching)
    • F — the EDM "preconditioned" target (Karras et al. 2022)
    • flow-matching velocity — \(u_t(x) = \mathbb{E}[x_1 - x_0 \mid x_t]\) (a different "velocity" from Salimans & Ho's v)

    These objects do not all form one affine equivalence class. For one VP perturbation and an interior noise level, sampled \(\epsilon\), sampled \(x_0\), and VP \(v\) are related by an invertible rotation when \(x_t\) is known. Their population-optimal conditional means are related by the same linear maps. The marginal score is obtained through a conditional expectation, EDM wraps a denoiser in a chosen preconditioner, and an arbitrary flow-matching velocity belongs to a separately declared probability path. The distinctions matter before optimization is even considered.

    Conversion boundaries for prediction targets¤

    Pick any two of \((x_t, x_0, \epsilon)\) and the third is determined:

    \[ x_0 = \frac{x_t - b_t\, \epsilon}{a_t}, \qquad \epsilon = \frac{x_t - a_t\, x_0}{b_t}, \qquad v_t \;\equiv\; a_t\, \epsilon - b_t\, x_0. \]

    For the conditional perturbation kernel, the score is a rescaled negative sampled noise. For the noisy marginal, the unknown clean sample and noise draw have been integrated out, so the corresponding identity uses a conditional expectation:

    \[ \nabla_{x_t} \log q(x_t\mid x_0) = -\frac{\epsilon}{b_t}, \qquad \nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(x_t) = -\frac{\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t]}{b_t}. \]

    A diagonal sanity check on \(v_t\):

    Table DIFF.6. Conversion boundaries for prediction targets
    Target Limit at high noise (\(a_t \to 0\), \(b_t \to 1\)) Limit at low noise (\(a_t \to 1\), \(b_t \to 0\))
    \(\epsilon\) \(\epsilon\) — strongly expressed in \(x_t\) \(\epsilon\) — unit target but weakly expressed in the nearly clean input
    \(x_0\) \(x_0\) — data-scale (large), signal-starved \(x_0\) — data-scale, well-scaled
    \(v_t\) \(-x_0\) (since \(a_t \to 0\), \(b_t\to1\)) \(\epsilon\) (since \(a_t \to 1\), \(b_t\to0\))
    Conditional \(\nabla \log q(x_t\mid x_0)\) \(-\epsilon\) — finite \(-\epsilon/b_t\) — scale grows as \(b_t\to0\)

    The practical asymmetry is here: ε-prediction uses a unit-variance target at every time, but at low noise that target is only weakly visible in the almost-clean input; \(x_0\)-prediction is easy to interpret at low noise but becomes posterior-mean prediction from an almost uninformative input at high noise. The \(v\) rotation exchanges these endpoint roles smoothly, while EDM preconditioning explicitly controls input, skip, and output scales. Loss weighting still determines which SNR regions dominate optimization.

    ε-Prediction (Noise Prediction) — DDPM Default¤

    Network predicts \(\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \approx \epsilon\):

    \[ L_\epsilon \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\,x_0,\,\epsilon}\!\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\right]. \]
    • Pros: The sampled regression target has unit marginal variance at every timestep, avoiding a target-scale change caused solely by the schedule. It is used by DDPM, Stable Diffusion 1.x, classifier-guided ADM-style recipes, and many public implementations.
    • Cons: At low noise, the injected perturbation is small relative to the data, so recovering its particular realization can be statistically difficult even though the target variance remains one. Converting an ε error to \(x_0\) scales it by \(b_t/a_t\): this vanishes at low noise and grows at high noise, not the reverse. Weighting and preconditioning therefore matter across the whole SNR range.

    x₀-Prediction (Data Prediction)¤

    Network predicts \(\hat x_\theta(x_t, t) \approx x_0\):

    \[ L_{x_0} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{t,\,x_0,\,\epsilon}\!\left[\|x_0 - \hat x_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\right]. \]
    • Pros: The output is directly interpretable in data space, especially at low noise, and supports explicit clipping or dynamic-thresholding operations when the observation convention warrants them. Some consistency and distillation formulations predict a clean endpoint directly; others retain noise, velocity, score, or transition targets.
    • Cons: At high noise, \(x_t\) carries little information about the paired clean sample. Under squared error the population predictor approaches a posterior mean—and eventually an unconditional mean—not a sample from the full data prior. Unconstrained network outputs can also leave a bounded data range, so any clipping rule must be declared as part of the sampler.

    v-Prediction (Velocity)¤

    Salimans & Ho (2022) define a velocity target by parameterizing the unit circle with an angle \(\phi\):

    \[ a_t=\cos\phi(t),\qquad b_t=\sin\phi(t),\qquad v_\phi \equiv \frac{\partial x_\phi}{\partial\phi} =a_t\epsilon-b_tx_0. \]

    Thus VP \(v\) is the tangent with respect to the angular coordinate. For an arbitrary clock \(t\),

    \[ \frac{\partial x_t}{\partial t}=\phi'(t)v_\phi, \]

    so writing \(v=\partial x_t/\partial t\) silently assumes \(\phi'(t)=1\). With the convention that \(t=0\) is clean, \(v\to\epsilon\) at the clean endpoint and \(v\to-x_0\) at the high-noise endpoint. The rotation can balance target scale under normalized-data assumptions, but it does not guarantee equal gradient conditioning at every timestep; the schedule and loss weighting still matter.

    • Pros: Often improves numerical balance under wide-SNR schedules and is especially useful in progressive distillation, where stable conversions between time levels matter.
    • Cons: Conceptually less transparent than ε / \(x_0\); requires care converting back for sampling.
    • Documented uses: Progressive distillation studies and the public Stable Diffusion 2 768-pixel configuration use VP-v parameterization. Imagen instead documents ε-prediction. A rectified-flow target reduces to VP \(v\) only under the corresponding path and time rescaling; a closed system should not be assigned a target without a technical disclosure.

    The conversion back to ε / \(x_0\) from a v-prediction is just inverting the rotation:

    \[ \hat\epsilon = a_t\, v_\theta + b_t\, x_t,\qquad \hat x_0 = a_t\, x_t - b_t\, v_\theta. \]

    Score Prediction (Score Matching)¤

    For a sampled VP perturbation, the conditional denoising-score target is \(-\epsilon/b_t\); its scale grows as \(b_t\to0\). The population MSE optimum is the noisy marginal score \(-\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t]/b_t\). That marginal score need not diverge at the data endpoint when the data law has a smooth, full-dimensional density. Score-based models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021) pair noise conditioning, weighting, and endpoint handling with SDE or probability-flow-ODE samplers; one must not transfer the conditional target's pointwise scale claim to every marginal data score.

    EDM "F-Prediction" with Preconditioning¤

    Karras et al., 2022 — EDM reframe parameterisation choice as a preconditioning problem. In the variance-exploding (VE) parameterisation \(x_t = x_0 + \sigma \epsilon\), they wrap the network \(F_\theta\) with input/output rescalings:

    \[ D_\theta(x, \sigma) \;=\; c_{\mathrm{skip}}(\sigma)\, x \;+\; c_{\mathrm{out}}(\sigma)\, F_\theta\!\big(c_{\mathrm{in}}(\sigma)\, x;\; c_{\mathrm{noise}}(\sigma)\big), \]

    with closed-form \(c_{\mathrm{skip}}, c_{\mathrm{out}}, c_{\mathrm{in}}, c_{\mathrm{noise}}\) designed to normalize input and target scales under the assumed data variance. EDM is a useful design-space decomposition, not proof of one universally optimal parameterization, and its denoiser preconditioning should not be called literal \(\epsilon\)/\(x_0\)/\(v\) equivalence without deriving the path and weights.

    Flow-Matching Velocity (a different "v")¤

    To avoid notation collision: flow-matching velocity (Lipman et al., 2023) is a different object from Salimans & Ho's v-prediction. Flow matching defines a probability path \(p_t\) between data (\(p_0\)) and noise (\(p_1\)) via a conditional trajectory \(\psi_t(x_0, x_1)\), and trains a vector field

    \[ u_\theta(x_t, t) \;\approx\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\partial \psi_t}{\partial t} \;\middle|\; x_t\right]. \]

    For the linear interpolant \(\psi_t = (1-t)x_0 + t\, x_1\) (rectified flow, Liu et al., 2023), the target is simply \(u_t = x_1 - x_0\). For the VP-Gaussian interpolant, the flow-matching velocity coincides with Salimans & Ho's v only after the schedule-dependent rescaling is derived. The Stable Diffusion 3 report combines rectified-flow training with a logit-normal time distribution and evaluates that choice in its own architecture; public FLUX materials describe a flow-matching system. Neither fact makes a flow target and VP-v interchangeable in an unmatched system comparison.

    How the Choice Interacts with Loss Weighting¤

    The three classical losses are not the same loss with a relabelled target: they correspond to identical math only after the right per-timestep weighting. Hang et al. (2023) — Min-SNR make this explicit: for a common Min-SNR-\(\gamma\) schedule, the loss weight depends on the parameterisation:

    \[ w(t) \;=\; \begin{cases} \min\!\big(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\, \gamma\big) & \text{(}x_0\text{-prediction)} \\[2pt] \min\!\big(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\, \gamma\big) \,/\, \mathrm{SNR}(t) & \text{(}\epsilon\text{-prediction)} \\[2pt] \min\!\big(\mathrm{SNR}(t),\, \gamma\big) \,/\, \big(\mathrm{SNR}(t) + 1\big) & \text{(v-prediction)} \end{cases} \]

    where \(\mathrm{SNR}(t) = a_t^2 / b_t^2\). With these weights the three are equivalent in expectation; with the naive unweighted MSE used in the simplified DDPM objective, ε-prediction implicitly upweights low-noise steps and \(x_0\)-prediction implicitly upweights high-noise steps.

    Loss Re-weighting: Logit-Normal, EDM2, and P2-Weighting¤

    The implicit per-step weighting introduced by the parameterisation choice is now actively designed rather than accidental:

    • EDM (Karras et al., 2022) samples \(\sigma\) from a log-normal distribution at training time, focusing capacity on the perceptually-relevant SNR band.
    • EDM2 (Karras et al., 2024) tunes the training-noise distribution as a hyperparameter, uses adaptive reciprocal weighting to balance observed per-noise losses, introduces magnitude-preserving architecture changes, and reconstructs EMA profiles after training. It does not make the noise prior a learned model component.
    • Logit-Normal sampling (Esser et al., 2024 — SD3) is the rectified-flow analogue: \(t \sim \mathrm{LogitNormal}(\mu, s^2)\) keeps mid-range timesteps oversampled.
    • P2 weighting (Choi et al., 2022) downweights very high-SNR denoising terms in the paper's perceptual-prioritized objective. Its benefit is protocol-specific rather than a default for ImageNet pipelines.

    Equivalence vs. preference

    Where the stated linear-Gaussian parameterisation is invertible, targets convert through linear changes of variable. Finite-training preference depends on scaling, sampled time distribution, endpoint error amplification, weighting, preconditioning, and downstream solver. Flow velocity and VP \(v\) are different objects except under an explicitly derived path/schedule relation.

    Worked example: convert one VP prediction four ways¤

    Let \(a_t=0.8\), \(b_t=0.6\), \(x_t=1\), and a scalar VP velocity prediction \(\hat v=0.5\). Then

    \[\hat x_0=a_tx_t-b_t\hat v=0.5,\qquad \hat\epsilon=a_t\hat v+b_tx_t=1.\]

    The implied score estimate is \(\hat s=-\hat\epsilon/b_t=-5/3\), and \(a_t\hat x_0+b_t\hat\epsilon=1=x_t\). This catches swapped signs and reversed endpoint interpretations. It does not identify \(\hat v\) with the velocity of an arbitrary rectified-flow path.

    Training Process¤

    Training Process: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. implement one training step with explicit RNG, shapes, reduction, target, time distribution, and weight;
    2. convert Min-SNR weights between parameterizations;
    3. distinguish EMA decay per update from a wall-clock half-life; and
    4. design validation that separates denoising loss, likelihood bounds, sample metrics, conditioning, and numerical stability.

    The Simplified Training Objective¤

    The simplified loss ignores theoretical weightings from ELBO:

    \[ L_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{t \sim U[1,T], \, x_0 \sim q(x_0), \, \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\mathbf{I})}\left[\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2\right] \]

    Training Algorithm:

    1. Sample training image \(x_0 \sim q(x_0)\)
    2. Sample timestep \(t \sim \text{Uniform}(1, T)\)
    3. Sample noise \(\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\)
    4. Compute noisy image \(x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t} \, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t} \, \epsilon\)
    5. Predict noise \(\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\)
    6. Compute loss \(L = \|\epsilon - \hat{\epsilon}\|^2\)
    7. Update \(\theta\) via gradient descent

    The code path can be compact, but the statistical objective still depends on time sampling, target convention, per-example/per-dimension reduction, weighting, data scaling, conditioning dropout, and precision.

    Loss Function Variants¤

    Variational Lower Bound (VLB):

    The full ELBO includes weighted terms for each timestep. While theoretically principled, optimizing full VLB is harder in practice.

    Hybrid Objective (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021):

    For intermediate steps with \(\tilde\beta_t>0\), the Improved DDPM recipe predicts a reverse log-variance between the forward variance \(\beta_t\) and posterior variance \(\tilde\beta_t\):

    \[ \Sigma_\theta(x_t,t) =\exp\!\left(v_\theta(x_t,t)\log\beta_t +[1-v_\theta(x_t,t)]\log\tilde\beta_t\right). \]

    This is log-space interpolation when \(v_\theta\in[0,1]\); the paper reports that it did not constrain the network output to that interval. The \(t=1\) decoder term retains its separate observation-model treatment. The recipe combines \(L_{\text{simple}}\) for mean prediction with VLB terms for variance learning:

    \[ L_{\text{hybrid}} = L_{\text{simple}} + \lambda L_{\text{vlb}} \]

    In that paper's experiments, \(\lambda=0.001\), and the mean prediction is stop-gradiented inside \(L_{\mathrm{vlb}}\) so the added term trains the variance head rather than perturbing the simplified mean objective. These are recipe-specific choices, not part of the definition of diffusion and not the Stable Diffusion v1 configuration.

    Worked example: what a learned variance can and cannot repair¤

    For a scalar true reverse conditional \(q=\mathcal N(\mu_q,s_q^2)\) and model \(p=\mathcal N(\mu_p,s_p^2)\),

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p) =\frac12\left[ \log\frac{s_p^2}{s_q^2} +\frac{s_q^2+(\mu_q-\mu_p)^2}{s_p^2}-1 \right]. \]

    If \(\mu_p=\mu_q\), the optimum is \(s_p^2=s_q^2\). If the mean error is \(\delta=\mu_q-\mu_p\), differentiating with respect to \(s_p^2\) gives the likelihood-optimal value \(s_p^2=s_q^2+\delta^2\). The model can therefore widen its conditional to account for unresolved mean error, but this does not correct the denoising mean. Reporting sample quality alone cannot tell whether the variance is calibrated.

    Min-SNR-γ Weighting (Hang et al., 2023):

    Min-SNR clamps a common \(x_0\)-space signal-to-noise weighting, but the coefficient multiplying the implemented MSE depends on the prediction target. With \(\operatorname{SNR}(t)=\bar\alpha_t/(1-\bar\alpha_t)\), the equivalent weights are

    \[ w_{x_0}(t)=\min(\operatorname{SNR}(t),\gamma),\qquad w_{\epsilon}(t)=\frac{\min(\operatorname{SNR}(t),\gamma)}{\operatorname{SNR}(t)}, \]

    and, under the standard VP rotation,

    \[ w_v(t)=\frac{\min(\operatorname{SNR}(t),\gamma)}{\operatorname{SNR}(t)+1}. \]

    Therefore an ε-prediction implementation must not multiply directly by \(\min(\operatorname{SNR},\gamma)\) as though it were an \(x_0\) loss. The paper reports up to 3.4× faster convergence in its evaluated settings; \(\gamma=5\) is a common starting point, not a universal optimum.

    Training Stability and Best Practices¤

    Essential Training Practices

    Exponential Moving Average (EMA): Often improves evaluated checkpoints. Maintain a documented update rule:

    \[ \theta_{\text{ema}} = \beta \, \theta_{\text{ema}} + (1-\beta) \, \theta \]

    A value such as \(\beta=0.9999\) has meaning only with update frequency and training horizon. Compare raw, online EMA, or post-hoc EMA checkpoints under the same sampler; do not assume one is always superior.

    Gradient clipping: If used, clip the true global norm and report its threshold and frequency; 1.0 is a starting point, not a family constant.

    Mixed precision: Speed and memory gains are hardware, kernel, shape, and dtype dependent. Track overflows, loss scaling, attention reductions, and a higher-precision reference.

    Normalization is architecture- and batch-semantics dependent:

    • Group normalization is common in DDPM-derived U-Nets because it avoids cross-example batch statistics.
    • Layer normalization and RMS-style variants are common in transformer backbones, often with adaptive conditioning.
    • Batch-dependent normalization is not prohibited by the probabilistic model, but its train/eval statistics, distributed aggregation, and interaction with mixed noise levels must be specified. It is uncommon in canonical DDPM U-Nets.

    Regularization must follow a named baseline:

    • Weight decay, dropout, augmentation, conditioning dropout, stochastic depth, and data filtering act on different objects.
    • A numeric value has no portable meaning without architecture, optimizer, effective batch, update count, and loss reduction. Tune one axis at a time on held-out data.

    Hyperparameter selection as a named protocol¤

    Timesteps: \(T=1000\) is the original DDPM convention. Continuous-time or alternate discretizations decouple training-time levels from sampling NFE; more training levels do not require using all levels at inference.

    Noise Schedules:

    • The cosine schedule outperformed the original linear schedule in the IDDPM protocol; schedule, target, weighting, and solver must be evaluated jointly.
    • Report the terminal signal level and terminal KL rather than calling finite \(x_T\) “pure noise” without qualification.

    Learning rate, optimizer moments, weight decay, warm-up, effective batch, resolution, augmentation, and distributed reduction should reproduce a named baseline before tuning. A numeric interval detached from architecture, loss normalization, and total batch can be actively misleading.

    Illustrative optimizer declaration (a record format, not a default):

    tx = optax.adamw(
    learning_rate=1e-4,
    b1=0.9,
    b2=0.999,
    weight_decay=1e-4,
)

    Training Dynamics and Monitoring¤

    Common Training Issues

    Loss plateaus: inspect per-time losses and held-out denoising error; a plateau can reflect convergence, weighting imbalance, optimizer failure, or data issues.

    NaN losses: locate the first nonfinite activation, target, reduction, gradient, or optimizer state; clipping can hide rather than solve the cause.

    Poor sample quality: separate model, solver, guidance, decoder, data-scaling, and checkpoint errors with fixed seeds and controlled ablations.

    What to Monitor:

    1. Training Loss: Should decrease initially, then plateau
    2. Fixed-seed and fresh-seed samples at a declared cadence and sampler
    3. Distributional metrics at a cadence justified by their estimator cost, with sample count and uncertainty
    4. Gradient Norms: Should be stable, not exploding
    5. Learning Rate: Track warmup and decay schedules

    Checkpoint Management:

    • Save raw and EMA weights when both are part of the experiment, together with optimizer, scheduler, scaler, RNG, and data-position state needed for a faithful resume.
    • Choose checkpoint cadence from failure-recovery cost and evaluation budget; record examples or tokens seen as well as optimizer steps.
    • Select checkpoints on a declared validation protocol. Do not select on the test split, and do not reduce a conditional generator to one FID value when alignment, coverage, calibration, and safety also matter.
    • Retain enough intermediate states to diagnose divergence, EMA sensitivity, and overfitting rather than preserving only the winning evaluation.

    Computational Requirements¤

    Hardware feasibility depends on parameters and active parameters, optimizer/EMA state, activation shapes, attention implementation, codec resolution, dtype, sharding, checkpointing, and effective batch. Report peak allocated and reserved memory, accelerator and interconnect, compiler/kernel versions, throughput, training FLOPs, images/tokens seen, and wall-clock. Avoid portable-looking GPU-count or day estimates without a reproducible configuration.

    Memory Optimizations:

    • Gradient checkpointing trades recomputation for activation memory; measure both on the target graph.
    • Mixed precision changes memory, throughput, and numerical error by hardware and kernel.
    • Smaller Batch Sizes: Use gradient accumulation to maintain effective batch size

    Do not multiply headline speedups

    A solver may reduce evaluations, a latent codec may reduce the tensor size, and a fused kernel may reduce time per evaluation. Their gains need not multiply: bottlenecks move, memory traffic overlaps, batching changes utilization, and decoding becomes a larger fraction of latency. Compare the complete systems at a matched quality and conditioning protocol.

    Compute ledger: from outer transitions to end-to-end latency¤

    Let \(K\) be the number of outer transitions. At transition \(i\), let \(m_i\) be the number of principal-network stages, \(b_{ij}\) the number of logical guidance branches at stage \(j\), and \(\ell_{ij}\) the number of launched backbone calls after batching or fusion. In the usual serial-versus-fully-batched case, \(1\leq\ell_{ij}\leq b_{ij}\). Two useful counts are

    \[ E_{\mathrm{branch}} =\sum_{i=1}^{K}\sum_{j=1}^{m_i} b_{ij}, \qquad L_{\mathrm{backbone}} =\sum_{i=1}^{K}\sum_{j=1}^{m_i}\ell_{ij}. \]

    \(E_{\mathrm{branch}}\) counts branch-equivalent evaluations; \(L_{\mathrm{backbone}}\) counts actual launches. NFE ordinarily refers to evaluations of the learned field, but reports differ in whether fused guidance branches, multi-stage formulas, or nested heads count separately. A report must therefore define its NFE convention and record launched calls as an additional systems quantity. A complete ledger also records:

    • calls to a transition head, corrector, classifier, reward model, or safety model;
    • conditioning-encoder, codec-encoder, codec-decoder, cascade, renderer, and reranker calls;
    • tensor shapes, batch including fused guidance branches, precision, sparsity, compiler, and hardware;
    • peak memory and memory traffic, which cannot be inferred from call counts;
    • training or distillation cost when the deployed checkpoint has changed; and
    • quality, coverage, conditioning, and output resolution at the reported operating point.

    The wall-clock decomposition is implementation-specific, but it should expose at least

    \[ T_{\mathrm{end\text{-}to\text{-}end}} =T_{\mathrm{condition}} +\sum_{i=1}^{K}\sum_{j=1}^{m_i}T^{\mathrm{backbone}}_{ij} +T_{\mathrm{heads}} +T_{\mathrm{codec/cascade}} +T_{\mathrm{rerank/safety}} +T_{\mathrm{I/O}}. \]

    This is an accounting identity, not a claim that unlike calls have equal FLOPs or can be added into a universal NFE.

    DDPM Sampling: The Iterative Reverse Process¤

    The foundational DDPM sampling starts from pure noise \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\) and iteratively denoises:

    \[ x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \right) + \sigma_t z \]

    where \(z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\) and \(\sigma_t\) controls stochasticity.

    Algorithm:

    1. Sample \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\)
    2. For \(t = T, T-1, \ldots, 1\):
    3. Predict noise: \(\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\)
    4. Compute mean: \(\mu_t = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \hat{\epsilon} \right)\)
    5. Sample: \(x_{t-1} = \mu_t + \sigma_t z\)
    6. Return \(x_0\)

    Characteristics:

    • Stochastic: Introduces randomness at each step.
    • Original cost: the historical chain uses about \(T=1000\) network evaluations, but latency depends on architecture, resolution, decoder, dtype, batching, compiler, and hardware.
    • Quality: increasing steps can reduce solver error until model error or another bottleneck dominates; it is not monotone for every sampler and guidance scale.
    • Comparisons: NFE ratios are not wall-clock speedups. Few-step solvers and distilled students change the operating point and sometimes the trained model itself.

    Why a large reverse jump changes the statistical problem¤

    A coarse reverse conditional can be multimodal even when a fine-step model is built from simple local transitions. Consider a one-dimensional data law

    \[ \Pr(X_0=-1)=\Pr(X_0=+1)=\tfrac12 \]

    and an independent corruption \(E\in\{-1,+1\}\) with equal probabilities, followed by \(X_t=X_0+E\). The event \(X_t=0\) has probability \(1/2\): it occurs through either \((X_0,E)=(-1,+1)\) or \((+1,-1)\), with equal joint mass. Bayes' rule therefore gives

    \[ \Pr(X_0=-1\mid X_t=0) =\Pr(X_0=+1\mid X_t=0) =\tfrac12. \]

    The exact coarse reverse conditional is therefore two point masses, not one Gaussian centered at zero. A Gaussian transition can match its conditional mean and perhaps its variance, but it cannot represent the two atoms. In higher-dimensional data, a large skip can similarly merge many plausible clean explanations into one reverse conditional.

    This example separates two acceleration strategies:

    1. A numerical solver takes larger steps while evaluating a learned marginal score, denoiser, or velocity field. Its error is a discretization-and-model-error question.
    2. A learned coarse transition tries to represent the finite conditional directly. Its expressivity and training target are additional statistical questions.

    An exact local score does not make an arbitrary coarse discretization exact, and a one-step Gaussian reverse model does not become multimodal merely because its mean network is expressive.

    DDIM: Fast Deterministic Sampling¤

    DDIM (Song, Meng & Ermon, 2021 — ICLR) constructs non-Markovian forward processes sharing DDPM's marginals but enabling much larger reverse steps:

    \[ \begin{aligned} x_{t-1} &= \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \underbrace{\left( \frac{x_t-\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)} {\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}} \right)}_{\text{"predicted }x_0\text{"}} \\ &\quad+ \underbrace{ \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}-\sigma_t^2}\, \epsilon_\theta(x_t,t) }_{\text{"direction pointing to }x_t\text{"}} \\ &\quad+ \underbrace{\sigma_t\epsilon}_{\text{random noise}}. \end{aligned} \]

    When \(\sigma_t = 0\), sampling becomes fully deterministic.

    Key Advantages:

    • Fewer evaluations: commonly evaluates a selected subset of time levels; measure wall-clock rather than converting the step ratio directly into speedup
    • No retraining of the denoiser: Works with a DDPM-compatible checkpoint when the schedule, target conversion, time convention, and input/output scaling are matched; “any checkpoint” is too broad
    • Deterministic conditional on the initial noise and implementation when \(\eta=0\); inversion/reconstruction additionally requires an inverse procedure and is approximate for a learned field
    • Interpolation: trajectories between selected initial noises can be inspected, but semantic or geodesic meaning is not guaranteed

    Algorithm (Deterministic \(\sigma_t = 0\)):

    1. Sample \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\)
    2. Choose subset of timesteps \(\{\tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots, \tau_S\}\) where \(S \ll T\)
    3. For \(i = S, S-1, \ldots, 1\):
    4. Predict \(x_0\): \(\hat{x}_0 = \frac{x_{\tau_i} - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_i}} \epsilon_\theta(x_{\tau_i}, \tau_i)}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_i}}}\)
    5. Compute \(x_{\tau_{i-1}} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_{i-1}}} \hat{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_{i-1}}} \epsilon_\theta(x_{\tau_i}, \tau_i)\)
    6. Return \(x_0\)

    DDIM in Practice

    DDIM remains a widely implemented baseline, while deployed systems use many schedulers. A step count is checkpoint-, spacing-, guidance-, and implementation-dependent; report the scheduler configuration rather than a universal “production default.”

    Consistency Models: One-Step Generation¤

    Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023 — ICML) and Improved Techniques for Training Consistency Models learn a consistency function that maps points on the same probability-flow trajectory toward a common boundary value:

    \[ f(x_t,t)=x_\epsilon \quad \text{along an ideal trajectory, with the declared boundary convention} \]

    The self-consistency property:

    \[ f(x_t,t)=f(x_s,s) \quad \text{when }x_t,x_s\text{ lie on the same ideal trajectory} \]

    Consistency Distillation:

    Train by distilling from pre-trained diffusion model:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{CD}} = \mathbb{E}\left[d(f_\theta(x_{t_{n+1}}, t_{n+1}), f_{\theta^-}(\hat{x}_{t_n}^\phi, t_n))\right] \]

    where \(\hat{x}_{t_n}^\phi\) is one step of ODE solver from \(x_{t_{n+1}}\) using teacher model.

    Results:

    • FID 3.55 on CIFAR-10 in one step
    • Improved techniques: FID 2.51 in one step, 2.24 in two steps
    • Consistency Trajectory Models (CTM): FID 1.73 in one step
    • Zero-shot editing without task-specific training

    Interpret NFE reductions carefully

    Consistency models target one- or few-evaluation generation. A 1000-to-1 NFE ratio is not a portable 1000× latency result, and quality must be compared at matched data, architecture, conditioning, decoder, and hardware.

    Guidance Techniques¤

    Classifier Guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021):

    Modifies the score using gradients from a separately trained noise-aware classifier:

    \[ \tilde{\epsilon}_\theta(x_t, c) = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, c) - w \cdot \sigma_t \cdot \nabla_{x_t} \log p_\phi(c|x_t) \]

    where \(w\) is guidance scale, \(c\) is class label.

    Reported comparison: Under the paper's ImageNet protocol, classifier guidance produced FID 4.59, compared with the cited BigGAN-deep baseline of 6.95. This is a protocol-specific comparison, not a family-wide ordering. Disadvantages: Requires training noise-aware classifiers at all noise levels.

    The exact conditional-score identity, its finite-state Doob-transform analogue, particle twisting, and the distinction between soft guidance and hard feasibility are derived in Conditioning, Steering, Constraints, and Selection.

    Classifier-Free Guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022):

    Eliminates the auxiliary classifier by jointly training conditional and unconditional predictions. Define the guidance scale \(s\) with the common implementation convention

    \[ \tilde{\epsilon}_\theta(x_t,c) = \epsilon_\theta(x_t,\emptyset) + s\bigl(\epsilon_\theta(x_t,c)-\epsilon_\theta(x_t,\emptyset)\bigr). \]

    Then \(s=0\) is unconditional, \(s=1\) is the ordinary conditional prediction without extrapolative guidance, and \(s>1\) extrapolates toward the condition. The algebraically equivalent convention \((1+w)\epsilon_c-w\epsilon_u\) uses \(w=s-1\), so “no extra guidance” is \(w=0\). Mixing those two parameter names is a common off-by-one error.

    During training, randomly drop condition \(c\) with probability ~10%.

    Advantages:

    • No auxiliary classifier needed
    • Avoids a separately trained noisy classifier
    • Exposes a scalar guidance convention, while rescaling, thresholding, negative prompts, and solver interactions add further controls
    • Widely used in public image systems; proprietary-system details and defaults require versioned official evidence

    Illustrative Guidance Scales (highly model-, target-, and sampler-dependent):

    • \(s = 0\): unconditional branch
    • \(s = 1\): conditional model without CFG extrapolation
    • \(s > 1\): increasing conditional pressure, often improving prompt/class alignment while reducing diversity
    • Excessive \(s\): saturation, oversharpening, or artifacts; the useful range must be measured for the specific checkpoint and parameterization

    Worked example: resolve the CFG off-by-one¤

    Let the unconditional prediction be \(\epsilon_u=2\) and conditional prediction be \(\epsilon_c=1\). Under

    \[\tilde\epsilon=\epsilon_u+s(\epsilon_c-\epsilon_u),\]

    \(s=0\) gives 2, \(s=1\) gives the ordinary conditional prediction 1, and \(s=3\) gives \(-1\). Under the alternative \((1+w)\epsilon_c-w\epsilon_u\), the same guided value uses \(w=s-1=2\). Logging only “guidance = 3” without the formula is therefore ambiguous.

    Conditional Diffusion Models¤

    Class-Conditional Generation:

    Add class label \(y\) as conditioning:

    \[ p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t, y) = \mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t, t, y), \Sigma_\theta(x_t, t, y)) \]

    Typically implemented via:

    • Class embeddings concatenated with time embeddings
    • Conditional batch normalization: Modulate batch norm with class info
    • Cross-attention: Attend to class token

    Text-to-Image Models:

    DALL·E 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022 — unCLIP):

    • Two-stage: CLIP prior diffusion + decoder.
    • CLIP prior maps text embeddings to image embeddings.
    • Decoder is a 64×64 diffusion model + two cascaded super-resolution diffusion models (→ 256² → 1024²).
    • Successors: DALL·E 3 (Betker et al., 2023) — improves prompt following via better caption supervision.

    Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022):

    • Frozen T5-XXL (4.6B parameters) as text encoder.
    • Cascaded diffusion: 64×64 → 256×256 → 1024×1024.
    • Key finding: scaling the text encoder improves quality more than scaling the U-Net.
    • The paper reports FID 7.27 on its COCO evaluation protocol; the number is meaningful only with its captioning, sampling, feature implementation, and comparison set.
    • Imagen 3 (Google, 2024) reports improved photorealism and text rendering under its evaluation protocol.

    Midjourney and other closed systems disclose insufficient architecture, data, and training detail for precise family classification across versions. Treat interface behavior and vendor claims separately from reproducible diffusion evidence.

    Cascade Diffusion Models¤

    Generate through multiple resolution stages, each a separate diffusion model:

    1. Base model: Generate at 64×64
    2. Super-resolution 1: Upscale to 256×256
    3. Super-resolution 2: Upscale to 1024×1024

    Advantages:

    • Each stage focuses on different detail scales
    • More efficient than single high-resolution model
    • Better quality through specialized models

    Disadvantages:

    • Complexity of training multiple models
    • Error accumulation across stages

    Used in DALL·E 2 and Imagen.

    Worked example: the codec changes the modeled distribution¤

    Suppose an encoder maps two distinct images \(x^{(1)}\) and \(x^{(2)}\) to the same latent \(z\). A perfect latent diffusion model can reproduce the latent distribution exactly, yet its decoder cannot recover both originals deterministically from \(z\). Pixel-space fidelity is therefore bounded by codec information loss before denoising error is considered. An honest system evaluation reports codec reconstruction distortion, prior sample quality, decoder cost, latent scaling, and end-to-end metrics separately.

    EDM: Elucidating the Design Space¤

    The EDM line of work — Karras et al., 2022 (NeurIPS) — Elucidating the Design Space and Karras et al., 2024 (CVPR Oral) — Analyzing and Improving Training Dynamics — provides a common framework separating the otherwise-tangled design choices (preconditioning, schedule, sampler, EMA):

    Optimal Preconditioning:

    Normalizing inputs/outputs of network for better training:

    \[ D_\theta(x, \sigma) = c_{\text{skip}}(\sigma) x + c_{\text{out}}(\sigma) F_\theta(c_{\text{in}}(\sigma) x; c_{\text{noise}}(\sigma)) \]

    Karras / EDM \(\rho\)-schedule for \(N\) sampling levels:

    \[ \sigma_i = \left( \sigma_{\max}^{1/\rho} + \frac{i}{N-1} \left(\sigma_{\min}^{1/\rho}-\sigma_{\max}^{1/\rho}\right) \right)^\rho, \qquad i=0,\ldots,N-1. \]

    The exponent \(\rho\) controls how solver steps concentrate across noise scales; EDM commonly uses \(\rho=7\) for image experiments. Geometric interpolation \(\sigma(t)=\sigma_{\min}^{1-t}\sigma_{\max}^{t}\) is a different log-linear schedule. Schedule quality depends on the trained model, solver, discretization budget, and data—not a universal ordering over “linear” or “cosine” schedules.

    The EDM paper reports FID 1.79 on CIFAR-10 under its sampler and evaluation protocol. EDM2 analyzes training dynamics, introduces magnitude-preserving architectural changes and post-hoc EMA reconstruction, and reports FID 1.81 on ImageNet 512² under its evaluated configuration. These values are reference points for the named experiments, not timeless rankings or matched cross-dataset comparisons.

    Flow Matching and Rectified-Flow Systems¤

    Continuous-time flow models are common in large-scale visual generators, especially when paired with Transformer backbones.

    Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2023 — ICLR) trains a vector field \(v_\theta(x, t)\) to regress against a target conditional probability path (Gaussian, optimal-transport, etc.):

    • Simulation-free conditional regression — the standard conditional flow-matching loss does not solve the learned ODE during each training example.
    • Path-dependent optimization — numerical conditioning and learned error depend on the probability path, coupling, time distribution, weight, architecture, and optimizer; stability relative to DDPM must be measured.
    • Declared endpoint couplings — conditional paths can be built from independent, optimal-transport-inspired, minibatch, or problem-specific couplings. Stochastic interpolants (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Albergo et al., 2024) provide a broader ODE/SDE construction.

    Rectified Flow (Liu, Gong & Liu, 2023 — ICLR) is a special case that learns ODEs following straight paths between noise and data:

    \[ \frac{dx}{dt} = v_\theta(x, t), \]

    where the conditional trajectories are straight lines \(\psi_t = (1-t)x_0 + t\, x_1\). The reflow operation iteratively straightens trajectories:

    1. Sample endpoint pairs \((x_0, x_1)\) from an initial data–reference coupling.
    2. Train \(v_\theta\) on straight conditional paths between those paired endpoints; the learned marginal trajectories may still curve.
    3. Solve the learned ODE to induce a new coupling between reference and generated endpoints.
    4. Retrain on straight conditional paths under the induced coupling.
    5. Repeat.

    InstaFlow (Liu et al., 2023) evaluates one-step text-to-image generation after reflow and distillation and reports roughly 0.12 seconds per image in its disclosed hardware setting. The latency is not architecture- or hardware-independent.

    System scale: rectified-flow and flow-matching objectives appear in MMDiT-based Stable Diffusion 3 / 3.5 (Esser et al., 2024), FLUX.1 (Black Forest Labs, 2024), and several video systems. Product names do not establish the exact objective, weighting, or sampler without a corresponding technical disclosure.

    Distillation, Flow Matching, and Discrete State Spaces: Diffusion Language Models¤

    Discrete diffusion applies categorical corruption and iterative refinement to text and other token sequences. D3PM, absorbing-mask models, and continuous-time categorical processes lead to several language-model constructions:

    • LLaDA (Nie et al., 2025) — a masked-diffusion LLM trained from scratch at 8B parameters, competitive with LLaMA3-8B on in-context learning and instruction following after SFT.
    • Mercury (Inception Labs, 2025) — a commercial-scale diffusion LLM with multi-token parallel decoding, reporting substantially lower latency than autoregressive baselines in selected settings.

    A Brief Note on Critiques of Diffusion¤

    Several lines of work question which parts of diffusion are essential:

    • MAR (Li et al., 2024) shows masked-AR generation can match diffusion quality.
    • VAR (Tian et al., 2024) — visual autoregression with next-scale prediction; first AR model to beat DiT on ImageNet.
    • LDM-without-VAE (2025) — argues that a VAE bottleneck can hurt its diffusion setup and explores a VAE-free latent construction.

    These are not replacements for diffusion as a whole. They are controlled opportunities to ask which benefit came from the probability path, architecture, representation, objective, transition parameterization, or systems implementation rather than attributing it to a family name.

    Worked example: equal marginals do not imply equal algorithms¤

    Assume an exact reverse SDE and its probability-flow ODE start from the same terminal distribution. At every continuous time they have the same marginal density, but conditional on one initial noise the ODE follows one deterministic trajectory while the SDE produces a distribution over trajectories. Euler integration of the ODE and Euler–Maruyama integration of the SDE then introduce different finite-step errors. Adding CFG changes the field again. Therefore “same marginals under exact dynamics” is a distributional equivalence under assumptions, not algebraic equivalence of samplers or equality at a fixed NFE.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    Diffusion models declare a corruption path and learn information needed to simulate a reverse process or associated transport. The path may be fixed or, in a tractable construction, jointly learned. Regression-style training avoids a two-player game but does not guarantee optimization stability, calibrated likelihoods, coverage, or efficient sampling.

    Core Principles:

    • Forward diffusion gradually corrupts data toward a reference law; a finite endpoint may retain residual signal
    • Reverse diffusion learns to progressively denoise, reconstructing data from noise
    • Training objectives often use weighted denoising regression, whose target, time law, reduction, and parameterization matter
    • Sampling iteratively applies learned denoising, refining noise into data

    Key Variants:

    • DDPM: Foundational stochastic reverse-chain construction; the original image experiments used a 1,000-step discretization.
    • DDIM: Implicit deterministic or stochastic paths compatible with a DDPM-trained denoiser; the selected time grid and step count are protocol choices.
    • Latent diffusion: operates in a learned codec space and is common in deployed visual systems; observed-space and other representations remain active.
    • Consistency, adversarial, and distribution-matching methods: one- or few-evaluation generation; NFE reduction is not a portable wall-clock factor.
    • Rectified Flow / Flow-Matching DiTs: straight-path ODEs powering SD3, FLUX.1, HunyuanVideo, Wan 2.2.
    • DiT, U-ViT, MMDiT, and SiT: transformer backbones or training combinations with strong scaling evidence in named regimes, not a universal dominance theorem over U-Nets.
    • Autoregressive visual challengers (MAR, VAR): transformer image generators that borrow GPT-style scaling ideas rather than diffusion denoising.
    • Diffusion Language Models (LLaDA, Mercury, Dream, ReFusion): masked discrete diffusion competitive with autoregressive baselines in selected 8B-scale settings.

    Architecture Innovations:

    • U-Net with skip connections — the classical backbone (DDPM, SD 1.x / 2.x, Imagen, ControlNet).
    • Diffusion Transformers (DiT, U-ViT, MMDiT, PixArt, HDiT, SiT, FiT) — an important backbone family for large-scale systems; covered in Beyond U-Net.
    • adaLN / adaLN-Zero / adaLN-single conditioning — the standard timestep-injection mechanism for transformer backbones.
    • Joint attention (MMDiT) — bidirectional text/image interaction used by SD3-like systems; its isolated contribution to typography is not established by public system comparisons.
    • Sparse / linear-attention backbones — DiT-MoE (16B sparse), DiM, DiG, EDiT, HDiT-style neighbourhood attention for high-resolution efficiency.
    • 2-D RoPE positions — variable-resolution / variable-aspect-ratio support (FiT, FLUX.1).
    • FlashAttention-⅔ + feature caching (DeepCache, L2C, TeaCache) — the implementation foundation that makes everything above trainable and deployable.

    Training contract: reproduce a named schedule/target/weighting baseline; report reduction and data units; validate raw and EMA checkpoints; inspect per-time errors, likelihood/path measures, samples, gradients, precision, and conditioning; and treat Min-SNR's reported convergence improvement as protocol-bounded.

    Sampling Methods:

    • DDPM: historical ancestral chain, often 1,000 evaluations in the original discretization.
    • DDIM: implicit deterministic or stochastic paths on a chosen time subset; quality and latency depend on spacing and checkpoint.
    • DPM-Solver / DPM-Solver++ / UniPC / DEIS: 10–20 steps with high-order ODE solvers.
    • Consistency / LCM / SiD / DMD2 / ADD: distinct one- to four-evaluation methods with different teacher, adversarial, or distributional objectives.

    Guidance Techniques:

    • Classifier-free guidance is widely used, with multiple scale conventions.
    • The useful scale is checkpoint-, parameterization-, solver-, prompt-, and rescaling-dependent; no universal 7–8 setting exists.
    • Increasing scale often strengthens conditional alignment while changing fidelity, coverage, calibration, and artifacts non-monotonically.

    Selection conditions:

    • Use a diffusion or score model when its declared forward path and reverse conditional naturally express the observation, corruption, or inverse problem.
    • For super-resolution, inpainting, editing, and scientific inverse problems, compare posterior or conditional correctness—not only unconditional perceptual quality.
    • For text-conditioned image or video generation, include codec, text encoder, guidance, filtering, and decoder cost in the system comparison.
    • Prefer the family only when matched evidence meets the required fidelity, coverage, calibration, latency, memory, and data constraints; denoising regression does not guarantee easier optimization.

    System comparison:

    • Diffusion and flow-based systems are prominent across many image/video protocols, while AR, GAN, implicit, and hybrid systems remain competitive in selected regimes.
    • Few-step methods substantially reduce NFE; whether they close a deployment speed/quality gap must be measured on matched hardware and operating points.
    • Rectified-flow DiTs (SD3, FLUX.1, HunyuanVideo, Wan 2.2) are used in several large-scale visual systems; this does not make U-Net/DDPM recipes categorically obsolete.
    • Diffusion language models such as LLaDA and Mercury report parallel-token updates and latency results in selected settings. Compare output length, number of refinement steps, hardware, batching, quality, and likelihood semantics with autoregressive baselines.
    • Open-weight video systems such as HunyuanVideo, Wan, Mochi-1, CogVideoX, and LTX-Video expose more reproducible evidence than closed interfaces, but cross-system quality gaps remain undefined without matched prompts, data disclosures, sampling settings, and human protocols.
    • Preference and reward post-training is one important layer whose contribution should be isolated from base data, captions, architecture, filtering, and inference changes.

    Research questions:

    • Which teacher information must one-step video distillation preserve to retain temporal coverage, conditional alignment, and physical consistency at a declared latency budget?
    • When do parallel-token diffusion language models improve the quality–latency trade-off after matching active parameters, output length, refinement NFE, batching, and hardware?
    • Does sharing one denoising backbone across image, video, audio, and text improve transfer or create modality interference under matched parameter and data budgets?
    • Which combinations of distillation and quantization retain rare modes and calibration on edge hardware when decoder and preprocessing cost are included?
    • In protein design, docking, materials, and medical imaging, which invariances, measurement models, and constraint metrics are required before perceptual sample quality is scientifically relevant?
    • Which properties of DiT scaling, representation alignment, or adversarial distillation follow from population objectives, and which are contingent on architecture and optimization?

    “Diffusion” now covers related discrete and continuous corruptions, reverse processes, score estimators, and hybrid systems. GAN losses, codecs, masked-token kernels, flows, rewards, and few-step transitions can compose with them. This is a useful shared substrate only when each relationship and system boundary is stated; future dominance is an empirical question, not a scientific conclusion.

    Cumulative worked example: design an auditable multimodal diffusion study¤

    Design a conditional generator for 10-second scientific videos with irregular sensor observations and a 200 ms serving budget. A defensible proposal must:

    1. declare the observation/function-space target, masks, time grid, codec and reconstruction error;
    2. define the forward kernel or probability path and its endpoint/reference law;
    3. specify target (\(\epsilon\), \(x_0\), VP \(v\), score, or flow velocity), time distribution, weight, and reduction;
    4. choose an architecture whose temporal/spatial symmetries and conditioning are testable;
    5. compare stochastic and deterministic solvers at matched NFE and wall-clock, with explicit CFG convention;
    6. report likelihood/bounds where meaningful, fidelity, temporal consistency, coverage, conditional calibration, physical-constraint violations, memorization, and uncertainty;
    7. compare observed-space, latent, autoregressive, and flow baselines under matched data and compute; and
    8. attach evidence maturity, reproduction status, hardware, active parameters, decoder cost, and verification date to every benchmark claim.
    One defensible solution outline

    Use a stochastic-process latent model rather than treating frames as independent images. Encode only after measuring codec error on rare events; include observation masks in the conditional model. Train a continuous-time velocity or score target with units and time sampling declared, then validate conversions on a Gaussian process toy problem. Use an equivariant temporal/spatial backbone if the domain symmetry warrants it, but measure constraint violations. Compare a stochastic solver for calibrated ensembles with a probability-flow/ODE solver for latency, and test whether few-step distillation loses rare modes. The 200 ms gate is end-to-end—including encoder, denoiser evaluations, decoder, and postprocessing—not an NFE target.

    Final misconception check¤

    • A conditional perturbation score is not the noisy marginal score for an individual example.
    • A DiT is an architecture, not inherently DDPM, flow matching, or diffusion.
    • VP \(v\) and flow-matching velocity require separate conventions.
    • Same continuous-time marginals do not make reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE finite solvers identical.
    • CFG scale is ambiguous without its formula.
    • Latent diffusion cannot recover information discarded by its codec.
    • One NFE, exact likelihood, world-model behavior, and a benchmark-quality claim all require additional qualifiers.
