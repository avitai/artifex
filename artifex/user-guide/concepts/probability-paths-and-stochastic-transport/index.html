    Diffusion, probability-flow ODEs, flow matching, stochastic interpolants, and Schrödinger bridges all construct paths between probability distributions. Their equations overlap, but “same path,” “same marginals,” “same endpoints,” and “same finite-step sampler” are different claims.

    This volume supplies the missing comparison layer. It follows a path from endpoint couplings and continuity equations through deterministic and stochastic dynamics, then audits exactly what each claimed correspondence preserves.

    The motivating question is concrete: when two generators both transform noise into data, what must be equal before it is meaningful to call them “the same model”? Equal endpoints are the weakest answer. A coupling, an entire path of marginals, a law on trajectories, a learned vector field, and a finite numerical algorithm carry progressively more information. The book builds that ladder before comparing families so that familiar words such as flow, diffusion, bridge, and transport cannot silently change meaning.

    How to read this book

    Read continuously the first time; each chapter reuses the six-object comparison contract.
    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2 after the continuous-time foundation.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–3 and 6.
    Research: all chapters, including bridge objectives, coupling dependence, and evidence appraisal.

    Book-length routes into the subject¤

    Probability-path modeling sits at the intersection of probability, stochastic processes, optimal transport, numerical analysis, and modern generative learning. These complementary references were checked against author, publisher, or proceedings pages:

    • Probability and stochastic-process route: Øksendal's Stochastic Differential Equations develops Itô calculus, generators, and diffusion equations. Pavliotis's Stochastic Processes and Applications gives a complementary Fokker--Planck and multiscale route. They supply the classical machinery behind Chapters 1–2, not a neural-network recipe.
    • Optimal-transport route: Peyré and Cuturi's freely available Computational Optimal Transport connects couplings, entropic regularization, algorithms, and the dynamic Benamou--Brenier formulation. Villani's Optimal Transport: Old and New is the deeper mathematical route. Neither says that an arbitrary learned probability path is the minimum-action optimal-transport path.
    • Diffusion route: Song and collaborators' Score-SDE paper gives the forward SDE, reverse-time SDE, probability-flow ODE, and likelihood construction used in Chapter 2. Read it together with the finite-error analysis here: an exact continuous identity does not certify a coarse solver.
    • Flow-matching route: the self-contained Flow Matching Guide and Code develops conditional paths, marginalization, couplings, parameterizations, and implementations. This volume adds an explicit object-by-object comparison with diffusion and bridges.
    • Stochastic-interpolant and bridge route: Albergo, Boffi, and Vanden-Eijnden's stochastic-interpolant framework derives transport and Fokker--Planck representations from a shared interpolant. Chen, Georgiou, and Pavon's stochastic-control account connects Schrödinger bridges, controlled reference processes, and zero-noise optimal transport. Chapter 4 keeps the finite-noise and zero-noise problems distinct.

    Translate every source into the invariant used below before transferring a claim: state space, endpoint laws, endpoint coupling, marginal path, trajectory or transition law, learned target, and finite algorithm.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to distinguish endpoint laws, couplings, marginal paths, trajectory laws, learned fields, and finite solvers; derive the continuity and Fokker--Planck views used by probability-flow ODEs; state when score diffusion and deterministic transport share marginals; build conditional flow-matching and bridge objectives without suppressing their coupling or reference process; transfer the framework to discrete, manifold, and function-valued data; and design comparisons that expose coupling, estimation, discretization, and systems error separately.

    A path of distributions is not a path of particles

    The family \((p_t)\) specifies one-time marginals. It does not identify which sample at time zero became which sample at time one, whether trajectories are deterministic or stochastic, or how a finite solver approximates them.

    1. Probability Paths, Couplings, and Conservation¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes probability densities, conditional expectation, and basic ODE/SDE notation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate six probability-path objects: distinguish endpoint laws, endpoint couplings, marginal paths, trajectory laws, fields, and finite algorithms.
    2. O2 — Construct coupling-dependent paths: compute intermediate laws for independent, identity, and reflection couplings with the same endpoints.
    3. O3 — Derive deterministic conservation: obtain the continuity equation in strong and weak form and interpret \(J_t=p_tv_t\) as probability flux.
    4. O4 — Derive stochastic conservation: obtain the Fokker--Planck equation and its current velocity for constant or state-dependent diffusion.
    5. O5 — Diagnose nonuniqueness: exhibit different velocity fields or path laws that preserve the same marginals.
    6. O6 — Transfer the contract: specify the six objects for a new path-based generator before choosing a model name or solver.

    A probability path is a family \((p_t)_{t\in[0,1]}\) connecting source \(p_0\) and target \(p_1\). Endpoint agreement alone does not determine the intermediate distributions or how individual particles move.

    The word path is overloaded. A sample path is one realized trajectory \(t\mapsto X_t(\omega)\); a law on path space assigns probabilities to whole trajectories; and a marginal probability path retains only \(\mathcal L(X_t)=p_t\) at each time. Two processes can agree on every \(p_t\) and still disagree on correlations such as \(\operatorname{Cov}(X_s,X_t)\).

    Couplings¤

    A coupling \(\pi(x_0,x_1)\) has marginals \(p_0\) and \(p_1\). Independent coupling uses \(p_0p_1\); an optimal-transport coupling minimizes an expected cost; paired data may supply a semantic coupling. Different couplings can yield the same endpoint distributions and radically different trajectories.

    For linear interpolation,

    \[ X_t=(1-t)X_0+tX_1, \qquad (X_0,X_1)\sim\pi, \]

    the path \(p_t\) depends on \(\pi\), not only on \(p_0,p_1\).

    Three coupling questions should be answered separately:

    1. Validity: do the row and column marginals of \(\pi\) equal \(p_0,p_1\)?
    2. Semantics: does the pairing encode independent sampling, observed pairs, an OT cost, or an artifact of minibatch matching?
    3. Geometry: under the chosen conditional interpolation, what path length, crossing, congestion, or target variance does the coupling induce?

    An optimal coupling is always relative to a declared cost. A semantic pairing need not minimize Euclidean squared distance, and a minibatch OT plan need not equal the population OT coupling. The dynamic formulation in Computational Optimal Transport minimizes kinetic action under the continuity equation; ordinary flow matching can learn a prescribed non-minimal path.

    Continuity equation¤

    A deterministic velocity field \(v_t(x)\) transports density according to

    \[ \partial_t p_t(x)+\nabla\cdot(p_t(x)v_t(x))=0. \]

    Particles solve

    \[ \frac{dX_t}{dt}=v_t(X_t). \]

    The continuity equation constrains only the divergence of probability flux. Multiple velocity fields can realize the same marginal path; divergence-free components may alter trajectories without altering density evolution.

    The weak form is often the correct one when a density is nonsmooth. For every smooth compactly supported test function \(\varphi\),

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\int \varphi(x)p_t(x)\,dx =\int \nabla\varphi(x)\cdot v_t(x)p_t(x)\,dx, \]

    assuming boundary flux vanishes or is accounted for. This equation says that the rate of change of every observable is explained by transport. It also exposes what must change on a bounded domain: reflecting boundaries impose zero normal flux; absorbing boundaries permit loss of total mass unless a cemetery state is included.

    Writing \(J_t=p_tv_t\) gives \(\partial_t p_t+\nabla\cdot J_t=0\). The marginal path determines only the divergence of \(J_t\). In one dimension, a boundary condition can largely fix the flux by integration. In two or more dimensions, divergence-free circulation can remain.

    Fokker–Planck equation¤

    For an Itô SDE with \(a_t(x)=G_t(x)G_t(x)^\top\),

    \[ dX_t=f_t(X_t)\,dt+G_t(X_t)\,dW_t, \]

    the marginal density obeys

    \[ \partial_t p_t =-\sum_i\partial_i(f_i p_t) +\frac12\sum_{i,j}\partial_i\partial_j(a_{ij}p_t). \]

    For isotropic, state-independent \(G_t=g_tI\), this becomes

    \[ \partial_t p_t=-\nabla\cdot(f_tp_t)+\frac12g_t^2\Delta p_t. \]

    Diffusion spreads mass while drift moves it. Rewriting the equation as deterministic probability flux yields the current velocity

    \[ v_{t,i}(x)=f_{t,i}(x) -\frac{1}{2p_t(x)}\sum_j\partial_j\!\left(a_{ij,t}(x)p_t(x)\right). \]

    Only when \(a_t=g_t^2I\) is spatially constant does this simplify to \(v_t=f_t-\tfrac12g_t^2\nabla\log p_t\). Omitting the derivative of a state-dependent diffusion matrix is a genuine equation error, not a notation choice.

    Worked coupling contrast¤

    Let both endpoints be standard normal. If \(X_1=X_0\), linear interpolation keeps \(X_t=X_0\) and every marginal standard normal. If \(X_1=-X_0\), then \(X_{1/2}=0\) deterministically even though both endpoints are standard normal. Same endpoints do not imply the same path or transport.

    The independent coupling supplies a third path. With independent standard normals, \(X_t=(1-t)X_0+tX_1\) is Gaussian with variance

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X_t)=(1-t)^2+t^2. \]

    At \(t=1/4\), the identity, reflection, and independent couplings therefore give variances \(1\), \(1/4\), and \(5/8\). All have exactly the same endpoint laws.

    Worked stationary rotation: same marginals, different particles¤

    Let \(p(x)\propto\exp(-\|x\|^2/2)\) on \(\mathbb R^2\). The zero field leaves every particle fixed. The rotational field

    \[ v(x_1,x_2)=(-x_2,x_1) \]

    moves particles around circles. Yet \(\nabla\cdot v=0\) and \(v\cdot\nabla p=0\), so \(\nabla\cdot(pv)=0\) and both fields preserve the same stationary Gaussian marginal path. The path \(p_t=p\) cannot reveal whether a particular particle stayed still or rotated.

    Misconception checks: objects and conservation¤

    • “A coupling is a path.” No. It pairs endpoints; a conditional interpolation or transition law is still required.
    • “The continuity equation identifies the velocity.” Not generally. It identifies a divergence constraint on probability flux.
    • “Fokker--Planck diffusion has no deterministic representation.” Its one-time marginals can be represented by a current velocity under suitable smoothness and positivity, but the resulting ODE does not reproduce sample paths or transition laws.
    • “Optimal transport means the shortest visible straight line.” OT is relative to a cost, admissible coupling class, and marginal constraints; the dynamic problem minimizes an integrated action, not a drawing.

    Independent practice and assessment map: objects and conservation¤

    Attempt each prediction before doing algebra. Every solution must name which of the six objects has changed and which has stayed fixed.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: three Gaussian couplings. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. For standard-normal endpoints and linear interpolation, rank the midpoint variances under identity, reflection, and independent coupling, then compute the variance of each at \(t=1/4\).
    2. P1.2 — Construct a finite coupling counterexample. Type: design · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. Put equal endpoint mass on \(\{-1,+1\}\). Write the identity and swap coupling matrices and compute the midpoint law under linear interpolation.
    3. P1.3 — Verify weak conservation. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Let \(X_t=X_0+t\) with \(X_0\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\). State \(p_t\), \(v_t\), and use \(\varphi(x)=x\) and \(x^2\) in the weak continuity equation to recover the derivatives of the first two moments.
    4. P1.4 — Diagnose velocity nonuniqueness. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For a stationary standard Gaussian on \(\mathbb R^2\), compare \(v=0\) with \(v=(-x_2,x_1)\). Verify both flux divergences vanish and state what temporal information differs.
    5. P1.5 — Correct a state-dependent Fokker--Planck shortcut. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. In one dimension take \(a(x)=e^x\), \(f=0\), and a standard-normal density. Derive the current velocity and evaluate it at \(x=0\) and \(x=1\). Compare with the incorrect shortcut \(-a(x)s(x)/2\).
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a constrained state space. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Specify endpoint laws, coupling, marginal path, path law, infinitesimal field, boundary condition, and finite solver for particles moving inside a disk without leaving it. Name one audit for every object.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Endpoint laws, couplings, marginal paths, and particle dynamics are separate objects. Conservation equations connect dynamics to marginals but do not always make the dynamics unique. Chapter 2 applies this distinction to score diffusion and its deterministic probability-flow representation.

    If two models share \(p_0\) and \(p_1\), are their samplers equivalent?

    No. They can use different couplings, intermediate marginals, trajectories, stochasticity, and numerical errors.

    2. Score Diffusion and Probability-Flow ODEs¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and score-based diffusion fundamentals. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive reverse dynamics: obtain the reverse-time score correction while keeping the time convention explicit.
    2. O2 — Derive probability flow: rewrite Fokker--Planck evolution as a deterministic current velocity.
    3. O3 — State the equivalence boundary: identify exactly which marginals the exact reverse SDE and probability-flow ODE share and which path objects they do not.
    4. O4 — Recover a marginal score: use posterior averaging of conditional corruption scores and distinguish sample targets from population optima.
    5. O5 — Audit likelihood and finite error: separate score, endpoint, divergence, solver, and preprocessing errors.
    6. O6 — Transfer the audit: design a controlled ODE-versus-SDE comparison with matched probability target and declared compute.

    Consider a forward diffusion

    \[ dX_t=f_t(X_t)\,dt+g_t\,dW_t \]

    with marginals \(p_t\). Under regularity, its reverse-time SDE has drift

    \[ f_t(x)-g_t^2\nabla_x\log p_t(x), \]

    when integrated from terminal time toward zero with the reverse-time convention. Sign mistakes arise if negative \(dt\) and reverse Brownian notation are suppressed.

    The probability-flow ODE is

    \[ \frac{dX_t}{dt} =f_t(X_t)-\frac12g_t^2\nabla_x\log p_t(X_t). \]

    Score-SDE theory shows that the ODE and SDE have the same one-time marginals when the exact score and regularity assumptions hold. This is distributional/dynamical equivalence under assumptions, not pathwise equality:

    • the ODE is deterministic conditional on its initial state;
    • the reverse SDE injects noise;
    • transition laws and couplings across times differ;
    • finite score error and numerical solvers perturb the equivalence.

    Time orientation without sign ambiguity¤

    There are two safe conventions:

    • retain the original coordinate \(t\) and integrate from \(T\) down to \(0\), so the numerical step \(\Delta t\) is negative; or
    • introduce forward-running reverse time \(\tau=T-t\) and transform every drift, diffusion coefficient, and score argument.

    Do not copy a reverse drift written in the first convention into a solver using the second. A unit test should verify the mean and variance evolution for an analytic Gaussian path before any learned score is involved.

    Conditional versus marginal scores¤

    For a corruption kernel \(q_t(x_t\mid x_0)\),

    \[ \mathbb E[\nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(X_t\mid X_0)\mid X_t=x] =\nabla_x\log p_t(x). \]

    Denoising regression therefore targets the marginal score at population optimum. A particular conditional target is not itself the marginal score before averaging over the clean posterior.

    For a Gaussian corruption \(X_t=\alpha_tX_0+\sigma_t\epsilon\),

    \[ \nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(x_t\mid x_0) =-\frac{x_t-\alpha_tx_0}{\sigma_t^2} =-\frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t}. \]

    An \(x_0\) predictor, noise predictor, score predictor, and velocity predictor can be converted by path-dependent linear formulas when the required coefficients are nonzero. This is algebraic equivalence for the chosen Gaussian path. Loss weighting, endpoint singularities, architecture, optimization, and solver behavior are not made equal by the conversion.

    Likelihood through the ODE¤

    For an ODE flow,

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(X_t)=-\nabla\cdot v_t(X_t). \]

    Integrating divergence can estimate likelihood. Hutchinson trace estimation, adaptive integration, score error, and endpoint dequantization affect the result. An ODE existing in theory does not make likelihood cheap.

    For a sample transported from time \(0\) to \(T\),

    \[ \log p_0(X_0) =\log p_T(X_T)+\int_0^T\nabla\cdot v_t(X_t)\,dt, \]

    with the sign determined by the direction in which the trajectory and density are integrated. In high dimensions, a Hutchinson estimator replaces the trace of \(\nabla v\) by \(\epsilon^\top(\nabla v)\epsilon\). Its random probes add variance; reusing or resampling probes changes estimator dependence; and the ODE state and log-density integral share numerical error.

    3. Flow Matching, Conditional Paths, and Rectification¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional expectation and deterministic transport from Chapters 1–2. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive conditional flow matching: integrate conditional continuity equations to obtain the marginal velocity as a posterior average.
    2. O2 — Decompose regression risk: separate learnable marginal-field error from coupling-induced conditional variance.
    3. O3 — Analyze path design: compute how endpoint coupling and interpolation coefficients change marginals, velocities, and ambiguity.
    4. O4 — Convert matched targets safely: relate data, noise, score, and velocity targets only for the same nonsingular Gaussian path.
    5. O5 — Audit rectification: distinguish straight conditional segments, marginal flow geometry, reflow rounds, and few-step solver accuracy.
    6. O6 — Transfer to an experiment: design a coupling/path/solver ablation whose conclusion cannot outrun its controlled variables.

    Flow Matching specifies a probability path and regresses its velocity without simulating the learned ODE during training. Let a conditional construction produce \(X_t\) and pathwise derivative \(\dot X_t\). The squared objective

    \[ \mathbb E\|v_\theta(t,X_t)-\dot X_t\|^2 \]

    has population minimizer

    \[ v^*(t,x)=\mathbb E[\dot X_t\mid X_t=x]. \]

    This conditional expectation is the marginal velocity satisfying the continuity equation under appropriate regularity. Regressing tractable conditional velocities and regressing the inaccessible marginal velocity share a population-optimum correspondence.

    To see why the posterior average solves the marginal equation, write a conditional density \(p_t(x\mid z)\) and conditional field \(u_t(x\mid z)\) with

    \[ \begin{aligned} \partial_t p_t(x\mid z) &+\nabla\cdot\left[p_t(x\mid z)u_t(x\mid z)\right] \\ &=0. \end{aligned} \]

    Integrating over \(z\) gives the marginal flux

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_t(x)v_t(x) &=\int p_t(x\mid z)u_t(x\mid z) \\ &\qquad p(z)\,dz, \end{aligned} \]

    hence \(v_t(x)=\mathbb E[u_t(X_t\mid Z)\mid X_t=x]\) wherever \(p_t(x)>0\). The result requires compatible regularity and interchange of differentiation and integration; it is not merely a neural-network heuristic.

    Squared loss also yields the orthogonal decomposition

    \[ \mathbb E\|v_\theta(t,X_t)-U_t\|^2 =\mathbb E\|v_\theta(t,X_t)-v^*(t,X_t)\|^2 +\mathbb E\|U_t-v^*(t,X_t)\|^2. \]

    The second term is conditional label variance. It cannot be removed by a more expressive deterministic predictor; coupling and path design determine it.

    Path and coupling design¤

    Straight conditional interpolation is easy but can cause crossing trajectories. OT or minibatch-OT couplings can reduce conditional path length. Gaussian conditional paths connect flow matching with common diffusion schedules. Changing the coupling changes regression variance and transport geometry even when endpoints remain fixed.

    For a linear conditional path

    \[ X_t=\alpha_tX_0+\beta_tX_1, \qquad U_t=\dot\alpha_tX_0+\dot\beta_tX_1, \]

    the coefficients, endpoint coupling, and time-sampling distribution jointly define the training problem. The usual straight path uses \(\alpha_t=1-t,\beta_t=t\). A Gaussian diffusion-style path typically pairs data with Gaussian noise and chooses coefficients to reproduce a declared noising schedule. Calling both “flow matching” does not make their \(p_t\) or target variance equal.

    Rectified flow¤

    Rectified Flow repeatedly couples generated endpoints and retrains straighter transports. Straightness can reduce discretization error, but “straight conditional paths” do not imply the learned marginal field is constant or exactly solvable in one Euler step. Endpoint dependence and crossing still matter.

    A reflow round changes the endpoint coupling by pairing generated samples with their transported endpoints, then trains a new field on the induced straight segments. It may preserve an ideal endpoint map under its assumptions while changing intermediate densities. Distillation, consistency training, or a one-step map adds another objective and error layer; it is not implied by the word rectified.

    Worked crossing example¤

    In one dimension, couple \(X_0=-1\) to \(X_1=1\) and \(X_0=1\) to \(X_1=-1\). Both conditional paths meet at \(X_{1/2}=0\) with velocities \(+2\) and \(-2\). Their conditional expectation at the crossing is zero. The marginal velocity cannot remember which path a particle came from without extra state, illustrating why coupling-induced crossings increase ambiguity.

    At the crossing, a scalar predictor \(a\) has conditional risk

    \[ \frac12(a-2)^2+\frac12(a+2)^2=a^2+4. \]

    The best deterministic field predicts zero and still incurs irreducible risk four. A latent-conditioned field could retain endpoint identity, but that would change the model interface and its sampling state.

    Flow matching versus diffusion¤

    With matched Gaussian paths, velocity, noise, data, and score targets admit exact linear conversions when coefficients are nonsingular. That is algebraic equivalence of parameterizations for the chosen path. A flow-matching ODE with an OT coupling and a score-SDE with a prescribed noising process need not share intermediate marginals or finite algorithms.

    The Flow Matching Guide and Code provides a broader derivation and implementations. Before transferring a formula, record the time direction, which endpoint is data, \(\alpha_t,\beta_t\) or \(\sigma_t\), the prediction convention, and loss weighting. Endpoint values where a conversion divides by a vanishing coefficient require a separate parameterization or limiting argument.

    Misconception checks: conditional flow matching¤

    • “Simulation-free training means simulation-free generation.” Training labels may avoid integrating the learned ODE; generation still integrates a field unless a separate map or distillation is learned.
    • “A straight conditional path gives a constant neural field.” The marginal field averages all conditional labels that reach \((t,x)\) and can vary sharply.
    • “OT-CFM is population OT.” A minibatch transport plan is a finite-sample construction with batch-dependent bias and cost.
    • “Low training MSE proves low endpoint error.” Field error propagates through an ODE and interacts with solver and off-training-distribution states.

    4. Stochastic Interpolants and Schrödinger Bridges¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes stochastic dynamics, KL divergence, and the path distinctions from Chapter 1. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Build a stochastic interpolant: identify the endpoint coupling, interpolation map, latent noise schedule, induced marginal path, and learned targets.
    2. O2 — Define a Schrödinger bridge: formulate path-space relative-entropy projection with a declared reference process and two endpoint constraints.
    3. O3 — Connect static and dynamic views: relate endpoint matrix scaling, reference bridges, Doob transforms, and controlled stochastic dynamics.
    4. O4 — Separate bridges from OT: state what finite reference noise changes and what assumptions are required in a zero-noise limit.
    5. O5 — Diagnose identifiability and support: determine what unpaired endpoint marginals cannot specify and when reference zeros make a bridge infeasible.
    6. O6 — Transfer to a bridge experiment: design reference, endpoint, discretization, and semantic-coupling ablations with calibrated evidence.

    A stochastic interpolant constructs

    \[ X_t=I(t,X_0,X_1)+\gamma(t)Z, \]

    where \(Z\) supplies controllable noise and \(\gamma(0)=\gamma(1)=0\). Stochastic Interpolants derive velocity and score-based dynamics from this shared construction. Deterministic interpolation appears when \(\gamma=0\); diffusion-like paths arise for suitable schedules and couplings.

    The framework is a unifying language, not a claim that all choices are identical. \(I\), \(\gamma\), endpoint coupling, learned target, and sampler determine the actual algorithm.

    For \(t\in(0,1)\), the score of the Gaussian-latent interpolant obeys

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_t(x) =-\gamma(t)^{-1}\mathbb E[Z\mid X_t=x] \]

    under the framework's regularity assumptions. Velocity and score objectives therefore arise from the same declared interpolant, but their finite losses and endpoint conditioning differ. The stochastic-interpolant source also derives a family of forward and backward Fokker--Planck dynamics with adjustable diffusion; these dynamics share the constructed marginal path, not one common path law.

    Schrödinger bridge¤

    Given a reference path measure \(R\)—often Brownian diffusion—the Schrödinger bridge solves

    \[ \min_{P:\,P_0=p_0,\,P_1=p_1} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|R). \]

    It selects the path law closest in relative entropy to the reference while matching both endpoint marginals. This differs from merely learning any transport between endpoints. Diffusion Schrödinger Bridge uses iterative forward/backward fitting for generative modeling.

    Static endpoint projection and dynamic lift¤

    Let \(R_{01}\) be the joint law of \((X_0,X_1)\) under the reference. The static Schrödinger problem is

    \[ \min_{\pi\in\Pi(p_0,p_1)}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi\|R_{01}). \]

    Under standard positivity conditions, the solution has scaling form \(\pi^*(dx_0,dx_1)=a(x_0)R_{01}(dx_0,dx_1)b(x_1)\). The full path law then keeps the reference conditional bridge between endpoints:

    \[ P^*(d\omega) =\int R(d\omega\mid x_0,x_1)\,\pi^*(dx_0,dx_1). \]

    This factorization separates two roles: endpoint potentials choose how much mass each reference bridge receives, while the reference process specifies the conditional temporal fluctuations. Iterative proportional fitting or Sinkhorn scaling alternately enforces the two marginals in finite settings.

    For a reference diffusion with drift \(b_R\) and noise scale \(\sqrt\varepsilon\), the dynamic control view penalizes deviation from the reference:

    \[ \min_u\ \mathbb E\!\left[\frac{1}{2\varepsilon} \int_0^1\|u_t(X_t)\|^2dt\right], \quad dX_t=[b_R(t,X_t)+u_t(X_t)]dt+\sqrt\varepsilon\,dW_t, \]

    subject to both endpoint marginals. The exact coefficient depends on convention, but the invariant is stable: changing \(R\) or \(\varepsilon\) changes the optimization problem.

    Relation to optimal transport¤

    For Brownian reference with noise level tending to zero, entropic transport can converge toward dynamic optimal transport under suitable assumptions. At nonzero noise, the Schrödinger bridge is a stochastic entropy-regularized transport with different path law and cost. “SB equals OT” without the limiting regime is false.

    The stochastic-control treatment makes the relationship precise for controlled references and studies zero-noise limits. One must specify the scaling of the reference noise, topology of convergence, cost, endpoint regularity, and whether the statement concerns endpoint couplings, marginal paths, or controlled path laws. A finite-\(\varepsilon\) entropic plan is often smoother and computationally convenient; that is a different object, not a defective OT solution.

    Paired translation and inverse problems¤

    Bridges can connect two nontrivial data distributions, making them useful for translation, restoration, and trajectory inference. If only unpaired endpoints are available, the bridge is selected relative to the reference process—not necessarily the semantic pairing a user intends. Conditioning observations or boundary constraints changes the bridge problem and identification assumptions.

    Worked discrete bridge intuition¤

    On a finite Markov chain, choose a reference transition matrix and endpoint distributions. Reweighting reference paths by endpoint potentials can satisfy both marginals while minimally changing path KL. Changing the reference transition graph changes which paths are possible and therefore changes the bridge, even with identical endpoints.

    For an explicit one-step example, let the reference endpoint joint be uniform,

    \[ R_{01}=\begin{pmatrix}1/4&1/4\\1/4&1/4\end{pmatrix}, \]

    and require \(p_0=(3/4,1/4)\) and \(p_1=(2/5,3/5)\). Row scaling first gives \(\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}3/8&3/8\\1/8&1/8\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\); column scaling then gives

    \[ \pi^*=p_0p_1^\top =\begin{pmatrix}3/10&9/20\\1/10&3/20\end{pmatrix}. \]

    Because the reference endpoints are independent, the KL projection is the independent coupling with the required marginals. A non-product reference would retain some reference dependence after scaling.

    Brownian bridge, Schrödinger bridge, and diffusion bridge¤

    A Brownian bridge usually conditions a reference process on fixed endpoint values. A Schrödinger bridge constrains endpoint distributions and selects a new path law by KL projection. “Diffusion bridge” may refer broadly to conditioned diffusions or to learned generative bridge methods. Always ask which endpoints are fixed, which are distributions, and which reference law is being conditioned or controlled.

    Misconception checks: interpolants and bridges¤

    • “The endpoints identify the bridge.” They identify constraints; the reference path law selects among feasible paths.
    • “A Brownian bridge and a Schrödinger bridge are synonyms.” One commonly pins endpoint values; the other imposes endpoint marginals through a KL projection.
    • “Sinkhorn scaling proves a learned diffusion bridge is exact.” Matrix scaling solves a discretized static projection; learning, time discretization, and state approximation remain.
    • “Small noise can simply be set to zero.” A convergence theorem and scaling regime are required; finite-noise formulas may become singular.

    PFGM++ and augmented geometry¤

    PFGM++ embeds data into an augmented space and interprets generation through electric-field-like dynamics. Its augmentation dimension controls a family connecting Poisson-flow behavior with diffusion-like limits. This is a distributional/dynamical correspondence under a specific construction, not a statement that arbitrary electrostatic solvers and diffusion models are interchangeable.

    If \(\Pi(y,z)=y\) projects a lifted state onto observations, the observed marginal is \(p_t^Y=\Pi_\#p_t^{Y,Z}\). A correct lifted continuity equation does not by itself identify the projected density: integration over fibers, boundary flux, and possible multiplicities matter. Two lifted paths can have identical \(p_t^Y\) while moving differently in \(z\), or can share lifted endpoints while projecting to different intermediate observed laws.

    Discrete paths¤

    Discrete state spaces use probability-flow generators, jump rates, masking processes, or CTMCs rather than Euclidean velocities. A generator matrix \(Q_t\) evolves masses by

    \[ \partial_t p_t=p_tQ_t. \]

    Many \(Q_t\) can realize the same mass derivative. Positivity of off-diagonal rates, zero row sums, graph connectivity, and finite tau-leaping error replace Euclidean integrability constraints.

    With row-vector convention, a valid generator satisfies

    \[ Q_{ij,t}\ge0\quad(i\ne j), \qquad Q_{ii,t}=-\sum_{j\ne i}Q_{ij,t}. \]

    For two states, \(\dot p_1=p_0q_{01}-p_1q_{10}\). At \(p=(0.8,0.2)\) and \((q_{01},q_{10})=(0.5,1)\), the derivative is \((-0.2,0.2)\). A step of size \(0.1\) gives \((0.78,0.22)\) under explicit Euler, but larger steps can make masses negative even though the exact CTMC semigroup is stochastic. Exact event simulation, uniformization, tau leaping, and Euler mass integration are different finite algorithms.

    Discrete Flow Matching constructs general discrete probability paths and samples them through learned posterior parameterizations. Its CTMC semantics and normalized transition requirements should not be replaced by an unconstrained neural vector on one-hot embeddings.

    Manifolds and symmetry¤

    On a manifold, divergence, gradient, Brownian motion, and score depend on the metric and volume measure. Ambient Gaussian corruption can leave the manifold; intrinsic diffusion can be expensive or topologically constrained. Equivariant paths must commute with symmetry actions, while coordinate charts must not introduce singular artifacts.

    For density \(p_t\) relative to Riemannian volume and tangent field \(v_t(x)\in T_x\mathcal M\), conservation is

    \[ \partial_t p_t+\operatorname{div}_{g}(p_tv_t)=0. \]

    An ambient network must be projected to the tangent space or parameterized intrinsically. A tangent Euler step in the ambient space usually leaves a curved manifold; exponential maps, retractions, constraint-preserving integrators, or projection steps have different accuracy and cost. Riemannian Flow Matching uses geodesic conditional paths and tangent fields for declared geometries.

    Accepted ICML 2026 manifold flow-matching theory relates estimation complexity to intrinsic structure for a specified transformer/path setting. It is Peer-reviewed research, not universal evidence that every learned flow escapes ambient dimensionality.

    Function-space paths¤

    For random functions or fields, paths should remain coherent across discretizations. Training on one mesh can define a finite vector transport without a consistent function-space limit. Noise covariance, boundary conditions, operator topology, and projective consistency must be specified.

    If \(P_T\) is the law of evaluations \((f(t_1),\ldots,f(t_n))\) on a finite query set \(T\), consistency requires that restricting \(P_T\) to any \(S\subset T\) gives \(P_S\). It also requires permutation consistency. These Kolmogorov-style conditions are necessary for a stochastic-process interpretation; low error on one fixed mesh is not enough.

    Functional Flow Matching learns function-valued transport from a Gaussian-process reference, while Operator Flow Matching uses neural-operator machinery for stochastic-process priors and finite-query inference. Their finite bases, sampled query sets, numerical ODEs, trace estimators, and posterior samplers remain approximation layers.

    Same marginals, different finite algorithms¤

    Copula Models adds a stricter path-design example: every intermediate coordinate marginal must remain uniform while the joint dependence changes. A process that only matches independent and dependent endpoints is not thereby a valid copula path.

    Even when two exact continuous dynamics share \(p_t\):

    • Euler, Heun, Runge–Kutta, exponential, and stochastic solvers have different error;
    • learned vector/score errors are amplified differently;
    • stochastic dynamics can mix across modes after intermediate perturbation;
    • deterministic dynamics preserve a one-to-one coupling under uniqueness;
    • adaptive step counts and wall time differ;
    • guidance can break the original marginal equivalence.

    Therefore benchmark comparison belongs at matched error, NFE, tolerance, hardware, and conditioning—not solely at the continuous equation.

    Worked nonunique two-state generator¤

    At \(p=(1/2,1/2)\), demand \(\dot p_1=0.1\). The generator constraint is

    \[ \frac12q_{01}-\frac12q_{10}=0.1, \quad\text{or}\quad q_{01}-q_{10}=0.2. \]

    Both \((q_{01},q_{10})=(0.2,0)\) and \((0.7,0.5)\) realize the same instantaneous mass derivative. The second process has much greater bidirectional jump activity. A marginal derivative does not identify transition rates, just as a continuity equation does not identify a unique Euclidean velocity.

    Worked cost audit: equal nominal NFE, unequal execution¤

    Suppose Euler uses 20 steps and one field call per step, while Heun uses 10 steps and two stages per step. Without guidance, both use 20 network evaluations.
