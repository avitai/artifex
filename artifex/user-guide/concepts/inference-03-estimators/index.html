    4. Gradient Estimators¤

    Chapter 4 chapter prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Assume the expectation and ELBO notation from Chapter 2. By the end, you should be able to derive pathwise and score-function estimators, explain how BBVI and ADVI instantiate them, identify when exact enumeration or Rao–Blackwellization is preferable, and state separately whether an objective estimator and its gradient estimator are biased. The exhaustive estimator treatment, including discrete relaxations, antithetic couplings, multi-sample gradients, and stochastic computation graphs, lives in Gradients and Geometry; this chapter keeps the inference-facing derivation beside the ELBO.

    Pathwise reparameterization¤

    For

    \[ J(\phi)=\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}[f_\phi(z)], \qquad z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon),\quad \epsilon\sim s(\epsilon), \]

    differentiate the fixed-noise computation under the required domination and smoothness conditions:

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E_s\!\left[ \nabla_z f_\phi(z)\big|_{z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon)} \nabla_\phi g_\phi(x,\epsilon) +(\partial_\phi f_\phi)(z)\big|_{z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon)} \right]. \]

    Here \(\partial_\phi f_\phi\) differentiates only the explicit dependence of \(f_\phi\) on \(\phi\), holding its argument \(z\) fixed. The first term accounts for the dependence carried through the sampled state \(z=g_\phi(x,\epsilon)\); using a total derivative in the second term would count that path twice.

    For a diagonal Gaussian, \(z=\mu+\sigma\odot\epsilon\). This often has lower variance than a score-function estimator because it uses local derivatives, but there is no universal ordering: discontinuities, parameter-dependent support, heavy tails, or an ill-conditioned transform can invalidate the identity or make it unstable.

    Score-function estimator¤

    Without a convenient pathwise construction, differentiate the density:

    \[ \nabla_\phi\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z)}[f_\phi(z)] =\mathbb E_q\!\left[ f_\phi(z)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(z) +\nabla_\phi f_\phi(z) \right]. \]

    For the ELBO, the score-function term simplifies because \(\mathbb E_q[\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi]=0\):

    \[ \nabla_\phi\mathcal L =\mathbb E_{q_\phi}\!\left[ \bigl(\log p(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z)\bigr) \nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(z) \right], \]

    when \(p\) does not share \(\phi\) and the usual support/interchange conditions hold. This likelihood-ratio construction underlies black-box VI. A constant baseline \(b\) can replace the parenthesized learning signal by that signal minus \(b\), because the expected parameter score is zero. Structured baselines must satisfy the corresponding conditional zero-mean argument; an arbitrary function of a sampled child can bias the result.

    BBVI is “black box” with respect to model-specific conjugate algebra, not with respect to computation. It can still suffer severe gradient variance, guide misspecification, support mismatch, and poor local optima. ADVI instead combines valid unconstraining transforms with pathwise Gaussian samples and automatic differentiation. The two labels identify estimator pipelines, not posterior accuracy guarantees.

    Discrete relaxations such as Gumbel–Softmax trade temperature-dependent bias for pathwise differentiation of a relaxed objective; straight-through rules substitute a backward derivative and are generally biased. Exact marginalization is preferable when feasible, while score estimators with control variates remain available for the original discrete objective.

    Rao–Blackwellization and enumeration¤

    Before reaching for a relaxed categorical variable, ask whether part of the latent state can be summed out. If \(z=(z_a,z_b)\) and \(z_a\) has small finite support, compute

    \[ \mathbb E_{q(z_b)}\left[\sum_{z_a}q(z_a\mid z_b)f(z_a,z_b)\right] \]

    instead of sampling both variables. This conditional expectation is a Rao–Blackwellized estimator and cannot have higher variance than the original estimator under the usual square-integrability conditions. Exact enumeration also avoids the temperature bias of continuous relaxations.

    Biased and unbiased gradients¤

    An unbiased objective estimator need not produce an unbiased gradient after nonlinear operations such as self-normalization, clipping, or taking a logarithm. Conversely, a deliberately biased estimator can be useful if its mean-squared error is lower. A rigorous report should name the target objective, state whether the estimator and gradient are unbiased, and measure variance or effective sample size where possible.

    The distinction remains active in current work. For example, importance-weighted VI without pathwise reparameterization (Preprint, 2026) analyzes score-function estimators for multi-sample bounds and proposes an estimator under its stated conditions. Until peer review and broader reproduction, it is evidence that the discrete/nonreparameterizable case is still technically open—not evidence that one estimator dominates VIMCO, marginalization, or model-specific alternatives.

    Retrieval practice: latent gradient estimators
    1. When is pathwise reparameterization available?
    2. What identity underlies score-function estimators for discrete latent variables?
    3. Why can a baseline reduce variance without changing expectation?
    4. What bias can a straight-through estimator introduce?

    Solutions. (1) When samples can be expressed as a differentiable transformation of parameter-free noise with suitable regularity. (2) \(\nabla_\phi \mathbb E_{q_\phi}[f]=\mathbb E_{q_\phi}[f\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi]\) plus any direct derivative term. (3) A baseline independent of the sampled action/latent in the required conditional sense has zero expected score contribution. (4) It uses a backward derivative different from the forward discrete computation, so it generally is not the gradient of the stated expected objective.

    Chapter 4 chapter synthesis and bridge¤

    Gradient estimators are computational interfaces to a declared objective. BBVI and ADVI automate different estimator pipelines; pathwise, likelihood-ratio, enumerated, relaxed, and Rao–Blackwellized routes make different support and variance trade-offs. Relaxed and straight-through routes can change the differentiated target, so the change must be explicit. Chapter 4 turns from estimating derivatives to estimating integrals and posterior expectations with weighted and sequential particles.
