    5. Langevin Algorithms¤

    Gradient, Hamiltonian, and diagnostic learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes differentiable log targets and Markov-kernel concepts. By the end, you should be able to distinguish ULA, MALA, SGLD, overdamped, and underdamped dynamics; derive leapfrog and Metropolis correction for HMC; explain preconditioning and constrained transformations; interpret rank-normalized \(\widehat R\), ESS, MCSE, divergences, and energy diagnostics; and state the extra contracts for stochastic-gradient, kinetic, transdimensional, or structure- changing chains.

    The overdamped Langevin SDE targeting \(\pi(x)\propto e^{-E(x)}\) is

    \[ \begin{aligned} dX_t &=\nabla_x\log\pi(X_t)dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t\\ &=-\nabla E(X_t)dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t. \end{aligned} \]

    Euler–Maruyama gives unadjusted Langevin (ULA):

    \[ x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\nabla E(x_k)+\sqrt{2\eta}\,\xi_k. \]

    ULA has discretization bias at finite \(\eta\) and no accept/reject step. MALA uses the ULA proposal inside Metropolis–Hastings, correcting discretization bias under exact arithmetic. SGLD replaces full-data gradients with minibatch estimates and typically uses decreasing steps for asymptotic arguments.

    With the convention above, the MALA proposal is

    \[ q_\eta(x'\mid x) =\mathcal N\!\left(x';x+\eta\nabla\log\pi(x),2\eta I\right). \]

    It must use the asymmetric MH ratio

    \[ \alpha(x,x')= \min\left\{1, \frac{\widetilde\pi(x')q_\eta(x\mid x')} {\widetilde\pi(x)q_\eta(x'\mid x)} \right\}. \]

    The reverse proposal mean is evaluated at \(x'\). Treating a Langevin proposal as symmetric silently removes the correction that makes MALA exact under its assumptions.

    Energy need not decrease along a valid stochastic chain. Noise permits uphill moves required by the target distribution.

    ULA, MALA, and SGLD Are Not Interchangeable¤

    • ULA has no accept/reject step and targets a step-size-dependent approximation at fixed \(\eta\).
    • MALA corrects the proposal with an MH test, giving the intended invariant target under standard assumptions and exact computation.
    • SGLD replaces exact log-posterior gradients with stochastic minibatch gradients. Classical asymptotic guarantees use decreasing step sizes; constant-step deep-learning implementations have additional stationary bias.

    Annealed Langevin sampling used with score models is a finite schedule across changing noise levels. It is not a stationary chain targeting one fixed density at every step.

    In an EBM, the same updates become part of a learned negative-phase estimator; replay initialization, energy drift during training, and model change between updates add failure modes that a fixed-target MCMC theorem does not cover. See Langevin sampling in the EBM chapter.

    Preconditioning changes both drift and noise¤

    For a constant symmetric positive-definite mobility \(A\), a preconditioned diffusion can be written

    \[ dX_t=A\nabla\log\pi(X_t)dt+\sqrt{2A}\,dW_t. \]

    Both terms use the same geometry. Scaling only the gradient generally changes the stationary law. If \(A(x)\) depends on position, an additional divergence correction is normally required under the Itô convention; manifold Langevin methods must state that term and the volume convention. An RMSProp- or Adam-shaped update is not automatically a valid Riemannian sampler merely because it rescales a stochastic gradient.

    Discrete spaces require a transition construction, not a formal gradient¤

    A categorical or combinatorial state has no ordinary Euclidean state gradient. Gibbs, locally balanced proposals, continuous-time jump rates, discrete Langevin analogues, and continuous relaxations define different kernels. Some admit MH adjustment; unadjusted variants generally need their own bias result. The discrete iterative-generation chapter develops learned CTMCs, while General Markov Generators owns their operator semantics.

    6. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo¤

    HMC augments position \(x\) with momentum \(p\). This chapter uses one convention throughout:

    \[ \begin{aligned} p&\sim\mathcal N(0,M),\\ H(x,p)&=E(x)+\frac12p^\top M^{-1}p, \end{aligned} \]

    where \(M\) is the mass matrix and momentum covariance. Some software calls \(M^{-1}\) the metric or inverse mass matrix; never transfer a tuned matrix between implementations without checking that convention.

    The joint density is proportional to \(e^{-H(x,p)}\), so marginalizing momentum returns \(\pi(x)\propto e^{-E(x)}\). Hamilton's equations are

    \[ \begin{aligned} \dot x&=M^{-1}p,\\ \dot p&=-\nabla E(x). \end{aligned} \]

    Exact Hamiltonian flow is volume preserving, reversible after a momentum flip, and conserves \(H\). These properties motivate a distant proposal, but the exact flow is rarely available.

    Leapfrog supplies a reversible numerical proposal¤

    For step size \(\epsilon\), one leapfrog step is

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_{n+1/2}&=p_n-\frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla E(x_n),\\ x_{n+1}&=x_n+\epsilon M^{-1}p_{n+1/2},\\ p_{n+1}&=p_{n+1/2}-\frac{\epsilon}{2}\nabla E(x_{n+1}). \end{aligned} \]

    After \(L\) steps, negate momentum and accept the proposal with

    \[ \alpha=\min\{1,\exp[-H(x',p')+H(x,p)]\}. \]

    Leapfrog is volume preserving and reversible, which makes this ordinary MH correction valid. A finite integrator almost always has nonzero energy error; that does not mean the transition automatically diverged or must be rejected. Small error is accepted with high probability. A divergence is an implementation diagnostic for unusually large or unstable numerical error, often caused by a step size that is too large for the local curvature.

    A complete fixed-trajectory transition therefore:

    1. draws or refreshes momentum using the declared refresh scheme;
    2. integrates \(L\) leapfrog steps;
    3. applies the MH correction; and
    4. discards momentum if only positions are retained.

    Full independent momentum refresh is standard, but it is not the only valid choice; generalized HMC uses partial refresh with a compatible augmented-state kernel.

    Geometry controls step size and trajectory length¤

    For a Gaussian target \(x\sim\mathcal N(0,\Sigma)\),

    \[ \ddot x=-M^{-1}\Sigma^{-1}x. \]

    The eigenvalues of \(M^{-1}\Sigma^{-1}\) determine the oscillation frequencies. One scalar step size must resolve the fastest direction, while useful movement along slow directions may require a long trajectory. Adapting the coordinate geometry can make those frequencies more similar. Under the convention in this chapter, choosing \(M\) proportional to \(\Sigma^{-1}\) equalizes this ideal Gaussian system; an implementation that adapts an “inverse mass matrix” may store the corresponding covariance-like object instead.

    The lesson is not that one matrix solves nonlinear geometry. Curvature can vary across the state space, as in hierarchical funnels, and separated modes remain separated under local deterministic dynamics. Position-dependent Riemannian HMC introduces a metric \(G(x)\), a log-determinant term, and generalized Hamiltonian equations. Simply inserting a Hessian into the Euclidean update is not RMHMC: a Hessian can be indefinite, and omitting the position-dependent terms changes the target.

    NUTS adapts path length, not all geometry¤

    The No-U-Turn Sampler builds a trajectory and stops expansion when an implementation-specific U-turn condition indicates that continuing would retrace the path. Candidate selection and stopping are constructed together to preserve the target. NUTS removes a fixed user-chosen \(L\); it still needs a step size, a mass-matrix convention, a maximum tree depth, an adaptation protocol, and diagnostics.

    During warmup, dual averaging commonly tunes the step size and windowed estimation tunes the mass matrix. Those iterations belong to an adaptive phase. During retained sampling, the tuned quantities are normally frozen. Tiny-step trajectories can accept almost always while wasting gradient evaluations, so compare ESS and MCSE per gradient evaluation and wall-clock, not acceptance per iteration.

    Constraints require a measure-correct transformation¤

    If \(x=T(u)\) maps unconstrained \(u\in\mathbb R^d\) to a constrained parameter, HMC in \(u\) coordinates must target

    \[ \log\pi_U(u) =\log\pi_X(T(u))+\log|\det J_T(u)|. \]

    The Jacobian is part of the transformed density, not an optional post-processing correction. Simplexes, positive scales, ordered vectors, and correlation matrices each need an appropriate bijection and base measure.

    7. Warmup, Geometry, and MCMC Diagnostics¤

    Diagnostics test particular failure modes; none proves convergence. Use several chains, scientifically relevant observables, and a cost denominator.

    Start chains so disagreement can be seen¤

    Initial states should be valid and should probe meaningfully different regions without relying on impossible or numerically pathological values. Identical initialization can hide between-chain disagreement. Overdispersion is useful only when the resulting warmup can enter the typical set; absurdly diffuse initial states can instead create overflow or waste computation.

    After warmup, inspect rank plots, traces of relevant functions, energy or log density, mode labels, tail events, constraints, and replicated summaries. Trace plots are views of finite paths, not tests with universal pass/fail thresholds.

    Rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\) checks location and scale disagreement¤

    The modern construction first splits each chain, pools the draws of a scalar quantity, replaces them by rank-based normal scores, and compares between- and within-chain variation. A folded version applies the same idea to absolute deviations from the pooled median, making scale disagreement more visible. The reported diagnostic is the more conservative of rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\) and folded-split \(\widehat R\).

    Values near one are necessary evidence of agreement, not proof that every chain found every mode. A rule such as \(\widehat R<1.01\) is a practical screen under a specific workflow, not a theorem that the Monte Carlo error is small. Traditional unsplit, unranked \(\widehat R\) can miss heavy-tail and unequal-scale failures; do not teach it as the default merely because its algebra is shorter.

    ESS and MCSE must match the query¤

    Bulk ESS applies the multi-chain autocorrelation calculation to rank-normalized draws and measures information about central location. Tail ESS uses indicator functions for selected tail quantiles and diagnoses tail-probability accuracy. Local or quantile-specific ESS can target other features. These remain observable-specific variance equivalents.

    For an estimated posterior mean with marginal standard deviation \(s\) and an appropriate ESS,

    \[ \widehat{\operatorname{MCSE}}(\widehat\mu) \approx\frac{s}{\sqrt{N_{\mathrm{eff}}}}. \]

    Quantiles and tail probabilities need their corresponding localized MCSE, not the mean formula. Report MCSE in the units of the scientific quantity and set a tolerance before interpreting the last decimal place.

    HMC adds algorithm-specific evidence¤

    Record at least:

    • divergent transitions and where they occur;
    • maximum-tree-depth saturation or trajectory-length truncation;
    • acceptance statistic and adapted step size;
    • mass-matrix structure and warmup schedule;
    • rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\), bulk and tail ESS, and MCSE; and
    • gradient evaluations, wall-clock, precision, and chain count.

    Energy diagnostics can reveal poor momentum-to-energy movement. They do not replace target observables. Zero divergences is reassuring only at the declared threshold and precision; increasing the threshold until warnings disappear can hide bias rather than fix geometry.

    Hierarchical funnels require parameterization checks¤

    Consider the generative hierarchy

    \[ V\sim\mathcal N(0,3^2), \qquad X\mid V=v\sim\mathcal N(0,e^v). \]

    The centered coordinates \((v,x)\) contain a narrow neck for negative \(v\) and a wide mouth for positive \(v\). One global HMC step size must negotiate both. Writing

    \[ Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad X=e^{V/2}Z \]

    gives an equivalent prior generative story in \((v,z)\) coordinates. It can make weak-data geometry easier, while the centered form can be better when data strongly identify group effects. This is not a universal “noncentering wins” rule. The complete hierarchical-model discussion is in Bayesian Learning and Model Assessment.

    A diagnostic panel can agree inside the wrong region

    If every chain starts in and remains inside one metastable mode, ranks, \(\widehat R\), and ESS may all look acceptable. Add mode-sensitive initializations, observables, tempering or nonlocal transitions, and application-specific checks. Diagnostics quantify evidence from runs that occurred; they do not reveal an unknown region by magic.

    8. Stochastic-Gradient and Kinetic MCMC¤

    Large datasets make an exact log-posterior gradient expensive. For

    \[ E(\theta)=-\log p(\theta)-\sum_{n=1}^N\log p(x_n\mid\theta), \]

    a minibatch \(B\) gives the unbiased gradient estimator

    \[ \widehat g(\theta) =-\nabla\log p(\theta) -\frac{N}{|B|}\sum_{n\in B}\nabla\log p(x_n\mid\theta). \]

    SGLD inserts this estimator into the Langevin update. The stochastic-gradient noise is not generally Gaussian, constant, or equal to the thermal noise required by the target diffusion. Classical decreasing-step results use explicit regularity and schedule assumptions; constant-step implementations trade asymptotic exactness for throughput and retain gradient-noise and discretization bias. Variance-reduced estimators can reduce that bias and variance but add reference gradients, memory, or periodic full-data passes.

    Momentum produces underdamped dynamics¤

    With mass \(M\) and symmetric friction \(\Gamma\succeq0\), one convenient underdamped Langevin convention is

    \[ \begin{aligned} dX_t&=M^{-1}P_t\,dt,\\ dP_t&=-\nabla E(X_t)\,dt -\Gamma M^{-1}P_t\,dt+\sqrt{2\Gamma}\,dW_t. \end{aligned} \]

    Under regularity conditions its invariant joint density is proportional to \(\exp[-E(x)-p^\top M^{-1}p/2]\). Friction dissipates momentum while calibrated noise restores the intended temperature. Underdamped and overdamped Langevin are different processes; a second-order discretization does not universally require fewer target evaluations on every nonconvex problem.

    Naively replacing HMC gradients by minibatch gradients can inject uncontrolled energy error while also making the exact MH test expensive. SGHMC introduces friction and noise intended to compensate for stochastic-gradient noise. Practical exactness still depends on how that noise is estimated, the discretization, step size, and any omitted acceptance correction. “Uses Hamiltonian momentum” is not equivalent to exact HMC.

    Scaling choices form an error budget¤

    Table SAMP.5. Scaling choices form an error budget
    Method Full-data target correction Persistent finite-step error Main reportable cost
    MALA/HMC exact MH ratio when target and gradients are exact Monte Carlo error, numerical precision full log target and gradients per proposal
    ULA none discretization bias full gradients per step
    decreasing-step SGLD no finite-step MH correction transient and schedule-dependent error; asymptotic claim needs conditions minibatch gradients and total data passes
    constant-step SGLD none stochastic-gradient plus discretization bias minibatch gradients and sensitivity sweep
    SGHMC/kinetic stochastic-gradient methods method-specific friction/noise construction gradient-noise calibration and integrator bias minibatch gradients, momentum steps, calibration

    The same update can be used as posterior MCMC, an EBM negative-phase heuristic, or an optimizer with injected noise. Its statistical meaning comes from the target, noise calibration, correction, and limiting regime—not from the name “Langevin.”

    9. Transdimensional and Structure-Changing MCMC¤

    Mixture component counts, change points, graph structures, program traces, and other latent structures can place states in spaces of different dimension. Write the target on a disjoint union

    \[ \mathcal X=\bigcup_m\{m\}\times\mathcal X_m, \qquad \pi(m,x_m). \]

    An ordinary density ratio between \(x_m\in\mathbb R^{d_m}\) and \(x_{m'}\in\mathbb R^{d_{m'}}\) is undefined when the base measures have different dimension. Reversible-jump MCMC augments each direction with random variables so the forward and reverse transformations have matching total dimension.

    Draw \(m'\) with probability \(r(m'\mid m)\) and auxiliary \(u\sim q_{m\to m'}(u\mid x_m)\). A differentiable bijection produces

    \[ (x_{m'}',u')=T_{m\to m'}(x_m,u). \]

    To expose every measure correction without forcing one oversized expression, define four ratios:

    \[ \begin{aligned} R_{\mathrm{target}} &=\frac{\widetilde\pi(m',x_{m'}')}{\widetilde\pi(m,x_m)},\\ R_{\mathrm{move}} &=\frac{r(m\mid m')}{r(m'\mid m)},\\ R_{\mathrm{aux}} &=\frac{q_{m'\to m}(u'\mid x_{m'}')} {q_{m\to m'}(u\mid x_m)},\\ J &=\left|\det\frac{\partial(x_{m'}',u')} {\partial(x_m,u)}\right|. \end{aligned} \]

    The acceptance probability is then

    \[ \alpha=\min\!\left\{1, R_{\mathrm{target}}R_{\mathrm{move}}R_{\mathrm{aux}}J \right\}. \]

    Every factor has a role: target mass, model-move probability, auxiliary proposal, and change of variables. Birth/death and split/merge proposals are special cases. A hand-designed inverse is not enough; dimensions, proposal probabilities, and the Jacobian must agree in both directions.

    Collapsing continuous parameters can turn some structure moves into ordinary discrete MCMC. When analytic integration is unavailable, learned transport maps can make the between-model proposal better matched, but the reversible jump correction remains the source of target invariance. Diagnose model-index occupancy, cross-model round trips, within-model ESS, and sensitivity to move types—not just the aggregate acceptance rate.

    Transdimensional MCMC answers a posterior-over-structures query. It is not the same object as a GFlowNet that amortizes a reward-proportional constructive sampler, an autoregressive structure generator, or a variational posterior, although those models can supply proposals. Continue to Bayesian nonparametrics, random graphs, and GFlowNets for those distinct constructions.

    Gradient and Hamiltonian retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: Langevin, HMC, and diagnostics
    1. What bias does ULA retain that MALA corrects under ordinary conditions?
    2. Why must a preconditioner alter both Langevin drift and noise?
    3. Which numerical property makes leapfrog suitable for HMC proposals?
    4. What different failures do \(\widehat R\), ESS/MCSE, and HMC divergences target?

    Solutions. (1) Finite-step discretization bias in the invariant law. (2) Fluctuation and drift must remain matched to preserve the desired diffusion/target geometry. (3) It is reversible and volume preserving, with controlled Hamiltonian error that can be Metropolis corrected. (4) \(\widehat R\) detects between/within-chain disagreement, ESS/MCSE quantify query-specific autocorrelation error, and divergences expose unstable numerical exploration of difficult geometry.

    Gradient and Hamiltonian synthesis¤

    Gradient samplers couple target geometry to both drift and randomness. Metropolis correction separates proposal discretization from target invariance; HMC uses reversible volume-preserving dynamics to propose distant states. Warmup and diagnostics are part of the inference contract, and specialized stochastic- gradient or transdimensional variants require additional validity arguments.
