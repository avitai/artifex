    5. Augmented, Discrete, Manifold, and Function-Space Paths¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the continuous Euclidean constructions of Chapters 1–4. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Audit lifted paths: distinguish an augmented-space law and field from their projected observed-space distribution and multiplicity.
    2. O2 — Derive discrete transport: use a valid CTMC generator, master equation, and finite jump simulation rather than a Euclidean derivative on one-hot vectors.
    3. O3 — Preserve manifold geometry: state the reference volume, tangent field, intrinsic divergence or score, boundary, and geometry-compatible integrator.
    4. O4 — Specify function-space consistency: distinguish a finite-grid vector model from a coherent stochastic-process law under restriction and refinement.
    5. O5 — Separate exact paths from solvers: identify positivity, feasibility, constraint, mesh, projection, and stochastic-simulation errors.
    6. O6 — Transfer across state spaces: build a complete six-object contract for a discrete, manifold-valued, or function-valued generator.

    PFGM++ and augmented geometry¤

    PFGM++ embeds data into an augmented space and interprets generation through electric-field-like dynamics. Its augmentation dimension controls a family connecting Poisson-flow behavior with diffusion-like limits. This is a distributional/dynamical correspondence under a specific construction, not a statement that arbitrary electrostatic solvers and diffusion models are interchangeable.

    If \(\Pi(y,z)=y\) projects a lifted state onto observations, the observed marginal is \(p_t^Y=\Pi_\#p_t^{Y,Z}\). A correct lifted continuity equation does not by itself identify the projected density: integration over fibers, boundary flux, and possible multiplicities matter. Two lifted paths can have identical \(p_t^Y\) while moving differently in \(z\), or can share lifted endpoints while projecting to different intermediate observed laws.

    Discrete paths¤

    Discrete state spaces use probability-flow generators, jump rates, masking processes, or CTMCs rather than Euclidean velocities. A generator matrix \(Q_t\) evolves masses by

    \[ \partial_t p_t=p_tQ_t. \]

    Many \(Q_t\) can realize the same mass derivative. Positivity of off-diagonal rates, zero row sums, graph connectivity, and finite tau-leaping error replace Euclidean integrability constraints.

    With row-vector convention, a valid generator satisfies

    \[ Q_{ij,t}\ge0\quad(i\ne j), \qquad Q_{ii,t}=-\sum_{j\ne i}Q_{ij,t}. \]

    For two states, \(\dot p_1=p_0q_{01}-p_1q_{10}\). At \(p=(0.8,0.2)\) and \((q_{01},q_{10})=(0.5,1)\), the derivative is \((-0.2,0.2)\). A step of size \(0.1\) gives \((0.78,0.22)\) under explicit Euler, but larger steps can make masses negative even though the exact CTMC semigroup is stochastic. Exact event simulation, uniformization, tau leaping, and Euler mass integration are different finite algorithms.

    Discrete Flow Matching constructs general discrete probability paths and samples them through learned posterior parameterizations. Its CTMC semantics and normalized transition requirements should not be replaced by an unconstrained neural vector on one-hot embeddings.

    Manifolds and symmetry¤

    On a manifold, divergence, gradient, Brownian motion, and score depend on the metric and volume measure. Ambient Gaussian corruption can leave the manifold; intrinsic diffusion can be expensive or topologically constrained. Equivariant paths must commute with symmetry actions, while coordinate charts must not introduce singular artifacts.

    For density \(p_t\) relative to Riemannian volume and tangent field \(v_t(x)\in T_x\mathcal M\), conservation is

    \[ \partial_t p_t+\operatorname{div}_{g}(p_tv_t)=0. \]

    An ambient network must be projected to the tangent space or parameterized intrinsically. A tangent Euler step in the ambient space usually leaves a curved manifold; exponential maps, retractions, constraint-preserving integrators, or projection steps have different accuracy and cost. Riemannian Flow Matching uses geodesic conditional paths and tangent fields for declared geometries.

    Accepted ICML 2026 manifold flow-matching theory relates estimation complexity to intrinsic structure for a specified transformer/path setting. It is Peer-reviewed research, not universal evidence that every learned flow escapes ambient dimensionality.

    Function-space paths¤

    For random functions or fields, paths should remain coherent across discretizations. Training on one mesh can define a finite vector transport without a consistent function-space limit. Noise covariance, boundary conditions, operator topology, and projective consistency must be specified.

    If \(P_T\) is the law of evaluations \((f(t_1),\ldots,f(t_n))\) on a finite query set \(T\), consistency requires that restricting \(P_T\) to any \(S\subset T\) gives \(P_S\). It also requires permutation consistency. These Kolmogorov-style conditions are necessary for a stochastic-process interpretation; low error on one fixed mesh is not enough.

    Functional Flow Matching learns function-valued transport from a Gaussian-process reference, while Operator Flow Matching uses neural-operator machinery for stochastic-process priors and finite-query inference. Their finite bases, sampled query sets, numerical ODEs, trace estimators, and posterior samplers remain approximation layers.

    Same marginals, different finite algorithms¤

    Copula Models adds a stricter path-design example: every intermediate coordinate marginal must remain uniform while the joint dependence changes. A process that only matches independent and dependent endpoints is not thereby a valid copula path.

    Even when two exact continuous dynamics share \(p_t\):

    • Euler, Heun, Runge–Kutta, exponential, and stochastic solvers have different error;
    • learned vector/score errors are amplified differently;
    • stochastic dynamics can mix across modes after intermediate perturbation;
    • deterministic dynamics preserve a one-to-one coupling under uniqueness;
    • adaptive step counts and wall time differ;
    • guidance can break the original marginal equivalence.

    Therefore benchmark comparison belongs at matched error, NFE, tolerance, hardware, and conditioning—not solely at the continuous equation.

    Worked nonunique two-state generator¤

    At \(p=(1/2,1/2)\), demand \(\dot p_1=0.1\). The generator constraint is

    \[ \frac12q_{01}-\frac12q_{10}=0.1, \quad\text{or}\quad q_{01}-q_{10}=0.2. \]

    Both \((q_{01},q_{10})=(0.2,0)\) and \((0.7,0.5)\) realize the same instantaneous mass derivative. The second process has much greater bidirectional jump activity. A marginal derivative does not identify transition rates, just as a continuity equation does not identify a unique Euclidean velocity.

    Retrieval practice: paths beyond Euclidean vectors
    1. Why does adding an auxiliary dimension require a projection-law audit?
    2. What replaces an unconstrained Euclidean velocity on a manifold?
    3. Why is a categorical transition generator not obtained by merely writing a Euclidean score on one-hot vectors?
    4. What consistency condition should a function-space generator satisfy across query grids?

    Solutions. (1) Different lifted paths can project to different densities or multiplicities on the observed space. (2) A tangent vector field with an intrinsic measure, connection/retraction, and compatible numerical update. (3) Discrete evolution requires normalized jump rates or transition kernels on the actual state graph. (4) Overlapping finite-dimensional marginals should agree under restriction or refinement, subject to the declared approximation.

    Misconception checks: generalized state spaces¤

    • “One-hot vectors make categorical data Euclidean.” The embedding permits arithmetic; it does not make off-simplex velocities valid jump dynamics.
    • “Tangent velocity guarantees a manifold-preserving solver.” The exact flow may stay on the manifold while a finite ambient step leaves it.
    • “Neural operator means function-space law.” Coherent finite-dimensional distributions, boundary conditions, and refinement behavior still require evidence.
    • “An auxiliary dimension is invisible.” Its law, boundary, and projection can alter observed density and solver behavior.

    Independent practice and assessment map: generalized state spaces¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: two-state mass flow. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For \(p=(0.8,0.2)\) and rates \((q_{01},q_{10})=(0.5,1)\), predict which state gains mass. Compute \(\dot p\) and one Euler step with \(h=0.1\).
    2. P1.2 — Construct nonunique generators. Type: design · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. At \(p=(1/2,1/2)\), find two additional nonnegative rate pairs realizing \(\dot p_1=0.1\). Compare their total expected jump activity \(p_0q_{01}+p_1q_{10}\).
    3. P1.3 — Diagnose an off-manifold step. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. On the unit circle at \(x=(1,0)\) use tangent velocity \(v=(0,1)\). Compute the ambient Euler point for step \(h\), its norm, and the normalized-projection point. State which is the exact exponential-map step.
    4. P1.4 — Audit an augmented projection. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Let lifted states be \((Y_t,Z_t)\) and observations be \(Y_t\). Construct two lifted processes with identical \(Y_t\) law but different \(Z_t\) trajectories, and state which evaluation cannot distinguish them.
    5. P1.5 — Test restriction consistency. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. A zero-mean Gaussian process is evaluated on \(T=(t_1,t_2,t_3)\) with covariance \(K_T\). State the mean and covariance required on \(S=(t_1,t_3)\) and explain how independently trained incompatible \(K_S\) refutes a single process law.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a molecular path. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 22 min. Design a generator jointly evolving atom types and three-dimensional coordinates modulo rigid motion. Specify discrete rates, geometric field, symmetry, support, coupling, path, solver, and separate validity/refinement tests.

    P1.1 The signed derivative sums to zero and the Euler mass remains normalized and nonnegative.
    P1.2 Both pairs satisfy the same mass derivative but have different jump activity.
    P1.3 Tangency, ambient drift, projection, and exact geodesic update are distinguished.
    P1.4 Projected marginals agree while latent path laws differ; an observed-only metric is identified.
    P1.5 The principal submatrix and restricted mean are required by one coherent Gaussian process.
    P1.6 Discrete, geometric, symmetry, numerical, and evaluation contracts are all explicit.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. State one gains mass because incoming current is \(0.8(0.5)=0.4\) and outgoing current is \(0.2(1)=0.2\). Thus \(\dot p=(-0.2,0.2)\) and Euler gives \((0.8,0.2)+0.1(-0.2,0.2)=(0.78,0.22)\). Check. The derivative sums to zero and the update sums to one.
    2. P1.2. Any pair \((0.2+c,c)\) with \(c\ge0\) works. For example \((1.2,1)\) and \((2.2,2)\) both give derivative \(0.1\), while activities are \(1.1\) and \(2.1\) respectively; the worked pair \((0.2,0)\) has activity \(0.1\). Check. Equal net flow does not imply equal jump counts or transition law.
    3. P1.3. Ambient Euler gives \((1,h)\) with norm \(\sqrt{1+h^2}>1\). Normalized projection gives \((1,h)/\sqrt{1+h^2}\). The exact unit-speed exponential-map update is \((\cos h,\sin h)\); projection agrees only to a finite order, not exactly for general \(h\). Check. The field is tangent even though Euler leaves the manifold.
    4. P1.4. Choose the same observed process \(Y_t\) in both lifts, but set \(Z_t=0\) in one and \(Z_t=t\) or an independent Brownian path in the other. Projection produces identical \(Y_t\) marginals at every time, while lifted trajectories and costs differ. Any metric using only generated observations \(Y\) cannot distinguish them. Check. A lifted-energy or auxiliary-path diagnostic can.
    5. P1.5. The restricted law must be Gaussian with the coordinates-one-and-three mean, here zero, and covariance \(K_S=K_T[\{1,3\},\{1,3\}]\). If a separately trained two-point model gives another covariance, the three-point and two-point laws disagree under marginalization and cannot both be restrictions of one process. Check. Positive semidefiniteness alone does not ensure cross-grid compatibility.
    6. P1.6. Use counting-measure CTMC rates for atom types and a permutation- and \(SE(3)\)-equivariant coordinate field on collision-free configurations or a quotient/centered representation. Declare endpoint coupling and joint conditional path, constrain rate rows and coordinate support, then use event/tau-leap updates interleaved with an equivariant ODE/SDE integrator. Test chemical validity and rate normalization separately from rigid-motion equivariance, collision frequency, solver-step convergence, and representation/refinement stability. Rubric. Full credit states how simultaneous versus split updates affect the finite algorithm.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Probability-path ideas extend beyond Euclidean continuous data, but each state space changes the admissible generator and numerical semantics. Chapter 6 consolidates the relationship labels, evaluation protocol, and peer-reviewed research.
