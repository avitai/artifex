    5. Discrete, Mixed, and Neural Copulas¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–4, latent-variable models, normalizing flows, diffusion, and density-ratio estimation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Compute discontinuous likelihoods: obtain discrete, ordinal, censored, and mixed observation probabilities from CDF differences and derivatives.
    2. O2 — State the identifiability boundary: distinguish an observed joint PMF from the many whole-cube copula extensions that can induce it.
    3. O3 — Audit latent randomization: compare randomized distributional transforms, mid-ranks, jitter, and learned dequantization by the observed law they imply.
    4. O4 — Classify neural validity mechanisms: determine whether constraints, re-uniformization, density correction, or marginal-preserving paths establish exact or approximate copula margins.
    5. O5 — Separate computational interfaces: distinguish normalized density, pointwise ratio, direct IID sample, MCMC sample, conditional query, and observed-data likelihood access.
    6. O6 — Transfer and connect constructions: compose a mixed-data or stepwise discrete generator without conflating a transition coupling, latent copula, and static observed-data copula.

    Discrete likelihood uses rectangle probabilities¤

    For discrete bivariate \(X,Y\) with CDFs \(F_X,F_Y\), the probability of the cell \((x,y)\) is

    \[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} p(x,y)={}&C(F_X(x),F_Y(y)) -C(F_X(x^-),F_Y(y))\\ &-C(F_X(x),F_Y(y^-)) +C(F_X(x^-),F_Y(y^-)). \end{aligned} } \]

    This is a CDF rectangle volume, not a continuous copula density evaluated at ranks. In \(d\) dimensions the inclusion–exclusion sum has up to \(2^d\) corners, motivating latent-variable, composite-likelihood, or structured approximations.

    When marginals are discrete, different whole-cube copulas can agree at every marginal range-grid point and induce the same observed PMF. A learned latent copula can still be useful for simulation or interpretation, but those off-grid features are not identified from the observed joint table without extra assumptions.

    Complete worked example: Bernoulli dependence¤

    Let \(X,Y\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) marginally and write \(q=\Pr(X=1,Y=1)\). The joint table is

    Table COP.4. Complete worked example: Bernoulli dependence
    \(Y=0\) \(Y=1\)
    \(X=0\) \(q\) \(1/2-q\)
    \(X=1\) \(1/2-q\) \(q\)

    with \(0\le q\le1/2\). For the \((1,1)\) cell,

    \[ q =1-C(1/2,1)-C(1,1/2)+C(1/2,1/2) =C(1/2,1/2). \]

    Thus the table identifies \(C(1/2,1/2)=q\), plus boundary values, but not the shape of \(C\) between those points. At \(q=1/4\) the variables are independent; at \(q=1/2\) they agree perfectly. Infinitely many valid continuous-cube extensions can share either table.

    Distributional transforms and latent jitter¤

    The randomized transform from Chapter 1 produces uniform marginals even for atoms. It can support a continuous latent model, but the random variables \(V_j\) and their dependence convention matter. Independent jitter is a declared augmentation; deterministic mid-ranks are not exactly uniform; learned dequantization introduces another conditional distribution. Evaluation must return to the observed discrete likelihood or observed-scale queries rather than validating only the augmented latent samples.

    For mixed data, each coordinate may use a different observation model:

    • continuous exact transform;
    • interval transform for counts or categories;
    • censoring interval for limits of detection;
    • point mass plus continuous tail for zero-inflated variables; or
    • ordinal thresholds on a latent continuous variable.

    A single “rank everything” preprocessing step can erase these distinctions.

    The likelihood operator follows the observation type. A continuous copula density evaluated at a convenient rank is not a universal substitute for the rectangle, interval, or mixed-measure contribution.

    Four neural validity strategies¤

    Neural models enter copula modeling through different contracts.

    1. Structured valid families¤

    A network can output parameters of a known valid family: a positive-definite Gaussian correlation matrix, compatible vine pair parameters, or a \(d\)-monotone Archimedean generator. Validity follows only if the parameterization enforces the family's constraints. Deep Archimedean Copulas is an example in which the architecture targets generator structure rather than treating an arbitrary scalar network as a copula CDF.

    Generative Archimedean constructions can parameterize the latent mixing distribution whose Laplace transform is the generator. This gives a route to valid sampling and avoids treating an unconstrained scalar network as a generator.

    2. Implicit generation followed by marginal uniformization¤

    An implicit generator first produces \(Y\in\mathbb R^d\) with the desired dependence, then applies its own marginal CDFs:

    \[ U_j=G_j(Y_j). \]

    With continuous exact \(G_j\), the resulting \(U_j\) are uniform and monotone marginal transforms preserve the copula of \(Y\). In practice, empirical or learned \(G_j\) introduce calibration error. This route can sample but need not provide a tractable density.

    Vine Copula Autoencoders make a different composition: an autoencoder supplies a latent representation, a vine models the aggregate latent law, and the decoder maps samples back to data space. The vine is a copula model for the chosen latent coordinates; unless the decoder is a coordinatewise monotone bijection, it is not the observed-space copula of the decoded image or vector.

    3. Density or density-ratio models¤

    A normalized flow can parameterize a density on the cube or a Gaussianized scale. A classifier can estimate the ratio \(c=h/(1-h)\). Neither route automatically gives exact uniform marginals. One must impose a marginal-preserving architecture, re-uniformize with tracked Jacobians, or measure and correct marginal error. MCMC sampling from an unnormalized ratio has different cost and diagnostics from direct flow sampling.

    4. Marginal-preserving probability paths¤

    Diffusion- and flow-based copulas can construct forward processes that remove dependence while preserving each marginal distribution at every time, then learn the reverse density or sampling dynamics. The crucial theorem is marginal preservation under the stated process, not the use of a diffusion vocabulary. A standard Euclidean Gaussian noising process on \(U\) leaves the cube and destroys uniform marginals, so it is not a copula path without additional construction.

    Transition couplings are not automatically observation copulas¤

    Discrete Copula Diffusion uses an additional dependency model to couple categorical predictions inside a discrete denoising step. This is an important connection to iterative generation: componentwise reverse predictions can miss same-step dependence. The word copula still needs a layer label. A coupling among \(p(x_{t-1,j}\mid x_t)\) outputs is a transition-level object; it is not automatically the static Sklar copula of clean observations, and its validity is judged together with the reverse-kernel and terminal-law construction.

    Conditional and covariate-dependent copulas¤

    A conditional copula may vary with covariates \(Z=z\):

    \[ F_{X\mid Z=z}(x) =C_z\!\left(F_{1\mid z}(x_1),\ldots,F_{d\mid z}(x_d)\right). \]

    A network can output \(C_z\) parameters or a conditional sampler. It must maintain copula validity for every relevant \(z\), not merely on average. Apparent changing dependence can also be caused by misspecified conditional marginals. Extrapolating \(C_z\) outside the covariate support is a model assumption, not a rank-invariance theorem.

    Independent practice and assessment map: mixed and neural copulas¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Compute a discrete law. Type: derive · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. In the half-Bernoulli example take \(q=0.4\). Write all four cell probabilities, compute the cross-product odds ratio, and state the copula range-grid value identified by the table.
    2. P1.2 — Evaluate a rectangle contribution. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 8 min. A discrete cell has the four copula-CDF corner values \(0.70\), \(0.40\), \(0.45\), and \(0.25\) in upper, lower-\(x\), lower-\(y\), and lower-both order. Compute its probability and identify the sign pattern.
    3. P1.3 — Predict before computing: mid-rank failure. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/4)\), replace randomized PIT values by the midpoint of each jump interval. List the two resulting values and masses, then predict whether the transform is uniform.
    4. P1.4 — Audit four neural claims. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 15 min. For each claim—positive-definite Gaussian correlation output, unconstrained flow on the cube, implicit generator followed by exact marginal CDFs, and a provably marginal-preserving path—state what establishes or fails to establish copula validity, density access, and direct sampling.
    5. P1.5 — Connect to discrete diffusion. Type: compare · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. A discrete denoiser couples the coordinates of \(x_{t-1}\) conditional on \(x_t\) with a learned copula. Explain which dependence object this specifies and why it is not automatically the static copula of \(x_0\).
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design a conditional model for a zero-inflated count, an ordinal severity, and a continuous duration given covariates. Specify marginal observation models, latent transforms, dependence validity, observed likelihood or approximation, sampling interface, and diagnostics.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The cells are \(p_{00}=p_{11}=0.4\) and \(p_{01}=p_{10}=0.1\), which sum to one. The odds ratio is \((0.4\cdot0.4)/(0.1\cdot0.1)=16\). The table identifies \(C(1/2,1/2)=0.4\) plus the required boundary/range-grid values, not a unique whole-cube shape. Check. A strong observed association does not resolve the off-grid extension.
    2. P1.2. Inclusion–exclusion gives \(0.70-0.40-0.45+0.25=0.10\). The sign pattern is upper minus each singly lowered corner plus the doubly lowered corner. Check. A negative result would expose inconsistent supplied CDF values or numerical approximation, because a cell mass cannot be negative.
    3. P1.3. The midpoints are \(0.375\) with probability \(0.75\) and \(0.875\) with probability \(0.25\). This two-point distribution is not continuous uniform. Check. Randomized PIT fills the two intervals; deterministic mid-ranks collapse each interval to one point.
    4. P1.4. A positive-definite correlation matrix followed by Gaussian-CDF transforms defines a valid Gaussian copula with tractable density and direct sampling. An unconstrained cube flow may have a normalized density and direct sampler but need not have uniform marginals. An implicit generator followed by its exact continuous marginal CDFs has valid copula samples but generally no tractable density. A proved marginal-preserving path defines copulas along the exact process; density versus direct-sampling access depends on the specific forward/reverse construction and finite solver. Rubric. Full credit does not infer all three interfaces from any one of them.
    5. P1.5. The learned object couples coordinate predictions in the conditional reverse kernel \(p(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\). The clean terminal law is produced by composing every reverse transition with the terminal initialization, so its dependence need not equal any single transition copula. Its static Sklar copula additionally depends on the final coordinate marginals and observation type. Check. “Copula” labels the layer of dependence, not one interchangeable object across time.
    6. P1.6. One valid design uses a hurdle or zero-inflated count CDF, ordered thresholds for severity, and a continuous conditional duration CDF, all conditioned on covariates. Randomized interval transforms are explicit for the count and ordinal coordinates; the continuous coordinate uses an ordinary PIT. A constrained vine or marginal-preserving conditional sampler supplies dependence for each covariate regime. Training uses exact rectangle/derivative combinations where feasible or a named composite/latent approximation. Sampling draws a valid latent uniform vector and applies the three inverse observation maps. Diagnostics cover marginal calibration, discrete cell/ordinal probabilities, copula-scale dependence, conditional coverage, and observed-scale decisions. Rubric. Full credit declares the latent augmentation and never validates the system only on jittered uniforms.

    Chapter 5 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Evaluating a continuous density at discrete ranks is not the discrete cell likelihood.
    • Randomized PIT makes augmented uniforms; it does not identify a unique observed-data copula.
    • A normalizing flow on \([0,1]^d\) need not have uniform output marginals.
    • Low marginal KS error does not prove correct joint dependence.
    • Direct density evaluation, exact conditional queries, and fast IID sampling are distinct capabilities.
    • Neural flexibility does not create tail observations absent from the dataset.
    Retrieval questions
    1. Why does a bivariate discrete likelihood use four copula-CDF evaluations?
    2. What does the Bernoulli table identify about \(C\)?
    3. How can an implicit generator be converted into a copula sampler?
    4. Why is ordinary Gaussian diffusion on the cube not automatically a copula diffusion?
    5. What must a covariate-dependent copula satisfy for each \(z\)?

    Solutions. (1) The cell is a rectangle whose probability is obtained by inclusion–exclusion over its four corners. (2) Values on the product of marginal ranges, such as \(C(1/2,1/2)\), not a unique whole-cube extension. (3) Apply each generated coordinate's continuous marginal CDF, exactly or with an explicitly estimated approximation. (4) It generally leaves the cube and does not preserve uniform coordinate marginals. (5) The copula boundary, volume, and uniform-marginal conditions, as well as the declared conditional observation model.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Discrete and mixed observations turn copula densities into rectangle probabilities and whole-cube uniqueness into a latent modeling choice. Neural constructions are best classified by how they prove or approximate uniform marginals, whether they expose a density, and how they sample. Architecture names do not replace the copula validity proof.

    Why Chapter 6 follows. The separation between marginals and dependence must also structure evaluation; otherwise a model can appear accurate by trading one kind of error for another.
