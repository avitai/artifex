    Cumulative Worked Example: A Bernoulli Mixture from Generation to EM¤

    Classical-model capstone learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes all preceding classical chapters. By the end, you should be able to generate and score complete and observed Bernoulli-mixture records, infer responsibilities, perform an exact EM update, sample from the updated model, identify its label and data-identifiability limits, and use the diagnostic checklist to connect mixture failures to factor, graph, state-space, and dependency-structure models.

    This finite model carries one set of numbers through the operations introduced in Section 2. It is deliberately small enough to enumerate—and deliberately non-identifiable enough to show why a latent explanation is not automatically learned from observations.

    Let \(Z\in\{1,2\}\) choose one of two Bernoulli components:

    \[ p(Z=1)=p(Z=2)=\frac12, \qquad p(X=1\mid Z=1)=\mu_1=0.2, \qquad p(X=1\mid Z=2)=\mu_2=0.8. \]

    1. Generate and Score a Complete Pair¤

    Generation first samples \(Z\), then samples \(X\) from the selected component. For example,

    \[ p(X=1,Z=2)=p(Z=2)p(X=1\mid Z=2)=0.5(0.8)=0.4. \]

    The four joint masses are

    Table CLASS.11. 1. Generate and Score a Complete Pair
    \(X=0\) \(X=1\)
    \(Z=1\) \(0.4\) \(0.1\)
    \(Z=2\) \(0.1\) \(0.4\)

    They sum to one, so they can be checked without numerical integration.

    2. Marginalize and Infer Responsibilities¤

    Summing down each column gives

    \[ p(X=0)=p(X=1)=0.5. \]

    Conditioning reverses the generative direction. For an arbitrary observation \(x_n\), the responsibility of component 2 is

    \[ r_{n2}= \frac{0.5\,\mu_2^{x_n}(1-\mu_2)^{1-x_n}} {0.5\,\mu_1^{x_n}(1-\mu_1)^{1-x_n} +0.5\,\mu_2^{x_n}(1-\mu_2)^{1-x_n}}. \]

    Thus

    \[ p(Z=2\mid X=1)=0.8, \qquad p(Z=2\mid X=0)=0.2. \]

    These are posterior probabilities under the current parameters, not observed labels.

    3. Evaluate the Observed-Data Likelihood¤

    For the dataset \((x_1,x_2,x_3)=(1,1,0)\), the initial observed-data likelihood is

    \[ p(x_{1:3})=(0.5)^3=\frac18, \qquad \log p(x_{1:3})=-3\log2\approx-2.07944. \]

    Multiplying the most likely complete-data assignments would answer a different question; the observed likelihood sums over both assignments for every item.

    4. Perform One Exact E-Step and M-Step¤

    For the two ones, the component-two responsibilities are \(r_{12}=r_{22}=0.8\); for the zero, \(r_{32}=0.2\). The effective counts are therefore \(N_2=1.8\) and \(N_1=1.2\). The M-step gives

    \[ \pi_2'=\frac{1.8}{3}=0.6, \qquad \mu_2'=\frac{0.8+0.8}{1.8}=0.888\ldots, \]

    while

    \[ \pi_1'=0.4, \qquad \mu_1'=\frac{0.2+0.2}{1.2}=\frac13. \]

    Soft assignments let every observation contribute to each component. The updated marginal success probability is

    \[ p_{\theta'}(X=1) =0.4\left(\frac13\right)+0.6\left(\frac89\right) =\frac23. \]

    Consequently,

    \[ p_{\theta'}(x_{1:3}) =\left(\frac23\right)^2\left(\frac13\right) =\frac4{27}, \qquad \log p_{\theta'}(x_{1:3})\approx-1.90954. \]

    The likelihood rose from \(1/8\) to \(4/27\), exactly as the EM lower-bound argument predicts. This is one monotone update, not a proof of a global optimum.

    5. Sample from the Updated Model¤

    To draw a new observation, sample \(Z=2\) with probability \(0.6\) (otherwise \(Z=1\)), then sample

    \[ X\mid Z=2\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(8/9), \qquad X\mid Z=1\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/3). \]

    Marginally, the new draw is Bernoulli with success probability \(2/3\). Sampling the component exposes a latent explanation; sampling only \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(2/3)\) produces the same observed law.

    6. Diagnose What the Data Cannot Identify¤

    A mixture of univariate Bernoulli distributions is itself Bernoulli:

    \[ p(X=1)=\pi_1\mu_1+\pi_2\mu_2. \]

    Infinitely many triples \((\pi_2,\mu_1,\mu_2)\) yield \(2/3\), so observations of one binary coordinate cannot identify the two components. The responsibilities are internally consistent with the fitted parameters but are not uniquely determined by the observed law. Multiple binary features with conditional structure, repeated measurements, informative priors, or other assumptions can make a mixture more informative, yet label permutation still remains.

    With only three points, even the identifiable observed Bernoulli rate is uncertain. The purpose of the example is to expose generation, joint scoring, marginalization, posterior responsibilities, EM learning, and sampling—not to claim a reliable population decomposition.

    Failure and Diagnostic Checklist¤

    • Mixtures: inspect tiny component weights, covariance eigenvalues, label stability, and multiple initializations.
    • Factor and matrix models: inspect residual covariance, held-out likelihood, posterior uncertainty, rank sensitivity, and invariance under rotations or rescalings.
    • ICA/source separation: test held-out likelihood and residual dependence, repeat whitening and initialization, report the equivalence class, and validate sources against external information.
    • Sparse dictionaries: normalize columns, vary sparsity and width, compare code stability across runs, and separate MAP reconstruction from marginal predictive evidence.
    • HMMs/state-space models: verify that emissions are normalized in the declared direction, compare filtered and smoothed beliefs, inspect transition entropy and dwell-time fit, test stability, and evaluate long-rollout calibration.
    • State estimators: declare the information set, posterior representation, approximation family, and whether future observations entered training or evaluation.
    • Undirected models: assess chain mixing and partition-function estimators rather than relying on reconstruction alone.
    • Learned dependency graphs: declare the graph object and assumptions; vary regularization, resample data, report edge stability or posterior uncertainty, test held-out joint queries, and examine latent-variable sensitivity.
    • Topic models: report held-out likelihood and stability/coherence under multiple seeds; do not name topics as if labels were identified.
    • Gaussian processes: check kernel assumptions, numerical conditioning, posterior calibration, and extrapolation behavior.
    • Graphical inference: record whether results are exact, variational, loopy-message, or Monte Carlo approximations.

    Misconception Checks¤

    A graphical factorization is not automatically causal

    A DAG can encode conditional independence without making intervention or counterfactual claims. Causal semantics require structural assumptions and an intervention model.

    EM is not generic gradient descent

    EM alternates an exact or approximate posterior expectation with parameter maximization of a lower bound. Generalized EM may only improve the M-step objective, but the latent-variable decomposition remains essential.

    A state score is not automatically an emission density

    A discriminative score can rank states for one observation without defining a normalized law over observations. Generation and sequence likelihood require the latter.

    An exact E-step is not Bayesian parameter inference

    Standard mixture EM computes exact assignment responsibilities conditional on current point parameters and then optimizes those parameters. It does not integrate parameter uncertainty, guarantee a global optimum, or identify a unique latent interpretation.

    A named latent component is not identified

    Mixture labels can permute, topic meanings can rotate or split, and latent factors can be observationally equivalent.

    Non-Gaussian does not mean semantically identified

    Classical ICA can reduce linear rotational ambiguity under its source and mixing assumptions. It still permits permutation and scaling, and it supplies no domain meaning for an axis. Nonlinear models require stronger assumptions still.

    Local linearity does not restore exact global inference

    A switching or nonlinear model may have tractable local conditionals while its path-marginal posterior is an exponentially growing mixture or a non-Gaussian law.

    Temporal precedence is not an intervention

    A state-space graph orders variables in time. Counterfactual claims additionally require intervention semantics, identification assumptions, and an appropriate comparison distribution.

    An estimated edge does not have one universal meaning

    A nonzero Gaussian precision entry, a Bayesian-network parent, a causal arrow, a sampled random-graph edge, and a learned GNN connection answer different questions. Shared adjacency notation does not transfer their semantics or guarantees.

    Sparsity does not remove hidden-variable bias

    A sparse estimator can select a compact observed graph even when latent common variables induced the observed dependencies. Direct-interaction language requires a latent-variable argument, not merely a sparse picture.

    Primary Reading Trail¤

    • Wainwright and Jordan, Graphical Models, Exponential Families, and Variational Inference — base measures, minimal and overcomplete representations, mean-parameter geometry, maximum entropy, and moment matching under explicit assumptions.
    • Geyer, Likelihood Inference in Exponential Families and Directions of Recession — finite-MLE failure, boundary completions, and directions in which natural parameters diverge.
    • Dempster, Laird, and Rubin, Maximum Likelihood from Incomplete Data via the EM Algorithm — the general incomplete-data likelihood and EM construction.
    • Andrews and Mallows, Scale Mixtures of Normal Distributions — exact representability conditions for univariate normal scale mixtures.
    • Tipping and Bishop, Probabilistic Principal Component Analysis — probabilistic linear factors, likelihood, posterior coordinates, and EM.
    • Ghahramani and Hinton, The EM Algorithm for Mixtures of Factor Analyzers — local low-rank Gaussian components and exact EM.
    • Bach and Jordan, A Probabilistic Interpretation of Canonical Correlation Analysis — the shared Gaussian latent model and its relationship to canonical subspaces.
    • Collins, Dasgupta, and Schapire, A Generalization of Principal Component Analysis to the Exponential Family — point-estimated factors and observation-matched Bregman objectives.
    • Cemgil, Bayesian Inference for Nonnegative Matrix Factorisation Models — a Gamma–Poisson generative interpretation and Bayesian inference boundary for NMF.
    • Lawrence, Probabilistic Non-linear Principal Component Analysis with Gaussian Process Latent Variable Models — nonlinear GP decoders with latent inputs and the inference boundary of the original GP-LVM.
    • Comon, Independent Component Analysis, A New Concept? — linear ICA, independence contrasts, and its classical identification equivalence.
    • Mairal et al., Online Learning for Matrix Factorization and Sparse Coding — scalable dictionary learning with explicit constraint and optimization choices.
    • Hyvärinen, Sasaki, and Turner, Nonlinear ICA Using Auxiliary Variables and Generalized Contrastive Learning — an assumption-explicit auxiliary-variable route to nonlinear identification.
    • Khemakhem et al., Variational Autoencoders and Nonlinear ICA — conditional latent priors and the assumptions under which nonlinear latent identification becomes possible.
    • Blei, Ng, and Jordan, Latent Dirichlet Allocation — document-level simplex latents and token-level topic assignments.
    • Rabiner, A Tutorial on Hidden Markov Models and Selected Applications in Speech Recognition — forward–backward, decoding, and HMM parameter learning.
    • Baum et al., A Maximization Technique Occurring in the Statistical Analysis of Probabilistic Functions of Markov Chains — the likelihood-maximization construction now called Baum–Welch.
    • Ghahramani and Jordan, Factorial Hidden Markov Models — distributed discrete state through multiple coupled-to-observation Markov chains.
    • Yu, Hidden Semi-Markov Models — explicit duration modeling and the HMM/HSMM inference boundary.
    • Adams and MacKay, Bayesian Online Changepoint Detection — online run-length inference under a hazard and segment predictive model.
    • Shumway and Stoffer, An Approach to Time Series Smoothing and Forecasting Using the EM Algorithm — parameter learning for linear state-space models through smoothed sufficient statistics.
    • Linderman et al., Bayesian Learning and Inference in Recurrent Switching Linear Dynamical Systems — discrete–continuous switching with state-dependent transitions and approximate Bayesian inference.
    • Krishnan, Shalit, and Sontag, Structured Inference Networks for Nonlinear State Space Models — neural transitions and emissions with a separately structured variational posterior.
    • Chung et al., A Recurrent Latent Variable Model for Sequential Data — deterministic recurrent history combined with per-step stochastic latents.
    • Brodersen et al., Inferring Causal Impact Using Bayesian Structural Time-Series Models — counterfactual prediction with explicit design and identification assumptions.
    • Brady, Nüsken, and Li, Efficient Learning of Deep State Space Models via Importance Smoothing — an ICML 2026 parallel particle-smoothing estimator for supervised deep state-space learning, with claims bounded to its stated algorithms and experiments.
    • Dempster, Covariance Selection — precision zeros and conditional structure in multivariate Gaussian models.
    • Friedman, Hastie, and Tibshirani, Sparse Inverse Covariance Estimation with the Graphical Lasso — penalized Gaussian precision estimation and its coordinate-descent algorithm.
    • Meinshausen and Bühlmann, High-Dimensional Graphs and Variable Selection with the Lasso — sparse neighborhood selection for Gaussian graphs under explicit high-dimensional assumptions.
    • Chandrasekaran, Parrilo, and Willsky, Latent Variable Graphical Model Selection via Convex Optimization — sparse-plus-low-rank decomposition and its identifiability conditions.
    • Chow and Liu, Approximating Discrete Probability Distributions with Dependence Trees — the KL-optimal tree-structured approximation and maximum-spanning-tree construction.
    • Chickering, Learning Equivalence Classes of Bayesian-Network Structures — score-equivalent search over observational DAG equivalence classes.
    • Bhattacharyya et al., Distribution Learning Meets Graph Structure Sampling — distribution learning for Bayes-net classes under declared chordal-skeleton and indegree restrictions.
    • Yang et al., Variational Graph Structure Learning for GNNs by Using Marginal Likelihood — a TMLR graph-learning method whose adjacency is tied to a GNN prediction model and approximate marginal-likelihood objective.
    • Kingma and Welling, Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes — nonlinear latent conditionals, amortized inference, and stochastic variational learning.

    Solutions
    1. Its first-order Markov and local-emission structure permits reusable forward messages over a finite state space.
    2. The bipartite graph makes hidden units conditionally independent given visibles and vice versa.
    3. Induced treewidth under the elimination order.
    4. Structural mechanisms, exogenous variables, and defined interventions/counterfactuals.
    5. Viterbi maximizes one jointly valid path under transition constraints; marginal modes optimize each time separately and may not form the MAP path.
    6. Its finite-dimensional predictions form coherent joint Gaussian distributions whose covariances couple function values across inputs.
    7. The Kalman recursion relies on linear-Gaussian closure. A nonlinear transition or emission generally produces a non-Gaussian predictive or posterior law that cannot be represented exactly by only a mean and covariance.
    8. Latent labels may permute, and invertible coordinate changes can transform dynamics and emissions while leaving the observation law unchanged.
    9. Generation, complete-data scoring, observed-data likelihood, posterior responsibilities, parameter learning by EM, and sampling from the updated model.
    10. Linear-Gaussian closure. A nonlinear decoder generally makes the latent integral and posterior non-Gaussian and analytically intractable.
    11. It supplies factors and a reconstruction objective but not necessarily priors, an observation likelihood, a new-object sampling law, or a declaration of which quantities are random.
    12. The inverse map changes volume. The factor \(|\det B|\) converts the product source density into a normalized density with respect to observation-space volume.
    13. Source order and every nonzero coordinate scale, including sign, remain arbitrary. The theorem's non-Gaussianity and mixing assumptions must also hold.
    14. Scaling a column by \(c\) and its code by \(1/c\) preserves reconstruction while reducing the code penalty as \(c\) grows, so the unconstrained objective has a scale degeneracy.
    15. Flexible measure-preserving transformations can produce alternative independent coordinates with the same observed law. Temporal, auxiliary, multi-view, environmental, structural, or interventional information is needed to reduce that equivalence class.
    16. Bayes' rule converts calibrated posterior state probabilities into likelihood ratios only after accounting for the classifier's state prior. The unknown common observation marginal cancels in normalized state weights, but it is still required for a normalized emission density, ancestral observation sampling, and sequence likelihood.
    17. It normalizes the smoothed expected counts \(\sum_t\xi_t(i,j)\) over destination \(j\), equivalently dividing by the expected number \(\sum_t\gamma_t(i)\) of transitions out of state \(i\).
    18. A self-transition probability imposes a memoryless geometric duration. An HSMM introduces an explicit duration variable and a state-dependent duration law, enlarging both the model and the dynamic-programming state.
    19. Several small chains can represent a combinatorial joint state, but exact messages may still range over their Cartesian product. Factorized representation does not guarantee factorized posterior inference after the chains meet at an observation.
    20. The spectral radius must satisfy \(\rho(A)<1\): every eigenvalue magnitude, not every signed eigenvalue, lies inside the unit circle. Then a finite stationary covariance solves the discrete Lyapunov equation under finite process noise.
    21. Conditioning on one mode path gives a Gaussian calculation, but marginalizing the mode path creates a Gaussian mixture whose unreduced component count grows exponentially with time.
    22. Each unobserved transition adds an independent process-noise variance \(q\); the future observation adds \(r\) on top of the current filtered uncertainty \(P_t\), giving \(P_t+hq+r\).
    23. A defined intervention and counterfactual estimand, appropriate unaffected comparison information or structural restrictions, stable mechanisms, and diagnostics or sensitivity analysis sufficient for the identification claim.
    24. In a nonsingular joint Gaussian, the relevant conditional covariance or partial correlation is determined by the precision matrix. Marginal covariance can remain nonzero because an unconditioned path through other variables transmits association.
    25. Sparse direct conditional interactions among observed variables and low-rank dependence induced by a smaller latent subsystem, subject to assumptions that make those components distinguishable.
    26. Its objective can select information-flow edges useful for one architecture and supervised task without defining normalized factors, observational conditional independences, interventions, or a graph-valued sampling law.
    27. When the structure is paired with fitted local normalized conditionals or potentials, a declared support and base measure, and valid normalization and sampling procedures for the resulting joint law.
    28. No nonzero linear combination of its sufficient statistics is constant almost everywhere. In an overcomplete representation, an affine set of natural-parameter vectors can describe the same law, so the parameter vector and strict convexity in all chosen coordinates fail to be unique.
    29. The empirical mean lies on the boundary. Matching it requires \(\sigma(\eta)=0\), approached only as \(\eta\to-\infty\) in the open natural parameter space; the limiting boundary law exists but no finite natural-parameter maximizer does.
    30. The factorization theorem preserves the parameter-free carrier \(r\), which counts or weights datasets sharing a statistic. The proposed equality drops that carrier and equates densities on different state spaces.
    31. Their precisions and information vectors may be added as an unnormalized quadratic potential. A standalone Gaussian additionally requires a positive-definite combined precision and the correct normalizing constant.

    Summary and Next Step¤

    Classical models make structure and inference explicit: mixtures expose the full latent workflow and motivate EM; scale mixtures expose adaptive heavy tails; factor analysis separates low-dimensional shared variation from residual noise; ICA and sparse coding show exactly which assumptions can reduce latent equivalences; HMMs separate generation, recognition, filtering, smoothing, decoding, and learning; duration and factorial extensions expose the cost of enlarging hidden structure; linear-Gaussian models expose exact belief updates, identification, and stability; switching and nonlinear systems show why local tractability need not survive path marginalization; structural time-series models propagate forecast uncertainty without manufacturing causal semantics; directed graphs motivate conditional factorization; undirected models motivate energies; dependency-structure learning separates precision support, DAG equivalence, latent-induced dependence, and task-specific learned adjacency; and GPs motivate function-space generation. Neural parameterizations can enlarge a factor family without changing the graph or query, but they often trade analytic inference and classical identification results for learned approximation. Continue with Bayesian Learning and Model Assessment for priors, posterior prediction, hierarchical pooling, model comparison, criticism, and missingness; then use Latent Variables and Inference for the exact, variational, Monte Carlo, and amortized computational boundaries. Graphical Models and Structured Probability develops factorization, separation, normalization, conditional structured laws, and mixed representations; Graphical Inference and Message Passing develops queries once factors are fixed; Random Graphs and Relational Generators develops graph-valued probability; Causal Generative Models adds intervention semantics and causal discovery; VAEs introduce nonlinear amortized latent models; Representations and Multimodal Systems separates query-specific representation claims; World Models and Causal Simulators treats controlled neural dynamics; and Stochastic-Process Generators develops continuous-time path laws.
