    5. Progressive, Consistency, Adversarial, and Distribution Distillation¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes diffusion/flow sampling and basic adversarial objectives. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Classify preservation targets: distinguish paired maps, trajectories, endpoint distributions, moments, scores, features, and rewards.
    2. O2 — Audit resource provenance: identify pretrained teachers, real data, synthetic teacher samples, auxiliary scores/discriminators, and staged cost.
    3. O3 — Derive progressive compression: compute the exact two-step target and explain how approximation error propagates across halving stages.
    4. O4 — Interpret distribution objectives: distinguish reverse and forward KL behavior under model restrictions and avoid treating a pseudo-loss as an exact scalar divergence.
    5. O5 — Diagnose preservation failure: test paired accuracy, coverage, guidance, conditional fidelity, and inverse behavior separately.
    6. O6 — Transfer the distillation contract: design a matched student study for a new modality without hiding teacher or auxiliary compute.

    Progressive distillation¤

    Before naming algorithms, fill one row per experiment:

    Table FEW.19. Progressive distillation
    Family Primary matched object Typical extra resources Main blind spot
    progressive/trajectory paired multi-step teacher update pretrained teacher and rollouts teacher-path bias; staged error
    consistency distillation common endpoint on teacher paths teacher solver or adjacent pairs intermediate-map fidelity
    distribution/score noisy student marginal versus target score/law real or teacher data; auxiliary fake score mode coverage under restricted models
    moment matching selected conditional moments teacher/noisy samples unmatched higher moments
    adversarial discriminator features or density-ratio signal discriminator and often teacher features coverage and optimization stability
    inverse/real-data teacher inverse/posterior relation plus data teacher and real observations identifiability and model-specific assumptions

    Hybrids legitimately occupy several rows. The table is a disclosure schema, not a partition of paper names.

    Progressive Distillation trains a student step to match two teacher steps, repeatedly halving the schedule. It preserves teacher trajectories locally but requires multiple stages and can accumulate approximation error.

    Worked linear progressive target¤

    Suppose two deterministic teacher half-steps multiply a scalar by \(0.8\) and \(0.75\). The exact paired full-step target is

    \[ x_2=(0.75)(0.8)x_0=0.6x_0. \]

    A student multiplier \(0.61\) has one-stage paired error \(0.01|x_0|\). If a later distillation stage composes two copies of this student to make its next target, the inherited target multiplier is \(0.61^2=0.3721\), whereas four original teacher half-steps give \(0.6^2=0.36\). The inherited discrepancy is \(0.0121|x_0|\) before the new student's own fitting error. Progressive distillation can work extremely well; the example shows why every stage must be evaluated against the original teacher, not only its immediate predecessor.

    Consistency distillation¤

    Consistency distillation teaches states on one teacher trajectory to share an endpoint prediction. It can reach one or few steps without repeating a halving curriculum. The result inherits the teacher path and solver used to construct targets.

    Two teacher solvers can discretize the same nominal probability-flow ODE into different finite training pairs. If the student is trained on those pairs, its finite target includes solver choice and tolerance. Report the solver, schedule, guidance, precision, and whether targets are cached or generated online.

    Latent adversarial diffusion distillation¤

    Latent Adversarial Diffusion Distillation (LADD) replaces ADD's fixed pretrained DINOv2 discriminator with generative features from a pretrained latent diffusion model. The resulting adversarial distillation method supports few-step, high-resolution synthesis while avoiding that specific fixed-discriminator dependency. It still adds a discriminator-mediated objective to the teacher signal, so its feature choice, optimization behavior, and distribution coverage must be evaluated separately rather than inferred from perceptual quality alone.

    The archival publication is the SIGGRAPH Asia 2024 paper identified by DOI 10.1145/3680528.3687625. The arXiv record remains useful for open access, but should not be mislabeled as a CVPR paper. “Avoiding a fixed DINOv2 discriminator” also does not mean “no discriminator” or “no pretrained feature network.”

    Distribution Matching Distillation¤

    DMD updates a one-step generator using score differences between teacher/data-related and student distributions, with an additional regression component. DMD-style methods match distributions rather than individual teacher trajectories. Their learned auxiliary score, mode coverage, and data access are central qualifiers.

    For generator \(x=G_\theta(z)\), the state-space score difference becomes a parameter update only through the generator Jacobian. Schematically,

    \[ \nabla_\theta\mathcal D \propto \mathbb E_{z,t,\epsilon} \left[J_\theta G_\theta(z)^\top w(t)\bigl(s_{\mathrm{fake}}(x_t,t)-s_{\mathrm{target}}(x_t,t)\bigr)\right]. \]

    The fake score is itself estimated from student samples and changes during training. Stability, stale-target effects, weighting, and auxiliary-model quality therefore belong to the result.

    Score differences become parameter updates only after they are pulled through the student Jacobian and assigned noise-time weights. The score-distillation gradient treatment explains why a stopped-gradient pseudo-loss can install that update without proving that its displayed scalar is a global divergence.

    Worked KL caution: direction matters only with restrictions¤

    For a two-mode target \(p=(1/2,1/2)\) and candidate \(q=(0.99,0.01)\),

    \[ \operatorname{KL}(q\|p)\approx0.637, \qquad \operatorname{KL}(p\|q)\approx1.614. \]

    The forward direction penalizes the nearly missing second mode more strongly in this example. But if \(q\) can represent every distribution and optimization is exact, both KL directions have the same unique optimum \(q=p\). Saying “reverse KL is mode-seeking” describes behavior under restrictions, approximation, and optimization—not a different unconstrained optimum. With an exact zero where \(p>0\), forward KL is infinite; reverse KL can remain finite because it averages under \(q\).

    Modern methods combine objectives to address different failures. For example, the ICCV 2025 paper Trajectory Distribution Matching matches student and teacher distributions at intermediate trajectory times, while Adversarial Distribution Matching adds adversarial distributional machinery. These accepted results expand the design space; their text-to-image/video protocols do not establish a universal ordering over consistency or trajectory methods.

    Inverse and real-data distillation¤

    RealUID, ICLR 2026 oral, gives a universal inverse-distillation construction for matching models with real-data supervision and no GAN discriminator. Its theoretical coverage and empirical protocols do not imply that all teacher families can be compressed losslessly.

    Here “universal” belongs to the paper's declared construction and assumptions, not to every inverse problem or architecture. Audit observation operators, posterior identifiability, access to paired or unpaired real data, teacher queries, and the metric used to compare inverse outputs.

    Gumbel Distillation for Parallel Text Generation (Preprint, 2026) uses a Gumbel-noise coupling to define training targets from an autoregressive teacher for parallel students. Its reported language-model results are evidence for that teacher–student construction, not a general proof of distribution preservation. In particular, shared Gumbel latent randomness, exact configuration-level Gumbel-Max, local perturb-and-MAP, and a relaxed argmax are different objects; the randomized-optimization chapter derives the distinction.

    What distillation can and cannot preserve¤

    A student may match:

    • teacher endpoints for coupled noise;
    • teacher marginal distribution;
    • selected intermediate maps;
    • perceptual features or rewards;
    • or a combination.

    These targets are different. Low FID does not prove paired trajectory agreement; low paired error does not prove diversity; preference gains do not prove density matching.

    Preservation test matrix¤

    Table FEW.20. Preservation test matrix
    Claimed preservation Direct diagnostic Important orthogonal diagnostic
    paired teacher map same-noise endpoint error and trajectory checkpoints distributional coverage over many noises
    endpoint distribution precision/recall-style and task-specific statistics paired error may remain large
    conditional generation condition-stratified fidelity and calibration unconditional quality and prompt leakage
    guidance behavior sweep trained and untrained scales branch count and actual latency
    invertibility/editability round-trip and intervention-specific error output realism after the round trip
    mode coverage exact small-mode occupancy or coverage metrics fidelity within each mode

    Always include the original teacher, the undistilled checkpoint under the same evaluation implementation, a compute-matched direct model where available, and an intentionally weak baseline. Report teacher target-generation cost separately from student optimization cost and from inference cost.

    Retrieval practice: distillation contracts
    1. What is progressively halved in progressive distillation?
    2. How does distribution matching differ from reproducing individual teacher trajectories?
    3. Why can adversarial distillation improve perceptual sharpness while reducing coverage?
    4. Which system settings must be included when distilling a guided sampler?

    Solutions. (1) The number of teacher transitions represented by the student's next transition, usually through repeated stagewise compression. (2) It aligns a selected output distribution or statistics without requiring pathwise correspondence for each noise input. (3) The adversarial signal rewards realism under its discriminator and may underpenalize missing modes. (4) Conditioning, guidance scale and formulation, noise schedule, decoder, precision, and any selection or filtering used at deployment.

    Misconception checks: distillation¤

    • “A student beating its teacher metric means it copied the teacher.” It may optimize that metric, data distribution, or preference signal differently.
    • “Data-free means supervision-free.” Teacher samples, scores, and features are information and compute resources.
    • “Distribution matching preserves the noise-to-sample coupling.” A law can match while paired outputs differ completely.
    • “One-step is automatically cheaper overall.” Teacher construction and distillation may dominate lifecycle compute.

    Independent practice and assessment map: distillation contracts¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: inherited stage error. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. With teacher full-step multiplier \(0.6\) and first student multiplier \(0.61\), predict whether composing the student twice overshoots or undershoots the original four-half-step multiplier, then calculate both.
    2. P1.2 — Compare KL directions. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Reproduce both KL values for \(p=(0.5,0.5)\) and \(q=(0.99,0.01)\) and state why neither direction has a mode-collapsed optimum in an unrestricted exact family.
    3. P1.3 — Type a score-distillation update. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 14 min. Identify the spaces and roles of the target score, fake score, generator Jacobian, time weight, and stop-gradient boundary in the schematic update.
    4. P1.4 — Audit resource provenance. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Compare progressive, DMD-style, and adversarial distillation by teacher calls, real-data access, auxiliary networks, stages, and deploy-time components.
    5. P1.5 — Design a two-mode preservation study. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. Use a known mixture and shared noise coupling to measure paired map error, marginal mode occupancy, within-mode fidelity, and repeated-seed uncertainty.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to parallel language generation. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Specify the coupling, teacher query budget, decoding unit, quality/coverage measures, and a dependence test for a student distilled from an autoregressive teacher.

    Table FEW.23. Outcome-to-task alignment: distillation contracts
    Learning objective Independently assessed by

    O2 — Audit resource provenance P1.4, P1.6

    O4 — Interpret distribution objectives P1.2, P1.3
    O5 — Diagnose preservation failure P1.5

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The next inherited target is \(0.61^2=0.3721\); the original teacher's corresponding four-half-step multiplier is \(0.6^2=0.36\). The inherited target overshoots by \(0.0121\). Check. A new student can add fitting error on top of this inherited discrepancy.
    2. P1.2. \(\operatorname{KL}(q\|p)=0.99\log1.98+0.01\log0.02\approx0.637\). \(\operatorname{KL}(p\|q)=0.5\log(0.5/0.99)+0.5\log(0.5/0.01)\approx1.614\). Both nonnegative divergences are uniquely zero at \(q=p\) when \(q\) is unrestricted and probabilities are represented exactly. Check. Directional mode behavior arises with restrictions, finite optimization, or zero-support boundaries.
    3. P1.3. \(s_{\mathrm{target}}\) and \(s_{\mathrm{fake}}\) are vectors in noisy sample space; their weighted difference is pulled into parameter space by \(J_\theta G_\theta(z)^\top\). The time weight changes emphasis across noise levels. Stop-gradient treats score estimates as a vector target during the generator update. Rubric. A constructed pseudo-loss that yields this gradient is not automatically equal to a globally defined scalar KL.
    4. P1.4. Progressive distillation uses a fixed teacher, paired rollouts, and repeated stages but may ship only the student. DMD-style training uses a target/teacher score and a learned fake score, often plus real or teacher-generated samples and regression. Adversarial variants add a discriminator or pretrained feature hierarchy. For each, log target generation, auxiliary training, student training, and deploy-time networks separately. Check. “No discriminator at inference” does not erase discriminator training cost.
    5. P1.5. Draw a known two-component mixture and fix a teacher/student noise coupling. Measure paired endpoint/component agreement per seed, marginal counts per component, within-component distance, and confidence intervals over independent training and sampling seeds. A student can have exact 50/50 occupancy while swapping every paired assignment, or low paired error while dropping rare modes outside the paired set. Check. All four measurements answer different preservation questions.
    6. P1.6. Declare whether shared Gumbel variables, teacher samples, logits, or local conditionals define the coupling. Count teacher token evaluations and student parallel refinement calls. Evaluate sequence likelihood/proxy, task quality, diversity, latency, and a copy/agreement constraint across positions. Check. Matching single-position marginals does not preserve the autoregressive joint law.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Distillation transfers selected behavior from a costly teacher into fewer evaluations. Progressive and consistency methods emphasize trajectories/maps; adversarial and distribution-matching methods emphasize output laws or features. Chapter 6 defines evidence and compute contracts for comparing all approaches.
