    6. Evaluation and Research Practice¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes Chapters 1–5 and controlled-comparison methodology. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Type the claim: classify endpoint, map, kernel, path, distribution, conditional, and deployed-system claims before selecting evidence.
    2. O2 — Construct matched baselines: hold data, representation, guidance, evaluation, and compute controls fixed where the research question permits.
    3. O3 — Build a complete compute ledger: report outer transitions, component calls, FLOPs, memory, latency, energy, and lifecycle training cost.
    4. O4 — Quantify uncertainty: separate sampling, prompt/data, training-seed, and hardware-run variation with suitable intervals and paired comparisons.
    5. O5 — Diagnose failure: combine exact finite oracles, composition tests, coverage, calibration, conditional fidelity, and systems profiling.
    6. O6 — Write a bounded conclusion: state the strongest supported result, important non-results, scope, artifacts, and reproduction conditions.

    Compute-matched evaluation¤

    Report:

    1. outer transitions, backbone calls, head calls, total NFE, and guidance evaluations;
    2. decoder/tokenizer cost, rejection or reranking, batch size, precision, compiler, and hardware;
    3. training from scratch versus teacher distillation, teacher generation cost, and total training FLOPs;
    4. dataset, resolution, conditioning, guidance scale, sample count, and metric implementation;
    5. endpoint quality, precision/recall or coverage, memorization, and paired/map error when claimed;
    6. quality across 1, 2, 4, and higher NFE rather than only the best operating point;
    7. composition, identity, inversion, and off-grid interval residuals for flow-map claims.

    For component \(c\), record calls \(n_c\), per-call floating-point work \(f_c\), measured energy \(e_c\), and latency contribution under the actual execution graph. Then report, without pretending the quantities are interchangeable,

    \[ F_{\mathrm{inference}}=\sum_cn_cf_c, \qquad E_{\mathrm{inference}}=\sum_cn_ce_c, \qquad T_{\mathrm{end}}\ne\sum_cn_ct_c \]

    in general because calls can overlap, fuse, synchronize, transfer data, or wait on other components. Lifecycle accounting additionally includes teacher training if performed for the study, teacher-target generation, student training, hyperparameter sweeps, failed runs, and the expected deployment volume. Amortization assumptions must be explicit rather than used to declare one method globally “efficient.”

    Statistical unit and uncertainty¤

    Thousands of generated samples from one trained checkpoint do not constitute thousands of independent training replicates. Distinguish:

    • sampling uncertainty: finite generated/reference samples for a fixed checkpoint;
    • prompt or condition uncertainty: which evaluation conditions were drawn;
    • training uncertainty: initialization, data order, and optimization seed;
    • systems uncertainty: warm-up, compiler, clock, contention, and repeated hardware runs.

    Use paired comparisons when methods share prompts or latent seeds, bootstrap at the correct sampling unit, and report variation across independent training runs when claiming method stability. If only one checkpoint exists, say so; a narrow sample-level interval is not a training-stability interval.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • MeanFlow, NeurIPS 2025 oral — Peer-reviewed research for direct average-velocity one-step training.
    • Transition Matching, NeurIPS 2025 poster — Peer-reviewed research for expressive discrete-time continuous-state kernels and continuous AR variants.
    • Exploring the Design Space of Transition Matching, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research from a large controlled TM head/sampler study; conclusions remain architecture and text-to-image protocol specific.
    • CMT, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a mid-training stage intended to stabilize consistency, MeanFlow, and flow-map training.
    • RealUID, ICLR 2026 oral — Peer-reviewed research for real-data universal inverse distillation without adversarial training.
    • Improved Mean Flows, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research on training and guidance issues in fastforward models.
    • Temporal Equilibrium MeanFlow, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for a scale-oriented one-step construction.

    Workshop theory and submitted variants remain labeled workshop/preprint rather than being used as settled evidence.

    Worked exact comparison: accuracy, composition, and cost¤

    Use \(\dot x=-2x\), \(x_0=1\), and horizon one. The exact endpoint is \(e^{-2}\approx0.135335\). Compare four declared programs:

    Table FEW.25. Worked exact comparison: accuracy, composition, and cost
    Program Endpoint Absolute error Principal calls
    exact analytic map \(0.135335\) \(0\) 0 neural
    one Euler step \(-1\) \(1.135335\) 1 field
    two Euler half-steps \(0\) \(0.135335\) 2 field
    learned direct multiplier \(0.14\) \(0.14\) \(0.004665\) 1 map
    learned half multiplier \(0.38\), composed twice \(0.1444\) \(0.009065\) 2 maps

    The direct learned map is more accurate than its composed half-map in this example, while the analytic map is exact without a network. No universal method ranking follows: the learned multipliers were stipulated, not trained, and latency depends on the field/map architectures. The oracle nevertheless catches sign errors, verifies composition arithmetic, and forces the study to name the counted component.

    Now suppose measured warm latency for the one-call direct map is \(6\) ms, two half-map calls take \(9\) ms because of cache reuse, and one/two field programs take \(4\)/\(7\) ms. Accuracy-versus-call and accuracy-versus-latency order the programs differently. Report both; neither substitutes for distributional quality on a real generative task.

    Reproduction and limitation card¤

    Every few-step result should ship or record:

    1. source revision, environment/containers, weights, licenses, and data split;
    2. target-generation code, teacher revision, solver, schedule, guidance, and cache provenance;
    3. student architecture, objective weights, time/interval sampler, JVP and stop-gradient choices, optimizer, precision, and independent seeds;
    4. exact inference pseudocode with outer transitions and every component call;
    5. metric implementations, reference statistics, prompts/conditions, sample counts, confidence intervals, and failed/excluded runs;
    6. hardware, warm-up, synchronization, batching, compilation, memory, latency, throughput, and energy protocol;
    7. known unsupported intervals, guidance scales, modalities, resolutions, editing/inverse queries, and failure cases.

    An artifact can reproduce a number without validating the scientific attribution. Conversely, a mathematically correct identity can be implemented incorrectly. Preserve source, semantic, numerical, statistical, and systems evidence as separate layers.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Verify identity and composition residuals for an analytic linear flow map.
    2. Compare one Euler step, an exact map, and an average-velocity map on a nonlinear ODE.
    3. Build a stochastic two-mode transition where a deterministic conditional mean fails.
    4. Separate outer transitions from NFE for a nested transition head.
    5. Evaluate a distilled student with both paired teacher error and distributional precision/recall.
    6. Reproduce one 2026 claim with matched guidance, tokenizer, sample count, and hardware.
    Exercise 1 solution

    For \(dx/dt=ax\), \(F(x,t,s)=e^{a(s-t)}x\). Identity follows at \(s=t\); composition follows because \(e^{a(r-s)}e^{a(s-t)}=e^{a(r-t)}\). Any learned residual measures approximation, not a failure of the exact flow.

    Cumulative retrieval: few-step generation
    1. Why should quality-versus-NFE and quality-versus-latency both be reported?
    2. Which baseline separates architectural improvement from additional teacher compute?
    3. What diagnostic distinguishes mode loss from ordinary sampling variance?
    4. What must a “one-step” research claim state explicitly?

    Solutions. (1) Equal NFE can hide different network sizes, guidance calls, hardware utilization, and system overhead. (2) A compute- and data-matched direct model alongside the teacher and distilled student, with teacher-generation cost reported separately. (3) Repeated seeds plus class/region coverage, precision–recall-style distributional diagnostics, and comparison to reference sampling variability. (4) The unit called a step, number of model evaluations, conditioning/guidance, decoder and post-processing, hardware/precision, target distribution, and evaluation budget.

    Misconception checks: evidence and deployment¤

    • “More generated samples establish training stability.” They narrow sampling error for one checkpoint, not variation across trained models.
    • “Matched NFE is matched compute.” Architecture, internal heads, guidance, shapes, and hardware remain uncontrolled.
    • “A better FID at one budget dominates the frontier.” Quality is multi-objective and budgets form a curve.
    • “Reproducible means scientifically valid.” Repeating an implementation does not repair a confounded comparison or overbroad claim.

    Independent practice and assessment map: evidence and research practice¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: exact-program ranking. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. Before calculating, predict which of one Euler step, two Euler half-steps, direct multiplier \(0.14\), and two composed \(0.38\) multipliers is closest to \(e^{-2}\); then reproduce the table.
    2. P1.2 — Build a compute ledger. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A two-transition method uses two fused guidance branches per backbone, one launch per transition, four head calls per transition, and one decoder. Report outer transitions, branch-equivalent backbone work, backbone launches, head calls, and auxiliary calls.
    3. P1.3 — Choose the statistical unit. Type: design · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 14 min. A study has three training seeds, 10,000 samples per checkpoint, and 500 shared prompts. Specify intervals or resampling for training, sampling, and prompt uncertainty without treating all images as independent training runs.
    4. P1.4 — Construct a matched ablation. Type: design · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 16 min. Test whether a new finite-map objective—not a larger model or different guidance—improves a four-step text-to-image system.
    5. P1.5 — Bound a conclusion. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Rewrite “our one-step method is faster and preserves the diffusion model” after learning that only one GPU, one resolution, FID, and three latency runs were measured, with no paired trajectories.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a scientific simulator. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design an evidence stack for replacing a 100-step stochastic simulator with four learned transitions while preserving invariants, rare events, and uncertainty.

    Feedback contract: evidence and research practice¤

    Table FEW.26. Feedback contract: evidence and research practice — table 1
    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 Exact endpoint and all four absolute errors are recovered; no general ranking is inferred from stipulated multipliers.
    P1.2 Two outer transitions, four branch-equivalent evaluations, two launches, eight head calls, and one decoder are separate.
    P1.3 Training seeds are the method-stability unit; sample and paired-prompt uncertainty are handled conditionally/hierarchically.
    P1.4 Data, architecture, representation, guidance, budget, evaluator, and tuning effort are held fixed or explicitly crossed.
    P1.5 The conclusion is limited to measured endpoint metric and hardware/protocol; trajectory preservation is withdrawn.
    P1.6 Analytic invariants, transition/path laws, rare-event tails, uncertainty, refinement, and deployment cost all receive tests.

    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup
    P1.1 Compute \(e^{-2}\) once, then absolute differences. Two half-step multipliers give \(0.38^2=0.1444\).

    Table FEW.28. Outcome-to-task alignment: evidence and research practice
    Learning objective Independently assessed by
    O1 — Type the claim P1.5, P1.6
    O2 — Construct matched baselines P1.4

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. \(e^{-2}\approx0.135335\). One Euler step gives \(-1\) and error \(1.135335\); two half-steps give zero and error \(0.135335\); the direct map gives \(0.14\) and error \(0.004665\); two half maps give \(0.1444\) and error \(0.009065\). The stipulated direct map is closest. Check. This finite oracle does not predict learned performance on another system.
    2. P1.2. There are two outer transitions. Two logical branches at each give four branch-equivalent backbone evaluations, while fusion produces two launched backbone calls. Four head calls per transition give eight head calls, followed by one auxiliary decoder call. Check. Add shapes, FLOPs, memory, precision, and measured latency before making an efficiency claim.
    3. P1.3. Compute each metric per checkpoint and summarize across the three independently trained checkpoints; with only three, show all points and use cautious intervals. Estimate fixed-checkpoint sampling uncertainty by resampling generated/reference samples. Pair method differences by the same 500 prompts and bootstrap prompts, nesting checkpoint effects rather than flattening 30,000 images. Rubric. Images from one checkpoint are not independent training replicates.
    4. P1.4. Use the same training data/order, architecture, codec, initialization seeds, optimizer budget, guidance training and inference scale, four-step schedule, precision, and evaluator for baseline and new objective. Match tuning budgets and report failures. A factorial extension can cross objective with guidance, but the main effect remains identifiable. Check. Comparing against a smaller or differently guided baseline would not isolate the objective.
    5. P1.5. A defensible rewrite is: “On the declared GPU, resolution, batch, precision, and three-run timing protocol, our one-step implementation had lower measured latency than the selected baseline and achieved the reported FID. We did not test other hardware, metrics, paired teacher maps, intermediate trajectories, or path-law preservation.” Check. Even “faster” is scoped to the measured deployment configuration.
    6. P1.6. Start with an exact small reference and test invariant conservation, one-time marginals, transition kernels, autocorrelation/path statistics, rare-event probabilities, and predictive calibration. Sweep four-transition schedules and internal tolerances, compare with 100-step and exact baselines, evaluate held-out parameters/initial conditions, repeat seeds, and report component plus end-to-end cost. Check. Matching mean trajectories while losing rare events or uncertainty is a failed preservation claim.

    Final synthesis¤

    Few-step generation learns finite-time behavior instead of repeatedly evaluating infinitesimal dynamics. Consistency, flow-map, MeanFlow, Transition-Matching, and distillation methods differ in whether they target endpoints, deterministic two-time maps, average velocities, stochastic kernels, or teacher distributions. Their fairest common currency is not the word “step,” but complete compute plus the exact behavior trained to be preserved.
