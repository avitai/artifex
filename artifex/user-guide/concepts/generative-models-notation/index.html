    Generative-model papers often reuse the same symbol for different objects or reverse the direction of a map without warning. This chapter fixes the conventions used throughout the Artifex curriculum and records alternate conventions explicitly.

    This is a reference chapter, but it should still be read once from beginning to end. Most silent implementation errors in this field are direction, reduction, endpoint, or parameterization errors rather than failures of the high-level theory.

    Why this reference remains one page

    This compact registry keeps notation, direction, reduction, and endpoint conventions searchable together. It is intentionally a single reference surface, not an unprojected instructional volume.

    Use Notation as a Translation Layer¤

    Do not begin by matching symbol names across two papers. Begin by naming what each symbol does. The same \(x_0\) can mean data in one source and noise in another; the same word forward can mean data-to-base likelihood evaluation or base-to-data generation.

    • Object

      What random variable, measure, vector field, or parameter does the symbol denote?

    • Direction

      Which endpoint is input, which is output, and which way does time run?

    • Shape and reduction

      Which dimensions are event axes, and which are averaged or summed?

    • Boundary check

      Do units, support, signs, endpoints, and inverse operations reproduce known special cases?

    The tables below are therefore not a vocabulary list to memorize. They are the book's common coordinate system for translating claims and implementations.

    Learning Objectives and Reading Routes¤

    After this chapter you should be able to translate between laws and densities, pushforwards and inverse maps, Markov invariance and stationarity, common flow directions, diffusion targets, score/energy signs, guidance scales, and NFE reports.

    Routes

    Beginner: read Sections 1–7 and perform the conversion check.
    Practitioner: also use the shape/reduction and reporting conventions as an implementation checklist.
    Research: state every alternate convention before reproducing a paper and verify conversions at both endpoints.

    1. Probability and Data¤

    Read a symbol in a complete sentence

    Instead of saying “\(x\) is data,” say “\(x\) is one observed event in \(\mathcal X\), distributed according to the law currently named.” The longer sentence forces support, conditioning, and batch-versus-event distinctions into view.

    Table NOT.1. 1. Probability and Data
    Symbol Meaning
    \(x\in\mathcal X\) Observed data point
    \(P_X\) Probability law of \(X\) on measurable subsets of \(\mathcal X\)
    \(\mu\) or \(\nu\) Reference or base measure, stated locally
    \(p_X=dP_X/d\mu\) Density of \(P_X\) relative to \(\mu\) when it exists
    \(\delta_x\) Unit point mass at \(x\)
    \(f_\#P\) Pushforward law: \((f_\#P)(A)=P(f^{-1}(A))\)
    \(p_{\mathrm{data}}\) Unknown population data distribution
    \(\hat p_{\mathrm{data}}\) Empirical distribution of a finite dataset
    \(p_\theta(x)\) Model mass or density with parameters \(\theta\)
    \(z\in\mathcal Z\) Latent/base variable
    \(p(z)\) Prior or base distribution
    \(c\) Observed condition, context, label, text, or control
    \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) Variational or amortized inference distribution
    \(\mathbb E_p[f]\) Expectation of \(f\) under \(p\)

    Uppercase \(P,Q\) usually denote probability measures and lowercase \(p,q\) their densities or masses under the stated reference measure. Subscripts name distributions; hats indicate estimators or predictions. The curriculum never uses \(p(x)\) for a point probability when \(x\) is continuous without stating the reference density. The notation \(f^{-1}(A)\) denotes a preimage and does not assert that \(f\) is invertible; an actual inverse function is stated explicitly.

    Samples, Batches, and Dimensions¤

    Table NOT.2. Samples, Batches, and Dimensions
    Symbol Meaning
    \(N\) Dataset or sample count, stated locally
    \(B\) Minibatch size
    \(D\) or \(d\) Event/latent dimension
    \(T\) Sequence length or terminal time; never both in one derivation
    \(K\) Number of classes, mixture components, particles, or importance samples; stated locally
    \(x^{(n)}\) The \(n\)th independent observation
    \(x_t\) State at sequence index or continuous/discrete time \(t\)

    The event dimension is not the batch dimension. A log density \(\log p(x^{(n)})\) usually sums over event dimensions and is then averaged over \(n\). Broadcasting a time coefficient over image or sequence axes must be written with its shape in implementation examples.

    2. Losses and Information Quantities¤

    Table NOT.3. 2. Losses and Information Quantities
    Symbol Meaning
    \(\mathcal L\) Objective being minimized unless explicitly stated otherwise
    \(\mathrm{NLL}\) Negative log-likelihood
    \(\mathrm{ELBO}\) Evidence lower bound, maximized; implementations often minimize its negative
    \(H(p)\) Entropy
    \(H(p,q)\) Cross-entropy
    \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)\) KL divergence, expectation under the first argument
    \(W_p\) \(p\)-Wasserstein distance
    \(\operatorname{MMD}\) Maximum mean discrepancy

    Every equation states whether it is minimized or maximized. A loss named “ELBO loss” must specify whether it means the ELBO or negative ELBO.

    Reduction Convention¤

    Unless a chapter says otherwise:

    • log probabilities sum over event dimensions;
    • reported training losses average over the minibatch;
    • token NLL may additionally divide by the number of non-padding tokens;
    • bits per dimension divides total negative log likelihood by \(D\log2\);
    • KL terms state whether they are per latent dimension, per example, or batch averages.

    This convention prevents meaningless universal thresholds. A KL of 20 can be tiny for a 4,096-dimensional code and large for a scalar latent; a per-token NLL cannot be compared with a per-sequence sum.

    3. Autoregressive Conventions¤

    For an ordered sequence \(x_{1:T}\),

    \[ x_{<t}=(x_1,\ldots,x_{t-1}), \qquad p_\theta(x_{1:T})=\prod_{t=1}^{T}p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t}). \]

    \(T\) denotes sequence length in AR chapters. Because \(T\) also denotes a terminal diffusion time elsewhere, the local meaning is stated at the beginning of each derivation.

    4. VAE Conventions¤

    \[ p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z), \qquad q_\phi(z\mid x)\approx p_\theta(z\mid x). \]

    Encoder outputs are \(\mu_\phi(x)\) and \(\log\sigma_\phi^2(x)\). The reparameterized sample is

    \[ z=\mu_\phi(x)+\sigma_\phi(x)\odot\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I). \]

    log_var always means \(\log\sigma^2\), never a positive variance parameter. If a network emits a positive scale through softplus, the logarithmic variance is recovered as \(2\log\sigma\).

    5. Classical Flow Direction¤

    The direction table is easiest to remember as a round trip: generation pushes a base draw into data space; likelihood evaluation pulls an observation back to base space and accounts for the inverse change in volume.

    The canonical generative map is

    \[ x=f_\theta(z), \qquad z=f_\theta^{-1}(x). \]

    Therefore

    \[ \log p_X(x) =\log p_Z(z)-\log\left|\det\frac{\partial f_\theta(z)}{\partial z}\right| =\log p_Z(f_\theta^{-1}(x)) +\log\left|\det\frac{\partial f_\theta^{-1}(x)}{\partial x}\right|. \]

    Some libraries call data-to-base the “forward” direction because it is used for training likelihood; others call base-to-data forward because it is the mathematical map \(f\). Every implementation table must name the actual input and output rather than rely on “forward” or “inverse” alone.

    Direction Table¤

    Table NOT.4. Direction Table
    Operation Input → output Jacobian term
    Generative map \(z\to x=f(z)\) subtract $\log
    Density/evaluation map \(x\to z=f^{-1}(x)\) add $\log

    IAF sampling direction base coordinates can be transformed in parallel density inverse is sequential

    “Parallel” refers to dependency structure, not guaranteed hardware speed. Coupling layers are parallel in both directions but transform only part of the state per layer.

    6. Energy and Score¤

    \[ p_\theta(x)=\frac{e^{-E_\theta(x)}}{Z_\theta}, \qquad Z_\theta=\int e^{-E_\theta(x)}dx. \]

    The score is a gradient with respect to the state, not parameters:

    \[ s_\theta(x)=\nabla_x\log p_\theta(x). \]

    For a differentiable normalized EBM,

    \[ s_\theta(x)=-\nabla_xE_\theta(x), \]

    because \(Z_\theta\) does not depend on \(x\). An arbitrary learned vector field is not necessarily a score: it must be conservative/integrable under the relevant domain and boundary conditions.

    Energy Sign and Gaps¤

    Lower energy means higher unnormalized probability under the convention \(p\propto e^{-E}\). Therefore a data-versus-negative energy gap is declared explicitly. This curriculum uses

    \[ \Delta_E=\mathbb E_{\mathrm{negative}}E(x^-) -\mathbb E_{\mathrm{data}}E(x^+), \]

    so a positive gap means data have lower average energy. If a source defines the reverse order, the sign is converted before comparison.

    7. Diffusion and Noise Direction¤

    This picture fixes roles, not a numerical schedule. A source may index the reverse path from zero, swap endpoint names, or use an unbounded noise level \(\sigma\); translate those choices before importing a sign or coefficient.

    The clean endpoint is \(t=0\) and the high-noise endpoint is \(t=T\) or \(t=1\). For a variance-preserving parameterization,

    \[ x_t=a_t x_0+b_t\epsilon, \qquad a_t^2+b_t^2=1, \qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I). \]

    The individual conditional-kernel score and marginal score are distinct:

    \[ \nabla_{x_t}\log q(x_t\mid x_0)=-\frac{\epsilon}{b_t}, \qquad \nabla_{x_t}\log q_t(x_t)=-\frac{\mathbb E[\epsilon\mid x_t]}{b_t}. \]

    Velocity prediction uses

    \[ v_t=a_t\epsilon-b_t x_0, \]

    so \(v_t\to\epsilon\) at the clean endpoint and \(v_t\to-x_0\) at the high-noise endpoint. Flow-matching velocity is a path derivative and must not be assumed identical to this VP rotation without deriving the chosen path.

    Diffusion Target Conversion Table¤

    For \(x_t=a_tx_0+b_t\epsilon\) with \(a_t^2+b_t^2=1\) and \(a_t,b_t>0\):

    Table NOT.5. Diffusion Target Conversion Table
    Predicted target Recover \(x_0\) Recover \(\epsilon\) Relation to score
    \(\hat x_0\) \(\hat x_0\) \((x_t-a_t\hat x_0)/b_t\) \(\hat s=(a_t\hat x_0-x_t)/b_t^2\)
    \(\hat\epsilon\) \((x_t-b_t\hat\epsilon)/a_t\) \(\hat\epsilon\) \(\hat s=-\hat\epsilon/b_t\)
    \(\hat v\) \(a_tx_t-b_t\hat v\) \(b_tx_t+a_t\hat v\) substitute \(\hat\epsilon\) above
    \(\hat s\) \((x_t+b_t^2\hat s)/a_t\) \(-b_t\hat s\) \(\hat s\)

    These are algebraic conversions of outputs for one parameterization. Their training errors receive different time-dependent amplification. For example, an \(\epsilon\) error \(\delta_\epsilon\) becomes \(-(b_t/a_t)\delta_\epsilon\) in reconstructed \(x_0\), which is ill-conditioned when \(a_t\) is small at high noise.

    EDM Noise-Level Convention¤

    EDM-style presentations often use an unbounded noise scale \(\sigma\) with \(x=x_0+\sigma\epsilon\) rather than a VP pair \((a_t,b_t)\). Dividing by \(\sqrt{1+\sigma^2}\) maps it to VP-like coefficients

    \[ a=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+\sigma^2}}, \qquad b=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{1+\sigma^2}}. \]

    Schedules, preconditioning coefficients, and loss weights must be translated together; converting only the time coordinate is incomplete.

    8. Flow Matching and ODEs¤

    For a probability path \(p_t\) and velocity field \(u_t\),

    \[ \frac{dX_t}{dt}=u_t(X_t), \qquad \partial_t p_t(x)+\nabla\cdot(p_t(x)u_t(x))=0. \]

    \(x_0\) and \(x_1\) may denote data/noise in either order across papers. This curriculum states the endpoints for every path rather than inferring them from the subscripts.

    A path sample may be written \(x_t=I_t(x_0,x_1,\epsilon)\) and its conditional velocity as \(\partial_t I_t\). The marginal velocity is a conditional expectation given \(x_t\). A stochastic interpolant can also induce a score and forward/backward drifts. Equality between these objects requires the path identities; it is not inferred from reusing \(t\).

    9. Guidance Convention¤

    For unconditional prediction \(o_u\) and conditional prediction \(o_c\), the default scale is

    \[ o_{\mathrm{cfg}}=o_u+s(o_c-o_u). \]

    Thus \(s=0\) is unconditional, \(s=1\) is the ordinary conditional prediction, and \(s>1\) extrapolates. The alternate expression \((1+w)o_c-wo_u\) uses \(w=s-1\). The target \(o\)—score, noise, velocity, or logits—must be stated because linear combination in different parameter spaces need not induce the same generated distribution.

    For autoregressive logits, guidance often combines conditional and unconditional log probabilities, possibly with temperature and renormalization. For diffusion targets, algebraic target conversions may make linear combinations coincide at one time step under exact models, but nonlinear clipping, thresholding, approximate networks, and solver dynamics can break system-level equivalence.

    10. Network Evaluations and Wall-Clock¤

    NFE means the number of sequential neural-network evaluations used by a sampler. It is not comparable unless the network, input resolution, conditional branches, batching, caching, and auxiliary decoders are specified. An autoregressive inverse requiring one masked-network call per generated patch is not “one NFE” in the same operational sense as a single direct map.

    Compute Reporting Vocabulary¤

    Table NOT.6. Compute Reporting Vocabulary
    Quantity Report
    Parameters total and active parameters per token/sample
    Training compute FLOPs convention, tokens/examples, resolution, hardware, precision
    Sampling NFE sequential calls plus conditional/unconditional multiplicity
    Latency batch, hardware, compilation/warm-up, codec and post-processing
    Memory peak device memory and cache policy
    Solver work accepted/rejected adaptive steps and function evaluations

    One “step” may contain two model evaluations, multiple corrector evaluations, or a conditional/unconditional pair. Prefer exact call counts over method nicknames.

    11. Discrete-Time and Continuous-Time Indexing¤

    For a Markov kernel \(K(x,A)\) and row-law convention,

    \[ \mu_{t+1}=\mu_tK, \qquad \pi K=\pi \]

    defines law propagation and invariance. Reusing \(K\) at every step is time homogeneity. Calling the process stationary additionally means it is initialized with an invariant law (or otherwise has shift-invariant finite-dimensional distributions). Irreducibility, aperiodicity, recurrence, and quantitative mixing assumptions are stated with each theorem rather than hidden inside the word “ergodic.”

    Discrete diffusion commonly uses \(t\in\{1,\ldots,T\}\) with cumulative \(\bar\alpha_t=\prod_{s=1}^t\alpha_s\). Continuous formulations use \(t\in[0,1]\) and coefficients \(a(t),b(t)\). A Riemann discretization of an SDE is not automatically the same as a particular DDPM transition schedule.

    For sequences, \(x_t\) may mean the \(t\)th token rather than a noisy state. Chapters use \(x_{1:T}\) for sequences and \(x(t)\) or an explicit “noise time” label when ambiguity is possible.

    Worked Conversion Check¤

    Given \(x_t=a_tx_0+b_t\epsilon\) and \(v_t=a_t\epsilon-b_tx_0\), the pair is a rotation:

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}x_t\\v_t\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}a_t&b_t\\-b_t&a_t\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}x_0\\\epsilon\end{bmatrix}. \]

    Since the inverse is the transpose,

    \[ x_0=a_tx_t-b_tv_t, \qquad \epsilon=b_tx_t+a_tv_t. \]

    Substituting \(a_t=1,b_t=0\) recovers \(x_t=x_0\) and \(v_t=\epsilon\); substituting \(a_t=0,b_t=1\) gives \(x_t=\epsilon\) and \(v_t=-x_0\). Endpoint substitution is an effective defense against sign errors.

    Worked Convention Translation: Guidance Scale¤

    Suppose one paper uses

    \[ o=o_u+s(o_c-o_u) \]

    with \(s=7.5\), while another uses

    \[ o=(1+w)o_c-wo_u. \]

    Expanding the first expression gives \(o=so_c+(1-s)o_u\), so \(w=s-1=6.5\). Copying the numeric value \(7.5\) into the second convention would produce an effective first-convention scale of \(8.5\).

    The conversion aligns the linear formula only. To reproduce a result one must also match the prediction target, conditioning dropout, negative prompt/unconditional input, rescaling or thresholding, sampler, and precision.

    Convention Audit Checklist¤

    Before implementing an equation from a paper, record:

    1. the random variable and its support/reference measure;
    2. whether the map runs data→base or base→data;
    3. whether time runs data→noise or noise→data;
    4. the predicted target and exact conversion equations;
    5. whether expectations and losses sum or average each dimension;
    6. the energy and guidance sign conventions;
    7. what one “step” or NFE counts;
    8. tensor shapes, dtypes, broadcasting, and boundary values.

    Misconception Checks¤

    The same symbol \(v\) means the same velocity in diffusion and flow matching.

    No. VP \(v=a\epsilon-bx_0\) is a rotated prediction target. A flow-matching velocity is the time derivative/marginal field of a specified probability path. They can be related for particular paths, but the relation must be derived.

    If two target parameterizations are algebraically convertible, training them is identical.

    No. Finite-network errors, time weighting, preconditioning, clipping, and numerical conditioning differ even when ideal outputs can be converted.

    One NFE means one unit of comparable compute.

    No. Architecture size, active parameters, conditional branches, sequence dependencies, resolution, codec work, and hardware determine actual cost.

    Retrieval Check¤

    1. Why do forward-map and inverse-map Jacobian formulas have opposite signs?
    2. Why is \(-\epsilon/b_t\) not automatically the marginal score for one sampled training pair?
    3. Under the curriculum guidance convention, what do \(s=0\) and \(s=1\) mean?
    4. Why is NFE insufficient for comparing TarFlow and a one-step MeanFlow model?
    5. If one guidance convention uses \(s=1\), what does that mean? What is the equivalent \(w\) in \((1+w)o_c-wo_u\)?
    6. Why should diffusion target conversions be checked at both endpoints?
    Solutions
    1. The determinants of a bijection and its inverse are reciprocals, so their log absolute determinants differ by a sign.
    2. It is the conditional perturbation-kernel score. The marginal score averages it over the posterior uncertainty in \(x_0\) or \(\epsilon\) given \(x_t\).
    3. \(s=0\) selects the unconditional output; \(s=1\) selects the ordinary conditional output without extrapolation.
    4. TarFlow's inverse is autoregressive and requires sequential conditional generation, while MeanFlow's headline sampler is one direct network evaluation.
    5. It is the ordinary conditional output, and the equivalent alternate scale is \(w=0\).
    6. Endpoint substitution exposes swapped signs, reversed time direction, and incorrect coefficient placement immediately.

    Summary¤

    Notation is part of the model specification. Direction, endpoint, conditioning, and parameterization conventions must be declared before equations are compared. All later chapters link back here when a paper uses a different convention.
