    Filtering and Smoothing for State-Space Generators¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Classical Generative Models and Graphical Structure
    • Latent Variables and Inference
    • Objectives and Statistical Distances
    • Graphical Inference and Message Passing

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A state-space generator defines a law over a latent trajectory and its observations. Filtering, smoothing, forecasting, path sampling, and parameter learning are computations performed with that law; they are not interchangeable names for the model. This volume develops the exact recursions, the linear-Gaussian case, the principal approximation families, and the numerical and scientific checks needed to know what a finite implementation has actually computed.

    The model family remains defined in Classical Generative Models. This volume owns the inference methods. Continuous-time path laws and particle implementations continue in Stochastic-Process Generators, while action-conditioned beliefs and planning appear in World Models and Causal Simulators.

    Reading routes

    • Entry after prerequisites: read Chapters 1–3 and reproduce the two scalar calculations.
    • Practitioner: add Chapters 4–6, implement the limiting-case tests, and use the method-selection ledger.
    • Research: read continuously, derive every projection step, compare at least two posterior representations on a controlled simulator, and trace research claims to their primary sources.

    Reader orientation¤

    Chapter map¤

    The complete volume is one continuous argument. Six projections provide optional stopping points without maintaining a second copy of the instructional content:

    1. Bayesian Recursions and Information Sets — model law, filtering, smoothing, forecasting, evidence, and a finite exact example;
    2. Linear-Gaussian Filtering — Kalman prediction and correction, exactness, likelihood, missing observations, and stable covariance computation;
    3. Smoothing, Sampling, and Information Forms — RTS smoothing, backward simulation, information coordinates, sufficient statistics, and temporal parallelization;
    4. Nonlinear Gaussian Approximations — extended, iterated, sigma-point, cubature, quadrature, and moment-based filters and smoothers;
    5. Projection, Mixture, Ensemble, and Robust Filters — ADF, Gaussian sums, switching models, EnKF, robust updates, and flow-based constructions; and
    6. Particles, Learning, Validation, and Method Choice — non-Gaussian alternatives, differentiable filters, diagnostics, controlled comparison, and current research.

    Prerequisite refresh¤

    For a transition density \(f_t(z_t\mid z_{t-1},u_t)\) and observation density \(g_t(y_t\mid z_t,u_t)\), the controlled joint law is

    \[ p(z_{0:T},y_{1:T}\mid u_{1:T}) =p(z_0) \prod_{t=1}^{T} f_t(z_t\mid z_{t-1},u_t) g_t(y_t\mid z_t,u_t). \]

    The input sequence is suppressed below when it is fixed. A density is always relative to a declared base measure; sums replace integrals for discrete states. Review Graphical Inference and Message Passing for factor elimination and Latent Variables and Inference for the five-field contract: target, query, returned representation, guarantee, and finite-compute validation.

    The arrows define conditional factors. They do not prescribe a Kalman filter, particle filter, neural encoder, or any other reverse computation.

    1. Bayesian Recursions and Information Sets¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Conditional probability, Bayes' rule, Markov structure, and normalized message passing.

    Learning objectives. Derive the exact predict–correct recursion; distinguish filtering, smoothing, forecasting, and path sampling by their information sets; recover the sequence likelihood from predictive normalizers; and compute a finite filter and smoother without invoking an algorithm name as an accuracy guarantee.

    One law, several questions¤

    At time \(t\), the following distributions answer different questions:

    Table FILTER.1. One law, several questions
    Query Target Information available Typical output
    prediction \(p(z_t\mid y_{1:t-1})\) observations before \(t\) a prior belief for the next correction
    filtering \(p(z_t\mid y_{1:t})\) observations through \(t\) current-state posterior
    fixed-lag smoothing \(p(z_{t-L}\mid y_{1:t})\) a bounded amount of future evidence delayed state posterior
    fixed-interval smoothing \(p(z_t\mid y_{1:T})\) the complete recorded interval retrospective state posterior
    forecasting \(p(y_{t+h}\mid y_{1:t})\) past observations and declared future inputs future-observation law
    path sampling \(p(z_{0:T}\mid y_{1:T})\) complete observations correlated posterior trajectory

    An encoder that reads \(y_{1:T}\) is a smoother even if its output variable is called a filtered state. Conversely, a filter used online cannot use later observations without leakage. Fixed-lag smoothing is a third information contract rather than an informal compromise between the two.

    Deriving the exact Bayesian filter¤

    Suppose the previous filtering law is \(b_{t-1}(z_{t-1})=p(z_{t-1}\mid y_{1:t-1})\). Markov structure gives the one-step predictive law

    \[ b_{t\mid t-1}(z_t) =\int f_t(z_t\mid z_{t-1})b_{t-1}(z_{t-1})\,dz_{t-1}. \]

    The new observation contributes a likelihood factor. Its predictive density is

    \[ \ell_t =p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1}) =\int g_t(y_t\mid z_t)b_{t\mid t-1}(z_t)\,dz_t, \]

    and Bayes' rule gives the normalized correction

    \[ b_t(z_t) =p(z_t\mid y_{1:t}) =\frac{g_t(y_t\mid z_t)b_{t\mid t-1}(z_t)}{\ell_t}. \]

    These are exact probability identities. They are not yet a finite algorithm: the integrals may be unavailable, the returned density may require an unbounded representation, and finite arithmetic may lose normalization or positive semidefiniteness.

    The normalizer is also the incremental evidence. Therefore

    \[ \log p(y_{1:T})=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log \ell_t. \]

    Replacing \(\ell_t\) by an unnormalized score breaks both the posterior update and the likelihood calculation. When an approximation supplies \(\widehat\ell_t\), the sum is an approximate log likelihood unless a separate unbiased-estimation argument applies.

    Deriving the backward smoothing recursion¤

    The Markov graph implies

    \[ p(z_t\mid z_{t+1},y_{1:T}) =p(z_t\mid z_{t+1},y_{1:t}). \]

    Bayes' rule expresses this backward kernel in terms of filtered quantities:

    \[ p(z_t\mid z_{t+1},y_{1:t}) =\frac{f_{t+1}(z_{t+1}\mid z_t)b_t(z_t)} {b_{t+1\mid t}(z_{t+1})}, \]

    on the support where the denominator is positive. Marginalizing the next smoothed state yields

    \[ p(z_t\mid y_{1:T}) =b_t(z_t) \int \frac{f_{t+1}(z_{t+1}\mid z_t) p(z_{t+1}\mid y_{1:T})} {b_{t+1\mid t}(z_{t+1})} \,dz_{t+1}. \]

    This identity explains why smoothing needs stored or recomputed forward information. It also exposes a support requirement that an arbitrary learned backward network need not satisfy.

    Worked finite example: future evidence changes an earlier belief¤

    Let \(z_t\in\{0,1\}\). Initially \(p(z_1=0)=3/5\) and \(p(z_1=1)=2/5\). The state stays unchanged with probability \(4/5\). Observations are binary, with

    \[ p(y_t=1\mid z_t=0)=\frac{1}{10}, \qquad p(y_t=1\mid z_t=1)=\frac{7}{10}. \]

    After observing \(y_1=1\), the two unnormalized masses are \(3/50\) and \(14/50\). Thus

    \[ p(z_1\mid y_1=1) =\left(\frac3{17},\frac{14}{17}\right). \]

    Prediction through the transition gives

    \[ p(z_2\mid y_1=1)=\left(\frac{26}{85},\frac{59}{85}\right). \]

    Now observe \(y_2=0\). The likelihoods are \(9/10\) and \(3/10\), so the filtering law at time two is

    \[ p(z_2\mid y_1=1,y_2=0) =\left(\frac{78}{137},\frac{59}{137}\right). \]

    For smoothing time one, the backward likelihood of \(y_2=0\) is \(39/50\) when \(z_1=0\) and \(21/50\) when \(z_1=1\). Multiplying these values by the time-one filter and renormalizing gives

    \[ p(z_1\mid y_1=1,y_2=0) =\left(\frac{39}{137},\frac{98}{137}\right). \]

    The filtered probability of state one at time one was \(14/17\approx0.824\); after seeing a future zero, its smoothed probability is \(98/137\approx0.715\). Neither result supersedes the other: they condition on different information.

    A point state is not a belief

    Replacing the time-one distribution by its most likely state discards the alternative trajectory before the second observation arrives. Later evidence cannot recover a hypothesis that an approximate representation assigned exactly zero mass.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Filtering is sequential Bayes conditioning, smoothing is posterior inference with later evidence, forecasting propagates without later evidence, and path sampling preserves posterior dependence across time. The predictive normalizer connects online correction to sequence likelihood. Exact identities become algorithms only after a representation and numerical procedure are specified.

    Chapter 1 retrieval practice¤

    1. Why is \(\ell_t\) more than a disposable normalizer?
    2. Which conditional-independence statement permits the backward kernel to ignore \(y_{t+1:T}\) once \(z_{t+1}\) is known?
    3. Does a smaller smoothed variance imply that the online filter was wrong?
    4. What support failure can make the displayed smoothing ratio undefined?
    Complete solutions
    1. It equals \(p(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1})\) and its log contributes exactly to the sequence log likelihood.
    2. \(z_t\perp y_{t+1:T}\mid(z_{t+1},y_{1:t})\) under the declared first-order state-space graph.
    3. No. The smoother is allowed to use later observations that were unavailable online.
    4. A next-state value can have positive smoothed mass while the forward predictive law assigns it zero; valid exact models avoid this inconsistency, while approximate supports must be checked.

    The next chapter identifies the principal finite-dimensional closure in which all these integrals reduce to linear algebra.

    2. Linear-Gaussian Filtering¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Multivariate Gaussians, covariance matrices, affine maps, and Chapter 1.

    Learning objectives. Derive Kalman prediction and correction from Gaussian conditioning; state the exactness boundary; compute predictive likelihoods and a complete scalar example; and distinguish algebraically equivalent covariance, Joseph, square-root, and information implementations from numerically identical finite programs.

    The closure assumptions¤

    Consider the time-varying controlled model

    \[ \begin{aligned} z_t&=A_tz_{t-1}+B_tu_t+a_t+q_t, &q_t&\sim\mathcal N(0,Q_t),\\ y_t&=C_tz_t+d_t+r_t, &r_t&\sim\mathcal N(0,R_t), \end{aligned} \]

    with a Gaussian initial state and mutually independent Gaussian noise terms. Linear prediction maps a Gaussian to a Gaussian. Multiplication by a linear Gaussian likelihood produces another Gaussian. This closure—not the words state-space model—makes the filter exact.

    With non-Gaussian initial or noise laws, the same matrix recursion can still be the best linear mean-square estimator under suitable moment assumptions, but it does not generally return the complete Bayesian posterior. With nonlinear maps, even Gaussian noise does not preserve Gaussian closure.

    Prediction¤

    Write the filtered belief at time \(t-1\) as \(\mathcal N(m_{t-1\mid t-1},P_{t-1\mid t-1})\). Affine Gaussian propagation gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} m_{t\mid t-1} &=A_tm_{t-1\mid t-1}+B_tu_t+a_t,\\ P_{t\mid t-1} &=A_tP_{t-1\mid t-1}A_t^\top+Q_t. \end{aligned} \]

    The predicted observation and innovation covariance are

    \[ \widehat y_{t\mid t-1}=C_tm_{t\mid t-1}+d_t, \qquad S_t=C_tP_{t\mid t-1}C_t^\top+R_t. \]

    The residual \(e_t=y_t-\widehat y_{t\mid t-1}\) is called the innovation only relative to this prediction. Its temporal whiteness and covariance \(S_t\) are model-dependent diagnostic claims, not properties of every residual sequence.

    Correction from one joint Gaussian¤

    Before observing \(y_t\), the joint predictive law is

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}z_t\\y_t\end{bmatrix} \Bigm|y_{1:t-1} \sim \mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}m_{t\mid t-1}\\\widehat y_{t\mid t-1}\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} P_{t\mid t-1}&P_{t\mid t-1}C_t^\top\\ C_tP_{t\mid t-1}&S_t \end{bmatrix} \right). \]

    Conditioning this Gaussian gives the Kalman gain and posterior mean:

    \[ K_t=P_{t\mid t-1}C_t^\top S_t^{-1}, \qquad m_{t\mid t}=m_{t\mid t-1}+K_te_t. \]

    The compact covariance update is \(P_{t\mid t}=P_{t\mid t-1}-K_tS_tK_t^\top\). In exact arithmetic it equals \((I-K_tC_t)P_{t\mid t-1}\). A form that retains both noise contributions is

    \[ P_{t\mid t} =(I-K_tC_t)P_{t\mid t-1}(I-K_tC_t)^\top +K_tR_tK_t^\top. \]

    This Joseph form is algebraically equivalent when \(K_t\) is the exact gain, but is more resistant to loss of symmetry and positive semidefiniteness in finite arithmetic. Production code solves systems with \(S_t\) rather than forming \(S_t^{-1}\).

    The incremental log likelihood is

    \[ \log \ell_t =-\frac12\left[ n_y\log(2\pi)+\log\det S_t+e_t^\top S_t^{-1}e_t \right]. \]

    A singular \(S_t\) requires a separately justified degenerate-Gaussian treatment; blindly adding jitter changes the observation model.

    Worked scalar filter with two observations¤

    Let

    \[ z_0\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\qquad z_t=z_{t-1}+q_t,\quad q_t\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\qquad y_t=z_t+r_t,\quad r_t\sim\mathcal N(0,1). \]

    For \(y_1=1\), prediction gives \(m_{1\mid0}=0\) and \(P_{1\mid0}=2\). Therefore \(S_1=3\), \(K_1=2/3\), and

    \[ m_{1\mid1}=\frac23, \qquad P_{1\mid1}=\frac23. \]

    At time two, the predictive moments are \(m_{2\mid1}=2/3\) and \(P_{2\mid1}=5/3\). For \(y_2=2\),

    \[ S_2=\frac83, \qquad K_2=\frac58, \qquad e_2=\frac43. \]

    Hence

    \[ m_{2\mid2} =\frac23+\frac58\frac43 =\frac32, \qquad P_{2\mid2}=\frac58. \]

    The two predictive log-density contributions are approximately \(-1.63491\) and \(-1.74269\), so the log likelihood of \((1,2)\) under this fixed model is \(-3.37760\). This is a density value for the observation sequence, not a state estimation error.

    Missing, partial, and correlated observations¤

    If \(y_t\) is entirely missing and the absence is ignorable under the declared model, perform prediction and skip correction; the evidence contribution is zero on the log scale. For a partially observed vector, select the observed rows of \(C_t\), \(d_t\), and \(R_t\). Dropping rows of a correlated \(R_t\) is valid only by taking the corresponding marginal covariance, not by pretending the remaining noise is independent.

    If missingness depends on the unseen value or latent state, skipping the update omits an informative missingness likelihood. The model must include that mechanism or report a sensitivity analysis.

    Numerical representations¤

    Table FILTER.2. Numerical representations
    Representation Stored uncertainty Useful structure Important boundary
    covariance \(P\) intuitive marginal variances and direct prediction subtraction in correction can lose symmetry or positive semidefiniteness
    Joseph covariance \(P\) exposes both propagated state uncertainty and measurement noise improves finite behavior but does not cure misspecification
    square-root \(P=LL^\top\) or an information factor QR/orthogonal transformations preserve factor structure implementation conventions differ; reconstructing \(P\) at every step discards much of the benefit
    information \(\Lambda=P^{-1}\) and \(\eta=\Lambda m\) additive independent measurement factors and sparse precision structure prediction may destroy sparsity and must not use an explicit inverse blindly

    Square-root and factorized filters descend from numerical work on sequential estimation; modern QR-only formulations make the stability objective explicit. They compute the same Gaussian law in exact arithmetic, but roundoff, rank handling, conditioning, and differentiation can make finite programs differ.

    Small covariance is not proof of accuracy

    A filter can become confidently wrong when \(Q_t\) or \(R_t\) is underestimated, the observation map is biased, a latent direction is unobservable, or numerical code silently destroys covariance structure. Posterior covariance is conditional uncertainty under the fitted model, not an empirical error bar certified by its own magnitude.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    The Kalman filter is exact Bayesian filtering for the declared linear-Gaussian model. Its update is Gaussian conditioning, and its predictive normalizer supplies the likelihood. Stable implementations preserve matrix structure and solve systems; they do not infer correctness from a successful matrix inversion.

    Chapter 2 retrieval practice¤

    1. Under what assumptions is the complete Kalman posterior exact?
    2. Why is the Joseph update not a new statistical model?
    3. What should happen when an observation is ignorable and missing?
    4. Why can a covariance-only implementation and a square-root implementation disagree in floating-point arithmetic?
    Complete solutions
    1. Gaussian initial state, affine transitions and emissions, and the declared independent Gaussian noise structure, with well-defined conditioning.
    2. It is an algebraically equivalent covariance computation under the same gain and model; it changes numerical realization, not the target law.
    3. Propagate the state distribution and omit the correction; partial observations use the corresponding marginal observation model.
    4. They perform different finite sequences of factorization, multiplication, and subtraction, so conditioning and roundoff affect them differently even when exact algebra agrees.

    Filtering has used data only through the present. The next chapter adds future evidence without discarding temporal dependence.

    3. Smoothing, Sampling, and Information Forms¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapter 2, Gaussian conditioning, block covariances, and backward messages.

    Learning objectives. Derive the Rauch–Tung–Striebel recursion; distinguish smoothed marginals from correlated path samples; compute a complete scalar smoothing update; use information coordinates without confusing precision with certainty; and explain when temporal scans change parallel depth without changing the probabilistic target.

    Rauch–Tung–Striebel smoothing¤

    After a forward Kalman pass, define the smoothing gain

    \[ J_t =P_{t\mid t}A_{t+1}^\top P_{t+1\mid t}^{-1}. \]

    Again, implementations solve against \(P_{t+1\mid t}\). Starting from the last filtered state, \(m_{T\mid T}\) and \(P_{T\mid T}\), the RTS recursion is

    \[ \begin{aligned} m_{t\mid T} &=m_{t\mid t} +J_t(m_{t+1\mid T}-m_{t+1\mid t}),\\ P_{t\mid T} &=P_{t\mid t} +J_t(P_{t+1\mid T}-P_{t+1\mid t})J_t^\top. \end{aligned} \]

    The correction is driven by how the smoothed next state differs from its one-step prediction. The covariance difference is negative semidefinite under the exact regular model, but careless subtraction can still damage a finite implementation. Square-root smoothers avoid forming the most fragile differences directly.

    Worked continuation: smoothing the first state¤

    Continue the scalar model from Chapter 2. The smoothing gain is

    \[ J_1 =\frac{P_{1\mid1}}{P_{2\mid1}} =\frac{2/3}{5/3} =\frac25. \]

    The smoothed time-one mean is

    \[ m_{1\mid2} =\frac23+\frac25\left(\frac32-\frac23\right) =1, \]

    and the smoothed variance is

    \[ P_{1\mid2} =\frac23 +\left(\frac25\right)^2 \left(\frac58-\frac53\right) =\frac12. \]

    The future observation moves the earlier mean from \(2/3\) to \(1\) and reduces its variance from \(2/3\) to \(1/2\). The lag-one smoothed covariance is \(P_{1,2\mid2}=J_1P_{2\mid2}=1/4\). That cross-time covariance matters for path uncertainty and parameter learning; the two marginal Gaussians alone do not determine it.

    Forward filtering, backward sampling¤

    RTS produces smoothed Gaussian moments. To draw a coherent trajectory, first sample

    \[ z_T\sim\mathcal N(m_{T\mid T},P_{T\mid T}), \]

    then move backward using

    \[ z_t\mid z_{t+1},y_{1:T} \sim \mathcal N\!\left( m_{t\mid t}+J_t(z_{t+1}-m_{t+1\mid t}), P_{t\mid t}-J_tP_{t+1\mid t}J_t^\top \right). \]

    In the scalar example, conditioning on a sampled \(z_2\) gives variance \(2/5\). If \(z_2=3/2\), the backward conditional mean is \(1\). Drawing each smoothed marginal independently would destroy the \(1/4\) cross-time covariance and would not sample from \(p(z_{1:2}\mid y_{1:2})\).

    This forward-filtering backward-sampling construction also supports blocked state updates inside MCMC for parameter-uncertain or switching models. Its correctness is conditional on the parameters and model used in that block.

    Information coordinates and factor addition¤

    A nonsingular Gaussian can be written in moment coordinates \((m,P)\) or information coordinates

    \[ \Lambda=P^{-1}, \qquad \eta=\Lambda m. \]

    For the linear observation \(y=Cz+d+r\) with \(r\sim\mathcal N(0,R)\), the measurement update is additive:

    \[ \Lambda_{t\mid t} =\Lambda_{t\mid t-1}+C_t^\top R_t^{-1}C_t, \qquad \eta_{t\mid t} =\eta_{t\mid t-1}+C_t^\top R_t^{-1}(y_t-d_t). \]

    This is the Gaussian factor-product rule. It is valuable when observation factors are sparse or arrive from several conditionally independent sensors. Prediction is not generally additive in the same coordinates and can fill in a sparse precision matrix. Choosing information form therefore follows the linear-algebra structure, not a belief that precision parameters are inherently more Bayesian.

    In two-filter smoothing, a forward message and a backward information message are multiplied at each time. Boundary conditions and double counting require care: the local observation factor must belong to exactly one side or be corrected explicitly.

    Sufficient statistics for learning¤

    Linear-Gaussian EM does not need only \(m_{t\mid T}\) and \(P_{t\mid T}\). Its expected complete-data log likelihood uses

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E[z_t\mid y]&=m_{t\mid T},\\ \mathbb E[z_tz_t^\top\mid y] &=P_{t\mid T}+m_{t\mid T}m_{t\mid T}^\top,\\ \mathbb E[z_tz_{t-1}^\top\mid y] &=P_{t,t-1\mid T}+m_{t\mid T}m_{t-1\mid T}^\top. \end{aligned} \]

    Omitting lagged cross-moments changes the transition-parameter update. Direct likelihood differentiation instead differentiates through the filtering recursion; it targets the same fixed-model likelihood only when both routes use consistent parameterization and numerical semantics.

    Sequential work and parallel depth¤

    The ordinary forward and backward passes perform \(O(T)\) sequential steps. Associative formulations compose local filtering or smoothing elements with a binary operator, permitting prefix scans with \(O(\log T)\) parallel depth and \(O(T)\) total work under an idealized parallel machine. The temporal-parallel smoothing construction applies this idea to general Bayesian recursions and specializes it to the linear-Gaussian case.

    This is an implementation equivalence under an associative construction. It does not make a nonlinear approximation exact, reduce total arithmetic to \(O(\log T)\), or guarantee wall-clock speedup when matrices, memory transfers, and device occupancy dominate. Parallel reduction order can also change floating-point results.

    Independent smoothed draws are not a smoothed path

    Correct one-time marginals do not determine cross-time dependence. A path metric, transition residual, or downstream simulator can fail even when every independently sampled time slice has the right marginal histogram.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    RTS smoothing is exact Gaussian backward conditioning, and backward simulation turns its conditional kernels into correlated trajectories. Information form adds Gaussian observation factors naturally; sufficient-statistic learning also needs lagged moments. Parallel scans change execution structure while preserving the declared algebra only under their associativity and numerical contracts.

    4. Nonlinear Gaussian Approximations¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 2–3, Taylor expansion, Gaussian moments, numerical quadrature, and KL divergence.

    Learning objectives. Locate the approximation made by EKF, iterated EKF, sigma-point, cubature, quadrature, and general Gaussian filters; derive the joint-moment update; identify cases where accurate moments still yield a poor posterior representation; and select diagnostics that test approximation rather than merely successful execution.

    The nonlinear model and the representation decision¤

    Let

    \[ z_t=f_t(z_{t-1},u_t)+q_t, \qquad y_t=h_t(z_t)+r_t, \]

    with Gaussian additive noise for the moment. Nonlinearity makes the predictive and posterior laws non-Gaussian in general. A Gaussian filter chooses to retain only a mean and covariance after each stage. Three questions must then be separated:

    1. Which nonlinear moments are being approximated?
    2. How are those moments estimated?
    3. Why is one Gaussian an adequate representation of the resulting belief?

    A better answer to question 2 does not settle question 3.

    Extended Kalman filtering: pointwise linearization¤

    The EKF linearizes the transition around the previous filtered mean and the observation around the current predicted mean:

    \[ F_t=\left.\frac{\partial f_t}{\partial z}\right|_{m_{t-1\mid t-1}}, \qquad H_t=\left.\frac{\partial h_t}{\partial z}\right|_{m_{t\mid t-1}}. \]

    It propagates

    \[ \begin{aligned} m_{t\mid t-1}&=f_t(m_{t-1\mid t-1},u_t),\\ P_{t\mid t-1}&=F_tP_{t-1\mid t-1}F_t^\top+Q_t, \end{aligned} \]

    then applies a Kalman-style correction with predicted observation \(h_t(m_{t\mid t-1})\), Jacobian \(H_t\), and \(S_t=H_tP_{t\mid t-1}H_t^\top+R_t\).

    This is local Taylor approximation, not exact Bayesian conditioning. The reported covariance does not automatically include neglected curvature. Linearization can be poor when the belief is broad, the map has strong curvature, the state lies near a coordinate singularity, or multiple regions of state space explain the same observation.

    An iterated EKF relinearizes the same measurement around successive state estimates. With prior \((m^-,P^-)\), one common Gauss–Newton form is

    \[ m^{(i+1)} =m^-+K^{(i)} \left[y-h(m^{(i)})+H^{(i)}(m^{(i)}-m^-)\right]. \]

    The iteration searches for a local posterior approximation. It must not shrink the covariance as though each relinearization were an independent repeat of the observation. Convergence is local and can depend on initialization, damping, and coordinates.

    The extended smoother uses linearized transition models in a backward pass. Iterated smoothers relinearize around a whole trajectory and are closely related to structured Gauss–Newton or Laplace approximations; they do not inherit global optimality from the linear RTS formula.

    Joint-moment Gaussian filtering¤

    A broader construction approximates the predictive joint law of state and observation by a Gaussian. Let

    \[ \mu_y=\mathbb E[h(Z)], \qquad S_y=\operatorname{Cov}(h(Z))+R, \qquad C_{zy}=\operatorname{Cov}(Z,h(Z)), \]

    where \(Z\sim\mathcal N(m^-,P^-)\). Gaussian conditioning then gives

    \[ m=m^-+C_{zy}S_y^{-1}(y-\mu_y), \qquad P=P^--C_{zy}S_y^{-1}C_{zy}^\top. \]

    The update is exact for the fitted joint Gaussian. Its relationship to the original nonlinear posterior is a shared-moment approximation, not an algebraic equivalence of distributions.

    Statistical linear regression expresses the same moment fit as

    \[ h(Z)\approx a+BZ+\varepsilon, \qquad B=C_{yz}(P^-)^{-1}, \qquad a=\mu_y-Bm^-, \]

    with a residual covariance chosen to reproduce \(S_y\). Unlike an EKF tangent, this linear model is fitted over the current distribution. Changing the belief changes the regression.

    Estimating transformed Gaussian moments¤

    Table FILTER.3. Estimating transformed Gaussian moments
    Method Moment construction Strength Boundary
    Taylor / EKF function value and Jacobian at one point few model calls; derivatives expose local sensitivity ignores higher-order variation over the belief
    unscented transform deterministic weighted sigma points matching selected input moments derivative-free and compact weights and scaling conventions matter; no universal parameter setting or accuracy ordering
    cubature Kalman filter spherical–radial cubature points systematic third-degree rule with point count linear in state dimension degree exactness for polynomial integration is not posterior exactness

    The unscented-filter construction and cubature Kalman filter are specific deterministic integration rules. “Derivative free” does not mean assumption free, and polynomial exactness of a quadrature rule does not imply that a one-Gaussian posterior represents separated modes.

    Sigma-point smoothers reuse approximate forward joint moments to define a backward Gaussian conditional. The smoother can therefore amplify a forward moment error; it does not repair a lost mode by using future data.

    Worked failure case: correct moments can still hide the answer¤

    Let the predictive state be \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and observe

    \[ Y=Z^2+\varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,r). \]

    The EKF linearizes at zero, where \(h(0)=0\) and \(h'(0)=0\). It predicts no state–observation coupling and performs no update.

    Exact Gaussian moments give a more accurate predictive summary:

    \[ \mathbb E[Y]=1, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(Y)=2+r, \qquad \operatorname{Cov}(Z,Y)=\mathbb E[Z^3]=0. \]

    Yet the joint-moment Gaussian gain is still zero. For an observation near \(y=4\) and small \(r\), the true posterior is approximately split between states near \(-2\) and \(2\):

    \[ p(z\mid y) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{z^2}{2} -\frac{(y-z^2)^2}{2r}\right). \]

    Its mean is zero by symmetry, so a mean-only diagnostic can report success while a one-Gaussian belief entirely misses the two explanations. This example separates three failures: EKF misestimates transformed moments, a moment filter can estimate them correctly, and Gaussian conditioning still cannot represent the posterior geometry.

    Coordinates, constraints, and geometry¤

    Angles, rotations, positive scales, simplices, and manifolds should not be treated as unconstrained Euclidean vectors merely because a covariance matrix can be written. Local-coordinate or invariant filters must declare the group action, retraction, error coordinates, and chart transitions. Renormalizing a quaternion after a Euclidean update is a projection step whose uncertainty semantics require analysis.

    Hard bounds can truncate or split a posterior. Clipping the Gaussian mean does not condition the distribution on the feasible set. Use a constrained model, truncated update, mixture, particles, or another declared approximation when boundary mass matters.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    EKF approximates nonlinear maps locally. Sigma-point, cubature, quadrature, and Monte Carlo methods approximate moments over a distribution. General Gaussian filtering then conditions a fitted joint Gaussian. These are distinct approximation layers, and none guarantees that a single Gaussian represents a multimodal, skewed, bounded, or manifold-valued posterior.

    4. No. It describes integration accuracy for a function class; nonlinear Bayes conditioning and the chosen posterior family add separate errors.

    5. Projection, Mixture, Ensemble, and Robust Filters¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapter 4, mixture distributions, forward and reverse KL, importance weighting, and normalizing flows.

    Learning objectives. Derive assumed-density filtering as predict–update–project; prove the KL direction for Gaussian moment matching; explain mixture growth in switching systems; distinguish ensembles from weighted particles; and audit robust and flow-based filters by the law each construction changes or approximates.

    Assumed-density filtering¤

    Let \(\mathcal Q\) be a tractable family. Suppose \(q_{t-1}\in\mathcal Q\) approximates the previous filter. ADF performs:

    1. predict through the transition using \(q_{t-1}\);
    2. update that approximate prediction by the exact new likelihood when the local calculation is available; and
    3. project the resulting distribution back into \(\mathcal Q\).

    Writing the locally updated distribution as \(\widetilde p_t\), the usual moment projection is

    \[ q_t =\arg\min_{q\in\mathcal Q} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\widetilde p_t\|q). \]

    For a regular exponential family, the optimum matches the sufficient-statistic moments when they exist and the optimum is attainable. With a Gaussian family, this means matching mean and covariance.

    The KL direction matters. For \(q=\mathcal N(m,S)\),

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) =\text{constant} +\frac12\log\det S +\frac12\mathbb E_p[(X-m)^\top S^{-1}(X-m)]. \]

    Differentiating shows that the optimum is \(m=\mathbb E_p[X]\) and \(S=\operatorname{Cov}_p(X)\). This is not the generic solution of \(\min_qD_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)\).

    Worked mixture projection and its failure¤

    Consider

    \[ p(x)=\frac12\mathcal N(x;-3,1)+\frac12\mathcal N(x;3,1). \]

    Its mean is zero and its variance is

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X) =1+\frac12(-3)^2+\frac12(3)^2 =10. \]

    The forward-KL Gaussian projection is therefore \(\mathcal N(0,10)\). It covers both components but assigns density to the low-probability gap around zero. For well-separated components, a reverse-KL Gaussian optimization can instead settle near one component, depending on initialization and parameterization. Neither projection preserves the original bimodality.

    Projection errors accumulate and can depend on order

    ADF projects after every factor. Once a tail, mode, or dependence is discarded, a later observation is conditioned on the projected belief. Reordering nonconjugate factors can therefore change the approximation even though exact Bayes multiplication is order invariant. Expectation propagation revisits approximate factors, but convergence and accuracy are separate questions.

    The corrected expectation-propagation record connects ADF, moment projection, and iterative local factor updates. It should not be summarized as a general guarantee that EP converges or dominates variational inference.

    Switching systems and Gaussian-sum filters¤

    In a switching linear dynamical system, a discrete mode selects local linear dynamics. Conditional on a complete mode path, Kalman inference is exact. After marginalizing paths, the state belief is a Gaussian mixture whose component count can multiply at every step.

    A Gaussian-sum filter retains several components and periodically merges, prunes, or caps them. For mixture weights \(\pi_k\), means \(m_k\), and covariances \(P_k\), moment-preserving collapse gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} m&=\sum_k\pi_km_k,\\ P&=\sum_k\pi_k\left[P_k+(m_k-m)(m_k-m)^\top\right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The between-component term is essential. Omitting it makes uncertainty shrink when modes disagree most.

    Generalized pseudo-Bayes and interacting-multiple-model filters differ in when they mix mode-conditioned beliefs and how many Kalman updates they retain. They are structured approximations, not exact inference merely because every local submodel is linear Gaussian. Pruning requires a lost-mass or query-error audit; keeping the top component is not probability-preserving renormalization over the original path space.

    Ensemble Kalman filtering¤

    An ensemble Kalman filter propagates \(N\) state replicas through the transition, then estimates forecast and state–observation covariances from the ensemble. In a stochastic observation-update form,

    \[ z_t^{a,i} =z_t^{f,i} +\widehat C_{zy} (\widehat C_{yy}+R)^{-1} \left(y_t+\varepsilon_t^i-h(z_t^{f,i})\right), \]

    where \(\varepsilon_t^i\sim\mathcal N(0,R)\) and sample covariances define the gain. Deterministic square-root ensemble updates transform anomalies without perturbed observations; the two finite ensembles are not identical.

    The original ensemble data-assimilation construction uses Monte Carlo forecast error statistics. For linear-Gaussian systems, ensemble estimates converge to the Kalman quantities under suitable large-ensemble conditions. Existing convergence results must be read with their model assumptions. In a nonlinear system, an infinite ensemble does not automatically turn the covariance-style analysis update into the exact nonlinear Bayesian posterior.

    An EnKF ensemble is not a particle filter in disguise:

    Table FILTER.4. Ensemble Kalman filtering
    Property EnKF Particle filter
    correction affine covariance-based movement likelihood or proposal importance weights, often followed by resampling
    finite representation usually equally weighted ensemble weighted empirical measure before resampling
    multimodality can be distorted or collapsed by affine update representable if particles reach and retain the modes
    common high-dimensional device localization and inflation factorization, localization, tempering, transport, or problem-specific proposals
    exact nonlinear Bayes limit not generic consistency can hold as particle count grows under support and regularity conditions

    Covariance localization and inflation can control finite-ensemble sampling error, but they alter the update. Their radius, taper, inflation schedule, and tuning data belong in a reproducible protocol.

    Robust observations change the update contract¤

    Heavy-tailed likelihoods, mixture contamination models, latent outlier scales, and generalized Bayes losses address different robustness goals. A Student \(t\) observation law remains an ordinary probabilistic model but usually breaks Gaussian posterior closure. A generalized Bayesian update changes how loss is converted into posterior weight and need not correspond to the original data likelihood.

    The generalized-Bayes robust filter is a peer-reviewed construction with declared robustness assumptions and extended/ensemble implementations. It is evidence for that method—not a license to clip every innovation or to call a heuristic covariance inflation Bayesian robustness.

    Outlier robustness can reduce sensitivity to bad measurements while reducing efficiency under the nominal Gaussian model. Evaluate both contaminated and clean regimes, temporal bursts, leverage directions, and uncertainty coverage.

    Flow-based filters: three different roles¤

    Normalizing flows can enter state-space inference in at least three ways:

    1. transform observations or states into coordinates with a tractable local conditional;
    2. parameterize a flexible filtering or smoothing approximation; or
    3. construct a proposal or transport inside a weighted particle method.

    Only the first can retain an analytic Kalman calculation, and only when the invertible map, Jacobian, latent linear-Gaussian law, and conditioning direction support the claimed query. A flexible approximate posterior does not become exact because its density is normalized. A learned proposal inside SMC remains corrected by its importance ratio.

    Normalizing Kalman Filters provide one peer-reviewed model composition for multivariate time series. The result belongs simultaneously to a state-space model, a flow transformation, and an inference construction; its tractability claims do not transfer to an arbitrary flow attached to an arbitrary nonlinear filter.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    ADF projects after local Bayes updates, and forward-KL Gaussian projection matches moments. Gaussian sums postpone collapse, EnKF estimates covariance updates with ensembles, robust filters change likelihood or update semantics, and flows can transform models, approximate beliefs, or improve proposals. Every method must name which probability law and representation supplies its guarantee.

    6. Particles, Learning, Validation, and Method Choice¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–5, importance sampling, SMC, variational inference, MCMC, automatic differentiation, and proper scoring rules.

    Learning objectives. Compare state-space inference by target and returned representation; separate differentiable learning from finite-filter correctness; validate means, uncertainty, paths, evidence, and compute; place recent learned and control-based filters at the right abstraction layer; and choose a method with an auditable approximation ledger.

    Beyond a single Gaussian¤

    The Bayesian recursion can be approximated with several representations:

    Table FILTER.5. Beyond a single Gaussian
    Representation Update mechanism Principal strength Principal failure
    grid or basis numerical integration and normalized tables transparent multimodality in very low dimension state-space resolution grows exponentially with dimension
    weighted particles proposal, importance correction, and optional resampling flexible nonlinear and non-Gaussian beliefs weight and ancestral degeneracy; severe dimension dependence

    The canonical MCMC chapter owns invariance, blocked versus scalar updates, warmup, and MCSE. In this chapter, trajectory MCMC specializes those rules to a coupled latent path, where one-site updates can deteriorate as sequence length grows.

    Particle filtering, resampling, proposal design, particle smoothing, and particle MCMC are derived in the Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods foundation. The stochastic-process chapter specializes the construction to irregular-time and continuous-transition laws, while Latent Variables and Inference compares particle objectives with other posterior approximations. The key boundary here is that resampling improves current weight allocation while coalescing ancestral paths. A filter can estimate the current state well and still give poor early-time smoothing trajectories.

    Rao–Blackwellized filters integrate a tractable conditional submodel exactly and sample the remainder. This reduces Monte Carlo variance when the decomposition is valid; it does not make the sampled variables exact or erase particle-support requirements.

    Differentiating through a filter¤

    A differentiable filter is a parameterized computation. Gradients can update transition, observation, proposal, representation, or initialization parameters using supervised state loss, predictive likelihood, an ELBO, an SMC bound, or another declared objective. These objectives need not have the same population optimum.

    For a deterministic Kalman recursion, reverse-mode differentiation targets the finite sequence of solves and updates actually executed. Near-singular covariances, adaptive jitter, clipping, stopped gradients, or custom factorizations become part of that derivative program.

    For particles, resampling introduces discrete ancestor choices. Straight-through, soft-resampling, score-function, transport, marginalization, and stopped-resampling estimators have different bias and variance. End-to-end differentiability therefore does not prove that the gradient is unbiased for the exact state-space likelihood.

    Differentiable Particle Filters demonstrate one algorithm-prior construction with learned motion and measurement components. A 2025 peer-reviewed learned mixture proposal and transition method provides another. Their experimental results belong to the named objectives, data, representations, particle counts, and baselines—not to differentiable filtering as a universal ranking.

    Learned analytic structure and expressive beliefs¤

    Learned filters occupy several distinct design points:

    • a neural transition or observation map inside EKF, UKF, or a moment filter;
    • an encoder that emits parameters of one Gaussian or mixture belief;
    • an invertible map that preserves a declared tractable calculation;
    • a learned proposal inside an importance-corrected particle filter; or
    • a recurrent belief representation trained only for a downstream query.

    The 2025 ICML Deep Bayesian Filter introduces an additional latent variable, constrains latent transition structure, and learns an inverse observation operator so that its stated recursive computation is analytic within that construction. This is a hybrid system composition: learned structure is designed around a tractable posterior interface. It does not establish that every learned latent coordinate has a Gaussian true posterior or identifiable physical meaning.

    As of the verification date, Neural Bayesian Filtering is a revised submission, not peer-reviewed evidence, for embedding and tracking multimodal beliefs. It is useful as a preprint research direction only; its status and experiments must not be narrated as a settled field result.

    A current smoothing direction¤

    A 2026 peer-reviewed probabilistic optimal-control representation of smoothing represents a posterior trajectory law through the closed-loop behavior of a synthetic controlled system and reports equally weighted forward trajectories under its constructed policy. This is a distributional correspondence under the paper's construction, not an algebraic identity between every smoother and every control problem. The policy approximation, particle representation, dynamic-programming computation, and model-learning experiment retain their own errors and compute ledger.

    A June 2026 Bayesian-filtering approach to learned Lagrangian dynamics is a preprint example of joint physical-model and state learning with Gaussian-approximation filters. It is included to mark a research interface—structured mechanics plus approximate filtering—not as evidence of broad superiority or mature reproduction.

    Validation starts with the query¤

    No single residual plot validates a filter. Use a ladder:

    1. Algebraic oracle. Compare with enumeration or dense Gaussian conditioning on a tiny model.
    2. Limiting cases. Recover the linear model from nonlinear code; skip an absent observation; test nearly noiseless and uninformative observations; verify that permuting equivalent independent sensor factors changes no exact result.
    3. Simulation calibration. Draw states and observations from the declared model, then measure coverage, rank behavior, and path statistics.
    4. Predictive checks. Evaluate held-out log predictive density or another proper score, calibration by horizon, and residual dependence.
    5. Application queries. Test the downstream forecast, event probability, control cost, or scientific functional, not only state RMSE.
    6. Model criticism. Check whether simulated observations resemble relevant real-data structure. Inference calibration under a simulator does not validate the simulator.

    For a \(d_y\)-dimensional linear-Gaussian innovation, the normalized innovation squared is

    \[ \operatorname{NIS}_t=e_t^\top S_t^{-1}e_t. \]

    Under the correctly specified regular model it has a \(\chi^2_{d_y}\) marginal law, with additional dependence considerations for a time series. If true states are available in simulation, the normalized estimation error squared is

    \[ \operatorname{NEES}_t =(z_t-m_{t\mid t})^\top P_{t\mid t}^{-1}(z_t-m_{t\mid t}), \]

    with an analogous \(\chi^2_{d_z}\) reference under its assumptions. Repeated time points from one trajectory are not independent replicates. Tune-on-test inflation can make these diagnostics look calibrated without producing a generalizing model.

    Also record:

    • innovation mean, autocorrelation, tail behavior, and condition-specific structure;
    • marginal and simultaneous coverage across horizons;
    • effective sample size, unique ancestors, mode occupancy, and path diversity for particles;
    • covariance symmetry, factorization failures, minimum eigenvalues, and jitter;
    • convergence residuals and initialization sensitivity for iterated methods;
    • predictive likelihood with the exact approximation status of each normalizer; and
    • wall-clock time, peak memory, model evaluations, linear solves, factorizations, particles or sigma points, sequential depth, and hardware.

    Worked method-selection audit¤

    Suppose a two-dimensional tracking simulator has a bearing-only observation. A single measurement admits two separated range hypotheses, but repeated motion usually resolves the ambiguity. A defensible study would:

    1. enumerate or grid a reduced two-step instance as a reference;
    2. compare EKF, sigma-point, Gaussian mixture, and particle filters with matched transition and observation models;
    3. report current-state RMSE and posterior mass on both range hypotheses;
    4. evaluate path samples after the ambiguity resolves;
    5. repeat under clean data, outliers, altered motion, and particle or mixture budgets; and
    6. distinguish model calls and wall time from nominal update count.

    An EKF can have good mean RMSE after resolution while being overconfident during ambiguity. A particle filter can preserve both modes yet suffer ancestor collapse. A mixture filter can work well until pruning deletes the eventual correct branch. These are different failure mechanisms, so one aggregate RMSE cannot select the method.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Filtering and smoothing are posterior queries over a state-space generator. Exact Gaussian closure, projection, deterministic quadrature, ensembles, mixtures, variational paths, particles, learned beliefs, and control-based representations place approximation in different locations. A research-quality comparison identifies that location, validates the query at finite compute, and separates inference error from model misspecification.
