    Research and Production¤

    Research and Production: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the evaluation contract from Chapter 4 and the six relationship labels from the landscape guide. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. distinguish a pure GAN, a diffusion–GAN hybrid, and adversarial post-training or distillation;
    2. label peer-reviewed results separately from preprints and system reports;
    3. audit a one-step benchmark claim for data, conditioning, metric, NFE, model size, and hardware;
    4. explain why a discriminator can remain useful even when the pretrained model was diffusion- or flow-based; and
    5. construct a deployment record that makes quality–latency comparisons reproducible.

    Progressive Growing¤

    Karras et al. (2018) — PGGAN train GANs by gradually increasing resolution:

    1. Start at 4×4
    2. Stabilize training
    3. Add layers for 8×8
    4. Fade in new layers smoothly
    5. Repeat to target resolution

    Paper-era evidence: the authors reported improved high-resolution training behavior and sample metrics relative to their baselines. Later StyleGAN variants returned to end-to-end fixed-resolution training, so progressive growing is a historical technique rather than a required modern component.

    Self-Attention¤

    SAGAN (Zhang et al., 2019) adds self-attention to capture long-range dependencies:

    \[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V \]

    SAGAN reported improved ImageNet metrics and qualitative long-range structure relative to its convolutional baselines. BigGAN subsequently combined self-attention with large-scale class-conditional residual training; “ancestor” denotes architectural lineage, not a causal ablation by itself.

    BigGAN¤

    BigGAN (Brock, Donahue & Simonyan, 2019) scales GANs to ImageNet with:

    • Larger batch sizes (2048)
    • Larger architectures (more channels)
    • Truncated-normal latent sampling at inference (the truncation trick — trade diversity for fidelity)
    • Orthogonal regularisation on weights
    • Class-conditional batch normalisation

    Established, protocol-bounded result: BigGAN reported IS \(166.3\) and FID \(9.6\) at ImageNet 128×128 for one reported truncation setting, while BigGAN-deep reported other resolution/truncation operating points. Truncation explicitly trades diversity for fidelity, so the score must be paired with its sampling setting and evaluation code.

    3D-Aware Generation¤

    EG3D (Chan et al., 2022) generates 3D-consistent faces from 2D images:

    • Tri-plane hybrid 3D representation for efficient volumetric rendering.
    • Differentiable neural rendering from multi-view.
    • Adversarial training on 2D image collections only.
    • Learns a 3D-aware representation from 2D image supervision and camera assumptions, without ground-truth 3D shapes; that does not guarantee physically correct hidden geometry.

    Successors include GET3D (Gao et al., 2022) and GeNVS (Chan et al., 2023).

    Applications: 3D face synthesis, novel-view synthesis, relighting.

    Adversarial Training: Pure GANs and Diffusion Distillation¤

    Adversarial generative modelling includes two distinct but composable lines of work: (a) developing pure-GAN architectures, regularizers, and convergence analyses, and (b) using adversarial objectives with pretrained diffusion or flow models to distill them into 1–4-step samplers. The shared discriminator machinery does not make the resulting probabilistic models or training pipelines equivalent.

    Pure GAN Architectures and Training¤

    Table GAN.12. Pure GAN Architectures and Training
    Model Year Contribution
    StyleGAN-XL (Sauer et al., 2022) 2022 Peer-reviewed research at publication. Projected discriminators and class conditioning scaled style-based GANs to diverse ImageNet protocols; comparisons are bounded to named resolutions and baselines.
    GigaGAN (Kang et al., 2023) 2023 Peer-reviewed research. A billion-parameter text-to-image GAN with reported 512² and super-resolution latency on the authors' hardware; reproduce latency before transporting it to another stack.
    StyleGAN-T (Sauer et al., 2023) 2023 Peer-reviewed research. Adapts style-based generation to large-scale text conditioning and reports quality–speed comparisons against named contemporaneous baselines.
    R3GAN (Huang et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) 2024 Peer-reviewed research. Combines a regularized relativistic paired loss with a modernized residual backbone. Its local convergence theorem has explicit regularity and neighborhood assumptions; experiments compare favorably with StyleGAN2 and selected GAN/diffusion baselines on FFHQ, ImageNet, CIFAR, and Stacked MNIST.

    GAN-Style Distillation of Diffusion Models¤

    Adversarial losses are also used for few-step post-training or distillation: initialize or supervise a student from a pretrained diffusion/flow model and optionally train a discriminator using real data, teacher outputs, noisy states, or frozen features. These signals are method-specific; a diffusion teacher is not automatically a real-data oracle.

    Table GAN.13. GAN-Style Distillation of Diffusion Models
    Method Year Core idea
    Diffusion-GAN (Wang et al., 2022) 2022 Peer-reviewed. Uses adaptive forward-diffusion noise and timestep conditioning to reduce discriminator overfitting in the evaluated settings; it does not remove overfitting universally.
    DDGAN (Xiao, Kreis & Vahdat, 2022) 2022 Peer-reviewed research. Parameterizes coarse reverse-diffusion transitions with conditional GANs and reports few-step image results under the paper's CIFAR-10, CelebA-HQ, and LSUN protocols. It is an iterative diffusion–GAN composition, not a direct one-call GAN.
    ADD / SDXL Turbo (Sauer et al., 2023) 2023 Technical report / product-associated preprint. Combines score distillation with an adversarial loss in pretrained feature space for one- to four-step sampling. Product behavior and paper checkpoints should not be assumed identical without a versioned model card.
    Diffusion2GAN (Kang et al., 2024) 2024 Preprint. Distills paired teacher trajectories into a conditional one-step student with E-LatentLPIPS and an adversarial loss. Its megapixel and latency results inherit the reported teacher, data, decoder, metric, and hardware protocol.
    DMD2 (Yin et al., 2024 — NeurIPS) 2024 Peer-reviewed research. Removes the regression loss used by DMD, corrects score-estimator mismatch with two time-scale updates, and adds a real-data adversarial loss. The reported FID 1.28 is ImageNet 64² and 8.35 is zero-shot COCO 2014 under the paper's one-step protocols.
    APT — Adversarial Post-Training (Lin et al., 2025) 2025 Preprint. Studies one-step video post-training with an approximated R1 regularizer. Claims about interactivity and comparisons inherit the authors' model, clip, metric, and hardware protocol.
    WaDi (Wang et al., 2026 — CVPR) 2026 Peer-reviewed research. Uses low-rank rotations to parameterize weight-direction changes inside variational score distillation, reporting one-step COCO results with roughly 10% trainable parameters. It is a distillation result, not a pure-GAN result.
    APEX (Liu et al., 2026) 2026 Preprint. Derives a discriminator-free, GAN-aligned correction from condition-shifted flow fields and reports one-step full-parameter and LoRA tuning. “GAN-aligned” is an objective correspondence, not hybrid composition with an external discriminator.
    AAD-1 (Li et al., 2026) 2026 Preprint. Uses a causal autoregressive video generator, bidirectional discriminator, and staged distribution-matching/adversarial training for one-step image-to-video generation; its VBench claims await independent reproduction.

    Diffusion–GAN Hybrids (Architecturally)¤

    Beyond distillation, several architectures interleave diffusion and GANs:

    • DDGAN (Xiao et al., 2022) parameterises each reverse step with a GAN.
    • Wavelet Diffusion-GAN (Phung et al., 2024) operates in the wavelet domain for cheaper high-resolution training.
    • Flow2GAN (2025) hybridises flow matching with adversarial training for few-step audio generation.

    The throughline is narrower: adversarial objectives remain useful both in pure implicit generators and as one component of some diffusion/flow post-training systems. Consistency, score, transport, regression, and the discriminator-free APEX construction demonstrate that few-step generation does not require an external discriminator.

    Production Considerations¤

    Deployment Challenges¤

    Record parameter and active-parameter counts, serialized and peak resident memory, latent and output resolution, decoder or renderer cost, precision, compiler, batch size, warm-up, and exact hardware. “Real time,” a checkpoint size in megabytes, or a GPU count has no portable meaning without this record. Compression, quantization, pruning, distillation, and runtime-specific compilation can each alter quality and numerical behavior and therefore require post-optimization evaluation.

    Monitoring and Maintenance¤

    Quality Drift:

    Monitor generated samples over time for:

    • Artifacts or distortions
    • Mode collapse
    • Distribution shift

    A/B Testing:

    Compare model versions using:

    • FID scores on held-out data
    • Human evaluation studies
    • Production metrics (click-through, engagement)

    Ethical Considerations¤

    Responsible Use

    Deepfakes and Misinformation:

    • GANs can generate realistic fake images/videos
    • Require watermarking and detection systems
    • Need clear disclosure when using synthetic media

    Bias and Fairness:

    • GANs inherit biases from training data
    • May amplify stereotypes or lack diversity
    • Require diverse, representative datasets

    Privacy:

    • Memorization of training data possible
    • Differential privacy techniques recommended
    • Careful handling of sensitive data

    Open Research Directions¤

    Open Research Problems¤

    • Scaling pure GANs: GigaGAN studies billion-parameter text-to-image adversarial training, while R3GAN studies a simpler modern baseline at different scales. Whether either recipe remains favorable on broader prompt distributions and matched compute is open.
    • One-step video: APT and AAD-1 report promising one-step protocols, but long-horizon coherence, motion diversity, prompt alignment, memory, and independent reproduction remain open.
    • Convergence theory beyond local: R3GAN gives provable local convergence, but global convergence under realistic data + architectures is still open.
    • Pretrained-feature discriminators: ADD and related methods reuse frozen or pretrained vision features, while DMD2 uses a separate real/fake discriminator alongside score estimators. Which feature sources, update rules, and data regimes transfer reliably remains open.
    • Adaptive stability mechanisms: Learning or controlling update schedules, regularization, and critic difficulty without hiding new state variables or eliminating the need for held-out diagnostics.

    Research Directions Across System Types¤

    Adversarial distillation as one fast-sampler route:

    • ADD, DMD2, and Diffusion2GAN establish the discriminator as one available few-step post-training ingredient. Many production diffusion deployments and non-adversarial distillers do not use one.
    • Adversarial signals are being studied with diffusion, flow, autoregressive video, and consistency-style students; which combinations remain useful at scale is empirical.

    Retrieval-Augmented and Multimodal GANs:

    • Combine generators with retrieval systems for few-shot adaptation.
    • Cross-modal (text-image-audio-video) generation with shared latent spaces.

    Efficient Fine-Tuning:

    • Parameter-efficient adapters are being explored across modern generators; StyleGAN-NADA is an earlier CLIP-guided domain-adaptation method, not itself evidence that LoRA is intrinsic to GAN fine-tuning.
    • Few-shot domain adaptation from foundation models.

    Quality-Aware Training:

    • DINOv2 / SigLIP / CLIP features as discriminator backbones (ADD).
    • Wavelet-driven discriminators (Wavelet Diffusion-GAN).
    • Learned perceptual losses tuned to specific generators.

    Hybrid Approaches¤

    Hybrid systems are an important branch, not a foregone universal endpoint:

    • GAN + Diffusion — one family of fast-inference post-training methods (ADD, DMD2, APT), alongside non-adversarial alternatives.
    • GAN + VAE — VAE-GAN style perceptual codecs (the SD-VAE training recipe).
    • GAN + Transformers / DiTs — adversarial post-training can operate on transformer backbones; production fitness still requires system-specific quality, safety, latency, and cost evidence.

    Worked example: audit a one-step headline¤

    Suppose Method A reports FID 8.0 at one NFE and Teacher B reports FID 7.5 at 50 NFEs. Before concluding that A preserves teacher quality at 50× speed, recover: checkpoint and parameter count; latent decoder calls; guidance; prompt or class conditioning; data and split; feature extractor and sample count; teacher sampling solver; hardware, precision, batch size, compilation and warm-up; wall-clock distribution; and independent reproduction status. If A uses a larger backbone or expensive decoder, “50×” from NFE may vanish. If its FID uses a different reference set, the quality comparison is invalid. The honest result may still be valuable, but it is narrower.

    Independent practice and assessment map: research and production¤

    Treat every family name, benchmark number, and latency headline as a claim with a system boundary, evidence level, and reproducible measurement protocol.

    P1 — Classify a hybrid, audit its evidence, and write its deployment record¤

    A report describes a one-step student initialized from a diffusion model, trained with a teacher score term and a real/fake discriminator, then served through a separately trained latent decoder.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether the system is a pure GAN, whether one top-level student step equals one network evaluation or one decoder-free sample, whether adversarial training guarantees teacher coverage, and whether a preprint latency transfers to another accelerator. Attach the missing composition or protocol detail to each answer.
    2. P1.2 — System and evidence classification. Classify DCGAN, R3GAN, DDGAN, ADD, DMD2, WaDi, APEX, and the described student as pure adversarial generators, architectural hybrids, adversarial distillation/post-training, or discriminator-free objective correspondences. For each, record the guide's evidence maturity and the exact claim that does not transfer by family name.
    3. P1.3 — One-step benchmark audit. Given student and teacher FID/NFE headlines, construct the minimum comparison table covering data/split, conditioning, guidance, model and active parameters, student/teacher/critic calls, decoder and renderer calls, sample count, feature extractor, precision, batch, compiler/warm-up, hardware, latency distribution, memory, and independent reproduction. State the conditions required to report a speedup ratio.
    4. P1.4 — Deployment and maintenance record. Define versioned quality, per-class coverage, conditional alignment, memorization/privacy, safety, latency, throughput, peak memory, power, and drift gates. Specify rollback thresholds, A/B-test denominators, data-lineage records, and post-quantization validation for the whole composed system.
    5. P1.5 — Transfer. Move the system to one-step conditional video generation for a safety-critical simulation workload. Add temporal/motion coverage, long-horizon coherence, causal-condition fidelity, renderer cost, streaming memory, subgroup failure, and independent-reproduction tests; state which image headline claims become invalid.

    Open Research Directions: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    “GAN” can name a complete implicit generator, an adversarial term inside a hybrid, or an objective correspondence without a discriminator. Research literacy requires naming the system composition and attaching an evidence level and protocol to every result.

    Retrieval practice: research claims
    1. Is WaDi a pure GAN?
    2. Why is APEX described as GAN-aligned but discriminator-free?
    3. Which evidence label does AAD-1 carry at this guide's verification date?
    4. Why can one NFE fail to mean one comparable unit of latency?

    Solutions. (1) No; it is a one-step diffusion-distillation method using variational score distillation and parameter-efficient weight adaptation. (2) Its derived update corresponds to an adversarial correction, but it obtains that signal from condition-shifted flow fields rather than an external discriminator. (3) Preprint at the recorded verification date. (4) Evaluations can differ in model size, decoder/renderer calls, guidance, hardware, precision, batching, and compilation.
