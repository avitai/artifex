    Learning path

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    An implicit generator defines a distribution by a sampling program

    \[ z\sim p(z),\qquad x=G_\theta(z), \]

    even when the induced density \(p_\theta(x)\) cannot be evaluated. GANs are the best-known example, but adversarial density-ratio estimation is not part of the definition. This volume studies non-adversarial ways to fit implicit generators: kernel moment matching, implicit maximum likelihood, sliced distances, feature moments, and hybrids.

    Evidence convention

    Population identities, finite-sample estimators, optimization behavior, and complete-system benchmarks are labeled separately. Established denotes mature statistical facts or algorithms; peer-reviewed research denotes archival work; preprint is reserved for non-archival work. “Likelihood-free” never means assumption-free.

    How to read this book

    Read continuously for the comparison. Chapter pages are exact projections of this canonical source.
    Beginner: Chapters 1–3.
    Practitioner: add Chapters 4 and 6.
    Research: derive the estimators and audit which metric, feature space, or nearest-neighbor approximation defines the training signal.

    Chapter map¤

    The sequence deliberately separates the sampler, population discrepancy, finite estimator, gradient program, and evaluation test. Two methods can share one of those objects without sharing the others.

    1. Implicit Distributions and Likelihood-Free Learning¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand pushforward measures, densities versus probability laws, Monte Carlo estimation, and basic statistical distances. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. define deterministic and stochastic implicit generators as sampling programs;
    2. explain when a pushforward lacks a Lebesgue density and when a density may exist but remain unavailable;
    3. separate unconditional generation, conditional generation, and simulation-based inference;
    4. distinguish population identifiability, estimator accuracy, and optimization geometry;
    5. construct a complete misspecified one-dimensional example; and
    6. determine whether a sampling program exposes pathwise, score-function, surrogate, or no direct gradient access.

    If \(G_\theta:\mathbb R^{d_z}\to\mathbb R^{d_x}\) is deterministic, the pushforward law is

    \[ p_\theta=(G_\theta)_\#p_Z. \]

    Sampling is straightforward. Density evaluation may be unavailable because \(G\) is non-invertible, many-to-one, dimension-changing, or has an intractable Jacobian. If \(d_z<d_x\) and no observation noise is added, the distribution may live on a lower-dimensional manifold and have no ordinary Lebesgue density in data space.

    An implicit generator need not be deterministic. More generally,

    \[ Z\sim p_Z,\qquad \epsilon\sim p_\epsilon,\qquad X=G_\theta(Z,\epsilon,C), \]

    defines a conditional law given \(C=c\). Randomness can enter through several layers, a simulator, discrete branching, or an internal Markov process. “Implicit” says that sampling is the primary accessible interface; it does not say the model has no mathematical density. A dimension-preserving noisy generator may have a smooth density that is merely intractable to evaluate.

    Conversely, adding arbitrarily small observation noise changes the measure class. If \(X_0=G(Z)\) lies on a manifold and \(X=X_0+\sigma\epsilon\) with Gaussian noise, \(X\) has a full-dimensional density for \(\sigma>0\). It is a noise-convolved model, not the original singular law, and its likelihood can still be computationally unavailable.

    Implicit Does Not Mean Unprincipled¤

    A model can be specified exactly as a simulator while its likelihood is intractable. Scientific simulators, graphics engines, agent/environment rollouts, and neural generators all induce distributions. Learning then compares real and simulated samples through statistics that can be estimated without pointwise likelihood.

    The central questions are:

    1. Which discrepancies identify equality of the two distributions at population scale?
    2. Can the discrepancy be estimated from finite samples?
    3. Does it provide useful gradients through \(G_\theta\)?
    4. Which modes or features can remain invisible?

    These correspond to three separate contracts:

    • population contract: does \(D(p,q)=0\) imply \(p=q\) in the chosen model and state space?
    • estimation contract: how biased, variable, or computationally expensive is \(\widehat D\) from finite minibatches?
    • optimization contract: does differentiating \(\widehat D(p_{\mathrm{data}},(G_\theta)_\#p_Z)\) provide useful parameter gradients?

    A method can satisfy the first and fail badly on the other two.

    A Comparison-First Map of Sample-Based Learning¤

    An implicit generator does not determine how it should be trained. The learning rule specifies which differences between real and generated samples are visible:

    Table IMPL.1. A Comparison-First Map of Sample-Based Learning
    Route Witness or comparison Is an auxiliary adversary required? Population zero means
    variational \(f\)-divergence an admissible critic with the conjugate penalty \(f^*\) usually, when the witness is parameterized equality of laws for an identifying \(f\) and unrestricted optimum
    neural IPM a constrained learned class, such as approximately 1-Lipschitz critics yes equality only if the declared class separates the relevant laws
    fixed finite moments a declared feature vector \(\phi(x)\) no equality of those feature means only
    fixed-kernel MMD the RKHS unit ball, whose optimum has a closed form no equality of laws for a characteristic kernel on the stated domain

    The full function-class comparison derives these relations. Fixed-feature moment matching is a linear IPM, and fixed-kernel MMD is an RKHS IPM. Neither needs an alternating critic. If the features or kernel are instead learned to maximize discrepancy, an auxiliary game has been introduced and its estimation and optimization contracts must be audited separately.

    This map also prevents a common category error: the optimum of an auxiliary comparison problem, the generator loss evaluated against its current witness, and the trajectory of alternating stochastic updates are three different objects. They may share a population fixed point without producing the same finite-step gradient.

    Three Distinct Likelihood-Free Settings¤

    • Unconditional implicit generation: learn \(G_\theta(z)\) from examples \(x\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}\).
    • Conditional implicit generation: learn \(G_\theta(z,c)\); coverage must be assessed across conditions, not only after pooling.
    • Simulation-based inference: a fixed simulator provides \(x\sim p(x\mid\vartheta)\) and a learned model approximates a posterior, likelihood, or ratio over simulator parameters. This is related but has a different inferential target.

    The distinction is operational. In unconditional or conditional generation, \(\theta\) usually parameterizes the distribution being learned. In simulation-based inference, the simulator may be fixed and \(\vartheta\) is an unknown scientific parameter; the learned object may instead be an emulator, posterior, likelihood, or likelihood ratio. A discrepancy between simulated and observed summaries does not by itself say which inferential object is being estimated.

    Sampling access is not gradient access¤

    The statement “we can sample \(X\sim p_\theta\)” leaves open how \(\theta\) can be learned. If a differentiable reparameterization \(X=G_\theta(\xi)\) is available and a sample loss \(\ell\) is differentiable, the pathwise identity is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \nabla_\theta\mathbb E_\xi[\ell(G_\theta(\xi))] &=\mathbb E_\xi\!\Bigl[ J_{G_\theta}(\xi)^\top \\ &\qquad\nabla_x\ell(G_\theta(\xi)) \Bigr], \end{aligned} \]

    under the usual interchange and integrability conditions. MMD, sliced losses, and selected IMLE distances commonly use this route for continuous neural generators. It requires more than sample access: the realized computation path must be differentiable and retained for automatic differentiation.

    Discrete branching, rejection loops, rendering decisions, external simulators, and hidden stateful code can break that path. The alternatives have different targets and failure modes:

    Table IMPL.2. Sampling access is not gradient access
    Available interface Possible learning route What must be audited
    differentiable reparameterized sample pathwise gradient derivative correctness, discontinuities, generator Jacobian conditioning
    evaluable sampling-trace probability score-function estimator support, variance, baselines, delayed credit
    differentiable relaxation or surrogate pathwise gradient of a changed program relaxation bias and train/deploy mismatch
    simulator calls only finite differences, SPSA, Bayesian/derivative-free optimization, emulator, or likelihood-free inference query budget, stochastic noise, surrogate error, dimensional scaling

    The exhaustive gradient identities live in Gradients and Geometry. The important boundary here is simpler: implicit density does not imply implicit gradient, and sampleable does not imply differentiable. The foundational review Learning in Implicit Generative Models organizes moment matching and hypothesis testing inside a wider likelihood-free landscape; it does not make every simulator end-to-end differentiable.

    Latent coordinates and programs are not identifiable from samples¤

    Even perfect equality of generated and data laws does not identify a unique sampling program. If \(T\) preserves the latent law, so that \(T_\#p_Z=p_Z\), then \(G\) and \(G\circ T\) induce the same observable distribution. Extra unused noise, permuted mixture labels, and measure-preserving latent rotations create further equivalent programs. Therefore a semantic interpretation of a latent coordinate needs supervision, architectural restrictions, interventions, or another identifying assumption; distribution matching alone supplies none.

    This is different from population identification by a discrepancy. A characteristic-kernel MMD can identify the observable law while leaving the generator parameters and latent explanation non-identifiable.

    A One-Dimensional Running Example¤

    Let data be the equal mixture \(p=\tfrac12\delta_{-2}+\tfrac12\delta_2\). A generator with one Bernoulli latent can represent both points. A generator constrained to \(G_\theta(z)=\theta\) cannot; every objective must choose a compromise. Comparing objectives on this intentionally misspecified family exposes their geometry without blaming optimization.

    Under squared reconstruction or 2-Wasserstein cost, the best single point is the mean \(\theta=0\). Under an RBF MMD, \(\theta=0\) is symmetric but its local geometry depends strongly on bandwidth. Under data-to-sample nearest-neighbor matching with a single generated candidate, the same compromise appears; with a pool that can contain two candidates, IMLE can represent both training points only if the generator and latent allocation permit it. An objective cannot create capacity absent from the model family.

    Why Reconstruction Alone Is Insufficient¤

    An autoencoder can reconstruct each training point while its chosen latent prior decodes to unrealistic samples. Reconstruction evaluates an encoder–decoder pair on codes produced by the encoder; generation evaluates the pushforward of the prior. Moment, adversarial, or prior-matching objectives connect those distributions, but each connection has assumptions.

    Worked example: same sampler interface, different density semantics¤

    Let \(Z\sim\mathcal U[0,2\pi)\) and \(G(Z)=(\cos Z,\sin Z)\). Sampling is exact and produces the uniform law on the unit circle. Relative to two-dimensional Lebesgue measure, this law has no ordinary density because all mass lies on a one-dimensional set.

    Now set \(X=G(Z)+0.1\epsilon\) with \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I_2)\). The sampling API looks almost identical, but the law is now a Gaussian convolution with a two-dimensional density. Evaluating that density requires integrating over \(z\):

    \[ p_X(x)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\mathcal N(x;G(z),0.01I)\,dz, \]

    which generally needs numerical approximation. “Has a density,” “can sample,” and “can evaluate density” are three different capabilities.

    Counterexample: pooled conditional success

    Suppose condition \(C=0\) contains only the left mode and \(C=1\) only the right mode. A generator that ignores \(C\) but emits the pooled two-mode mixture can match the marginal \(p(x)\) perfectly while both conditional laws are wrong.

    Chapter 1 Synthesis¤

    Implicit models define samples without requiring tractable density. Density existence, density access, and sampling access are separate. The training problem is to expose distributional differences through sample-based estimators while preserving population, estimation, and optimization distinctions. Chapter 2 develops MMD, the canonical direct moment-matching objective.

    Independent practice and assessment map: implicit laws and interfaces¤

    Keep the observable law, sampling program, density/base measure, discrepancy, finite estimator, and gradient interface in separate columns. A claim about one column cannot silently establish another.

    P1 — Audit a likelihood-free sampling program¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: an implicit model has no mathematical density; adding observation noise leaves the same model; a sampleable program is differentiable; a characteristic discrepancy identifies generator parameters; a conditional generator that matches the pooled marginal matches every condition; and low reconstruction error certifies prior samples. For each false statement, name the missing measure, interface, identifiability, condition, or prior-pushforward assumption.
    2. P1.2 — Measure-class calculation. Let \(Z\sim\mathcal U[-1,1]\) and \(G(Z)=(Z,Z^2)\). Prove that the pushforward is supported on a one-dimensional curve in \(\mathbb R^2\), state why it lacks a two-dimensional Lebesgue density, and write the Gaussian-convolved density for \(X=G(Z)+\sigma\epsilon\). Explain which law is approached as \(\sigma\downarrow0\) and why pointwise densities need not converge to a density of the singular limit.
    3. P1.3 — Gradient-interface ledger. For a differentiable neural map, a Bernoulli branch with known probabilities, a renderer with hard visibility, and a proprietary simulator that returns samples only, choose among pathwise, score-function, relaxation, finite-difference/SPSA, emulator, or derivative-free routes. Write the estimand for each choice, identify bias and variance sources, and specify one finite gradient check where a reference derivative is available.
    4. P1.4 — Misspecification and identifiability. For \(p=\tfrac12\delta_{-2}+\tfrac12\delta_2\) and \(G_\theta(z)=\theta\), compute the squared-error/Wasserstein compromise and analyze the RBF-MMD stationary point as bandwidth varies. Then construct two distinct latent programs that induce the same two-mode law and prove why no sample-only population discrepancy can choose between them.
    5. P1.5 — Conditional falsification. Construct a two-condition data law and a generator that exactly matches \(p(x)\) while ignoring \(C\). Compute pooled and condition-wise frequencies, define a joint-kernel or stratified test that exposes the error, and state the sample-size limitation for a rare condition. Distinguish conditional generation from posterior inference over a simulator parameter.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Specify an implicit program for a molecular simulator, stochastic policy rollout, graphics engine, or scientific field. Declare randomness, conditions, state space, base measure, support, density access, trace access, gradient interface, training witness, simulation budget, evaluation witness, and identifiable scientific target. Include one enumerated or analytically tractable small-instance oracle and reject any claim that the available interfaces cannot test.

    Retrieval practice: implicit foundations
    1. Does adding a discriminator make a model implicit?
    2. Can an implicit model possess a mathematical density?
    3. Why does \(d_z<d_x\) matter for a deterministic smooth generator?
    4. What three contracts must a likelihood-free objective satisfy in practice?
    5. Why does a fixed-kernel GMMN not require an adversary even though MMD is an IPM?

    Solutions. (1) No. Implicitness concerns density access; a discriminator is one training tool. (2) Yes. The density may exist but be unavailable or expensive to evaluate. (3) Its pushforward is typically supported on a lower-dimensional image and is singular relative to ambient Lebesgue measure. (4) A population identification contract, a finite-sample estimation contract, and an optimization/gradient contract. (5) The RKHS supremum has an analytic solution in terms of mean embeddings for a fixed kernel, so the empirical discrepancy can be differentiated directly through generated samples.

    Bridge. Chapter 2 chooses a fixed witness class—an RKHS—and derives both its population discrepancy and finite-sample training estimators. Continue continuously or open Chapter 2.

    2. MMD and Generative Moment Matching Networks¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes expectations, positive-definite kernels, and differentiating through generated samples. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive MMD as an integral probability metric and as a distance between mean embeddings;
    2. distinguish characteristic-kernel identification from finite-sample test power;
    3. derive biased, unbiased, and reduced-cost estimators;
    4. backpropagate an empirical MMD through a generator;
    5. explain exactly what changes when MMD is computed in learned feature space; and
    6. derive the optimal RKHS witness and state when MMD convergence implies weak convergence.

    For RKHS \(\mathcal H_k\), MMD is the integral probability metric

    \[ \operatorname{MMD}_k(p,q) =\sup_{\|f\|_{\mathcal H_k}\le1} \left|\mathbb E_p f(X)-\mathbb E_q f(Y)\right| =\|\mu_p-\mu_q\|_{\mathcal H_k}, \]

    where \(\mu_p=\mathbb E_p[k(X,\cdot)]\) when the embedding exists. Expanding the squared norm gives the familiar kernel formula.

    For a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space with kernel \(k\), the squared maximum mean discrepancy is

    \[ \operatorname{MMD}^2(p,q) =\mathbb E_{x,x'\sim p}k(x,x') +\mathbb E_{y,y'\sim q}k(y,y') -2\mathbb E_{x\sim p,y\sim q}k(x,y). \]

    It equals the squared distance between kernel mean embeddings. With a characteristic kernel, population MMD is zero exactly when \(p=q\).

    “Characteristic” is relative to the domain and class of probability measures. Gaussian and Laplace kernels are characteristic on common Euclidean settings under standard conditions. A linear kernel identifies only the mean; a finite polynomial kernel identifies only finitely many moments. Characteristicness does not specify how many samples are needed to distinguish two nearby high-dimensional distributions.

    The optimal witness shows where the laws differ¤

    When \(\mu_p\ne\mu_q\), the unit-norm RKHS function attaining the MMD supremum is

    \[ f^*(t)=\frac{\langle \mu_p-\mu_q,k(t,\cdot)\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}} {\|\mu_p-\mu_q\|_{\mathcal H_k}} =\frac{\mathbb E_p k(X,t)-\mathbb E_q k(Y,t)} {\operatorname{MMD}_k(p,q)}. \]

    The numerator is often plotted without normalization as the witness function. Positive regions are more similar to data than model samples under the kernel; negative regions reverse that relation. It is a kernel-smoothed diagnostic, not a density difference unless additional deconvolution conditions hold. Statistical Model Criticism using Kernel Two Sample Tests uses this witness to localize model mismatch.

    For empirical samples, replace expectations by averages. The resulting witness uses the same data and kernel that defined the discrepancy, so inspecting its largest extrema after tuning the kernel is exploratory unless a separate confirmatory protocol is used.

    Identification and weak-convergence topology are different claims¤

    A characteristic kernel makes the embedding injective: zero MMD identifies equality. A stronger and separate statement is that \(\operatorname{MMD}_k(p_n,p)\to0\) is equivalent to weak convergence \(p_n\Rightarrow p\). This depends on the state space and continuity, boundedness, integrability, and universality properties of the kernel. The conditions are developed in Hilbert Space Embeddings and Metrics on Probability Measures and later complete characterizations of kernel distribution embeddings. Do not attach “metrizes weak convergence” to every characteristic kernel without its domain assumptions.

    Generative Moment Matching Networks sample \(y_j=G_\theta(z_j)\) and minimize an empirical MMD. No adversary is optimized; gradients flow through the kernel similarities into generated samples and then through the generator.

    Empirical Estimators¤

    For equal sample sizes \(m\), a biased V-statistic is

    \[ \widehat{\operatorname{MMD}}_V^2 =\frac1{m^2}\sum_{i,j}k(x_i,x_j) +\frac1{m^2}\sum_{i,j}k(y_i,y_j) -\frac2{m^2}\sum_{i,j}k(x_i,y_j). \]

    It is nonnegative but includes diagonal terms. An unbiased U-statistic removes within-set diagonals; it can be negative at finite \(m\). Linear-time estimators pair samples to reduce the usual quadratic kernel cost but increase variance.

    For equal sizes, the unbiased estimator is

    \[ \widehat{\operatorname{MMD}}_U^2 =\frac{1}{m(m-1)}\sum_{i\ne j}k(x_i,x_j) +\frac{1}{m(m-1)}\sum_{i\ne j}k(y_i,y_j) -\frac{2}{m^2}\sum_{i,j}k(x_i,y_j). \]

    It is unbiased for a fixed pair \((p,q)\). During training, \(q=q_\theta\) changes adaptively using the same dataset, so unbiased minibatch estimates do not by themselves give an unbiased estimate of held-out model quality or a calibrated two-sample test after model selection.

    The V-statistic costs \(O(m^2)\) kernel evaluations and memory if the full Gram matrices are materialized. Streaming block sums reduce memory without changing all terms; random features replace the kernel by an approximation; linear estimators change the variance structure. Report which one is used rather than writing only “MMD loss.”

    For even \(m\), one linear-time unbiased construction groups independent pairs. Writing

    \[ h_i=k(x_{2i-1},x_{2i})+k(y_{2i-1},y_{2i}) -k(x_{2i-1},y_{2i})-k(x_{2i},y_{2i-1}), \]

    the estimator \(\frac{2}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m/2}h_i\) has expectation \(\operatorname{MMD}^2\). It uses only \(O(m)\) kernel evaluations but discards most pair interactions and is typically noisier than the quadratic U-statistic. A block estimator interpolates between those regimes. The original kernel two-sample test derives quadratic and linear procedures; changing complexity changes the statistic's variance and null calibration, not only runtime.

    Worked Gradient for Two Point Masses¤

    With RBF kernel \(k(x,y)=\exp(-(x-y)^2/(2\sigma^2))\), data \(p=\delta_0\), and generator \(q_\theta=\delta_\theta\),

    \[ \operatorname{MMD}^2=2-2\exp\left(-\frac{\theta^2}{2\sigma^2}\right). \]

    Its gradient is

    \[ \frac{d}{d\theta}\operatorname{MMD}^2 =\frac{2\theta}{\sigma^2} \exp\left(-\frac{\theta^2}{2\sigma^2}\right). \]

    When \(|\theta|\gg\sigma\), the discrepancy is large but the gradient is exponentially small. A characteristic kernel guarantees population identifiability, not useful gradients for every parameter state.

    For a generated batch \(y_j=G_\theta(z_j)\), the chain rule has the form

    \[ \nabla_\theta\widehat{\operatorname{MMD}}^2 =\sum_j J_{G_\theta}(z_j)^\top \nabla_{y_j}\widehat{\operatorname{MMD}}^2. \]

    Thus even an informative witness gradient in data space can be attenuated or distorted by the generator Jacobian. Kernel choice and generator conditioning are separate optimization bottlenecks.

    Data Space or Code Space?¤

    The original GMMN work also matches encoded representations. If an autoencoder feature map \(h(x)\) preserves task-relevant differences, a kernel on \(h(x)\) can be more effective than raw-pixel distances. But the objective then matches \(h_\#p\) and \(h_\#q\). A non-injective encoder can map distinct data distributions to the same feature distribution.

    If the encoder is trained jointly to minimize MMD, it can collapse features and make matching trivial. If trained to maximize distinguishability, an adversarial witness game returns. Freezing a pretrained encoder avoids those dynamics but imports its training data and invariances. These are three distinct methods.

    Fixed kernels, selected kernels, and learned-kernel games¤

    There are at least four non-equivalent protocols:

    1. fix \(k\) before seeing the comparison data and minimize its empirical MMD;
    2. choose bandwidth/kernel on a training or selection split, then freeze it;
    3. maximize an estimated test-power criterion over kernels, then test on fresh data; or
    4. alternate a learned feature kernel against the generator.

    Optimized MMD develops the third route for model criticism, while MMD-GAN uses an adversarially learned kernel for training. The fourth route is a min--max game even though its critic output enters a kernel rather than a logistic classifier.

    Estimator unbiasedness must also be conditioned on what was selected. Demystifying MMD GANs separates an unbiased gradient for a fixed MMD objective from the bias introduced when a critic/kernel is fitted on finite samples and then reused to form a generator gradient. “The U-statistic is unbiased” does not erase adaptive witness selection.

    Counterexample: a characteristic kernel with an unusable scale

    The RBF kernel remains characteristic for every fixed \(\sigma>0\), yet the point-mass gradient above tends to zero when the distributions are far apart relative to \(\sigma\). Population identification is an asymptotic uniqueness statement, not an optimization guarantee.

    3. Kernel Choice, Estimator Bias, and Statistical Power¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 2's RKHS and estimator derivations. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. interpret bandwidth and feature maps as modeling assumptions;
    2. compare isotropic, anisotropic, multi-kernel, learned, and structured kernels;
    3. account for random-feature, block, and distributed approximation error;
    4. separate generator training from a valid post-selection two-sample test;
    5. design conditional and rare-mode kernel audits; and
    6. calibrate a kernel test under the degenerate null without reusing selection data.

    Bandwidth Is an Inductive Bias¤

    For an RBF kernel, a tiny bandwidth makes almost all distinct high-dimensional samples orthogonal; a huge bandwidth makes them nearly identical. Median-distance heuristics adapt to scale but can fail for multimodal, anisotropic, or mixed-type data.

    A multi-kernel objective

    \[ k(x,y)=\sum_{r=1}^{R}\alpha_r k_r(x,y),\qquad\alpha_r\ge0, \]

    exposes several scales. Learning \(\alpha_r\) jointly with the generator risks choosing kernels that weaken rather than strengthen discrimination unless the optimization is designed as a test-power or adversarial-kernel problem.

    An anisotropic RBF uses \((x-y)^\top\Lambda^{-1}(x-y)\) and therefore encodes relative coordinate scales and correlations. Product kernels can combine continuous, categorical, graph, or sequence components. The resulting kernel is useful only if it is positive definite where required and its invariances match the data—not merely because each component seems reasonable.

    High Dimension and Features¤

    Euclidean distance concentrates in high dimension. Kernels on pretrained perceptual features can improve sample gradients, but they import the encoder's dataset, invariances, and blind spots. For scientific data, use domain invariants, conserved quantities, and structural kernels rather than assuming an image encoder defines fidelity.

    “MMD fails in high dimension” is too broad. Power depends on the alternative, kernel, bandwidth, dimension/sample regime, and studentization. Mean shifts, variance changes, sparse alternatives, and structured functional alternatives can behave differently. Two Sample Testing in High Dimension via Maximum Mean Discrepancy develops joint dimension/sample asymptotics for a studentized statistic; those results do not imply that the median heuristic or a fixed image embedding is powerful for every neural-generator failure.

    The practical response is an alternative suite, not a slogan: inject a missing mode, wrong covariance, heavy tail, local corruption, memorized sample, and domain-specific constraint violation, then estimate which kernels and sample sizes detect each one at controlled type-I error.

    Random Features and Block Estimators¤

    Random Fourier features approximate shift-invariant kernels:

    \[ k(x,y)\approx\phi(x)^T\phi(y). \]

    Then MMD becomes the squared difference of empirical feature means, reducing pairwise cost. Approximation dimension introduces another error source. Block U-statistics trade memory and variance, and distributed reductions must preserve unbiased pairing assumptions.

    With \(R\) random features, the approximate objective is

    \[ \left\|\frac1m\sum_i\phi_R(x_i)-\frac1n\sum_j\phi_R(y_j)\right\|_2^2. \]

    Its cost is roughly linear in samples times \(R\), but it matches the approximate feature map, not the exact kernel. Fixing random features across updates gives a stationary approximate objective; resampling them adds Monte Carlo noise and changes the witness seen at each step.

    Statistical Testing Versus Generator Training¤

    Two-sample testing asks whether finite evidence rejects \(p=q\) at a controlled significance level. Generator training uses a discrepancy as a differentiable objective. A kernel with high hypothesis-test power near one alternative may give poor global optimization geometry. Conversely, a useful training loss is not automatically a calibrated test statistic after parameters were selected on the same data.

    A valid final test needs held-out data or a selection-aware protocol. Choosing bandwidth, feature encoder, checkpoint, and seed to minimize a test-set MMD and then applying the null distribution to the same samples invalidates the nominal type-I error.

    Why the ordinary null is not a standard error bar¤

    Under \(H_0:p=q\), the quadratic unbiased MMD is a degenerate U-statistic: its first-order projection vanishes. Its limiting null is generally a weighted sum of centered chi-square variables rather than an ordinary Gaussian with the same variance formula used under a fixed alternative. Permutation or wild bootstrap calibration is therefore common; linear/block/studentized variants have different limits and power.

    A defensible confirmatory pipeline is:

    1. choose representations, kernels, bandwidth candidates, and alternatives on training data;
    2. select among them on an independent selection split or use a test that adjusts for aggregation;
    3. freeze the complete test before opening the final samples;
    4. compute a permutation/bootstrap/asymptotic threshold valid for that statistic; and
    5. report level, power on controlled alternatives, sample size, and Monte Carlo error.

    MMD Aggregated Two-Sample Test shows one way to aggregate bandwidths while controlling level under its conditions. A Martingale Kernel Two-Sample Test provides a 2026 permutation-free alternative with a limiting standard Gaussian null. These are testing procedures; replacing a minibatch generator loss by one of them is a separate optimization design.

    Conditional MMD¤

    Matching pooled \((x,c)\) samples can hide rare-condition failure. Conditional mean embeddings and stratified objectives compare \(p(x\mid c)\), but continuous/high-dimensional conditions require kernels or learned representations over \(c\). Report performance across condition slices and do not infer conditional calibration from marginal MMD.

    If the condition marginal is the same by construction, equality of the joint laws \(p(x,c)=q(x,c)\) implies equality of the conditional laws for \(p(c)\)-almost every condition. A characteristic product kernel \(k_X(x,x')k_C(c,c')\) can therefore test the joint law. But it weights conditions according to their marginal frequency, so a rare conditional error can have little finite-sample influence. A pooled kernel on \(x\) alone discards \(c\) entirely. A conditional-mean-embedding estimator instead introduces an operator inverse or regularization choice; it is not obtained merely by concatenating \(c\) to \(x\) and writing “conditional MMD.”

    For discrete conditions, report both natural-frequency and macro-averaged stratified MMD. For continuous conditions, declare the condition kernel, localization bandwidth, overlap, boundary behavior, and the region over which the conditional claim is made.

    Practical Kernel Audit¤

    1. Normalize each input dimension or justify the metric.
    2. Plot within-data, within-model, and cross distances.
    3. Sweep bandwidths and feature encoders.
    4. Compare biased, unbiased, and block estimators.
    5. Track gradient magnitude versus sample quality.
    6. Test synthetic alternatives: missing mode, wrong variance, memorization, and corrupted structure.

    Worked example: the median heuristic misses a rare mode¤

    Let \(99\%\) of data lie near zero and \(1\%\) near 100. A model reproduces only the common component. In small minibatches, most batches contain no rare sample, so any empirical MMD is blind to the missing mode. Even when the full-dataset median distance sets a sensible local bandwidth near zero, rare-mode evidence enters only through the few cross terms containing rare points.

    Three fixes address different problems: stratified batches increase rare-mode estimator exposure; a multi-scale kernel changes distance sensitivity; condition- or group-wise metrics expose the failure directly. None changes the generator family or guarantees it allocates the correct \(1\%\) mass.

    Counterexample: the training kernel certifies itself

    Evaluating only with the same learned feature kernel used for training can reward shared blind spots. An independent representation, task metric, or targeted synthetic alternative is required to detect errors outside the training witness class.

    4. Implicit Maximum Likelihood Estimation¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand nearest-neighbor search, latent-variable marginal likelihood, and feature distances. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive the practical IMLE assignment/update objective;
    2. explain its connection to a noise-smoothed likelihood and the assumptions behind that connection;
    3. distinguish covering training examples from assigning correct generator probability mass;
    4. analyze latent-pool size, stale assignments, and approximate search;
    5. design conditional and few-shot IMLE without cross-condition leakage; and
    6. derive hard and soft assignment gradients and quantify latent-pool occupancy.

    Implicit Maximum Likelihood Estimation draws many latent codes, generates candidates, assigns each data example its nearest candidate, and updates the generator to reduce those matched distances.

    For data batch \(\{x_i\}\) and latent pool \(\{z_j\}_{j=1}^{M}\):

    \[ j^*(i)=\arg\min_j d(x_i,G_\theta(z_j)), \]
    \[ \mathcal R_M(\theta) =\mathbb E_{z_{1:M}\sim p_Z^M}\left[ \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n\min_{1\le j\le M} d(x_i,G_\theta(z_j))\right]. \]

    Every data point requests a nearby generated sample. This reverses the common sample-to-data matching direction and creates explicit pressure not to ignore training examples.

    The nearest-neighbor index is piecewise constant in \(\theta\). Practical training selects \(j^*(i)\), treats that discrete choice as fixed for an update, and differentiates the selected distance through \(G_\theta(z_{j^*(i)})\). When assignments change, the objective is nonsmooth. Refresh cadence is therefore part of the algorithm.

    The expectation over latent pools is part of the objective. One realized pool produces the conditional loss

    \[ \widehat{\mathcal R}_M(\theta;z_{1:M}) =\frac1n\sum_i d(x_i,G_\theta(z_{j^*(i)})), \]

    not the expectation itself. Reusing a pool, drawing one pool per batch, drawing separate pools per datum, and mining a global pool define different stochastic programs and dependence structures.

    The practical assignment/update loop¤

    A reproducible implementation records: pool distribution and size, whether latents are shared across data, feature/distance version, exact or approximate search, tie rule, assignment refresh cadence, number of optimizer steps per refresh, candidate reuse, and distributed sharding. The loop is closer to alternating discrete assignment and continuous update than to ordinary minibatch regression.

    What “Implicit Maximum Likelihood” Requires¤

    The connection to maximum likelihood holds under conditions on the noise/kernel view and model/data likelihood geometry; it is not an identity for arbitrary perceptual distance, finite latent pool, and optimizer. Adaptive IMLE explicitly notes restrictive conditions in the original guarantee and develops a generalized formulation under weaker assumptions.

    One useful bridge adds an observation kernel around generated samples. For squared Euclidean distance and Gaussian noise,

    \[ p_{\theta,\sigma}(x) =\mathbb E_{Z}\left[\mathcal N(x;G_\theta(Z),\sigma^2I)\right]. \]

    Using a finite latent pool gives a log-sum-exp over distances. In the small-noise limit,

    \[ -2\sigma^2\log\left[\frac1M\sum_{j=1}^{M} \exp\left(-\frac{\|x-G_\theta(z_j)\|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \right] \longrightarrow \min_j\|x-G_\theta(z_j)\|^2. \]

    This explains a nearest-neighbor connection, but finite \(M\), finite \(\sigma\), non-Euclidean perceptual distances, latent resampling, and assignment-based optimization all matter. The hard minimum also discards contributions from near-tied candidates that the finite-noise likelihood retains.

    For the finite Gaussian mixture, define responsibilities

    \[ w_{ij} =\frac{\exp(-\|x_i-G_\theta(z_j)\|^2/(2\sigma^2))} {\sum_{r=1}^M\exp(-\|x_i-G_\theta(z_r)\|^2/(2\sigma^2))}. \]

    The gradient of its negative log likelihood is a weighted sum of candidate distance gradients. Hard IMLE replaces the weights by a one-hot nearest assignment. As \(\sigma\) shrinks, weights concentrate when the nearest candidate is unique; at ties, the limit and derivative require care. At finite \(\sigma\), the soft objective preserves information from near neighbors but pays for all candidate contributions. This creates a continuum among soft likelihood-like matching, hard nearest-neighbor matching, and truncated top-\(k\) approximations.

    The likelihood bridge also fixes the ground geometry: squared Euclidean distance corresponds to isotropic Gaussian observation noise. A perceptual distance generally does not equal the negative log density of a normalized observation kernel unless that kernel and its normalizer are explicitly constructed.

    Use the evidence label Established for the algorithmic objective, but attach precise assumptions to likelihood-equivalence claims.

    Coverage and Memorization¤

    Because each training point gets a matched latent, IMLE discourages dropping examples. Yet perfect nearest-neighbor reconstruction can memorize a finite dataset. Coverage of training examples is not generalization to the population, and a perceptual metric can declare semantically different samples close.

    Nor does one nearby sample guarantee correct mass. A generator can reserve tiny latent regions for rare training points while mapping almost all prior mass to a common mode. Every datum then has a neighbor in a sufficiently large pool, but ordinary sampling almost never visits the rare modes. Measure coverage and support proximity are different.

    Pool occupancy turns latent mass into a measurable prediction¤

    If a target region receives generator mass \(\pi\), an i.i.d. pool of size \(M\) contains at least one sample from that region with probability

    \[ 1-(1-\pi)^M. \]

    This identity exposes an extreme-value confound. A region with only \(\pi=10^{-3}\) mass appears in a pool of \(M=100\) with probability about \(9.5\%\), but in a pool of \(M=5000\) with probability about \(99.3\%\). A large-pool nearest neighbor can therefore look covered even though an ordinary batch of generated samples almost never visits the region.

    Estimate both proximity and mass: nearest data-to-model distance at matched \(M\), occupancy/frequency under unconditioned prior draws, latent-cell or local-volume diagnostics where meaningful, and uncertainty for rare-region frequency. Do not tune \(M\) until every datum has a neighbor and then report that same event as independent coverage evidence.

    Computational Bottleneck¤

    Naive matching costs \(O(BM)\) distances and stores many candidates. Approximate nearest-neighbor search, feature-space matching, adaptive latent sampling, and periodically refreshed assignments reduce cost but change the effective objective. Reusing a matched latent for several gradient steps creates stale assignments as \(G_\theta\) moves.

    The relevant memory can be \(O(Md_f)\) for stored candidate features plus index overhead, while generator evaluation may dominate time. Approximate search can return a different neighbor; feature quantization can change ties; distributed pools can bias assignments toward local shards. Record recall of the search procedure on a manageable exact subset.

    Worked One-Dimensional Assignment¤

    Let data be \(x=(-2,2)\) and generated candidates be \(y=(-1.5,-0.2,1.7)\). Under squared distance, assignments are \(-2\mapsto-1.5\) and \(2\mapsto1.7\), with loss

    \[ (-2+1.5)^2+(2-1.7)^2=0.25+0.09=0.34. \]

    The middle candidate receives no direct gradient from this batch. IMLE is data-covering, not necessarily balanced over latent-space usage. Regularizing the mapping or resampling the pool affects how much prior mass reaches each mode.

    If the pool contains only \((-1.5,-0.2)\), both data points must choose among those candidates and the right mode receives a poor match. Increasing \(M\) lowers the empirical nearest-neighbor loss even with fixed \(\theta\) through an extreme-value effect. Comparisons must therefore match pool size and resampling policy.

    Conditional IMLE¤

    In conditional generation, candidates must be generated for the same condition as each target. Cross-condition nearest neighbors would train the wrong conditional law. Few-shot settings make this appealing because every example supplies a target, but overfitting and condition leakage require explicit tests.

    Conditional IMLE generates candidates for each semantic layout and matches each observed output to a candidate under that same input. This solves a finite conditional matching problem; one observed output per input still does not empirically reveal the full conditional law at that exact input. Generalization relies on shared model structure across conditions and must be tested on held-out inputs.

    Hierarchical candidate search can allocate coarse latents before refining later latent groups, reducing the cost of finding useful high-dimensional codes. It changes the proposal/search procedure and may restrict which joint latent combinations are explored. Report level-wise pool sizes, pruning, beam width, and exact-search recall on a smaller problem rather than describing hierarchy as a free acceleration.

    Peer-reviewed research: Adaptive IMLE, ICML 2023 adapts perturbation/difficulty and weakens parts of the original theoretical setup for few-shot generation. Its reported gains belong to that conditional few-shot protocol; they are not a theorem that adaptive matching universally improves coverage or outperforms likelihood/adversarial training.

    5. Sliced, Projected, and Feature-Moment Objectives¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know one-dimensional empirical Wasserstein distance, random projections, and finite feature matching. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. derive sliced Wasserstein from projected laws and empirical sorting;
    2. distinguish all-projection identification from finite random or learned projections;
    3. relate finite feature matching to a finite-rank kernel MMD;
    4. separate amortized neural generators from nonparametric particle transports;
    5. place energy, Cramér, rank-based, and hybrid discrepancies without treating them as interchangeable; and
    6. compare average, max, generalized-slice, and Sinkhorn objectives with their solver and gradient contracts.

    Sliced Wasserstein Distance¤

    For unit direction \(u\), project \(x\) to \(u^Tx\). One-dimensional Wasserstein distance is computed by sorting empirical projections. The sliced distance averages across directions:

    \[ \operatorname{SW}_p^p(p,q) =\int_{\mathbb S^{d-1}} W_p^p((P_u)_\#p,(P_u)_\#q)\,du. \]

    Monte Carlo uses finitely many random or learned directions. Sliced Wasserstein Generative Models introduce learned orthogonal projections in generative frameworks. Asymptotic consistency results exist for minimum sliced-Wasserstein estimators under assumptions, but finite projection count and learned projection bias remain operational concerns.

    The Cramér–Wold theorem says equality of all one-dimensional linear projections identifies a probability law on \(\mathbb R^d\). Sliced Wasserstein integrates over all directions, so its population identification is not the same as comparing a fixed finite set. Monte Carlo directions introduce projection-sampling error in addition to empirical-distribution error.

    For equal empirical sizes and a fixed direction \(u\), sort the projected scalars:

    \[ W_p^p(\widehat p_u,\widehat q_u) =\frac1m\sum_{i=1}^{m} \left|u^\top x_{(i)}-u^\top y_{(i)}\right|^p. \]

    Sorting costs \(O(m\log m)\) per direction. Ties make the chosen matching nondifferentiable, though subgradients or automatic differentiation through a deterministic sort are used in practice. Unequal sample weights or sizes require quantile/transport handling rather than this simple paired formula.

    For fixed \(u\), \(p>1\), no ties, and a generated point \(y_j\) whose projected rank pairs it with \(x_{\pi(j)}\), the local derivative of the empirical cost is

    \[ \nabla_{y_j}\widehat W_{p,u}^p =\frac{p}{m} |u^\top(y_j-x_{\pi(j)})|^{p-2} u^\top(y_j-x_{\pi(j)})\,u. \]

    The gradient moves \(y_j\) along the slice direction toward its current order-statistic partner. It is valid inside a region where the sorting permutation is fixed. When projected values cross or tie, the assignment changes and the objective is only piecewise smooth. Differentiable sorting relaxations instead optimize a temperature-dependent approximation and must be reported as such.

    Why One-Dimensional Slices Help¤

    Sorting gives an exact empirical coupling in one dimension. Many projections can reveal location, scale, and shape differences without solving high-dimensional OT. However, a finite set of directions can miss differences orthogonal to their span. Optimizing directions adversarially reintroduces a game; fixing them trades adaptivity for stability.

    Max-sliced or learned-projection objectives emphasize directions with large discrepancy. If the projection network is trained to maximize while the generator minimizes, the method is adversarial even without a binary discriminator. Orthogonality constraints diversify linear directions but do not guarantee coverage of nonlinear features.

    Average, maximum, and generalized slices¤

    Ordinary sliced Wasserstein averages over linear directions. Max-sliced uses a supremum or optimized direction and therefore emphasizes a worst exposed projection. Projection-robust OT extends the maximization to \(k\)-dimensional subspaces; sample-complexity and misspecification results belong to the declared projection-robust estimators, not to every learned projection network.

    Generalized Sliced Wasserstein replaces \(u^\top x\) by an admissible nonlinear slicing function \(g(x,u)\) and uses the generalized Radon-transform viewpoint. Nonlinear slices can expose curved structure that linear projections need many directions to see, but metric/identification claims require injectivity and regularity conditions on the slicing family. A neural slicer with insufficient capacity can miss alternatives; an unconstrained maximizer can create another unstable game.

    Feature and Moment Matching¤

    One can match finite statistics

    \[ \left\|\mathbb E_p\phi(X)-\mathbb E_q\phi(Y)\right\|^2. \]

    If \(\phi\) contains mean and quadratic terms, this matches first and second moments. Neural feature matching uses activations from a classifier, autoencoder, or discriminator. Unless the feature family is characteristic or sufficiently rich, many distributions share the same moments.

    Finite feature matching is exactly MMD with the finite-rank kernel \(k(x,y)=\phi(x)^\top\phi(y)\). This makes its limitation explicit: only the empirical feature mean is matched. Adding covariance corresponds to augmenting \(\phi\) with quadratic products, which increases cost and still does not identify arbitrary distributions.

    Counterexample: Moments Do Not Determine a Distribution¤

    A standard Gaussian and many non-Gaussian distributions have mean zero and variance one. Matching only those moments cannot distinguish tail shape or multimodality. Even matching every polynomial moment may not uniquely identify a distribution without moment-determinacy conditions.

    Cramér and Energy Distances¤

    Energy distance is an IPM-like sample discrepancy related to distances between observations. It can avoid some saturated-gradient behavior of particular adversarial divergences. As with MMD, empirical geometry depends on the metric and feature space.

    With Euclidean distance and suitable moment conditions,

    \[ \mathcal E^2(p,q) =2\mathbb E\|X-Y\|-\mathbb E\|X-X'\|-\mathbb E\|Y-Y'\|, \]

    which is zero only at equality for the standard setting. Changing the distance or applying a non-injective feature map changes that guarantee. Cramér distances name related but not universally identical constructions; state the exact formula used.

    Energy distance and MMD are not unrelated entries in a taxonomy. For a semimetric \(\rho\) of negative type and a chosen anchor \(x_0\), the distance-induced kernel

    \[ k_\rho(x,y)=\tfrac12\left[ \rho(x,x_0)+\rho(y,x_0)-\rho(x,y) \right] \]

    makes the corresponding squared MMD equal to one half of the energy-distance formula under consistent conventions. The anchor terms cancel in the two-sample discrepancy. Thus a Euclidean energy objective can be implemented as a particular kernel MMD, while arbitrary “Cramér,” feature, or learned distance variants need not preserve the same equivalence. The derivation and generator-gradient implications are discussed in Demystifying MMD GANs.

    Sinkhorn divergence bridges transport and kernel regimes¤

    Entropically regularized transport supplies smoother finite-sample geometry than exact discrete OT, but the regularized self-cost is generally nonzero. The debiased Sinkhorn divergence subtracts it:

    \[ S_\varepsilon(p,q) =\operatorname{OT}_\varepsilon(p,q) -\tfrac12\operatorname{OT}_\varepsilon(p,p) -\tfrac12\operatorname{OT}_\varepsilon(q,q). \]

    Learning Generative Models with Sinkhorn Divergences uses differentiable Sinkhorn iterations to train implicit generators, while Interpolating between Optimal Transport and MMD formalizes interpolation and positivity properties under its assumptions. This is a high-value connection, not an equivalence of implementations:

    • small and large regularization regimes change geometry, bias, numerical conditioning, and iteration count;
    • minibatch empirical measures introduce their own sampling bias;
    • stopping early differentiates a truncated solver rather than an exact fixed point;
    • unrolled, implicit, and envelope-theorem gradients have different memory and validity conditions; and
    • the ground cost or feature map still decides which differences are visible.

    The full solver derivation appears in Optimal Transport as an Objective and a Construction. Here, Sinkhorn belongs because it trains a sampleable generator without pointwise model density and explicitly connects the MMD/energy and transport branches.

    Rank-based discrepancies and explicit density approximations¤

    Peer-reviewed research: dual-ISL, AISTATS 2026 Spotlight studies a rank-based likelihood-free discrepancy, reverses the target/model roles relative to ISL, and derives a Bernstein-polynomial density approximation with convexity and convergence results in the paper's density-space formulation. It is evidence for that estimator and optimization problem—not a theorem that arbitrary neural-generator parameterization becomes convex or that the approximation recovers singular manifold laws without its assumptions.

    This work illustrates a useful boundary: an implicit sampler can be paired with an auxiliary explicit density estimator. The resulting density estimate inherits smoothing, basis, truncation, and finite-sample assumptions. It is not the inaccessible exact pushforward density merely because it is closed form.

    Particle flows versus amortized generators¤

    Sliced-Wasserstein Flows and conditional sliced-Wasserstein flows iteratively move a collection of particles using projected transport information. They are implicit generative procedures, but they are not necessarily one-pass maps \(G_\theta(z)\) after training. Report particle count, iterations, projection count, initialization, and whether a separate network amortizes the transport.

    Hybrid Objectives¤

    Practical implicit generators may combine MMD, sliced distance, reconstruction, adversarial, or perceptual losses. The weighted sum is not automatically one named statistical distance. Report reductions, coefficient sweeps, gradient conflicts, and ablations. A discriminator used only to define features still introduces learned, nonstationary geometry.

    Worked example: finite projections miss a changed coordinate¤

    In \(\mathbb R^3\), suppose every chosen projection lies in the span of \(e_1,e_2\). Let \(p\) and \(q\) have identical \((X_1,X_2)\) distributions but different \(X_3\) distributions. Every selected one-dimensional projection is identical, so empirical sliced loss is zero even with infinite samples. A fresh direction with nonzero third component exposes the error.

    Random directions avoid a permanently fixed blind subspace with probability one in this toy case, but finite samples and small projection components can still make the signal weak. Learned projections can focus on \(X_3\) only if their optimization and inputs reveal it.

    Chapter 5 Synthesis¤

    Projected and feature-moment methods trade high-dimensional comparison for lower-dimensional or finite witnesses. All projections can identify a Euclidean distribution, while finitely many cannot. Particle transports, amortized generators, and explicit auxiliary density approximations are different systems even when they share a discrepancy.

    Choose from the failure you need to expose¤

    • Missing broad moments or known invariants: begin with explicit features or structured MMD.
    • Unknown but smooth sample differences with moderate dimension: characteristic multi-scale kernels are a defensible baseline.
    • Data examples receive no nearby samples: test IMLE-style data-to-model matching while measuring latent mass and memorization.
    • High-dimensional OT is too costly but linear projections are meaningful: use sliced objectives with projection-count audits.
    • Novel graph/molecule regions matter: design thin-support splits and domain-validity checks rather than relying on aggregate moments.
    • Pointwise density or calibrated posterior queries are requirements: an implicit sampler alone is the wrong interface; add a justified estimator or select another family.

    This is a routing aid, not an automatic model selector. The final choice still depends on state-space geometry, generator gradient access, sample size, compute, and the weakest failure that the deployment cannot tolerate.

    Evaluation Contract¤

    For an implicit generator, report:

    1. sample quality and diversity with modality-appropriate metrics;
    2. precision/recall or density/coverage-style diagnostics;
    3. nearest-neighbor and membership/memorization checks;
    4. condition-wise performance for conditional models;
    5. objective estimator settings: kernel, features, bandwidth, projection count, latent pool, and distance;
    6. latency, parameter count, training compute, and sampling cost;
    7. multiple seeds and uncertainty;
    8. failure cases that the chosen witness class cannot detect.

    Use at least one independent representation and targeted alternatives. FID-like feature distances, MMD, precision/recall, and nearest-neighbor metrics can share encoders and therefore share blind spots. Metrics estimated from the same finite reference set are correlated; multiple numbers do not imply independent evidence.

    Production Considerations¤

    Sampling is often one generator pass, but preprocessing, conditioning encoders, rejection filters, and decoders must be included in latency. Deterministic generators require latent randomness for diversity; accidental fixed seeds or collapsed latent paths can make a healthy model appear deterministic.

    Monitor distribution drift in the same feature space used for training and independent spaces. Reusing one encoder for objective and evaluation can hide shared blind spots.

    Record RNG algorithm and seed handling, latent dimension/distribution, truncation or rejection, batch-dependent layers, conditioning dropout, and post-processing. Exported inference can differ from training because BatchNorm statistics, stochastic augmentation, or feature normalization changed. A sampler that only works with a large rejection budget is not one-pass in end-to-end cost.

    • Peer-reviewed research testing: Chatterjee and Ramdas, ALT 2026 derive a martingale MMD statistic with a limiting standard-Gaussian null and consistency against fixed alternatives, reducing calibration cost in the large-sample regime.     Final Synthesis¤

    Implicit generation is a density-access category, not a synonym for GANs. GMMNs compare kernel mean embeddings; IMLE assigns generated candidates to every data point; sliced methods compare projected laws; Sinkhorn objectives differentiate through regularized transport; feature matching chooses explicit witnesses. Each method replaces an intractable pointwise likelihood with a sample-based view of distributional error. The research obligation is to make that view visible: specify its population target, witness, estimator, gradient route, solver/search approximation, deployed sampling law, and blind spots.

    Cumulative retrieval practice: the complete implicit-generator volume
    1. Distinguish a law without a density from a law whose density is merely unavailable.
    2. What does characteristicness guarantee for MMD, and what does it not guarantee?
    3. Why can unbiased MMD estimation coexist with biased model evaluation?
    4. Derive the small-noise bridge from a generated Gaussian mixture to nearest-neighbor IMLE.
    5. Why do finite sliced projections and finite feature moments have blind alternatives?
    6. What evidence is required before claiming coverage of a rare scientific region?
    7. Why does sample access not imply pathwise-gradient access?
    8. Why is the quadratic MMD U-statistic's null not handled by an ordinary alternative-standard-error calculation?
    9. What does \(1-(1-\pi)^M\) reveal about IMLE pool coverage?
    10. Why does Sinkhorn divergence subtract two self-costs?

    Solutions. (1) A singular law has no density relative to the chosen base measure; another law may have one that cannot be computed. (2) Population zero iff equality for the stated domain/measures; it does not guarantee finite-sample power or useful gradients. (3) A fixed-model estimator can be unbiased while adaptive training/model selection and reuse of data bias held-out conclusions. (4) Negative scaled log-sum-exp of Gaussian-kernel distances tends to their minimum as \(\sigma\to0\). (5) Finitely many witnesses leave distribution changes outside their span/features. (6) Region-aware splits, sufficient samples, mass/coverage and validity metrics, independent witnesses, uncertainty, memorization checks, oracle error, and prospective validation where appropriate. (7) A sampler may contain discrete, rejected, external, or hidden stateful operations and may expose neither a differentiable path nor a trace probability. (8) Under equality the U-statistic is degenerate and has a different, generally non-Gaussian limiting null; use a statistic-specific permutation, bootstrap, or justified asymptotic calibration. (9) It is the probability that a pool of size \(M\) visits a region of generator mass \(\pi\), showing how a huge pool can find a neighbor for a tiny-mass mode. (10) Entropic self-transport is generally nonzero; subtracting both self-costs removes that entropic bias from the discrepancy.
