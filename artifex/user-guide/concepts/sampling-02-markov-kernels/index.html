    2. Markov Chains and Stationarity¤

    Markov-kernel learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes finite probability and Monte Carlo estimation. By the end, you should be able to distinguish stationarity, invariance, irreducibility, aperiodicity, ergodicity, and mixing; compute the two-state examples; derive a Metropolis–Hastings kernel; compare scan/block/auxiliary Gibbs constructions; and diagnose adaptation, pseudo-marginal, bottleneck, autocorrelation, and augmentation effects on the estimator rather than only on acceptance rate.

    A Markov chain is best imagined as a moving probability distribution, not one decorative sample path. Applying the kernel repeatedly should move the law toward \(\pi\) while preserving \(\pi\) if it is already reached.

    Invariance describes the self-loop at the target. Mixing describes how quickly the other arrows approach it. Ergodicity connects the long path average to the target expectation.

    A transition kernel \(K(x,dx')\) leaves \(\pi\) invariant if

    \[ \int\pi(dx)K(x,A)=\pi(A). \]

    Detailed balance,

    \[ \pi(x)K(x,x')=\pi(x')K(x',x), \]

    is sufficient but not necessary for invariance. Summing either side over the source state proves \(\pi K=\pi\); irreducibility, aperiodicity, or a mixing-rate bound is not needed for that algebraic implication. Those conditions answer different questions. A stationary target says nothing about whether it is unique, whether a chosen initialization converges to it, or how quickly a finite run explores it.

    Time Homogeneity Is Not Stationarity¤

    A chain is time-homogeneous when it reuses the same transition kernel at every step. It is stationary when its joint law is invariant to a shift in time. For a homogeneous Markov chain, initializing \(X_0\sim\pi\) with \(\pi K=\pi\) makes every marginal equal to \(\pi\) and gives a stationary Markov process. Reusing one kernel from a point-mass initialization does not.

    For a finite transition matrix, keep four statements separate:

    1. Existence: at least one invariant probability vector exists.
    2. Uniqueness: irreducibility is sufficient; more generally, uniqueness holds when there is one closed recurrent class, even if transient states precede it.
    3. Convergence of the time-\(t\) law: a finite irreducible, aperiodic chain converges to its unique invariant law from every initial distribution.
    4. Mixing rate: the convergence theorem does not say that the required \(t\) is computationally useful; bottlenecks can make it enormous.

    On a countably infinite or general state space, finite-state existence no longer transfers automatically. For an irreducible countable chain, positive recurrence is the relevant condition for an invariant probability law; aperiodicity is additionally used for ordinary distributional convergence. Terminology such as “ergodic” varies across fields, so a theorem should list the properties it actually needs.

    Worked Two-State Boundary: Unique but Not Convergent¤

    Consider the deterministic flip chain

    \[ K= \begin{bmatrix} 0&1\\ 1&0 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    It is irreducible and has the unique invariant law \(\pi=(1/2,1/2)\), which also satisfies detailed balance. It is periodic with period two. Starting from state zero,

    \[ \delta_0K^{2t}=\delta_0, \qquad \delta_0K^{2t+1}=\delta_1, \]

    so the time-\(t\) distribution never converges to \(\pi\). Nevertheless, the empirical fraction of visits to either state converges to one half. Thus an invariant law, convergence of marginals, and convergence of path averages are different claims.

    Adding a nonzero self-loop removes the period. For

    \[ K_\varepsilon= \begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon&1-\varepsilon\\ 1-\varepsilon&\varepsilon \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 0<\varepsilon<1, \]

    the nontrivial eigenvalue is \(2\varepsilon-1\), whose magnitude is below one; the law now converges geometrically to \((1/2,1/2)\). By contrast, the identity kernel is reducible and makes every initial distribution invariant. It has many stationary laws and performs no exploration at all.

    Invariance, Ergodicity, and Mixing¤

    • Invariance: starting at \(\pi\) leaves the next state distributed as \(\pi\).
    • Ergodicity: long-run averages converge to target expectations under suitable conditions.
    • Mixing: the finite-time law approaches \(\pi\) at a useful rate.
    • Monte Carlo efficiency: correlated draws provide enough information about the observables of interest.

    A chain can be invariant but reducible, ergodic but extremely slow, or apparently stable while trapped in one metastable mode. These properties must therefore be checked at the level of the kernel and the scientific query.

    Mixing time is a theorem; warmup is a workflow¤

    For probability laws \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), use the total-variation convention

    \[ \lVert\mu-\nu\rVert_{\mathrm{TV}} =\sup_A|\mu(A)-\nu(A)| =\frac12\int|d\mu-d\nu|. \]

    For a finite chain, the worst-case \(\varepsilon\)-mixing time is

    \[ t_{\mathrm{mix}}(\varepsilon) =\min\left\{t:\sup_x \lVert K^t(x,\cdot)-\pi\rVert_{\mathrm{TV}}\le\varepsilon\right\}. \]

    This is a property of a specified kernel, target, tolerance, and state space. It is not a number that a trace plot reveals, and it is rarely known for a neural or hierarchical target.

    Practical warmup is different. During warmup, an implementation may move away from deliberately overdispersed initial states and adapt a proposal scale, mass matrix, or trajectory rule. Adaptation usually changes the transition kernel, so warmup iterations are normally excluded from estimators intended for the subsequent fixed-kernel chain. Exclusion is an accounting decision, not a proof that the retained states began in stationarity. The older word burn-in often collapses these distinct ideas:

    Table SAMP.3. Mixing time is a theorem; warmup is a workflow
    Question Correct object What discarding early states does not prove
    Has initialization bias become negligible? distance of the current law from \(\pi\) for relevant queries that every mode was found
    Has adaptation finished? a frozen or theoretically valid adaptive-kernel protocol stationarity of the current state
    Is the retained estimator precise? long-run variance, MCSE, and observable-specific ESS independence of retained draws
    Is the worst-case chain mixed? a quantitative kernel bound such as \(t_{\mathrm{mix}}\) anything from a chosen warmup length alone

    Run multiple chains from meaningfully dispersed initial conditions, retain the adaptation record, and diagnose the retained draws. A single chain that looks stable can simply be stable inside one mode.

    Bottlenecks explain why local movement can be globally slow¤

    For a finite reversible chain, define stationary flow

    \[ Q(A,A^c)=\sum_{x\in A}\sum_{y\notin A}\pi(x)K(x,y) \]

    and conductance

    \[ \Phi=\min_{0<\pi(A)\le 1/2}\frac{Q(A,A^c)}{\pi(A)}. \]

    A small \(\Phi\) means that some set carries appreciable target mass but little one-step probability flow across its boundary. Spectral and conductance bounds make this obstruction quantitative for the chains to which those theorems apply. The general lesson survives beyond the finite reversible setting: excellent local acceptance cannot compensate for a proposal that almost never crosses the scientifically important barrier. Tempering, blocking, auxiliary variables, transport, and nonlocal proposals are alternative attempts to alter that geometry.

    Correlated draws change estimator uncertainty¤

    For a stationary chain and scalar observable \(Y_t=f(X_t)\), define the lag-\(k\) autocovariance

    \[ \gamma_k=\operatorname{Cov}(Y_t,Y_{t+k}). \]

    Under conditions that make the autocovariances summable, the long-run variance is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2 &=\gamma_0+2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\gamma_k,\\ \operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_N) &\approx\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2}{N}. \end{aligned} \]

    When \(\gamma_0>0\), the integrated autocorrelation time and an observable-specific effective sample size are commonly written

    \[ \begin{aligned} \tau_{\mathrm{int}} &=\frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{LR}}^2}{\gamma_0} =1+2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\rho_k,\\ N_{\mathrm{eff}} &=\frac{N}{\tau_{\mathrm{int}}}. \end{aligned} \]

    This is not the importance-weight ESS from Section 1. It compares the variance of a correlated path average with an iid average for one declared observable. Different observables can have radically different \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}\) on the same chain; negative autocorrelation can even make \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}>N\) under this variance-equivalence definition. Estimating the infinite sum from a finite, nonstationary, or metastable run requires a window, batch-means, spectral, or related long-run-variance method. A numerical ESS cannot prove that an undiscovered mode was explored.

    Worked two-state example: the same number of states can carry different information¤

    Return to \(K_\varepsilon\) and encode its two states by \(Y_t\in\{-1,+1\}\). At stationarity, this centered observable has \(\gamma_0=1\) and lag correlation

    \[ \rho_k=(2\varepsilon-1)^k. \]

    When \(\varepsilon=3/4\), the eigenvalue is \(1/2\) and the geometric series gives

    \[ \tau_{\mathrm{int}} =1+2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\left(\frac12\right)^k=3. \]

    Thus a path of 900 states carries the same asymptotic variance for the mean of \(Y_t\) as about 300 iid draws. The chain tends to repeat its current state, so adjacent observations are redundant. With \(\varepsilon=1/4\), the eigenvalue is \(-1/2\), \(\tau_{\mathrm{int}}=1/3\), and the same variance-equivalence convention gives \(N_{\mathrm{eff}}=2700\). Alternation cancels noise in this particular mean. Neither number says how well an unrelated nonlinear observable mixes, and neither detects a separate mode that the two-state example does not contain.

    The operator view of discrete and continuous processes continues in General Markov Generators. State-space sequences, where observations and latent states arrive through time, continue in Filtering, Smoothing, and State-Space Inference.

    3. Metropolis–Hastings¤

    Suppose only an unnormalized target \(\widetilde\pi(x)\) is evaluable. From the current state \(x\), propose \(x'\sim q(x'\mid x)\) and accept with

    \[ \alpha(x,x')=\min\left( 1, \frac{\tilde\pi(x')q(x\mid x')}{\tilde\pi(x)q(x'\mid x)} \right). \]

    If the proposal is rejected, retain \(x\). The target normalizer cancels, which is why MH is useful for posterior and energy-based targets. The proposal density does not cancel unless it is symmetric.

    The acceptance probability follows from detailed balance. For \(x\ne x'\), accepted proposal flow is

    \[ \pi(x)q(x'\mid x)\min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')q(x\mid x')}{\pi(x)q(x'\mid x)}\right), \]

    which equals the corresponding reverse flow because both reduce to the minimum of the two directional proposal masses. Rejections supply the remaining self-transition probability.

    Acceptance is only one part of validity. The ratio must be defined wherever a move can occur, and the resulting chain must be able to reach every target-relevant region. It is unnecessarily strong to demand \(q(x'\mid x)>0\) for every pair of target states: a local random walk can reach a distant state through several steps. The correct question is whether the multi-step transition graph is irreducible with respect to the target's support, together with the recurrence and regularity conditions required by the claimed convergence theorem.

    Acceptance rate is meaningful here, but a high rate can indicate tiny moves and poor mixing; a low rate can indicate overly aggressive proposals. Autocorrelation, ESS, multiple-chain agreement, and task observables are also needed. The familiar random-walk target near \(0.234\) is an asymptotic result for particular high-dimensional product-like limits, not a universal tuning law.

    Proposal families answer different geometric problems¤

    Table SAMP.4. Proposal families answer different geometric problems
    Proposal Construction Useful when Characteristic failure
    random walk \(x'=x+\epsilon\) local geometry is smooth and similarly scaled dimension, anisotropy, and separated modes force small moves
    independence \(x'\sim q\) without using \(x\) \(q\) covers the target, including its tails sticky states when \(\widetilde\pi/q\) is large
    local gradient Langevin or related informed proposal a differentiable log target gives useful local direction finite-step bias without correction; curvature and multimodality remain
    transport proposal propose in coordinates learned to simplify the target a fitted map captures dependence or modes training error can leave poor overlap; a correction is still required for exactness
    discrete informed move bias a local categorical or combinatorial proposal using target differences neighboring states have evaluable energy changes local neighborhoods can retain exponential bottlenecks
    transdimensional move jointly propose a model index and dimension-matching variables the number of components or structural objects is unknown low acceptance from poorly matched cross-model proposals

    A mixture of proposals can be irreducible even when no component is. Likewise, composing invariant kernels—such as a local update followed by a global independence proposal—preserves invariance. Random mixtures and ordered compositions need their own kernel argument; it is not necessary for each component to solve the whole exploration problem.

    Adaptation must preserve an asymptotic contract¤

    Learning a covariance or proposal scale from the chain history can greatly improve performance, but the state process is then generally not a homogeneous Markov chain by itself. A vague instruction to “adapt less over time” is not a theorem. Classical adaptive-MCMC results use explicit conditions such as diminishing adaptation, where consecutive kernels become close, together with a stability condition such as containment. An alternative practical contract is to adapt during warmup, freeze the kernel, and diagnose only the fixed-kernel sampling phase.

    Never update a proposal using future retained states or report fixed-kernel diagnostics as if they automatically applied to an indefinitely adapting algorithm. State which parameters adapt, over what iterations, and under which validity result.

    Exact correction can use a random likelihood estimate¤

    Sometimes \(\widetilde\pi(x)\) contains an intractable likelihood but admits a nonnegative unbiased estimate \(\widehat L(x,U)\) with auxiliary randomness \(U\). Pseudo-marginal MH runs an ordinary MH algorithm on the augmented state \((x,U)\), targeting a joint density proportional to

    \[ p(x)\,m(u\mid x)\widehat L(x,u). \]

    Its \(x\)-marginal is the desired target because integrating over \(u\) returns the exact likelihood. Replacing the likelihood by a fresh noisy estimate inside an otherwise ordinary ratio is not automatically equivalent: the auxiliary randomness must be part of the state and proposal. Nonnegativity and unbiasedness establish the marginal identity; estimator variance controls practical stickiness.

    Delayed-acceptance schemes use a cheap approximation to reject bad proposals before an expensive exact second stage. The two acceptance stages must be derived jointly so that their product preserves the target. “A surrogate was only used for screening” is not sufficient without that correction.

    A learned proposal is not a learned target

    A flow, diffusion, autoregressive model, or variational approximation can propose states. Before correction, its outputs follow the proposal law. Importance weights or a valid MH transition can correct target mismatch under their support assumptions; training the proposer well does not make the correction optional.

    4. Gibbs, Blocks, and Auxiliary Variables¤

    Metropolis–Hastings proposes and corrects a move. Gibbs sampling uses a conditional distribution that is already compatible with the joint target. For \(x=(x_1,\ldots,x_d)\), a single-coordinate Gibbs update draws

    \[ x_j'\sim\pi(x_j\mid x_{-j}) \]

    and keeps \(x_{-j}\) fixed. Viewed as MH on coordinate \(j\), the target and proposal terms cancel, so the acceptance probability is one. That algebra proves invariance of the coordinate update; it says nothing about how far the draw moves or how quickly a sequence of coordinate updates explores the joint law.

    Scan order and parallelism are parts of the kernel¤

    • Systematic scan applies coordinate kernels in a declared order. Their composition is invariant, although it need not be reversible.
    • Random scan chooses a coordinate or block at random and applies its invariant update.
    • Blocked Gibbs draws a group jointly from its full conditional. A good block can cross strong within-group dependence that defeats scalar updates.
    • Metropolis-within-Gibbs replaces an unavailable conditional draw by an MH kernel that leaves that conditional invariant.
    • Parallel Gibbs is valid only for conditionally independent coordinates or for another explicitly derived parallel kernel. Simultaneously drawing dependent coordinates from stale conditionals generally changes the target.

    Blocking trades statistical movement against computation. One exact joint conditional can be expensive; many cheap scalar updates can be highly autocorrelated. The relevant comparison is ESS or MCSE per wall-clock and per target/gradient evaluation for declared observables, not acceptance per update.

    Worked example: a bivariate Gaussian exposes scalar Gibbs slowdown¤

    Let

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}X\\Y\end{bmatrix} \sim\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1&\rho\\\rho&1\end{bmatrix} \right),\qquad |\rho|<1. \]

    The full conditionals are

    \[ X\mid Y=y\sim\mathcal N(\rho y,1-\rho^2), \qquad Y\mid X=x\sim\mathcal N(\rho x,1-\rho^2). \]

    One systematic sweep first draws \(X_{t+1}\) from \(Y_t\) and then \(Y_{t+1}\) from \(X_{t+1}\). Substitution gives

    \[ Y_{t+1}=\rho^2Y_t+\rho\sqrt{1-\rho^2}\,\epsilon_{1,t} +\sqrt{1-\rho^2}\,\epsilon_{2,t}, \]

    with independent standard-normal noises. Thus the sweep-to-sweep lag-one correlation of \(Y\) is \(\rho^2\). It approaches one when \(|\rho|\) approaches one, even though every conditional draw is accepted. Drawing \((X,Y)\) jointly is an exact blocked update and produces an independent target draw in this toy case. Real models rarely offer that ideal block, but the example shows what blocking is trying to approximate.

    Collapsing and Rao–Blackwellization remove different randomness¤

    If a variable can be integrated out analytically, a collapsed sampler runs on the marginal target and reconstructs the removed variable conditionally when needed. This can reduce posterior dependence and dimension, as in collapsed LDA or mixture-model assignment updates. It can also make each update more global and expensive, destroy conditional independences, or complicate parallelism. Collapsing is therefore not universally faster.

    Rao–Blackwellization instead improves an estimator by replacing sampled randomness with a conditional expectation. A chain may remain unchanged while the reported query has lower variance. Keep transition design and estimator design separate.

    Augmentation can make a hard marginal easy¤

    Introduce an auxiliary variable \(U\) and a joint law \(\overline\pi(x,u)\) whose \(x\)-marginal is \(\pi(x)\). Alternating conditionals in the augmented space can be easier than updating \(x\) directly. Data augmentation, latent indicators, Polya–Gamma variables, momentum in HMC, and cluster labels in spin models all follow this pattern. The added variable does not change the desired marginal, but it can improve or worsen the augmented chain's geometry.

    For slice sampling, write an unnormalized nonnegative target as the marginal of

    \[ \overline\pi(x,u)\propto \mathbf 1\{0<u<\widetilde\pi(x)\}. \]

    Given \(x\), sample \(u\) uniformly below \(\widetilde\pi(x)\); given \(u\), sample \(x\) from the horizontal slice \(S_u=\{x:\widetilde\pi(x)>u\}\). One-dimensional stepping-out and shrinkage are implementation strategies for finding and sampling a slice, not the definition of multivariate slice sampling. In several dimensions one may update directions, coordinates, ellipses, or other blocks; there is no rule requiring one auxiliary height per coordinate.

    Cluster algorithms such as Swendsen–Wang augment certain lattice models with random bonds, then update whole connected components. They can reduce critical slowing in important regimes, but “cluster update” does not imply rapid mixing for every graph, temperature, or model.

    Applied instances continue in RBMs and LDA, Bayesian nonparametric partitions, and blocked state trajectories.

    Markov-kernel retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: stationarity, MH, and Gibbs
    1. Can a chain have a unique stationary distribution and still fail to converge from every start?
    2. Which ratio makes a Metropolis–Hastings proposal invariant to the target?
    3. Why is high acceptance not sufficient evidence of fast mixing?
    4. How do collapsing and Rao–Blackwellization remove different randomness?

    Solutions. (1) Yes; periodicity can prevent convergence, as in a deterministic two-state alternation. (2) The target-times-reverse-proposal divided by current-target-times-forward-proposal, clipped at one. (3) Tiny local moves can be accepted almost always while crossing global bottlenecks extremely slowly. (4) Collapsing integrates variables out of the transition state; Rao–Blackwellization conditions/averages an estimator over sampled auxiliary randomness.

    Markov-kernel synthesis¤

    An invariant kernel preserves a target, while convergence also requires access, recurrence, and aperiodicity conditions appropriate to the state space. Metropolis correction, blocking, collapsing, and augmentation create different kernels. Their value is measured through mixing and query-specific Monte Carlo error, not acceptance rate alone.
