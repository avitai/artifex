    6. From Model Design to Research Audit¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand all previous chapters. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to design, learn, query, and audit a structured probability model without conflating its graph, architecture, objective, or algorithm.

    The eight-part model contract¤

    For any graphical generative model, record:

    1. Variables and support. What is random, observed, latent, selected, repeated, or fixed?
    2. Reference measure. Is the law a mass function, Euclidean density, manifold density, point-process law, or mixed measure?
    3. Factorization. Which terms are normalized kernels and which are unnormalized potentials?
    4. Graph semantics. DAG, MRF, factor graph, chain graph under which convention, ADMG, plate, or computation graph?
    5. Query. Prior sample, conditional, marginal, evidence, MAP, structure, intervention, or counterfactual?
    6. Estimator. Likelihood, variational, score, ratio, pseudolikelihood, Bayesian posterior, or another declared objective?
    7. Algorithm. Enumeration, elimination, messages, MCMC, SMC, optimization, compilation, or amortization?
    8. Validation. Which exact small case, calibration target, convergence diagnostic, and misspecification check can falsify the implementation?

    This record prevents a common category error: an attractive graph can describe factor scopes while leaving the distribution improper, the query unidentified, or the proposed algorithm inexact.

    Capstone: the monitoring model from generation to diagnosis¤

    Return to

    \[ p(f,o,a,s)=p(f)p(o)p(a\mid f,o)p(s\mid f). \]

    Suppose both sensors are positive. Enumerating the four \((f,o)\) states gives unnormalized posterior weights:

    Table GRAPH.8. Capstone: the monitoring model from generation to diagnosis
    \(f\) \(o\) \(p(f)p(o)p(A=1\mid f,o)p(S=1\mid f)\)
    0 0 \(0.9(0.8)(0.01)(0.1)=0.00072\)
    0 1 \(0.9(0.2)(0.60)(0.1)=0.01080\)
    1 0 \(0.1(0.8)(0.80)(0.9)=0.05760\)
    1 1 \(0.1(0.2)(0.95)(0.9)=0.01710\)

    The evidence probability is

    \[ p(A=1,S=1)=0.08622. \]

    Hence

    \[ p(F=1\mid A=1,S=1) =\frac{0.05760+0.01710}{0.08622} \approx0.866. \]

    This calculation supplies several independent tests:

    • ancestral samples should approach the declared joint frequencies;
    • exact enumeration must normalize to one;
    • an elimination or message implementation must reproduce \(0.866\);
    • posterior samples should match all four normalized table entries; and
    • removing the \(F\to S\) edge changes both the factorization and the posterior, whereas changing the inference schedule should not change an exact answer.

    A counterfactual implementation audit¤

    Suppose code labels the arrow \(F\to A\) as causal and answers “what if the component were repaired?” by setting \(F=0\) in the observational conditional. That result is justified only if the model has declared stable structural mechanisms and the repair intervention replaces the \(F\) mechanism as assumed. A statistical DAG alone supports

    \[ p(A\mid F=0), \]

    not automatically

    \[ p(A\mid \operatorname{do}(F=0)). \]

    If fault status is confounded with an unmodeled maintenance regime, the two can differ. The correct response is to expand or qualify the causal model, not to infer intervention semantics from arrow direction.

    Diagnostics by abstraction layer¤

    Table GRAPH.9. Diagnostics by abstraction layer
    Layer A useful check A failure means
    probability law finite normalization or analytic integral the stated factors do not define the claimed law
    graph semantics hand-checked separation and factor scopes arrows or factors encode the wrong assumptions
    estimator population optimum and finite estimator derivation training may target a different object
    inference exact enumeration on a small instance implementation or approximation error
    sampler invariant-law check plus mixing diagnostics transitions may not represent target samples
    learning simulation-based parameter recovery under identifiability optimizer, estimator, or model can fail even on-model
    criticism discrepancies not used to fit the same feature blindly the model may miss scientifically relevant structure
    causality intervention/counterfactual identification audit predictive fit cannot support the causal query

    Passing one row never certifies the others. A perfectly normalized misspecified model can generate poor data. A sampler can mix well for the wrong energy. An accurate conditional predictor can be useless for an intervention.

    Misconception checks¤

    If the graph is sparse, is exact inference cheap?

    No. Sparse local degree can still produce large intermediate factors. Treewidth, state cardinalities, factor structure, and the query determine exact inference cost.

    Does an absent edge mean marginal independence?

    Not generally. In an undirected model it usually means conditional independence given all other vertices; in a DAG the relevant statement depends on d-separation. In a covariance graph, a missing bidirected edge can encode marginal independence under that graph's semantics.

    Does an MRF energy have to be positive?

    No. The unnormalized density \(e^{-E(x)}\) is positive for finite real energy, and additive energy constants cancel after normalization.

    Is pseudolikelihood just a fast way to compute likelihood?

    No. It is a different composite objective built from local conditionals. It can be statistically useful under stated conditions, but its efficiency and uncertainty differ from maximum likelihood.

    Are all arrows causal?

    No. A causal interpretation requires structural mechanisms, intervention semantics, and identifying assumptions beyond a statistical DAG factorization.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Unfaithful parameterization. Construct a linear-Gaussian DAG with two directed paths from \(X\) to \(Y\) whose effects cancel. List the graph-implied and parameter-specific independences.
    2. Hard constraint. Define a three-bit even-parity law. Determine which full conditionals exist on supported contexts and identify where a positivity-based theorem cannot be invoked.
    3. Composite likelihood. For the two-spin Ising model, derive the pseudolikelihood estimate from repeated complete observations and compare its finite objective with exact likelihood.
    4. Global normalization. Build a two-step state graph with unequal outgoing degrees. Show which history-dependent score shifts a local softmax discards and a global path normalizer retains.
    5. Latent projection. Start with a DAG containing one latent common cause and one latent mediator. Compute the observed ADMG and verify two m-separation queries.
    6. Causal boundary. Give two SCMs with the same observational DAG law but different counterfactual coupling. Explain which graph-level checks cannot distinguish them.

    Final synthesis and next steps¤

    Graphical models turn a large probability law into local objects with explicit structural meaning. Directed graphs provide normalized generative kernels and ancestral samples. D-separation identifies graph-wide conditional independences, including the exceptional behavior of colliders. Undirected graphs provide symmetric potentials, energies, and local full conditionals but usually introduce a global normalizer. Learning, conversion, and causal interpretation each add assumptions that a drawing cannot supply.

    The next foundation volume, Graphical Inference and Message Passing, takes correctly declared factor scopes and derives elimination, exact tree messages, junction trees, loopy approximations, and learned solvers. Use Energy-Based Models for nonlinear energies and MCMC training, Probabilistic Circuits for representation-level tractability, Random Graphs and Relational Generators when the graph itself is generated, and Causal Generative Models when mechanisms and interventions—not only conditional independence—define the query.
