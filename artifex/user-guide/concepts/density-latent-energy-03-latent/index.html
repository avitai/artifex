    3. Latent Marginals: VAEs, Hierarchies, and Diffusion¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes latent-variable probability; the ELBO derivation is reviewed before it is used. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate joint and marginal tractability: prove that a normalized, sampleable joint need not expose its observed likelihood or posterior.
    2. O2 — Interpret free energy: derive an observed energy by integrating a latent joint and identify the hidden log-sum-exp computation.
    3. O3 — Audit variational substitutions: distinguish the generative model, the ELBO objective, the inference approximation, and their errors.
    4. O4 — Derive posterior score identities: use Fisher's identity and the missing-information Hessian identity under stated regularity.
    5. O5 — Connect denoising and posterior means: derive Tweedie's identity and distinguish conditional corruption scores from marginal noisy scores.
    6. O6 — Transfer layered-latent reasoning: trace likelihood, support, and approximation through diffusion paths and learned codecs.

    Joint normalization versus marginal tractability¤

    A latent-variable model defines

    \[ p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z), \qquad p_\theta(x)=\int p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)dz. \]

    The joint can be normalized and easy to sample while the marginal likelihood and posterior

    \[ p_\theta(z\mid x)=\frac{p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)}{p_\theta(x)} \]

    remain intractable. The observed-space energy is

    \[ E_X(x)=-\log\int e^{-E_{XZ}(x,z)}dz, \]

    a negative log-sum-exp or free energy over latent states.

    The identity assumes a common dominating measure and a finite, positive integral at the observed \(x\). If latent support depends on \(x\), differentiating the marginal can introduce boundary terms; the posterior identities below are not licensed merely by writing an integral sign.

    Variational inference changes the objective, not the model identity¤

    For any \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\),

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\mathcal L(x;\theta,\phi) +D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)), \]

    where

    \[ \mathcal L =\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)]. \]

    A VAE is not “an approximate distribution” merely because it uses an ELBO: its generative joint is exact as specified, while likelihood learning and posterior inference use approximations.

    The distinction is operational. Sampling \(z\sim p(z)\) and then \(x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z)\) is exact sampling from the specified joint even when \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is poor. Conversely, a tight ELBO on observed data does not make every posterior functional or out-of-distribution sample accurate.

    Fisher identity for the observed score¤

    Under differentiation/integration regularity,

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_\theta(x) =\mathbb E_{p_\theta(z\mid x)} [\nabla_x\log p_\theta(x,z)]. \]

    The marginal score is a posterior average of joint scores. Replacing the true posterior with an amortized approximation yields a biased score unless the approximation is exact.

    Differentiating once more gives the observed-information or missing-information identity

    \[ \nabla_x^2\log p_\theta(x) =\mathbb E[\nabla_x^2\log p_\theta(x,z)\mid x] +\operatorname{Cov}(\nabla_x\log p_\theta(x,z)\mid x). \]

    The positive-semidefinite covariance term shows why the curvature of a marginal is not merely the posterior mean of complete-data curvature. This is the \(x\)-derivative analog of the missing-information decomposition associated with Louis's observed-information identity.

    Worked Gaussian latent model¤

    Let \(z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(x\mid z\sim\mathcal N(z,\sigma^2)\). Then

    \[ x\sim\mathcal N(0,1+\sigma^2), \qquad s(x)=-\frac{x}{1+\sigma^2}. \]

    The posterior is Gaussian with

    \[ z\mid x\sim\mathcal N\left( \frac{x}{1+\sigma^2},\frac{\sigma^2}{1+\sigma^2} \right). \]

    Averaging the joint derivative

    \[ \nabla_x\log p(x,z)=-\frac{x-z}{\sigma^2} \]

    under that posterior gives exactly \(-x/(1+\sigma^2)\).

    The Hessian identity also checks. The complete-data curvature is \(-1/\sigma^2\), while the posterior variance of the complete-data score is \(1/[\sigma^2(1+\sigma^2)]\); their sum is \(-1/(1+\sigma^2)\), the marginal Gaussian curvature.

    Gaussian corruption, Tweedie's identity, and denoising¤

    Let \(Y=X+\tau\epsilon\) with \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\) and noisy marginal \(p_\tau(y)\). Applying Fisher's identity to the latent clean variable gives

    \[ \nabla_y\log p_\tau(y) =\frac{\mathbb E[X\mid Y=y]-y}{\tau^2}, \qquad \mathbb E[X\mid Y=y] =y+\tau^2\nabla_y\log p_\tau(y). \]

    This is Tweedie's identity in the Gaussian location model. It explains why a denoiser and a noisy-marginal score encode one another at population optimum. It does not say that the sample-level conditional target \(\nabla_y\log p(y\mid x)=(x-y)/\tau^2\) equals the marginal score for each training pair. Efron's treatment of Tweedie's formula provides the empirical-Bayes perspective, while Vincent's denoising score-matching analysis establishes the score-matching connection.

    Diffusion as a hierarchical latent model¤

    A finite-step diffusion generative model defines

    \[ p_\theta(x_{0:T}) =p(x_T)\prod_{t=1}^{T}p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t). \]

    The intermediate states \(x_{1:T}\) are latent for the observed datum \(x_0\). Its marginal is

    \[ p_\theta(x_0)=\int p_\theta(x_{0:T})dx_{1:T}, \]

    and the forward noising chain commonly supplies the variational distribution used for the ELBO. This is an algebraic latent-variable formulation of a particular discrete diffusion model. It does not make every VAE training algorithm or latent representation equivalent to diffusion.

    The original denoising diffusion probabilistic-model construction makes this variational path explicit. Later score/SDE formulations reorganize the same broad ingredients around time-indexed marginals and reverse dynamics; the relationship label must be assigned to the exact formulation being compared.

    In continuous time, the score \(\nabla_x\log p_t(x)\) belongs to the noisy marginal at time \(t\). It is not generally the conditional corruption score for one clean \(x_0\), although denoising-score objectives have matching population optima under the stated expectation identity.

    Learned codecs and nested latent systems¤

    Latent diffusion first maps data to a learned representation \(z_0\) and then runs diffusion in that space. There are two latent layers: codec uncertainty/distortion and diffusion path variables. Likelihood, support, and counterfactual claims must account for the decoder and representation, not only the latent sampler.

    Independent practice and assessment map: latents and free energy¤

    Attempt the posterior calculations exactly. When an approximation is introduced, name the exact quantity it replaces and the resulting error.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: Gaussian posterior. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. In the worked model set \(\sigma^2=3\) and observe \(x=2\). Predict the sign of the marginal score, then compute the posterior mean and variance, marginal score, and posterior mean of the complete-data score.
    2. P1.2 — Verify missing information. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. For the same \(\sigma^2=3\) model, compute the complete-data \(x\)-Hessian, posterior variance of the complete-data score, and their sum. Compare with the marginal \(x\)-Hessian.
    3. P1.3 — Derive a two-point Tweedie example. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 14 min. Let clean \(X\) be uniform on \(\{-1,1\}\), \(Y=X+\epsilon\), \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\), and \(y=\tfrac12\log3\). Compute posterior odds, \(\mathbb E[X\mid y]\), the noisy-marginal score from Tweedie's identity, and the expectation of the two conditional corruption scores.
    4. P1.4 — Quantify a variational gap. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. At one observation suppose \(p(z=1\mid x)=0.8\), \(q(z=1\mid x)=0.5\), and \(\log p(x)=-1\). Compute \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)\) and the corresponding ELBO. State which distribution remains the specified generative model.
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose a diffusion equivalence claim. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. A paper says “diffusion is just a VAE, so any VAE encoder is the reverse diffusion posterior.” Classify the exact finite-chain statement, then list the architecture, posterior, objective, and sampler claims that do not follow.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a learned codec. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Specify a stochastic codec \(p_\psi(x\mid z_0)\) with a diffusion prior over \(z_0\). Draw the augmented joint, identify every latent variable, state what must be integrated for \(p(x)\), and design separate diagnostics for codec distortion, latent-prior fit, and reverse-path error.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The score is negative. The posterior is \(\mathcal N(2/4,3/4)=\mathcal N(1/2,3/4)\), and the marginal \(\mathcal N(0,4)\) score at two is \(-2/4=-1/2\). The posterior mean complete score is \(-\mathbb E[2-z\mid x]/3=-(2-1/2)/3=-1/2\). Check. Fisher's identity holds exactly.
    2. P1.2. The complete-data Hessian is \(-1/3\). The score variance is \(\operatorname{Var}(z\mid x)/9=(3/4)/9=1/12\). Their sum is \(-1/3+1/12=-1/4\), equal to the marginal \(\mathcal N(0,4)\) log-density Hessian. Check. The missing-information covariance makes the observed log density less negatively curved.
    3. P1.3. The posterior odds are \(e^{2y}=3\), so the posterior probabilities of \(1\) and \(-1\) are \(3/4\) and \(1/4\), giving \(\mathbb E[X\mid y]=1/2\). Tweedie's identity gives \(s_Y(y)=1/2-y=1/2-(\log3)/2\). The conditional scores are \(1-y\) and \(-1-y\); their posterior mean is \((3/4)(1-y)+(1/4)(-1-y)=1/2-y\). Check. Neither conditional target alone equals the marginal score.
    4. P1.4. The gap is \(\tfrac12\log(0.5/0.8)+\tfrac12\log(0.5/0.2)=\tfrac12\log(25/16)\approx0.22314\). Therefore \(\mathcal L=-1-0.22314\approx-1.22314\). The specified generative model remains \(p_\theta(x,z)\); \(q\) is an inference distribution used in the bound. Check. A looser bound did not denormalize the joint.
    5. P1.5. A finite reverse diffusion \(p(x_T)\prod_t p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\) is exactly a hierarchical latent joint for observed \(x_0\). It does not imply an arbitrary VAE encoder equals the chain posterior, that objectives or architectures match, that latent semantics match, or that one-step VAE generation equals an iterative diffusion sampler. Check. The defensible label is algebraic formulation, not implementation identity.
    6. P1.6. A valid joint is \(p(z_T)\prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(z_{t-1}\mid z_t)p_\psi(x\mid z_0)\); \(z_{0:T}\) are latent for \(x\), with \(z_0\) the codec representation and \(z_{1:T}\) path latents. Computing \(p(x)\) integrates them all. Report observation reconstruction or rate–distortion diagnostics for the codec, held-out latent statistics for the prior, and transition calibration or solver diagnostics for the reverse path. Rubric. Full credit does not attribute decoder distortion to the diffusion sampler.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Latent models move tractability from observed likelihood to a normalized joint plus inference problem. Diffusion admits a hierarchical latent formulation, while its score interface provides a different route to sampling. Chapter 4 derives the exact bridge between energy and score and explains when a score field corresponds to any density.

    Is a diffusion model simply a VAE?

    A finite diffusion chain can be written as a hierarchical latent-variable model and trained with a variational bound. That exact formulation does not equate architectures, inference networks, objectives, or samplers across all VAEs and diffusions.
