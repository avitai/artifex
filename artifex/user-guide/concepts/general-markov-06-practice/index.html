    6. Comparison, Evidence, and Research Directions¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes the full operator and simulation framework. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Type-check unification claims: identify the exact object shared by flows, diffusions, jumps, and hybrids and the family-specific properties not transferred.
    2. O2 — Build a validation ladder: test operator validity, infinitesimal action, marginal evolution, transition/path behavior, and finite deployment separately.
    3. O3 — Compare compute fairly: match physical horizon and report NFE, stages, proposals, accepted events, latency, memory, and hardware.
    4. O4 — Appraise evidence maturity: distinguish classical theorem, peer-reviewed modern result, workshop result, preprint, empirical claim, and local reproduction.
    5. O5 — Design decisive benchmarks: use exact finite chains, solvable diffusions, and controlled hybrids to localize error sources.
    6. O6 — Transfer the review protocol: write a publication-ready claim, evidence, and reproducibility audit for a new general-generator method.

    What the framework unifies¤

    Table MARKOV.2. What the framework unifies
    Model Generator component Forward equation Learned object
    flow matching first-order drift continuity velocity
    score diffusion drift plus second order Fokker–Planck score/drift/target
    discrete diffusion jump-rate matrix master equation ratios/rates/posterior
    birth/death generator nonlocal dimension-changing jump master equation event intensity and proposal
    hybrid multimodal sum/coupling of components adjoint of joint generator constrained joint coefficients

    The table is an operator-level unification. It does not equalize likelihood access, endpoint coupling, path regularity, simulator cost, or architecture.

    Use the following comparison questions before collapsing two rows:

    Table MARKOV.21. What the framework unifies — table 2
    Layer Question Exact small-system oracle
    state and operator Are domain, boundary, positivity, covariance/rate constraints valid? rate-matrix signs/rows; PSD eigenvalues; boundary flux
    infinitesimal action Does the implementation reproduce \(L\varphi\) on a separating test set? finite differences of \(P_h\varphi\) as \(h\downarrow0\)
    marginal path Does \(d\mathbb E\varphi(X_t)/dt=\mathbb E L_t\varphi(X_t)\) hold? exact moments or matrix exponential
    transition/path law Are conditional transitions and multi-time observables correct? \(e^{(t-s)Q}\); autocovariance; event-time law
    learning Does the sampled target regress to the intended coefficient? enumerated posterior conditional expectation
    finite deployment Does the solver converge without hidden clipping/truncation? mesh, tolerance, event-cap, and state-space refinement

    Likelihood access is especially family specific. Invertible ODE flows can use a change-of-variables trace integral; diffusions and jump processes generally use pathwise, variational, transition-density, or specialized likelihood tools. Generator Matching unifies a conditional learning pattern, not a universal exact-likelihood estimator.

    Birth, death, add, thin, and move generators can evolve finite point configurations, but an operator alone does not declare the endpoint point-process law or its reference measure. The configuration target, likelihood, and process-specific diagnostics are developed in Point Processes and Random Finite Sets.

    Evidence Audit¤

    • Generator Matching, ICLR 2025 — Established modern foundation / peer-reviewed for the general conditional-generator framework, superposition, and reported image and multimodal experiments.
    • Energy-Based Generator Matching, NeurIPS 2025 — Peer-reviewed research for estimating generator-matching targets from an energy oracle using self-normalized importance sampling, demonstrated on bounded discrete and hybrid benchmarks; it does not make energy-only training unbiased or solve arbitrary high-dimensional mixing.
    • Exploring Diffusion and Flow Matching under Generator Matching — Preprint comparison; useful analysis, not an independently peer-reviewed foundation by the cutoff.
    • Statistical Analysis of Markovian Generative Modeling, April 2026 — Preprint / lecture notes connecting generator learning error with finite-sample and stability questions. It is not labeled peer-reviewed research.
    • Latent Process Generator Matching, ICLR 2026 DeLTa — Workshop research extending the projection argument from static latent variables to latent Markov processes; it is not labeled main-conference evidence.
    • Neural CTMC, April 2026 — Preprint separating reverse jump timing from destination distributions and factorizing the path-space objective; acceptance was not verified by the cutoff.
    • Optimal Inference Schedules and Efficient Sampling with Discrete Diffusion, COLT 2026 — Peer-reviewed research analyses for particular masked/uniform discrete generators and error controls, not arbitrary Markov processes.

    The evidence audit found submitted and workshop Markov-generator applications, but none is promoted solely for being recent. Venue and acceptance status remain explicit.

    The audit cutoff for the statuses above is 2026-07-20. The PMLR proceedings pages confirm the two COLT 2026 papers; the OpenReview records identify Generator Matching as ICLR 2025 and Energy-Based Generator Matching as a NeurIPS 2025 poster; the arXiv pages label the April 2026 items as submissions. A future venue appearance should update the label rather than leave both an obsolete preprint claim and a new citation in parallel.

    Evaluation contract¤

    State the process class, state space, base measure, generator domain, boundary conditions, endpoint path, and whether the learned coefficients guarantee validity. Report objective weighting, time sampling, simulator, NFE/event count, tolerances, wall time, memory, hardware, invalid-event handling, and endpoint metrics. For mixed generators, include ablations and splitting order.

    A minimum decisive benchmark suite¤

    1. Two-state CTMC: exact matrix exponential, reverse rates, uniformization, and event frequencies expose sign, transpose, and clock bugs.
    2. Ornstein--Uhlenbeck diffusion: exact Gaussian moments and transitions expose score, reverse-time, and weak-solver error.
    3. State-dependent one-dimensional diffusion: checks covariance-divergence terms hidden by constant-noise formulas.
    4. Pure birth/death chain: exposes non-explosion assumptions, boundary at zero, tau-leap negativity, and state truncation.
    5. Noncommuting finite generators: distinguishes exact sum dynamics from Lie/Strang splitting and path-law mixtures.
    6. Coupled discrete/continuous toy system: tests resets, shared events, equivariance/invariants, and multimodal dependence before large models hide failures.

    For every benchmark, separate an exact-coefficient simulator test from a learned-coefficient test. Otherwise an accurate network can be blamed for a bad solver, or an exact simulator can conceal that the learning target was wrong.

    Worked end-to-end two-state validation¤

    Take the true rates \((a,b)=(2,1)\) and horizon \(t=\log2/3\). Chapter 1 gives

    \[ P_t=\begin{pmatrix}2/3&1/3\\1/6&5/6\end{pmatrix}. \]

    Suppose a learned generator has \((\widehat a,\widehat b)=(2.1,0.9)\). Its total rate is still three, so at the same horizon

    \[ \widehat P_t =\begin{pmatrix}0.65&0.35\\0.15&0.85\end{pmatrix}. \]

    The maximum row-wise total-variation error is \(1/60\approx0.0167\). This is the coefficient/model error under exact exponentiation. Next simulate the true \(Q\) and \(\widehat Q\) separately with exact events or uniformization; confidence intervals around their empirical transition frequencies quantify Monte Carlo error. Finally run the deployed approximate sampler and compare it with \(\widehat P_t\); that difference isolates numerical error. Comparing only the deployed samples with \(P_t\) would merge all three.

    Misconception checks: evidence and evaluation¤

    • “A broad theorem validates a particular network.” The coefficients must satisfy the theorem's hypotheses and the implementation must realize them.
    • “A better endpoint metric proves a better generator.” Different paths and even invalid intermediate dynamics can share endpoint samples.
    • “One hundred steps is compute matched.” A step can contain different neural evaluations, event proposals, linear solves, or rejected stages.
    • “Recent means stronger evidence.” Venue, assumptions, reproducibility, and relevance determine support; date alone does not.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Derive \(\mathcal L\) and \(\mathcal L^*\) for a one-dimensional drift–diffusion.
    2. Recover the two-state CTMC transition matrix \(e^{tQ}\) and compare it with tau-leaping.
    3. Show that coordinate test functions identify drift but not diffusion covariance.
    4. Superpose a birth process and deterministic decay, then simulate by a joint event/ODE scheme.
    5. Derive the reversed rates of a three-state CTMC from known \(p_t\).
    6. Compare \(e^{h(L_1+L_2)}\) with Lie and Strang splitting and measure noncommutation error.
    Exercise 3 solution

    For \(\varphi(x)=x_i\), the Hessian vanishes, so \(\mathcal L\varphi=b_i\). Quadratic tests \(\varphi(x)=x_ix_j\) introduce Hessian terms and expose the covariance \(a\).

    Independent practice and assessment map: evidence and validation¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: classify six claims. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. Classify each by the strongest supported object: (a) two rate matrices have equal row sums; (b) two generators agree on coordinate tests; © two processes share all one-time marginals; (d) two methods share a population minimizer; (e) two exact semigroups agree; (f) two seeded programs return identical arrays.
    2. P1.2 — Reproduce the two-state error oracle. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Derive \(\widehat P_t\) for learned rates \((2.1,0.9)\) at \(t=\log2/3\) and compute row-wise total variation against the true \((2,1)\) semigroup.
    3. P1.3 — Design a falsification ladder. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. A new model sums a state-dependent diffusion and graph jump generator. Order at least six tests from structural validity to realistic samples, and state which failure each localizes.
    4. P1.4 — Compare compute beyond step counts. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Sampler A uses 20 classical RK4 steps and one neural call per stage. Sampler B proposes 120 events, accepts 75, and uses one neural call per proposal. Compute NFE and report what additional measurements are required before calling either cheaper.
    5. P1.5 — Appraise an evidence claim. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. A workshop paper reports improved image metrics for a hybrid generator, while a theorem in a different paper proves finite-state CTMC convergence under bounded exact rates. Write one justified empirical claim and one unjustified generalization.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a publication review. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 22 min. Draft a review checklist for a new manifold jump-diffusion generator: theorem assumptions, evidence labels, baselines, exact toys, geometry, simulator, compute, ablations, uncertainty, artifacts, and claim wording.

    Feedback contract: evidence and validation¤

    Table MARKOV.22. Feedback contract: evidence and validation — table 1
    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 Every statement is assigned to the narrowest correct level and no implication is silently promoted.
    P1.2 Both learned transition rows and maximum TV \(1/60\) are reproduced.
    P1.3 Structural, infinitesimal, marginal, transition/path, learning, numerical, and realistic layers are independently testable.
    P1.4 NFE values \(80\) and \(120\) are correct and latency/hardware/event overhead remain unresolved.
    P1.5 The empirical statement stays within the workshop experiment and the bounded finite-state theorem is not generalized to arbitrary hybrids.
    P1.6 Every requested review dimension has a concrete evidence item and a scoped failure condition.
    Graduated hints for P1
    Table MARKOV.23. Feedback contract: evidence and validation — table 2
    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup
    P1.1 Equal row sums may only show both kill constants. Semigroup equality is stronger than marginal equality; byte equality is a software claim.
    P1.2 The learned total rate remains three. Its stationary law is \((0.3,0.7)\) and the transient factor is \(1/2\).
    P1.3 Begin with signs, PSD structure, domain, and boundary. End with separate exact-coefficient and learned-coefficient solver studies.
    P1.4 RK4 has four stages. NFE omits event bookkeeping, control flow, memory traffic, and device utilization.
    P1.5 Separate what was observed from what was proved. State space and bounded-rate assumptions block the transfer.
    P1.6 Use one row per four-contract layer and evidence maturity. Require a claim to name its state space, metric, and finite compute budget.

    Table MARKOV.24. Outcome-to-task alignment: evidence and validation
    Learning objective Independently assessed by

    O2 — Build a validation ladder P1.2, P1.3
    O3 — Compare compute fairly P1.4, P1.6

    O6 — Transfer the review protocol P1.6
    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. (a) Both annihilate constants under the row convention; this does not establish rate validity. (b) They agree only on the drift exposed by coordinates. © They share a marginal path. (d) They share a population objective optimum. (e) They share every finite-time transition kernel in the declared homogeneous setting and hence the Markov path law under standard construction. (f) They have byte-level finite-program equality for that environment and seed. Check. None of (a)--(d) implies (e) or (f).
    2. P1.2. With stationary law \((0.3,0.7)\) and transient factor \(1/2\), \(\widehat P_t=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0.65&0.35\\0.15&0.85\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\). For two-point rows, TV is the absolute difference in either one coordinate: both rows differ from truth by \(1/60\). Check. Row sums remain one, so the error is not a validity failure.
    3. P1.3. A defensible order is: graph support/rate signs and covariance PSD; domain/boundary/tangency; finite-difference \(L\varphi\) tests; exact marginal moments; exact transition and multi-time observables; enumerated conditional-target regression; exact-coefficient solver refinement; learned-coefficient refinement; then realistic quality, constraints, and compute. Each layer localizes respectively structure, geometry, implementation of the operator, law evolution, temporal law, learning, numerics, interaction error, and external performance. Rubric. Endpoint samples alone cannot replace any earlier layer.
    4. P1.4. A uses \(20\times4=80\) NFE and B uses \(120\) NFE, not 75. Compare wall-clock distributions, batch size, device, precision, memory, proposal/event bookkeeping, rejected ODE steps if any, compiler, and output quality at a matched physical horizon. Check. Accepted events measure path activity, not total neural compute.
    5. P1.5. Justified: “In the workshop's stated image benchmarks and implementation, the reported hybrid improved the named metrics over its baselines.” Unjustified: “The bounded-rate finite-state convergence theorem proves arbitrary state-dependent multimodal jump diffusions converge or explains the image improvement.” Check. Empirical scope and theorem assumptions remain attached.
    6. P1.6. The checklist records a tangent/domain and well-posedness theorem with exact assumptions; labels every result by venue and reproduction status; compares intrinsic diffusion, jump-only, and matched hybrid baselines; includes sphere/finite-graph exact toys; tests equivariance and manifold constraint error; declares reverse formula and solver; reports NFE/events/latency/memory/hardware; ablates components and splitting order; gives confidence intervals and seeds; releases configurations/checkpoints/logs; and phrases conclusions for the tested manifold, metric, and budget. Rubric. A citation list without assumption-to-claim mapping is incomplete.

    Primary reference trail¤

    • Generator Matching, ICLR 2025: conditional generators, Bregman objectives, superposition, and multimodal construction.
    • Statistical Analysis of Markovian Generative Modeling: 2026 lecture-note derivations of weak forward equations, conditional projection, operator classes, and stability; Preprint / lecture notes.
    • A Continuous Time Framework for Discrete Denoising: CTMC corruption, reverse rates, ELBO, and simulation.
    • Davis (1984), Piecewise-Deterministic Markov Processes: deterministic motion with random events and extended generators.
    • Gillespie (1977), Exact Stochastic Simulation: event-driven exact simulation for coupled reaction CTMCs.
    • Lévy Processes and Stochastic Calculus, generator chapter: semigroups, Lévy–Khintchine generators, and the scope of Courrège-type representations.
    Cumulative retrieval: general Markov generators
    1. What common mathematical interface unifies flows, diffusions, and jump processes?
    2. Which properties remain family-specific despite that interface?
    3. Why is matching terminal samples insufficient to validate a learned generator?
    4. What should a compute-matched comparison report for a hybrid jump–diffusion?

    Solutions. (1) Their infinitesimal action on an appropriate class of test functions and the induced forward evolution of laws. (2) Path regularity, noise type, event structure, generator domain, reversal formula, and simulator. (3) Different dynamics can share endpoints while disagreeing on paths, transitions, likelihoods, and conditional queries. (4) Drift/diffusion evaluations, event proposals and accepted jumps, solver tolerances, wall time, hardware/precision, truncation, and end-to-end sample metrics.

    Final synthesis¤

    Infinitesimal generators provide the broadest rigorous bridge among flows, diffusions, and jumps. The abstraction succeeds when it preserves each operator's admissibility, reversal, and simulation requirements. It fails when “same generator framework” is shortened to “same model or cost.”

    Continue to the transitions and few-step generation volume, where infinitesimal generators are replaced or distilled into long-jump maps.

    That continuation is Transitions and Few-Step Generative Models, which compares deterministic maps, stochastic kernels, MeanFlow, Transition Matching, and distillation under one compute contract.
