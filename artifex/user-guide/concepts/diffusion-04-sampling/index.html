    Sampling Methods¤

    Sampling Methods: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. distinguish ancestral, implicit, ODE, SDE, predictor–corrector, and consistency sampling;
    2. count NFE separately from solver steps and decoder calls;
    3. state the assumptions behind solver order;
    4. convert CFG conventions without an off-by-one error; and
    5. evaluate inverse-problem or posterior guidance as a target distribution rather than a visual trick.

    Four axes of diffusion acceleration¤

    “Faster diffusion” can mean changing the numerical algorithm, the learned model, the state in which generation occurs, or the training problem. Those changes are not interchangeable. A system may combine all four, so first record which object differs from the baseline.

    Table DIFF.8. Four axes of diffusion acceleration
    Axis What changes? Representative constructions What must remain matched before attributing a gain?
    1. Numerical solver or evaluation schedule The pretrained denoiser, score, or velocity field can stay fixed while evaluation times, update formulas, order, stochasticity, or solver coefficients change. DDIM uses a non-Markovian construction with the DDPM training objective; DPM-Solver and related methods integrate a diffusion ODE with specialized formulas. S4S learns solver parameters against a teacher while retaining the pretrained generative field, so it belongs on this axis but is not training-free. Checkpoint, prediction parameterization, guidance rule, initial law, output representation, tolerance or grid, and quality target.
    2. Learned finite transition or student The deployed generator changes: it learns an endpoint map, finite-time map, average velocity, or stochastic transition rather than repeatedly applying the original local field. Progressive distillation, consistency models, flow-map methods, MeanFlow, and Transition Matching. Teacher and target behavior, student architecture, distillation data and cost, outer transitions, internal head calls, and paired as well as distributional error.
    3. State representation Generation moves between pixels, continuous latents, discrete codes, cascades, or another compressed state space. A codec or renderer becomes part of the generator. Latent diffusion, learned video codecs, cascaded diffusion, and token-space models. Codec rate and distortion, latent shape, encoder/decoder and cascade cost, reconstruction ceiling, resolution, and whether the compared data laws live in the same space.
    4. Training target, probability path, or parameterization The learned approximation problem changes: for example, its target, time weighting, preconditioning, interpolation path, or endpoint convention. Noise, data, score, and VP velocity prediction; EDM preconditioning; flow matching and rectified paths. Training data and compute, loss weighting, time sampling, path, endpoint law, architecture, and sampler. Algebraic target conversions for exact functions do not make finite trained networks or their losses identical.

    Backbone architecture, compilation, attention kernels, caching, pruning, quantisation, parallelism, and hardware form an orthogonal systems axis. They alter the cost of one logical evaluation and sometimes its numerical error, but do not by themselves specify which probability law or finite transition is learned.

    Peer-reviewed research: GAS, an ICLR 2026 paper, makes the boundary between axes visible: it parameterizes a generalized ODE solver, distils teacher behavior, and adds adversarial training. It is therefore a hybrid learned-solver system, not merely a training-free integrator swap. Its reported gains remain specific to the checkpoints, resource constraints, baselines, and metrics in that paper.

    Do not multiply headline speedups

    A solver may reduce evaluations, a latent codec may reduce the tensor size, and a fused kernel may reduce time per evaluation. Their gains need not multiply: bottlenecks move, memory traffic overlaps, batching changes utilization, and decoding becomes a larger fraction of latency. Compare the complete systems at a matched quality and conditioning protocol.

    Compute ledger: from outer transitions to end-to-end latency¤

    Let \(K\) be the number of outer transitions. At transition \(i\), let \(m_i\) be the number of principal-network stages, \(b_{ij}\) the number of logical guidance branches at stage \(j\), and \(\ell_{ij}\) the number of launched backbone calls after batching or fusion. In the usual serial-versus-fully-batched case, \(1\leq\ell_{ij}\leq b_{ij}\). Two useful counts are

    \[ E_{\mathrm{branch}} =\sum_{i=1}^{K}\sum_{j=1}^{m_i} b_{ij}, \qquad L_{\mathrm{backbone}} =\sum_{i=1}^{K}\sum_{j=1}^{m_i}\ell_{ij}. \]

    \(E_{\mathrm{branch}}\) counts branch-equivalent evaluations; \(L_{\mathrm{backbone}}\) counts actual launches. NFE ordinarily refers to evaluations of the learned field, but reports differ in whether fused guidance branches, multi-stage formulas, or nested heads count separately. A report must therefore define its NFE convention and record launched calls as an additional systems quantity. A complete ledger also records:

    • calls to a transition head, corrector, classifier, reward model, or safety model;
    • conditioning-encoder, codec-encoder, codec-decoder, cascade, renderer, and reranker calls;
    • tensor shapes, batch including fused guidance branches, precision, sparsity, compiler, and hardware;
    • peak memory and memory traffic, which cannot be inferred from call counts;
    • training or distillation cost when the deployed checkpoint has changed; and
    • quality, coverage, conditioning, and output resolution at the reported operating point.

    The wall-clock decomposition is implementation-specific, but it should expose at least

    \[ T_{\mathrm{end\text{-}to\text{-}end}} =T_{\mathrm{condition}} +\sum_{i=1}^{K}\sum_{j=1}^{m_i}T^{\mathrm{backbone}}_{ij} +T_{\mathrm{heads}} +T_{\mathrm{codec/cascade}} +T_{\mathrm{rerank/safety}} +T_{\mathrm{I/O}}. \]

    This is an accounting identity, not a claim that unlike calls have equal FLOPs or can be added into a universal NFE.

    Worked counterexample: eight reported steps can be faster than four¤

    Consider two hypothetical implementations producing the same output shape on the same device. This arithmetic example makes no quality claim.

    Table DIFF.9. Worked counterexample: eight reported steps can be faster than four
    System Outer transitions \(K\) Backbone stages per transition CFG branches per stage Branch handling Branch evaluations \(E_{\mathrm{branch}}\) Backbone launches \(L_{\mathrm{backbone}}\) Backbone time Decoder End to end
    A 4 2 2 serial, 10 ms per branch 16 16 160 ms 20 ms 180 ms
    B 8 1 2 fused, 12 ms per two-branch launch 16 8 96 ms 20 ms 116 ms

    System A is the “four-step” system, yet System B is faster in this stated implementation. Both have 16 branch-equivalent evaluations; their actual launch counts, batch shapes, and latency differ. Reversing the fusion efficiency or changing the decoder could reverse the conclusion. Therefore step count is a structural descriptor, not a portable speed measurement. The few-step volume applies the same ledger to learned maps and nested transition heads.

    DDPM Sampling: The Iterative Reverse Process¤

    The foundational DDPM sampling starts from pure noise \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\) and iteratively denoises:

    \[ x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \right) + \sigma_t z \]

    where \(z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\) and \(\sigma_t\) controls stochasticity.

    Algorithm:

    1. Sample \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\)
    2. For \(t = T, T-1, \ldots, 1\):
    3. Predict noise: \(\hat{\epsilon} = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\)
    4. Compute mean: \(\mu_t = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}} \hat{\epsilon} \right)\)
    5. Sample: \(x_{t-1} = \mu_t + \sigma_t z\)
    6. Return \(x_0\)

    Characteristics:

    • Stochastic: Introduces randomness at each step.
    • Original cost: the historical chain uses about \(T=1000\) network evaluations, but latency depends on architecture, resolution, decoder, dtype, batching, compiler, and hardware.
    • Quality: increasing steps can reduce solver error until model error or another bottleneck dominates; it is not monotone for every sampler and guidance scale.
    • Comparisons: NFE ratios are not wall-clock speedups. Few-step solvers and distilled students change the operating point and sometimes the trained model itself.

    Why a large reverse jump changes the statistical problem¤

    A coarse reverse conditional can be multimodal even when a fine-step model is built from simple local transitions. Consider a one-dimensional data law

    \[ \Pr(X_0=-1)=\Pr(X_0=+1)=\tfrac12 \]

    and an independent corruption \(E\in\{-1,+1\}\) with equal probabilities, followed by \(X_t=X_0+E\). The event \(X_t=0\) has probability \(1/2\): it occurs through either \((X_0,E)=(-1,+1)\) or \((+1,-1)\), with equal joint mass. Bayes' rule therefore gives

    \[ \Pr(X_0=-1\mid X_t=0) =\Pr(X_0=+1\mid X_t=0) =\tfrac12. \]

    The exact coarse reverse conditional is therefore two point masses, not one Gaussian centered at zero. A Gaussian transition can match its conditional mean and perhaps its variance, but it cannot represent the two atoms. In higher-dimensional data, a large skip can similarly merge many plausible clean explanations into one reverse conditional.

    This example separates two acceleration strategies:

    1. A numerical solver takes larger steps while evaluating a learned marginal score, denoiser, or velocity field. Its error is a discretization-and-model-error question.
    2. A learned coarse transition tries to represent the finite conditional directly. Its expressivity and training target are additional statistical questions.

    An exact local score does not make an arbitrary coarse discretization exact, and a one-step Gaussian reverse model does not become multimodal merely because its mean network is expressive.

    DDIM: Fast Deterministic Sampling¤

    DDIM (Song, Meng & Ermon, 2021 — ICLR) constructs non-Markovian forward processes sharing DDPM's marginals but enabling much larger reverse steps:

    \[ \begin{aligned} x_{t-1} &= \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}} \underbrace{\left( \frac{x_t-\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\,\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)} {\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}} \right)}_{\text{"predicted }x_0\text{"}} \\ &\quad+ \underbrace{ \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{t-1}-\sigma_t^2}\, \epsilon_\theta(x_t,t) }_{\text{"direction pointing to }x_t\text{"}} \\ &\quad+ \underbrace{\sigma_t\epsilon}_{\text{random noise}}. \end{aligned} \]

    When \(\sigma_t = 0\), sampling becomes fully deterministic.

    Key Advantages:

    • Fewer evaluations: commonly evaluates a selected subset of time levels; measure wall-clock rather than converting the step ratio directly into speedup
    • No retraining of the denoiser: Works with a DDPM-compatible checkpoint when the schedule, target conversion, time convention, and input/output scaling are matched; “any checkpoint” is too broad
    • Deterministic conditional on the initial noise and implementation when \(\eta=0\); inversion/reconstruction additionally requires an inverse procedure and is approximate for a learned field
    • Interpolation: trajectories between selected initial noises can be inspected, but semantic or geodesic meaning is not guaranteed

    Algorithm (Deterministic \(\sigma_t = 0\)):

    1. Sample \(x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})\)
    2. Choose subset of timesteps \(\{\tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots, \tau_S\}\) where \(S \ll T\)
    3. For \(i = S, S-1, \ldots, 1\):
    4. Predict \(x_0\): \(\hat{x}_0 = \frac{x_{\tau_i} - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_i}} \epsilon_\theta(x_{\tau_i}, \tau_i)}{\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_i}}}\)
    5. Compute \(x_{\tau_{i-1}} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_{i-1}}} \hat{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_{\tau_{i-1}}} \epsilon_\theta(x_{\tau_i}, \tau_i)\)
    6. Return \(x_0\)

    DDIM in Practice

    DDIM remains a widely implemented baseline, while deployed systems use many schedulers. A step count is checkpoint-, spacing-, guidance-, and implementation-dependent; report the scheduler configuration rather than a universal “production default.”

    Advanced ODE Solvers¤

    DPM-Solver (Lu et al., 2022 — NeurIPS):

    Treats diffusion sampling as solving ODEs with specialised numerical methods:

    • Higher-order formulas with convergence results under smoothness, model parameterization, and numerical assumptions; learned-field error and large guidance can dominate truncation order.
    • The NeurIPS paper reports FID 4.70 at 10 NFEs and 2.87 at 20 NFEs on CIFAR-10.
    • Its 4–16× comparison is a paper-protocol result against the selected training-free samplers and datasets, not a general wall-clock factor for arbitrary checkpoints.

    DPM-Solver++ (Lu et al., 2023):

    Addresses instability with large classifier-free-guidance scales:

    • Uses data prediction with dynamic thresholding.
    • Performs well with 15–20 steps for guided sampling.
    • Better numerical stability than DPM-Solver under large CFG scales.

    PNDM — Pseudo Numerical Methods (Liu et al., 2022 — ICLR):

    Treats DDPMs as solving differential equations on manifolds:

    • The ICLR paper reports better sample metrics at 50 steps than its 1000-step DDIM baseline on CIFAR-10, CelebA, and LSUN.
    • The paper calls the 50-versus-1000 step ratio a 20× speedup; without a matched wall-clock record it is more precisely a 20× reduction in reported outer steps for that protocol.

    UniPC (Zhao et al., 2023) and DEIS (Zhang & Chen, 2022) provide additional numerical methods with distinct order conditions and empirical error profiles.

    Consistency Models: One-Step Generation¤

    Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023 — ICML) and Improved Techniques for Training Consistency Models learn a consistency function that maps points on the same probability-flow trajectory toward a common boundary value:

    \[ f(x_t,t)=x_\epsilon \quad \text{along an ideal trajectory, with the declared boundary convention} \]

    The self-consistency property:

    \[ f(x_t,t)=f(x_s,s) \quad \text{when }x_t,x_s\text{ lie on the same ideal trajectory} \]

    Consistency Distillation:

    Train by distilling from pre-trained diffusion model:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{CD}} = \mathbb{E}\left[d(f_\theta(x_{t_{n+1}}, t_{n+1}), f_{\theta^-}(\hat{x}_{t_n}^\phi, t_n))\right] \]

    where \(\hat{x}_{t_n}^\phi\) is one step of ODE solver from \(x_{t_{n+1}}\) using teacher model.

    Results:

    • FID 3.55 on CIFAR-10 in one step
    • Improved techniques: FID 2.51 in one step, 2.24 in two steps
    • Consistency Trajectory Models (CTM): FID 1.73 in one step
    • Zero-shot editing without task-specific training

    Interpret NFE reductions carefully

    Consistency models target one- or few-evaluation generation. A 1000-to-1 NFE ratio is not a portable 1000× latency result, and quality must be compared at matched data, architecture, conditioning, decoder, and hardware.

    Guidance Techniques¤

    Classifier Guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021):

    Modifies the score using gradients from a separately trained noise-aware classifier:

    \[ \tilde{\epsilon}_\theta(x_t, c) = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, c) - w \cdot \sigma_t \cdot \nabla_{x_t} \log p_\phi(c|x_t) \]

    where \(w\) is guidance scale, \(c\) is class label.

    Reported comparison: Under the paper's ImageNet protocol, classifier guidance produced FID 4.59, compared with the cited BigGAN-deep baseline of 6.95. This is a protocol-specific comparison, not a family-wide ordering. Disadvantages: Requires training noise-aware classifiers at all noise levels.

    The exact conditional-score identity, its finite-state Doob-transform analogue, particle twisting, and the distinction between soft guidance and hard feasibility are derived in Conditioning, Steering, Constraints, and Selection.

    Classifier-Free Guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022):

    Eliminates the auxiliary classifier by jointly training conditional and unconditional predictions. Define the guidance scale \(s\) with the common implementation convention

    \[ \tilde{\epsilon}_\theta(x_t,c) = \epsilon_\theta(x_t,\emptyset) + s\bigl(\epsilon_\theta(x_t,c)-\epsilon_\theta(x_t,\emptyset)\bigr). \]

    Then \(s=0\) is unconditional, \(s=1\) is the ordinary conditional prediction without extrapolative guidance, and \(s>1\) extrapolates toward the condition. The algebraically equivalent convention \((1+w)\epsilon_c-w\epsilon_u\) uses \(w=s-1\), so “no extra guidance” is \(w=0\). Mixing those two parameter names is a common off-by-one error.

    During training, randomly drop condition \(c\) with probability ~10%.

    Advantages:

    • No auxiliary classifier needed
    • Avoids a separately trained noisy classifier
    • Exposes a scalar guidance convention, while rescaling, thresholding, negative prompts, and solver interactions add further controls
    • Widely used in public image systems; proprietary-system details and defaults require versioned official evidence

    Illustrative Guidance Scales (highly model-, target-, and sampler-dependent):

    • \(s = 0\): unconditional branch
    • \(s = 1\): conditional model without CFG extrapolation
    • \(s > 1\): increasing conditional pressure, often improving prompt/class alignment while reducing diversity
    • Excessive \(s\): saturation, oversharpening, or artifacts; the useful range must be measured for the specific checkpoint and parameterization

    Worked example: resolve the CFG off-by-one¤

    Let the unconditional prediction be \(\epsilon_u=2\) and conditional prediction be \(\epsilon_c=1\). Under

    \[\tilde\epsilon=\epsilon_u+s(\epsilon_c-\epsilon_u),\]

    \(s=0\) gives 2, \(s=1\) gives the ordinary conditional prediction 1, and \(s=3\) gives \(-1\). Under the alternative \((1+w)\epsilon_c-w\epsilon_u\), the same guided value uses \(w=s-1=2\). Logging only “guidance = 3” without the formula is therefore ambiguous.

    Independent practice and assessment map: sampling¤

    Separate the learned local field, finite transition, initial law, numerical method, guidance or likelihood correction, representation, and deployed call graph. Step count alone is neither an error bound nor a latency measurement.

    P1 — Audit a sampler from target law to wall clock¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: fewer outer steps means lower latency; a higher-order solver always gives better samples; deterministic DDIM exactly reconstructs any real image; one consistency evaluation is 1,000 times faster than a 1,000-step chain; exact score knowledge makes an arbitrary large step exact; and stronger CFG samples the exact conditional law. Name the model, numerical, systems, or target-distribution evidence missing from each false claim.
    2. P1.2 — Compute the call ledger. Compare a four-transition method with two backbone stages and two serial CFG branches per stage against an eight-transition method with one stage and fused CFG. Calculate branch-equivalent evaluations, actual launches, auxiliary-head calls, decoder calls, and end-to-end latency from explicitly chosen per-call times. Vary fusion efficiency and decoder cost until the latency ranking reverses, and state why neither outer steps nor a convention-free NFE predicts that crossover.
    3. P1.3 — Solver assumptions and error decomposition. For an affine Gaussian probability-flow ODE, take one Euler step and one second-order step over the same interval, then compare them with an analytic solution. Repeat the accounting conceptually for Euler--Maruyama on the reverse SDE. State smoothness, stability, score-accuracy, endpoint, grid, and guidance assumptions behind an order claim, and separate terminal, model, and discretization error.
    4. P1.4 — Guidance and posterior target audit. With \(\epsilon_u=2\) and \(\epsilon_c=1\), convert among \(s\in\{0,1,3\}\) in \(\epsilon_u+s(\epsilon_c-\epsilon_u)\) and the equivalent \(w=s-1\) convention. Then add a linear-Gaussian observation \(y=Ax_0+\eta\) and write the exact posterior score decomposition where available. Identify what approximation is introduced when a clean-space likelihood gradient is applied at a noisy state.
    5. P1.5 — Classify the acceleration intervention. For ancestral DDPM, DDIM subsampling, DPM-Solver, predictor–corrector SDE sampling, latent diffusion, progressive distillation, consistency models, flow-map learning, feature caching, and quantization, classify the reverse object and what changes: numerical solver, stochastic correction, learned transition/student, state representation, training path/target, or systems implementation. Choose two methods from different axes and define a matched quality–coverage–latency comparison including teacher or retraining cost.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Design posterior sampling for an undersampled medical image or irregular scientific field with a declared measurement operator and noise law. Specify the unconditional or conditional prior, likelihood, reverse dynamics, solver, correction or guidance approximation, uncertainty target, NFE/call ledger, physical or measurement-consistency checks, and a small linear-Gaussian case that can falsify posterior mean and variance claims.

    Sampling Methods: Chapter synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    The trained estimator does not select its own sampler. Solver family, time/noise grid, stochasticity, corrections, guidance, clipping, precision, and decoder define a new approximation whose errors and cost must be measured.

    Retrieval practice: sampling and guidance
    1. Why is solver order not a complete quality ranking?
    2. What does deterministic DDIM require for exact reconstruction of a real image?
    3. In the \(s\) convention, which value is the ordinary conditional model?
    4. Why is one consistency-model evaluation not automatically 1000× faster than DDPM?
    5. How can an eight-step sampler be faster than a four-step sampler?

    Solutions. (1) Smoothness, learned-model error, grid, stiffness, guidance, and evaluation cost differ. (2) A compatible inversion plus an exact field/integration; ordinary encoding or approximate inversion is insufficient. (3) \(s=1\). (4) Network sizes, decoder calls, hardware, batching, compilation, and achieved quality differ. (5) Each step can contain a different number of stages and guidance branches; branch fusion, tensor shape, kernels, auxiliary models, and decoder cost determine end-to-end time.

    Bridge to Chapter 5. Samplers act on a state representation. The next chapter changes that state to learned latents, cascades, discrete variables, language, video, graphs, manifolds, and scientific fields, where the corruption kernel itself must be redesigned.
