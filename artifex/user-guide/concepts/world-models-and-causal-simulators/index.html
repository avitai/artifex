    World Models and Causal Simulators¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Representations and Multimodal Generative Systems
    • Stochastic-Process and Function-Space Generators Explained
    • Causal Generative Models Explained
    • Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics
    • Canonical Notation and Transformation Conventions

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A world model predicts consequences of actions well enough to answer a specified query. That query may be next-observation prediction, long-horizon simulation, value estimation, planning, policy learning in imagination, intervention, or an individual counterfactual. These requirements are not interchangeable: a photorealistic video predictor can be useless for control, while a compact value-equivalent model can plan well without reconstructing pixels.

    This volume separates predictive, decision-aware, and causal claims. It develops POMDP semantics, latent state-space models, rollout error, value equivalence, model-predictive control, learned simulators, confounding, counterfactual noise, evaluation, and peer-reviewed research.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2, then the worked finite-state examples.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–4 and the evaluation protocol in Chapter 6.
    Research: all chapters, including identification, planner exploitation, certification, and the evidence audit.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning objectives¤

    After this volume, you should be able to:

    1. specify exactly which state, action, observation, reward, and query a world model represents;
    2. distinguish observation prediction, belief-state filtering, value equivalence, and causal simulation;
    3. distinguish an environment state, learned latent, deterministic memory, posterior belief, and sampled rollout state;
    4. derive how one-step transition error can compound over a rollout;
    5. compare online planning, latent imagination, and policy distillation;
    6. separate a world model, planning target, inference engine, and environment evidence loop;
    7. diagnose confounded action data and state when an intervention is identifiable;
    8. design evaluation that exposes model exploitation, uncertainty failure, and long-horizon drift.

    1. What a World Model Models¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional probability and sequential data. By the end, you should be able to distinguish observation, latent-state, transition, reward, termination, and policy components; define predictive, generative, and decision queries separately; explain why a video predictor is not automatically a world model; and specify the environment, agent, observation, action, and deployment boundaries of a claimed simulator.

    Environment semantics¤

    A partially observable controlled process contains latent environment state \(s_t\), action \(a_t\), observation \(o_t\), and reward \(r_t\). A common factorization is

    \[ p(s_0)\prod_{t=0}^{T-1} p(o_t\mid s_t) \pi(a_t\mid h_t) p(r_t,s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t), \]

    where \(h_t=(o_{\le t},a_{<t})\) is observable history and \(\pi\) is the behavior policy that generated actions. The environment transition and the policy are separate objects. Treating policy-generated actions as ordinary observations can hide confounding and selection.

    The Markov state \(s_t\) need not be observable. A sufficient belief state is

    \[ b_t(s)=p(s_t=s\mid h_t), \]

    which summarizes history for future prediction and control when the model is correct. A neural recurrent state is only a candidate statistic; low reconstruction or prediction loss does not prove sufficiency.

    Five different model targets¤

    Table WORLD.1. Five different model targets
    Target Learns Appropriate evidence
    observation predictor \(p(o_{t+1:T}\mid h_t,a_{t:T-1})\) calibrated rollout prediction
    latent dynamics model \(p(z_{t+1}\mid z_t,a_t)\) plus encoder latent consistency and downstream queries
    reward/value model rewards or Bellman-relevant quantities return and policy-ranking accuracy
    interactive renderer action-conditioned perceptual observations controllability, persistence, latency, coverage
    causal simulator interventional/counterfactual distributions identification assumptions and intervention tests

    A model can satisfy one row and fail another. MuZero, for example, is intentionally decision-aware: it learns latent dynamics that predict policy, value, and reward quantities useful to tree search rather than reconstructing every observation. This aligns with the value-equivalence principle, under which models are equivalent relative to specified policies and value functions if they induce the same Bellman updates.

    Prediction, rendering, and planning¤

    Planning requires comparing consequences of candidate actions. Passive video continuation predicts under the action distribution implicit in its data; it is not automatically an action-conditioned simulator. Conversely, a decoder-free latent model may plan effectively while producing no viewable video.

    The learned world model is also not the planner. Cross-entropy method (CEM), model-predictive control (MPC), Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS), gradient optimization, and an imagined actor are different inference algorithms over a model. Their compute and failure modes must be reported separately.

    Worked two-state world¤

    Let \(s_t\in\{L,R\}\), \(a_t\in\{stay,switch\}\), and reward \(r_t=1\) only in \(R\). The transition is deterministic: stay preserves the state and switch flips it. If the observation is the state, a one-step model can plan exactly. If both states emit the same observation, the current observation alone is insufficient; history or a belief is required. A model predicting only the marginal frequency of \(R\) may have good average likelihood yet cannot determine whether switch improves reward.

    Misconception checks¤

    • “World model” means a photorealistic video generator. No; that is one observation model.
    • A perfect next-frame model is a perfect planner. Only with the needed horizon, actions, rewards/constraints, coverage, and usable inference.
    • A useful planning state must reconstruct pixels. Value-equivalent and decoder-free models show otherwise.
    • Any recurrent hidden state is a belief state. It is a learned representation unless sufficiency is established.
    Retrieval question: what must be named before comparing two world models?

    Name the modeled variables, conditioning history, behavior/action mechanism, query, horizon, planner, and evaluation distribution. Without them, “better world model” is underspecified.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A world model is defined by its variables and queries, not its marketing label. Chapter 2 studies how latent dynamics represent uncertainty and why small local errors can become large planning errors.

    2. Latent Dynamics, Partial Observability, and Uncertainty¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and state-space inference. By the end, you should be able to formulate belief-state filtering under partial observability, distinguish aleatoric, epistemic, posterior, and model uncertainty, compare deterministic and stochastic latent dynamics, diagnose latent collapse and open- loop drift, and evaluate multi-step calibration rather than only one-step error.

    State, Memory, and Belief Are Different Objects¤

    Consider a controlled latent state-space model

    \[ p(z_1,o_1) \prod_{t=1}^{T-1} p(r_t,z_{t+1}\mid z_t,a_t) p(o_{t+1}\mid z_{t+1}). \]

    This is the neural continuation of the HMM and linear-Gaussian state-space grammar, not a separate use of the word latent. The classical comparison, inference-regime map, and state-space filtering and smoothing volume distinguish exact filtering, structured variational smoothing, particle inference, ensemble and Gaussian approximations, and amortized encoders before the nonlinear model is introduced here.

    Here \(z_t\) is a latent variable in the model, not automatically the physical environment state. The observation \(o_t\) may reveal only part of the environment. A deterministic recurrent memory \(h_t\) is a computational summary. The Bayesian belief

    \[ b_t(z)=p(z_t=z\mid o_{1:t},a_{1:t-1}) \]

    is a distribution conditioned on the online history. Finally, a rollout state \(\tilde z_t\sim b_t\) or \(\tilde z_{t+h}\sim p(z_{t+h}\mid z_t,a_{t:t+h-1})\) is one sample, not the belief itself.

    These distinctions matter even if every object is represented by a neural vector. A single sampled latent can support a rollout while failing to represent ambiguity. A recurrent memory can retain useful history without being a calibrated posterior. An exact belief is a sufficient information state for planning in a specified POMDP, but a learned encoder is only an approximation until that property is established.

    A predictive state need only retain distinctions required for a declared future-observation or decision query under the modeled action regime. A causally sufficient state must support the intended mechanism changes or interventions. Two histories may have the same observational future under the behavior policy yet respond differently to an action that policy rarely or never takes. Predictive sufficiency therefore does not establish causal sufficiency; neural expressivity does not remove the identification boundary.

    The action-conditioned model above is a conditional law given an action sequence. If training actions were chosen by a behavior policy \(\mu(a_t\mid o_{1:t},a_{1:t-1})\), that policy is also part of the observational data law. Conditioning a predictor on logged actions does not, by itself, identify what would happen under a new intervention policy; Chapter 5 develops that boundary.

    One Model, Four Execution Modes¤

    Table WORLD.2. One Model, Four Execution Modes
    Mode State distribution Observation use What the result supports
    online filtering \(p(z_t\mid o_{1:t},a_{1:t-1})\) past and present only current belief and closed-loop control
    offline smoothing \(p(z_t\mid o_{1:T},a_{1:T-1})\) includes observations after \(t\) representation learning and retrospective state estimation
    posterior-seeded imagination propagate a declared posterior at the boundary through the transition prior none after the boundary; privileged or future-informed initialization must be disclosed conditional future generation under declared actions
    prior generation sample the initial law, transitions, and emissions no observed prefix unconditional or action-conditional trajectories

    Smoothing can be a stronger training signal because future observations disambiguate earlier states. It cannot be silently used by a deployed online controller. Similarly, a reconstruction that incorporates every real observation is not an open-loop forecast: it repeatedly corrects the latent path with information that autonomous generation will not receive.

    The classical two-state calculation in Classical Generative Models shows these four modes exactly. Deep models change the parameterization and inference approximation, not the meaning of the queries.

    Recurrent state-space models¤

    PlaNet introduced planning from pixels with a recurrent state-space model (RSSM) combining deterministic memory \(h_t\) and stochastic state \(z_t\):

    \[ h_t=f(h_{t-1},z_{t-1},a_{t-1}),\qquad p(z_t\mid h_t),\qquad q(z_t\mid h_t,o_t). \]

    The posterior \(q\) filters real observations; the prior \(p\) rolls forward in imagination. Observation and reward decoders train the state. Dreamer trains actor and critic on imagined latent trajectories; DreamerV3 combines this design with robust normalization, categorical latents, return transformations, and one configuration across its reported task suite.

    Deterministic recurrence stores predictable history. Stochastic state represents alternative futures or irreducible observation ambiguity. If the stochastic variable collapses or the decoder ignores it, sampled rollouts can be diverse in noise yet fail to represent meaningful multimodality.

    The Amortized Posterior Is an Inference Algorithm¤

    In the displayed RSSM, \(q(z_t\mid h_t,o_t)\) is a learned filtering approximation because \(h_t\) contains earlier latents and actions while the update sees the current observation. A bidirectional encoder with access to \(o_{t+1:T}\) instead approximates a smoother. Structured inference networks for nonlinear state-space models make this model/inference separation explicit: neural transition and emission factors define the generative law, while a separately parameterized structured variational distribution approximates its posterior.

    There are therefore at least four error sources before planning begins:

    1. model error: the learned transition or emission law is wrong;
    2. posterior-family error: the chosen \(q\) cannot express the model posterior;
    3. amortization error: the inference network does not reach the best member of that family for this sequence;
    4. information-set mismatch: training uses future observations or privileged state that deployment lacks.

    A low reconstruction error does not isolate these errors. Compare an amortized posterior with stronger per-sequence inference on held-out trajectories; compare filtered and smoothed estimates; and evaluate calibration under the exact observation/action history available at deployment.

    Query-Explicit Imagination¤

    The following pseudocode makes the rollout boundary visible. update may be exact, particle-based, or amortized; predict uses only the latent transition.

    belief = update(initial_belief(), first_observation)
for (action, next_observation) in observed_transitions:
    belief = update(predict(belief, action), next_observation)

for action in proposed_future_actions:
    belief = predict(belief, action)
    latent = sample(belief)
    observation, reward = sample_decoders(latent, action)
    record(latent, observation, reward)

    The first loop is filtering; the second is conditional generation. Replacing belief by one posterior mean is a computational choice that can erase multimodality. Feeding a real future observation into the second loop converts it back into filtering or smoothing.

    Aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty¤

    • Aleatoric uncertainty is variability remaining under the true state and action, such as dice outcomes or sensor noise.
    • Epistemic uncertainty is uncertainty about the learned transition due to finite or shifted data.

    A single conditional Gaussian mainly expresses aleatoric spread under its model. Ensembles, Bayesian parameter approximations, or distributional model sets can approximate epistemic uncertainty, but ensemble disagreement is not calibrated automatically. Planning should treat unfamiliar actions conservatively instead of interpreting model variance as free exploration reward without validation.

    Open-loop and closed-loop prediction¤

    Teacher forcing conditions every prediction on real history. Open-loop rollout feeds model samples back into the model and exposes compounding error. Closed-loop control observes the real environment after acting and replans; this can correct drift, but only after the action's real consequences occur.

    Report all three when relevant:

    1. one-step teacher-forced prediction;
    2. open-loop rollout at multiple horizons;
    3. closed-loop task performance with a fixed replanning frequency.

    Also report how the rollout was initialized: from the model prior, a filtered belief, a smoothed training state, privileged simulator state, or a single deterministic encoding. These starting conditions answer different questions and can dominate short-horizon results.

    A rollout-error bound¤

    Assume the learned and true transition kernels differ by at most \(\epsilon\) in total variation for every state-action pair visited, use the same policy and initial distribution, and rewards lie in \([0,R_{max}]\). A maximal coupling can make the next states disagree with probability at most \(\epsilon\) while their histories still agree. By a union bound, disagreement by step \(t\) is at most \(t\epsilon\). Therefore the finite-horizon return difference is bounded by

    \[ \left|\mathbb E\hat G_H-\mathbb E G_H\right| \le R_{max}\epsilon\sum_{t=1}^{H}t =R_{max}\epsilon\frac{H(H+1)}2. \]

    This is a conservative bound, not a prediction that error always grows quadratically. It exposes the key assumption: a uniform error bound over the states and actions the evaluated policy visits. Average one-step test loss under a behavior policy does not provide that guarantee.

    Representation aliasing¤

    If encoder \(e\) maps two states to the same latent but their action consequences differ, no latent transition can be correct for both. Reconstruction can discourage aliasing of visual details, while decision-aware losses discourage aliasing of reward-relevant distinctions. Neither alone guarantees causal or task-independent state.

    TD-MPC2 exemplifies decoder-free local trajectory optimization in a learned latent model. DIAMOND instead uses a diffusion observation model and demonstrates that visual details can affect Atari control. The apparent tension is resolved by the query: irrelevant texture may be discarded, but visually small task variables must not be.

    Aliasing is different from reparameterization. Aliasing maps states with different relevant futures to the same latent and loses predictive information. An invertible change of latent coordinates may preserve every modeled observation and reward distribution while changing the coordinate meanings. The former is a sufficiency failure; the latter is an identifiability issue.

    Worked hidden-mode example¤

    A machine has an unobserved mode \(m\in\{healthy,damaged\}\). The same image appears in both modes, but action push succeeds with probability \(0.9\) when healthy and \(0.1\) when damaged. A recent failure is evidence for damaged. A memoryless image model predicts the population average; a belief model updates \(p(m\mid h_t)\) and changes the action distribution. More pixels do not solve the missing-history problem.

    Retrieval question: why is one-step likelihood insufficient?

    It averages over the behavior distribution and teacher-forced histories. Planning queries model-generated histories and often different actions, so small average local error can hide aliasing, miscalibration, and severe off-support error.

    Retrieval question: when is a learned state encoder a smoother?

    When its estimate for time \(t\) uses observations after \(t\). Future context may improve offline state estimation, but it is unavailable to a zero-lag online controller and must not be presented as filtered performance.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Latent state must preserve information needed by the intended horizon and query, while the inference algorithm must respect the available history. Chapter 3 compares generative, self-supervised, and decision-aware learning objectives that choose what such a state retains.

    3. Learning Objectives and Architecture Families¤

    Likelihood and variational sequence learning¤

    A latent state-space model can maximize an ELBO over a trajectory:

    \[ \sum_t \mathbb E_q[ \log p(o_t\mid z_t,h_t)+\log p(r_t\mid z_t,h_t)] -D_{KL}\big(q(z_t\mid h_t,o_t)\|p(z_t\mid h_t)\big). \]

    The reconstruction terms choose retained information; the KL aligns posterior states available during filtering with prior states available during imagination. KL balancing, free bits, discrete latents, and decoder capacity change this trade-off. A tight training ELBO still does not certify off-policy rollouts.

    Pixel, token, feature, and semantic targets¤

    Observation-space autoregression or diffusion preserves renderable detail but is expensive and may spend capacity on nuisance variation. Token models inherit codec loss. Feature prediction—such as joint-embedding predictive architectures—can focus on predictable semantics but depends on what the target encoder retains. Semantic masks or object states impose a stronger bottleneck and can improve robustness while discarding appearance needed by other tasks.

    V-JEPA 2 is a 2025 technical report/system release coupling self-supervised feature prediction with a post-trained action-conditioned model for reported robot planning tasks. Cosmos is a 2025 technical report and platform for video world-model pretraining and adaptation. Neither report alone establishes universal physical understanding.

    Decision-aware and value-equivalent targets¤

    Given reward \(r\) and value \(V\), a Bellman expectation backup is

    \[ (\mathcal T^\pi V)(s) =\mathbb E_{a\sim\pi,,s'\sim P} [r(s,a)+\gamma V(s')]. \]

    Two models can be value equivalent for selected sets \(\Pi,\mathcal V\) while predicting different observations. Enlarging those sets strengthens the equivalence and can eventually require a more faithful environment model. Value equivalence is therefore relative, not a license to call any high-return latent “the true world model.”

    MuZero predicts rewards, policy, and values along imagined latent transitions for MCTS. It shows that planning-relevant abstraction can outperform full reconstruction in its domains; it does not show that observation dynamics are unimportant for every downstream query.

    Inverse dynamics and latent actions¤

    Large video corpora often lack action labels. An inverse model infers a latent action \(u_t\) from \((o_t,o_{t+1})\), then a forward model predicts consequences conditioned on \(u_t\). Identifiability is weak: latent actions can mix agent control, camera motion, and exogenous events. Connecting them to a robot or game action space requires grounding data or alignment.

    Architecture is not semantics¤

    RNNs, state-space models, Transformers, diffusion Transformers, and neural ODE/SDEs can all parameterize dynamics. The architecture does not determine whether the model is causal, Markov, calibrated, or decision sufficient. Those properties depend on variables, data collection, objective, and tests.

    Objective conflict¤

    A combined system may optimize

    \[ \mathcal L=lambda_o\mathcal L_{obs} +\lambda_r\mathcal L_{reward} +\lambda_v\mathcal L_{value} +\lambda_c\mathcal L_{consistency} +\lambda_a\mathcal L_{action}. \]

    Loss weights change the effective state. A representation that improves video fidelity can worsen control by allocating capacity to unpredictable texture; a narrow value loss can fail when rewards change. Measure gradient conflict, task transfer, and performance after changing goals rather than assuming a universal optimum.

    Retrieval question: what does value equivalence omit?

    It omits distinctions irrelevant to the chosen policies, value functions, rewards, and backup depth. Change those sets or the task, and previously equivalent models may no longer be equivalent.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Objectives decide which aspects of the world become predictable. Chapter 4 turns learned dynamics into decisions and shows how planners actively search for model errors.

    4. Planning, Imagination, and Model Exploitation¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes latent dynamics, objectives, and elementary planning. By the end, you should be able to distinguish prediction quality from planning utility, formulate rollout and value queries under model uncertainty, explain model exploitation and compounding error, compare online and offline imagination policies, and design conservative or uncertainty-aware controls with real- environment validation.

    Model-predictive control¤

    At time \(t\), MPC chooses an action sequence by solving

    \[ a_{t:t+H-1}^{*} =\arg\max_a\; \mathbb E_{\hat P} \left[\sum_{k=0}^{H-1}\gamma^k r_{t+k} +\gamma^H\hat V(s_{t+H})\right], \]

    executes only the first action, observes the environment, and replans. CEM samples action sequences, retains high-scoring elites, refits a proposal, and repeats. Gradient planners differentiate through the model. MCTS builds a selective search tree. Each adds approximation beyond model error.

    Imagination and actor learning¤

    Dreamer rolls the latent prior forward, trains a critic on imagined returns, and trains an actor using those returns. This amortizes planning into a policy. It reduces online search cost but can propagate model bias into both actor and critic. Mixing real and imagined targets, short horizons with terminal values, and repeated data collection can limit—but not eliminate—the feedback loop.

    Planner's curse¤

    Optimization does not query the model like a held-out predictor. It deliberately finds action sequences with unusually high predicted reward, including sequences where the model extrapolates badly. Suppose data contain \(a\in[-1,1]\) and a learned model predicts \(\hat r(a)=a\). An unconstrained optimizer selects \(a=100\) even if the real actuator saturates and breaks. Low test error inside \([-1,1]\) is irrelevant to the selected action.

    Defenses include action constraints, uncertainty penalties, pessimistic model sets, ensembles, behavior regularization, conservative value estimates, short-horizon replanning, and real-environment validation. All encode assumptions; excessive pessimism can also prevent useful exploration.

    Planning versus rendering¤

    Interactive video models such as Genie 2 and GameNGen demonstrate action-conditioned neural rendering in reported game-like settings. They are Vendor claim / technical report evidence until an archival paper and independent reproduction establish broader conclusions. Visual plausibility does not by itself measure reward accuracy, collision constraints, persistent object state, or policy regret.

    Conversely, latent planning can choose effective actions without generating pixels. A fair comparison reports the intended query: interactive media, control, data generation, or causal analysis.

    Distribution shift and feedback¤

    The behavior policy \(\mu\) determines collected actions and visited states. A new planner induces policy \(\pi\), changing its own input distribution. Online model-based RL can collect corrective data; offline planning cannot and therefore needs coverage assumptions or conservative objectives. A simulator trained on expert data may fail precisely on recovery states a learner visits.

    Safety and constraints¤

    Expected reward hides tail risk. For safety-critical planning report collision/constraint probabilities, calibration, worst-case or risk-sensitive objectives, and recovery behavior. A plausible sampled future is not evidence that rare hazards receive correct probability.

    The five-component loop for planning¤

    A world-model system is easier to reason about when it is decomposed into the same five components used for generative design:

    Table WORLD.3. The five-component loop for planning
    Component World-model realization Typical failure
    1. reference and support posterior belief over current state, behavior-policy coverage, and the learned trajectory support the planner requests states or actions absent from data or impossible in the environment
    2. predictive model transition, observation, reward, termination, and uncertainty models one-step or compounding model error; uncalibrated epistemic uncertainty
    3. target construction return, terminal value, goal likelihood, risk functional, and hard constraints proxy reward omits what matters or creates an unsafe trade-off
    4. inference engine CEM, MPC, MCTS, gradient planning, SMC, or an imagined actor finite-search error or deliberate exploitation of model error
    5. evidence and update execute, observe the environment, compare forecast with outcome, update belief/model, and replan open-loop deployment, delayed failures, or feedback that is never used to correct the model

    For a learned trajectory law \(\hat p(\tau\mid h_t)\), one possible stochastic planning target is

    \[ p_{\beta}(\tau\mid h_t) \propto \hat p(\tau\mid h_t) \exp\!\big(\beta U(\tau)\big) \mathbf 1\{\tau\in\mathcal C\}, \]

    where \(U\) is a declared utility and \(\mathcal C\) is a feasible set. Sampling this law, estimating an expectation under it, and taking an approximate maximizer are different queries. The limit \(\beta\to\infty\) may concentrate on model-optimal trajectories, but it can also magnify tiny optimistic errors. A deterministic planner need not admit this probabilistic interpretation; the equation is a useful target construction, not a definition of planning.

    The component boundary matters operationally. Replacing CEM by a differentiable planner changes inference without changing the learned dynamics. Replacing pixel prediction by value-equivalent dynamics changes the predictive target. Adding an uncertainty penalty changes component 3, while improving ensemble calibration changes component 2. Executing only the first action and observing the real transition activates component 5; generating a long video without correction does not.

    The three-candidate design example has a direct planning analogue. Candidate designs become action sequences, predicted property becomes predicted return, and prospective assay becomes real execution. In both cases, the optimizer selects where the learned model is most favorable, so planner-selected trajectories must be evaluated rather than relying only on behavior-policy holdout data.

    Retrieval question: which component is a world model?

    The learned transition/observation/reward system is component 2, together with the current-state inference needed to query it. A complete decision system additionally needs reachable support, an objective and constraints, a planner or policy, and an environment evidence loop. Calling the whole stack a “world model” hides which component failed.

    Complete planning accounting¤

    Report model parameters and training data, planning horizon, candidate count, optimization iterations, particles/samples, world-model NFE, decoder calls, replanning frequency, terminal value, hardware, latency, and environment interactions. “One planning step” can contain thousands of model evaluations.

    Retrieval question: why can a better predictor yield a worse controller?

    Aggregate predictive loss can improve on common or visually salient variables while worsening small decision-critical variables. The planner may also exploit a new extrapolation error, and its finite search algorithm may interact differently with the new model.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Planning is an adversarial query generator for model weaknesses. The five-component decomposition localizes whether failure came from support, prediction, the target, inference, or evidence. Chapter 5 asks when action-conditioned prediction supports the stronger language of interventions and counterfactuals.

    5. From Predictive Dynamics to Causal Simulation¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes structural causal models and the predictive world-model contract. By the end, you should be able to distinguish conditioning on actions from intervening on mechanisms, identify which policy and environment changes are supported by the data, explain why observational prediction does not identify counterfactual coupling, and specify assumptions and evidence required to call a world model a causal simulator.

    Actions as interventions¤

    If the state contains every common cause of action and outcome, and the environment is invariant to how an action was selected, then

    \[ p(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,do(a_t)) =p(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t). \]

    This equality is an assumption-backed identification result, not a property of neural conditioning. It can fail when an unobserved variable affects both the behavior policy and next state, when logged “action” is only a proxy, or when agents respond to information absent from the model state.

    Sequential randomization, sufficiently rich adjustment variables, valid instruments, proxies, front-door structures, or environment interventions can restore identification under their respective assumptions. Model-based RL for confounded POMDPs gives one 2024 identification and estimation framework under explicit confounded-POMDP and coverage conditions; it is not a universal solution to hidden confounding.

    Worked umbrella confounder¤

    Let hidden rain \(U\sim Bernoulli(0.5)\). A behavior policy carries an umbrella with probability \(0.9\) in rain and \(0.1\) otherwise. Wetness has probability \(0.9\) under rain without an umbrella, \(0.1\) under rain with one, and \(0\) without rain.

    Bayes' rule gives \(p(U=1\mid A=1)=0.9\) and \(p(U=1\mid A=0)=0.1\). Therefore

    \[ p(Y=1\mid A=1)=0.9\times0.1=0.09, \]

    and

    \[ p(Y=1\mid A=0)=0.1\times0.9=0.09. \]

    The observational association suggests no benefit. Intervening does not change rain:

    \[ p(Y=1\mid do(A=1))=0.5\times0.1=0.05, \]
    \[ p(Y=1\mid do(A=0))=0.5\times0.9=0.45. \]

    An accurate conditional generator trained on \((A,Y)\) alone reproduces the observational equality and gives the wrong policy effect.

    Structural transitions and mechanism shift¤

    A structural dynamical model writes

    \[ s_{t+1}=f(s_t,a_t,u_{t+1}), \]

    with exogenous noise \(u_{t+1}\) and modular mechanisms. Intervention replaces an action or mechanism while holding the rest fixed. This representation can support mechanism-specific transfer, but factorization and independent noise are modeling assumptions requiring evidence.

    Changing camera, friction, embodiment, other agents, or the reward function are different shifts. A monolithic transition may interpolate within training environments yet offer no mechanism to update only the changed component.

    Population intervention versus individual counterfactual¤

    An interventional simulator samples outcomes for a population after setting \(A=a\). An individual counterfactual asks what would have happened to the same unit under another action. It requires:

    1. abduction: infer exogenous variables from the factual history;
    2. action: replace the selected mechanism;
    3. prediction: propagate the same inferred exogenous realization.

    Running an ordinary stochastic simulator twice with fresh noise compares two independent possible worlds, not paired individual counterfactuals. Counterfactual identification can remain impossible even when all interventional distributions are known.

    Causal language discipline¤

    • action conditioning is not automatically intervention;
    • temporal precedence is not causal direction;
    • object slots are not causal variables by construction;
    • controllable video is not proof of physical mechanism recovery;
    • high policy return is not structural identifiability;
    • a counterfactual-looking sample is not an identified counterfactual.

    The companion Causal Generative Models volume develops SCMs, representation identifiability, causal discovery, and deep family-specific constructions in greater depth.

    Retrieval question: what distinguishes a counterfactual rollout from two conditional samples?

    A counterfactual conditions on the factual unit to infer its exogenous variables, changes one mechanism, and reuses those variables. Independent conditional samples generally redraw latent noise and describe different units/worlds.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Causal simulation requires variables, graphs/mechanisms, and identification assumptions beyond predictive accuracy. Chapter 6 builds an evaluation protocol and audits what peer-reviewed work actually establishes.

    6. Evaluation and Research Practice¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes all previous world-model chapters. By the end, you should be able to build an evaluation ladder from one-step prediction through long- horizon rollout and closed-loop decisions, test uncertainty and model exploitation under distribution shift, maintain a reproduction record, and classify evidence separately for representation, dynamics, planning, and causal claims.

    Evaluation ladder¤

    Evaluate from the local model to the complete decision system:

    1. Codec/state: reconstruction, information retention, latent collapse, state aliasing.
    2. One step: likelihood/calibration, reward and termination error, action sensitivity.
    3. Open-loop rollout: error versus horizon, diversity, persistence, constraint violations.
    4. Policy-conditioned rollout: errors on states/actions visited by each evaluated policy.
    5. Planning: return, regret, policy ranking, optimizer budget, model exploitation.
    6. Closed loop: recovery after surprise, replanning latency, safety and uncertainty.
    7. Shift: new goals, policies, environments, embodiments, mechanisms, and sensors.
    8. Causal: randomized interventions, adjustment assumptions, paired counterfactual tests where ground truth exists.

    No single FID, video preference, one-step loss, or task return covers this ladder. Keep the planner fixed when comparing models and the model fixed when comparing planners.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • Parallel Stochastic Gradient-Based Planning, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for differentiable planning with lifted virtual states and soft dynamics constraints; results establish performance in the reported visual-control settings, not immunity to model bias.
    • World Models in Pieces, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for transition-local structural certification of goal-conditioned agents; certificates depend on its formal assumptions and covered transitions.
    • SC²-WM, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for internal feedback and closed-loop refinement in continuous vision-language navigation; navigation gains do not by themselves certify a general simulator.
    • Learning Disentangled Multi-Agent World Models, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for factorized agent/environment latents in reported decentralized-control benchmarks; learned factorization is not automatically causal disentanglement.
    • Predicting What Matters, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for semantic-mask future prediction as a robustness bottleneck in reported robot tasks; it deliberately omits appearance information other queries may need.
    • Motus, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for combining understanding, generation, inverse dynamics, and action experts; system-level gains do not identify its latent actions as unique physical causes.
    • Dexterous World Models, CVPR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for hand- and scene-conditioned interaction video in its synthetic and real datasets; visual physical plausibility remains narrower than causal or safety certification.
    • Causal Process Models, CLeaR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for sparse time-varying interaction graphs in reported physical-prediction tasks; the learned graph's causal meaning remains tied to its environment and assumptions.

    Withdrawn, desk-rejected, and workshop-only submissions are not used as peer-reviewed evidence. Vendor demonstrations and technical reports—including Genie ⅔, V-JEPA 2, and Cosmos—remain labeled separately even when influential.

    Reproduction record¤

    For every result record environment version, observation/action interface, data source and behavior policy, episode/horizon definition, model size, tokenizer, training interactions, planner budget, model evaluations, seeds, hardware, wall-clock, uncertainty method, and whether evaluation is author-only or independently reproduced.

    Video world models additionally require resolution, frame rate, temporal context, sampling NFE, action frequency, teacher forcing, codec baseline, and whether human clips compare short cherry-picked or randomly sampled rollouts.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Implement the two-state POMDP and compare a memoryless predictor with exact belief filtering.
    2. Empirically test the rollout-error bound while varying \(H\) and where transition error occurs.
    3. Train pixel-reconstruction and value-aware models at matched capacity; then change the reward.
    4. Construct a CEM planner that exploits an off-support reward-model error and compare pessimistic defenses.
    5. Simulate the umbrella example and estimate observational and interventional effects.
    6. Pair counterfactual rollouts by reusing exogenous noise, then contrast them with independent samples.
    7. Reproduce one peer-reviewed claim with a fixed planner, policy-conditioned rollouts, total model-evaluation count, and uncertainty intervals.
    Exercise 2 solution sketch

    Use two finite kernels whose total-variation difference is controlled on every state-action pair. Couple transitions with shared randomness, estimate the probability that trajectories first disagree at each step, and compare empirical return error with \(R_{max}\epsilon H(H+1)/2\). Then concentrate error on a rarely visited state to show why average behavior-policy loss can look small while a new planner fails.

    Cumulative retrieval: world models
    1. What components must be named before a learned predictor has a complete world-model contract?
    2. Why can a lower one-step prediction loss produce a worse planner?
    3. What distinguishes an action-conditioned forecast from a causal intervention claim?
    4. Which evaluation ladder tests a model intended for closed-loop control?

    Solutions. (1) State/observation spaces, action and policy process, transition and observation laws, rewards/termination if used, inference state, uncertainty, and deployment query. (2) Small local errors can compound differently, distort decision-relevant rare events, or be exploitable by the planner. (3) Intervention requires that action replaces or modifies a declared mechanism under causal assumptions; conditioning can reflect policy selection and confounding. (4) One-step and open-loop prediction, calibrated multi-step rollouts, counterfactual or intervention tests where identified, offline planning, stress/adversarial exploitation tests, and ultimately guarded closed-loop evaluation.

    Final synthesis¤

    World models are query-relative generative models of controlled futures. Useful systems align representation, dynamics, reward/constraints, uncertainty, and planner with the states and actions actually visited. Causal simulators require the semantics and assumptions needed to replace mechanisms and preserve unit-level exogenous context. Predictive fidelity, planning competence, and causal validity remain distinct achievements.
