    2. Structural Properties and Their Guarantees¤

    The four most important properties answer different questions. None should be silently substituted for another.

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the node and scope semantics from Chapter 1. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. audit smoothness, decomposability, determinism, and structured decomposability independently;
    2. prove the marginal-evaluation recursion for smooth decomposable circuits;
    3. explain why max-product is exact for appropriate deterministic circuits but not overlapping mixtures;
    4. distinguish ordinary, marginal, and query-specific determinism; and
    5. identify when operations between two circuits require compatible vtrees or can cause size blow-up.

    Smoothness¤

    A sum node is smooth when all children have the same scope:

    \[ \operatorname{sc}(c_i)=\operatorname{sc}(c_j) \quad\text{for all children }c_i,c_j. \]

    Then the sum mixes distributions over the same variables. Missing variables can sometimes be repaired by multiplying a branch by a normalized unit distribution, but the repair changes circuit size and must preserve other properties.

    Decomposability¤

    A product node is decomposable when its child scopes are pairwise disjoint:

    \[ \operatorname{sc}(c_i)\cap\operatorname{sc}(c_j)=\varnothing \quad(i\ne j). \]

    This makes marginalization distribute through products without double-counting a variable. Smoothness plus decomposability supports linear-time evaluation of marginals in the circuit size, assuming tractable leaf marginals.

    Determinism¤

    A sum node is deterministic when at most one child has nonzero value for any complete assignment. Its mixture components have disjoint support. Determinism turns summation over alternatives into mutually exclusive case analysis and enables additional queries, including tractable MPE and query-specific MAP transformations for appropriate circuit classes. It does not make arbitrary marginal MAP tractable.

    Determinism is a representational restriction. Ordinary soft mixtures are nondeterministic because several components can explain the same observation. Determinism may improve query tractability while requiring a larger circuit to represent a smooth overlapping density.

    Determinism can also be query-relative. A circuit may become deterministic after projecting onto a subset of variables, or be deterministic with respect to the variables maximized in a marginal-MAP query. This is often called marginal or \(Q\)-determinism. The qualifier matters: exclusivity over complete assignments does not automatically survive marginalizing variables that distinguished the branches.

    Structured Decomposability¤

    Decomposable circuits may split scopes differently at different product nodes. A structured-decomposable circuit conforms to a shared hierarchical variable decomposition, commonly represented by a vtree. Compatible structure makes operations between circuits—products, expectations, logical compositions, and some causal queries—tractable because their partitions align.

    Strudel is a primary example of learning structured-decomposable circuits. Restructuring Tractable Probabilistic Circuits studies transforming a structured PC to conform to a target vtree, illustrating that circuit compatibility is an algorithmic problem rather than superficial graph formatting.

    A vtree is a binary tree whose leaves are variables. Each internal vtree node specifies a left/right split of a scope. A circuit respects the vtree when product decompositions follow those splits recursively. This restricts which context-specific factorizations can coexist compactly, but makes separately built circuits align for operations such as products and expectations.

    Sentential decision diagrams and probabilistic SDDs add logical decision structure, typically combining structured decomposability with determinism. Their tractability comes from the compiled logical/circuit properties, not from the name “decision diagram.” A vtree is therefore both an inductive bias and an interface contract.

    Property-to-Query Map¤

    Table PC.1. Property-to-Query Map
    Query or operation Typical sufficient requirements Caveat
    Complete-assignment likelihood tractable leaves and arithmetic evaluation normalization still requires valid parameters/structure
    Marginal probability smooth + decomposable leaf marginals must be tractable
    Conditional probability tractable joint marginals and nonzero evidence ratio can be numerically unstable for rare evidence
    Exact sampling normalized smooth/decomposable generative circuit requires posterior branch probabilities under evidence for conditional sampling
    MPE determinism plus compatible max replacement in the circuit class marginal MAP is generally harder
    Entropy/cross-entropy/KL between circuits additional compatibility, often structured decomposability not guaranteed for arbitrary pairs of PCs
    Multiply circuits compatible decompositions or a compilation procedure product may blow up in size

    These are sufficient-condition summaries, not universal biconditionals. A particular non-smooth circuit may still answer a particular query cheaply; the structural properties give family-wide guarantees.

    Why the marginal recursion works¤

    Let \(M_n(E=e)\) denote the integral of node \(n\) over variables in its scope that are not observed. At a leaf, compute the declared leaf marginal. At a smooth sum,

    \[ M_n(e)=\sum_c w_{nc}M_c(e), \]

    because every child represents the same variables. At a decomposable product,

    \[ M_n(e)=\prod_c M_c(e), \]

    because disjoint child scopes allow the integral to factor. Applying these identities in topological order gives time linear in the represented circuit edges plus leaf-query costs. The proof does not say the circuit is small; a polynomial-time algorithm in an exponentially large compiled circuit is not a practical victory.

    Semirings: Reusing the Graph for New Queries¤

    Sum-product evaluation occurs over the ordinary \((+,\times)\) semiring. Replacing operations can compute related quantities when the circuit properties justify the transformation:

    • max-product for most-probable explanations;
    • expectation semirings for moments or sufficient statistics;
    • Boolean operations for logical support queries;
    • automatic differentiation for all node/edge sensitivities in one upward/downward pass.

    The circuit is therefore both a distribution and a compiled dynamic program.

    Semiring replacement is not a license to swap operators blindly. Max-product gives exact MPE in a suitable deterministic, decomposable circuit because only one sum child contributes to any complete assignment. In a nondeterministic mixture, two branches can support the same assignment; replacing their sum by max discards probability mass and can change the maximizing assignment.

    Worked example: audit properties before choosing an algorithm¤

    Consider

    \[ C(X,Y)=\tfrac12[I(X=0)p_0(Y)+I(X=1)p_1(Y)] +\tfrac12[I(X=0)q_0(Y)+I(X=1)q_1(Y)]. \]

    Each bracket can be a deterministic sum over \(X\), and each indicator–\(Y\) product is decomposable. The root sum is generally nondeterministic because both large children support \(X=0\) and \(X=1\). Thus the circuit can be smooth and decomposable while failing determinism at one node. Marginals remain tractable; blindly replacing every sum with max is not justified.

    Now force the first root child to support only \(X=0\) and the second only \(X=1\). The root becomes deterministic with respect to \(X\), enabling max over \(X\) after summing \(Y\) when the remaining structure satisfies the marginal-MAP conditions. The graph shape barely changed, but the query guarantee did.

    Counterexample: compatible sizes are not free

    Two structured-decomposable circuits can each be compact under different vtrees. Multiplying them may require restructuring one or both, and the resulting compatible circuit can grow substantially. "Both inputs are tractable" does not imply every binary operation is linear in their original sizes.

    Independent practice and assessment map: circuit properties¤

    Treat every property as a local, executable contract and every tractability claim as query-specific. A property name without its node set, variable set, and leaf assumptions is not a completed answer.

    P1 — Derive a property-to-query guarantee¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each implication is valid: decomposable implies deterministic; smooth plus decomposable makes any leaf marginal cheap; complete-assignment determinism always survives marginalization; max-product is exact in every normalized PC; two compact structured PCs can always be multiplied with linear output size; and a vtree is only a visualization choice. For each failure, give the smallest counterexample or the missing qualifier.
    2. P1.2 — Node-by-node property audit. Analyze \(C=0.5\,[I(X=0)p_0(Y)+I(X=1)p_1(Y)]r(Z)+0.5\,[I(X=0)q_0(Y)+I(X=1)q_1(Y)]s(Z)\). Compute every scope and audit smoothness, decomposability, and determinism at each internal node. Explain why the inner sums may be deterministic while the root is generally nondeterministic, and list the marginals and maximization queries justified by exactly those results.
    3. P1.3 — Prove and break the marginal recursion. For P1.2, derive the upward marginal for evidence on \(Y\) while \(X\) and \(Z\) are unobserved, first algebraically and then by complete finite enumeration for chosen Bernoulli leaves. Modify one product so two children contain \(Y\) and show the precise equality in the factorized-integral proof that fails.
    4. P1.4 — Determinism and the max/sum boundary. Construct a two-level circuit in which every complete assignment activates one child at each sum, and verify MPE by max-product and enumeration. Then hide a variable that distinguishes the branches and test whether determinism survives for the remaining maximized variables. If it does not, exhibit a marginal-MAP disagreement and state the query-specific determinism needed to repair the argument.
    5. P1.5 — Vtree compatibility ledger. Draw vtrees \(((A,B),(C,D))\) and \(((A,C),(B,D))\) for two structured-decomposable circuits. Identify which product partitions align, what restructuring or compilation would be required before a circuit product or expectation, and which size quantity must be reported. Give a compatible control pair so the extra cost is attributable to structure rather than leaf complexity.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Transfer the audit to either a weighted logical circuit for constrained configurations or a tensor contraction representing a four-variable probability table. Map logical cases or contraction factors to sums, products, scopes, and support; identify the property required by one marginal and one maximization query; and state where logical compilation size or tensor rank is not captured by ordinary PC edge count.

    Chapter 2 Synthesis¤

    Smoothness aligns mixture scopes; decomposability separates product scopes; determinism makes mixture branches exclusive for a stated variable set; structured decomposability aligns partitions across the graph or between circuits. Complexity is linear in the compiled representation, not necessarily in the original graphical model or data dimension. Chapter 3 uses these guarantees to execute exact queries.

    Retrieval practice: structural properties
    1. Does decomposability imply determinism?
    2. Which two properties suffice for the standard linear-time marginal recursion?
    3. Why can nondeterminism invalidate max-product MPE?
    4. What additional issue arises when multiplying two structured circuits?

    Solutions. (1) No. Decomposability constrains products; determinism constrains sums. (2) Smoothness and decomposability, together with tractable leaf marginals. (3) Overlapping children can contribute jointly to one assignment, so max discards mass that the sum would combine. (4) Their variable decompositions must be compatible or be restructured/compiled, potentially increasing circuit size.

    Bridge. The property audit establishes which recursions are legal. Chapter 3 executes those recursions and separates complete MAP, marginal MAP, conditioning, sampling, and differentiation. Continue continuously or open Chapter 3.
