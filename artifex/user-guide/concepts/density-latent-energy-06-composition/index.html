    6. Composition, Evaluation, and Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes all five interface chapters. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive three compositions: normalize mixtures, products, and exponential tilts while preserving their different support semantics.
    2. O2 — Compute composed energies and scores: use log-sum-exp for mixtures, addition for products, and reward gradients for tilts.
    3. O3 — Audit classifier energies: distinguish a normalized conditional softmax from existence and calibration of a joint input–label law.
    4. O4 — Diagnose support and normalizers: test overlap, integrability, component normalization, and inaccessible rare-event regions.
    5. O5 — Design query-matched evaluation: separate representation, estimator, sampler, and systems evidence with comparable units and compute.
    6. O6 — Calibrate research maturity: connect claims to primary evidence while keeping theorem, peer-reviewed result, workshop result, and proposal at their actual evidentiary levels.

    Product, mixture, and tilt¤

    Products of normalized experts obey

    \[ p_{\Pi}(x)\propto\prod_k p_k(x)^{\alpha_k}, \qquad E_{\Pi}(x)=\sum_k\alpha_kE_k(x), \]

    for nonnegative weights when the product is normalizable. Products emphasize intersection. The new partition function is generally unknown even if each expert was normalized.

    More precisely, the product exists as a probability law only when

    \[ 0<Z_\Pi=\int\prod_kp_k(x)^{\alpha_k}d\mu(x)<\infty. \]

    Disjoint hard supports make \(Z_\Pi=0\); heavy tails combined with negative weights can make the integral diverge, which is one reason this chapter keeps the default \(\alpha_k\ge0\). Even with positive finite \(Z_\Pi\), a sampler may rarely reach the overlap region.

    A mixture

    \[ p_{\mathrm{mix}}(x)=\sum_k\pi_kp_k(x) \]

    has energy

    \[ E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x)=-\log\sum_k\pi_ke^{-E_k(x)} \]

    when the component energies are normalized negative log densities. Mixtures emphasize union and retain a latent component variable.

    If instead \(E_k\) are unnormalized with unknown \(Z_k\), then the apparent mixture weights inside log-sum-exp are not the probability mixture weights: each component contributes its own unknown scale. Normalize components or carry those constants explicitly before interpreting \(\pi_k\).

    An exponential tilt \(p_R\propto p_0e^{R}\) combines a reference generator with reward or constraint. It exists only when \(0<\mathbb E_{p_0}[e^R]<\infty\) and cannot create mass outside the support of \(p_0\). All three constructions require support and normalization checks, but only a product requires overlap among distinct expert supports.

    Worked Gaussian product and mixture¤

    The product of \(\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(\mathcal N(2,1)\) is proportional to \(\mathcal N(1,1/2)\). Precisions add, and the mean is the precision-weighted average. This is an exact product calculation, but the overlap integral is a new constant.

    For a mixture, the geometry is different. At \(x=0\), the model \(0.75\mathcal N(-1,1)+0.25\mathcal N(1,1)\) has responsibilities \((0.75,0.25)\) because the two component densities happen to agree there. Its score is \(0.75(-1)+0.25(1)=-1/2\), not the unweighted sum zero. Product scores add; mixture scores average with posterior responsibilities.

    Classifiers as joint energies¤

    Given logits \(f_y(x)\), a joint energy model may define

    \[ p(x,y)\propto e^{f_y(x)}, \qquad E(x)=-\log\sum_y e^{f_y(x)}. \]

    The usual conditional softmax \(p(y\mid x)\) cancels the input partition and can be well trained while the implied \(p(x)\) is poor. Discriminative calibration does not establish generative calibration.

    Indeed, finite logits always define a conditional softmax over a finite label set, but a joint density exists only if

    \[ 0<\int\sum_y e^{f_y(x)}d\mu(x)<\infty. \]

    For example, \(f_0(x)=0\) and \(f_1(x)=x\) define a valid binary conditional at every real \(x\), while \(\int_{\mathbb R}(1+e^x)dx=\infty\). There is no normalized joint with Lebesgue base measure from those logits. A data marginal or explicit input energy must supply the missing generative structure.

    Evaluation contract¤

    Whenever converting or composing interfaces, report:

    1. support and base measure;
    2. whether normalization is known, estimated, or unnecessary for the query;
    3. the relationship label and exact assumptions;
    4. which queries remain tractable after conversion;
    5. posterior, score, transport, or MCMC approximation error;
    6. mixing diagnostics and effective sample size for energy targets;
    7. likelihood/BPD only when base measure and preprocessing match;
    8. NFE, wall time, memory, and hardware for sampling comparisons.

    Test conversions on a finite state space or low-dimensional Gaussian where \(Z\), marginals, score, and samples are known before scaling to neural benchmarks.

    Research Evidence¤

    Established primary results anchor the exact parts of the chapter:

    • Score matching, denoising score matching, and noise-contrastive estimation justify specific estimator and population-optimum arrows under their respective assumptions; none establishes efficient mixing for an arbitrary learned energy.
    • The normalizing-flow review supplies the exact change-of-variables foundation and catalogs architectural restrictions. It does not imply that a tractable invertible transport exists within every chosen architecture for every support.
    • Discrete diffusion by ratio estimation is peer-reviewed evidence that graph-local ratios can support effective discrete generation; its discrete score-entropy contract is not an ordinary Euclidean derivative.

    Two recent peer-reviewed results illustrate active—not settled—directions:

    • Scalable Energy-Based Models via Adversarial Training, ICLR 2026, studies a joint discriminative/generative energy construction that avoids the original JEM training recipe's dependence on SGLD during training. This is Peer-reviewed research evidence for a particular hybrid and benchmark regime, not proof that adversarial training solves generic EBM normalization or sampling.
    • DualScore, ICML 2026, learns additive score corrections for probabilistic constraints through a dual optimization. This is Peer-reviewed research evidence for constraint-specific score composition, not a guarantee that arbitrary learned score sums are normalized, integrable, or unbiased.

    The TMLR-accepted potential-flow approach and Energy-Weighted Flow Matching, published at the 2nd DeLTa Workshop at ICLR 2026 after a withdrawn conference submission, explore further energy-to-transport conversions. Their different evidence status must remain visible: an accepted journal result and a workshop paper can motivate new tests, but neither is silently promoted to generic consensus about energy-to-flow conversion.

    Worked composition failure¤

    Let \(p_1\) be uniform on \([0,1]\) and \(p_2\) uniform on \([2,3]\). Their product is zero everywhere, so no normalized product-of-experts distribution exists. Each expert is valid and normalized, yet their intersection is empty. Softening supports with noise produces a distribution but changes the model and can yield severe rare-event sampling.

    Independent practice and assessment map: composition and evidence¤

    Compute the composed law before selecting a sampler. Every empirical proposal must include an exact small-case oracle and a maturity label for external evidence.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: Gaussian product. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. Predict where the product of \(\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(\mathcal N(2,1)\) is centered. Derive its normalized mean and variance and compute the product integral \(Z_\Pi\).
    2. P1.2 — Derive a mixture score. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For \(p=\tfrac12\mathcal N(-2,1)+\tfrac12\mathcal N(2,1)\) at \(x=1\), compute the responsibility ratio, both responsibilities, component scores, and mixture score.
    3. P1.3 — Diagnose empty intersection. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For the two disjoint uniforms in the worked failure, compute \(Z_\Pi\), explain why neither expert is invalid, and classify Gaussian support softening as identity, approximation, or model change.
    4. P1.4 — Reject a false classifier joint. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. For \(f_0(x)=0\), \(f_1(x)=x\) on \(\mathbb R\), derive \(p(y=1\mid x)\) and test whether \(e^{f_0}+e^{f_1}\) has a finite Lebesgue integral. State what extra object would make a joint \(p(x,y)=p(x)p(y\mid x)\) valid.
    5. P1.5 — Compute a bounded-support tilt. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Tilt the uniform law on \([0,1]\) by \(R(x)=x\). Compute the normalizer, normalized density, interior score, and support. Explain why the tilt cannot generate \(x=2\).
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a composition study. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 22 min. Design a study comparing a product-of-experts sampler, mixture sampler, and reward-tilted sampler on the same two-mode Gaussian benchmark. Specify exact oracles, representation errors, sampler diagnostics, compute controls, uncertainty, primary baselines, and a claim template that cannot overstate a workshop result.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. Precisions add to two, so variance is \(1/2\) and mean is \((0+2)/2=1\). The overlap integral is the \(\mathcal N(0,2)\) density of the mean difference two: \(Z_\Pi=(2\sqrt\pi)^{-1}e^{-1}\). Thus \(p_1p_2=Z_\Pi\mathcal N(1,1/2)\). Check. A product of normalized densities need not integrate to one.
    2. P1.2. The density ratio of the mean-\(2\) component to the mean-\(-2\) component is \(e^4\). Responsibilities are \(r_+=e^4/(1+e^4)\) and \(r_-=1/(1+e^4)\). Component scores are \(1\) and \(-3\), hence \(s(1)=(e^4-3)/(e^4+1)\approx0.9281\). Check. The score points toward the dominant right component but retains a correction from the left one.
    3. P1.3. The indicator products vanish everywhere, so \(Z_\Pi=0\) and no normalized product exists. Each uniform separately integrates to one. Convolution or replacement by Gaussian-tailed experts changes support and therefore changes the model; it is not an algebraic identity. Check. Composition failure does not imply component failure.
    4. P1.4. The conditional is \(p(y=1\mid x)=e^x/(1+e^x)\), valid for every finite \(x\). But \(\int_{\mathbb R}(1+e^x)dx=\infty\), so the logits do not define the proposed joint energy. Any normalized input marginal \(p(x)\) produces a valid discriminative joint \(p(x)p(y\mid x)\). Check. That added marginal is new generative information.
    5. P1.5. \(Z_R=\int_0^1e^x dx=e-1\), so \(p_R(x)=e^x/(e-1)\) on \([0,1]\) and zero outside. Its interior score is one. Since tilting multiplies the base law, the density at two remains zero. Check. Reward gradients do not create missing support.
    6. P1.6. A defensible protocol analytically records each composed Gaussian target and normalizer, then reports law approximation separately from burn-in, autocorrelation, discretization, and mode-coverage errors. It controls NFE or energy evaluations, wall time, hardware, memory, seeds, confidence intervals, and baseline tuning. The conclusion template names benchmark and compute regime and says a workshop result is preliminary external evidence, not generic superiority. Rubric. Full credit makes every headline number traceable to a query and error layer.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Convert a four-state normalized table into an energy, score analog on a grid, and AR factorization; list the cost of each query.
    2. Derive the prefix partition functions for a three-bit Ising model and measure how the number of suffix terms grows.
    3. Verify Fisher's identity for a multivariate linear Gaussian latent model.
    4. Train an unconstrained two-dimensional score network and measure antisymmetric Jacobian error.
    5. Compose two Gaussian experts analytically, then compare exact samples with finite-step Langevin samples.
    6. Audit a paper's “unification” claim and assign one of the six relationship labels to each stated equivalence.
    Exercise 5 solution sketch

    If \(p_k=\mathcal N(\mu_k,\Sigma_k)\) and weights are one, the product has precision \(\Lambda=\Sigma_1^{-1}+\Sigma_2^{-1}\) and mean \(\Lambda^{-1}(\Sigma_1^{-1}\mu_1+\Sigma_2^{-1}\mu_2)\). Finite-step Langevin error should be separated into burn-in, autocorrelation, and discretization bias.

    Cumulative retrieval: density, latent, energy, and score
    1. Which transformations among density, energy, and score are exact, and what information can each discard?
    2. Why does a normalized latent joint not make its observed marginal tractable?
    3. What condition must a learned vector field satisfy to be a global Euclidean score?
    4. How do mixture, product, and exponential-tilt composition differ in normalization and support?

    Solutions. (1) \(E=-\log p+C\) and \(s=\nabla\log p=-\nabla E\) are exact on a fixed connected support and base measure; energies discard an additive constant and scores can also lose component masses. (2) Marginalization may require an intractable integral or sum despite joint normalization. (3) It must be integrable/conservative with compatible boundary and support conditions. (4) Mixtures average normalized laws, products multiply and require a new overlap-sensitive normalizer, and tilts reweight one base law so cannot create mass outside its support.

    Final synthesis¤

    Every normalized model has an energy; every smooth positive density has a score; every latent joint induces an observed free energy; and suitable transports can represent many target laws. What distinguishes families is where normalization, inference, and sampling complexity live. A useful unification preserves those costs rather than erasing them.
