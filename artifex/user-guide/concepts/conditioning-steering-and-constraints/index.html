    Conditioning, Steering, Constraints, and Selection¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Probability and Generative Modeling
    • Objectives and Statistical Distances
    • Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics
    • Autoregressive Models Explained
    • Diffusion Models Explained
    • Discrete Iterative Generation Explained

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A generator answers a distributional or constructive question. A controlled generative system adds a second question: which outputs should be preferred, observed, ruled out, or selected? Those two questions are easy to collapse. Conditioning can define a new probability law during training; guidance can approximate a conditional law during sampling; projection can enforce feasibility while changing density; a verifier can select one candidate without changing the base model; and retrieval can change the system input without changing model parameters.

    This volume supplies one vocabulary for these mechanisms across autoregressive, diffusion, flow, energy, discrete-iterative, latent-variable, and constructive generators. It treats probability semantics as optional but explicit: exact conditional laws are distinguished from heuristic steering, and deterministic constraints are treated as feasible-set operations when no probability law is claimed.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2 and the finite examples in Chapters 4–5.

    Practitioner: Chapters 1–5, including compute and diagnostic tables.

    Research: read continuously, rederive both change-of-measure examples, and audit the evidence boundaries in Chapter 6.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to define a target law separately from the mechanism used to reach it; distinguish training-time conditioning, inference-time guidance, exact conditioning, hard feasibility, selection, and retrieval; derive continuous score guidance and a finite-state Doob transform; explain why local token masking need not sample the globally conditioned base law; compute the output law of rejection and best-of-\(N\) selection; and evaluate a composed system without attributing every effect to its base generator.

    The arrows name alternative compositions, not an instruction to use every component.

    1. Begin with the Target, Not the Steering Algorithm¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know conditional probability and normalized versus unnormalized densities. By the end, you should be able to write the desired endpoint law, identify its support and normalizer, and state whether a proposed algorithm targets it exactly, approximately, or not at all.

    A base law and a preference factor¤

    Let \(p_0(x\mid c)\) be a base conditional generator and let \(w(x,c)\ge 0\) be a measurable preference, likelihood, reward factor, or feasibility weight. When

    \[ 0<Z(c)=\mathbb E_{p_0(\cdot\mid c)}[w(X,c)]<\infty, \]

    the reweighted target is

    \[ \pi(x\mid c)=\frac{p_0(x\mid c)w(x,c)}{Z(c)}. \]

    This equation defines a probability law. It does not say how to sample it. Rejection sampling, importance resampling, MCMC, a Doob transform, sequential Monte Carlo, fine-tuning, or a separately trained conditional model may approximate or realize the target under different assumptions and costs.

    A common soft preference uses \(w(x,c)=\exp\{\beta r(x,c)\}\). The inverse temperature \(\beta\) changes the target, not only sampler aggressiveness. A hard feasible event \(F_c\) uses \(w(x,c)=\mathbf 1\{x\in F_c\}\) and is defined only when \(p_0(F_c\mid c)>0\).

    Worked finite target¤

    Suppose the base law on \((a,b,d)\) is \((0.6,0.3,0.1)\) and the preference factors are \((1,2,4)\). The unnormalized masses are

    \[ (0.6,0.6,0.4),\qquad Z=1.6, \]

    so the target is

    \[ \pi=(0.375,0.375,0.25). \]

    The rare base outcome \(d\) becomes more likely, but it does not become the mode. Reporting only the reward ranking would miss the interaction between base mass and preference.

    Exact Bayesian conditioning is one special case¤

    If \(w(x,c)=p(y\mid x,c)\) is a valid observation likelihood, then

    \[ \pi(x\mid y,c)=\frac{p_0(x\mid c)p(y\mid x,c)}{p(y\mid c)} \]

    is an exact posterior under the declared joint model. A discriminative score, aesthetic predictor, simulator loss, or human-preference reward is not automatically a likelihood. It can still define an exponential tilt, but the resulting law should be called a reward-tilted or energy-reweighted target rather than a posterior unless a joint observation model is specified.

    Support cannot be created by reweighting¤

    If \(p_0(A\mid c)=0\), then every absolutely continuous reweighting of \(p_0\) also assigns \(A\) zero probability. A reward cannot recover modes absent from the base support. Approximate neural models complicate empirical support tests, but the mathematical boundary remains: selection and reweighting redistribute existing mass. A method that introduces mutation, external proposals, retrieval, or a new decoder is changing the proposal mechanism as well.

    Target-and-mechanism register¤

    Table STEER.1. Target-and-mechanism register
    Claim Required declaration Common category error
    Conditional sampling joint or conditional law and evidence/event calling an arbitrary reward a likelihood
    Reward steering base law, reward, temperature, normalizer semantics implying the base model itself was retrained
    Hard feasibility feasible set, tolerance, and base support overlap equating projection with conditional sampling
    Candidate selection proposal, candidate count, scorer, tie rule reporting the selected item as a base-model draw
    Retrieval composition corpus/index, retriever, context rule, generator treating retrieved text as verified evidence

    A high score is not evidence of target correctness

    Even if an algorithm maximizes \(r(x,c)\) exactly, the reward can be misspecified, exploitable, shifted, or causally irrelevant. Optimization error and target error are separate. Prospective measurement is required when the score stands in for a real scientific or human outcome.

    Retrieval practice: target semantics
    1. Which quantity normalizes a reward-tilted law?
    2. Can reweighting place mass outside the base support?
    3. When is a preference factor a likelihood?

    Solutions. (1) \(Z(c)=\mathbb E_{p_0}[w(X,c)]\). (2) No, not without changing the proposal or state construction. (3) When it is part of a declared joint observation model with the required normalization and support semantics.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Write the target before the algorithm. This separates what the system should sample from how finite computation tries to reach it.

    Bridge to Chapter 2. The first way to reach a target is to train a model that conditions on the relevant information directly.

    2. Training-Time Conditioning and Architectural Interfaces¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know conditional maximum likelihood and neural network inputs. By the end, you should be able to separate conditional probability semantics from the architecture carrying the condition, identify missing-condition shifts, and keep observational conditioning distinct from intervention.

    Conditional training answers a data-law question¤

    Given paired observations \((X,C)\sim p_{\text{data}}\), conditional maximum likelihood minimizes

    \[ \mathcal L(\theta) =-\mathbb E_{p_{\text{data}}(x,c)}\log p_\theta(x\mid c). \]

    At the population optimum with sufficient capacity and identifiable support, the model conditional matches the observational conditional. That statement does not select an architecture and does not imply performance for conditions absent from the training law.

    For an implicit generator \(X=G_\theta(Z,C)\) or an iterative denoiser, the conditional law may be trained through adversarial, score, flow, reconstruction, or other objectives. The condition still names information supplied to the generator; the objective determines which population property is matched.

    Worked binary conditional¤

    Let \(C\in\{0,1\}\) be uniform and

    \[ p(X=1\mid C=0)=0.1, \qquad p(X=1\mid C=1)=0.8. \]

    The unconditional probability is \(p(X=1)=0.45\). A model that ignores \(C\) can fit this marginal perfectly while failing both conditional queries. Marginal sample quality therefore cannot validate conditional control.

    The conditioning carrier is an architecture choice¤

    Table STEER.2. The conditioning carrier is an architecture choice
    Interface Mechanism Main practical trade-off What it does not establish
    Concatenation or additive embedding append condition features to state/token features simple, but can bottleneck rich context calibration or causal semantics
    Feature-wise modulation condition predicts scale/shift or adaptive normalization parameters efficient global control; layer placement matters that every spatial/token detail is represented
    Cross-attention generated states query a context sequence flexible alignment with quadratic or sparse attention cost faithful use of the retrieved/encoded context
    Adapter or control branch separate module injects residual features modular tuning and reuse preservation of the base distribution
    Prefix or in-context tokens encode control in the modeled sequence uses the same sequence interface that instructions are constraints rather than evidence

    The same cross-attention block can parameterize an autoregressive conditional, a diffusion score, a flow velocity, or a VAE decoder. Shared architecture is an implementation analogy, not a family equivalence.

    Conditional dropout and classifier-free training¤

    Classifier-free guidance commonly trains conditional and condition-dropped predictions in one network. Let \(M\) be the dropout indicator. The training distribution contains \((X,C,M)\), and the unconditional branch is learned from examples in which the condition is replaced by a null representation. The null token, dropout probability, data distribution, and parameter sharing affect both estimates. The method does not magically recover a separately optimal unconditional model.

    Missing and conflicting conditions¤

    A multimodal system may receive any subset \(C_S\) of available modalities. Robust missing-modality generation requires training on a declared subset distribution or a valid inference rule; replacing an unseen modality by zeros is an architectural convention, not marginalization. When conditions conflict, the system needs a priority, likelihood, energy, or arbitration rule. Cross-attention alone supplies none.

    Conditioning is not intervention¤

    An observational conditional \(p(x\mid C=c)\) can reflect confounding, selection, and reverse causation. An interventional law \(p(x\mid\operatorname{do}(C=c))\) replaces a causal mechanism. They agree only under causal assumptions. A prompt, class label, or property predictor can offer useful control without identifying an intervention.

    Condition leakage can make control look stronger than it is

    If condition text, target labels, or near-duplicate references leak across the train/test split, a model can retrieve or copy instead of generalizing. Evaluate held-out sources and condition combinations, measure diversity at fixed condition, and audit whether the condition uniquely identifies a training item.

    Retrieval practice: conditional interfaces
    1. Can a model match \(p(x)\) while failing \(p(x\mid c)\)?
    2. Does cross-attention define the conditional objective?
    3. Does a null condition equal exact marginalization over conditions?

    Solutions. (1) Yes; the binary example does exactly that. (2) No; it is an architecture interface. (3) Not automatically; it is learned under the condition-dropout protocol and shared parameterization.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Conditional training changes the learned map or law; the carrier only determines how information enters. Evaluation must condition on the same variables and support used by the claim.

    Bridge to Chapter 3. When retraining is unavailable or a new condition arrives after training, the sampler itself can be changed.

    3. Guidance, Doob Transforms, and Twisted Sampling¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know scores, Markov transitions, and importance weights. By the end, you should be able to derive the continuous conditional-score identity, construct a finite-state Doob transform, and distinguish an exact change of measure from a finite guidance approximation.

    Continuous score guidance¤

    For a noisy state \(X_t\) and condition \(Y=y\), Bayes' rule gives

    \[ \log p_t(x\mid y) =\log p_t(x)+\log p_t(y\mid x)-\log p_t(y). \]

    Differentiating with respect to \(x\) yields the exact identity

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_t(x\mid y) =\nabla_x\log p_t(x)+\nabla_x\log p_t(y\mid x). \]

    Classifier guidance substitutes learned approximations for both terms. The classifier must operate at the sampler's noise levels, and its gradient is only as reliable as its calibration and off-distribution behavior.

    This is a state-space derivative used during sampling, not the parameter gradient that trained either network. See State Scores, Functional Gradients, and Distributional Flows for the state/parameter distinction and the geometry of the resulting flow.

    One common classifier-free convention is

    \[ s_{\text{cfg}}(x,t,c) =s_u(x,t)+w\bigl(s_c(x,t,c)-s_u(x,t)\bigr). \]

    Here \(w=0\) returns the unconditional estimate and \(w=1\) returns the conditional estimate. Other libraries shift the scale by one, so the formula must accompany every value. For \(w>1\), this extrapolates the score estimates. It often changes alignment, fidelity, and diversity, but it should not be described as exact sampling from \(p(x\mid c)^w p(x)^{1-w}\) for an arbitrary finite DDPM or DDIM sampler. Bradley and Nakkiran (TMLR 2025) show that the finite-sampler interpretation is more subtle and depends on the dynamics.

    Worked finite-state Doob transform¤

    Let a Markov state \(S\) transition to terminal outcomes \(a,b\) with

    \[ K(a\mid S)=0.8, \qquad K(b\mid S)=0.2. \]

    Suppose evidence \(Y\) has likelihoods \(p(Y\mid a)=0.25\) and \(p(Y\mid b)=1\). Define the backward information function \(h(x)=p(Y\mid x)\). The conditioned transition is

    \[ K^h(x'\mid S) =\frac{K(x'\mid S)h(x')} {\sum_z K(z\mid S)h(z)}. \]

    Both unnormalized branches have mass \(0.2\), so \(K^h(a\mid S)=K^h(b\mid S)=0.5\). The rare base branch becomes equally likely because it is four times as compatible with the evidence.

    For a multi-step process, \(h_t(x)=\mathbb E[w(X_T)\mid X_t=x]\) carries future compatibility. For a continuous-time jump process with off-diagonal rates \(q_t(x,x')\), the ideal transformed rates are

    \[ q_t^h(x,x')=q_t(x,x')\frac{h_t(x')}{h_t(x)}, \qquad x'\ne x, \]

    with the diagonal chosen so rows sum to zero. The construction is exact when \(h_t\) is exact and regularity/support conditions hold. A learned guide approximates this future-value function.

    Discrete guidance needs discrete algebra¤

    Replacing a finite difference by a continuous gradient can produce large error when a single jump changes a token, edge, or category. Discrete Guidance Matching, an ICLR 2026 paper, derives target transition rates for discrete flow matching and trains the needed guidance object. Its exactness claim concerns the derived rate given the paper's learned quantities and assumptions; network error and time discretization remain.

    Sequential Monte Carlo and twisting¤

    SMC maintains weighted particles rather than one guided trajectory. A proposal transition \(M_t\) and incremental potential \(G_t\) define weights that correct proposal bias. Resampling concentrates compute on promising partial states but introduces ancestry collapse. With correct weights and increasing particle count under standard assumptions, SMC can approximate a declared Feynman--Kac target; finite particles do not make every output an exact conditional draw.

    The complete particle-method foundation derives learned twists, resampling-gradient choices, finite-particle evaluation, and their relationship to Doob transforms. This section retains the conditioning-specific interpretation: the potential and backward value must be tied to the desired endpoint law or reward-tilted target.

    Twisted Diffusion Sampler applies this principle to conditional diffusion. Syntactic and Semantic Control via SMC, published at ICLR 2025, applies programmable potentials to language generation. Report particles, proposal, resampling schedule, lookahead/twist approximation, reward evaluations, and terminal correction.

    Guidance error budget¤

    Table STEER.3. Guidance error budget
    Error source Diagnostic Possible consequence
    Base model error unconditional held-out and sample diagnostics missing target support before guidance
    Guide or likelihood error calibration and held-out conditional tests by noise/time wrong direction or reward exploitation
    Value/lookahead approximation oracle small-state comparison or more lookahead myopic trajectories and particle collapse
    Numerical discretization step/NFE refinement at fixed models biased endpoint despite correct fields
    Finite particles/candidates repeated runs and particle-count sweep high variance and lost modes
    Aggressive scale guidance curve, diversity, constraint, and artifact audits overconcentration or off-support states

    Peer-reviewed research: ICLR 2026 papers on Monte Carlo correction for diffusion guidance, projected coupled diffusion, and exact discrete flow guidance provide current method-specific evidence. None establishes one universal guide across state spaces, rewards, or finite samplers.

    Retrieval practice: guidance
    1. Which term changes the unconditional score into a conditional score?
    2. What does \(h_t(x)\) represent?
    3. Why does SMC need weights even when its proposal is learned?

    Solutions. (1) \(\nabla_x\log p_t(y\mid x)\). (2) Future evidence or terminal-weight compatibility conditional on the current state. (3) The proposal generally differs from the target transition; weights record the likelihood/potential and proposal correction.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Guidance is a change-of-measure problem. Exact identities define the target correction; learned fields, finite transitions, and finite particles determine whether the deployed sampler realizes it.

    Bridge to Chapter 4. Soft guidance changes relative mass. Hard constraints additionally require every returned object to belong to a feasible set or formal language.

    4. Hard Constraints, Formal Languages, and Feasible Geometry¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know conditional probability, token factorization, and change of variables. By the end, you should be able to distinguish feasibility from distribution preservation, explain the global suffix-mass correction for constrained decoding, and audit projection- and map-based constraints.

    Rejection gives an exact conditional law¤

    If \(X\sim p_0\) and the algorithm accepts exactly when \(X\in F\), then accepted samples follow

    \[ p_0(x\mid F)=\frac{p_0(x)\mathbf 1_F(x)}{p_0(F)}. \]

    The expected proposal count is \(1/p_0(F)\). Rejection is exact but can be unusable for rare constraints. If an approximate filter has false accepts or false rejects, the returned law is conditioned on the filter decision, not the true event.

    Local token masking is not generally global conditioning¤

    Let \(A\) be a generated prefix and \(F\) the event that the final sequence belongs to a formal language. The exact constrained next-token law is

    \[ p(x_t\mid A,F) \propto p(x_t\mid A) \underbrace{p(F\mid A,x_t)}_{\text{valid-completion mass}}. \]

    A grammar mask often replaces the suffix mass by a binary viability test: allow a token if at least one valid completion exists. That guarantees syntactic feasibility when the parser and termination rules are correct, but it preserves the base model's globally conditioned law only when the surviving completion-mass factors are equal or are computed explicitly.

    Worked prefix counterexample¤

    The first token is \(A\) with probability \(0.9\) and \(B\) with probability \(0.1\). Both prefixes can be completed validly, but

    \[ p(F\mid A)=0.1, \qquad p(F\mid B)=1. \]

    Binary viability masking leaves first-token probabilities \((0.9,0.1)\). Exact conditioning uses masses \((0.09,0.10)\) and therefore gives

    \[ p(A\mid F)=\frac{9}{19}, \qquad p(B\mid F)=\frac{10}{19}. \]

    Both algorithms return valid sequences; they target different laws.

    Finite-state automata, context-free parsers, dynamic programming, and tractable auxiliary models can supply increasingly informative suffix masses. Ctrl-G combines an autoregressive language model with an auxiliary HMM and logical automata. Constrained Decoding of Diffusion LLMs extends formal-language viability to arbitrary-order infilling. Their guarantees apply to the declared grammar/automaton and algorithm, not semantic truth or program correctness.

    The complete Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations volume derives the locally masked law, gives a finite counterexample to global-conditional preservation, and separates grammar support from program typing, execution, and task correctness.

    Projection ensures location, not density¤

    For Euclidean projection

    \[ \Pi_F(x)=\arg\min_{y\in F}\|y-x\|, \]

    \(\Pi_F(X)\) lies in \(F\) when the projection is exact and \(F\) is nonempty. Its distribution is the pushforward \((\Pi_F)_\#p_0\), generally not \(p_0(\cdot\mid F)\). A large outside region can collapse onto one boundary face, creating atoms and distorting density. Approximate or nonunique projection also needs a tolerance and tie rule.

    Projection can still be the correct engineering goal when feasibility matters more than conditional-law fidelity. Report both constraint violation and distributional distortion.

    Feasibility by parameterization¤

    Suppose a differentiable bijection \(g:\mathbb R^d\to\operatorname{int}(F)\) maps an unconstrained latent \(U\) into the feasible interior. Then \(X=g(U)\) is feasible and, when dimensions match,

    \[ \log p_X(x)=\log p_U(g^{-1}(x)) +\log\left|\det J_{g^{-1}}(x)\right|. \]

    The map changes geometry and may exclude boundary mass. Nonconvex or disconnected sets may require charts, mixtures, discrete components, or rejection. Gauge Flow Matching, ICLR 2026, develops a gauge-map construction for declared convex and related sets; its feasibility and approximation results inherit the paper's set and map assumptions.

    Constraint audit¤

    Table STEER.4. Constraint audit
    Mechanism Feasibility statement Distribution statement Compute risk
    Exact rejection every accepted sample passes \(F\) exact \(p_0(\cdot\mid F)\) \(1/p_0(F)\) proposals on average
    Token/parser mask every completed sequence satisfies represented syntax locally renormalized law unless suffix mass is exact parser state and vocabulary intersection
    Projection output lies in \(F\) to solver tolerance pushforward under projection iterative projection and boundary collapse
    Feasible map output lies in map range change-of-variables or manifold law inverse/Jacobian and chart coverage
    Penalty guidance no hard guarantee at finite penalty reward-tilted approximation scale tuning and gradient error
    Constrained MCMC/SMC target-dependent asymptotic or particle claim exact only under kernel/weight assumptions mixing, particles, constraint evaluations

    Zero measured violation can conceal the wrong constraint

    A syntax checker can certify JSON grammar while required fields are false. A physics residual can be zero under an incorrect discretization. A molecular valence check can miss synthesis feasibility. Validate the formal constraint implementation independently and keep semantic, causal, and prospective criteria separate.

    Retrieval practice: hard constraints and feasible geometry
    1. Why does local token masking not necessarily sample the globally conditioned base law?
    2. What distribution does rejection sampling produce when acceptance depends on the candidate?
    3. How can projection enforce feasibility while distorting density?
    4. Which property must a constrained continuous sampler preserve in addition to pointwise feasibility?

    Solutions. (1) A prefix-local mask can remove or reweight future completion paths without accounting for their total base probability. (2) The base/proposal law reweighted by acceptance probability and renormalized. (3) A many-to-one map accumulates mass according to preimage geometry/Jacobians and can create boundary concentrations. (4) The intended measure or invariant/conditional law on the feasible set, with boundary, tangent, and numerical behavior specified.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Feasibility is a support property; conditional sampling is a probability-law property. A method may guarantee either, both under assumptions, or neither.

    Bridge to Chapter 5. When constraints are soft or evaluation is available only after completion, systems often generate a pool and select among candidates.

    5. Reweighting, Candidate Selection, Search, and Decisions¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should know sampling and order statistics. By the end, you should be able to derive rejection and best-of-\(N\) output laws, separate posterior decisions from posterior samples, and account for verifier and search compute.

    Probabilistic acceptance and resampling¤

    If \(X\sim p_0\) is accepted with probability \(a(X)\in[0,1]\), then the accepted law is

    \[ q_a(x)=\frac{p_0(x)a(x)}{\mathbb E_{p_0}[a(X)]}. \]

    Choosing \(a(x)=w(x)/M\) with \(M\ge\sup_x w(x)\) realizes the target proportional to \(p_0w\). Unknown or loose \(M\) makes rejection inefficient. Clipping an invalid ratio changes the target.

    Importance resampling draws a finite candidate pool, assigns weights, and resamples. It approaches the tilted target under standard support and moment assumptions as pool size grows, but finite self-normalization and candidate coverage remain part of the estimator.

    Best-of-\(N\) has its own output law¤

    Draw \(X_1,\ldots,X_N\overset{iid}{\sim}p_0\) and return the candidate with largest scalar reward \(R=r(X)\). If rewards are continuous with CDF \(F_R\), the selected density is

    \[ q_N(x)=N p_0(x)F_R(r(x))^{N-1}. \]

    This is an order-statistic transformation, not generally an exponential tilt. Ties need an explicit rule.

    Worked binary selection¤

    Let a base generator return a satisfactory candidate with probability \(0.2\). If every satisfactory candidate outranks every unsatisfactory one, best-of-three succeeds unless all three fail:

    \[ p(\text{satisfactory output})=1-0.8^3=0.488. \]

    Selection more than doubles the success rate, but it uses three generator calls plus scoring and still fails on 51.2% of requests. If the verifier has false positives, increasing \(N\) can increase the chance of selecting an exploit.

    Regularized Best-of-\(N\) with MBR gives peer-reviewed evidence about reward overoptimization in its language-model protocols. The lesson is broader than that benchmark: candidate count changes both compute and exposure to scorer error.

    Search is not sampling unless its law is defined¤

    Beam search, tree search, iterative refinement, and verifier-guided exploration return objects according to an algorithmic decision rule. Beam search approximates a high-probability sequence under length and pruning conventions; it is not an ancestral sample. A randomized search has a distribution, but it is determined by proposal, branching, pruning, score, stopping, and tie-breaking together.

    At ICLR 2026, GuidedSampling studies an exploration-then-generation procedure for diverse language-model candidates, and self-speculative decoding for any-subset autoregressive models studies lossless acceleration under a different factorization. These are distinct component claims: one changes the candidate procedure, while the other seeks to preserve a target joint under an acceptance/correction mechanism.

    Minimum Bayes risk is a decision rule¤

    Given posterior or model law \(p(y\mid c)\) and loss \(\ell(a,y)\), minimum Bayes risk chooses

    \[ a^*=\arg\min_a\mathbb E_{Y\sim p(\cdot\mid c)}[\ell(a,Y)]. \]

    The result is an action minimizing expected loss, not necessarily a draw from \(p\). Monte Carlo MBR adds sampling and finite-candidate error. This distinction matters in translation, summarization, molecular design, and planning: a good decision can be atypical under the predictive distribution, and a calibrated predictive distribution can still be paired with a poor loss.

    Compute and evidence contract¤

    Report:

    • base generator calls and emitted tokens or NFE;
    • candidate count, dependence, temperature, and stopping rule;
    • scorer/verifier identity, training data, calibration, and calls;
    • search width/depth, pruning, backtracking, and cache reuse;
    • constraint checks, simulator/tool calls, and wall-clock hardware;
    • selected-score and independently measured outcome distributions; and
    • diversity and coverage before and after selection.

    Selection creates a new evaluation distribution

    Evaluating only the winning candidate conditions on the scorer and search. The selected set can be much easier, more duplicated, or more reward-hacked than raw generations. Preserve raw candidates and measure selection uplift, false-selection rate, and outcome calibration under the selected distribution.

    Retrieval practice: selection
    1. Is best-of-\(N\) generally an exponential reward tilt?
    2. What is the expected proposal count for hard-event rejection?
    3. Is an MBR output a posterior sample?

    Solutions. (1) No; its law is an order-statistic transformation. (2) \(1/p_0(F)\). (3) No; it is a decision minimizing expected loss.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Selection can improve a measured outcome without changing the base model, but it creates a new output law and a new attack surface at the verifier.

    Bridge to Chapter 6. Real systems also retrieve context, call tools, apply filters, and route among generators. Their evidence must be assigned to the correct component.

    6. Retrieval and Composed Generative Systems¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand conditional models and system evaluation. By the end, you should be able to write retrieval as a random system component, distinguish provenance from truth, and design component-level plus end-to-end evaluations.

    Retrieval changes the conditioning path¤

    Let a retriever sample or select context \(R\sim q_\eta(r\mid c,\mathcal I)\) from index \(\mathcal I\), and let the generator use \(p_\theta(x\mid c,r)\). The system output law is

    \[ p_{\text{sys}}(x\mid c,\mathcal I) =\sum_r q_\eta(r\mid c,\mathcal I) p_\theta(x\mid c,r), \]

    with an integral for continuous retrieval state. Changing the corpus, index version, embedding model, filters, top-\(k\), reranker, or context formatting changes the system even when \(\theta\) is frozen.

    Retrieval-Augmented Generation established a trainable composition of parametric generation and nonparametric memory for knowledge-intensive NLP. The composition pattern now appears across text, code, images, audio, scientific design, and personalized generation, but each modality needs its own retrieval unit, rights, leakage, and alignment evaluation.

    Worked retrieval mixture¤

    A retriever returns a relevant document with probability \(0.8\). The generator answers correctly with probability \(0.9\) given a relevant document and \(0.2\) otherwise. Then

    \[ p(\text{correct})=0.8(0.9)+0.2(0.2)=0.76. \]

    Improving the generator's relevant-context accuracy to \(0.95\) raises the system to \(0.80\). Improving retrieval recall to \(0.9\) while retaining the original generator raises it to \(0.83\). A system-only score cannot attribute the error without the conditional breakdown.

    Retrieved evidence is not automatically true evidence¤

    Retrieval can provide source text and updateable memory, but a retrieved passage may be irrelevant, stale, malicious, contradictory, copyrighted, private, or misquoted. A citation can point to a source that does not support the generated claim. Evaluate retrieval recall/precision, answer attribution, source entailment, temporal validity, and abstention separately.

    Filters and fallbacks also define the output law¤

    If a filter accepts generated \(X\) with probability \(a(X,c)\) and the system retries until acceptance, the visible law is proportional to \(p_\theta(x\mid c)a(x,c)\). If instead it returns a fixed refusal after the first rejection, the law includes an atom at the refusal. Routing to a fallback model creates a mixture whose weights depend on the router. These choices affect calibration, demographic performance, latency, and auditability.

    Research checklist¤

    1. Write the base law, target law or feasible set, and returned output law separately.
    2. State which component is trained, frozen, retrieved, optimized, or sampled.
    3. Test support overlap before increasing guidance or search compute.
    4. Compare exact small-state calculations with approximate algorithms where possible.
    5. Sweep guide scale, particles, candidates, constraint tolerance, and retrieval depth.
    6. Evaluate the scorer or verifier under the selected distribution, not only raw data.
    7. Preserve component logs needed to reproduce the composed output.
    8. Verify real outcomes prospectively when rewards or simulators are proxies.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    A controlled generative system is a composition of probability laws, deterministic maps, approximate inference, decisions, and external state. Scientific attribution requires naming the output law of each step and measuring both component and end-to-end behavior.
