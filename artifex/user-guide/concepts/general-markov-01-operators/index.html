    1. Semigroups, Generators, and Forward Equations¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional probability and elementary linear algebra. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate operator levels: distinguish a transition kernel, semigroup, infinitesimal generator, adjoint, and finite simulator.
    2. O2 — Derive dual evolution: obtain backward observable evolution and forward distribution evolution under an explicit row/column convention.
    3. O3 — Compute a finite semigroup: exponentiate a two-state rate matrix and verify stochasticity, stationarity, and limiting behavior.
    4. O4 — Audit a candidate generator: test constants, positivity, the positive maximum principle, domain, boundary, and non-explosion.
    5. O5 — Use weak characterization: convert the generator into Dynkin's formula and a martingale diagnostic.
    6. O6 — Transfer the contract: specify the missing operator and process data for a neural generator on a new state space.

    Markov transition operators¤

    For a time-homogeneous Markov process \((X_t)\), define the transition semigroup on test functions \(\varphi\) by

    \[ (P_t\varphi)(x)=\mathbb E[\varphi(X_t)\mid X_0=x]. \]

    It satisfies \(P_0=I\) and \(P_{s+t}=P_sP_t\). The infinitesimal generator is

    \[ (\mathcal L\varphi)(x) =\lim_{h\downarrow0}\frac{P_h\varphi(x)-\varphi(x)}{h}, \]

    for functions in the generator's domain. Equivalently,

    \[ \mathbb E[\varphi(X_{t+h})-\varphi(X_t)\mid X_t=x] =h\mathcal L_t\varphi(x)+o(h). \]

    Time-inhomogeneous generative processes use \(\mathcal L_t\). The generator is generally an unbounded operator, so its domain and boundary conditions are part of the model—not optional functional-analysis decoration.

    For a Feller process on a locally compact state space, \(P_t\) is usually studied as a strongly continuous positive contraction semigroup on \(C_0(\mathcal X)\):

    \[ \|P_t\varphi\|_\infty\leq\|\varphi\|_\infty, \qquad \lim_{t\downarrow0}\|P_t\varphi-\varphi\|_\infty=0. \]

    Positivity means \(\varphi\geq0\Rightarrow P_t\varphi\geq0\); conservativity means \(P_t\mathbf1=\mathbf1\) when the constant function belongs to the space or an appropriate extension. These are probabilistic statements. A generic linear operator can generate a perfectly respectable evolution equation without generating probability-preserving Markov transitions.

    The domain

    \[ D(\mathcal L)=\left\{\varphi:\lim_{h\downarrow0} \frac{P_h\varphi-\varphi}{h}\ \text{exists in the chosen norm}\right\} \]

    is dense in the usual semigroup setting but generally not the whole function space. A convenient smaller core can determine the closure of the generator. In learning papers, smooth compactly supported functions are often used as a formal test class; on a bounded domain, manifold, or configuration space, that choice may miss exactly the boundary or topology conditions that distinguish processes.

    Backward and forward equations¤

    The generator acts on observables in the backward equation. Its adjoint acts on distributions:

    \[ \partial_t p_t=\mathcal L_t^*p_t. \]

    This is the master conservation statement. For deterministic flows it becomes the continuity equation; for diffusions, Fokker–Planck; for CTMCs, the chemical/master equation.

    The adjoint depends on the base measure and boundary conditions. Reflecting, absorbing, periodic, and open boundaries produce different processes even when the interior differential expression looks identical.

    The two directions can be remembered through the scalar pairing

    \[ \langle p_t,\varphi\rangle=\int\varphi(x)p_t(dx). \]

    If differentiation and adjunction are justified, then

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\langle p_t,\varphi\rangle =\langle p_t,\mathcal L_t\varphi\rangle =\langle\mathcal L_t^*p_t,\varphi\rangle. \]

    The middle expression is the weak forward equation. It remains meaningful when \(p_t\) has no smooth density, which is why generators transfer naturally to discrete, manifold, and mixed state spaces.

    Generator versus transition kernel¤

    A transition kernel \(K_{s,t}(x,dy)\) describes finite-time movement. A generator describes only the infinitesimal limit. Recovering a unique process from an operator requires well-posedness of the associated martingale problem or evolution equation. Neural coefficients that explode, violate positivity, or fail regularity can make the intended process non-unique or non-existent.

    For time-homogeneous finite chains there is no domain subtlety: a rate matrix \(Q\) has nonnegative off-diagonal entries and zero row sums, and \(P_t=e^{tQ}\) is row-stochastic. In infinite dimensions, a useful necessary diagnostic is the positive maximum principle. If a real test function in the domain attains a nonnegative global maximum at \(x\), a conservative Feller generator satisfies

    \[ (\mathcal L\varphi)(x)\leq0. \]

    At a maximum, a valid infinitesimal evolution cannot immediately increase the already largest observable value. This condition alone is not a universal existence theorem, but violating it is a decisive failure. The semigroup generation theorems add closedness, range/resolvent, density, and growth conditions; the martingale-problem route instead proves existence and uniqueness of a path law for the proposed local characteristics.

    Worked two-state CTMC¤

    For states \(0,1\) with rates \(a:0\to1\) and \(b:1\to0\),

    \[ Q=\begin{pmatrix}-a&a\\b&-b\end{pmatrix}. \]

    For a function \(\varphi\),

    \[ (\mathcal L\varphi)(0)=a[\varphi(1)-\varphi(0)], \]

    and analogously at state 1. With row-vector probabilities,

    \[ \frac{dp_t}{dt}=p_tQ. \]

    The stationary law is \((b/(a+b),a/(a+b))\). Negative off-diagonal “rates” would break the Markov interpretation even if a neural operator still returned numbers.

    The matrix exponential makes the finite-time meaning explicit. With \(r=a+b\),

    \[ e^{tQ}= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{b}{r}+\frac{a}{r}e^{-rt}& \frac{a}{r}(1-e^{-rt})\\ \frac{b}{r}(1-e^{-rt})& \frac{a}{r}+\frac{b}{r}e^{-rt} \end{pmatrix}. \]

    Every row sums to one, every entry is nonnegative for \(a,b\geq0\), \(P_0=I\), and both rows converge to the stationary law when \(a,b>0\). These four checks form a small exact oracle for rate-matrix code. Euler's approximation \(I+hQ\) is stochastic only when \(h\leq1/\max(a,b)\); the continuous generator can be valid while a coarse discrete update is not.

    Worked boundary-domain contrast¤

    The formal interior expression for one-dimensional Brownian motion is \(\mathcal L\varphi=\tfrac12\varphi''\) on \((0,1)\). It does not specify what happens at \(0\) and \(1\).

    • Reflecting Brownian motion uses a Neumann-type domain with \(\varphi'(0)=\varphi'(1)=0\) and preserves total probability inside the interval.
    • Brownian motion killed at the boundary uses a Dirichlet-type domain with \(\varphi(0)=\varphi(1)=0\) for its sub-Markov semigroup; surviving mass decreases when paths hit the boundary.
    • Periodic Brownian motion identifies the endpoints and instead lives on a circle, with periodic function and derivative conditions.

    The displayed differential expression is identical in the interior. The state space, domain, and conservation law are not. Clipping an unconstrained Euler step to \([0,1]\) defines yet another finite algorithm and is not automatically a simulation of reflection.

    Martingale characterization¤

    For suitable \(\varphi\),

    \[ M_t^\varphi=\varphi(X_t)-\varphi(X_0) -\int_0^t\mathcal L_s\varphi(X_s)ds \]

    is a martingale. This weak characterization is valuable on manifolds, graphs, and mixed spaces where coordinate SDE notation can be awkward.

    Taking expectations gives Dynkin's formula,

    \[ \mathbb E[\varphi(X_t)]-\mathbb E[\varphi(X_0)] =\mathbb E\int_0^t\mathcal L_s\varphi(X_s)\,ds. \]

    It suggests a data diagnostic: for several bounded test functions, estimate the left side from trajectory endpoints and the integral from recorded states. A zero empirical residual does not prove the entire martingale problem---the test class and sampling error matter---but systematic residuals falsify the proposed generator or simulator.

    Misconception checks: operators and processes¤

    • “The generator is a small transition matrix.” In a finite CTMC, \(I+hQ\) is only a first-order approximation and can become non-stochastic.
    • “The differential formula determines the process.” Boundary conditions, domain, and well-posedness complete the specification.
    • “The forward equation proves a unique path law.” It describes one-time law evolution; uniqueness of that equation and uniqueness of the martingale problem require assumptions.
    • “A constant-preserving neural operator is valid.” Mapping constants to zero is necessary for conservativity, but not sufficient for positivity, existence, or non-explosion.

    Independent practice and assessment map: operators and processes¤

    Attempt the prediction before calculating. State whether each conclusion is about an operator, a marginal law, a path law, or a finite algorithm.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: two-state relaxation. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. For \(a=2\), \(b=1\), and \(t=\log2/3\), predict whether \(P_t(0,1)\) is above or below its limiting value \(2/3\), then compute all four entries of \(e^{tQ}\).
    2. P1.2 — Audit an invalid neural rate matrix. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For \(A=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}-1&1\\-1&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\), test row sums and off-diagonal signs, then construct a nonnegative function whose value immediately becomes negative under \(I+hA\) from one state.
    3. P1.3 — Derive forward/backward duality. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. With row probabilities and column observables, derive \(p_t'=p_tQ\), \(u_t'=Qu_t\), and a fixed-horizon law-observable pairing that stays constant.
    4. P1.4 — Verify a martingale moment. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For the constant-rate birth process \(N_t=N_0+\operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda t)\) and \(\varphi(n)=n\), write \(M_t^\varphi\) and verify its expectation is zero.
    5. P1.5 — Separate three boundary processes. Type: explain · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Starting from \(\tfrac12\varphi''\) on \((0,1)\), specify reflecting, killed, and periodic domains and predict whether total mass is conserved on the interval representation.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to learned graph dynamics. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. A network outputs one scalar per ordered edge. Specify the transformations and audits needed before calling the output a conservative, non-explosive graph CTMC generator and name one finite simulator check.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The limiting transition probability is \(2/3\). Since \(e^{-3t}=1/2\), \(P_t=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}2/3&1/3\\1/6&5/6\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\). Thus \(P_t(0,1)=1/3<2/3\). Both rows sum to one and all entries are nonnegative. Check. The first row is halfway between \((1,0)\) and \((1/3,2/3)\).
    2. P1.2. Both rows sum to zero, but \(A_{10}=-1\) is illegal. For \(\varphi=(1,0)^T\geq0\), \([(I+hA)\varphi]_1=-h<0\). Thus the first-order operator fails positivity from state 1 for every \(h>0\). Check. Preserving constants did not preserve nonnegative functions.
    3. P1.3. \(P_t=e^{tQ}\) gives \(u_t=P_tu_0\) and \(u_t'=Qu_t\); a row law is \(p_t=p_0P_t\) and satisfies \(p_t'=p_tQ\). For fixed horizon, \(d[p_{T-t}P_t\varphi]/dt=-p_{T-t}QP_t\varphi+p_{T-t}QP_t\varphi=0\). Check. The law-observable pairing is invariant under opposite time shifts.
    4. P1.4. Because \(\mathcal L\varphi(n)=\lambda[(n+1)-n]=\lambda\), \(M_t=N_t-N_0-\lambda t\). Since \(\mathbb E[N_t-N_0]=\lambda t\), its expectation is zero; independent Poisson increments give the martingale property. Check. Compensation uses elapsed physical time, not event count.
    5. P1.5. Reflection uses \(\varphi'(0)=\varphi'(1)=0\) and is conservative. Killing uses \(\varphi(0)=\varphi(1)=0\) and its surviving sub-probability mass decreases. Periodicity uses \(\varphi(0)=\varphi(1)\) and \(\varphi'(0)=\varphi'(1)\) on the circle and is conservative. Check. One interior formula has three global meanings.
    6. P1.6. Mask nonedges; map edge logits through a nonnegative function; set \(q_{xx}=-\sum_{y\ne x}q_{xy}\); declare the graph state space and time dependence; bound exit rates or prove a Lyapunov non-explosion condition; and establish the martingale problem. A finite-graph oracle compares sampled short-time transition frequencies with \(e^{hQ}\) under mesh or tolerance refinement. Rubric. Predicting unconstrained diagonals independently loses exact row sums and is not full credit.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    The generator specifies infinitesimal changes of observables, while its adjoint evolves distributions. Validity requires a domain, boundary behavior, positivity, and a well-posed process. Chapter 2 instantiates the operator for flows, diffusions, jumps, and hybrids.

    Does a neural matrix define a CTMC generator?

    Only if off-diagonal entries are nonnegative, rows sum to zero under the chosen convention, and the resulting process is non-explosive.
