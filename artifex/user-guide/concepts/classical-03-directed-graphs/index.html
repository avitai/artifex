    5. Directed Graphical Models¤

    Directed and state-space learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes conditional factorization and latent-variable models. By the end, you should be able to read a DAG, plate, or factor graph as a joint law; separate graph structure from tabular or neural factor parameterization; derive HMM generation, filtering, smoothing, and forecasting queries; execute the scalar Kalman update; and diagnose duration, stability, switching, observation, and latent-coordinate assumptions in state-space models.

    A Bayesian network is a directed acyclic graph with factorization

    \[ p(x_1,\ldots,x_D)=\prod_{i=1}^{D}p(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}). \]

    The graph encodes conditional independences through d-separation. An autoregressive model is a dense ordered DAG; a VAE is a latent DAG \(z\to x\) plus an auxiliary inference model; a causal model adds semantics about interventions that an ordinary Bayesian network does not obtain from factorization alone.

    Graph Structure Is Not Factor Parameterization¤

    A directed graph specifies parent sets and therefore a factorization. It does not prescribe how each conditional distribution is stored or computed. For a node \(X_i\), the same conditional

    \[ p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}) \]

    may be represented by a conditional-probability table, a generalized linear model, a decision tree, a spline, a probabilistic circuit, or a neural network that outputs normalized distribution parameters. Replacing a table with a network changes the factor's function class, parameter sharing, evaluation cost, and learning behavior. It does not by itself change the DAG or the conditional-independence statements encoded by that DAG. Neural estimators have been used inside Bayesian-network conditionals for decades (Monti and Cooper, 1996); the graph and the estimator remain distinct objects.

    Worked example: one DAG, tabular and neural conditionals¤

    Let \(A,B\in\{0,1\}\) and choose the graph \(A\to B\). Define

    \[ p(A=1)=0.4, \]

    and the conditional table

    Table CLASS.3. Worked example: one DAG, tabular and neural conditionals — table 1
    \(a\) \(p(B=0\mid A=a)\) \(p(B=1\mid A=a)\)
    \(0\) \(0.8\) \(0.2\)
    \(1\) \(0.2\) \(0.8\)

    The factorization \(p(a,b)=p(a)p(b\mid a)\) gives

    Table CLASS.4. Worked example: one DAG, tabular and neural conditionals — table 2
    \((a,b)\) \((0,0)\) \((0,1)\) \((1,0)\) \((1,1)\)
    \(p(a,b)\) \(0.48\) \(0.12\) \(0.08\) \(0.32\)

    Now parameterize the same two factors with logistic units. With \(\sigma(u)=1/(1+e^{-u})\), set

    \[ p_\theta(A=1)=\sigma(\alpha), \qquad p_\theta(B=1\mid A=a)=\sigma(b+wa), \]

    where

    \[ \begin{aligned} \alpha &= \operatorname{logit}(0.4) \approx -0.4055,\\ b &= \operatorname{logit}(0.2) \approx -1.3863,\\ w &= \operatorname{logit}(0.8)-\operatorname{logit}(0.2)\\ &\approx 2.7726. \end{aligned} \]

    Substitution gives

    \[ \sigma(\alpha)=0.4, \qquad \sigma(b)=0.2, \qquad \sigma(b+w)=0.8. \]

    The neural and tabular parameterizations therefore define exactly the same four joint probabilities. This is algebraic equivalence for the chosen parameters, not a claim that their optimization landscapes, statistical efficiency, or numerical costs are identical.

    Because the state space is tiny, all principal queries can be checked directly:

    \[ p(B=1)=0.6(0.2)+0.4(0.8)=0.44, \]

    and

    \[ p(A=1\mid B=1)=\frac{p(A=1,B=1)}{p(B=1)} =\frac{0.32}{0.44}\approx0.7273. \]

    The complete-data likelihood needs two local factor evaluations, and ancestral sampling draws \(A\) before \(B\). The marginal and posterior additionally sum over an unobserved value of \(A\). Nothing about that sum changes when the conditional table is replaced by a neural network.

    What changes as the model grows¤

    For a binary child with \(d\) binary parents, an unrestricted conditional table stores \(2^d\) Bernoulli probabilities. A neural conditional can share parameters across parent configurations and may be much smaller, but compact representation does not guarantee accurate learning or cheap probabilistic inference. Exact variable elimination still depends on variable cardinalities, elimination order, and the width of the induced factors. Bounded treewidth can make exact inference tractable; unrestricted Bayesian-network inference is hard in general (Korhonen and Parviainen, 2013). Parametric structure such as determinism or context-specific independence can sometimes reduce costs beyond a crude treewidth bound, so treewidth is an important control variable rather than the only possible explanation.

    Table CLASS.5. What changes as the model grows
    Model structure Easy by construction Query that may dominate cost Why “neural” does not settle it
    Fully observed directed DAG Joint likelihood and ancestral sampling when every local conditional is normalized and evaluable Arbitrary marginals and conditionals A network can evaluate each factor while elimination still creates large intermediate factors
    Directed model with latent variables Joint \(p_\theta(x,z)\) and prior-to-observation sampling \(p_\theta(x)=\sum_z p_\theta(x,z)\) and \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\) A neural decoder does not perform the latent sum or posterior normalization
    Tree-structured discrete undirected model Local energy/factor evaluation; exact message passing for \(Z\) and marginals State-cardinality and message cost Neural potentials can parameterize messages' inputs without changing the tree computation
    Loopy or global undirected model Unnormalized energy evaluation Partition function, marginals, and mixing A global neural energy need not encode sparse conditional independences
    Ordered autoregressive DAG Exact observed-sequence likelihood and ancestral sampling for tractable normalized conditionals Arbitrary infilling, order marginalization, and long sequential sampling A Transformer changes conditional parameterization; the chain-rule query contract remains

    Neural Autoregressive Distribution Estimation provides a canonical example: a neural architecture shares parameters across chain-rule conditionals while retaining tractable joint evaluation (Uria et al., 2016). By contrast, a VAE decoder also parameterizes a directed conditional, but its observed-data likelihood usually requires marginalizing a latent variable. “Uses a neural network” consequently predicts neither likelihood access nor posterior tractability.

    Counterexample: a compact factor is not an inference algorithm

    Suppose one network evaluates a conditional distribution in constant time with respect to the number of graph nodes. Variable elimination can still create an intermediate factor over many variables, requiring exponentially many configurations in its width. Fast local evaluation and fast global inference answer different complexity questions.

    Retrieval practice: structure, parameterization, inference
    1. If a conditional table is replaced by a neural softmax with the same parents, which graphical-model assumptions change?
    2. Why can a smaller neural conditional fail to reduce exact marginal-inference cost?
    3. Which two standard operations remain direct in a fully observed directed model with evaluable normalized conditionals?
    4. Why can a VAE and an autoregressive model both use neural conditionals yet expose different likelihood queries?

    Solutions. (1) None solely from that replacement; the factor function class and parameter sharing change. (2) Elimination complexity depends on the global graph, state cardinalities, ordering, and exploitable factor structure. (3) Joint likelihood evaluation and ancestral sampling. (4) The VAE ordinarily marginalizes a latent variable, whereas the autoregressive joint is a product of observed-variable conditionals.

    Plates, Factor Graphs, and Separation¤

    The plate-notation key repeats variables over data or time. Factor graphs separate variable nodes from factor nodes and make message passing explicit. In a directed graph, d-separation determines which paths are blocked after conditioning; a collider \(X\to Z\leftarrow Y\) blocks association until \(Z\) or a descendant is observed. Conditioning can therefore create dependence rather than remove it.

    This collider behavior matters in generative evaluation: selecting only examples that pass a filter can induce correlations absent in the source population. It also underlies explaining-away effects in latent-cause models.

    Hidden Markov Models¤

    An HMM factorizes as

    \[ p(z_{1:T},x_{1:T}) =p(z_1)\prod_{t=2}^{T}p(z_t\mid z_{t-1}) \prod_{t=1}^{T}p(x_t\mid z_t). \]

    A plate cannot show a time chain with one generic repeated arrow unambiguously, so sequence models are often unrolled for a few steps. The repeated visual motif below is the factorization above:

    Filtering moves left to right using observations available so far; smoothing sends information back from later observed nodes. The graph stays fixed while the information set changes.

    The forward algorithm computes the marginal likelihood in \(O(TK^2)\), forward–backward computes state marginals, Viterbi computes the most probable state path, and Baum–Welch applies EM. Neural state-space and world models replace transition/emission tables with networks but inherit filtering, smoothing, rollout, and identifiability questions.

    The same finite HMM observation law can be written as a constrained nonnegative matrix product state: hidden-state values become bond indices, and transition/emission tables become site tensors. This is an algebraic correspondence under the HMM's positivity and stochastic constraints, not a claim that every signed or Born MPS of the same rank is an HMM. The complete tensor/Born volume develops that boundary and the effect of variable order.

    Define the scaled forward message

    \[ \alpha_t(j)=p(x_{1:t},z_t=j) =p(x_t\mid z_t=j)\sum_i\alpha_{t-1}(i)A_{ij}. \]

    Multiplying many probabilities underflows, so implementations normalize messages at each step or operate with log-sum-exp. The normalization constants recover the sequence log likelihood. Viterbi replaces the sum with a maximum; its most probable path is not the sequence of independently most probable marginal states.

    Emission Models: Normalized Factors, Not Arbitrary Scores¤

    The HMM graph does not require a categorical observation. Its local emission \(B_j(x)=p(x\mid z_t=j)\) may be categorical for symbols, Gaussian or a mixture for continuous vectors, Poisson or negative binomial for counts, or an autoregressive density for a structured observation. A neural network can parameterize any of these distributions. In every case, however, \(B_j\) must be a normalized probability mass or density over the declared observation space if the model is to support ancestral generation and sequence likelihoods.

    This requirement separates an emission model from a classifier. Bayes' rule gives

    \[ p(x\mid z=j) =\frac{p(z=j\mid x)\,p(x)}{p(z=j)}. \]

    Consequently, calibrated classifier probabilities and compatible state priors can provide relative state evidence: the common factor \(p(x)\) cancels when a filter normalizes across \(j\). They do not by themselves specify the missing observation marginal \(p(x)\), a sampler for \(x\mid z\), or a normalized sequence likelihood. Raw logits have a weaker interpretation still. Calling every state-dependent score an "emission probability" silently changes which model queries are valid.

    The same network, two different probabilistic roles

    A network outputting a Gaussian mean and positive-definite covariance defines \(p(x_t\mid z_t)\) and can generate an observation. A network outputting a softmax over states defines \(p(z_t\mid x_t)\) after calibration; it can help recognize a state, but it does not tell us how to sample \(x_t\) from that state. Architecture alone does not determine the direction of the conditional.

    Learning HMM Parameters: One Model, Four Estimators¤

    For a sequence \(x_{1:T}\), forward–backward produces the smoothed one- and two-slice probabilities

    \[ \begin{aligned} \gamma_t(i) &=p(z_t=i\mid x_{1:T}),\\ \xi_t(i,j) &=p(z_t=i,z_{t+1}=j\mid x_{1:T}). \end{aligned} \]

    Baum–Welch is exact EM for a finite HMM. Its transition M-step normalizes expected transition counts:

    \[ A_{ij}^{\mathrm{new}} =\frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\xi_t(i,j)} {\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\gamma_t(i)}. \]

    Categorical emission counts are normalized in the same way; Gaussian emissions use responsibility-weighted means and covariances. For example, if the expected transition-count matrix is

    \[ N=\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\2&4\end{bmatrix}, \]

    then row normalization gives

    \[ A^{\mathrm{new}} =\begin{bmatrix}3/4&1/4\\1/3&2/3\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The expected complete-data log likelihood \(Q\) is only one term in the EM lower-bound identity; the posterior entropy completes the ELBO. With an exact E-step and a proper M-step, EM does not decrease the observed-data likelihood. It does not guarantee the global maximum, and its limiting behavior still depends on regularity, initialization, boundaries, and nonidentifiability.

    The same HMM supports other parameter-learning procedures:

    Table CLASS.6. Learning HMM Parameters: One Model, Four Estimators
    Estimator Computation Principal boundary
    Baum–Welch / EM alternate exact smoothing and expected-count maximization monotone likelihood improvement is not global optimization
    direct gradient likelihood differentiate the forward likelihood, often in log or scaled form still nonconvex and sensitive to parameterization
    spectral or moment learning recover an observable representation from empirical moments needs rank, identification, and sample conditions; may not return stochastic parameters without projection
    Bayesian learning place priors on transitions, emissions, and possibly state cardinality parameter integration generally needs conjugacy, dynamic programming, variational inference, or Monte Carlo

    Thus "learning an HMM" does not name one algorithm. It names a model whose parameters, latent paths, and even number of states can be treated differently.

    Durations and Structured Discrete States¤

    An ordinary HMM implies a geometric dwell time. Write the self-transition probability of state \(j\) as \(A_{jj}\). The duration \(D_j\) of one visit satisfies

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(D_j=d) &=A_{jj}^{d-1}(1-A_{jj}),\\ \mathbb E[D_j] &=\frac{1}{1-A_{jj}}. \end{aligned} \]

    For \(A_{jj}=0.8\), a five-step visit has probability \(0.8^4(0.2)=0.08192\) and the mean duration is \(5\). The geometric law is memoryless: after any number of steps in the state, the probability of leaving at the next step remains \(0.2\). A hidden semi-Markov model (HSMM) instead draws an explicit state-dependent duration, permitting peaked, heavy-tailed, bounded, or covariate-dependent dwell-time laws. The expanded state must include the remaining duration or run length, so exact messages cost more than ordinary HMM messages and depend on the supported duration range.

    Several other extensions solve different structural problems:

    Table CLASS.7. Durations and Structured Discrete States
    Extension Latent construction What it buys What it costs
    hierarchical HMM states invoke lower-level state machines multi-scale segmentation and reusable subroutines more latent paths and termination bookkeeping
    factorial HMM several Markov chains jointly generate each observation distributed state using a compact factorization exact inference over the Cartesian product can be exponential in the number of chains
    coupled HMM multiple chains have cross-chain transition dependencies interacting sequences denser messages and more parameters
    dynamic Bayesian network arbitrary repeated two-slice graphical template a common language for these constructions tractability depends on interface treewidth, not the name DBN
    Bayesian online changepoint model a run-length state and hazard govern segment resets online posterior probabilities for change and segment age a changepoint is not automatically a recurring named regime

    The HSMM duration construction and Bayesian online changepoint recursion both augment the hidden state, but they answer different questions. An HSMM typically models recurring regimes with explicit durations. Online changepoint detection asks when the current segment began under a declared hazard and within-segment predictive model.

    State-Space Queries: Generation, Filtering, Smoothing, and Forecasting¤

    The HMM is the finite-state member of a larger state-space family. With optional inputs \(u_t\), a controlled state-space model has joint law

    \[ p(z_{1:T},x_{1:T}\mid u_{1:T}) =p(z_1\mid u_1) \prod_{t=2}^{T}p(z_t\mid z_{t-1},u_t) \prod_{t=1}^{T}p(x_t\mid z_t,u_t). \]

    This one probability law supports several different computations. They should not all be called “prediction.”

    Table CLASS.8. State-Space Queries: Generation, Filtering, Smoothing, and Forecasting
    Query Distribution or operation Information available
    unconditional generation sample \(z_1\), then each transition and emission model and any declared inputs
    sequence likelihood \(p(x_{1:T}\mid u_{1:T})\), integrating or summing over every latent path complete observed sequence
    filtering \(p(z_t\mid x_{1:t},u_{1:t})\) observations no later than \(t\)
    smoothing \(p(z_t\mid x_{1:T},u_{1:T})\) past and future observations
    forecasting \(p(x_{t+h}\mid x_{1:t},u_{1:t+h})\) a filtered belief plus declared future inputs
    path decoding \(\arg\max_{z_{1:T}}p(z_{1:T}\mid x_{1:T},u_{1:T})\) complete observed sequence, declared inputs, and one joint-path criterion

    For a \(K\)-state HMM, let \(b_{t-1}(i)=p(z_{t-1}=i\mid x_{1:t-1})\), \(A_{ij}=p(z_t=j\mid z_{t-1}=i)\), and \(B_j(x)=p(x\mid z_t=j)\). The normalized forward filter is the predict–score–update recursion

    \[ \pi_t(j)=\sum_i b_{t-1}(i)A_{ij},\qquad c_t=\sum_j B_j(x_t)\pi_t(j),\qquad b_t(j)=\frac{B_j(x_t)\pi_t(j)}{c_t}. \]

    Here \(c_t=p(x_t\mid x_{1:t-1})\) is not a disposable normalization constant. It gives

    \[ \log p(x_{1:T})=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log c_t. \]

    Forecasting propagates \(b_t\) through transitions without conditioning on future observations. Smoothing instead sends information backward from observations after \(t\). A recognition network that reads the complete sequence is therefore a smoother, even if its output is called an “encoded state.”

    Linear Gaussian State-Space Models¤

    \[ z_t=Az_{t-1}+\xi_t, \qquad x_t=Cz_t+\epsilon_t, \]

    with Gaussian noise. The Kalman filter gives exact recursive posterior means and covariances; the Rauch–Tung–Striebel smoother conditions on future observations. These are the tractable ancestors of deep latent dynamical models.

    The filter alternates a linear prediction with a Bayesian correction. The Kalman gain balances predicted uncertainty against observation noise. If the transition or emission is nonlinear, extended and unscented Kalman filters approximate these operations; particle filters replace the Gaussian belief with weighted samples. Learned world models inherit the same distinction between filtering from past observations and smoothing with future context.

    For the linear-Gaussian model \(z_t=Az_{t-1}+q_t\), \(x_t=Cz_t+r_t\), with \(q_t\sim\mathcal N(0,Q)\) and \(r_t\sim\mathcal N(0,R)\), a Gaussian filtered belief remains Gaussian. Writing its mean and covariance as \((m_{t-1},P_{t-1})\), the exact update is

    \[ \begin{aligned} m_t^-&=Am_{t-1}, & P_t^-&=AP_{t-1}A^\top+Q,\\ S_t&=CP_t^-C^\top+R, & K_t&=P_t^-C^\top S_t^{-1},\\ m_t&=m_t^-+K_t(x_t-Cm_t^-), \end{aligned} \]

    with posterior covariance computable in the numerically robust Joseph form

    \[ P_t=(I-K_tC)P_t^-(I-K_tC)^\top+K_tRK_t^\top. \]

    Implementations solve a linear system involving \(S_t\) rather than forming \(S_t^{-1}\). Exactness comes from closure of Gaussian distributions under linear conditioning, not from the words “state-space model.” Neural transitions, nonlinear emissions, discrete–continuous switching, and non-Gaussian noise generally destroy that closure.

    Worked Scalar Kalman Update¤

    Suppose the current belief is \(z_0\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\), the transition is \(z_1=z_0+q_1\) with \(q_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1/4)\), and the observation is \(x_1=z_1+r_1\) with \(r_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\). Before observing \(x_1\), the predictive state is \(\mathcal N(0,5/4)\) and the predictive observation variance is \(9/4\). For \(x_1=2\),

    \[ K_1=\frac{5/4}{9/4}=\frac59,\qquad m_1=\frac59(2)=\frac{10}{9},\qquad P_1=\left(1-\frac59\right)\frac54=\frac59. \]

    The measurement moves the state estimate toward \(2\) and reduces its variance. A deep state-space model may replace the linear mean maps by neural networks, but it must then choose an approximate filtering or smoothing representation; a network outputting one vector does not by itself preserve this posterior calculation.

    System Identification and Stability¤

    Inference estimates latent states under fixed parameters; system identification estimates the transition, emission, and noise parameters from sequences. If states are observed, linear dynamics reduce to regression. If states are hidden, linear-Gaussian EM uses Kalman smoothing to obtain expected states, cross-products, and lagged cross-products before updating the matrices. Direct likelihood gradients differentiate through the Kalman recursion. Subspace methods instead estimate a finite-dimensional predictive realization from structured covariance or Hankel matrices. These routes optimize or match different objects and need not return the same finite-sample parameters.

    For the unforced process \(z_t=Az_{t-1}+q_t\) with finite noise covariance \(Q\), the standard discrete-time stationarity condition is

    \[ \rho(A)=\max_k|\lambda_k(A)|<1. \]

    When it holds, the stationary covariance is the unique finite solution of the discrete Lyapunov equation

    \[ P_\infty=AP_\infty A^\top+Q. \]

    For the scalar process \(z_t=0.8z_{t-1}+q_t\) with \(\operatorname{Var}(q_t)=0.36\), the solution is \(P_\infty=0.36/(1-0.8^2)=1\). Replacing \(0.8\) by \(1.05\) makes the autonomous latent dynamics unstable; adding process noise increases uncertainty and does not stabilize the transition. Filtering-error stability can hold under broader detectability and stabilizability conditions, so it should not be conflated with stationarity of the unforced latent process.

    A negative eigenvalue can still be stable

    The criterion concerns magnitudes. A scalar transition \(A=-0.9\) is stable even though trajectories alternate sign; \(A=1.01\) is unstable even though the numerical eigenvalue is only slightly greater than one.

    Switching, Nonlinear, and Non-Gaussian Systems¤

    A switching linear dynamical system combines a discrete mode \(s_t\) with a continuous state \(z_t\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} s_t &\sim p(s_t\mid s_{t-1}),\\ z_t &\sim\mathcal N(A_{s_t}z_{t-1},Q_{s_t}),\\ x_t &\sim\mathcal N(C_{s_t}z_t,R_{s_t}). \end{aligned} \]

    The unrolled graph shows why this is not merely an HMM attached to an unrelated Kalman filter. Each regime selects the local continuous dynamics, the continuous state carries information forward, and the observation is emitted from that state:

    Conditioning on a regime path fixes the upper chain and leaves one linear-Gaussian calculation below it. Marginalizing regimes retains every compatible upper-chain path unless an approximation merges or discards them.

    Conditioned on one complete mode path, Kalman inference remains exact. After summing over paths, however, the filtering law is a Gaussian mixture. With two modes, its unreduced number of path-indexed components can double at every step: \(2,4,8,\ldots,2^t\). Generic exact inference is therefore exponential before identical or approximated components are merged. Variational methods, Gaussian-sum pruning, interacting multiple-model filters, particles, and MCMC make different approximations; none inherits exact Kalman closure merely because every local regime is linear Gaussian.

    For nonlinear transitions \(f_\theta\) or emissions \(g_\theta\), an extended Kalman filter linearizes locally, an unscented filter propagates selected sigma points, a particle filter represents the belief by weighted samples, and structured variational methods optimize an approximate path posterior. For non-Gaussian count, binary, censored, or heavy-tailed observations, even a linear transition usually loses Gaussian posterior closure. Each method should therefore report the posterior representation it preserves and the approximation it introduces.

    Deep state-space models change conditional parameterizations and inference machinery, not the meaning of the latent-path queries:

    Table CLASS.9. Switching, Nonlinear, and Non-Gaussian Systems
    Construction Generative state Inference pattern Important distinction
    deep Markov model stochastic Markov state with neural transition and emission structured amortized smoother or filter encoder is an inference model, not part of the generative joint
    variational recurrent model deterministic recurrent history plus a stochastic latent at each step recurrent amortized posterior deterministic memory and uncertain state play different roles
    recurrent state-space model deterministic recurrent memory plus stochastic state, often action-conditioned filtering during interaction; smoothing during training when allowed a policy-time belief cannot use future observations

    The continuous-time and scalable path-inference extensions are developed in Stochastic-Process Generators, including particle methods and parallel importance smoothing. Controlled rollouts and planning appear in World Models and Causal Simulators. The complete inference route through Bayes recursions, Kalman and RTS derivations, nonlinear Gaussian projection, mixtures, ensembles, robust updates, particles, learned filters, and validation is Filtering and Smoothing for State-Space Generators.

    Structural Probabilistic Time-Series Models¤

    A structural time-series model builds observations from interpretable latent components such as local level, trend, seasonality, regression effects, and intervention terms. These components form one state-space model, so posterior uncertainty propagates through forecasts rather than being appended after a point predictor.

    The local-level model is the smallest example:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mu_t &=\mu_{t-1}+\eta_t, &\eta_t &\sim\mathcal N(0,q),\\ y_t &=\mu_t+\epsilon_t, &\epsilon_t &\sim\mathcal N(0,r). \end{aligned} \]

    If filtering gives \(\mu_t\mid y_{1:t}\sim\mathcal N(m_t,P_t)\), then

    \[ y_{t+h}\mid y_{1:t} \sim\mathcal N\!\left(m_t,\;P_t+hq+r\right). \]

    With \(P_t=0.5\), \(q=0.2\), \(r=0.3\), and \(h=4\), the predictive variance is \(0.5+4(0.2)+0.3=1.6\). The growing uncertainty is a consequence of repeatedly integrating future process noise, not an arbitrary confidence-band rule. Seasonal and regression components enlarge the state and may preserve exact Kalman inference when the complete model remains linear Gaussian.

    Forecasting does not become causal because state arrows point forward in time. A counterfactual-impact analysis additionally needs a well-defined intervention, unaffected comparison information or a defensible structural model, stability of the relevant mechanisms, and diagnostics for pre-treatment fit and sensitivity. Without those assumptions, the model estimates a conditional predictive distribution under observed temporal associations, not an interventional or counterfactual distribution.

    Latent Coordinates Are Not Automatically Identified¤

    Even exact inference is inference about a model coordinate. HMM labels can be permuted without changing the observation law. In a linear state-space model, any invertible coordinate change \(z_t'=Tz_t\) produces observationally equivalent parameters

    \[ A'=TAT^{-1},\qquad C'=CT^{-1},\qquad Q'=TQT^\top, \qquad P_0'=TP_0T^\top. \]

    The initial mean transforms as \(m_0'=Tm_0\). Nonlinear latent models admit still broader reparameterizations. Calling a learned coordinate “velocity,” “object,” or “health” therefore needs supervision, structural constraints, interventions, or an identification argument. Results for switching dynamical systems, for example, obtain stronger identification only under declared model assumptions and up to a stated transformation class. Predictive adequacy alone does not assign physical meaning.

    Directed and state-space synthesis¤

    Directed graphs organize normalized conditional factors, while inference depends on their algebra and graph width. HMMs and state-space models distinguish path generation, filtering, smoothing, and forecasting. Neural factors can increase expressivity without changing those query definitions, and latent coordinates remain non-identifiable unless additional structure supports them.
