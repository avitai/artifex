    6. Undirected Models and Energies¤

    Undirected and dependency-structure learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes exponential families, conditional independence, and matrix algebra. By the end, you should be able to distinguish an energy from its normalized undirected law, derive RBM conditional structure, relate zeros in a Gaussian precision matrix to conditional independence, work the marginal- association counterexample, and compare neighborhood selection, score-based DAG search, equivalence classes, and latent-induced dependence.

    An undirected graphical model has

    \[ p(x)=\frac1Z\prod_{c\in\mathcal C}\psi_c(x_c) =\frac{e^{-E(x)}}Z. \]

    Local factors make constraints and symmetric relations easy to express, but the partition function couples all configurations. Conditional random fields apply the same structure to \(p(y\mid x)\).

    For a pairwise Markov random field,

    \[ p(x)\propto\prod_i\psi_i(x_i)\prod_{(i,j)\in E}\psi_{ij}(x_i,x_j). \]

    On a tree, messages from \(i\) to \(j\) take the form

    \[ m_{i\to j}(x_j)=\sum_{x_i}\psi_i(x_i)\psi_{ij}(x_i,x_j) \prod_{k\in N(i)\setminus j}m_{k\to i}(x_i). \]

    After two passes, local products of incoming messages yield exact node marginals. On graphs with loops, the same updates become loopy belief propagation: often useful, but no longer guaranteed to converge or return exact marginals.

    Restricted Boltzmann Machines¤

    An RBM uses visible \(v\) and hidden \(h\) units with bipartite energy

    \[ E(v,h)=-b^\top v-c^\top h-v^\top Wh. \]

    The bipartite graph makes \(p(h\mid v)\) and \(p(v\mid h)\) factorize, enabling block Gibbs updates. The canonical Gibbs and blocking contract explains why acceptance is one without implying rapid mixing. The marginal likelihood still contains an intractable partition function, motivating contrastive divergence. Deep belief networks stacked RBMs historically before end-to-end VAEs and GANs became dominant.

    Contrastive divergence initializes a short Markov chain at data and uses its endpoint as an approximate negative-phase sample. It is not the exact likelihood gradient unless the chain reaches equilibrium. Persistent contrastive divergence keeps chains across updates to reduce repeated burn-in, but changing parameters mean the chain target also moves. These limitations reappear in modern neural EBMs.

    A deep belief network is a historical hybrid with an undirected top pair and directed lower layers; it is not the generic name for any deep Bayesian network. A deep Boltzmann machine remains undirected across layers and requires approximate posterior inference.

    Learning Dependency Graphs from Data¤

    So far the graph has been part of the model specification. In many applications, however, the graph is unknown and must be selected from repeated observations. Before choosing an algorithm, identify which graph is being learned:

    These objects can share an adjacency-matrix representation while answering different questions. This section concerns the first object: selecting the factorization or conditional-independence structure of a distribution over a fixed collection of variables. The random-graph volume owns graph-valued observations, the causal volume owns intervention semantics, and the graphical-inference model volume owns the factorization, separation, and graph-semantics contract, while the graphical-inference volume owns queries once the factors have been declared.

    Gaussian graphical models: zeros in the precision matrix¤

    Let \(X\in\mathbb R^D\) have a nonsingular Gaussian law

    \[ X\sim\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma), \qquad \Theta=\Sigma^{-1}. \]

    For distinct coordinates \(i\) and \(j\),

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Theta_{ij}=0 &\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad X_i\perp X_j\mid R_{ij},\\ R_{ij} &=X_{\{1,\ldots,D\}\setminus\{i,j\}}. \end{aligned} \]

    Thus the off-diagonal support of the precision matrix, not the covariance matrix, defines the undirected conditional-independence graph. The equivalence uses the nonsingular multivariate-Gaussian model. Zero covariance does not encode conditional independence for arbitrary non-Gaussian distributions, and a nonzero covariance between two Gaussian coordinates can coexist with their conditional independence given the remaining coordinates.

    For centered IID observations \(x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(N)}\) with sample covariance \(S=N^{-1}\sum_n x^{(n)}x^{(n)\top}\), the average Gaussian log likelihood, up to constants independent of \(\Theta\), is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \ell(\Theta) &=\frac12\bigl[ \log\det\Theta-\operatorname{tr}(S\Theta) \bigr],\\ &\hspace{7em}\Theta\succ0. \end{aligned} \]

    When \(D\) is not small relative to \(N\), the unregularized sample covariance may be noisy or singular. One graphical-lasso convention defines

    \[ \widehat\Theta_\lambda =\underset{\Theta\succ0}{\arg\min}\;\mathcal J_\lambda(\Theta), \]

    where

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal J_\lambda(\Theta) &=-\log\det\Theta+\operatorname{tr}(S\Theta)\\ &\quad+\lambda\sum_{i\ne j}|\Theta_{ij}|. \end{aligned} \]

    Here the diagonal is not penalized; implementations that use \(\lVert\Theta\rVert_1\) instead must state whether diagonal entries are included. The penalty estimates parameters and selects edges simultaneously. It does not make \(\lambda\) a property of nature: changing \(\lambda\) changes the selected graph. Cross-validated held-out likelihood, stability under resampling, domain constraints, and error control answer different selection questions and need not choose the same graph.

    Worked example: marginal association without an edge¤

    Consider the precision matrix

    \[ \Theta= \begin{bmatrix} 2&-1&0\\ -1&2&-1\\ 0&-1&2 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Its inverse is

    \[ \Sigma=\Theta^{-1} =\frac14 \begin{bmatrix} 3&2&1\\ 2&4&2\\ 1&2&3 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    The covariance \(\Sigma_{13}=1/4\) is nonzero, so \(X_1\) and \(X_3\) are marginally associated. Yet \(\Theta_{13}=0\). Direct Gaussian conditioning gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} C_{13\mid2} &\equiv\operatorname{Cov}(X_1,X_3\mid X_2),\\ C_{13\mid2} &=\Sigma_{13}-\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{23},\\ &=\frac14-\frac12(1)\frac12=0. \end{aligned} \]

    The chain \(1-2-3\) therefore explains the endpoint association through the middle variable. Thresholding the covariance matrix would add the wrong edge; thresholding an estimated precision matrix without uncertainty analysis can still add or delete edges because the estimate is noisy.

    Discrete neighborhoods and tractable special cases¤

    For binary spins \(x_i\in\{-1,+1\}\), a pairwise Ising model has

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(x)&=\frac1Z\exp\bigl(U(x)\bigr),\\ U(x)&=\sum_i h_i x_i+\sum_{i<j}J_{ij}x_ix_j. \end{aligned} \]

    Under this strictly positive pairwise model, \(J_{ij}=0\) removes the direct conditional dependence between \(X_i\) and \(X_j\). The global normalizer is usually expensive, but each node conditional is logistic. Neighborhood selection fits one sparse conditional regression per node; a symmetric graph then needs an explicit AND or OR reconciliation rule. Pseudolikelihood multiplies such node conditionals and avoids evaluating \(Z\), but it is not the joint likelihood. Its statistical guarantees require the stated sparsity, signal, regularity, sample-size, and model-correctness assumptions.

    Trees provide an important exact boundary. For a discrete distribution, the Chow--Liu construction selects the maximum-weight spanning tree using empirical pairwise mutual informations. At the population level it gives the tree-structured distribution minimizing forward KL divergence to the target; with finite samples, mutual-information estimation and small edge gaps create structure uncertainty. A tractable tree approximation is not evidence that the data-generating graph is truly a tree.

    Hidden variables turn sparse structure into dense dependence¤

    A sparse graph on observed and hidden variables need not remain sparse after the hidden variables are marginalized. Partition a joint Gaussian precision matrix between observed variables \(O\) and latent variables \(H\). The marginal observed precision is the Schur complement

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Theta_{O,\mathrm{marg}} &=K_{OO}-K_{OH}K_{HH}^{-1}K_{HO}\\ &=S-L. \end{aligned} \]

    where \(S=K_{OO}\) may be sparse and \(L=K_{OH}K_{HH}^{-1}K_{HO}\succeq0\) has rank no greater than the number of latent coordinates. One hidden common neighbor can therefore induce many nonzero observed precision entries. Sparse-plus-low-rank estimators attempt to separate direct observed interactions from latent common variation, but that decomposition is not automatic: recovery needs sufficient samples, signal, rank and sparsity control, and geometric conditions that keep the sparse and low-rank components distinguishable.

    For example, take

    \[ \begin{aligned} K_{OO}&=2I_2,\\ K_{OH}&=\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix},\\ K_{HH}&=2. \end{aligned} \]

    The low-rank correction is

    \[ L=\frac12 \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}. \]

    There is no direct observed-to-observed precision edge in \(K_{OO}\), yet

    \[ \Theta_{O,\mathrm{marg}} =2I_2-L =\begin{bmatrix}3/2&-1/2\\-1/2&3/2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The induced off-diagonal entry is a concrete warning: an observed dependency edge may summarize an unobserved common cause rather than a direct relation.

    Directed structure: score, constraints, and equivalence classes¤

    For a Bayesian network, structure learning searches over DAG factorizations rather than precision-matrix supports. Common strategies are:

    Table CLASS.10. Directed structure: score, constraints, and equivalence classes
    Strategy Main signal Output and boundary
    score based likelihood, marginal likelihood, BIC, or another decomposable score one optimum, a search path, or weighted structures; combinatorial search and prior/score choices remain
    constraint based conditional-independence tests a graph or equivalence class under Markov, faithfulness, test, and sampling assumptions
    hybrid constraints restrict a score search inherits both sets of assumptions and tuning choices
    Bayesian \(p(G\mid D)\propto p(D\mid G)p(G)\) a posterior or approximation over graphs relative to the declared prior and mechanism family

    Many DAGs encode the same observational conditional independences. The three graphs \(X\to Y\to Z\), \(X\leftarrow Y\leftarrow Z\), and \(X\leftarrow Y\to Z\) share a skeleton and contain no unshielded collider, so an observational score that is equivalent across Markov-equivalent DAGs should not be read as orienting their edges. A fixed variable ordering, interventions, temporal assumptions, or a restricted functional/noise family can reduce the class, but each adds an assumption rather than extracting orientation from the same observational facts for free.

    The causal-discovery chapter develops these identification conditions, continuous acyclicity constraints, latent confounding, interventional data, and posterior sampling over DAGs. In this chapter a learned DAG is only a factorization of an observational joint law unless causal semantics have been separately justified.

    A selected adjacency is not yet a generator¤

    An adjacency matrix by itself specifies neither local probability factors nor a normalized joint law. After selecting structure, one must fit conditional distributions or potentials, declare the support and base measure, and establish the relevant normalizer, inference, and sampling procedures. Gaussian graphical models are a favorable case: a positive-definite fitted precision matrix immediately defines a normalized Gaussian law. A collection of unreconciled neighborhood regressions or attention weights does not.

    Graph learning inside a GNN supplies another instructive boundary. Variational graph structure learning by marginal likelihood learns a distribution over adjacencies jointly with a GNN for a declared node-prediction task. Its adjacency is conditioned on that task, architecture, graph prior, variational family, and approximation. It is not automatically the Markov graph of a joint distribution over measured variables, the law of a random graph, or a causal graph. Conversely, results on learning Bayes nets with a fixed chordal skeleton and bounded indegree are distribution-learning results under that structural regime, not generic evidence that unrestricted DAG search is easy.

    Counterexample: predictive edges need not recover dependencies

    Suppose a label is predictable from either of two strongly correlated features. A task-trained graph can retain one route and suppress the other with no loss in prediction, even when both variables participate in the observational dependency law. Predictive adequacy, conditional-independence recovery, and causal recovery use different targets.

    Retrieval practice: which graph was learned?
    1. Why does \(\Sigma_{13}\ne0\) not force an edge \(1-3\) in a Gaussian graphical model?
    2. What does the graphical-lasso penalty parameter control?
    3. How can one latent Gaussian variable make an observed precision matrix dense?
    4. Why should a score-equivalent Bayesian-network learner return an equivalence class rather than one confident orientation?
    5. What is still missing after an adjacency matrix has been selected?

    Solutions. (1) Gaussian edges correspond to nonzero precision entries, or equivalently nonzero partial correlations after conditioning on the other coordinates; the worked example has nonzero marginal covariance but zero conditional covariance. (2) It trades likelihood fit against sparsity and therefore changes both parameter shrinkage and edge selection; it is a tuning choice, not a discovered physical constant. (3) Marginalization subtracts the low-rank Schur-complement term \(K_{OH}K_{HH}^{-1}K_{HO}\), whose off-diagonal entries can connect many observed variables. (4) Markov-equivalent DAGs encode the same observational independences and receive the same score under the stated criterion; selecting one orientation would hide unresolved structure. (5) Local normalized conditionals or potentials, their parameters, support and base measure, and valid normalization, inference, and sampling procedures.

    Undirected and dependency-structure synthesis¤

    Undirected energies specify local compatibility only after a base measure and normalizer are supplied. Sparse precision or neighborhood structure represents conditional rather than marginal independence, and hidden variables can make marginals dense. Learning an adjacency or equivalence class is therefore a model-selection result, not yet a complete generator.
