    6. Evaluation, Applications, and Current Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the full probability, contraction, learning, and relationship development of Chapters 1–5. By the end, you should be able to do the following:

    1. design exact finite tests;
    2. separate representation and contraction error;
    3. compare rank/order choices at matched compute;
    4. audit continuous-data measures; and
    5. report current results at their actual evidence maturity.

    A tensor-network generative model should be evaluated as a probability model, a structured approximation, and an implemented contraction system. Sample quality alone cannot reveal normalization error, likelihood quality, contraction cost, rank sensitivity, or whether the advertised query is exact.

    Evaluation contract¤

    Before comparing systems, record:

    Table TN.4. Evaluation contract
    Axis Required disclosure
    probability semantics nonnegative, Born, or locally purified; real or complex; base measure and support
    structure topology, variable order, physical dimensions, boundary convention, maximum/effective ranks
    normalization exact contraction, canonical constraint, estimated normalizer, or unnormalized objective
    learning objective, optimizer or sweep schedule, initialization, truncation, regularization, restarts
    queries likelihood, marginal, conditional, MAP approximation, expectation, or sampling
    contraction order, asymptotic and measured FLOPs, peak intermediate size, caching, numeric precision
    data protocol split, preprocessing, dequantization or feature maps, missingness, leakage controls
    evidence paper version, peer-review status, code/data availability, independent reproduction

    If two systems use different variable orders, ranks, basis maps, or preprocessing, their parameter counts alone do not define a controlled comparison.

    Likelihood and normalization¤

    For an exactly normalized discrete model, report held-out

    \[ \operatorname{NLL} =-\frac1n\sum_{m=1}^{n}\log p(x^{(m)}). \]

    Bits per variable is

    \[ \operatorname{BPV}=\frac{\operatorname{NLL}}{d\log 2}, \]

    only when each example has the same declared number \(d\) of variables. Images often use bits per dimension, language uses bits per character/token or perplexity, and continuous densities require the base measure and preprocessing. These quantities are not interchangeable.

    Numerically test normalization even when theory guarantees it. For small systems, enumerate all states. For larger chains, compare canonical norm, direct environment contraction, and sampled frequencies on selected marginals. A deviation can expose a broken canonical update, complex-conjugation error, feature-map Gram error, or underflow.

    Marginal and conditional calibration¤

    Exact-query claims deserve query-level tests. Choose subsets \(S\) and compare model marginals \(p(x_S)\) with empirical frequencies or a known simulator law. For conditional generation, evaluate

    • conditional log score or Brier score for discrete events;
    • coverage and sharpness for predictive sets or intervals;
    • constraint satisfaction and probability assigned to the constraint;
    • calibration across common and rare evidence patterns; and
    • latency as the evidence set and query set change.

    Conditioning can create near-zero denominators. Report the policy for impossible or numerically negligible evidence rather than silently renormalizing arbitrary roundoff.

    Sampling checks¤

    Sequential contraction sampling is exact relative to the represented law when every conditional and categorical draw is exact. Validate the implementation by comparing sample frequencies with contracted marginals on a finite test case. Then report:

    • first-sample latency versus amortized throughput;
    • environment-cache construction and update costs;
    • batch size and accelerator utilization;
    • effective and maximum rank during sampling;
    • precision and normalization drift; and
    • any approximate truncation, beam, or top-\(k\) intervention.

    FID, KID, embedding distances, and visual inspection may supplement these checks for image-like observations, but they do not establish tensor likelihood or exact sampling.

    Approximation and capacity diagnostics¤

    A useful rank study reports held-out NLL against:

    1. maximum permitted rank;
    2. realized singular-value spectra and effective ranks;
    3. parameter count;
    4. contraction FLOPs and peak memory; and
    5. several variable orders.

    Compare at least rank one, a moderate rank, and a rank high enough to expose overfitting or computational saturation. Report all tested orders or define a validation-only order-selection protocol. For approximate contraction on trees or loopy networks, vary the truncation budget separately from the learned model rank so representation error and inference error are not conflated.

    Worked evaluation: the two-bit model¤

    For the target table

    \[ \mathcal P= \begin{bmatrix}0.4&0.1\\0.1&0.4\end{bmatrix}, \]

    an auditable unit test proceeds as follows:

    1. enumerate all four states and confirm \(\sum_xp(x)=1\);
    2. verify both one-bit marginals equal \((0.5,0.5)\);
    3. contract the conditional \(p(X_2\mid X_1=0)=(0.8,0.2)\);
    4. draw samples and attach binomial uncertainty to the observed frequency;
    5. verify the exact rank-two NLL approaches \(H(\mathcal P)\approx1.19355\);
    6. verify the best rank-one independent NLL is \(\log4\approx1.38629\); and
    7. confirm that a gauge transform changes cores but not any probability.

    This one test exercises normalization, marginalization, conditioning, sampling, capacity, and gauge invariance. A high-dimensional benchmark should not replace such finite exact tests.

    Applications¤

    Density estimation and compression¤

    MPS, tensor trains, and tensorized circuits provide normalized density estimators when their contractions are exact. Sequential probabilities can drive arithmetic coding; compression rate then evaluates log-loss operationally. The coding distribution, symbol order, precision, headers, and model cost must be included in a fair compression report.

    Missing-data and posterior inference¤

    Arbitrary observed/missing patterns can be answered by contraction when the topology and canonical structure keep those contractions tractable. Tensor trains can also approximate a posterior over many discrete latent assignments. Report approximation error against exact small instances or stronger importance/MCMC baselines, not only the tensor objective.

    Sequences, language, and biological data¤

    Chain structure matches sequences naturally, but fixed length and local physical dimension can be restrictive. Variable-length strings need an end symbol, length model, or weighted-automaton construction with finite total mass. Protein, genomic, and language evaluations must define tokenization, alphabet, homology or contamination splits, and whether generation conditions on length or metadata.

    Images and spatial data¤

    An image MPS requires a one-dimensional ordering or a higher-dimensional tensor topology. Raster, snake, space-filling-curve, patch, and learned orders induce different cut ranks. Report the order and compare it under matched capacity. Pixel likelihood and perceptual quality remain different axes.

    Continuous and scientific distributions¤

    Functional tensor trains and continuous Born models can represent densities, CDFs, and conditional laws through basis functions. Scientific use requires units, support, boundary conditions, symmetries, conservation constraints, and the reference measure. A low NLL in transformed coordinates is not sufficient if the inverse transform violates the intended physical state space.

    Research checkpoints¤

    The following results illustrate active directions without collapsing their evidence levels:

    • Established. Han et al. (2018) developed MPS Born-machine density estimation and direct sampling. Bonnevie and Schmidt (2021) developed MPS inference for discrete probabilistic models. Novikov et al. (2021) developed tensor-train density estimation with tractable probabilistic queries.
    • Peer-reviewed research. Miller et al. (2021) studied tensor networks for probabilistic sequence modeling. Loconte et al. (2025) formalized relationships between tensor factorizations and probabilistic circuits. Meiburg et al. (2025) developed continuous-valued Born MPS models under explicit feature-map assumptions. Loconte, Javaloy, and Vergari (2026) derived orthogonality conditions for efficient marginalization in squared circuits.
    • Workshop research. Wang and Luo (2026) use an MPS Born model to approximate lookahead probabilities for constrained text generation. This supports the studied method and tasks, not a general claim that a tensor guide preserves an arbitrary language model's exact conditional law.

    Evidence status is attached to each result, not to the family as a whole. Later papers can improve algorithms without invalidating the core probability and contraction semantics.

    Misconception checks¤

    • Exact NLL does not prove useful samples. It measures one declared density and must be supplemented for perceptual, scientific, or task utility.
    • Exact sampling does not mean constant-time sampling. Environment construction, rank, sequence length, batching, and cache updates still cost time and memory.
    • A lower training NLL does not select rank. Order and rank are chosen on held-out data under a declared search budget.
    • Canonical normalization does not excuse numeric tests. Broken retractions, conjugation, basis integrals, or dtype choices can violate the implementation even when the symbolic construction is sound.
    • One hardware benchmark is not a family property. Contraction layout, compiler, sparsity, batch, precision, and device determine measured speed.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Rank and order. Construct a four-bit law whose MPS rank differs under two orders. Compute the relevant unfolding ranks and relate them to a graph cut.
    2. Born versus nonnegative. Find a signed amplitude whose squared law is simple but whose intermediate contractions contain cancellation. Identify which parameters are not observable from basis probabilities.
    3. Contraction planning. Translate a small factor graph into tensors and compare two elimination orders by their largest intermediate tensor.
    4. Continuous density. Choose two nonorthogonal features, compute their Gram matrix, and show numerically how replacing it by the identity changes \(Z\).
    5. Controlled comparison. Compare an HMM, nonnegative MPS, and Born MPS on one sequence dataset with matched split, order, parameter budget, and likelihood implementation.
    6. Systems audit. Implement exact sequential sampling twice—with and without cached right environments—and separate arithmetic count from measured latency.

    Independent practice and assessment map: tensor evaluation and research¤

    Evaluate the represented law, contraction algorithm, approximation, and compiled system separately. Bind every exactness, efficiency, and research claim to a finite oracle or a protocol-bounded evidence record.

    P1 — Defend an end-to-end tensor-network generator¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Classify each statement as a theorem under named assumptions, an implementation claim, an empirical result, or an invalid inference: exact normalization implies calibrated conditionals; exact sampling implies constant latency; lower training NLL selects rank; equal parameter count matches contraction compute; one favorable variable order is a family property; continuous NLLs are comparable without a reference measure; a quantum-inspired label establishes hardware advantage; and an accepted paper establishes independent reproduction. Name the evidence required to strengthen each claim.
    2. P1.2 — Finite probability-model test. Extend the two-bit worked evaluation by calculating entropy, best rank-one NLL, KL gap, conditional Brier scores, and a 95% binomial interval for the frequency of 00 in a declared sample size. Apply an invertible gauge and verify every probability/query is invariant. Inject a conjugation or normalization bug and identify which enumeration, environment, sampling, and calibration checks detect it.
    3. P1.3 — Representation/contraction/system decomposition. Design a factorial study over at least three ranks, three validation-selected orders, exact versus truncated contractions, and two dtypes. Record training/held-out NLL, singular spectra, discarded mass, marginal error, sampling error, FLOPs, largest intermediate, peak memory, cold/warm latency, throughput, compilation/cache time, and uncertainty across restarts. State how the design separates representation error, contraction error, numerical error, optimization error, and systems overhead.
    4. P1.4 — Continuous-measure audit. Evaluate a continuous tensor density under an explicit support, product reference measure, feature basis, Gram matrices, units, preprocessing transform, and inverse map. Show how omitting a Jacobian or replacing a nonidentity Gram matrix changes normalization and NLL. Define a quadrature or analytic small-case oracle plus conditional calibration tests in physical units.
    5. P1.5 — Research and compiled-artifact audit. Select one established result, one peer-reviewed recent result, and one workshop/preprint result from the research checkpoints. Record exact claim, assumptions, topology/order/rank, query, approximation, data/split, optimization budget, hardware, metric uncertainty, released code/data, version, and independent reproduction. Reproduce one finite claim, benchmark the complete contraction system, and rewrite each headline at its actual evidence maturity.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Produce a model card and controlled comparison for a tensor generator of proteins, genomic sequences, image patches, missing sensor fields, or another domain not used in P1.2. Declare tokenization/units, support, length or cardinality law, order/topology, semantics, rank selection, exact and approximate queries, baselines, contamination/leakage controls, scientific constraints, calibration, compute, and a small falsification oracle. Route every unsupported likelihood, exactness, scaling, or advantage claim to a proof, executable test, controlled study, explicit limitation, or rejection.

    Retrieval question: what is the minimum evidence for an exact-sampling claim?

    A proof that the implemented conditionals come from the represented joint, finite-state tests matching contracted probabilities, disclosure of every approximation or truncation, and runtime measurements that include the environment construction and updates. Visual sample quality is not enough.

    Final synthesis¤

    Tensor-network generative models factor exponentially large tables or amplitudes into contractions whose cost is controlled by topology, order, and rank. Nonnegative models obtain probability through positive mass; Born models obtain it by squared magnitude and therefore allow phase, sign, and gauge non-identifiability. Canonical forms can make normalization and sequential sampling efficient, but only for the queries and structures they actually support.

    The family is most compelling when low-rank structure matches the variable order and exact marginals, conditionals, or samples are scientifically useful. It is least compelling when required rank grows rapidly, preprocessing destroys the intended semantics, or a general contraction graph creates prohibitive intermediates. The durable research habit is to state the probability object, contraction, approximation, and implementation separately—and to test all four.
