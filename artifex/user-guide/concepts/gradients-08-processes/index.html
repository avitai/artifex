    8. Differentiating Through Generative Processes and Teachers¤

    Chapter 8 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Reverse-mode differentiation, ODE and SDE solvers, Markov generators, importance sampling, and conditional generation.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Differentiate numerical dynamics: separate continuous sensitivity equations from derivatives of a finite adaptive solver.
    2. O2 — Specify stochastic paths: declare Brownian increments, mesh, coupling, random-number reuse, and pathwise versus likelihood-ratio meaning.
    3. O3 — Audit learned samplers: identify the finite-transition law, truncation, stationary-limit claim, and resampling derivative.
    4. O4 — Derive trajectory reward gradients: match credit assignment, baselines, temperature, truncation, and off-policy corrections to the sampled law.
    5. O5 — Interpret teacher fields: distinguish a pulled-back state vector, a pseudo-loss implementation, and an integrable population divergence.
    6. O6 — Select constrained derivatives: choose among subgradient, proximal, mirror, projected, Riemannian, implicit, and surrogate updates.

    Solver output is a parameterized computation¤

    Let an ODE state satisfy

    \[ \begin{aligned} \dot x(t)&=f_\theta(t,x(t)),\\ L(\theta)&=\ell(x(T)). \end{aligned} \]

    The continuous adjoint \(a(t)=\nabla_{x(t)}L\) satisfies

    \[ \begin{aligned} -\dot a(t) &=\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial x} \right)^{\!\top}a(t),\\ a(T)&=\nabla_x\ell(x(T)), \end{aligned} \]

    and, omitting explicit parameter dependence of the endpoints,

    \[ \nabla_\theta L =\int_0^T a(t)^\top \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial\theta}(t,x(t))\,dt. \]

    There are two finite derivative programs:

    • discretize then differentiate: backpropagate through the actual numerical solver operations; and
    • differentiate then discretize: derive continuous sensitivity equations and solve them numerically.

    They approach the same ideal only under convergence and consistency conditions. With finite tolerances, adaptive step decisions, interpolation, event handling, or an inaccurate reconstructed trajectory, their gradients can disagree. CNFs must additionally differentiate the numerical log-density computation, including any stochastic trace estimator. “Adjoint” names a sensitivity construction, not a promise of exact constant-memory gradients for every solver.

    SDE pathwise sensitivities require a noise contract¤

    For

    \[ dX_t=b_\theta(t,X_t)\,dt +\sigma_\theta(t,X_t)\,dW_t, \]

    a pathwise derivative holds a realization of the driving noise fixed while changing \(\theta\). A stochastic adjoint can propagate this sensitivity backward under regularity conditions (Li et al.). In a finite implementation, Brownian increments, adaptive interval queries, random seeds, and any Lévy-area approximation are part of the differentiated program. Reusing an ordinary pseudorandom seed does not by itself guarantee the same Brownian path when the adaptive mesh changes.

    Latent SDE likelihood or ELBO training may combine a pathwise SDE derivative, a Girsanov or KL term, Monte Carlo path sampling, and an adjoint solver. Bias from time discretization and variance from path sampling are different error axes.

    MCMC, SMC, and resampling create target choices¤

    Suppose an approximate posterior sample is produced by \(K\) transitions,

    \[ z_K=T_{\phi,K}(z_0,\xi_{1:K}). \]

    Backpropagating through all transitions differentiates the finite-\(K\) pushforward law. Stopping at \(z_K\), truncating after recent transitions, or replacing the chain by an equilibrium identity produces a different estimator. Even as \(K\to\infty\), interchange of differentiation and the stationary limit requires ergodicity and sensitivity conditions.

    Particle filters add a discrete resampling operation. Common choices include:

    • score-function terms for ancestor indices;
    • stopping gradients through resampling;
    • continuous or straight-through relaxations;
    • entropy-regularized transport resampling; and
    • likelihood-score identities that avoid differentiating the ancestor draw.

    These choices trade estimator variance, bias, and even the finite inference algorithm. Entropy-regularized transport yields a differentiable particle filter with its own convergence statement (Corenflos et al.); it does not make categorical multinomial resampling differentiable retroactively.

    Reward gradients for autoregressive and iterative generators¤

    For a trajectory law

    \[ p_\theta(x_{0:T}\mid c) =p(x_T)\prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,c) \]

    and a possibly non-differentiable terminal reward \(R(x_0,c)\),

    \[ \nabla_\theta\mathbb E[R] =\mathbb E \left[ \sum_{t=1}^T \bigl(R-b_t(x_t,c)\bigr) \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,c) \right] \]

    for valid state-dependent baselines \(b_t\) that do not depend on the sampled next state \(x_{t-1}\). An autoregressive token model has the same trajectory identity in the forward order. A discrete diffusion has reverse transitions; a continuous diffusion may admit pathwise as well as likelihood-ratio estimators. The estimator must match the sampling law actually used, including temperature, truncation, conditioning, and any off-policy correction.

    Worked two-token reward¤

    Let \(X_1,X_2\) be independent Bernoulli variables with \(q=\sigma(\phi)\) and let \(R=X_1X_2\). Then

    \[ J(\phi)=q^2, \qquad \frac{dJ}{d\phi}=2q^2(1-q). \]

    At \(q=1/2\), the exact derivative is \(1/4\). The trajectory estimator without a baseline is

    \[ \widehat g =R\bigl[(X_1-q)+(X_2-q)\bigr]. \]

    Only \((X_1,X_2)=(1,1)\) contributes; it occurs with probability \(1/4\) and then \(\widehat g=1\). Its expectation is therefore \(1/4\), but its variance is large. Per-prefix critics can reduce variance by assigning expected downstream reward to each token decision. They do not turn an on-policy sample into an off-policy one or repair reward misspecification.

    Policy-gradient fine-tuning has been instantiated for diffusion and discrete iterative generators, including text-to-image diffusion (DPOK), graph diffusion (GDPO), and discrete diffusion (Zekri and Boulle). These are family-specific constructions. A direct preference loss evaluated on fixed pairs uses an ordinary supervised parameter gradient unless its derivation and sampling loop explicitly introduce a policy-gradient estimator.

    Score distillation transfers a state field through a generator¤

    Let a differentiable generator or renderer produce \(x=g_\theta(z)\), and let a teacher diffusion model provide a noisy conditional state score. A broad class of distillation updates has the schematic form

    \[ \begin{aligned} u_\theta =\mathbb E_{z,t,\varepsilon}\bigl[ &J_\theta g_\theta(z)^\top w(t)\\ &\cdot\bigl(s_{q,t}(x_t)-s_{p,t}(x_t\mid c)\bigr) \bigr], \end{aligned} \]

    where the method determines the corruption convention, weight, teacher score, and whether and how the current generator score \(s_{q,t}\) is estimated. Omitting or approximating the source score, scaling guidance, and stopping derivatives through the teacher all change the update.

    In automatic-differentiation code, any supplied vector field \(v(x)\) can be installed through a pseudo-loss

    \[ L_{\mathrm{pseudo}}(x) =\operatorname{stopgrad}(v(x))^\top x, \]

    which yields \(\nabla_\theta L_{\mathrm{pseudo}}=J_\theta x^\top v(x)\). The reported scalar value of this pseudo-loss is not evidence that \(v\) is the gradient of a globally defined divergence. Integrability, omitted score terms, and the distribution over noise levels must be established separately.

    Score Distillation Sampling introduced this kind of teacher-score transfer for differentiable 3D rendering. Distribution- matching and score-implicit methods instead construct or estimate more of the student-distribution contribution (DMD2; SIM). They should be compared by target divergence, score estimator, teacher data, conditioning, guidance, and finite student architecture—not grouped as one generic “diffusion gradient.”

    Nonsmooth and constrained updates¤

    Some generative objectives contain an indicator constraint, an \(L_1\) penalty, a maximum, a clipping operation, top-\(k\) selection, or a discrete projection. A subgradient is a set-valued first-order object for an appropriate nonsmooth function. A proximal step solves a local regularized subproblem. Mirror descent uses a Bregman geometry. A projected or Riemannian step keeps an iterate on a constraint set or manifold. These are not interchangeable synonyms for a backpropagated Euclidean gradient.

    Typical applications include simplex-constrained mixture weights, sparse latent or energy parameters, spectral or Lipschitz constraints in critics, manifold-valued molecular states, and hard feasibility in inverse generation. Straight-through differentiation across a hard projection is a surrogate; implicit differentiation of a unique optimization layer requires its own regularity conditions.

    Independent practice and assessment map: differentiating generative processes¤

    For every task, distinguish the continuous mathematical system from the finite program whose output automatic differentiation actually sees.

    1. P1.1 — Compare two ODE derivatives. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Explain what is differentiated by continuous adjoint sensitivity and by reverse AD through an adaptive solver. Design a tolerance-refinement experiment that can reveal disagreement.
    2. P1.2 — Specify a Brownian coupling. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 13 min. For a parameterized SDE solved on adaptive meshes, specify how two nearby parameter evaluations share one Brownian path for a directional finite-difference check. Explain why merely resetting a pseudorandom seed can fail.
    3. P1.3 — Audit finite MCMC and resampling. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A variational objective uses \(K=10\) MCMC steps and particle resampling, stops gradients through ancestor indices, and calls its update an equilibrium posterior gradient. Identify the finite target, omitted derivative, and evidence required for an equilibrium claim.
    4. P1.4 — Predict before computing: re-derive the two-token reward gradient. Type: derive · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. With independent Bernoulli tokens, \(q=1/2\), and reward \(R=X_1X_2\), predict how many trajectories contribute, then enumerate all trajectories to compute the exact derivative, estimator mean, and estimator variance.
    5. P1.5 — Test teacher-field integrability. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. For state field \(v(x_1,x_2)=(-x_2,x_1)\), test whether it can be the Euclidean gradient of a smooth scalar potential on \(\mathbb R^2\). State what a stop-gradient pseudo-loss still computes after composing \(x=g_\theta(z)\).
    6. P1.6 — Transfer: select a constrained update. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 17 min. Match each case to a subgradient, proximal, mirror, projected/Riemannian, implicit, or explicitly surrogate method: sparse energy coefficients, simplex mixture weights, unit-norm molecular directions, a unique convex optimization layer, and hard top-\(k\) token selection. State the finite update and validation boundary for each.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. Continuous adjoints differentiate the ideal ODE solution map through sensitivity equations and then discretize those equations. Reverse AD differentiates the executed finite solver, including accepted steps, interpolation, events, and numerical log-density operations. Run both over decreasing tolerances, compare against central directional differences of the executed loss, and report forward-state error, gradient discrepancy, steps, and memory. Check. Agreement at one tolerance does not establish asymptotic consistency.
    2. P1.2. Define one Brownian path through a bridge, counter-based interval generator, or stored refinement tree so any interval increment is consistent with its parent and disjoint increments. Both \(\theta+hv\) and \(\theta-hv\) query that same path even if their solvers choose different meshes. Resetting a sequential seed can assign different random draws to different intervals after one adaptive branch changes the call order. Check. Common seed and common stochastic path are not synonymous.
    3. P1.3. Reverse AD targets the finite ten-transition pushforward combined with the implemented resampling surrogate. Stopping ancestor derivatives omits dependence of selected ancestry on parameters. An equilibrium claim additionally needs convergence of the chain, interchange of parameter differentiation and stationary limit, increasing-\(K\) sensitivity, resampling-bias checks against exact small filters, and diagnostics for mixing and effective particles. Rubric. Better posterior samples do not alone identify the omitted derivative.
    4. P1.4. \(J(q)=q^2\), so \(dJ/d\phi=2q^2(1-q)=1/4\). The score estimator is zero on \((0,0),(0,1),(1,0)\) and one on \((1,1)\), each trajectory having probability \(1/4\). Hence its mean is \(1/4\), second moment \(1/4\), and variance \(1/4-(1/4)^2=3/16\). Check. Exactness coexists with high relative variance.
    5. P1.5. \(\partial v_1/\partial x_2=-1\) while \(\partial v_2/\partial x_1=1\), so the Jacobian is not symmetric and no smooth global Euclidean potential has gradient \(v\) on \(\mathbb R^2\). Nevertheless \(L_{\mathrm{pseudo}}=\operatorname{stopgrad}(v(x))^\top x\) makes AD return \(J_\theta g_\theta(z)^\top v(g_\theta(z))\). Check. The pseudo-loss installs a vector--Jacobian product; its displayed scalar is not the missing potential.
    6. P1.6. Sparse coefficients admit a proximal soft-thresholding step for an \(L_1\) penalty; simplex weights admit mirror descent or a simplex projection with its declared finite convention; unit-norm directions use tangent projection plus a retraction/Riemannian integrator; a unique regular convex layer admits implicit differentiation of its KKT or stationarity system; hard top-\(k\) is nondifferentiable at selection changes and needs a score method, relaxation, search method, or explicitly biased straight-through surrogate. Validate feasibility, stationarity/prox residuals, boundary events, derivative checks where smooth, surrogate bias, and downstream hard-objective metrics. Rubric. “Use backprop” is incomplete for every case.

    Chapter 8 synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    Differentiating a generative process requires a contract for the finite solver, random path, resampling rule, and truncation. Trajectory score estimators permit non-differentiable rewards but inherit credit-assignment and on-policy sampling variance. Score distillation pulls a teacher state field back through a student Jacobian, but a pseudo-loss does not guarantee a global divergence. Nonsmooth, proximal, mirror, projected, and Riemannian updates add still different finite mathematical objects.

    Retrieval questions
    1. Why can a continuous adjoint disagree with backpropagation through an adaptive ODE solver?
    2. What law does a \(K\)-step unrolled MCMC gradient differentiate?
    3. Why is the two-token policy-gradient estimator unbiased but noisy?
    4. What does a stop-gradient pseudo-loss prove about its supplied vector field?

    Solutions. (1) One differentiates continuous dynamics and then discretizes; the other differentiates the actual finite solver, whose step decisions and interpolation affect the map. (2) The finite-\(K\) pushforward law, not automatically the equilibrium law. (3) Its score expectation is exact, but only the rare rewarding trajectory contributes in the example. (4) Only that automatic differentiation will return the installed vector-- Jacobian product; it does not prove integrability or identify a population divergence.

    Bridge. Chapter 9 leaves a particular solver trace and studies gradients of functions, maps, particles, and probability measures themselves.
