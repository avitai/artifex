    This chapter develops the latent-variable generative story, posterior approximation, ELBO, decoder likelihood, architecture, and reparameterization estimator. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), introduced concurrently by Kingma & Welling (2014) and Rezende, Mohamed & Wierstra (2014), combine neural latent-variable models with variational inference. Unlike a deterministic autoencoder, a VAE specifies a prior, observation model, and approximate posterior, enabling prior sampling and uncertainty-aware latent inference. Interpretability is a separate property that requires assumptions and evaluation. For a detailed tutorial by the original authors, see Kingma & Welling (2019).

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand joint, marginal, and conditional probability; expectations; Gaussian distributions; KL divergence; and Jensen's inequality. Review Latent Variables and Inference if marginalization or posterior inference is unfamiliar; its EM-to-amortized-learning bridge shows exactly which classical operation the encoder replaces. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. write the VAE generative model separately from its inference model;
    2. derive the ELBO and its exact posterior-KL gap;
    3. derive the dataset-averaged ELBO as a divergence between model and inference joints;
    4. calculate the ELBO of a one-dimensional Gaussian example;
    5. explain why reconstruction quality, aggregated-posterior matching, and posterior inference are distinct;
    6. explain the semantics of the decoder likelihood, including its observation scale; and
    7. parameterize and evaluate a full-covariance Gaussian posterior without confusing conditional covariance with aggregate independence; and
    8. distinguish a pathwise gradient from the false claim that stochastic sampling has no gradient estimator.

    Consequences of the model–inference split¤

    The combination of a latent generative model and amortized variational inference creates several useful capabilities, each with a separate condition:

    • Interpolation can be meaningful when the decoder behaves smoothly along the chosen path and that path stays in regions supported by the aggregate posterior.
    • Prior samples can be realistic when the learned aggregate posterior is sufficiently well matched to the prior and the decoder likelihood is appropriate.
    • Representations can become useful or controllable, but vanilla ELBO training does not identify human-interpretable factors without additional assumptions, supervision, inductive bias, or evaluation.

    Constructive interpretation: generation, inference, and reconstruction¤

    The model can be understood through four operations that should not be conflated:

    1. Generation. Draw \(z\sim p(z)\) and then draw \(x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z)\). This two-stage law, not the encoder, defines the model distribution.

    2. Inference. Given an observation \(x\), evaluate or sample from \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) as an approximation to the generally intractable posterior \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\).

    3. Reconstruction. Draw or summarize a latent under \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) and evaluate the decoder. This composes approximate inference with the observation model; it is not the same query as unconditional generation.

    4. Representation analysis. Study the geometry and information content of \(z\) under the aggregate posterior. The ELBO alone does not arrange coordinates according to human concepts or guarantee that paths between encoded examples stay in well-supported regions.

    The encoder returns a distribution rather than a single deterministic code. The KL term penalizes a conditional posterior–prior discrepancy but does not eliminate holes, aggregate mismatch, or decoder misspecification. Prior-sample validity and interpolation quality are therefore empirical properties to measure.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Generative Story¤

    VAEs model the data generation process as a two-step procedure:

    1. Sample latent code: \(z \sim p(z)\) from a simple prior distribution (typically standard normal)
    2. Generate data: \(x \sim p_\theta(x|z)\) using a decoder network parameterized by \(\theta\)

    The likelihood depends on \(\theta\), not on \(\phi\). VAE training learns \(\theta\) by maximizing a variational lower bound on the observed-data likelihood and learns \(\phi\) to supply a useful amortized posterior approximation and gradient estimator for that bound.

    Variational Inference: Why We Need Approximation¤

    Variational inference (Jordan et al., 1999; see Blei et al., 2017 for a modern review) replaces an intractable inference problem with a tractable optimisation problem.

    The true posterior \(p_\theta(z|x)\) tells us what latent code likely generated our data. However, computing it requires:

    \[ p_\theta(z|x) = \frac{p_\theta(x|z)p(z)}{p_\theta(x)} = \frac{p_\theta(x|z)p(z)}{\int p_\theta(x|z')p(z') dz'} \]

    The integral in the denominator (the evidence \(p_\theta(x)\)) is generally intractable for a neural nonlinear decoder, even though special conjugate or linear-Gaussian latent models admit exact inference. VAEs learn a tractable approximate posterior \(q_\phi(z|x)\) (the encoder), trading exact per-observation inference for amortized optimization.

    The ELBO: Evidence Lower BOund¤

    The key insight of VAEs is to maximize a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood called the Evidence Lower BOund (ELBO):

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]

    This inequality states that the log-likelihood is always at least as large as the ELBO. The gap between them equals exactly \(D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p_\theta(z|x))\)—when our approximate posterior perfectly matches the true posterior, there's no gap and we achieve the true likelihood.

    Derivation from First Principles¤

    Starting with the log-likelihood and introducing our approximate posterior:

    \[ \begin{align} \log p_\theta(x) &= \log \int p_\theta(x, z) dz \\ &= \log \int p_\theta(x, z) \frac{q_\phi(z|x)}{q_\phi(z|x)} dz \\ &= \log \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)} \left[\frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] \end{align} \]

    Applying Jensen's inequality (since log is concave):

    \[ \log \mathbb{E}[f(z)] \geq \mathbb{E}[\log f(z)] \]

    We get:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)} \left[\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] \]

    Expanding \(p_\theta(x, z) = p_\theta(x|z)p(z)\):

    \[ \begin{align} \text{ELBO} &= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] + \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\left[\log \frac{p(z)}{q_\phi(z|x)}\right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \end{align} \]

    From One Observation to Two Joint Distributions¤

    The pointwise ELBO answers a local question: for this \(x\), how well does \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) approximate the model posterior? Training over a data distribution raises a second question: which joint distribution over data and latents is being matched? Keeping these questions separate prevents three common mistakes:

    1. treating a reconstruction as evidence that prior samples are correct;
    2. treating an aggregated-posterior match as evidence that every posterior is accurate; and
    3. calling every failure an inference failure when the model marginal itself is wrong.

    Let \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\) denote the population data law. It may be replaced by a well-defined empirical mass function for a finite discrete example. The model and inference sides are:

    Table VAE.2. From One Observation to Two Joint Distributions
    Object Definition Question it answers
    Data marginal \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\) How often does the data-generating process produce \(x\)?
    Model joint \(p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)\) Which \((x,z)\) pairs does the generator assign mass or density to?
    Model marginal \(p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz\) What distribution results from prior sampling and decoding?
    Model posterior \(p_\theta(z\mid x)=p_\theta(x,z)/p_\theta(x)\) Under the model, which latents explain an observed \(x\)?
    Inference joint \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)=p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)\) What joint law results from drawing real data and encoding it?
    Aggregated posterior \(q_\phi(z)=\int q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\,dx\) What latent distribution results after averaging encodings over data?
    Reverse inference conditional \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x\mid z)=q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)/q_\phi(z)\) Which real-data examples share an inferred latent?

    Solid arrows construct, marginalize, or condition distributions. The dotted arrow compares two latent marginals; it is not a sampling transition. The observed-space and full-joint comparisons are given by the KL chain rules below.

    The inference joint is not a second generative model. Its \(x\) marginal is fixed to the data law, and its conditional \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is an inference approximation. The model joint is the object used for unconditional generation.

    Measure and support conditions

    The joint-KL identities below assume that the relevant distributions admit densities or mass functions under compatible reference measures and that \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(p_\theta(x,z)\). An empirical collection of point masses is singular with respect to an ordinary continuous density. In that setting, optimize the empirical average directly, or state a population, dequantized, or noised data law before interpreting the expression as a literal joint KL. The support-mismatch chapter develops this distinction.

    Deriving the augmented-space identity¤

    Average the pointwise ELBO under \(p_{\mathcal D}\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} \overline{\mathcal L}(\theta,\phi) &=\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)} \left[\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)\right]\\ &=\mathbb E_{q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)} \left[\log\frac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)} +\log p_{\mathcal D}(x)\right]\\ &=-D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\,\|\,p_\theta(x,z) \right)-H(p_{\mathcal D}). \end{aligned} \]

    The data entropy is constant with respect to \(\theta\) and \(\phi\). Thus, under the stated measure conditions, maximizing the dataset-averaged ELBO is equivalent to minimizing a joint KL in the augmented space \((x,z)\). This is an algebraic equivalence, not merely an analogy.

    The KL chain rule exposes two complementary views of that same joint mismatch:

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z)) &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\|p_\theta(x))\\ &\quad+\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)\right), \end{aligned} \]

    and

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z)) &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z))\\ &\quad+\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x\mid z)\|p_\theta(x\mid z) \right). \end{aligned} \]

    The first decomposition separates observed-space model fit from posterior inference fit. The second separates aggregated-posterior matching from conditional decoder fit. The last conditional KL is not identical to the usual reconstruction NLL: it also contains the conditional entropy of \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x\mid z)\), which changes when the encoder changes. InfoVAE uses these joint-distribution relationships to analyze how VAE objectives balance learning and inference.

    Finally, the per-example prior regularizer has its own exact decomposition:

    \[ \mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) =I_{q}(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)). \]

    It charges both information carried about the observation and aggregate mismatch to the prior. Consequently, neither a small per-example KL nor an aggregate match alone establishes that the representation is informative, that the decoder fits the data, or that the approximate posterior is accurate.

    Worked counterexample: perfect reconstruction, wrong prior samples¤

    Let \(X,Z\in\{0,1\}\) and let the data be balanced: \(p_{\mathcal D}(X=0)=p_{\mathcal D}(X=1)=1/2\). Choose a deterministic encoder \(q(Z=x\mid X=x)=1\) and deterministic decoder \(p_\theta(X=z\mid Z=z)=1\). Reconstructions are perfect. Now choose the mismatched prior

    \[ p(Z=0)=0.1, \qquad p(Z=1)=0.9. \]

    The two joints have support on the same diagonal pairs but assign different probabilities:

    Table VAE.3. Worked counterexample: perfect reconstruction, wrong prior samples
    \((x,z)\) \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\) \(p_\theta(x,z)\)
    \((0,0)\) \(0.5\) \(0.1\)
    \((0,1)\) \(0\) \(0\)
    \((1,0)\) \(0\) \(0\)
    \((1,1)\) \(0.5\) \(0.9\)

    The expected reconstruction log-likelihood is \(0\) nats, its largest possible value for this discrete deterministic decoder. Yet the aggregate posterior is balanced while the prior is not:

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)) &=\frac12\log\frac{0.5}{0.1} +\frac12\log\frac{0.5}{0.9}\\ &\approx0.5108\ \text{nats}. \end{aligned} \]

    The posterior is exact for both observations, so the posterior inference gap is zero. Nevertheless, prior sampling produces \(X=1\) with probability \(0.9\) instead of the data probability \(0.5\). The average regularizer is

    \[ \begin{aligned} R &\equiv\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z))\\ &=\frac12\log 10+\frac12\log\frac{10}{9}\\ &\approx1.2040,\\ I_q(X;Z)&=\log2\approx0.6931,\\ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z))&\approx0.5108,\\ R&=I_q(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)). \end{aligned} \]

    Therefore \(\overline{\mathcal L}\approx-1.2040\). The joint identity gives the same answer:

    \[ \begin{aligned} -H(p_{\mathcal D}) -D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}\|p_\theta) &=-\log2-0.5108\\ &\approx-1.2040. \end{aligned} \]

    This example isolates the failure: reconstruction and posterior inference are both perfect, while the model marginal and prior-sample frequencies are wrong.

    Where approximation and amortization enter¤

    For a fixed \(x\) and model \(\theta\), let \(\mathcal Q\) be the local variational family and let \(\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)\) be its best attainable ELBO. Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x),\\ G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x) &=\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x)+G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x). \end{aligned} \]

    The first term remains when the family cannot express the model posterior. The second remains when the shared encoder does not return the best member of that family for this observation. In a finite experiment, incomplete local or global optimization can contaminate the measured second term, so report optimization separately. This decomposition follows the analysis of Cremer, Li, and Duvenaud; more recent theory gives model-dependent conditions under which amortized and per-instance variational optima can coincide (Margossian and Blei, 2024).

    The augmented-space view contains the realized posterior gap averaged over data, but it does not collapse all errors into inference. Even zero approximation and amortization gaps leave the observed-space model mismatch in the first chain-rule decomposition—as the binary counterexample demonstrates. Conversely, matching \(p_\theta(x)\) does not certify that \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is accurate.

    A diagnostic quartet

    Evaluate four objects separately: reconstruction or conditional log-likelihood under encoded latents; prior samples and the model marginal; aggregate-code mismatch \(q_\phi(z)\) versus \(p(z)\); and posterior inference using tighter bounds, per-instance refinement, exact small cases, or other query-appropriate checks. No one member of the quartet certifies the other three.

    Two Interpretable Terms¤

    The ELBO naturally decomposes into two competing objectives:

    1. Reconstruction term¤

    \[ \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] \]
    • Measures how well we can reconstruct the input from sampled latent codes.
    • Encourages the model to preserve information.
    • Higher is better (less negative).

    2. KL divergence¤

    \[ D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]
    • Measures how close each learned encoding distribution is to the prior.
    • Regularises each approximate posterior relative to the prior; smoothness and semantic structure are possible outcomes, not identities of the KL term.
    • Charges information and mismatch relative to the chosen prior; it does not by itself enforce aggregate-posterior equality or semantic organization.
    • Lower is better (closer to the prior under this term).

    The fundamental trade-off: The reconstruction term rewards information useful to the observation model. The KL term charges rate relative to the prior. Their balance defines a rate–distortion operating point; finite optimisation can produce collapse, aggregate-posterior mismatch, unused dimensions, or irregular decoder geometry rather than an automatically structured and smooth latent space.

    Bits-back interpretation of the ELBO¤

    When \(x\) and the coder state are discrete and both sender and receiver share \(p(z)\), \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\), and \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\), a bits-back code gives the negative ELBO an operational lossless-coding interpretation. The sender uses initial random bits to select \(z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)\), encodes \(x\) under \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\) and \(z\) under \(p(z)\), and the receiver reverses those operations and recovers the sampling information under \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\). The ideal net cost is

    \[ \begin{aligned} -\operatorname{ELBO}_2(x) &=\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\!\left[ -\log_2p_\theta(x,z)\right.\\ &\qquad\left.+\log_2q_\phi(z\mid x) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    This statement does not turn every trained VAE into a compressor. A practical system must discretize the relevant distributions, keep probability tables synchronized, seed the coder with bits, and amortize startup and termination overhead across messages. Posterior approximation error remains part of the rate. The information and coding volume derives the operational accounting and its boundaries; the objective-centered treatment connects it to the ELBO and practical BB-ANS construction.

    Worked example: a scalar ELBO with an exact check¤

    Let the generative model and observation be

    \[ p(z)=\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad p(x\mid z)=\mathcal N(z,1), \qquad x=1. \]

    Choose \(q(z\mid x)=\mathcal N(0.5,0.5)\), where the second argument is the variance. The Gaussian KL is

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p) =\frac12\left(0.5+0.5^2-1-\log0.5\right) \approx0.222. \]

    The quadratic moment is

    \[ \mathbb E_q[(1-z)^2] =(1-0.5)^2+0.5 =0.75. \]

    Therefore

    \[ \mathbb E_q[\log p(x=1\mid z)] =-\frac12\left(\log(2\pi)+0.75\right) \approx-1.294. \]

    Thus \(\mathrm{ELBO}\approx-1.516\). This model has the analytic marginal \(p(x)=\mathcal N(0,2)\), so \(\log p(x=1)\approx-1.516\) as well: the chosen \(q\) is exactly the posterior \(\mathcal N(0.5,0.5)\) and the gap is zero. If we instead choose \(q=\mathcal N(0,1)\), the ELBO becomes strictly lower by \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z\mid x)\|p(z\mid x))\). This finite example makes the bound, its equality condition, and the two different KL terms concrete.

    Architecture Components¤

    Encoder: Variational Posterior \(q_\phi(z|x)\)¤

    The encoder is a neural network that maps inputs to parameters of a probability distribution over latent codes:

    \[ q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(z; \mu_\phi(x), \sigma^2_\phi(x) \mathbf{I}) \]

    For a diagonal Gaussian (most common choice), the encoder outputs:

    • Mean \(\mu_\phi(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d\) - the center of the latent distribution
    • Log-variance \(\log \sigma^2_\phi(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d\) - the spread/uncertainty

    Why output log-variance? Numerical stability. Variance must be positive, and learning \(\log \sigma^2\) allows the network to output any real number while ensuring \(\sigma^2 = \exp(\log \sigma^2) > 0\).

    Beyond a diagonal Gaussian posterior¤

    A diagonal Gaussian makes the coordinates of \(Z\) conditionally independent given one input under \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\). It does not imply that coordinates are independent under the aggregate posterior \(q_\phi(z)\), and it says still less about semantic or causal factors. The diagonal choice is popular because the encoder emits \(O(d)\) values and both sampling and the standard-normal KL are coordinatewise. It is a computational restriction on the inference family, not a discovered property of the data.

    For a full-covariance Gaussian, let the encoder emit a lower-triangular matrix \(L_\phi(x)\) with a positive diagonal, for example \(L_{ii}=\operatorname{softplus}(a_{ii})+\varepsilon\). Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Sigma_\phi(x)&:=L_\phi(x)L_\phi(x)^\top,\\ q_\phi(z\mid x) &=\mathcal N\!\left(z;\mu_\phi(x),\Sigma_\phi(x)\right),\\ &z=\mu_\phi(x)+L_\phi(x)\epsilon,\\ &\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I). \end{aligned} \]

    For \(p(z)=\mathcal N(0,I)\), the KL is

    \[ \begin{aligned} &D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)\right)\\ &\quad=\frac12\left[ \|\mu\|_2^2+\|L\|_F^2-d\right.\\[-2pt] &\qquad\left.-2\sum_{i=1}^{d}\log L_{ii} \right]. \end{aligned} \tag{VAE.1}\label{eq:vae-full-covariance-gaussian-kl} \]

    The trace, mean, dimension, and log-determinant contributions are all visible in Equation \(\eqref{eq:vae-full-covariance-gaussian-kl}\). This parameterization needs \(d(d+1)/2\) matrix outputs and \(O(d^2)\) work for a triangular matrix-vector product. A dense Cholesky factorization costs \(O(d^3)\) when the model first constructs an unrestricted covariance matrix; that factorization is unnecessary when the encoder emits \(L\) directly. Either route still has quadratic storage, which can be prohibitive for large latent groups. Low-rank-plus-diagonal covariance, sparse precision matrices, graph-structured factors, and normalizing-flow posteriors trade different dependencies, costs, and density-evaluation requirements.

    Worked two-dimensional posterior. Take

    \[ \mu=\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \qquad L=\begin{bmatrix} 1&0\\[2pt] \tfrac12&\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2} \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Then \(LL^\top\) has unit marginal variances and covariance \(1/2\). Because \(\|\mu\|^2=1\), \(\|L\|_F^2=2\), and \(\det(LL^\top)=3/4\),

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|\mathcal N(0,I)) =\frac12\left(1-\log\frac34\right) \approx0.6438\ \text{nats}. \]

    A diagonal posterior cannot represent this conditional correlation. A flow or structured posterior may represent much more than a single dense Gaussian, but greater expressiveness does not by itself remove amortization or optimization error. The inference guide separates those gaps and compares structured variational families.

    Decoder: Likelihood \(p_\theta(x|z)\)¤

    The decoder is a neural network that maps latent codes back to data space:

    \[ p_\theta(x|z) = \mathcal{N}(x; \mu_\theta(z), \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}) \quad \text{or} \quad \text{Bernoulli}(x; f_\theta(z)) \]

    The choice of output distribution depends on your data:

    • Gaussian (continuous): For real-valued images (often simplified to MSE loss with fixed variance)
    • Bernoulli (binary): For binary images or features (use sigmoid + BCE loss)
    • Categorical: For discrete data (use softmax + cross-entropy)

    The Reparameterization Trick¤

    The Problem: Backpropagation Through Sampling¤

    We need gradients of \(\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[f(z)]\) with respect to \(\phi\). Treating a sampled value as a constant and differentiating only \(f(z)\) omits how its distribution depends on \(\phi\). Stochastic gradients are still possible through score-function estimators, but they often have high variance. For reparameterizable distributions, the pathwise estimator exposes a lower-variance differentiable route.

    The Solution: Separate Randomness from Parameters¤

    Instead of sampling \(z\) directly from \(q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x), \sigma^2_\phi(x))\), reparameterize as:

    \[ z = \mu_\phi(x) + \sigma_\phi(x) \odot \epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I}) \]

    where \(\odot\) denotes element-wise multiplication.

    Why this works:

    1. The randomness (\(\epsilon\)) is now independent of our parameters \(\phi\)
    2. Gradients flow through the deterministic operations \(\mu_\phi\) and \(\sigma_\phi\)
    3. The expectation is taken over a parameter-independent noise law.
    4. We can approximate this expectation with Monte Carlo sampling: sample \(\epsilon\), compute gradients, average

    The transformed expectation is

    \[ \mathbb{E}_{p(\epsilon)}[f(g_\phi(\epsilon, x))], \]

    where \(g_\phi\) is deterministic in \(\epsilon\) and \(x\) for fixed parameters.

    This idea — proposed simultaneously by Kingma & Welling, 2014 and Rezende et al., 2014 — enabled practical VAE training and has since become fundamental to probabilistic deep learning.

    Counterexample: deterministic reconstruction is not a VAE likelihood

    If a decoder returns only \(\hat x=f_\theta(z)\) and training minimizes an unscaled MSE without declaring \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\), the code may resemble a VAE but its reported loss is not yet a complete ELBO. Choosing a Gaussian observation variance supplies the missing probabilistic semantics; changing that variance changes the relative rate–distortion trade-off even if the network output is unchanged.

    Independent practice and assessment map: foundations¤

    Solve this problem from the probability tables rather than adapting the scalar Gaussian worked example. Keep the generative joint, exact posterior, and variational approximation visibly separate throughout.

    P1 — Audit a finite latent-variable model from joint law to gradient path¤

    Let \(z,x\in\{0,1\}\) with \(p(z=1)=0.5\), \(p(x=1\mid z=0)=0.2\), and \(p(x=1\mid z=1)=0.8\). For the observed event \(x=1\), an encoder proposes \(q(z=1\mid x=1)=0.6\).

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether the encoder over- or underweights \(z=1\) relative to the exact posterior, whether its ELBO is tight, and whether a perfect reconstruction query would by itself validate prior samples. Name the probability object behind each prediction.
    2. P1.2 — Finite ELBO calculation. Calculate the four joint masses, \(p(x=1)\), \(p(z\mid x=1)\), the encoder ELBO for \(x=1\), and its posterior-KL gap. Verify numerically that \(\log p(x=1)=\mathrm{ELBO}+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p(z\mid x=1))\).
    3. P1.3 — Augmented-joint audit. Let \(p_{\mathcal D}(x=0)=p_{\mathcal D}(x=1)=0.5\) and additionally set \(q(z=1\mid x=0)=0.4\). Tabulate \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)q(z\mid x)\) and \(p(z)p(x\mid z)\), calculate their KL divergence, and identify the model-marginal and posterior-inference contributions in the observed-direction decomposition.
    4. P1.4 — Likelihood semantics. Replace binary \(x\) by a scalar measurement. Compare a fixed-variance Gaussian decoder, a learned-variance Gaussian decoder, and an unscaled MSE implementation. State the support, normalization, observation-scale parameter, and exact condition under which the MSE is proportional to a reconstruction NLL.
    5. P1.5 — Structured posterior and estimator. For a two-dimensional Gaussian encoder, write a Cholesky-factor reparameterization with one nonzero off-diagonal entry and identify its storage, multiplication, and factorization costs. Explain why conditional covariance under \(q(z\mid x)\) neither establishes nor refutes aggregate independence. Then contrast this pathwise estimator with a Bernoulli latent for which a score-function or relaxation estimator is needed.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Reinterpret \(z\) as an unobserved patient subtype and \(x\) as a diagnostic result. Specify unconditional generation, posterior inference, reconstruction/prediction, and representation analysis as four distinct queries, and state what additional evidence is required before treating a latent coordinate as a clinical factor.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A VAE contains two directional constructions with different roles. The joint \(p_\theta(x,z)\) defines generation; the conditional \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) approximates posterior inference. Pointwise, the ELBO gap is a posterior KL. Across a compatible data law, maximizing the average ELBO minimizes a joint KL between the inference and model joints. Its chain-rule decompositions separate model-marginal fit, posterior inference, aggregated-posterior matching, and decoder-conditional fit. Reparameterization changes the gradient estimator, not the model distribution. Decoder output types and scale parameters are part of the likelihood, not implementation decoration. A diagonal encoder posterior is a conditional factorization choice; it neither proves aggregate independence nor identifies semantic factors.

    Retrieval practice: VAE foundations
    1. Which distribution is used for unconditional generation: \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) or \(p(z)\)?
    2. When is the ELBO equal to \(\log p_\theta(x)\)?
    3. Does a diagonal \(q\) imply that the aggregated latent coordinates are statistically independent over the dataset?
    4. What probabilistic assumption turns fixed-scale MSE into a reconstruction NLL?
    5. Can perfect reconstruction and exact posterior inference guarantee correct prior samples?
    6. What two terms appear when the augmented-space joint KL is decomposed in the observed direction?
    7. If an encoder emits a dense Cholesky factor directly, which costs remain quadratic and which cubic factorization can be avoided?

    Solutions. (1) Draw \(z\) from the prior \(p(z)\), then \(x\) from the decoder likelihood. (2) When \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) equals the exact posterior almost everywhere relevant to the observation. (3) No; conditional diagonal covariance does not imply aggregate independence. (4) An isotropic Gaussian decoder with a fixed, declared observation variance and reduction convention. (5) No. Reconstruction uses encoded latents, whereas generation uses the prior; the model marginal can remain wrong even when the posterior inference gap is zero. (6) Observed-space model mismatch \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\|p_\theta(x))\) and the data-averaged posterior KL. (7) Storing and multiplying the triangular factor remain \(O(d^2)\); the \(O(d^3)\) Cholesky factorization of a separately constructed dense covariance is avoided.
