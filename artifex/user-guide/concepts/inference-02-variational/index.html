    2. ELBO Derivation¤

    Chapter 2 chapter prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the query and target distinctions from Chapter 1 and familiarity with KL divergence. By the end, you should be able to derive the ELBO, separate its functional from a finite estimator, decompose model and inference mismatch, diagnose approximation and amortization gaps, and distinguish a flow used as a variational posterior from the same map used as a corrected proposal or preconditioner.

    What problem is the ELBO solving?

    The evidence contains a hard log integral. The ELBO introduces a normalized distribution \(q(z)\), rewrites that log evidence as a computable expectation plus a nonnegative posterior KL, and then optimizes the computable part. The identity is exact; the approximation enters through the chosen \(q\) and how well it is optimized.

    Introduce any normalized \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) whose support covers the relevant posterior:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\log\int q_\phi(z\mid x) \frac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}dz. \]

    Jensen's inequality gives

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) \ge \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)} \left[\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)\right] \equiv\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ELBO}}. \]

    Adding and subtracting the posterior log density yields the exact gap:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ELBO}} +D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)\bigr). \]

    Therefore the ELBO is tight exactly when \(q_\phi\) equals the true posterior almost everywhere. Rewriting the joint gives the familiar VAE form:

    \[ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{ELBO}} =\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)). \]

    Bound, objective, and estimator are different objects¤

    Three layers are easy to conflate:

    1. The ELBO is a deterministic functional of \(p_\theta\) and \(q_\phi\).
    2. The optimization objective may be the ELBO, a weighted modification such as a \(\beta\)-objective, or a multi-sample bound.
    3. The estimator is the finite-sample computation used to estimate that objective and its gradient.

    Changing a minibatch size or number of latent samples changes estimator variance; it does not change the one-sample ELBO itself. Multiplying the KL term by \(\beta\ne1\) changes the objective and generally means it is no longer the ordinary evidence lower bound for the original model.

    Dataset-level decomposition¤

    Define the empirical aggregate posterior \(q_\phi(z)=\frac1N\sum_n q_\phi(z\mid x_n)\). Averaging the per-example KL gives

    \[ \mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) =I_q(X;Z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)). \]

    The KL budget therefore pays both for information about the individual observation and for mismatch between the aggregate code distribution and the prior. This identity is central to rate–distortion analysis: reducing the KL indiscriminately can improve prior matching while erasing information needed for reconstruction.

    Augmented-space view: model fit and inference fit are separate¤

    For a data law \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\), define the inference joint \(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)=p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)\). When this joint and \(p_\theta(x,z)\) have compatible reference measures and the required support, the average ELBO satisfies

    \[ \overline{\mathcal L}(\theta,\phi) =-H(p_{\mathcal D}) -D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left( q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z) \right). \]

    Applying the KL chain rule in the observed direction gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_{\mathcal D,\phi}(x,z)\|p_\theta(x,z)) &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\|p_\theta(x))\\ &\quad+\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)). \end{aligned} \]

    The first term is an observed-space model mismatch. The second is the average realized inference gap. Better inference can reduce the second term for a fixed model, but it cannot make an incorrect \(p_\theta(x)\) correct. Likewise, a flexible model can fit \(p_{\mathcal D}(x)\) while its amortized posterior remains inaccurate. The full derivation, graphical map, and a finite case with perfect reconstruction but incorrect prior samples appear in VAEs in augmented space.

    An empirical average is not automatically a continuous-distribution KL

    If \(p_{\mathcal D}\) is represented by point masses while \(p_\theta\) is a continuous density, the two are singular. The empirical ELBO average is still a valid training computation, but the displayed joint KL should be interpreted only after specifying a compatible population, dequantized, or noised data law. This is the same reference-measure issue that appears in continuous maximum likelihood.

    3. Three Sources of Inference Error¤

    Let \(\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)\) be the highest pointwise ELBO attainable in a declared local family \(\mathcal Q\). The posterior inference gap decomposes as

    \[ \begin{aligned} G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x) &=\log p_\theta(x)-\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x),\\ G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x) &=\mathcal L^*_{\mathcal Q}(x)-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\\ G_{\mathrm{inf}}(x) &=G_{\mathrm{approx}}(x)+G_{\mathrm{amort}}(x). \end{aligned} \]

    This idealized split, studied directly by Cremer, Li, and Duvenaud, assumes the best local value and trained amortized value are available. A practical error ledger should therefore keep four entries:

    1. Model mismatch: \(p_\theta(x)\) does not represent the data law. This is outside the posterior inference gap.
    2. Approximation gap: the chosen variational family cannot represent the model posterior.
    3. Amortization gap: a shared inference map fails to return the best member of that family for a particular observation. Whether it can close this gap depends on model structure and the function's inputs, not only network width (Margossian and Blei, 2024).
    4. Estimation and optimization error: finite samples, noisy gradients, numerical error, or incomplete optimization miss the intended objective or its best amortized parameters.

    A more expressive posterior can reduce the approximation gap while increasing optimization difficulty. Per-example refinement can reduce amortization error at additional inference cost.

    Variational families and the geometry of reverse KL¤

    A mean-field approximation assumes

    \[ q(z)=\prod_{j=1}^{d}q_j(z_j). \]

    It is computationally attractive but cannot represent posterior dependence. Full-covariance Gaussians restore linear dependence at \(O(d^2)\) storage and often \(O(d^3)\) factorization cost. Structured mean-field retains selected graphical dependencies. Normalizing-flow posteriors transform a tractable base density through invertible maps, while hierarchical and auxiliary-variable approximations introduce additional latent layers.

    Standard variational inference minimizes \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)\) because that is the gap in the ELBO. Reverse KL heavily penalizes placing mass where the posterior has very low density, but it does not directly penalize missing a separated mode. In a bimodal posterior, a unimodal \(q\) may concentrate on one mode rather than average across the low-density valley. This is a property of the direction and the restricted family—not a theorem that every reverse-KL optimization always selects exactly one mode.

    Counterexample: a small ELBO gap is query-dependent evidence

    If two posterior approximations have similar ELBOs, they can still differ substantially on a rare-event probability or tail expectation. The ELBO controls a KL divergence, not every downstream functional uniformly. Validate the posterior against the queries you will actually use.

    From the functional optimum to coordinate-ascent VI¤

    Mean field is more than a covariance restriction: it creates a reusable coordinate update. Hold every factor except \(q_j\) fixed and write \(q_{-j}(z_{-j})=\prod_{k\ne j}q_k(z_k)\). The terms of the ELBO that depend on \(q_j\) are

    \[ \mathcal L(q_j) =\int q_j(z_j) \left( \mathbb E_{q_{-j}}[\log p(x,z)] -\log q_j(z_j) \right)dz_j +\text{constant}. \]

    Normalizing the exponential of the expected log joint gives the functional optimum

    \[ \boxed{ q_j^*(z_j) \propto \exp\!\left\{ \mathbb E_{q_{-j}}[\log p(x,z)] \right\}. } \]

    This is coordinate-ascent variational inference (CAVI). In a conditionally conjugate exponential-family model, the expectation often reduces to an update of natural parameters or sufficient statistics. The same local algebra can be organized as variational message passing. An exact coordinate maximization cannot decrease the ELBO, but that statement does not imply a unique fixed point, a global optimum, or an accurate posterior. Parallel stale-coordinate updates, damping, approximate expectations, and finite numerical solves define different algorithms and need their own convergence analysis. The classical CAVI and stochastic-VI constructions are developed in Hoffman et al., and the local message organization in Winn and Bishop.

    Worked finite example: a factorized optimum can break a real symmetry¤

    Let \(z_1,z_2\in\{-1,+1\}\) and

    \[ p(z_1,z_2) =\frac{1}{4\cosh J} \exp(Jz_1z_2), \qquad J=1.6. \]

    The exact law is unchanged when both signs flip. Consequently \(\mathbb E_p[Z_1]=\mathbb E_p[Z_2]=0\), while

    \[ \Pr_p(Z_1=Z_2) =\frac{e^J}{e^J+e^{-J}} \approx0.96083, \qquad \mathbb E_p[Z_1Z_2]=\tanh J\approx0.92167. \]

    Parameterize a product approximation by \(m_j=\mathbb E_q[Z_j]\). The coordinate update above becomes

    \[ m_1\leftarrow\tanh(Jm_2), \qquad m_2\leftarrow\tanh(Jm_1). \]

    Starting at \(m_1=m_2=0\) stays at the symmetric fixed point, which represents neither positive correlation nor either high-probability diagonal pair. Starting with a small common positive or negative perturbation converges to \(m_1=m_2\approx\pm0.89064\). Those asymmetric solutions have a higher ELBO than the zero solution, yet invent nonzero singleton means even though the exact marginals are balanced. They also approximate the correlation by \(m_1m_2\approx0.79325\), below the exact \(0.92167\).

    The optimizer did not create a numerical accident. Reverse KL plus an independence restriction prefers one coherent diagonal region over a product law that spreads mass onto the low-probability off-diagonal states. Different initializations expose different fixed points; exact coordinate updates do not remove family error.

    An algorithm ladder: bespoke, message based, black box, and automatic¤

    “Variational inference” names an optimization principle, not one implementation.

    Table INFER.6. An algorithm ladder: bespoke, message based, black box, and automatic
    Construction What the system needs What is reused or automated What remains the researcher's responsibility
    Analytic CAVI Expected log-joint terms for each factor Closed-form coordinate updates Factorization, derivation, schedule, initialization, and local optima
    Variational message passing A supported factor graph, often conjugate exponential-family structure Local natural-parameter messages Model-specific nonconjugate factors, approximation structure, and convergence
    Stochastic VI Unbiased minibatch estimates of global sufficient statistics or gradients Dataset scaling and stochastic optimization Global/local separation, subsampling law, local solves, and step schedule
    Black-box VI (BBVI) Samples and model/guide log densities, plus valid score gradients Model-independent Monte Carlo gradient assembly Guide family, variance reduction, support, optimization, and diagnostics
    Automatic-differentiation VI (ADVI) A differentiable log joint and valid constraint transforms Unconstraining, a default Gaussian guide, autodiff, and pathwise optimization Parameterization, guide adequacy, multimodality, discrete variables, and validation
    Amortized VI A task distribution and inference network Repeated local inference through one learned map Coverage of the task distribution and approximation/amortization gaps

    In global/local models, a minibatch estimate is unbiased only after the subsampling distribution and scaling match the full-data sum. Local latent parameters may still require an inner optimization for every selected record. Replacing that inner optimum by a fixed number of steps changes the estimator; calling the outer method SVI does not make the result unbiased automatically.

    ADVI transforms the target as well as the sample¤

    Suppose a model parameter \(\theta\) lives in a constrained space and a differentiable bijection \(T:\mathbb R^d\to\Theta\) maps an unconstrained \(\zeta\) to \(\theta=T(\zeta)\). The transformed joint density is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \log p_T(x,\zeta) &=\log p(x,T(\zeta))\\ &\quad+\log\left|\det J_T(\zeta)\right|. \end{aligned} \]

    ADVI chooses a tractable \(q_\lambda(\zeta)\)—commonly a diagonal or full-rank Gaussian—samples \(\zeta=\mu+L\epsilon\) with \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\), and optimizes

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L(\lambda) &=\mathbb E_{q_\lambda(\zeta)}\!\left[ \log p_T(x,\zeta)\right.\\ &\qquad\left.-\log q_\lambda(\zeta) \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    The Jacobian term is part of the transformed target, not an optional regularizer. Invertibility is required here because ADVI changes coordinates and evaluates the resulting density. A generic pathwise derivative only needs a differentiable parameter-to-sample construction under its regularity conditions; it does not require every sampling map to be invertible.

    Automatic differentiation removes hand-derived gradients, not posterior geometry. A diagonal Gaussian cannot encode dependence; a full-rank Gaussian uses quadratic covariance storage and a factorization whose dense cost can be cubic in dimension; neither represents separated modes. Centered versus noncentered model parameterizations can also make the same posterior much easier or harder to optimize. These boundaries are explicit in the original ADVI formulation; mixture extensions change the family and estimator rather than making Gaussian ADVI universally multimodal (Morningstar et al.).

    Randomness design is a separate axis from the guide family. Fresh iid draws, deterministic low-discrepancy points, independently randomized QMC scrambles, or a fixed sample-average objective can all estimate or optimize related variational quantities while carrying different uncertainty and convergence contracts. Sampling and Continuous-Time Mathematics derives those distinctions. In particular, deterministic ADVI optimizes a fixed finite Monte Carlo objective rather than changing the posterior family, and ICLR 2026 quasi-Monte Carlo latent-variable models use randomized numerical integration for specialized very low-dimensional latent marginals rather than introducing a general replacement for posterior inference.

    Richer posterior families change different constraints¤

    Table INFER.7. Richer posterior families change different constraints
    Family Dependence or geometry gained Additional requirement or failure mode
    Structured mean field Retains selected chains, trees, blocks, or conditional dependencies Structure must match the posterior query; inference inside a block may still be expensive
    Full/low-rank Gaussian Adds dense or selected linear dependence Still unimodal; covariance conditioning and parameterization matter
    Mixture Represents separated regions Component collapse, label symmetry, discrete assignment gradients, and expensive scoring
    Hierarchical or auxiliary guide Produces richer marginals through extra latent variables The marginal guide entropy may become intractable; an auxiliary bound can introduce another gap
    Normalizing-flow guide Warps a tractable base through an evaluable transformation Topology, direction, Jacobian cost, and numerical inversion constrain the family
    Implicit guide Allows a flexible sampler without a tractable density The ordinary ELBO contains \(-\log q\) and is not directly evaluable; ratio, score, kernel, or adversarial alternatives define different objectives
    Particle or MCMC-refined guide Represents multimodality or corrects a proposal asymptotically Weight degeneracy, mixing, differentiating resampling/acceptance, and finite compute remain

    No row dominates the others. A structured Gaussian can be better than a more expressive generic flow when the posterior is a long correlated trajectory; a mixture can preserve modes that a single continuous bijection cannot create from a connected base support. As one current example, radial-transport VI (Peer-reviewed research, AISTATS 2026) augments Gaussian VI by optimizing a radial profile and proves results under its stated radial and regularity conditions. It is evidence for a useful missing geometry, not a universal replacement for structured, multimodal, or simulation-based inference.

    Flow posterior, flow proposal, and transport are three interfaces¤

    Suppose a conditional bijection turns \(U\sim r(u\mid x)\) into \(Z=f_\phi(U;x)\) and makes \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) tractable. That fact does not yet say how \(Z\) will be used. The outer inference procedure determines the contract:

    Table INFER.8. Flow posterior, flow proposal, and transport are three interfaces
    Interface Returned object Computation after the flow What remains approximate
    Variational posterior Unweighted draws and expectations under \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) Optimize an ELBO or another declared divergence/objective \(q_\phi\) may differ from \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\) because of family, amortization, optimization, and model error
    Direct proposal Proposed states with evaluable \(q_\phi\) Apply importance/SMC weights or an MH acceptance ratio Finite-weight variance, finite-particle error, or chain mixing can remain severe; the correction addresses target mismatch only under its support and regularity conditions
    Transport preconditioner A target density expressed in base coordinates Run a valid invariant kernel on \(\gamma_U(u)=\gamma(f_\phi(u))\lvert\det J_f(u)\rvert\), then map states forward A poor map can leave difficult geometry and slow mixing, even though the invariant target is correct

    For an independent proposal, for example, the Metropolis–Hastings acceptance probability is

    \[ \alpha(z,z') =\min\!\left\{1, \frac{\gamma(z')q_\phi(z\mid x)} {\gamma(z)q_\phi(z'\mid x)} \right\}. \]

    The proposal parameters can be learned by VI, maximum likelihood on target samples, a variance-oriented objective, or another criterion. None of those training labels replaces the acceptance ratio. Likewise, a sample pushed through an approximate transport is a draw from \(q_\phi\) until a valid weight or transition changes the statistical statement.

    Direction requirements also differ. Reparameterized VI needs fast base-to-posterior sampling and the density of those generated samples, motivating constructions such as inverse autoregressive flow. A proposal used in an acceptance ratio may need densities at states supplied by the chain, so arbitrary-state scoring can matter. A transport-preconditioned chain needs the target and forward log-Jacobian in base coordinates, as in NeuTra HMC.

    Exact flow density is not exact posterior inference

    Invertibility proves that the induced \(q_\phi\) is normalized and evaluable under the stated assumptions. It does not prove that \(q_\phi=p_\theta(z\mid x)\), that importance weights have tolerable variance, or that a transformed chain mixes. The one-bijection, three-roles derivation includes an affine-flow proposal whose density and inverse are exact while its asymptotic importance ESS fraction is only about \(4.50\times10^{-7}\).

    Retrieval practice: variational objectives and inference error
    1. Which identity decomposes log evidence into an ELBO and a posterior KL gap?
    2. What changes when the variational family, optimizer, or Monte Carlo estimator changes?
    3. Why does a tighter computed bound not by itself prove a better generative model?
    4. How do model, approximation, optimization, and estimation error differ?

    Solutions. (1) \(\log p(x)=\mathcal L(q)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z\mid x)\|p(z\mid x))\). (2) They change approximation capacity, the attained solution, or stochastic error respectively; only the model change alters the target joint law. (3) The bound can improve inference for the same model or favor a different training tradeoff without improving the desired predictive/sample query. (4) Model error is target misspecification, approximation error is the best-family gap, optimization error is failure to reach that best member, and estimation error comes from finite/noisy computation or data.

    Chapter 3 chapter synthesis and bridge¤

    The ELBO is an exact identity whose optimization becomes approximate through the chosen family, coordinate or amortized parameterization, objective estimator, and optimizer. CAVI, VMP, SVI, BBVI, ADVI, and amortized VI automate different operations and inherit different support, geometry, stochasticity, and convergence boundaries. A normalized and tractable \(q\) is not automatically the model posterior, and better inference cannot repair model misspecification. The next chapter asks how derivatives of variational and expectation objectives are estimated when latent states are sampled, enumerated, transformed, or relaxed.
