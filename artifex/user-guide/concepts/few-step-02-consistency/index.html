    2. Consistency Models and Flow-Map Matching¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1's finite-transition distinction and basic distillation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Separate endpoint and two-time targets: distinguish endpoint invariance, arbitrary-time traversal, score access, and stochastic sampling.
    2. O2 — State anchoring conditions: combine boundary, data, and identity constraints so trivial constant solutions are excluded.
    3. O3 — Audit composition: compute direct-versus-composed residuals and classify what small sampled residuals do and do not establish.
    4. O4 — Bound propagated map error: use a Lipschitz recursion to explain why a locally accurate map can fail after repeated composition.
    5. O5 — Classify supervision: distinguish teacher distillation, self-distillation, and standalone/direct consistency training by target provenance rather than method name.
    6. O6 — Transfer the map contract: design identity, composition, inversion, off-grid, and endpoint tests for a new state space.

    Consistency Models learn a function \(f_\theta(x_t,t)\) that maps points on the same probability-flow trajectory to a common endpoint, with a boundary condition near data time. Ideally,

    \[ f_\theta(x_t,t)=f_\theta(x_s,s) \]

    whenever \(x_s=\Phi_{t\to s}(x_t)\). Consistency distillation obtains adjacent trajectory pairs from a pretrained diffusion/ODE teacher; consistency training constructs a teacher through self/EMA targets.

    Endpoint consistency permits one-step generation. Multistep procedures may alternate endpoint prediction and controlled noise injection, but they no longer simply evaluate the original deterministic flow map at more points.

    Consistency alone is underidentified. Every constant function satisfies \(f(x_t,t)=f(x_s,s)\), including one that maps all inputs to the same image. A usable construction therefore combines trajectory consistency with a boundary parameterization or loss such as \(f(x_\epsilon,\epsilon)\approx x_\epsilon\), and with training pairs whose endpoint distribution is anchored to data. The three statements answer different questions:

    Table FEW.7. Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives
    Constraint Prevents Does not prove by itself
    same-trajectory consistency time-dependent disagreement along sampled paths the common value is the correct endpoint
    identity/boundary anchoring arbitrary constant endpoints near data time global consistency away from the boundary
    data/teacher target an endpoint unrelated to the intended law correct intermediate map or inversion

    When a multistep consistency sampler predicts an endpoint, adds fresh noise, and predicts again, its law is a composition of a deterministic predictor and a corruption kernel. It can improve sample quality, but it is not “a more accurate numerical integration” of the same deterministic map unless that equivalence is separately derived.

    Two-time flow maps¤

    Flow Map Matching, TMLR 2025, learns

    \[ F_\theta(x_t,t,s)\approx\Phi_{t\to s}(x_t) \]

    for arbitrary pairs \((t,s)\). This retains the endpoint model as \(s=0\) while supporting an a-posteriori step-count choice. Its mathematical framework relates consistency models, consistency trajectory models, progressive distillation, and neural-operator views.

    Useful constraints include:

    \[ F(x,t,t)=x, \]
    \[ F(F(x,t,s),s,r)=F(x,t,r), \]

    and, for invertible ODE maps,

    \[ F(F(x,t,s),s,t)=x. \]

    Matching one constraint on sampled states does not guarantee the others off distribution.

    The identity and composition laws can be built into a parameterization only in special cases. Usually they are learned or regularized on sampled states and times. Report the sampling distribution over \((x,s,t,r)\): excellent residuals on nearby teacher states do not establish long-jump composition from generated off-trajectory states.

    Consistency Trajectory Models learn arbitrary traversal along a probability-flow ODE and expose a score-like quantity; Flow Map Matching provides a broader stochastic-interpolant framework for two-time maps. These are useful connections, not synonyms. Compare their parameterized object, training identity, path construction, and sampling program before transferring a theorem.

    Teacher, self-distillation, and direct training¤

    • Teacher distillation: accurate trajectory pairs but costly preprocessing and inherited teacher bias.
    • Self-distillation: avoids a fixed teacher but creates moving-target stability problems.
    • Direct map objectives: avoid rollouts where identities permit, but may require Jacobian-vector products, inverse networks, or nested model calls.

    “Direct” and “teacher-free” are not interchangeable. A loss can avoid explicit teacher trajectories while using a pretrained score, or can bootstrap from an EMA copy whose targets depend on earlier model states. Conversely, standalone consistency training may use analytically corrupted data and an estimated score without a pretrained generative teacher. Record every target-producing network and whether gradients pass through it.

    The deployed map is deterministic unless it receives new noise. It can still generate a multimodal marginal because the initial noise varies, but a coarse conditional transition from a fixed intermediate state cannot branch without extra randomness.

    Worked composition residual¤

    Define

    \[ R=\|F(F(x,t,s),s,r)-F(x,t,r)\|. \]

    A model can have small endpoint error for \(r=0\) yet large \(R\) at intermediate times. It may be an effective one-step generator but an unreliable editable trajectory model. Evaluation must match the claimed query.

    Worked error propagation bound¤

    Let exact maps satisfy \(\Phi_{k+1}\circ\Phi_k\), and learned maps satisfy the uniform one-step bounds

    \[ \|F_k(x)-\Phi_k(x)\|\leq\varepsilon_k. \]

    If \(\Phi_{k+1}\) is \(L_{k+1}\)-Lipschitz, then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \|F_{k+1}(F_k(x))-\Phi_{k+1}(\Phi_k(x))\| &\leq \varepsilon_{k+1} +L_{k+1}\varepsilon_k. \end{aligned} \]

    For \(m\) maps with a common bound \(\varepsilon\) and \(L=1\), the worst-case bound is \(m\varepsilon\); for \(L>1\) it is \(\varepsilon( L^m-1)/(L-1)\). This is an upper bound, not a prediction that errors always align. It explains why direct long-jump and repeated-short-jump tests are complementary.

    Worked affine composition residual¤

    Suppose a true half-step is \(\Phi(x)=2x+1\), so the true full-step map is \(\Phi(\Phi(x))=4x+3\). Let a learned half-step be \(F_h(x)=2x+1.1\) and a learned full-step be \(F_{2h}(x)=4x+2.9\). At \(x=1\),

    \[ F_h(F_h(1))=7.3, \qquad F_{2h}(1)=6.9, \qquad R=0.4. \]

    Their errors relative to the exact full-step value seven are \(0.3\) and \(0.1\). The direct map is more endpoint-accurate here even though the two learned maps are inconsistent with each other. A composition residual diagnoses algebraic coherence; it is not an endpoint-quality metric.

    Retrieval practice: consistency and flow maps
    1. What algebraic identity should exact flow maps satisfy across an intermediate time \(u\)?
    2. Which extra condition anchors a consistency family to the desired endpoint rather than a trivial solution?
    3. How do teacher distillation and direct consistency training differ in their source of targets?
    4. What does a small sampled composition residual fail to prove?

    Solutions. (1) \(F_{s,t}=F_{u,t}\circ F_{s,u}\) with the chapter's time convention. (2) A boundary/data condition such as identity at equal times and correct mapping to clean data. (3) Teacher distillation uses trajectories or predictions from a pretrained process; direct training constructs consistency targets from data, corruption, and coupled model evaluations. (4) Global correctness, unsampled-time consistency, distributional fidelity, stability, or low-error one-step generation outside the tested states.

    Misconception checks: consistency and maps¤

    • “Consistency selects the correct solution.” Constant maps are consistent; boundary and data anchoring select the intended endpoint.
    • “Endpoint consistency gives arbitrary-time editing.” Editing and inversion need a two-time or bidirectional contract beyond \(s=0\).
    • “More consistency steps trace the same ODE.” Noise reinjection changes the sampling kernel.
    • “Zero composition residual proves correct dynamics.” A wrong semigroup can be internally consistent.

    Independent practice and assessment map: consistency and flow maps¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: the trivial solution. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. Decide whether \(f(x,t)=c\) satisfies same-trajectory consistency, then name the smallest extra condition that falsifies it for non-degenerate data.
    2. P1.2 — Audit affine composition. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Reproduce the \(F_h,F_{2h}\) residual at \(x=1\) and compare both routes with the exact value seven.
    3. P1.3 — Derive a two-step error bound. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Starting from one-step errors \(\varepsilon_1,\varepsilon_2\) and an \(L_2\)-Lipschitz second exact map, derive the displayed bound by adding and subtracting one term.
    4. P1.4 — Classify target provenance. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For four losses using (a) cached teacher pairs, (b) a stop-gradient EMA target, © analytically corrupted data plus a pretrained score, and (d) analytically corrupted data with no pretrained model, classify fixed-teacher use, bootstrap dependence, and data access.
    5. P1.5 — Design an off-grid audit. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. Specify state/time splits that distinguish interpolation from extrapolation and direct-map accuracy from composition consistency.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to a manifold. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Define identity, composition, and boundary/data checks for a learned two-time map on the unit circle and include one geometry-preservation diagnostic.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. A constant map gives \(c=c\) for every pair on a trajectory, so consistency alone accepts it. An identity boundary \(f(x,0)=x\) or an equivalent non-degenerate data anchor rejects one constant for two distinct endpoints. Check. An anchor at only one data point would still be insufficient.
    2. P1.2. \(F_h(1)=3.1\), \(F_h(3.1)=7.3\), and \(F_{2h}(1)=6.9\), so the composition residual is \(0.4\). The exact full step is \(4(1)+3=7\), making direct and composed endpoint errors \(0.1\) and \(0.3\). Check. Lower residual would not necessarily mean lower exact-map error.
    3. P1.3. Add and subtract \(\Phi_2(F_1(x))\). The triangle inequality gives \(\|F_2(F_1)-\Phi_2(F_1)\|+\|\Phi_2(F_1)-\Phi_2(\Phi_1)\|\leq\varepsilon_2+L_2\varepsilon_1\). Check. Lipschitzness is required only for the propagated term in this bound.
    4. P1.4. (a) uses a fixed teacher and cached pairs; (b) is bootstrapped through an EMA target; © uses a fixed pretrained score and real data even without cached trajectories; (d) is the only listed route with neither fixed pretrained model nor bootstrap, though it still uses data and analytic corruption. Rubric. “Teacher-free” must state whether auxiliary pretrained scores count.
    5. P1.5. Hold out individual times, interval lengths, and complete time regions. At each, evaluate teacher/on-trajectory and model-generated/off-trajectory states. Score the direct jump against an exact or high-accuracy reference and separately score direct-versus-composed consistency. Check. Randomly holding out examples at already-trained time pairs tests neither time extrapolation nor state shift.
    6. P1.6. Represent \(x=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\) and learn angular or tangent updates, or project with the projection included in the declared algorithm. Verify \(F(x,t,t)=x\), geodesic direct-versus-composed residuals, and the pushed-forward endpoint law. Check \(|\|F(x)\|_2-1|\) before any hidden projection. Check. Circle membership does not establish the correct angular distribution.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Consistency models learn endpoint invariance; flow maps learn two-time transitions and semigroup structure. Both replace repeated local integration with a harder global approximation problem. Chapter 3 derives average-velocity and shortcut parameterizations for direct long jumps.
