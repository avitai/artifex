    10. Annealing and Tempering¤

    Tempering and continuous-transport learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes importance sampling, Markov kernels, ODEs, and SDEs. By the end, you should be able to construct annealed/tempered paths and their weight updates, distinguish SMC rejuvenation from independent importance sampling, derive continuity and Fokker–Planck evolution, compare deterministic and stochastic transports, allocate numerical-solver and NFE error, and state how a Schrödinger bridge depends on its reference process and endpoint constraints.

    This foundation introduces temperature paths as sampling constructions. The complete estimator treatment—including AIS direction, SMC backward kernels, normalizer-ratio uncertainty, bridge/path alternatives, and reporting—lives in Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants.

    Three similarly named procedures return different objects:

    • Simulated annealing changes temperature while searching for a low-energy state. The nonhomogeneous path is an optimization algorithm; pooling its visited states does not produce samples from the final target.
    • Parallel tempering targets a joint product of distributions at several temperatures, alternates invariant within-temperature transitions with corrected swaps, and retains the cold chain for target expectations.
    • Annealed importance sampling propagates weighted paths through intermediate targets to estimate a normalizer ratio and weighted endpoint expectations.

    Intermediate distributions

    \[ \pi_t(x)\propto p_0(x)^{1-\beta_t}\tilde p(x)^{\beta_t} \]

    connect a tractable base to a target. Annealed importance sampling accumulates weight ratios and estimates normalizing constants; parallel tempering swaps states across temperatures to cross barriers. Schedule spacing matters when adjacent distributions have poor overlap.

    For power-tempered replicas

    \[ \pi_{\beta_i}(x)\propto\widetilde\pi(x)^{\beta_i}, \qquad 0<\beta_0<\cdots<\beta_L=1, \]

    a proposed swap of states \(x_i\) and \(x_j\) has log acceptance ratio and acceptance probability

    \[ \begin{aligned} \log R_{\mathrm{swap}} &=(\beta_i-\beta_j) [\log\widetilde\pi(x_j)-\log\widetilde\pi(x_i)],\\ \alpha_{\mathrm{swap}} &=\min\{1,\exp(\log R_{\mathrm{swap}})\}. \end{aligned} \]

    Within-replica moves and swap moves together preserve the product target. A high swap rate between one adjacent pair does not prove end-to-end movement; report replica round trips, occupancy across modes, and cost across all temperatures. Implement the decision on the log scale to avoid needless overflow or underflow.

    AIS uses transitions that leave each intermediate distribution invariant, but those transitions need not equilibrate fully for the standard importance-weight identity. Poor mixing still causes high-variance weights. Parallel tempering instead maintains chains at multiple temperatures and accepts swaps using an MH ratio; it seeks better exploration rather than directly producing the same normalizing-constant estimator.

    SMC turns one hard correction into a sequence¤

    Sequential Monte Carlo maintains weighted particles across targets \(\pi_t(x)=\gamma_t(x)/Z_t\). In the simplest reweighting step with unchanged particle states, the incremental factor is \(\gamma_t(x)/\gamma_{t-1}(x)\). General SMC samplers also require forward mutation kernels and, in their weight formula, a compatible backward-kernel construction. Propagation moves particles, weighting corrects the proposal, and resampling reallocates finite computation.

    Under standard proper-weighting conditions, multiplying average incremental weights gives an estimator of \(Z_T/Z_0\) that can be unbiased even though a normalized finite-particle posterior expectation is generally biased. Unbiasedness does not imply low variance: if adjacent targets barely overlap, all useful mass may concentrate in one lineage. Del Moral, Doucet, and Jasra (2006) give the general SMC-sampler construction.

    The distinction is developed in two complementary foundations. Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants owns evidence and ratio uncertainty. Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods owns target sequences, resampling offspring laws, particle and genealogical degeneracy, proposals, smoothing, PMCMC, static targets, learning, and generator composition. This sampling volume supplies the invariant mutation kernels and Monte Carlo error concepts reused by both.

    The same logic applies to controlled generation. A path of intermediate targets can introduce a likelihood, constraint, or reward gradually, while an amortized proposal attempts to reduce weight variance. The proposal does not supply correctness by itself; the target sequence and importance weights define what is being approximated. Ou, Pani, and Li (2026) instantiate this pattern for inference-time control of discrete diffusion models.

    Research directions that preserve the contract¤

    Modern learned samplers can serve as coordinates, proposals, or bridge maps without changing the invariant-target logic:

    • Preprint — 2019: NeuTra HMC runs HMC in coordinates defined by a learned invertible transport. The Jacobian-adjusted target and HMC correction preserve the intended target; transport quality controls efficiency.
    • Peer-reviewed research — AISTATS 2023: transport reversible-jump proposals learn reference-space maps for dimension-changing moves. The RJMCMC ratio, not the learned map alone, supplies invariance.
    • Peer-reviewed research — ICLR 2026: neural-transport parallel tempering combines flows or diffusion transports with a tempered system and reports improved sampling and normalizer estimation on its evaluated multimodal targets. The result is protocol-specific; it does not remove overlap or training costs in arbitrary dimensions.
    • Peer-reviewed research — JMLR 2026: WALNUTS adapts leapfrog step size within a NUTS orbit using a reversible construction and reports gains on multiscale targets. This is a new HMC kernel with a specific correctness argument, not permission to change step size arbitrarily inside any trajectory.
    • Peer-reviewed research — COLT 2024: diffusion-based Monte Carlo studies reverse- diffusion sampling from an energy target using nonparametric score estimation. Its complexity guarantees hold under the paper's assumptions; sharing reverse-SDE language with trained diffusion models does not make the two procedures identical.

    Discrete-space research similarly uses locally balanced, discrete-Langevin, cluster, and learned block proposals. Each must declare whether it is Metropolis adjusted, asymptotically exact under a limit, or deliberately biased. The architecture of the proposer never replaces that statement.

    11. Deterministic Transport and the Continuity Equation¤

    The next two sections describe two ways a whole probability law can move through continuous time. Deterministic transport moves particles along a vector field; stochastic transport adds diffusion. Their density equations reveal the difference.

    The shared endpoint label does not assert identical trajectories or finite-step samplers. It marks the design goal; equality requires the declared continuous- time assumptions and exact fields.

    For ODE

    \[ \frac{dX_t}{dt}=u_t(X_t), \]

    the density evolves by

    \[ \partial_t p_t(x)+\nabla\cdot(p_t(x)u_t(x))=0. \]

    Along a trajectory,

    \[ \frac{d}{dt}\log p_t(X_t)=-\nabla\cdot u_t(X_t), \]

    which gives CNF likelihoods after numerical integration. A regular ODE flow is a diffeomorphism: it preserves topology and cannot create disconnected support from a connected base without augmentation or changing assumptions.

    The divergence trace can be exact for structured fields or estimated stochastically with Hutchinson probes:

    \[ \nabla\cdot u(x)=\operatorname{tr}J_u(x) =\mathbb E_\epsilon[\epsilon^TJ_u(x)\epsilon], \]

    for zero-mean probes with identity covariance. This estimator is unbiased for the trace, but numerical integration and finite probes add variance and bias to a computed log likelihood.

    The continuous-flow chapter develops how these identities become learned CNF and flow-matching objectives. The probability-path chapter separates a path of marginals, a coupling, a velocity field, and a particular finite solver.

    12. Stochastic Transport and Fokker–Planck¤

    For Itô SDE

    \[ dX_t=f(X_t,t)dt+g(t)dW_t, \]

    the density obeys

    \[ \partial_t p_t =-\nabla\cdot(fp_t)+\frac12g(t)^2\Delta p_t. \]

    Under regularity assumptions, the reverse-time SDE depends on the marginal score \(\nabla_x\log p_t(x)\), while a probability-flow ODE can share the same one-time marginals with deterministic trajectories. Shared marginals do not mean identical paths, transition kernels, or discretization error.

    For state-independent scalar diffusion \(g(t)\), the reverse-time drift written in decreasing forward time contains the score correction \(-g(t)^2\nabla_x\log p_t(x)\). Sign conventions change when authors reparameterize time from noise to data. Always state time direction before copying a reverse-SDE formula.

    The probability-flow ODE replaces the stochastic diffusion contribution with half the score correction. Its deterministic map can support likelihood computation, while the reverse SDE samples stochastic paths. Exact equality of marginals is a continuous-time theorem; approximate scores and different solvers break exact equality at finite compute.

    For denoising objectives, parameterization conversions, schedules, reverse updates, and solver families, continue to the diffusion mathematical foundation and diffusion sampling chapter. For jumps and rate matrices rather than Brownian diffusion, continue to continuous-time discrete generation.

    13. Numerical Solvers and NFE¤

    Euler, Heun, Runge–Kutta, multistep, exponential, and stochastic solvers trade accuracy, stability, memory, and evaluations. Report:

    • solver and order;
    • fixed grid or adaptive tolerances;
    • accepted and rejected steps;
    • neural-network evaluations, including conditional/unconditional branches;
    • decoder or post-processing cost;
    • wall-clock hardware, precision, batch, and resolution.

    NFE is a sampler statistic, not an architecture-independent speed metric.

    Error Budget for a Learned Continuous-Time Sampler¤

    Separate at least four errors:

    1. model/field error: the learned score or velocity differs from its population target;
    2. path error: the chosen interpolation or forward process is poorly conditioned for the task;
    3. discretization error: finite solver steps approximate the learned dynamics;
    4. numerical/implementation error: precision, tolerances, clipping, batching, or compiler behavior changes the computation.

    Reducing solver tolerance cannot repair a wrong field. Distilling to one step changes the learned object and adds approximation error; it is not merely a lower-order numerical solve.

    Transitions and Few-Step Generation develops the distinction between numerically integrating a field and learning a finite transition directly. Inference accounting defines the compute ledger needed to compare those systems fairly.

    14. Schrödinger Bridges¤

    Given a reference path measure \(R\) and endpoint marginals \(p_0,p_1\), the Schrödinger bridge solves

    \[ P^*=\arg\min_{P:\,P_0=p_0,\,P_1=p_1}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|R). \]

    It is entropy-regularized optimal transport on path space. Iterative proportional fitting alternately adjusts endpoint constraints; diffusion Schrödinger bridge methods learn forward and backward dynamics. The first iteration can recover score-based constructions under particular references, but a full bridge is not synonymous with ordinary diffusion training.

    As reference noise tends toward zero under suitable scaling, Schrödinger bridges connect to deterministic optimal transport. At nonzero noise they solve a different, entropy-regularized path-space problem. Endpoint constraints, reference process, and regularization strength are part of the model—not implementation details.

    The broader construction—including stochastic interpolants, endpoint conditioning, paired translation, and its exact relationship to optimal transport—is developed in Stochastic Interpolants and Schrödinger Bridges.

    Tempering and transport retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: annealing, transport, and bridges
    1. Why can many intermediate distributions reduce importance-weight collapse?
    2. What role does an invariant MCMC move play inside SMC?
    3. Which PDE distinguishes deterministic density transport from diffusive evolution?
    4. Why is NFE an incomplete cost measure for adaptive ODE/SDE sampling?

    Solutions. (1) Each correction spans a smaller density-ratio change, with resampling/rejuvenation preventing a few ancestral weights from dominating. (2) It moves particles within the current intermediate target without changing that target, restoring diversity after weighting/resampling. (3) Deterministic flow obeys the continuity equation; diffusion adds the second-order Fokker–Planck term. (4) Evaluations may differ in network size, guidance calls, rejected/adaptive steps, precision, batching, and auxiliary score/divergence computation.

    Tempering and transport synthesis¤

    Annealing and SMC distribute a difficult correction across intermediate targets; transport methods instead learn or solve dynamics for moving probability. Continuity, Fokker–Planck, numerical solver, and bridge-reference contracts keep continuous-time identities separate from finite algorithms and deployed cost.
