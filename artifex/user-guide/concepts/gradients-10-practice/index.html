    10. Search Distributions, Method Selection, and Research Practice¤

    Chapter 10 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Evolution strategies, generative evaluation, and controlled comparisons.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive a search-law natural gradient: identify the score, Fisher, reward centering, and simplex or covariance motion.
    2. O2 — Keep search and data laws separate: state which distribution is geometrically preconditioned and what candidate is ultimately returned.
    3. O3 — Select a method systematically: answer the objective, variable, expectation, derivative, estimator, geometry, update, and validation questions in order.
    4. O4 — Design controlled comparisons: match data, architecture, budget, precision, parameter groups, and hyperparameter search.
    5. O5 — Interpret evidence maturity: distinguish established identities, method-specific peer-reviewed results, and open research claims.
    6. O6 — Transfer the complete contract: design and audit a gradient method for a new generative family, modality, or black-box design problem.

    Natural evolution strategies move a search law¤

    Let \(q_\psi(a)\) be a distribution over candidate model parameters, architectures, prompts, molecules, or other design objects, and let \(R(a)\) be a black-box reward. The search objective is

    \[ J(\psi)=\mathbb E_{A\sim q_\psi}[R(A)]. \]

    Its Fisher and natural gradient are

    \[ F_q(\psi) =\mathbb E_{q_\psi} [\nabla_\psi\log q_\psi(A)\nabla_\psi\log q_\psi(A)^\top], \qquad \widetilde\nabla_\psi J =F_q(\psi)^\dagger \mathbb E_{q_\psi}[R(A)\nabla_\psi\log q_\psi(A)]. \]

    This is the central construction in Natural Evolution Strategies. The Fisher belongs to the search distribution. It says nothing directly about the Fisher geometry of the data distribution produced by a selected candidate.

    Worked categorical search distribution¤

    Suppose three candidate generator designs have search probabilities

    \[ q=(0.5,0.3,0.2) \]

    and exact rewards \(R=(1,4,0)\). The mean reward is \(\bar R=1.7\). The categorical Fisher--Rao natural flow in probability coordinates has replicator form

    \[ \dot q_i=q_i(R_i-\bar R), \]

    giving

    \[ \dot q=(-0.35,0.69,-0.34). \]

    The components sum to zero, preserving total mass infinitesimally. A step of \(0.1\) gives \(q^+=(0.465,0.369,0.166)\), which remains in the simplex. With noisy rewards, rank shaping, baselines, entropy control, and covariance adaptation define different finite algorithms.

    Selection procedure¤

    Before adopting any gradient method, answer in order:

    1. Objective: which scalar functional, game field, conditional score, or outer query is differentiated?
    2. Variable: parameters, latent/state coordinates, a function, a particle map, a density, or a search distribution?
    3. Expectation: which data, model, posterior, path, or search law defines the population derivative?
    4. Exact derivative: what regularity, support, inner-solution, or boundary assumptions make it valid?
    5. Estimator: reverse AD, pathwise, score-function, relaxation, unrolling, implicit solve, particle average, or finite difference?
    6. Geometry: Euclidean, Fisher--Rao, GGN, manifold, Wasserstein, Sinkhorn, RKHS, or another declared metric?
    7. Finite update: what preconditioner, game schedule, damping, clipping, trust radius, timestep weighting, and numerical solver changes the ideal vector?
    8. Validation: is estimator bias/variance measured, does predicted geometry match actual movement, and does held-out generative behavior improve at matched compute?

    If the objective or differentiation variable cannot be answered, the word “gradient” is an analogy rather than an executable method specification.

    Controlled comparison protocol¤

    Compare against strong first-order and structured baselines using matched data, architecture, initialization distribution, global batch, precision, parameter groups, training examples or tokens, and hyperparameter-search budget. Report:

    • objective and held-out likelihood or family-appropriate quality metrics;
    • gradient, preconditioned direction, and actual update norms;
    • predicted and measured KL or declared divergence movement;
    • curvature samples, solve residual, damping fraction, and factor staleness;
    • wall-clock, peak memory, communication, and energy;
    • instability, skipped-step, and restart counts; and
    • uncertainty across seeds.

    A faster fall in training objective does not establish better density estimation, sampling, calibration, diversity, or compute efficiency.

    Evidence map and open questions¤

    Established. Reverse/forward differentiation, score-function and pathwise identities, Fisher--Rao local geometry, exponential-family duality, conjugate SVI natural updates, state scores, Langevin/Fokker--Planck correspondence, and exact infinitesimal invariance have mature foundations under their stated conditions.

    Peer-reviewed research. Implicit/pathwise estimators broaden variational families; SVGD, neural JKO schemes, and scalable Wasserstein flows provide functional or distributional constructions; K-FAC and FANG provide structured parameter-metric approximations; unrolling and extragradient-style methods alter game derivatives and dynamics; Sinkhorn natural gradient supplies a transport-based implicit-generator construction; the ELBO natural-gradient and time-varying-generator studies sharpen specific geometric connections; and the gradient-regularized natural-gradient study evaluates its variants on declared tasks.

    None of these results yields a family-independent ranking over gradient estimators, AdamW, Shampoo, K-FAC, natural-gradient VI, functional flows, transport pullbacks, guidance rules, and game optimizers. Important research questions include:

    • scalable model-Fisher estimation for large sequence generators without silently switching to the empirical Fisher;
    • geometries for singular implicit laws that remain stable in high dimension;
    • joint metrics and convergent finite algorithms for coupled adversarial games;
    • metrics that respect diffusion or flow path semantics and timestep weighting;
    • natural-gradient methods for structured discrete and manifold-valued laws;
    • low-variance unbiased gradients for discrete, branching, and long stochastic generative computations;
    • stable hypergradients through learned samplers and approximate inner inference without hiding truncation bias;
    • interaction among damping, normalization layers, parameter symmetries, and distributed optimizer state; and
    • evaluation that connects predicted local law movement to downstream sample quality and uncertainty.

    Independent practice and assessment map: selection and research evidence¤

    The final assessment asks for complete executable specifications and evidence boundaries. Method names receive no credit unless their target objects and finite approximations are recoverable.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: derive categorical NES motion. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A search distribution over three candidates has \(p=(0.5,0.3,0.2)\) and deterministic rewards \(R=(1,2,0)\). Predict which candidate gains mass, then compute expected reward and the natural-gradient ascent motion of probabilities. Verify mass conservation.
    2. P1.2 — Separate search and generated-data laws. Type: explain · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. An NES searches over diffusion hyperparameters and returns one generator. Identify the law whose Fisher NES uses, the law used to evaluate image quality, and why improving one objective does not establish Fisher-natural training of the returned generator.
    3. P1.3 — Complete a method-selection card. Type: design · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 15 min. For natural-gradient fine-tuning of an autoregressive language model, fill all eight selection fields: objective, variable, expectation, exact derivative, estimator, geometry, finite update, and validation. Include the context-law choice.
    4. P1.4 — Design a controlled comparison. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 17 min. Compare AdamW, diagonal empirical-Fisher scaling, and matrix-free model-Fisher natural gradient. Specify matched resources, method-specific tuning, diagnostics, uncertainty, and stopping rules.
    5. P1.5 — Classify evidence maturity. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. Classify each statement as an established identity, method-specific peer-reviewed result, or unsupported generalization: (a) regular-model KL has local Fisher Hessian; (b) K-FAC uses Kronecker-factored blocks; © K-FAC always improves diffusion sample quality; (d) a particular Sinkhorn pullback was evaluated for implicit generators; (e) every pseudo-loss field integrates to a divergence.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer the complete contract. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 22 min. Design a gradient method for a black-box protein generator with discrete sequences, a learned structural simulator, and a non-differentiable wet-lab reward. Choose training or search laws, estimator, geometry, constraints, finite algorithm, and a staged evidence plan from exact fixtures to prospective evaluation.

    Table GRAD.34. Outcome-to-task alignment: selection and research evidence

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. \(\bar R=0.5(1)+0.3(2)+0.2(0)=1.1\). Natural ascent on the categorical law gives \(\dot p=p\odot(R-\bar R)=(-0.05,0.27,-0.22)\). The entries sum to zero, so total probability is conserved; the highest-reward candidate gains mass. Check. A finite step still needs positivity-preserving coordinates or a mirror/exponential update.
    2. P1.2. NES uses the Fisher of the search law \(q_\psi(a)\) over hyperparameters or candidate parameter vectors. Image quality is evaluated under the generated-data law \(p_a(x)\) for sampled or selected \(a\). Optimizing \(\mathbb E_{q_\psi}[R(a)]\) naturally in search space can select a better candidate, but it does not precondition that candidate's internal training gradient by the Fisher of \(p_a(x)\). Check. The two laws can have different sample spaces and dimensions.
    3. P1.3. One complete card is: negative token log likelihood; model parameters \(\theta\); either model-generated histories for the exact joint Fisher or explicitly data histories for teacher-forced conditional curvature; exact reverse-AD likelihood derivative under fixed support; minibatch gradient; matrix-free model-Fisher score/GGN product under the chosen law; damped CG plus trust scaling and accepted finite update; JVP/VJP tests, residual, predicted/measured KL, held-out perplexity and generation metrics, compute, memory, and seeds. Rubric. Calling data-history curvature the exact joint-model Fisher loses credit.
    4. P1.4. Use identical data order, architecture, initialization distribution, global batch, precision, parameter groups, and evaluation cadence. Report both matched tokens/examples and matched wall-clock because matrix-free solves add cost. Give each method an equal, documented tuning budget over its learning rates and method-specific damping/rank/solve parameters. Track objective, held-out likelihood, generation quality/diversity, update norms, predicted/measured KL, residuals, damping fraction, time, memory, failures, and confidence intervals over seeds. Predeclare budget and instability stopping rules. Rubric. Equal hyperparameters are not required; equal opportunity and resources are.
    5. P1.5. (a) is an established local identity under regular dominated-family conditions. (b) is the defining structured approximation in a peer-reviewed method, with scope limited to its construction. © is an unsupported universal empirical generalization. (d) is a method-specific peer-reviewed construction and evaluation. (e) is false in general; a pseudo-loss can install a nonconservative field. Check. Evidence maturity follows the breadth of the claim, not the prestige of a method name.
    6. P1.6. One defensible plan treats sequence proposals as a categorical autoregressive policy or a higher-level search law, uses likelihood-ratio reward gradients/NES with centered baselines for wet-lab reward, and uses differentiable structural-simulator gradients only for an explicitly separate surrogate objective. Geometry can be Fisher--Rao on the sequence/search law; constraints use masked actions or a declared constrained mirror update. The finite loop specifies proposal temperature, off-policy reuse, simulator version, acquisition rule, assay budget, and safety filters. Validate gradients by exact enumeration on tiny alphabets, estimator bias/SNR in a calibrated simulator, structural predictions on held-out assays, retrospective reward optimization, then prospective blinded wet-lab batches with uncertainty and failure reporting. Rubric. Simulator gradients are not presented as derivatives of the wet-lab outcome without a validated causal/measurement bridge.

    Volume synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    There is no single “generative-model gradient.” Parameter derivatives train objectives; Monte Carlo rules estimate them; state scores and forces move samples; game fields coordinate competing players; hypergradients propagate through inner algorithms; functional gradients move maps or measures; and natural gradients transform parameter derivatives under a declared statistical metric. A complete method names the target, variable, law, geometry, estimator, finite algorithm, and validation protocol. Connections among these gradients are useful only when the conversion preserves those typed fields.

    Retrieval questions
    1. In NES, which distribution supplies the Fisher?
    2. What is the first question to ask about a proposed natural-gradient GAN optimizer?
    3. Which measurements distinguish a good linear solve from a good geometric model?
    4. Why is there no single “natural gradient for a VAE” without qualifiers?
    5. Why is a diffusion score not the parameter gradient used to train its score network?

    Solutions. (1) The search distribution over candidates. (2) Which generator-side law or divergence defines the metric. (3) Solve residual checks numerical accuracy; predicted-versus-measured law divergence and held-out generative metrics check geometric and task relevance. (4) A VAE contains distinct generative-joint, observed-marginal, and variational- conditional families with different Fishers. (5) The score differentiates a log density with respect to state; training differentiates a score-matching loss with respect to network parameters.
