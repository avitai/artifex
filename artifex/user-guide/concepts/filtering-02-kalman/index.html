    2. Linear-Gaussian Filtering¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Multivariate Gaussians, covariance matrices, affine maps, and Chapter 1.

    Learning objectives. Derive Kalman prediction and correction from Gaussian conditioning; state the exactness boundary; compute predictive likelihoods and a complete scalar example; and distinguish algebraically equivalent covariance, Joseph, square-root, and information implementations from numerically identical finite programs.

    The closure assumptions¤

    Consider the time-varying controlled model

    \[ \begin{aligned} z_t&=A_tz_{t-1}+B_tu_t+a_t+q_t, &q_t&\sim\mathcal N(0,Q_t),\\ y_t&=C_tz_t+d_t+r_t, &r_t&\sim\mathcal N(0,R_t), \end{aligned} \]

    with a Gaussian initial state and mutually independent Gaussian noise terms. Linear prediction maps a Gaussian to a Gaussian. Multiplication by a linear Gaussian likelihood produces another Gaussian. This closure—not the words state-space model—makes the filter exact.

    With non-Gaussian initial or noise laws, the same matrix recursion can still be the best linear mean-square estimator under suitable moment assumptions, but it does not generally return the complete Bayesian posterior. With nonlinear maps, even Gaussian noise does not preserve Gaussian closure.

    Prediction¤

    Write the filtered belief at time \(t-1\) as \(\mathcal N(m_{t-1\mid t-1},P_{t-1\mid t-1})\). Affine Gaussian propagation gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} m_{t\mid t-1} &=A_tm_{t-1\mid t-1}+B_tu_t+a_t,\\ P_{t\mid t-1} &=A_tP_{t-1\mid t-1}A_t^\top+Q_t. \end{aligned} \]

    The predicted observation and innovation covariance are

    \[ \widehat y_{t\mid t-1}=C_tm_{t\mid t-1}+d_t, \qquad S_t=C_tP_{t\mid t-1}C_t^\top+R_t. \]

    The residual \(e_t=y_t-\widehat y_{t\mid t-1}\) is called the innovation only relative to this prediction. Its temporal whiteness and covariance \(S_t\) are model-dependent diagnostic claims, not properties of every residual sequence.

    Correction from one joint Gaussian¤

    Before observing \(y_t\), the joint predictive law is

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}z_t\\y_t\end{bmatrix} \Bigm|y_{1:t-1} \sim \mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}m_{t\mid t-1}\\\widehat y_{t\mid t-1}\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} P_{t\mid t-1}&P_{t\mid t-1}C_t^\top\\ C_tP_{t\mid t-1}&S_t \end{bmatrix} \right). \]

    Conditioning this Gaussian gives the Kalman gain and posterior mean:

    \[ K_t=P_{t\mid t-1}C_t^\top S_t^{-1}, \qquad m_{t\mid t}=m_{t\mid t-1}+K_te_t. \]

    The compact covariance update is \(P_{t\mid t}=P_{t\mid t-1}-K_tS_tK_t^\top\). In exact arithmetic it equals \((I-K_tC_t)P_{t\mid t-1}\). A form that retains both noise contributions is

    \[ P_{t\mid t} =(I-K_tC_t)P_{t\mid t-1}(I-K_tC_t)^\top +K_tR_tK_t^\top. \]

    This Joseph form is algebraically equivalent when \(K_t\) is the exact gain, but is more resistant to loss of symmetry and positive semidefiniteness in finite arithmetic. Production code solves systems with \(S_t\) rather than forming \(S_t^{-1}\).

    The incremental log likelihood is

    \[ \log \ell_t =-\frac12\left[ n_y\log(2\pi)+\log\det S_t+e_t^\top S_t^{-1}e_t \right]. \]

    A singular \(S_t\) requires a separately justified degenerate-Gaussian treatment; blindly adding jitter changes the observation model.

    Worked scalar filter with two observations¤

    Let

    \[ z_0\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\qquad z_t=z_{t-1}+q_t,\quad q_t\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\qquad y_t=z_t+r_t,\quad r_t\sim\mathcal N(0,1). \]

    For \(y_1=1\), prediction gives \(m_{1\mid0}=0\) and \(P_{1\mid0}=2\). Therefore \(S_1=3\), \(K_1=2/3\), and

    \[ m_{1\mid1}=\frac23, \qquad P_{1\mid1}=\frac23. \]

    At time two, the predictive moments are \(m_{2\mid1}=2/3\) and \(P_{2\mid1}=5/3\). For \(y_2=2\),

    \[ S_2=\frac83, \qquad K_2=\frac58, \qquad e_2=\frac43. \]

    Hence

    \[ m_{2\mid2} =\frac23+\frac58\frac43 =\frac32, \qquad P_{2\mid2}=\frac58. \]

    The two predictive log-density contributions are approximately \(-1.63491\) and \(-1.74269\), so the log likelihood of \((1,2)\) under this fixed model is \(-3.37760\). This is a density value for the observation sequence, not a state estimation error.

    Missing, partial, and correlated observations¤

    If \(y_t\) is entirely missing and the absence is ignorable under the declared model, perform prediction and skip correction; the evidence contribution is zero on the log scale. For a partially observed vector, select the observed rows of \(C_t\), \(d_t\), and \(R_t\). Dropping rows of a correlated \(R_t\) is valid only by taking the corresponding marginal covariance, not by pretending the remaining noise is independent.

    If missingness depends on the unseen value or latent state, skipping the update omits an informative missingness likelihood. The model must include that mechanism or report a sensitivity analysis.

    Numerical representations¤

    Table FILTER.2. Numerical representations
    Representation Stored uncertainty Useful structure Important boundary
    covariance \(P\) intuitive marginal variances and direct prediction subtraction in correction can lose symmetry or positive semidefiniteness
    Joseph covariance \(P\) exposes both propagated state uncertainty and measurement noise improves finite behavior but does not cure misspecification
    square-root \(P=LL^\top\) or an information factor QR/orthogonal transformations preserve factor structure implementation conventions differ; reconstructing \(P\) at every step discards much of the benefit
    information \(\Lambda=P^{-1}\) and \(\eta=\Lambda m\) additive independent measurement factors and sparse precision structure prediction may destroy sparsity and must not use an explicit inverse blindly

    Square-root and factorized filters descend from numerical work on sequential estimation; modern QR-only formulations make the stability objective explicit. They compute the same Gaussian law in exact arithmetic, but roundoff, rank handling, conditioning, and differentiation can make finite programs differ.

    Small covariance is not proof of accuracy

    A filter can become confidently wrong when \(Q_t\) or \(R_t\) is underestimated, the observation map is biased, a latent direction is unobservable, or numerical code silently destroys covariance structure. Posterior covariance is conditional uncertainty under the fitted model, not an empirical error bar certified by its own magnitude.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    The Kalman filter is exact Bayesian filtering for the declared linear-Gaussian model. Its update is Gaussian conditioning, and its predictive normalizer supplies the likelihood. Stable implementations preserve matrix structure and solve systems; they do not infer correctness from a successful matrix inversion.

    Chapter 2 retrieval practice¤

    1. Under what assumptions is the complete Kalman posterior exact?
    2. Why is the Joseph update not a new statistical model?
    3. What should happen when an observation is ignorable and missing?
    4. Why can a covariance-only implementation and a square-root implementation disagree in floating-point arithmetic?
    Complete solutions
    1. Gaussian initial state, affine transitions and emissions, and the declared independent Gaussian noise structure, with well-defined conditioning.
    2. It is an algebraically equivalent covariance computation under the same gain and model; it changes numerical realization, not the target law.
    3. Propagate the state distribution and omit the correction; partial observations use the corresponding marginal observation model.
    4. They perform different finite sequences of factorization, multiplication, and subtraction, so conditioning and roundoff affect them differently even when exact algebra agrees.
