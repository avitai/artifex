    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A random-graph model generates a relational structure: a random number of entities, their attributes, and the edges or higher-arity relations among them. When entity names are arbitrary, the sample is not one adjacency matrix but an equivalence class of matrices under simultaneous relabeling. That single observation changes the meaning of likelihood, exchangeability, latent variables, neural parameterizations, sampling, and evaluation.

    This volume develops graph-valued probability from that semantic foundation. It begins with labeled and unlabeled sample spaces, then studies classical graph laws, exchangeability and sparsity, likelihood and posterior inference, neural family compositions, graph-valued diffusion and flow, structural constraints, scientific applications, and current evaluation practice. A graph neural network is treated as a parameterization; an autoregressive order, latent code, energy, flow, or denoising chain is treated as a modeling choice; the graph law remains the object that determines which probability and query are being discussed.

    Curriculum position

    Read Probability and Generative Modeling for sample spaces and densities and Classical Generative Models for latent-variable and exponential-family foundations. Chapter 1 introduces the permutation-group actions it needs, so the later Geometric and Equivariant Generative Modeling volume is optional deepening for continuous coordinates and broader symmetry, not a gate to starting this volume. Autoregressive Models, Diffusion Models, Flows, and GFlowNets supply reusable mechanisms; none of them removes the graph-specific probability contract developed here.

    1. Graph-Valued Probability and Relabeling¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes finite probability spaces and elementary graph terminology. It introduces group actions in the special case of vertex relabeling before using them. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Specify the graph-valued object: declare graph type, attributes, node-count law, and labeling convention.
    2. O2 — Compute orbit probability: translate between labeled and unlabeled mass by an explicit orbit sum.
    3. O3 — Use automorphisms correctly: apply orbit–stabilizer only when the labeled law is invariant.
    4. O4 — Audit symmetry claims: distinguish an invariant law from an equivariant node- or edge-level computation.
    5. O5 — Separate neighboring objects: distinguish a random graph from a graphical model, GNN, causal graph, or completion score.
    6. O6 — Transfer the semantic contract: expose count, identity, quotient, and representation choices in a new relational domain.

    The generated object¤

    For a fixed graph type, let \(\mathcal G_n\) denote the admissible labeled graphs on vertex set \([n]=\{1,\ldots,n\}\). A variable-size graph law lives on the disjoint union

    \[ \mathcal G=\bigsqcup_{n\ge 0}\mathcal G_n, \qquad p(G)=p_N(n)\,p(G\mid N=n). \]

    The factorization is conceptual, not compulsory: a constructive model may interleave node creation and edge decisions. Nevertheless, the final law still implies a marginal distribution over \(N\). Fixing a maximum size and padding unused nodes does not eliminate this obligation; the padding mask or “no-node” state becomes part of the representation and must induce a normalized count law.

    A graph type includes more than \(A\):

    • undirected, directed, bipartite, multi-edge, or hyperedge semantics;
    • allowed self-loops and relation arity;
    • discrete or continuous node and edge attributes;
    • persistent entity identities versus arbitrary labels;
    • optional coordinates, frames, chirality, or physical units; and
    • hard constraints such as acyclicity, connectedness, planarity, or valence.

    Changing any item changes the sample space or its quotient.

    Relabeling as a group action¤

    For a simple attributed graph and permutation \(\pi\in S_n\),

    \[ A^\pi=P_\pi A P_\pi^\top, \qquad X^\pi=P_\pi X. \]

    If node names have no meaning, the observable object is the orbit

    \[ [G]=\{G^\pi:\pi\in S_n\}, \]

    or the corresponding orbit under a smaller type-preserving group. A labeled mass \(p_{\mathrm{lab}}(G)\) induces the unlabeled mass

    \[ \boxed{p_{\mathrm{unlab}}([G])=\sum_{H\in[G]}p_{\mathrm{lab}}(H).} \]

    If the labeled law is invariant, so that \(p_{\mathrm{lab}}(G^\pi)=p_{\mathrm{lab}}(G)\) for every \(\pi\), the orbit–stabilizer theorem gives

    \[ \boxed{p_{\mathrm{unlab}}([G])= \frac{n!}{|\operatorname{Aut}(G)|}\,p_{\mathrm{lab}}(G).} \]

    The automorphism group must preserve every attribute regarded as part of the graph. Coloring one endpoint of an otherwise symmetric edge can shrink the automorphism group and enlarge the number of distinct labeled representatives.

    Invariant laws and equivariant computations¤

    A scalar density or energy is invariant if relabeling its input does not change its value. A nodewise output \(F(G)\in\mathbb R^{n\times d}\) is equivariant if

    \[ F(G^\pi)=P_\pi F(G). \]

    Equivariance is often the right architecture contract for node or edge predictions, but it is not by itself a probability proof. A model can use an equivariant network while introducing order bias through positional features, a non-invariant prior, padding, canonicalization, or a sampler. Conversely, an order-dependent factorization can induce an invariant final law if it exactly averages the appropriate orders.

    Complete worked example: three vertices¤

    Let \(G\sim G(3,p)\), so the three possible undirected edges are independent Bernoulli variables. A particular labeled two-edge path has mass

    \[ p_{\mathrm{lab}}(G)=p^2(1-p). \]

    The path has two automorphisms: identity and swapping its endpoints. Its orbit therefore has \(3!/2=3\) labeled representatives, giving

    \[ p_{\mathrm{unlab}}([G])=3p^2(1-p). \]

    At \(p=0.4\), the four unlabeled classes have probabilities

    Five objects that are often confused¤

    1. A random graph makes topology or relations random.
    2. A probabilistic graphical model uses a fixed graph to encode factorization or conditional independence among random variables.
    3. A graph neural network is a parameter-sharing architecture on graph inputs.
    4. A causal graph summarizes causal assumptions; a distribution over causal DAGs needs additional intervention semantics.
    5. A knowledge-graph completion model may score missing triples conditional on a fixed entity universe without defining a normalized law over entire graphs.

    These objects can be composed. They are not synonyms.

    When repeated vectors are observed and the unknown graph is meant to encode their conditional independences, the object is instead a graphical-model structure. The dependency-structure chapter develops Gaussian precision graphs, discrete neighborhoods, latent-induced dependence, and Bayesian-network equivalence classes without treating the graph itself as the generated observation.

    One graph, three probability questions¤

    The phrase “probability of this graph” is incomplete until the observable has been fixed. The same drawing can denote one labeled matrix, its entire isomorphism class, or an attributed object with a smaller symmetry group.

    This diagram is a decision procedure, not just a vocabulary map. In particular, attributes can destroy an uncolored automorphism, and a non-invariant model invalidates the multiplicity shortcut even though its orbit is still well-defined.

    Chapter 1 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • “The adjacency tensor is symmetric, so the model is permutation invariant.” Matrix symmetry exchanges edge endpoints; permutation invariance concerns simultaneous relabeling of every node and attribute.
    • “Canonical labeling solves the probability problem.” A deterministic canonicalizer can choose one representative, but discontinuities, ties, computational cost, and information encoded in the original labels still require analysis.
    • “All labels are nuisance.” In citation, family, transportation, or knowledge graphs, persistent identities may carry scientific meaning. Quotienting them out would change the task.
    • “A valid graph sample proves a valid density.” Sample validity, normalization, invariance, and likelihood tractability are different properties.
    Retrieval practice: graph probability
    1. Why is the graph space a disjoint union over \(n\)?
    2. When can \(n!/|\operatorname{Aut}(G)|\) replace an explicit sum over labelings?
    3. What is the difference between an invariant scalar and an equivariant node representation?
    4. Why is a causal DAG not implied by a generated directed graph?

    Solutions. (1) Different node counts have different adjacency and attribute spaces, and a variable-size law must allocate mass among them. (2) Only when the labeled law assigns equal mass to every representative in the orbit. (3) The scalar is unchanged by relabeling; the node representation is relabeled in the same way as its input. (4) Direction alone does not supply structural equations, intervention semantics, causal sufficiency, or identifiability.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Graph probability begins with the graph type, node-count law, and labeling convention. Unlabeled likelihood is an orbit sum, automorphisms control multiplicity, and equivariant architecture is only one part of an invariant generative system.

    Bridge to Chapter 2. With the sample space fixed, we can compare classical families by the dependencies they encode, the graph-size regimes they support, and the queries they make tractable.

    2. Classical Graph Laws, Exchangeability, and Sparsity¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Bernoulli likelihoods, latent-variable models, exponential families, and the labeling conventions of Chapter 1. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Calibrate an independent-edge baseline: derive and interpret the \(G(n,p)\) likelihood and MLE.
    2. O2 — Compare dependence mechanisms: distinguish degree, growth, block, latent-geometry, and sufficient-statistic assumptions.
    3. O3 — Compute a finite structured law: evaluate exact SBM and ERGM probabilities on small graphs.
    4. O4 — Interpret exchangeability: state what vertex exchangeability preserves and what remains unidentified.
    5. O5 — Separate size regimes: distinguish dense graphon scaling, sparse-process constructions, and finite-size generalization.
    6. O6 — Select and transfer a classical family: choose a model from the scientific dependence and observation mechanism rather than surface resemblance.

    Independent edges: \(G(n,p)\) and \(G(n,m)\)¤

    For a simple undirected graph with fixed \(n\), let \(M=\binom n2\) be the number of possible edges and \(m=m(G)\). The independent-edge model has

    \[ p(G\mid n,p)=p^m(1-p)^{M-m}. \]

    For one fully observed labeled graph, the log likelihood is

    \[ \ell(p)=m\log p+(M-m)\log(1-p), \]

    so \(\widehat p=m/M\). This is a useful calibration baseline: if a learned model cannot improve held-out queries over a count-matched independent-edge law, its additional capacity has not demonstrated relational dependence.

    The model \(G(n,m)\) instead places uniform mass \(1/\binom{M}{m}\) on graphs with exactly \(m\) edges. Conditioning \(G(n,p)\) on its edge count gives \(G(n,m)\), but their unconditional count laws and growing-graph behavior differ.

    Degree-constrained and growth models¤

    The configuration model pairs half-edges attached to vertices with declared degrees \(d_1,\ldots,d_n\). The direct pairing law naturally permits self-loops and parallel edges. Conditioning on simplicity or repeatedly rejecting invalid pairings changes the sampler and can become inefficient for heavy-tailed degrees. A degree sequence must also be graphical before a simple graph with exactly those degrees can exist.

    Preferential-attachment models generate a graph through arrival-time dynamics, often choosing an existing node with probability proportional to its current degree plus an offset. Arrival order is part of the construction. Such a process can express heavy-tailed degrees but is not automatically invariant to erasing and arbitrarily relabeling arrival times.

    Stochastic block models¤

    An undirected \(K\)-class SBM samples

    \[ z_i\sim\operatorname{Categorical}(\pi), \qquad A_{ij}\mid z_i,z_j\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(B_{z_i z_j}),\quad i<j, \]

    with joint mass

    \[ p(A,z\mid\pi,B)= \prod_{i=1}^n\pi_{z_i} \prod_{i<j}\left[ B_{z_i z_j}^{A_{ij}} (1-B_{z_i z_j})^{1-A_{ij}} \right]. \]

    Conditional independence of edges does not mean marginal independence: integrating out the shared \(z_i\) couples incident edges. SBMs represent stochastic equivalence—nodes in a block share relation probabilities—not necessarily assortative communities.

    Mixed-membership SBMs draw role proportions per node and may draw sender/receiver roles per interaction. Degree-corrected SBMs add node-specific propensity so that community structure need not explain every degree difference. Each extension changes identifiability and inference.

    Worked block-likelihood comparison¤

    Take four nodes, two equally likely blocks, within-block probability \(0.8\), and between-block probability \(0.2\). Observe edges \((1,2)\) and \((3,4)\) and no other edges. Under assignment \((1,1,2,2)\), both observed edges are within-block and all four absent edges are between-block, so

    \[ p(A\mid z=(1,1,2,2))=0.8^6. \]

    Under the all-one-block assignment, the two edges have probability \(0.8\) but the four nonedges each have probability \(0.2\):

    \[ p(A\mid z=(1,1,1,1))=0.8^2 0.2^4. \]

    Their likelihood ratio is \((0.8/0.2)^4=256\). This compares two specified assignments; a posterior over partitions must include their priors, label-swapped equivalents, and every competing assignment.

    Latent-position and random dot-product models¤

    Latent-position models give every node a coordinate \(u_i\) and relate edge probability to distance, for example

    \[ \operatorname{logit}\Pr(A_{ij}=1\mid u_i,u_j) =\beta_0-\|u_i-u_j\|. \]

    They express homophily and transitivity through geometry. Random dot-product graphs instead use a link such as \(\Pr(A_{ij}=1)=u_i^\top u_j\) on a domain where this lies in \([0,1]\). Coordinates are typically identifiable only up to rotations, reflections, or larger transformations permitted by the likelihood. A visually stable embedding is not automatically a uniquely identified scientific latent space.

    Exponential random-graph models¤

    An ERGM assigns

    \[ p_\theta(G\mid n)= \exp\!\left\{\theta^\top s(G)-\psi_n(\theta)\right\}, \qquad \psi_n(\theta)=\log\sum_{H\in\mathcal G_n} e^{\theta^\top s(H)}. \]

    Statistics may count edges, mutual dyads, stars, triangles, covariate mixing, or geometrically weighted configurations. The log odds of one edge conditional on all others is

    \[ \log\frac{\Pr(A_{ij}=1\mid A_{-ij})}{\Pr(A_{ij}=0\mid A_{-ij})} =\theta^\top\Delta_{ij}s(G), \]

    where \(\Delta_{ij}s=s(G^{+ij})-s(G^{-ij})\). This local conditional is tractable even when \(\psi_n\) is not. Strong positive triangle or star terms can concentrate mass near a few extreme graph configurations; degeneracy is a distributional pathology, not merely optimizer failure.

    Graphons and vertex exchangeability¤

    A common vertex-exchangeable model samples

    \[ U_i\stackrel{\mathrm{iid}}{\sim}\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1), \qquad A_{ij}\mid U_i,U_j\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(W(U_i,U_j)), \]

    where \(W:[0,1]^2\to[0,1]\) is symmetric. SBMs are step-function graphons. The Aldous–Hoover representation places this conditional-edge form inside the representation of jointly exchangeable binary arrays, while dense graph-limit theory connects graphons to limiting subgraph densities.

    Graphons are not pointwise identifiable. If \(\phi\) preserves Lebesgue measure, then \(W(u,v)\) and \(W(\phi(u),\phi(v))\) induce the same graph law. The estimand is an equivalence class under measure-preserving transformations, or a functional invariant to them.

    For a fixed nonzero graphon,

    \[ \mathbb E[m(G_n)] =\binom n2\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 W(u,v)\,du\,dv, \]

    which grows on the order of \(n^2\). Hence ordinary projective vertex-exchangeable graphons are dense unless empty. Sparse modeling requires a size-dependent kernel, a different sampling scheme, or a different exchangeability object. Caron and Fox use exchangeable random measures; graphex constructions formalize a broader point-process representation. These are not obtained by merely training the same dense adjacency decoder on fewer observed edges.

    What “consistency across graph size” means¤

    Several distinct properties are often called consistency:

    • projectivity: restricting a draw at size \(n+1\) to \(n\) labeled vertices has the size-\(n\) law;
    • sampling consistency: a declared random subsampling operation preserves the family;
    • estimator consistency: an estimator converges to its estimand as information grows;
    • generator size generalization: a learned finite model produces credible sizes outside training.

    One does not imply the others. The observation protocol—vertex sampling, edge sampling, induced subgraphs, snowball sampling, or time-window expansion—is part of the model comparison.

    First declare the data regime¤

    Graph learning problems that look syntactically similar can have different sampling units:

    1. Many IID graphs: molecules, circuits, parse graphs, or repeated interaction networks.
    2. One large graph: a social, citation, transportation, or infrastructure network.
    3. Subgraphs from one graph: correlated neighborhoods or ego networks, not IID graphs by default.
    4. A temporal graph process: nodes and edges arrive, disappear, or change attributes.
    5. Partially observed relations: node sampling, edge censoring, link tracing, or measurement error.

    A train/test split over edges of one graph estimates a link-prediction query under that split mechanism. It is not automatically an out-of-sample test of a law over new whole graphs.

    Labeled, aligned, and unlabeled likelihoods¤

    If entity identities align across observations, a labeled likelihood can be scientifically correct. If labels are arbitrary, the observed class likelihood is

    \[ p_\theta([G])=\sum_{H\in[G]}p_\theta(H). \]

    For a non-invariant order-based model this is a genuine log-sum-exp over distinct labelings. Training on random permutations optimizes a Monte Carlo surrogate; canonical ordering optimizes the chosen representative; teacher forcing on one order optimizes that order. None is automatically exact marginalization.

    For an invariant labeled law, automorphism multiplicity yields the exact conversion from Chapter 1. But even then, evaluating \(|\operatorname{Aut}(G)|\) or checking isomorphism can be expensive, and attributed or typed graphs change the stabilizer.

    Latent-variable inference in SBMs¤

    For an SBM, the evidence is

    \[ p(A\mid\pi,B)=\sum_{z\in[K]^n}p(A,z\mid\pi,B), \]

    whose \(K^n\) assignments motivate EM, variational inference, belief propagation, MCMC, and spectral initializers. A mean-field variational posterior \(q(z)=\prod_i q_i(z_i)\) maximizes

    \[ \mathcal L(q,\pi,B) =\mathbb E_q[\log p(A,z\mid\pi,B)] -\mathbb E_q[\log q(z)]. \]

    The ELBO can be optimized while the posterior approximation remains poor, especially near phase transitions, under degree heterogeneity, or with multimodal block assignments.

    Block labels are exchangeable: permuting the \(K\) names leaves the graph likelihood unchanged. This label switching is distinct from permuting graph vertices. Posterior summaries must align block labels or report label-invariant quantities such as co-clustering probabilities.

    Detectability is a property of a regime¤

    In the symmetric two-block sparse SBM with equal block sizes,

    \[ p_{\mathrm{in}}=\frac an, \qquad p_{\mathrm{out}}=\frac bn, \]

    weak recovery has the classical threshold

    \[ \boxed{(a-b)^2>2(a+b)} \]

    for the two-group model; below the strict reverse inequality, recovery correlated with the planted partition is impossible asymptotically. The threshold proof is a model-specific theorem, not a universal rule for unbalanced, degree-corrected, attributed, multi-group, or finite networks.

    For \(a=8,b=2\), the left side is \(36\) and the right side is \(20\); the asymptotic condition holds. For \(a=5,b=3\), they are \(4\) and \(16\); simply increasing optimizer capacity cannot recover information absent in that asymptotic observation model.

    ERGM normalizers and Monte Carlo¤

    The ERGM log-likelihood gradient is

    \[ \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(G) =s(G)-\nabla_\theta\psi_n(\theta) =s(G)-\mathbb E_\theta[s(H)]. \]

    The model moment is usually intractable because the sum spans \(2^{\binom n2}\) simple undirected labeled graphs. MCMC maximum likelihood estimates this expectation with samples from a current parameter value; Bayesian methods may use exchange algorithms or auxiliary-variable schemes to cancel an intractable normalizer. Both depend on chain mixing. A chain trapped in one graph mode can make the estimated gradient look stable while remaining wrong.

    The pseudolikelihood

    \[ \operatorname{PL}(\theta;G) =\prod_{i<j}p_\theta(A_{ij}\mid A_{-ij}) \]

    uses tractable edge conditionals derived from change statistics. It is an approximation to the joint likelihood, not a different normalized ERGM with the same finite-sample guarantees.

    Graphon and latent-position inference¤

    Graphon estimation is performed modulo measure-preserving transformations. Losses such as cut distance, subgraph-density error, or prediction risk avoid pretending that the coordinate system is identified. Latent-position posterior samples additionally require alignment under rotations, reflections, translations, or scale conventions before coordinate averages are meaningful.

    When only one large graph is observed, uncertainty comes from the declared network sampling model—not from treating its \(\binom n2\) edges as IID observations. Conditional edge independence given latents does not imply unconditional IID edge sampling.

    Missingness, censoring, and negative edges¤

    Let \(R_{ij}\) indicate whether dyad \((i,j)\) was measured. The observed-data likelihood is

    \[ p(A_{\mathrm{obs}},R) =\sum_{A_{\mathrm{mis}}} p(R\mid A,X)\,p(A\mid X). \]

    Dropping \(p(R\mid A,X)\) is justified only under an appropriate ignorability condition. In crawled or link-traced networks, high-degree nodes and present edges may be more likely to be observed. In knowledge graphs, an unrecorded triple is often unknown, not a verified negative. Negative sampling defines a training estimator and proposal distribution; it does not change the population meaning of absence unless the observation model says so.

    Complete worked inference example: the three-node ERGM¤

    Return to the edge–triangle model with \(Z=43\). Exact moments are available by enumeration:

    \[ \Pr_\theta(\text{triangle})=\frac{24}{43}, \qquad \mathbb E_\theta[m(G)] =\frac{3\cdot2+3\cdot2\cdot4+1\cdot3\cdot24}{43} =\frac{102}{43}. \]

    The middle expression is easier read by total class weights: one-edge graphs contribute \(6\) edge-count units, paths contribute \(24\), and the triangle contributes \(72\). If the observed graph is a path, its score component for edges is \(2-102/43<0\), so the exact likelihood gradient reduces the edge parameter at this \(\theta\). An MCMC estimate must reproduce this expectation in the finite toy case before being trusted on a larger graph.

    Three symmetries and one observation filter¤

    Several inferential ambiguities can coexist, but they act on different objects and require different repairs. Treating them all as “permutation invariance” can make an apparently stable estimator scientifically meaningless.

    A reusable decomposition¤

    Most neural graph generators can be analyzed through five maps:

    1. representation: graph \(G\) to adjacency, edge list, construction trace, motif tree, or latent code;
    2. parameterization: invariant/equivariant network producing logits, densities, energies, scores, velocities, or actions;
    3. population target: likelihood, ELBO, adversarial/IPM, flow, score, reward-proportional, or composite objective;
    4. sampler: one-shot decoding, sequential construction, MCMC, inverse flow, denoising, or policy rollout; and
    5. quotient handling: canonical order, augmentation, matching, marginalization, or invariant construction.

    Calling the whole system “a GNN generator” hides all five.

    One-shot decoders¤

    A one-shot decoder emits node-existence, node-type, and edge-type distributions up to a maximum size. GraphVAE is an early example: its reconstruction objective matches a decoded probabilistic graph to the input. Matching is necessary because latent slots have no intrinsic correspondence with input nodes.

    One-shot decoding provides parallel sampling but commonly faces:

    • \(O(n^2)\) pair outputs and severe absent-edge imbalance;
    • independent or weakly coupled edge heads that miss global constraints;
    • fixed maximum size and padding semantics;
    • non-differentiable or approximate node matching; and
    • limited message-passing expressivity for globally distinct graphs.

    SPECTRE uses spectral conditioning to inject global structure into a one-shot generator. Spectral features help a declared architecture; they do not uniquely identify a graph, and repeated eigenvalues introduce basis ambiguity.

    Autoregressive construction¤

    An ordered construction trace \(\tau\) factorizes as

    \[ p(\tau)=\prod_{t=1}^{T}p(a_t\mid a_{<t}), \]

    where actions may add nodes, edges, motifs, or a stop symbol. GraphRNN generates node/edge sequences; GRAN generates graph blocks with attention.

    The terminal graph probability is

    \[ p(G)=\sum_{\tau:\operatorname{yield}(\tau)=G}p(\tau). \]

    Training on one BFS, DFS, random, or canonical trace usually optimizes a lower-cost surrogate for this sum. Different traces can generate the same graph, and the stop action carries the node-count law. Autoregression offers exact trace likelihood and convenient prefix constraints, not automatically exact unlabeled graph likelihood.

    Variational latent graph models¤

    A graph VAE introduces \(z\) and optimizes

    \[ \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid G)}[\log p_\theta(G\mid z)] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid G)\|p(z)). \]

    The reconstruction term still needs a labeling/matching convention. Hierarchical decoders can generate motifs or tree decompositions before assembling a graph; Junction Tree VAE uses chemically meaningful substructures to improve molecular validity. That domain representation changes the support and action grammar. It should not be interpreted as a generic guarantee for social, circuit, or program graphs.

    Latent variables can represent global graph properties and accelerate generation, but posterior collapse, lossy graph codecs, approximate matching, and decoder factorization remain visible in the ELBO semantics.

    Implicit and adversarial graph generators¤

    NetGAN adversarially learns random-walk sequences from one network and assembles a graph from generated walk transition counts. Its discriminator compares walks, not whole-graph densities. Matching local walk statistics can preserve useful network structure while leaving other graph laws unidentified.

    GAN or MMD objectives can operate on entire invariant graph embeddings, construction traces, or graph statistics. Their answer depends on the critic or kernel. An invariant critic prevents one source of label leakage but does not provide likelihood, complete mode coverage, or graph validity.

    Flows and dequantized graph states¤

    Classical normalizing flows require a continuous invertible map of fixed dimension. Discrete, variable-size graphs therefore need choices such as dequantization, autoregressive discrete transforms, a fixed maximum size, or a continuous latent codec. GraphAF combines autoregressive graph construction with flow-based conditional transformations for molecular graphs.

    An exact density over a dequantized adjacency tensor is not automatically an exact PMF over unlabeled discrete graphs. The integration cell, symmetry constraint, padding, ordering, and inverse map determine that conversion. Continuous graph normalizing flows over node embeddings may support representation learning without defining a law over new topology.

    Energy-based graph models¤

    An invariant energy defines

    \[ p_\theta(G)=\frac{e^{-E_\theta(G)}}{Z_\theta}, \qquad Z_\theta=\sum_{H\in\mathcal G}e^{-E_\theta(H)}. \]

    GraphEBM studies permutation-invariant molecular graph energies and Langevin-style generation on relaxed graph representations. Invariance of \(E\) gives equal unnormalized weight to relabelings, but \(Z\), discrete relaxation, MCMC mixing, and projection back to valid graphs remain separate problems.

    Energies compose constraints and properties naturally by addition only when the intended target is the corresponding product-of-experts law and the terms share support and units. Low energy is not a calibrated probability without normalization or a validated sampler.

    Probabilistic graph circuits¤

    Probabilistic Graph Circuits, UAI 2025, construct tractable distributions over graph representations and analyze the tension among permutation invariance, expressivity, and exact query cost. A circuit can support exact marginals or conditionals under its structural conditions; symmetrizing over permutations can sacrifice efficiency, while restricting the circuit for native invariance can sacrifice expressivity.

    This is a hybrid composition with the probabilistic-circuit family. It is important precisely because “graph likelihood” and “arbitrary missing-edge conditional” are not tractable for most deep graph generators.

    GFlowNets and constructive graph laws¤

    A GFlowNet samples terminal graph \(G\) proportionally to a nonnegative reward \(R(G)\) when the declared state graph and exact balance conditions hold:

    \[ p_T(G)=\frac{R(G)}{\sum_H R(H)}. \]

    Multiple construction trajectories can reach the same graph, so flow conservation handles a multiplicity closely related to—but distinct from—node relabeling. The original molecular GFlowNet emphasizes diverse high-reward candidate generation.

    A GFlowNet trained from a property oracle is not by default a density estimator for the empirical graph distribution. The terminal target is reward-proportional, and its validity depends on action support, termination, reward calibration, and exploration.

    Worked example: trace likelihood is not graph likelihood¤

    Suppose a three-node path can be constructed by choosing any of its three nodes first and then adding its two edges in two possible orders. Even if six traces have equal probability \(r\), the graph mass is \(6r\) only when those are the complete, disjoint traces yielding the graph. A training objective using one canonical trace contributes \(\log r\), whereas the exact graph log likelihood is \(\log(6r)=\log r+\log6\). With unequal trace probabilities it becomes a log-sum-exp, not a constant correction.

    The count of construction traces is not generally \(n!/|\operatorname{Aut}(G)|\): each labeled representative may admit many action orders. Orbit multiplicity and trajectory multiplicity must be handled separately.

    From parameterization to graph law: where mass can split¤

    A neural system can preserve symmetry at one stage and lose the requested graph probability at another. Follow probability all the way through the composition, especially across many-to-one representations.

    The two sums at the right need not coincide. A single labeled graph can have many construction traces, while one unlabeled graph can have many labeled representatives; decoder matchings add a third multiplicity.

    Chapter 4 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • “An encoder reconstructs adjacency, so it is a graph generator.” A generative story, prior, normalized decoder or implicit sampling law, and node-count mechanism are still needed.
    • “One-shot means independent edges.” The network can couple logits through shared representations, but the decoder likelihood may still conditionally factorize.
    • “Exact flow likelihood means exact graph likelihood.” It is exact for the declared continuous representation and direction; quotienting and dequantization remain.
    • “Invariant energy means exact invariant generation.” Approximate relaxed MCMC and projection can introduce sampling bias.
    • “Reward-proportional means property maximizing.” A GFlowNet distributes mass across modes according to reward; greedy maximization is a different target.

    A graph-valued Markov path¤

    An iterative generator introduces states \(G_0,G_1,\ldots,G_T\) or \(G_t\) in continuous time. A complete specification states:

    • whether \(N\) is fixed, generated first, padded, or changed during the path;
    • node and edge alphabets, including absent and masked states;
    • whether corruption acts independently on components or through graph-level moves;
    • the forward transition or interpolation law;
    • the reverse parameterization and objective;
    • permutation equivariance of every transition and network;
    • constraint handling; and
    • the number and cost of reverse evaluations.

    For a categorical component with data-dependent one-hot row vector \(x_0\) and stationary marginal \(m\), a simple corruption kernel has

    \[ q_t(x_t\mid x_0)=x_0\left[\alpha_t I+(1-\alpha_t)\mathbf 1m^\top\right]. \]

    If an edge is present, \(m=(0.8,0.2)\) for absent/present, and \(\alpha_t=0.7\), the corrupted distribution is \((0.24,0.76)\). The prior retains the empirical imbalance rather than becoming uniform. Whether this helps depends on the reverse target and evaluation protocol.

    Discrete denoising diffusion¤

    DiGress applies categorical corruption to node and edge types and trains a permutation-equivariant graph Transformer to predict clean states. Discrete transitions preserve meaningful categorical states during noising, unlike adding Gaussian noise directly to binary adjacency and then thresholding.

    Independent forward corruption does not assert independent data edges. The reverse network couples predictions through the noisy graph. However, a dense node-pair representation still costs \(O(n^2)\) memory or computation, and a factorized reverse distribution can miss hard global dependencies at a finite step.

    EDGE uses edge-removal diffusion and degree guidance to scale toward graphs with thousands of nodes. Sparse training or edge-list operations can reduce work, but exact all-pair reverse sampling may reintroduce quadratic cost unless the candidate-edge mechanism is part of the model.

    Continuous scores and relaxed adjacency¤

    Score-based graph generators may dequantize \(A\) and node attributes into Euclidean tensors, diffuse them continuously, enforce symmetry in the score, and discretize at the end. This permits SDE machinery but changes the intermediate state space. Thresholding or argmax projection can alter the terminal law, and zero diagonal, symmetric edges, node existence, and categorical constraints must be enforced coherently.

    A continuous score on matrix entries is not a score on an unlabeled discrete graph space. It is the score of a declared continuous relaxation. Exact conversion requires integrating the density over every discretization cell and every relevant labeling.

    Discrete flow and Bayesian belief paths¤

    Discrete flow matching learns transition rates or probability velocities on categorical graph components. Directo, ICLR 2026, uses an asymmetric encoding and discrete flow matching for directed graphs. Its contribution is specific to directed topology and its benchmark protocol; it does not imply that an undirected architecture becomes direction-aware by dropping adjacency symmetrization.

    GraphBSI, ICLR 2026, evolves continuous distribution parameters representing beliefs over discrete graph variables and relates its construction to Bayesian sample inference, BFNs, and diffusion. The evolving object is a belief parameter, not a partially realized adjacency matrix. “One-shot” in that work refers to its sample-inference construction and should not be confused with a single network evaluation or an independent-edge decoder.

    Bures–Wasserstein Flow Matching, ICLR 2026, constructs paths using graph-associated Markov-random-field objects to couple node and edge evolution. Its theoretical path and convergence claims belong to that representation and set of assumptions; benchmark improvements do not establish one universal graph geometry.

    Higher-order and multiscale structure¤

    Finite-step denoisers can struggle to reconstruct long cycles, community organization, connectedness, or motif counts from locally corrupted states. Common responses include spectral features, hierarchical coarsening, motif variables, global latent codes, or explicit topological guidance.

    HOG-Diff, ICLR 2026, uses higher-order topology in a coarse-to-fine diffusion-bridge framework. DualDiff, ICLR 2026, couples local and global latent topology. These are peer-reviewed research results under reported graph benchmarks. They motivate multiscale state design; they do not show that any fixed collection of topological summaries identifies the target graph distribution.

    Hard constraints versus guided preferences¤

    Let \(\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal G\) be a feasible set. Exact conditioning of a base graph law is

    \[ p(G\mid G\in\mathcal C) =\frac{p(G)\mathbf 1\{G\in\mathcal C\}} {\Pr_p(G\in\mathcal C)}. \]

    A sampler that projects each invalid graph to a nearby valid graph generally produces a different pushforward distribution. Rejection is exact when proposals are exact and acceptance is feasible, but it can be exponentially inefficient. Constraint-preserving actions or transitions keep support inside \(\mathcal C\), though they can disconnect the state or construction graph and exclude valid modes.

    Examples require different mechanisms:

    • connectedness: grow from a connected seed, use connectivity-preserving edits, or condition globally;
    • DAGs: generate an order plus forward edges, use acyclicity-aware actions, or represent a partial order;
    • planarity: use planar construction grammars or validity-preserving edits; local edge logits do not guarantee it;
    • molecular valence: track atom/bond budgets during construction, while remembering that graph validity is weaker than geometric and chemical plausibility;
    • fixed degree: use degree-preserving edge swaps or a degree-sequence construction;
    • bipartite/typed relations: restrict allowed edge types in both forward and reverse kernels.

    Guidance by a learned property predictor targets an approximation to a reweighted law. Predictor error, off-support extrapolation, guidance scale, and finite-step integration can all break constraint satisfaction or diversity.

    Directed, dynamic, heterogeneous, and geometric graphs¤

    An asymmetric relation uses ordered pairs \((i,j)\) and \(A_{ij}\ne A_{ji}\) in general. Dynamic graphs add event time or snapshot transitions; they are graph-valued stochastic processes, not a static graph with time concatenated as a feature. Heterogeneous graphs use node and relation types whose allowed permutations may be type-specific. Hypergraphs generate subsets or ordered tuples beyond pairwise edges.

    Molecular and protein systems may include both a discrete bond graph and 3-D coordinates. The joint law must define whether topology precedes geometry, geometry precedes inferred bonds, or both evolve together. SE(3)-equivariance governs coordinate transformations; permutation symmetry governs atom identities. Neither replaces the other, and graph validity does not establish physical stability or experimental function.

    Complete worked sampler check¤

    Consider a two-state edge corruption with

    \[ Q_t=\alpha_t I+(1-\alpha_t)\mathbf1m^\top, \quad m=(0.8,0.2),\quad \alpha_t=0.7. \]

    The transition rows are

    \[ Q_t= \begin{bmatrix} 0.94 & 0.06\\ 0.24 & 0.76 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Each row sums to one and \(mQ_t=m\). Thus the chosen absent/present marginal is stationary. Applying this kernel independently to the upper-triangular edges and mirroring the result preserves adjacency symmetry; sampling both \(A_{ij}\) and \(A_{ji}\) independently does not. The reverse model still needs to recover cross-edge dependence and any global feasibility constraint.

    Condition, preserve, reject, or repair?¤

    Constraint handling changes probability, cost, or both. The appropriate route depends on whether the deployment query asks for the base law conditioned on a feasible event or merely for outputs inside a valid set.

    Rejection can realize exact conditioning when proposals are exact and accepted solely by \(C\). Repair instead merges proposal states through a many-to-one map; its output is generally not the base conditional law.

It must marginalize or bound order multiplicity, validate type rules and reachability, report invalid/repair/rejection rates, compare generated property distributions before and after any post-processing, and measure actions, candidate edges, and termination failures. Rubric. Full credit states whether the output law is exact, conditioned, guided, or repaired.

    Compute and implementation contract¤

    Report at least:

    • maximum, mean, and sampled node counts;
    • dense pair tensor versus sparse edge-list representation;
    • number of node, edge, motif, and global tokens;
    • training corruption draws per graph;
    • reverse NFE or transition count;
    • candidate-edge pruning and whether it changes support;
    • constraint checks, rejection rate, or repair rate;
    • wall-clock, peak memory, batch size, and hardware; and
    • validity measured before and after post-processing.

    An \(O(n^2)\) model that samples in 20 steps and an \(O(m)\) autoregressive model that makes \(n\) sequential decisions cannot be compared by NFE alone.

    Chapter 5 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • “Discrete diffusion preserves sparsity.” A categorical absent state preserves semantics, but dense all-pair tensors and reverse candidates can still be quadratic.
    • “Equivariant denoising makes the quotient likelihood exact.” It supports an invariant transition system under additional conditions; likelihood and orbit multiplicity remain separate.
    • “Projection enforces the conditional distribution.” It enforces membership in a feasible set while generally changing relative probabilities.
    • “A valid molecular graph is a valid molecule.” Sanitization, 3-D geometry, energetics, synthesizability, and experimental function are distinct tests.
    • “One-shot means one function evaluation.” Terminology varies; inspect the actual state evolution and sampler.
    Retrieval practice: iterative generation
    1. Why include absent edges in a categorical state space?
    2. What remains \(O(n^2)\) even when the observed graph is sparse?
    3. How does exact conditioning differ from deterministic repair?
    4. What evolves in GraphBSI-style generation?
    5. Which two symmetries coexist in a 3-D molecular graph?

    Solutions. (1) Absence is one possible relation state and is needed for normalized edge transitions. (2) Enumerating or predicting every possible node pair. (3) Conditioning renormalizes the base law on the feasible set; repair pushes all proposal mass through a many-to-one map. (4) Parameters of beliefs over discrete graph variables. (5) Node-permutation symmetry and Euclidean/SE(3) coordinate symmetry.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Graph-valued iterative generation requires coherent node-count, categorical, symmetry, and constraint semantics along the entire path. Current methods explore discrete corruption, continuous relaxations, belief-parameter paths, graph-aware transport, and multiscale guidance, each with distinct guarantees and compute.

    Bridge to Chapter 6. A sampler is useful only if evaluation can detect wrong counts, missing modes, label leakage, topology failures, invalid scientific structures, and memorization under a protocol matched to the deployment query.

    6. Evaluation, Scientific Use, and Current Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes statistical distances and Chapters 1–5. It develops the graph-specific evaluation concepts it needs; the later general evaluation volume consolidates them across model families. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Match evaluation to the query: distinguish whole-graph generation, completion, optimization, posterior, growth, and causal claims.
    2. O2 — Build a multi-level protocol: assess representation, local and global topology, distributional fidelity, domain utility, integrity, and cost.
    3. O3 — Bound descriptor metrics: explain exactly what statistic MMD and learned graph discrepancies can and cannot identify.
    4. O4 — Audit probability and calibration: name the likelihood convention and use proper scores for count or conditional predictions.
    5. O5 — Test integrity and scientific validity: investigate memorization, privacy, leakage, physical or executable constraints, and causal overreach.
    6. O6 — Interpret current evidence: write source-bounded conclusions and design reproducible comparisons across graph families.

    Begin with the scientific query¤

    Graph generation can serve different estimands:

    • sampling new IID graphs from a population;
    • simulating one larger network under a fitted process;
    • completing missing nodes, edges, or attributes;
    • generating conditional structures with specified properties;
    • sampling diverse high-reward candidates;
    • anonymizing or augmenting relational data; or
    • representing posterior uncertainty over a latent or causal structure.

    The evaluation unit must match the query. Whole-graph generation, link prediction, graph reconstruction, property optimization, and posterior calibration are not substitute leaderboards.

    A six-level evaluation protocol¤

    1. Representation and count: node-count distribution, type frequencies, padding leakage, and relabeling invariance.
    2. Local topology: degrees, wedges, clustering, small motifs, assortativity, and edge attributes.
    3. Global topology: components, distances, diameter, communities, spectra, homology, and higher-order motifs.
    4. Distributional fidelity: likelihood or bounds where meaningful, two-sample tests, precision/recall-like coverage, and uncertainty.
    5. Domain validity and utility: chemical, physical, program, circuit, social, or operational constraints and downstream decisions.
    6. Integrity and cost: novelty, memorization, privacy, contamination, compute, sampling failures, and post-processing.

    No one scalar spans all six.

    Statistic MMD: useful but conditional on its lens¤

    For graph descriptor \(\phi(G)\) and kernel \(k\), an empirical squared MMD compares real and generated samples. Its population value is

    \[ \operatorname{MMD}_k^2(P,Q) =\mathbb E_{P,P}k(\phi(G),\phi(G')) +\mathbb E_{Q,Q}k(\phi(H),\phi(H')) -2\mathbb E_{P,Q}k(\phi(G),\phi(H)). \]

    Degree, clustering, orbit, and spectral descriptors expose complementary discrepancies. Kernel bandwidth, histogram binning, normalization, sample size, and biased versus unbiased estimators materially change the result. O’Bray et al. document practical MMD pitfalls, and graph-kernel evaluation shows that kernels differ in which structural transitions they detect.

    Complete worked counterexample: degree matching is not distribution matching¤

    A six-cycle \(C_6\) and the disjoint union of two triangles both have six vertices and every vertex has degree two. A degree-histogram MMD between point masses on these graphs is zero for any kernel applied only to that histogram. Yet \(C_6\) is connected and triangle-free, while the other graph has two components and two triangles. The descriptor discarded the distinguishing information before MMD was computed.

    This does not make degree MMD useless. It makes its claim precise: the compared distributions agree under that chosen descriptor and kernel to the resolution supported by the samples.

    Learned and classifier-based graph metrics¤

    Graph embeddings from trained or random GNNs can capture attributes and structures missed by hand-crafted statistics, but they inherit architecture expressivity, training data, invariances, and task bias. A message-passing GNN may map non-isomorphic graphs to the same embedding; a powerful classifier may overfit a small test set.

    PolyGraph Discrepancy, ICLR 2026, fits classifiers on graph descriptors and combines their variational lower bounds on Jensen–Shannon divergence. This supplies a bounded, descriptor-aware comparison. It does not equal the full graph-distribution JS divergence unless the representation and classifier family achieve the relevant optimum. Report held-out classifier evaluation and uncertainty, not only training separability.

    Likelihood and calibration¤

    When likelihood is available, state whether it is for:

    • a labeled adjacency representation;
    • an isomorphism class;
    • one construction trace or a trajectory marginal;
    • a dequantized tensor;
    • an ELBO or importance-weighted bound; or
    • a pseudo/composite likelihood.

    Count calibration can be checked through predictive intervals or proper scores for \(N\). Conditional generators need property-calibration curves and constraint satisfaction over the requested condition distribution. Posterior graph models need coverage of graph functionals or credible sets, not only a high-scoring point estimate.

    Novelty, memorization, and privacy¤

    Exact graph isomorphism detects literal memorization for small or rigid graphs, but near-duplicate subgraphs, scaffold copies, motif templates, or identity-bearing attributes require domain-specific similarity. Compare generated graphs with train, validation, and external corpora; report nearest-neighbor distributions and overlap at multiple structural scales.

    Relational data can reveal membership and sensitive links even after names are removed. Node attributes and rare neighborhoods can re-identify individuals. A generator that preserves utility-relevant topology may also preserve identifying structure, so privacy needs a declared adversary and formal or empirical audit rather than an “anonymous graph” label.

    Scientific validity and causal restraint¤

    For molecules, report valence/sanitization, uniqueness, novelty, scaffold diversity, 3-D plausibility where applicable, property-predictor uncertainty, synthesizability, and prospective assays. For circuits or programs, execute or formally verify outputs. For infrastructure, test safety and constraints under simulation. For social graphs, inspect fairness, privacy, and observation bias.

    A generated graph that resembles observed networks is not a causal simulator. Homophily and influence can produce similar motifs; latent confounding and sampling can imitate dependence. Counterfactual network claims require mechanisms for interventions and interference, not only a fitted graph law.

    Chapter 6 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • “Several graph statistics match, so the graph laws match.” Every finite descriptor panel is a many-to-one representation unless an identification theorem is supplied for the declared family.
    • “A low validity failure rate proves scientific usefulness.” Syntactic graph validity, physical realizability, calibrated properties, prospective performance, and safety require separate tests.
    • “Held-out edges are IID test examples.” Dyads from one network share nodes, latent structure, and the collection mechanism; the estimand is not whole-graph generalization.
    • “A newer graph metric is an objective ground truth.” Hand-crafted and learned metrics retain descriptor, architecture, fitting, finite-sample, and implementation assumptions.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Enumerate all unlabeled simple graphs on four vertices and verify orbit sizes from automorphism groups.
    2. Fit \(G(n,p)\) and a two-block SBM to a small graph set; compare held-out graph likelihood and posterior predictive motifs.
    3. Enumerate the edge–triangle ERGM for \(n=4\) and locate parameter regions where mass concentrates near empty or complete graphs.
    4. Train an order-based graph model under one canonical order and random permutations; measure the variance and bias of an order-marginal estimator.
    5. Construct two graph distributions with equal degree and clustering summaries but different global connectivity, then test several kernels.
    6. Compare rejection, repair, and constraint-preserving construction for connected graphs; estimate both validity and target distortion.
    7. Audit a molecular benchmark for train/test scaffold overlap and report conclusions before and after de-duplication.
    8. Reproduce a 2026 graph metric or generator on an exact finite law where the true distribution is enumerable.
    Retrieval practice: evaluation
    1. Why can zero degree-MMD coexist with visibly different graph laws?
    2. What does a classifier-based variational JS score lower-bound?
    3. Which likelihood convention must be reported for an order-based model?
    4. Why are uniqueness and novelty insufficient for scientific generation?
    5. What distinguishes network simulation from causal simulation?

    Solutions. (1) The degree descriptor is many-to-one and discards other topology. (2) Divergence visible through the chosen descriptor and classifier family, at the achieved generalization optimum. (3) Whether the score is for one trace/order or marginalizes all traces/labelings yielding the graph. (4) They do not test validity, utility, physical realization, uncertainty, or prospective performance. (5) Causal simulation specifies intervention-responsive mechanisms and assumptions, not merely an observational graph distribution.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Reliable graph evaluation is multi-level, invariant to irrelevant labels, explicit about the sampling unit, and matched to the scientific query. Finite statistic panels and learned metrics are lenses rather than complete identifications of a graph law. Current research expands those lenses and generation mechanisms, but classical probability, exact small cases, domain baselines, and protocol disclosure remain indispensable.
