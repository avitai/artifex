    5. Causal Discovery, Bayesian Structure Learning, and GFlowNets¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Conditional independence, Bayesian model comparison, graph algorithms, and the GFlowNet volume.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Declare the discovery target: distinguish a DAG, CPDAG, PAG, interventional equivalence class, and posterior over structures.
    2. O2 — Match algorithms to assumptions: relate constraint, score, functional/noise, temporal, and interventional methods to their population guarantees.
    3. O3 — Audit continuous DAG optimization: separate exact acyclicity, penalty tolerance, thresholding, optimization, and causal identifiability.
    4. O4 — Interpret and calibrate graph posteriors: keep prior, score, parameter uncertainty, path multiplicity, and sampler error visible.
    5. O5 — Propagate structural uncertainty: average identified queries or report bounds/refusal when plausible structures do not identify them.
    6. O6 — Design targeted interventions: choose experiments for the downstream query while accounting for cost, safety, support, and misspecification.

    The target of discovery¤

    Causal discovery estimates a graph, an equivalence class, or a posterior over structures from observational and/or interventional data. It is not identical to effect estimation: one may identify an effect without identifying every edge, or learn a graph class while a desired counterfactual remains unidentified.

    It is also narrower than generic dependency-structure learning. A Gaussian precision graph or observational Bayesian network can encode conditional independences without assigning intervention semantics to an edge. The classical structure-learning chapter develops that observational problem and the boundary between dependency, random-graph, computation-graph, and causal targets.

    Constraint-based methods use conditional independences under Markov and faithfulness assumptions. Score-based methods optimize a penalized likelihood or Bayesian score. Functional causal models exploit restrictions such as additive noise or non-Gaussianity. Continuous differentiable methods relax acyclicity for optimization, but a smooth penalty does not remove statistical ambiguity.

    For a weighted adjacency matrix \(W\), NOTEARS-style methods use the smooth characterization

    \[ h(W)=\operatorname{tr}(e^{W\odot W})-d=0. \]

    This enforces acyclicity at an exact feasible solution; finite penalties/tolerances can leave cycles, thresholding can change the graph, and optimization can stop locally. Most importantly, acyclicity is a computational constraint, not evidence that the selected direction is statistically identifiable.

    Equivalence objects and algorithm families¤

    Under causal sufficiency, acyclicity, Markovness, faithfulness, and oracle conditional-independence information, PC-type search targets a CPDAG: directed edges are compelled within the observational Markov equivalence class and undirected edges remain reversible. With latent confounding or selection, FCI- type methods target a PAG whose endpoint marks summarize ancestral relations shared across a maximal ancestral graph equivalence class. A PAG is not a DAG with decorative edgeheads; it represents a different observed-variable uncertainty object.

    Score-equivalent search can target the same observational class under its score and model assumptions; functional/noise asymmetries can orient more edges only inside the restricted family. Known intervention targets define an interventional equivalence class, which can be smaller than the observational class without necessarily becoming a singleton. Unknown targets add another latent assignment problem.

    The discovery path in Causation, Prediction, and Search develops the conditional-independence and latent-variable foundations. The machine-learning path in Elements of Causal Inference connects those targets to restricted functional causal models. The different outputs should not be compared as though all methods promised one true DAG from the same information.

    Bayesian graph posterior¤

    For data \(D\) and graph \(G\),

    \[ p(G\mid D)\propto p(D\mid G)p(G). \]

    The marginal likelihood integrates mechanism parameters:

    \[ p(D\mid G)=\int p(D\mid \theta,G)p(\theta\mid G)d\theta. \]

    Returning one maximum-scoring graph discards structural uncertainty. Bayesian model averaging can propagate graph uncertainty into an effect:

    \[ p(y\mid do(x),D) =\sum_G p(y\mid do(x),G,D)p(G\mid D), \]

    provided the effect is defined and identified under the included graphs.

    The expression also suppresses parameter uncertainty:

    \[ p(y\mid do(x),D) =\sum_G\int p(y\mid do(x),\theta,G) p(\theta\mid G,D)p(G\mid D)d\theta. \]

    If some plausible graphs do not identify the query from available data, silently dropping them or plugging in an arbitrary mechanism understates uncertainty. Report partial identification, bounds, or assumption-conditional answers.

    DAG-GFlowNet¤

    DAG-GFlowNet constructs graphs edge by edge and trains a GFlowNet so terminal DAG probability is proportional to an unnormalized Bayesian score. This can amortize diverse posterior sampling rather than repeatedly running a separate MCMC chain.

    The construction requires a state representation, legal acyclic actions, a terminal reward, and balance training. It samples according to the supplied reward only to the degree optimization and exploration succeed. A posterior over graphs is conditional on the model class, prior, likelihood/score, and data; it is not proof that the high-probability arrows are causal truths.

    The terminal reward usually contains a Bayesian graph score, not prospective intervention utility. Path multiplicity must not distort graph mass, and graph-posterior calibration should be checked on enumerated small systems. Diversity of sampled DAGs is valuable only when their probabilities approximate the declared posterior.

    Worked three-node equivalence class¤

    Suppose data imply \(X\not\perp Z\) and \(X\perp Z\mid Y\). With no interventions or temporal knowledge, the chains

    \[ X\to Y\to Z, \qquad X\leftarrow Y\leftarrow Z, \qquad X\leftarrow Y\to Z \]

    share the same skeleton and no unshielded collider, so they are Markov equivalent. A discovery algorithm should not orient all edges confidently from these independences alone. An intervention on \(Y\), an ordering constraint, or a justified functional/noise assumption can add information.

    This class has the same skeleton and v-structures. An intervention on the middle node distinguishes fork-like from chain responses, but exactly which orientations become identifiable depends on intervention targets and whether interventions are perfect, soft, or unknown. Interventional data define their own equivalence classes rather than automatically revealing every arrow.

    Latent confounders and selection¤

    Unobserved common causes are represented by richer graph objects such as ancestral graphs and partial ancestral graphs. DiCoLa gives a recursive decomposition framework for constraint-based discovery with latent variables, with soundness/completeness inherited under its assumptions from the base discovery method. It is a computational framework, not a claim that latent confounding becomes harmless.

    Distributional equivalence in linear non-Gaussian latent-variable cyclic models illustrates the importance of characterizing what is recoverable before designing an algorithm. Its peer-reviewed conclusions are tied to linear non-Gaussian parametric structure.

    Unknown interventions and test-time adaptation¤

    Interventional data can orient edges, but real datasets may not label intervention targets and may differ from training tasks. Accepted ICML 2026 TICL combines test-time training with joint causal inference for structure and intervention-target detection. Its identifiability and empirical results belong to the stated interventional setting; they do not license target-free causal discovery for arbitrary uncontrolled domain shifts.

    Active Intervention Design¤

    When interventions can be chosen, discovery and generation form a sequential design problem. A policy may maximize expected information gain about graphs, reduce uncertainty in a target effect, or prioritize safety/expense. These objectives differ: orienting every edge may be wasteful if only one intervention response matters.

    A learned generator can simulate candidate experiments only under its current model posterior. If that posterior omits the true mechanism, simulated information gain can be overconfident. Evaluation should include intervention cost, posterior calibration, cumulative regret/information, and robustness to model misspecification.

    Diffusions and continuous-time causal structure¤

    Time order and stochastic dynamics can add structure, but dependence in time is not automatically causality. Stationary-diffusion causal modeling studies how properties of stationary stochastic differential equations can support causal modeling. Sampling trajectories from a fitted SDE and discovering an instantaneous causal graph are distinct tasks; discretization, latent processes, feedback, and observation frequency matter.

    Discovery-to-generation pipeline¤

    A careful workflow is:

    1. define variables and candidate intervention semantics;
    2. discover a graph/equivalence class under explicit assumptions;
    3. perform stability and sensitivity analyses;
    4. fit modular mechanisms for each candidate structure;
    5. validate on held-out interventions where available;
    6. average or report a range of causal queries across plausible structures;
    7. refuse an individual counterfactual when structural/exogenous uncertainty makes it unsupported.

    Jointly optimizing a graph and high-capacity generator can overfit: flexible mechanisms may make many graphs score similarly, while optimization relaxation can favor convenient rather than causal orientations.

    Chapter 5 retrieval Questions¤

    1. What does the NOTEARS acyclicity equality establish, and what does it not establish?
    2. Why can graph-posterior averaging still fail to identify a causal query?
    3. Should active discovery always maximize the number of oriented edges?
    Complete solutions
    1. At exact feasibility it establishes that the weighted graph is acyclic; it does not establish causal orientation, model fit, faithfulness, or global optimization.
    2. Some plausible graphs/mechanisms may disagree or leave the query unidentified; averaging arbitrary plug-ins hides that boundary.
    3. No. Targeted effect uncertainty, safety, intervention cost, or decision regret may be the relevant objective.

    Independent practice and assessment map: discovery and structural uncertainty¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. Preserve the declared discovery object throughout: a DAG answer is not interchangeable with a CPDAG, PAG, interventional equivalence class, or posterior over structures.

    1. P1.1 — Predict the equivalence-class output. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For three observed variables with skeleton \(X-Y-Z\), dependence \(X\not\perp Z\), and \(X\perp Z\mid Y\), list every DAG in the Markov equivalence class. State which edge marks an oracle PC procedure may return and what extra information could orient them.
    2. P1.2 — Audit a continuous acyclicity result. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Let \(W=\begin{bmatrix}0&a\\b&0\end{bmatrix}\). Derive \(h(W)=\operatorname{tr}(e^{W\odot W})-2\) and evaluate it at \(a=b=0.1\). Explain why a small value is not an acyclicity certificate.
    3. P1.3 — Propagate an incomplete graph posterior. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Three structures have posterior masses \((0.6,0.3,0.1)\). The target effect is identified as \(0.2\) and \(0.5\) in the first two; in the third it is only known to lie in \([-1,1]\). Compute (a) the normalized mean conditional on the identified structures, (b) a global posterior-weighted identified interval, and © the unsupported posterior mass that must remain visible.
    4. P1.4 — Diagnose a DAG-GFlowNet posterior error. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 13 min. A construction sequence adds one edge at a time. One terminal DAG has six legal construction orders and another has two, although both receive equal terminal score. Explain the path-multiplicity failure and specify a small-system calibration test.
    5. P1.5 — Design a query-targeted intervention. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. The only decision-relevant query is the effect of \(X\) on \(Y\), while the current equivalence class also contains uncertain edges among nuisance variables. Define an intervention-selection objective that prioritizes the query rather than the number of oriented edges. Include cost, safety, support, posterior misspecification, and a stopping rule.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer discovery uncertainty into a generator. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Observed variables may have latent confounders, and an FCI-style analysis returns a PAG. Design the downstream generative workflow without converting circles or bidirected relations into a single convenient DAG. State when it may average, bound, or refuse a causal query.

    Task Evidence of success

    P1.2 The exact two-node expression and numerical residual are separated from statistical identifiability.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The class is \(X\to Y\to Z\), \(X\leftarrow Y\leftarrow Z\), and \(X\leftarrow Y\to Z\). They have the same skeleton and no unshielded collider, so the CPDAG leaves both adjacencies undirected. An intervention with documented target semantics, temporal order, or a justified functional/noise restriction can shrink the class; observational conditional independences alone cannot. Check. \(X\to Y\leftarrow Z\) is excluded because it has a collider and implies the opposite conditioning pattern.
    2. P1.2. Here \(W\odot W=\begin{bmatrix}0&a^2\\b^2&0\end{bmatrix}\). Its even powers contribute equally to the two diagonal entries, so \(h(W)=2\cosh(|ab|)-2\). At \(a=b=0.1\), \(|ab|=0.01\) and \(h(W)=2\cosh(0.01)-2\approx0.000100000833\). Both directed edges are nonzero, hence the graph still contains a two-cycle. Check. Only exact \(h(W)=0\) certifies acyclicity; penalty tolerance, thresholding, and optimizer error require separate reporting.
    3. P1.3. Conditional on the \(0.9\) identified mass, the mean is \([0.6(0.2)+0.3(0.5)]/0.9=0.3\). Without discarding the third graph, the identified contribution is \(0.27\) and its interval contribution is \(0.1[-1,1]\), so the global set is \([0.17,0.37]\). Unsupported mass is \(0.1\). Check. Reporting only \(0.3\) answers a different, selection-conditional question.
    4. P1.4. If every construction trajectory receives flow independently without a balance condition that produces the terminal reward, the first graph can receive three times the mass solely because it has six rather than two paths. Enumerate every DAG and legal trajectory for a two- or three-node system, compute exact normalized terminal rewards, draw many samples, and compare empirical graph probabilities and credible-set coverage with the exact target. Rubric. Diversity alone is not calibration.
    5. P1.5. Define utility as expected reduction in posterior variance/identified-set width for the \(X\to Y\) effect, or expected reduction in downstream decision regret, minus intervention cost and safety risk. Restrict candidates to supported, feasible interventions; average utility over graph/mechanism uncertainty; stress-test plausible omitted models; and stop when no safe action has positive net utility or the query uncertainty is below the predeclared decision threshold. Check. The number of oriented nuisance edges is at most a diagnostic.
    6. P1.6. Preserve the PAG and its endpoint semantics, enumerate or sample compatible causal models only under explicit latent-variable and functional assumptions, and fit mechanisms within each. For each query, run an identification check: average compatible identified answers with calibrated weights, combine valid structure-conditional bounds conservatively, and retain/refuse posterior mass for unsupported models. Validate with held-out interventions and sensitivity to PAG completions. Rubric. Choosing one DAG for software convenience is not uncertainty propagation.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Causal discovery exposes uncertainty over structure; generative mechanisms expose uncertainty within a structure. GFlowNets and other posterior samplers can preserve diversity over graphs, but their result remains relative to a score and assumptions. Chapter 6 builds an evaluation contract that keeps those layers—and evidence maturity—visible.

    What does a posterior probability on an edge mean?

    It is probability under the chosen prior, likelihood/score, model class, and data. It is not a frequency-free certificate that the edge is a real-world cause.
