    6. Likelihood Is a Function of Parameters¤

    Likelihood and finite-example learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes densities, conditioning, and change of variables. By the end, you should be able to distinguish a sampling distribution from its likelihood function, relate empirical log loss to population cross-entropy, state why dequantization changes the continuous-density target, work the two-bit joint and conditional example, compute a one-dimensional Jacobian correction, and sum all inverse branches of a many-to-one transformation.

    Same expression, different argument

    With \(\theta\) fixed, \(p_\theta(x)\) is a law over possible \(x\). With the observed \(x\) fixed, the same numeric expression is a likelihood over candidate \(\theta\). It does not become a probability distribution over \(\theta\) until a parameter prior is supplied and the result is normalized.

    For observed data \(x\), the expression \(p_\theta(x)\) is a probability mass or density as a function of \(x\), but a likelihood as a function of \(\theta\) with \(x\) fixed:

    \[ \mathcal L(\theta;x)=p_\theta(x). \]

    Maximum likelihood chooses

    \[ \hat\theta=\arg\max_\theta\sum_{n=1}^{N}\log p_\theta(x^{(n)}). \]

    Likelihood is not a posterior over parameters unless a parameter prior is supplied and Bayes' rule is applied. It is also not automatically comparable across different data preprocessing, quantization, reference measures, or dimensionalities.

    Empirical and Population Risk¤

    Maximum likelihood uses the empirical objective

    \[ \hat\theta_N\in\arg\max_\theta\frac1N\sum_{n=1}^{N}\log p_\theta(x_n). \]

    Consistency requires assumptions: the data-generating law must be represented or suitably approximated, the parameterization must be identifiable enough for the target, optimization must find an appropriate solution, and regularity conditions must hold. Deep generative training violates simple textbook assumptions in multiple ways, so “MLE is consistent” should never substitute for held-out evaluation.

    Always state whether a loss is summed or averaged over batch, token, pixel, channel, and latent dimensions. A “typical” NLL, KL, or reconstruction-loss threshold does not transfer across different reductions.

    Dequantization¤

    For an integer pixel \(x\in\{0,\ldots,255\}^d\), define a continuous variable \(y=(x+u)/256\) with \(u\sim q(u\mid x)\) supported in \([0,1)^d\). Uniform dequantization gives a lower bound on the discrete mass assigned to each bin. Variational dequantization learns \(q\) and includes its log density in the bound. Adding noise without normalization or without matching the likelihood calculation changes the modeled variable.

    Worked Example: A Two-Bit Distribution¤

    Suppose

    Table PROB.4. Worked Example: A Two-Bit Distribution
    \((x_1,x_2)\) Probability
    \((0,0)\) 0.4
    \((0,1)\) 0.1
    \((1,0)\) 0.2
    \((1,1)\) 0.3

    Then \(p(x_1=0)=0.5\) and

    \[ p(x_2=1\mid x_1=0)=\frac{0.1}{0.5}=0.2, \qquad p(x_2=1\mid x_1=1)=\frac{0.3}{0.5}=0.6. \]

    The autoregressive factorization reconstructs, for example,

    \[ p(1,1)=p(x_1=1)p(x_2=1\mid x_1=1)=0.5\times0.6=0.3. \]

    Reversing the order gives a different set of conditionals but the same exact joint. A learned finite-capacity model may approximate one ordering more easily than the other.

    Worked Example: Change of Variables in One Dimension¤

    Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and \(X=Z^2\). The map is monotone on the support, with inverse \(z=\sqrt{x}\) and derivative \(dz/dx=(2\sqrt{x})^{-1}\). Therefore

    \[ p_X(x)=p_Z(\sqrt{x})\left|\frac{d\sqrt{x}}{dx}\right| =\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}},\qquad 0<x<1. \]

    The density diverges near zero but is valid because

    \[ \int_0^1\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}dx=1. \]

    This demonstrates three recurring facts: densities may exceed one, support must be transformed with the map, and the inverse Jacobian appears when the generative direction is \(z\mapsto x\). The normalizing-flow guide generalizes this calculation to learned multidimensional bijections.

    A Many-to-One Map Must Sum Its Branches¤

    Now let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(-1,1)\) and again set \(X=Z^2\). For \(0<x<1\), the output \(x\) has two preimages, \(z_1=\sqrt{x}\) and \(z_2=-\sqrt{x}\). For a differentiable scalar map \(g\) with finitely many regular preimages,

    \[ p_X(x)= \sum_{z\in g^{-1}(x)} \frac{p_Z(z)}{|g'(z)|}. \]

    Here each branch contributes \((1/2)/(2\sqrt{x})\), so

    \[ p_X(x)=\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}, \qquad 0<x<1. \]

    Keeping only the positive root would integrate to one half and lose the mass carried by the negative branch. If a map changes dimension or collapses a region to a point, an ordinary output density with respect to ambient Lebesgue measure may not exist at all; the pushforward law remains the correct object. Monte Carlo samples approximate that law by an empirical measure, not by an ordinary PDF unless an additional density estimator is introduced.

    Probability Integral Transforms Need an Atom Check¤

    If \(F\) is a continuous CDF and \(X\sim F\), then \(F(X)\) is uniform on \((0,1)\). With atoms, \(F(X)\) contains atoms too and is not uniform. A randomized transform works for discrete, continuous, and mixed univariate laws:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Delta F(X)&=F(X)-F(X^-),\\ U&=F(X^-)+V\,\Delta F(X). \end{aligned} \]

    Here \(V\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) is independent of \(X\). For \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(p)\), samples with \(X=0\) fill \([0,1-p]\) and samples with \(X=1\) fill \([1-p,1]\); the interval lengths and event probabilities match, so their mixture is uniform.

    The inverse direction uses the generalized quantile \(F^{-1}(u)=\inf\{x:F(x)\ge u\}\) and can generate samples even when \(F\) has jumps. These two directions should not be conflated: generalized-inverse sampling works for atomic laws, while the unrandomized diagnostic \(F(X)\) is uniform only under the relevant continuity condition. A calibrated goodness-of-fit test additionally needs its own null distribution and treatment of fitted parameters; plotting transformed values is not by itself a test.

    Likelihood and transformation retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: likelihood and transformed variables
    1. Over which argument must a likelihood normalize, and over which argument need it not normalize?
    2. What population quantity is minimized by expected negative log likelihood under a fixed representation?
    3. Which term appears in a one-to-one change-of-variables density?
    4. Why must a many-to-one map sum over inverse branches?

    Solutions. (1) The sampling density normalizes over observations for each parameter; the likelihood need not normalize over parameters. (2) Cross-entropy to the data law, equivalently KL up to the data entropy when defined. (3) The absolute inverse-Jacobian determinant, or the negative log absolute forward determinant. (4) Every preimage is a mutually exclusive route to the same observed value and contributes probability mass/density.

    Likelihood and transformation synthesis¤

    Likelihood is the observed-data law viewed as a function of parameters. Its empirical optimization, population target, dequantization convention, and base measure must remain aligned. Change of variables preserves probability through Jacobians and, for non-injective maps, an explicit sum over every valid branch.
