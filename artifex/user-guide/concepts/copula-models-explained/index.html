    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A copula model separates two questions that an ordinary joint distribution answers at once:

    1. how is each coordinate distributed by itself; and
    2. how do the coordinates vary together after their marginal scales have been removed?

    The second object is a probability distribution on the unit hypercube with uniform one-dimensional marginals. This is the copula. Combining it with declared marginal distributions produces a joint law. The separation supports rank-based dependence analysis, tail-risk modeling, conditional simulation, mixed-data models, marginal replacement, and neural dependence estimators. It also creates obligations that are easy to miss: discontinuous marginals destroy global uniqueness, estimated marginals contribute uncertainty, and a network whose outputs merely lie in \([0,1]^d\) is not necessarily a copula.

    This volume develops those obligations from first principles. It starts with Sklar's theorem and finite examples, studies classical copula families and tail behavior, derives estimation and conditional queries, builds high-dimensional pair-copula constructions, treats discrete and mixed observations, and then places implicit, classifier, flow, and diffusion copulas inside the same probability contract. The final chapter gives a model-checking protocol that tests both marginal uniformity and dependence rather than hiding one error inside the other.

    Curriculum position

    Read Probability and Generative Modeling for CDFs, densities, conditional laws, and generalized inverses. Objectives and Statistical Distances supplies likelihood, density-ratio, and two-sample objectives. Flows and Diffusion Models supply reusable parameterizations; neither automatically satisfies the uniform-marginal contract. The concise Extended Generative Landscape remains the orientation route.

    1. The Copula as a Generative Object¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes univariate and multivariate CDFs, densities, generalized inverses, and conditional probability. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Validate the copula object: check cube support, CDF volume, boundary values, and uniform one-dimensional marginals.
    2. O2 — Apply Sklar's theorem precisely: separate existence from whole-cube uniqueness for continuous, discrete, and mixed marginals.
    3. O3 — Derive the density factorization: identify the copula density as a joint-to-product density ratio under the required regularity.
    4. O4 — Execute both directions of the construction: transform observations to the copula scale and copula samples back through generalized quantiles.
    5. O5 — Diagnose invariance claims: distinguish marginal replacement, monotone reparameterization, dependence stability, and causal intervention.
    6. O6 — Transfer the semantic contract: specify a valid copula model for a new continuous, discrete, censored, or mixed observation system.

    Definition before parameterization¤

    A \(d\)-dimensional copula is a CDF \(C:[0,1]^d\to[0,1]\) whose one-dimensional marginals are uniform. Equivalently, if \(U\sim C\), then

    \[ U_j\sim \operatorname{Uniform}(0,1), \qquad j=1,\ldots,d. \]

    As a CDF, \(C\) must be grounded, \(d\)-increasing, and have the correct boundary values. In two dimensions these requirements include

    \[ C(u,0)=C(0,v)=0, \qquad C(u,1)=u, \qquad C(1,v)=v, \]

    and nonnegative rectangle volume

    \[ \Delta C =C(u_2,v_2)-C(u_1,v_2)-C(u_2,v_1)+C(u_1,v_1)\ge0 \]

    whenever \(u_1\le u_2\) and \(v_1\le v_2\). A smooth candidate additionally needs a nonnegative mixed derivative \(c(u,v)=\partial^2 C/(\partial u\,\partial v)\) and uniform marginal integrals. Merely mapping points into the cube proves none of these facts.

    Sklar's theorem, with its uniqueness clause¤

    For every joint CDF \(F\) with marginals \(F_1,\ldots,F_d\), there exists a copula \(C\) such that

    \[ \boxed{F(x_1,\ldots,x_d) =C\!\left(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d)\right).} \]

    If all marginals are continuous, \(C\) is unique on the whole cube. In general it is uniquely determined only on

    \[ \operatorname{Ran}(F_1)\times\cdots\times\operatorname{Ran}(F_d), \]

    and can have multiple extensions elsewhere. Conversely, combining any copula with univariate CDFs through the displayed equation produces a valid joint CDF with those marginals.

    This is an algebraic decomposition of a probability law. It does not say that estimating the pieces separately is always statistically efficient, that the pieces are independent parameters, or that dependence is causally invariant when marginals change.

    Density factorization¤

    Suppose \(F\) and all \(F_j\) are absolutely continuous with densities \(f\) and \(f_j\), and the copula has density \(c\). Repeated differentiation gives

    \[ \boxed{ f(x_1,\ldots,x_d) =c\!\left(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d)\right) \prod_{j=1}^{d}f_j(x_j). } \]

    Thus \(c\) is the density ratio between the joint law and the product of its marginals:

    \[ c(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d)) =\frac{f(x_1,\ldots,x_d)}{\prod_j f_j(x_j)}. \]

    The equation explains why copula likelihood, mutual information, and density-ratio estimation meet. It also explains why a poor marginal fit contaminates apparent dependence: the inputs to \(c\) and the denominator are both wrong.

    The generative program¤

    Given a copula \(C\) and marginal CDFs \(F_j\):

    1. sample \(U\sim C\);
    2. set \(X_j=F_j^{-1}(U_j)\) for every coordinate.

    Then \(X\) has joint CDF \(F(x)=C(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d))\). The generalized inverse handles atoms; it need not be a smooth bijection.

    Replacing \(F_j\) while holding \(C\) fixed defines a new joint distribution. It is a modeling intervention on the distribution, not automatically a causal intervention on a data-generating mechanism.

    Probability-integral transforms¤

    If \(F_j\) is continuous and \(X_j\sim F_j\), then \(U_j=F_j(X_j)\) is uniform. If \(F_j\) has jumps, \(F_j(X_j)\) is generally not uniform. A randomized distributional transform restores uniformity:

    \[ \boxed{ U_j=F_j(X_j^-)+V_j\bigl(F_j(X_j)-F_j(X_j^-)\bigr), \qquad V_j\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1). } \]

    Conditioned on an atom \(X_j=x\), the transform spreads its mass uniformly across the interval \((F_j(x^-),F_j(x)]\). The auxiliary randomization is part of the construction. Different treatments of it can imply different latent copula stories while producing the same observed discrete law.

    Complete worked example: a valid dependence density¤

    The bivariate Farlie–Gumbel–Morgenstern family is

    \[ C_\theta(u,v)=uv\left[1+\theta(1-u)(1-v)\right], \qquad -1\le\theta\le1. \]

    Its density is

    \[ c_\theta(u,v)=1+\theta(1-2u)(1-2v). \]

    For \(\theta\in[-1,1]\), the product \((1-2u)(1-2v)\) lies in \([-1,1]\), so \(c_\theta\ge0\). Moreover,

    \[ \int_0^1 c_\theta(u,v)\,dv =1+\theta(1-2u)\int_0^1(1-2v)\,dv=1, \]

    and symmetrically for the other coordinate. Therefore both marginals are uniform. At \(\theta=0\), \(c=1\) and the coordinates are independent. Even at \(|\theta|=1\), however, this family supports only modest rank dependence; a valid copula family need not cover every dependence pattern.

    Now choose \(X_1=\Phi^{-1}(U_1)\) and \(X_2=-\log(1-U_2)\). The resulting marginals are standard normal and unit exponential, while their dependence remains \(C_\theta\). This example separates marginal shape from dependence without claiming that the fitted \(\theta\) is invariant across environments.

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    2. P1.2 — Compute a copula rectangle. Type: derive · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. For the FGM copula with \(\theta=1\), compute the probability of \((U_1,U_2)\in(0,1/2]\times(0,1/2]\) and compare it with independence. Explain what this one calculation does not prove.
    3. P1.3 — Locate discrete identifiability. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Let \(X,Y\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) and \(\Pr(X=1,Y=1)=0.35\). State the interior copula value identified by the table and whether \(C(0.6,0.6)\) is identified.
    4. P1.4 — Evaluate the density factorization. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Use the FGM model with arbitrary valid \(\theta\), a standard-normal first marginal, and a unit-exponential second marginal. Compute the joint density at \(x_1=0\) and \(x_2=\log2\), and explain why the answer does not imply independence.
    5. P1.5 — Verify a randomized PIT. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. For \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/4)\), write the conditional distributional transform for \(X=0\) and \(X=1\), then prove by interval masses that the unconditional transform is uniform.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 18 min. Specify a copula contract for a zero-inflated laboratory measurement paired with a continuous biomarker. Check. The intervals partition \((0,1]\), so \(U\) is uniform.

One valid answer models a point mass at zero plus a positive continuous laboratory tail, a continuous biomarker marginal, and a latent copula scale obtained with explicit independent distributional-transform variables for the atom. The observed likelihood uses the corresponding rectangle/derivative contribution; generation samples \(U\sim C\) and applies generalized quantiles. The off-grid copula extension is not identified by the zero-inflated observations. A query might be the probability of a zero laboratory value jointly with an extreme biomarker. Full credit covers all six requested layers and does not call marginal replacement causal.

    Counterexamples and misconception checks¤

    • Cube-valued is not copula-valued. If \(U_1=U_2=Z^2\) for \(Z\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), both coordinates lie in \([0,1]\) but have CDF \(\sqrt u\), not the uniform CDF.
    • Zero correlation is not independence. A copula can encode nonlinear or tail dependence while Pearson correlation is zero.
    • A discrete copula is not globally identified. Bernoulli marginals constrain \(C\) only at four range-grid points; many continuous-cube extensions agree there.
    • Marginal replacement is not automatically causal. Holding a copula fixed under a policy or mechanism change is an extra invariance assumption.
    Retrieval questions
    1. Which part of Sklar's theorem changes when a marginal has atoms?
    2. Why does \(F(X)\) fail to be uniform for a Bernoulli variable?
    3. What two properties, beyond cube support, must a smooth neural density satisfy to be a copula density?
    4. Which operation turns a copula sample into an observation with declared marginals?

    Solutions. (1) The copula remains existent but is unique only on the product of marginal ranges, not necessarily on the whole cube. (2) Its CDF jumps, so \(F(X)\) takes only the jump endpoints. (3) Nonnegativity and unit marginal integrals, together with normalization implied by them. (4) Coordinatewise generalized inverse CDFs.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A copula is a probability distribution with uniform marginals, not a synonym for dependence score or a generic bounded generator. Sklar's theorem separates a joint CDF into marginals and a copula, uniquely on the whole cube only for continuous marginals. The density factorization and quantile sampling program make the separation computational; the discontinuous case makes its identifiability boundary unavoidable.

    Why Chapter 2 follows. Once the object is fixed, we can compare families by the dependence patterns, symmetries, and tail events they can represent.

    2. Classical Families, Rank Dependence, and Tails¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and elementary multivariate Gaussian and Student distributions. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Use reference copulas and bounds correctly: distinguish independence, comonotonicity, bivariate countermonotonicity, and invalid higher-dimensional lower envelopes.
    2. O2 — Compare elliptical dependence: translate correlation parameters into rank and tail behavior for Gaussian and Student-\(t\) copulas.
    3. O3 — Audit Archimedean validity: connect generator conditions, dimension, exchangeability, rotations, and asymmetric tail dependence.

    Bounds and reference copulas¤

    Every \(d\)-copula obeys the Fréchet–Hoeffding bounds

    \[ \max\!\left(1-d+\sum_{j=1}^d u_j,0\right) \le C(u_1,\ldots,u_d)\le \min_j u_j. \]

    The upper bound \(M(u)=\min_j u_j\) is a copula in every dimension and represents comonotonic variables \(U_1=\cdots=U_d\). The lower bound is the countermonotone copula \(W(u,v)=\max(u+v-1,0)\) only in two dimensions; for \(d\ge3\) the displayed lower envelope is generally not a copula. There is no universal multivariate analogue in which every pair can be perfectly countermonotone.

    The independence copula

    \[ \Pi(u_1,\ldots,u_d)=\prod_{j=1}^d u_j \]

    has density one. These references are useful exact baselines, but real dependence need not lie on a one-dimensional path between them.

    Elliptical copulas¤

    For correlation matrix \(R\), the Gaussian copula is

    \[ C_R^{\mathrm G}(u) =\Phi_R\!\left(\Phi^{-1}(u_1),\ldots,\Phi^{-1}(u_d)\right), \]

    where \(\Phi_R\) is a centered multivariate normal CDF with correlation \(R\). The marginal variances and means disappear under the transform; only \(R\) remains. Sampling is simple: draw \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,R)\) and set \(U_j=\Phi(Z_j)\).

    For a nondegenerate bivariate Gaussian copula with \(|\rho|<1\),

    \[ \lambda_L=\lambda_U=0. \]

    This does not mean the variables are independent. It says that asymptotic co-exceedance vanishes at the tail limit. Finite-threshold joint risk can still be substantial.

    The Student-\(t\) copula replaces the Gaussian vector by a standardized multivariate \(t\) vector with degrees of freedom \(\nu\). In the bivariate case its symmetric tail coefficient is

    \[ \boxed{ \lambda_L=\lambda_U =2t_{\nu+1}\!\left( -\sqrt{\frac{(\nu+1)(1-\rho)}{1+\rho}} \right), } \]

    where \(t_{\nu+1}\) is a univariate Student-\(t\) CDF. Shared radial scale variation creates joint extremes even when ordinary correlation matches a Gaussian copula.

    Archimedean copulas¤

    An Archimedean construction uses a decreasing generator \(\psi:[0,\infty)\to[0,1]\):

    \[ C(u_1,\ldots,u_d) =\psi\!\left(\psi^{-1}(u_1)+\cdots+\psi^{-1}(u_d)\right). \]

    Not every decreasing function is valid in every dimension. The precise finite-dimensional requirement is \(d\)-monotonicity under the standard generator conventions; complete monotonicity supports all dimensions through a Laplace-transform representation. This constraint is central when a neural network parameterizes \(\psi\).

    For the bivariate Clayton family,

    \[ C_\theta(u,v) =\left(u^{-\theta}+v^{-\theta}-1\right)^{-1/\theta}, \qquad \theta>0, \]

    with

    \[ \lambda_L=2^{-1/\theta}, \qquad \lambda_U=0. \]

    It emphasizes lower-tail association. The Gumbel family instead has upper-tail dependence and zero lower-tail dependence under its usual positive-dependence parameter range. Rotations of bivariate copulas move tail behavior among corners, but a rotation also changes the interpretation of concordance and should be reported.

    Archimedean families are parsimonious and interpretable. A single exchangeable generator is often too restrictive in high dimensions; nested or hierarchical constructions broaden the structure but require compatibility conditions among generators.

    Extreme-value copulas¤

    Bivariate extreme-value copulas can be written

    \[ C(u,v)=\exp\{-\ell(-\log u,-\log v)\}, \]

    where the stable tail-dependence function \(\ell\) is convex, homogeneous of order one, and satisfies

    \[ \max(x,y)\le\ell(x,y)\le x+y. \]

    These copulas arise as limits for componentwise maxima and encode max-stability:

    \[ C(u^{1/m},v^{1/m})^m=C(u,v). \]

    An arbitrary tail-dependent copula is not necessarily an extreme-value copula. Max-stability is the distinguishing structural property.

    Rank dependence is copula dependence—under continuity¤

    For continuous bivariate variables, Kendall's tau and Spearman's rho depend only on \(C\):

    \[ \tau=4\int_{[0,1]^2}C(u,v)\,dC(u,v)-1, \]
    \[ \rho_S=12\int_0^1\!\int_0^1 \bigl(C(u,v)-uv\bigr)\,du\,dv. \]

    Strictly increasing marginal transformations preserve the copula and these rank measures. Ties change their empirical and population normalization; formulas from the continuous case cannot simply be reused for count data.

    Complete worked example: equal correlation, unequal extremes¤

    Choose a Gaussian copula and a \(t\) copula with the same \(\rho=0.5\). For the Gaussian copula, \(\lambda_U=0\). For the \(t\) copula with \(\nu=4\),

    \[ \lambda_U =2t_5\!\left(-\sqrt{\frac{5(1-0.5)}{1+0.5}}\right) =2t_5(-\sqrt{5/3})\approx0.253. \]

    Both models can have similar central elliptical contours and the same linear correlation parameter, yet the limiting probability of a joint upper-tail event is qualitatively different. A validation set concentrated near the center may fail to distinguish them; a tail-risk query can distinguish them immediately.

    Three estimation regimes¤

    Let marginal parameters be \(\alpha=(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_d)\) and copula parameters be \(\theta\). For continuous observations \(x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(n)}\), the full log likelihood is

    \[ \ell(\alpha,\theta) =\sum_{r=1}^n\left[ \log c_\theta\!\left(F_{1,\alpha_1}(x_1^{(r)}),\ldots,F_{d,\alpha_d}(x_d^{(r)})\right) +\sum_{j=1}^d\log f_{j,\alpha_j}(x_j^{(r)}) \right]. \]

    Three common workflows make different trade-offs:

    1. Full maximum likelihood jointly estimates \(\alpha\) and \(\theta\). It can be efficient under correct specification but couples optimization and model error.
    2. Inference functions for margins (IFM) fits each marginal first, then fits \(\theta\) to the resulting transforms. It is computationally modular, not identical to joint MLE.
    3. Rank or pseudo-likelihood estimation uses pseudo-observations such as \(\hat u_j^{(r)}=R_j^{(r)}/(n+1)\) and estimates dependence semiparametrically. Dividing by \(n+1\) keeps points away from singular boundaries; other conventions have different finite-sample effects.

    Standard errors must reflect the selected regime. Treating estimated pseudo-observations as known IID uniforms generally understates uncertainty. Bootstrap procedures must refit the stages whose uncertainty is being claimed.

    Conditional laws from copula derivatives¤

    For a continuously differentiable bivariate copula,

    \[ h_{2\mid1}(v\mid u) =\Pr(U_2\le v\mid U_1=u) =\frac{\partial C(u,v)}{\partial u}. \]

    If \(h_{2\mid1}(\cdot\mid u)\) is invertible, conditional simulation uses

    \[ W\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1), \qquad U_2=h_{2\mid1}^{-1}(W\mid u). \]

    For observed-scale conditioning \(X_1=x\), set \(u=F_1(x)\) and finally \(X_2=F_2^{-1}(U_2)\). Conditioning on an interval, atom, censored value, or noisy observation requires integrating the appropriate likelihood; substituting a point derivative can be invalid.

    The Rosenblatt transform¤

    For an ordered continuous vector with evaluable conditional CDFs, define

    \[ Z_1=F_1(X_1), \qquad Z_j=F_{j\mid1:j-1}(X_j\mid X_{1:j-1}), \quad j=2,\ldots,d. \]

    Under the correct joint model and regularity conditions, \(Z_1,\ldots,Z_d\) are independent uniforms. Applying \(\Phi^{-1}\) yields independent standard normals. This gives a multivariate residual diagnostic and a constructive sampler through the inverse triangular map.

    The transform depends on coordinate order. Correct models yield independent uniforms for every valid order, but finite estimates and misspecification can make diagnostics order-sensitive. Testing only the one-dimensional histograms misses residual dependence.

    Density-ratio estimation¤

    On the copula scale, the independent reference density is one. Hence

    \[ c(u)=\frac{p_U(u)}{\prod_j p_{U_j}(u_j)}=p_U(u). \]

    Generate negative examples by independently permuting coordinates or sampling from the product of fitted marginals. With equal class priors, the Bayes-optimal probability \(h^*(u)=\Pr(Y=1\mid u)\) of labeling a point as dependent satisfies

    \[ \boxed{c(u)=\frac{h^*(u)}{1-h^*(u)}.} \]

    This is a population-optimum correspondence. A finite classifier can be miscalibrated, class priors change the ratio formula, negative sampling can leak dependence, and an estimated ratio need not integrate to one or have uniform marginal integrals. Density evaluation and sampling from the ratio are separate computational problems.

    Missingness, censoring, and partial observations¤

    If \(O\) indexes observed coordinates and \(M\) missing coordinates, the observed-data likelihood is

    \[ p(x_O)=\int p(x_O,x_M)\,dx_M. \]

    For a continuous copula model, this becomes a lower-dimensional marginal copula contribution after integrating the missing coordinates. Censoring contributes CDF differences or derivatives according to the observation region. The missingness mechanism remains a separate assumption: integrating unobserved values is valid for the declared observation model, not proof that informative missingness can be ignored.

    Complete worked example: condition an FGM copula¤

    For

    \[ C_\theta(u,v)=uv[1+\theta(1-u)(1-v)], \]

    differentiate with respect to \(u\):

    \[ h_{2\mid1}(v\mid u) =v\left[1+\theta(1-v)(1-2u)\right]. \]

    Take \(\theta=1\) and \(u=0.8\). Then

    \[ h(v\mid0.8)=v[1-0.6(1-v)]=0.4v+0.6v^2. \]

    To obtain the conditional median, solve \(0.4v+0.6v^2=0.5\):

    \[ v=\frac{-0.4+\sqrt{0.16+1.2}}{1.2} \approx0.6385. \]

    The conditional copula-scale median exceeds \(0.5\), consistent with positive association given a high \(u\). If \(X_2\) has exponential marginal \(F_2^{-1}(v)=-\log(1-v)\), the observed-scale median is approximately \(1.018\). The conditional quantile needs both the copula and the marginal.

    From the chain rule to pair copulas¤

    For three continuous copula-scale variables, one valid decomposition is

    \[ \boxed{ c(u_1,u_2,u_3) =c_{12}(u_1,u_2)c_{23}(u_2,u_3) c_{13\mid2}\!\left( F_{1\mid2}(u_1\mid u_2), F_{3\mid2}(u_3\mid u_2);u_2 \right). } \]

    The first tree models unconditional pairs. The next tree couples conditional probability-integral transforms. In \(d\) dimensions, a regular vine organizes \(d(d-1)/2\) bivariate copulas across \(d-1\) linked trees, subject to the proximity condition that adjacent higher-tree nodes share the required conditioning structure.

    C-vines emphasize one hub variable at each level; D-vines emphasize path-like sequences; R-vines allow general regular-vine structures. These are factorization structures, not causal graphs.

    The simplifying assumption¤

    In the exact formula, the conditional copula may depend directly on the conditioning value \(u_2\):

    \[ C_{13\mid2}(a,b;u_2). \]

    A simplified vine assumes this direct dependence is absent, leaving a fixed pair-copula family \(C_{13\mid2}(a,b)\). The assumption can make likelihood, selection, and simulation tractable. It is not part of the pair-copula identity and need not hold for the data-generating law.

    A conditional association that changes sign with \(u_2\) is an immediate counterexample to a constant conditional copula. Adding more flexible bivariate families cannot repair a dependence on the omitted conditioning value unless that dependence is modeled.

    Likelihood, h-functions, and simulation¤

    Each pair factor contributes a log density. Conditional arguments are computed recursively with h-functions such as

    \[ h_{1\mid2}(u_1\mid u_2) =\frac{\partial C_{12}(u_1,u_2)}{\partial u_2}. \]

    The same recursion supports inverse Rosenblatt sampling. Computational cost is influenced by dimension, tree structure, pair-family evaluation, parameter sharing, truncation, and whether non-simplified conditional dependence is modeled. Quoting only the number of pair factors is not a runtime analysis.

    Structure and family selection¤

    A common greedy workflow selects early-tree edges using empirical dependence strength, selects pair families by an information criterion or held-out score, then propagates conditional transforms to later trees. This is a heuristic search through a very large joint space. Early errors change later pseudo-observations and cannot always be repaired downstream.

    Principled reporting includes:

    • candidate structures and pair families;
    • selection criterion and held-out split;
    • treatment of rotations, independence factors, and ties;
    • simplifying-assumption diagnostics;
    • optimization restarts and parameter constraints; and
    • sensitivity to ordering or structure search.

    Truncation and sparsity¤

    Truncating after tree level \(k\) sets later conditional pair copulas to independence. This reduces parameters and computation, and can regularize small samples. It is a structural approximation: conditional dependencies beyond level \(k\) are declared absent in the fitted model.

    Sparse graphical copula models instead impose conditional independences through a graph, while factor copulas use a small latent vector to induce dependence. Vines, sparse graphs, and factors move complexity differently:

    Complete worked example: a three-variable Gaussian vine¤

    For a Gaussian copula, choose correlations \(\rho_{12}=0.6\), \(\rho_{23}=0.5\), and conditional correlation \(\rho_{13\mid2}=0\). The implied marginal correlation is

    \[ \rho_{13} =\rho_{12}\rho_{23} +\rho_{13\mid2}\sqrt{(1-\rho_{12}^2)(1-\rho_{23}^2)} =0.3. \]

    The correlation matrix is

    \[ R=\begin{pmatrix} 1&0.6&0.3\\ 0.6&1&0.5\\ 0.3&0.5&1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

    Its determinant is

    \[ 1+2(0.6)(0.5)(0.3)-0.6^2-0.5^2-0.3^2=0.48>0, \]

    so it is positive definite. The zero conditional correlation makes the last Gaussian pair factor an independence copula; \(U_1\) and \(U_3\) remain marginally dependent through \(U_2\). Truncation expresses conditional, not marginal, independence.

    Exact identity versus approximation ledger¤

    Only the first two arrows are identities. The simplifying assumption, search procedure, family library, and truncation level are separate approximations and must not be attributed to “the vine decomposition” as if they were the same mathematical claim.

    Discrete likelihood uses rectangle probabilities¤

    For discrete bivariate \(X,Y\) with CDFs \(F_X,F_Y\), the probability of the cell \((x,y)\) is

    \[ \boxed{ \begin{aligned} p(x,y)={}&C(F_X(x),F_Y(y)) -C(F_X(x^-),F_Y(y))\\ &-C(F_X(x),F_Y(y^-)) +C(F_X(x^-),F_Y(y^-)). \end{aligned} } \]

    This is a CDF rectangle volume, not a continuous copula density evaluated at ranks. In \(d\) dimensions the inclusion–exclusion sum has up to \(2^d\) corners, motivating latent-variable, composite-likelihood, or structured approximations.

    When marginals are discrete, different whole-cube copulas can agree at every marginal range-grid point and induce the same observed PMF. A learned latent copula can still be useful for simulation or interpretation, but those off-grid features are not identified from the observed joint table without extra assumptions.

    Complete worked example: Bernoulli dependence¤

    Let \(X,Y\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/2)\) marginally and write \(q=\Pr(X=1,Y=1)\). The joint table is

    Table COP.4. Complete worked example: Bernoulli dependence
    \(Y=0\) \(Y=1\)
    \(X=0\) \(q\) \(1/2-q\)
    \(X=1\) \(1/2-q\) \(q\)

    with \(0\le q\le1/2\). For the \((1,1)\) cell,

    \[ q =1-C(1/2,1)-C(1,1/2)+C(1/2,1/2) =C(1/2,1/2). \]

    Thus the table identifies \(C(1/2,1/2)=q\), plus boundary values, but not the shape of \(C\) between those points. At \(q=1/4\) the variables are independent; at \(q=1/2\) they agree perfectly. Infinitely many valid continuous-cube extensions can share either table.

    Distributional transforms and latent jitter¤

    The randomized transform from Chapter 1 produces uniform marginals even for atoms. It can support a continuous latent model, but the random variables \(V_j\) and their dependence convention matter. Independent jitter is a declared augmentation; deterministic mid-ranks are not exactly uniform; learned dequantization introduces another conditional distribution. Evaluation must return to the observed discrete likelihood or observed-scale queries rather than validating only the augmented latent samples.

    For mixed data, each coordinate may use a different observation model:

    • continuous exact transform;
    • interval transform for counts or categories;
    • censoring interval for limits of detection;
    • point mass plus continuous tail for zero-inflated variables; or
    • ordinal thresholds on a latent continuous variable.

    A single “rank everything” preprocessing step can erase these distinctions.

    The likelihood operator follows the observation type. A continuous copula density evaluated at a convenient rank is not a universal substitute for the rectangle, interval, or mixed-measure contribution.

    Four neural validity strategies¤

    Neural models enter copula modeling through different contracts.

    1. Structured valid families¤

    A network can output parameters of a known valid family: a positive-definite Gaussian correlation matrix, compatible vine pair parameters, or a \(d\)-monotone Archimedean generator. Validity follows only if the parameterization enforces the family's constraints. Deep Archimedean Copulas is an example in which the architecture targets generator structure rather than treating an arbitrary scalar network as a copula CDF.

    Generative Archimedean constructions can parameterize the latent mixing distribution whose Laplace transform is the generator. This gives a route to valid sampling and avoids treating an unconstrained scalar network as a generator.

    2. Implicit generation followed by marginal uniformization¤

    An implicit generator first produces \(Y\in\mathbb R^d\) with the desired dependence, then applies its own marginal CDFs:

    \[ U_j=G_j(Y_j). \]

    With continuous exact \(G_j\), the resulting \(U_j\) are uniform and monotone marginal transforms preserve the copula of \(Y\). In practice, empirical or learned \(G_j\) introduce calibration error. This route can sample but need not provide a tractable density.

    Vine Copula Autoencoders make a different composition: an autoencoder supplies a latent representation, a vine models the aggregate latent law, and the decoder maps samples back to data space. The vine is a copula model for the chosen latent coordinates; unless the decoder is a coordinatewise monotone bijection, it is not the observed-space copula of the decoded image or vector.

    3. Density or density-ratio models¤

    A normalized flow can parameterize a density on the cube or a Gaussianized scale. A classifier can estimate the ratio \(c=h/(1-h)\). Neither route automatically gives exact uniform marginals. One must impose a marginal-preserving architecture, re-uniformize with tracked Jacobians, or measure and correct marginal error. MCMC sampling from an unnormalized ratio has different cost and diagnostics from direct flow sampling.

    4. Marginal-preserving probability paths¤

    Diffusion- and flow-based copulas can construct forward processes that remove dependence while preserving each marginal distribution at every time, then learn the reverse density or sampling dynamics. The crucial theorem is marginal preservation under the stated process, not the use of a diffusion vocabulary. A standard Euclidean Gaussian noising process on \(U\) leaves the cube and destroys uniform marginals, so it is not a copula path without additional construction.

    Transition couplings are not automatically observation copulas¤

    Discrete Copula Diffusion uses an additional dependency model to couple categorical predictions inside a discrete denoising step. This is an important connection to iterative generation: componentwise reverse predictions can miss same-step dependence. The word copula still needs a layer label. A coupling among \(p(x_{t-1,j}\mid x_t)\) outputs is a transition-level object; it is not automatically the static Sklar copula of clean observations, and its validity is judged together with the reverse-kernel and terminal-law construction.

    Conditional and covariate-dependent copulas¤

    A conditional copula may vary with covariates \(Z=z\):

    \[ F_{X\mid Z=z}(x) =C_z\!\left(F_{1\mid z}(x_1),\ldots,F_{d\mid z}(x_d)\right). \]

    A network can output \(C_z\) parameters or a conditional sampler. It must maintain copula validity for every relevant \(z\), not merely on average. Apparent changing dependence can also be caused by misspecified conditional marginals. Extrapolating \(C_z\) outside the covariate support is a model assumption, not a rank-invariance theorem.

    A fitted copula system contains at least a marginal model and a dependence model. Evaluate them separately and jointly.

    If a sample metric is computed directly on \(U\), marginal nonuniformity and dependence error are mixed. That can be useful as a system test but should not be mislabeled as a pure dependence score.

    Marginal and Rosenblatt diagnostics¤

    For generated copula samples, inspect each marginal ECDF against the identity and report uncertainty across coordinates. Hundreds of uncorrected marginal tests will produce false positives; use multiplicity-aware summaries and effect sizes. Conversely, averaging across coordinates can hide a few catastrophic marginals.

    For models with conditional CDFs, Rosenblatt residuals should be both uniform and mutually independent. Useful checks include:

    • univariate uniformity and boundary occupancy;
    • autocorrelation or serial tests when rows are time-indexed;
    • pairwise and multivariate independence tests;
    • repeated coordinate orders; and
    • calibration conditional on relevant covariates or regimes.

    Ranks computed from the same evaluation set are not known oracle PITs. Calibration envelopes should reproduce the estimation procedure.

    Dependence and tail evaluation¤

    No finite summary identifies an arbitrary high-dimensional copula. Use a panel tied to the scientific query:

    • held-out copula log likelihood when a normalized density exists;
    • pairwise and conditional rank-dependence matrices;
    • lower-, upper-, and mixed-corner exceedance probabilities at several finite thresholds;
    • multivariate return regions or stable-tail summaries for extreme-value applications;
    • two-sample tests on the copula scale;
    • conditional coverage and proper scores; and
    • application losses such as portfolio shortfall, flood coincidence, reliability failure, or multivariate forecast value.

    Tail coefficients are limits estimated from few effective observations. Report thresholds, estimators, confidence intervals, and sensitivity curves instead of a single unqualified number.

    Controlled model comparison¤

    A fair comparison holds constant or discloses:

    • train/validation/test rows and any temporal grouping;
    • marginal estimators and pseudo-observation convention;
    • tie, missingness, censoring, and dequantization treatment;
    • whether test marginals were refit or leaked;
    • conditional covariates and observation regimes;
    • parameter counts, structure search budget, and restarts;
    • sampling algorithm, NFE, MCMC burn-in, ESS, and wall-clock hardware;
    • density availability and numerical boundary clipping; and
    • metric implementation and uncertainty across data/model seeds.

    Comparing a vine using empirical marginals with a neural model using oracle marginals is not a pure comparison of dependence estimators. Neither is comparing direct IID samples with a short unverified MCMC chain.

    Complete worked protocol: multivariate environmental risk¤

    Suppose \(X\) contains river level, rainfall, soil moisture, and wind at aligned times.

    1. Split by time blocks, not random rows, to avoid leaking seasonal episodes.
    2. Fit conditional marginals using season and location, then inspect PIT calibration and extreme quantiles on held-out blocks.
    3. Fit candidate copulas—for example Gaussian, \(t\), a truncated vine, and a marginal-preserving neural model—using identical pseudo-observations.
    4. Validate copula margins for every sampler. Reject claims of copula validity if coordinate ECDFs materially deviate from uniformity.
    5. Evaluate dependence using held-out copula log score where available, rank matrices, multiple joint-exceedance thresholds, and Rosenblatt residuals.
    6. Evaluate the decision query: estimate the probability that river level and rainfall exceed safety thresholds while wind impedes response. Use bootstrap intervals that refit marginals and copulas by time block.
    7. Stress the invariance assumption by season and climate regime. If dependence changes, a fixed copula with replaced marginals is not supported.

    The best central log score and the best rare-event decision estimate can belong to different models. The protocol makes that disagreement visible rather than forcing one universal ranking.

    Chapter 6 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • Passing all coordinatewise uniformity tests does not validate dependence.
    • Matching a Kendall-tau matrix does not identify the joint copula or its tails.
    • A lower Wasserstein distance can reward marginal correction rather than better dependence.
    • Synthetic-data utility does not establish privacy; privacy needs a formal mechanism and accounting.
    • Holding a copula fixed across a shift is an empirical and scientific assumption, not a consequence of Sklar's theorem.
    • An accepted current paper is peer-reviewed research, not established doctrine.
    Retrieval questions
    1. Why should marginal validity and dependence fit be reported separately?
    2. What two properties should Rosenblatt residuals have?
    3. Why must tail thresholds and uncertainty be reported?
    4. What makes a comparison between a direct sampler and MCMC incomplete?
    5. What does a successful marginal-preserving diffusion construction establish—and what does it not establish?

    Solutions. (1) Either can fail while the other succeeds, and many joint metrics mix the two errors. (2) Uniform marginals and mutual independence, with any serial/covariate structure also checked. (3) Tail coefficients are limits inferred from few effective observations, so estimates depend strongly on threshold and sampling variability. (4) MCMC needs transition count, burn-in, convergence, autocorrelation, and ESS, whereas direct sampling has a different cost contract. (5) It establishes validity for the stated process under its assumptions; it does not make arbitrary diffusions, learned approximations, or solvers copulas.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Copula evaluation begins by separating marginal calibration from dependence fit and ends by recombining them for the actual observed-scale decision. Conditional residuals, tail queries, computation, and shift tests are part of the model contract. Current neural work broadens valid constructions, but it does not relax Sklar's uniqueness clauses, discrete likelihoods, or marginal-preservation requirements.
