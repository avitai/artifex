    6. Unlearning, Audits, Incidents, and Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes Chapters 1–5 and basic security incident response. By the end, you should be able to choose among source deletion, retrieval removal, filtering, targeted updates, approximate unlearning, checkpoint replacement, and retraining; specify an unlearning reference and acceptance test; plan recurring audits; and execute an incident record that preserves evidence, affected-scope analysis, communication, remediation, and follow-up evaluation.

    Removal hierarchy¤

    Possible responses include source deletion for future runs, retrieval/index deletion, output blocking, targeted fine-tuning/editing, approximate machine unlearning, adapter/checkpoint replacement, or full retraining. These have different guarantees. “Unlearned” must state the target, access model, reference retraining procedure, and acceptance tests.

    Evaluate at least:

    1. forgetting on direct and paraphrased/extraction attacks;
    2. retain-set utility;
    3. neighboring/related knowledge damage;
    4. ability to use the forgotten fact when supplied in context;
    5. privacy/membership risk;
    6. robustness after fine-tuning or model merging;
    7. comparison with retraining without the data.

    Context-aware unlearning, ICML 2026, is Peer-reviewed research evidence that methods can erase parametric access while inadvertently damaging contextual use, and proposes a mitigation in its experiments. It does not establish exact deletion from all model behavior.

    Audit schedule¤

    At dataset freeze, pretraining checkpoints, pre-release, and post-incident, run:

    • lineage/license completeness and unresolved-source counts;
    • exact/near/semantic duplication and benchmark contamination;
    • PII/sensitive-data scans with manual validation;
    • extraction, membership, and canary audits under declared budgets;
    • subgroup representation and harm analysis;
    • synthetic-content/source-model proportions;
    • provenance credential validation/stripping tests;
    • removal propagation and disaster-recovery exercises.

    Incident response¤

    Preserve evidence, restrict access, identify affected data/models/outputs through lineage, assess severity and legal notification duties, deploy containment, notify appropriate owners/users, remediate, validate, and publish a bounded postmortem where safe. Do not destroy logs needed for investigation; apply retention/access policy.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • Natural Identifiers for Privacy and Data Audits, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for auditing with seeded natural identifier strings and for clarifying held-out-data requirements in dataset inference; canaries/identifiers change the audit setting.
    • Train Once, Answer All, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for multiplexing controlled pretraining experiments on contamination, poisoning, memorization, and related questions, with interaction testing in its setup.
    • Recursive Cross-Model Training, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for studying benefits and homogenization when models ingest other models' outputs under reported mixtures.
    • Context-Aware Unlearning, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for the distinction between parametric forgetting and contextual utility.
    • ZeroUnlearn, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for targeted few-shot knowledge remapping in reported models; editing success is not a formal privacy or exact-deletion guarantee.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Build a record-level lineage manifest and propagate one deletion through derived assets.
    2. Compare exact, MinHash, perceptual, and embedding deduplication; audit subgroup deletion rates.
    3. Plant user-level canaries and compare exposure, membership, and extraction metrics.
    4. Implement record- versus user-level DP accounting on a toy corpus.
    5. Sign and validate a C2PA 2.4 derived-asset chain, then test stripping and a false assertion.
    6. Evaluate an unlearning method against retraining, paraphrases, extraction, contextual use, and model merging.
    7. Create an incident tabletop exercise from leaked private output to checkpoint replacement.
    Exercise 5 solution criterion

    A successful exercise distinguishes three outcomes: cryptographic validation of the signed manifest, continuity of the ingredient/edit chain, and independent truth assessment of assertions. Stripping should produce unknown/absent provenance—not a verdict that the asset is fake.

    Cumulative retrieval: governance and provenance
    1. Why must a lineage graph preserve source identity after records are transformed or deduplicated?
    2. What threat-model statement is required before interpreting a privacy attack or defense?
    3. Which provenance mechanism can prove who signed a claim without proving that the depicted event is true?
    4. What evidence is needed before calling a model update “unlearning”?

    Solutions. (1) Rights, consent, quality, deletion, and incident obligations attach to sources and transformations, not only final bytes. (2) Adversary access, target unit, auxiliary knowledge, success metric, baseline, and population. (3) A cryptographically signed provenance record; it authenticates the signer and metadata, not real-world truth. (4) A declared removal target and access model, a reference such as retraining without the target, retained-utility tests, attack/query evaluations, uncertainty, and limitations on the guarantee.

    Final synthesis¤

    Trustworthy generative modeling requires traceable sources, independent splits, explicit privacy units, attack-aware testing, formal guarantees where promised, tamper-evident but non-oracular provenance, asset-level rights, removal paths, and maintained audits. Governance is a living system because datasets, laws, standards, attacks, and downstream distributions change.
