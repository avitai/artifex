    4. Learning, Latent Structure, and Inference¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–3, maximum likelihood, latent-variable inference, and Monte Carlo estimation.

    Numbered learning outcomes. After completing the chapter, you can:

    1. O1 — Locate the computational obstacle. Identify whether inference is obstructed by a compensator integral, latent branching, a random intensity, a partition function, association, or observation bias.
    2. O2 — Derive and approximate likelihood terms. Compute a finite intensity MLE and audit deterministic or Monte Carlo compensator estimates.
    3. O3 — Use latent augmentation carefully. Explain when Hawkes branching indicators or Cox latent fields give valid augmented inference problems.
    4. O4 — Distinguish objectives. Separate full likelihood, pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood, and an unbiased log-integral estimate.
    5. O5 — Model what was observed. Analyze censoring, truncation, thinning, dead time, superposition, and missing marks as parts of the data-generating process.
    6. O6 — Bound scientific claims. Separate statistical identifiability, predictive dependence, and causal identification in a complete inference plan.

    A query-first inference map¤

    Point-process learning is not one algorithm. The appropriate calculation depends on the model and query.

    Table POINT.4. A query-first inference map
    Model or query Typical obstacle Representative methods
    Parametric temporal intensity compensator integral analytic integration, quadrature, Monte Carlo
    Linear Hawkes latent parent of each event direct likelihood, branching EM, Bayesian augmentation
    Cox or log-Gaussian Cox latent random intensity field Laplace approximation, MCMC, variational inference
    Gibbs spatial process parameter-dependent partition function MCMC likelihood, exchange methods, pseudolikelihood, composite likelihood
    DPP determinants and eigendecompositions exact finite likelihood, low-rank methods, stochastic linear algebra
    Random finite-set filtering unknown count and data association exact finite recursion in small cases, PHD/CPHD, multi-Bernoulli and labeled approximations
    Neural temporal model flexible history encoder and integral constrained cumulative hazard, numerical integration, intensity-free conditional density

    The model, observation process, and approximation must remain separate. A variational posterior over a Cox intensity does not turn the Cox process into a VAE family; an RNN parameterization does not make the compensator disappear.

    Exact likelihood and numerical integration¤

    For a temporal intensity \(\lambda_\theta\), the negative log likelihood is

    \[ \mathcal L(\theta) =-\sum_{i=1}^{n}\log\lambda_\theta(t_i\mid\mathcal H_{t_i}) +\int_0^T\lambda_\theta(s\mid\mathcal H_s)\,ds. \]

    If the integral has no closed form, deterministic quadrature introduces discretization error. Uniform Monte Carlo points \(U_k\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,T)\) give

    \[ \widehat\Lambda_\theta(0,T) =\frac{T}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_\theta(U_k\mid\mathcal H_{U_k}), \]

    an unbiased estimator of the compensator integral under integrability. This makes the displayed log-likelihood estimator unbiased when the event log intensities are exact, but exponentiating it does not generally produce an unbiased likelihood estimator. Gradients require the same care about random nodes, variance, and history discontinuities.

    Complete worked example: a nonhomogeneous Poisson MLE¤

    Let \(\lambda_\theta(t)=\theta(1+t)\) on \([0,1]\) and observe \(n=3\) events. Then

    \[ \ell(\theta) =3\log\theta+\sum_{i=1}^{3}\log(1+t_i) -\theta\int_0^1(1+t)\,dt =3\log\theta+C-\frac32\theta. \]

    Differentiating gives

    \[ \frac{d\ell}{d\theta}=\frac3\theta-\frac32, \qquad \widehat\theta=2. \]

    The estimate depends on the count but not the individual times because \(\theta\) is only a global scale. The times identify the fixed shape \(1+t\) only if that shape is also parameterized. Omitting the compensator would make the objective increase without bound as \(\theta\to\infty\).

    Branching augmentation for linear Hawkes processes¤

    Under a nonnegative linear Hawkes model, an event can be represented as either an immigrant from the baseline or an offspring of one earlier event. Latent parent indicators turn the complete-data likelihood into weighted baseline and offspring terms. An EM algorithm alternates between:

    1. posterior probabilities that each event is an immigrant or child of each valid predecessor; and
    2. parameter updates for the baseline and kernel.

    This augmentation is exact for the declared Poisson-cluster representation. It is not available unchanged for arbitrary nonlinear or inhibitory neural intensities, and a high posterior parent probability is not an identified real-world causal parent.

    Latent intensities and random measures¤

    For a Cox process,

    \[ p(X)=\int p(X\mid\Xi)\,p(d\Xi). \]

    The conditional Poisson likelihood is tractable once the random intensity \(\Xi\) is known, but integrating the field can be difficult. Log-Gaussian Cox models combine a Gaussian-field prior with a non-Gaussian point-pattern likelihood, motivating grid approximations, Laplace methods, MCMC, or variational inference. Discretizing space changes the numerical representation, not the underlying continuous-space model, only to the extent that convergence and cell approximations are controlled.

    Bayesian nonparametric random measures can also drive Poisson or Cox constructions. A completely random measure may supply random atom weights; normalization, if any, changes the generated object. The same random-measure prior can support point processes, feature allocations, or mixture models through different likelihoods, so prior vocabulary alone does not determine the family.

    Gibbs likelihood, pseudolikelihood, and composite objectives¤

    For a Gibbs density \(f_\theta(X)=\widetilde f_\theta(X)/Z(\theta)\), the normalizer usually prevents direct likelihood evaluation. The spatial log pseudolikelihood based on a Papangelou intensity has the schematic form

    \[ \log\operatorname{PL}(\theta;X) =\sum_{x_i\in X}\log\lambda_{\mathrm P,\theta}(x_i\mid X\setminus\{x_i\}) -\int_W\lambda_{\mathrm P,\theta}(u\mid X)\,du. \]

    It resembles a temporal likelihood but has different conditioning semantics. Pseudolikelihood is an alternative estimating objective, not an exact rewrite of the global configuration likelihood. Consistency and efficiency depend on the asymptotic regime, model, window growth, and regularity assumptions.

    Observation mechanisms: censoring is part of the model¤

    Point-pattern data are commonly incomplete:

    • right censoring: the observation window ends after the last recorded event;
    • left truncation: the history before the start is unknown;
    • interval censoring or rounding: event times are known only within bins;
    • dependent thinning: detection probability depends on location, mark, or latent state;
    • dead time: nearby events cannot both be recorded;
    • missing marks: event occurrence is observed but an attribute is absent;
    • superposition: events from several sources are observed without source labels.

    Ignoring these mechanisms can alter both intensity and interaction. For example, independent thinning of a Poisson process remains Poisson with a reduced intensity, but dependent thinning can create apparent repulsion. Left-truncated Hawkes data omit ancestors and can distort excitation estimates unless the initial history is modeled or a burn-in approximation is justified.

    Identifiability and causal restraint¤

    Likelihood identifiability asks whether different parameters induce the same observed law. Causal identification asks whether an interventional quantity follows from the observational law plus assumptions. They are not the same question.

    Common confounders include unobserved event types, shared latent intensity, time-varying exposure, spatial covariates, reporting delays, and feedback from interventions. A Hawkes influence matrix, attention map, or learned parent distribution is observational unless an intervention model and identification conditions are added. Predictive ablation can diagnose model reliance; it does not by itself establish causal effect.

    Three laws, not one dataset¤

    The latent law, recording law, and inference approximation are three separate objects. A flexible optimizer cannot recover events that the observation model declares unknowable, and a surrogate objective should not be relabeled as exact likelihood after optimization.

    Independent practice and assessment map: learning and inference¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. Let \(\lambda_\theta(t)=\theta(1+2t)\) on \([0,1]\) and suppose four events are observed. Predict whether \(\widehat\theta\) is above or below two, derive the MLE, and explain why the event locations do not affect this scale estimate.
    2. P1.2 — Audit a Monte Carlo compensator. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. With \(T=2\), \(K=4\), and evaluated intensities \((1,2,3,4)\) at uniform Monte Carlo points, compute \(\widehat\Lambda\). If one exact event term is \(\log2\), compute the estimated log likelihood and state which quantity is unbiased under repeated uniform draws.
    3. P1.3 — Compute branching responsibilities. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. At one event, a linear Hawkes model assigns baseline contribution \(0.5\) and two predecessor contributions \(0.3\) and \(0.2\). Compute the posterior immigrant/parent responsibilities and state the modeling assumptions that make this interpretation valid.
    4. P1.4 — Compare objectives. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Explain why a Gibbs Papangelou pseudolikelihood can avoid \(Z(\theta)\), then name one empirical or theoretical check needed before treating its fitted parameters like full-likelihood estimates.
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose an observation mechanism. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 15 min. A rate-four Poisson process on \([0,1]\) is independently detected with probability \(q(x)=x\). Derive the recorded intensity and expected count. Then explain why replacing independent detection with detector dead time can create apparent repulsion.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design an inference plan for a log-Gaussian Cox process observed through location-dependent missingness. Separate the latent field, point law, detection law, posterior approximation, numerical checks, identifiability risks, and held-out evaluation.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The integrated shape is \(\int_0^1(1+2t)dt=2\). Up to terms independent of \(\theta\), \(\ell(\theta)=4\log\theta-2\theta\), so \(4/\theta-2=0\) and \(\widehat\theta=2\)—equal to, not above or below, the prediction threshold. Event locations contribute \(\sum_i\log(1+2t_i)\), which contains no \(\theta\). Check. The second derivative \(-4/\theta^2\) is negative.
    2. P1.2. \(\widehat\Lambda=(2/4)(1+2+3+4)=5\), giving estimated log likelihood \(\log2-5\approx-4.3069\). Under iid uniform points, \(\widehat\Lambda\) is unbiased for the integral; with exact event terms, the estimated log likelihood is therefore unbiased for the log likelihood. Its exponential is not generally unbiased for the likelihood. Check. Jensen's inequality warns that exponentiation does not preserve unbiasedness.
    3. P1.3. The contributions sum to one, so the immigrant and two parent responsibilities are \(0.5\), \(0.3\), and \(0.2\). This interpretation requires the declared nonnegative linear Hawkes Poisson-cluster representation and an observed candidate-parent history. Check. The responsibilities sum to one; they are latent model assignments, not identified physical causes.
    4. P1.4. In \(f_\theta(X\cup\{u\})/f_\theta(X)\), the common global partition function cancels, leaving a computable local energy ratio. Nevertheless, pseudolikelihood is a different estimating objective. Check its consistency regime and uncertainty calibration, or compare it with exact/simulation-based likelihood on a tractable finite case. Rubric. Full credit requires both the algebraic cancellation and one validity check beyond training fit.
    5. P1.5. Independent thinning of the Poisson process gives intensity \(4q(x)=4x\) and expected recorded count \(\int_0^1 4x\,dx=2\); the recorded process remains Poisson. Dead time makes retention depend on nearby events, suppressing close pairs and inducing apparent inhibition even if the latent process is Poisson. Check. Location-dependent does not mean configuration-dependent: the former alone preserves Poisson independence.
    6. P1.6. Specify \(Z(x)\) and its GP prior, \(\rho(x)=e^{Z(x)}\), the conditional Poisson law, and a detection model \(q_\phi(x,\text{covariates})\). Infer \((Z,\phi)\) jointly by a declared Laplace, MCMC, or variational approximation; test grid and quadrature refinement, posterior predictive counts and interaction, and held-out detection strata. Audit confounding between low intensity and low detection, prior sensitivity, boundary effects, and calibration. Rubric. Full credit requires all seven requested layers and an explicit intensity-versus-detection identifiability warning.

    Chapter 4 misconception checks¤

    If a Monte Carlo compensator estimate is unbiased, is the exponentiated likelihood estimate unbiased?

    No. In general \(\mathbb E[e^{-\widehat\Lambda}]\ne e^{-\mathbb E[\widehat\Lambda]}\). Unbiasedness does not survive a nonlinear transformation. Pseudo-marginal methods require a nonnegative unbiased estimator of the likelihood itself, not merely of its logarithm.

    Does a lower training NLL prove better point-process generation?

    No. It establishes an in-sample objective comparison under a particular observation and reference convention. Count calibration, mark calibration, residual structure, held-out windows, long-horizon rollouts, leakage, and domain utility still require evaluation.

    Chapter 4 retrieval questions¤

    1. Why does branching EM not apply automatically to every neural intensity?
    2. What is the difference between a Gibbs pseudolikelihood and its full likelihood?
    3. Name two observation mechanisms that can create false interaction signals.
    Complete solutions
    1. It relies on the nonnegative linear Hawkes Poisson-cluster representation with latent parents; nonlinear or inhibitory models need not have that representation.
    2. The pseudolikelihood uses local Papangelou insertion ratios and avoids the global normalizer, while the full likelihood assigns normalized mass to the whole configuration.
    3. Examples include dependent detection/thinning, dead time, missing event types, left truncation, shared exposure, and unmodeled reporting delays.

    Chapter 4 synthesis and bridge¤

    Inference difficulty comes from different sources: a compensator integral, latent branching, a random intensity field, a configuration normalizer, or an incomplete observation process. No single approximation solves all of them. Chapter 5 reverses the direction: given a declared law, it constructs samples and identifies the conditions under which inversion, thinning, spectral, cluster, or Markov-chain algorithms are exact.
