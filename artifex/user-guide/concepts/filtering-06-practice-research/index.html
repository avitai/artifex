    6. Particles, Learning, Validation, and Method Choice¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–5, importance sampling, SMC, variational inference, MCMC, automatic differentiation, and proper scoring rules.

    Learning objectives. Compare state-space inference by target and returned representation; separate differentiable learning from finite-filter correctness; validate means, uncertainty, paths, evidence, and compute; place recent learned and control-based filters at the right abstraction layer; and choose a method with an auditable approximation ledger.

    Beyond a single Gaussian¤

    The Bayesian recursion can be approximated with several representations:

    Table FILTER.5. Beyond a single Gaussian
    Representation Update mechanism Principal strength Principal failure
    grid or basis numerical integration and normalized tables transparent multimodality in very low dimension state-space resolution grows exponentially with dimension
    weighted particles proposal, importance correction, and optional resampling flexible nonlinear and non-Gaussian beliefs weight and ancestral degeneracy; severe dimension dependence
    structured variational path optimize a declared family over \(z_{0:T}\) scalable amortization and global smoothing objectives family, amortization, and optimization gaps
    trajectory MCMC invariant transitions over states and possibly parameters asymptotically exact expectations under kernel conditions mixing and long-path geometry can be poor
    expectation propagation revise local approximate factors by moment projection can reuse local structure and two-sided information convergence and positive-definiteness are not generic
    learned deterministic belief recurrent or set-valued state update low-latency amortized computation may not define a normalized posterior or calibrated uncertainty

    The canonical MCMC chapter owns invariance, blocked versus scalar updates, warmup, and MCSE. In this chapter, trajectory MCMC specializes those rules to a coupled latent path, where one-site updates can deteriorate as sequence length grows.

    Particle filtering, resampling, proposal design, particle smoothing, and particle MCMC are derived in the Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods foundation. The stochastic-process chapter specializes the construction to irregular-time and continuous-transition laws, while Latent Variables and Inference compares particle objectives with other posterior approximations. The key boundary here is that resampling improves current weight allocation while coalescing ancestral paths. A filter can estimate the current state well and still give poor early-time smoothing trajectories.

    Rao–Blackwellized filters integrate a tractable conditional submodel exactly and sample the remainder. This reduces Monte Carlo variance when the decomposition is valid; it does not make the sampled variables exact or erase particle-support requirements.

    Differentiating through a filter¤

    A differentiable filter is a parameterized computation. Gradients can update transition, observation, proposal, representation, or initialization parameters using supervised state loss, predictive likelihood, an ELBO, an SMC bound, or another declared objective. These objectives need not have the same population optimum.

    For a deterministic Kalman recursion, reverse-mode differentiation targets the finite sequence of solves and updates actually executed. Near-singular covariances, adaptive jitter, clipping, stopped gradients, or custom factorizations become part of that derivative program.

    For particles, resampling introduces discrete ancestor choices. Straight-through, soft-resampling, score-function, transport, marginalization, and stopped-resampling estimators have different bias and variance. End-to-end differentiability therefore does not prove that the gradient is unbiased for the exact state-space likelihood.

    Differentiable Particle Filters demonstrate one algorithm-prior construction with learned motion and measurement components. A 2025 peer-reviewed learned mixture proposal and transition method provides another. Their experimental results belong to the named objectives, data, representations, particle counts, and baselines—not to differentiable filtering as a universal ranking.

    Learned analytic structure and expressive beliefs¤

    Learned filters occupy several distinct design points:

    • a neural transition or observation map inside EKF, UKF, or a moment filter;
    • an encoder that emits parameters of one Gaussian or mixture belief;
    • an invertible map that preserves a declared tractable calculation;
    • a learned proposal inside an importance-corrected particle filter; or
    • a recurrent belief representation trained only for a downstream query.

    The 2025 ICML Deep Bayesian Filter introduces an additional latent variable, constrains latent transition structure, and learns an inverse observation operator so that its stated recursive computation is analytic within that construction. This is a hybrid system composition: learned structure is designed around a tractable posterior interface. It does not establish that every learned latent coordinate has a Gaussian true posterior or identifiable physical meaning.

    As of the verification date, Neural Bayesian Filtering is a revised submission, not peer-reviewed evidence, for embedding and tracking multimodal beliefs. It is useful as a preprint research direction only; its status and experiments must not be narrated as a settled field result.

    A current smoothing direction¤

    A 2026 peer-reviewed probabilistic optimal-control representation of smoothing represents a posterior trajectory law through the closed-loop behavior of a synthetic controlled system and reports equally weighted forward trajectories under its constructed policy. This is a distributional correspondence under the paper's construction, not an algebraic identity between every smoother and every control problem. The policy approximation, particle representation, dynamic-programming computation, and model-learning experiment retain their own errors and compute ledger.

    A June 2026 Bayesian-filtering approach to learned Lagrangian dynamics is a preprint example of joint physical-model and state learning with Gaussian-approximation filters. It is included to mark a research interface—structured mechanics plus approximate filtering—not as evidence of broad superiority or mature reproduction.

    Validation starts with the query¤

    No single residual plot validates a filter. Use a ladder:

    1. Algebraic oracle. Compare with enumeration or dense Gaussian conditioning on a tiny model.
    2. Limiting cases. Recover the linear model from nonlinear code; skip an absent observation; test nearly noiseless and uninformative observations; verify that permuting equivalent independent sensor factors changes no exact result.
    3. Simulation calibration. Draw states and observations from the declared model, then measure coverage, rank behavior, and path statistics.
    4. Predictive checks. Evaluate held-out log predictive density or another proper score, calibration by horizon, and residual dependence.
    5. Application queries. Test the downstream forecast, event probability, control cost, or scientific functional, not only state RMSE.
    6. Model criticism. Check whether simulated observations resemble relevant real-data structure. Inference calibration under a simulator does not validate the simulator.

    For a \(d_y\)-dimensional linear-Gaussian innovation, the normalized innovation squared is

    \[ \operatorname{NIS}_t=e_t^\top S_t^{-1}e_t. \]

    Under the correctly specified regular model it has a \(\chi^2_{d_y}\) marginal law, with additional dependence considerations for a time series. If true states are available in simulation, the normalized estimation error squared is

    \[ \operatorname{NEES}_t =(z_t-m_{t\mid t})^\top P_{t\mid t}^{-1}(z_t-m_{t\mid t}), \]

    with an analogous \(\chi^2_{d_z}\) reference under its assumptions. Repeated time points from one trajectory are not independent replicates. Tune-on-test inflation can make these diagnostics look calibrated without producing a generalizing model.

    Also record:

    • innovation mean, autocorrelation, tail behavior, and condition-specific structure;
    • marginal and simultaneous coverage across horizons;
    • effective sample size, unique ancestors, mode occupancy, and path diversity for particles;
    • covariance symmetry, factorization failures, minimum eigenvalues, and jitter;
    • convergence residuals and initialization sensitivity for iterated methods;
    • predictive likelihood with the exact approximation status of each normalizer; and
    • wall-clock time, peak memory, model evaluations, linear solves, factorizations, particles or sigma points, sequential depth, and hardware.

    Worked method-selection audit¤

    Suppose a two-dimensional tracking simulator has a bearing-only observation. A single measurement admits two separated range hypotheses, but repeated motion usually resolves the ambiguity. A defensible study would:

    1. enumerate or grid a reduced two-step instance as a reference;
    2. compare EKF, sigma-point, Gaussian mixture, and particle filters with matched transition and observation models;
    3. report current-state RMSE and posterior mass on both range hypotheses;
    4. evaluate path samples after the ambiguity resolves;
    5. repeat under clean data, outliers, altered motion, and particle or mixture budgets; and
    6. distinguish model calls and wall time from nominal update count.

    An EKF can have good mean RMSE after resolution while being overconfident during ambiguity. A particle filter can preserve both modes yet suffer ancestor collapse. A mixture filter can work well until pruning deletes the eventual correct branch. These are different failure mechanisms, so one aggregate RMSE cannot select the method.

    Decision table¤

    Table FILTER.6. Decision table
    Problem evidence Reasonable starting point Required challenge test
    verified linear-Gaussian model, moderate dense state square-root Kalman filter and RTS smoother dense batch Gaussian oracle and condition-number sweep
    sparse precision or many independent sensors information or factor-graph form fill-in, double counting, and rank handling
    mild local nonlinearity with good initialization EKF or iterated EKF/EKS broad-prior and alternate-initialization tests
    smooth nonlinear moments, one mode plausible sigma-point, cubature, or quadrature Gaussian filter multimodal and coordinate counterexamples
    high-dimensional simulator with useful covariance locality EnKF localization/inflation sensitivity and nonlinear posterior reference
    a few persistent regimes or hypotheses Gaussian-sum or switching approximation component growth, pruning loss, and mode recovery
    strongly non-Gaussian low-dimensional state grid, particles, mixtures, or flow belief support, mode occupancy, and path degeneracy
    tractable conditional substructure Rao–Blackwellized particle or hybrid message method verify the exact conditional and sampled remainder separately
    repeated related sequences with latency constraints amortized or learned filter plus reference inference shift, calibration, and objective-mismatch tests

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Filtering and smoothing are posterior queries over a state-space generator. Exact Gaussian closure, projection, deterministic quadrature, ensembles, mixtures, variational paths, particles, learned beliefs, and control-based representations place approximation in different locations. A research-quality comparison identifies that location, validates the query at finite compute, and separates inference error from model misspecification.

    Chapter 6 retrieval practice¤

    1. Why does differentiating through a particle filter not by itself produce an unbiased likelihood gradient?
    2. What does NIS test under its ideal reference model, and what does it not establish?
    3. Why can a low current-state RMSE coexist with a poor smoother?
    4. Which evidence label applies to the June 2026 learned-Lagrangian filtering work?
    5. What must be matched before comparing EnKF, mixture, and particle filters?
    Complete solutions
    1. Resampling and other discrete or stopped operations require a declared estimator; straight-through, relaxed, score-function, and stopped gradients target different finite programs and can be biased for the exact likelihood.
    2. It tests innovation scale relative to \(S_t\) under the declared regular model; it does not prove temporal independence, model adequacy, path calibration, or real-data correctness.
    3. Resampling or projection can preserve the latest marginal while destroying ancestral diversity and cross-time dependence.
    4. Preprint. It is a timely research direction, not peer-reviewed or independently reproduced evidence.
    5. The generative transition and observation laws, data, information set, representation, compute budget, initialization, tuning access, metrics, and approximation/evidence protocol.

    Primary reading¤

    • Kalman (1960), A New Approach to Linear Filtering and Prediction Problems — exact recursive linear-Gaussian filtering foundations.
    • Rauch, Tung, and Striebel (1965), Maximum Likelihood Estimates of Linear Dynamic Systems — the backward Gaussian smoother.
    • Carter and Kohn (1994), On Gibbs Sampling for State Space Models — blocked state-path simulation in Bayesian state-space inference.
    • Julier and Uhlmann (2004), Unscented Filtering and Nonlinear Estimation — deterministic sigma-point filtering.
    • Arasaratnam and Haykin (2009), Cubature Kalman Filters — spherical–radial cubature and square-root construction.
    • Evensen (1994), Sequential Data Assimilation with a Nonlinear Quasi-Geostrophic Model — ensemble forecast-error statistics.
    • Särkkä and García-Fernández (2021), Temporal Parallelization of Bayesian Smoothers — associative scan formulations.
    • Minka (2001/2004), Expectation Propagation for Approximate Bayesian Inference — corrected primary record for ADF and EP.
    • Tarumi, Fukuda, and Maeda (2025), Deep Bayesian Filter for Bayes-Faithful Data Assimilation — learned structure around an analytic recursive interface.
    • Vantilborgh et al. (2026), Efficient State and Parameter Estimation of Nonlinear State-Space Models through Probabilistic Optimal Control — a peer-reviewed smoothing representation through synthetic control.
