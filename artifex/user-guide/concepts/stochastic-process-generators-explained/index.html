    Newly unlocked in the default route

    A fixed-dimensional generator models a random vector. A stochastic-process generator models a random function, field, or trajectory whose values may be queried at arbitrary input locations. This distinction matters for irregular time series, spatial fields, operators, physical trajectories, and uncertainty that must remain coherent as the observation grid changes.

    This volume connects Gaussian processes, Conditional and latent Neural Processes, equivariant stochastic fields, neural SDEs, latent state-space models, function-space diffusion, and stochastic neural operators. It asks a strict question throughout: does the model define compatible finite-dimensional distributions, or only predictions on one chosen grid?

    Read continuously for the consistency argument. Numbered pages are exact projections.
    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–3.
    Practitioner: add Chapters 4 and 6.
    Research: all chapters, including the evidence audit and projective-consistency exercises.

    1. Random Functions and Projective Consistency¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes finite-dimensional probability and conditional laws. By the end, you should be able to distinguish a random function or path from a random fixed vector, state Kolmogorov-style consistency requirements, test whether overlapping query sets induce compatible marginals, separate process law from observation/noise model, and diagnose when fixed-grid training fails to define a coherent generator at new locations or resolutions.

    Prerequisites. Random variables, joint/marginal/conditional distributions, and elementary topology.

    Numbered learning outcomes. After completing the chapter, you can:

    1. O1 — Identify the random object. Distinguish a fixed random vector, a random function, its finite restriction, and its noisy observations.
    2. O2 — Test permutation consistency. Determine whether a reordered query presentation induces exactly the corresponding reordered joint law.
    3. O3 — Test projective consistency. Marginalize a larger finite law and compare it with the law generated directly on the retained query set.
    4. O4 — Separate existence from regularity. Explain why compatible finite laws need additional assumptions before continuity, differentiability, or Sobolev membership follows.
    5. O5 — Audit conditioning semantics. Distinguish probabilistic conditioning of one joint law from re-running a learned context encoder.
    6. O6 — Transfer the contract. State the proof and evaluation obligations for claiming that a variable-grid model defines a coherent stochastic-process generator.

    Let \(f:\mathcal X\to\mathcal Y\) be random. A stochastic process specifies a joint distribution for every finite query set \(X=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\):

    \[ p_X(y_1,\ldots,y_n), \qquad y_i=f(x_i). \]

    These finite-dimensional distributions must be compatible.

    The index \(x\) need not be time. It may be spatial position, wavelength, graph location, boundary condition, sensor coordinate, or an input function. A sample is one entire realization \(f(\cdot)\); evaluating it at \(X\) produces a random vector. A model trained only on arrays of one resolution instead defines a distribution on that array space unless an extension across queries is supplied.

    A random function and a random event configuration answer different queries. A Cox process may draw a random intensity function and then draw events from it, but the function draw and the resulting counting measure are two random objects. See Point Processes and Random Finite Sets for cardinality, Janossy density, empty-space, and mark semantics.

    Permutation Consistency¤

    Reordering the query points must reorder outputs without changing probability:

    \[ p_{(x_{\pi(1)},\ldots,x_{\pi(n)})} (y_{\pi(1)},\ldots,y_{\pi(n)})) =p_X(y_1,\ldots,y_n). \]

    The query set is not a sequence unless the domain itself supplies an order.

    Marginal Consistency¤

    If \(X'=(x_1,\ldots,x_n,x_{n+1})\), then integrating the additional value must recover the smaller distribution:

    \[ \int p_{X'}(y_1,\ldots,y_n,y_{n+1})dy_{n+1} =p_X(y_1,\ldots,y_n). \]

    Under regularity assumptions, the Kolmogorov extension theorem turns a consistent family into a process measure. A network that emits a distribution separately for every requested grid is not automatically consistent across grids.

    More precisely, the extension theorem constructs a measure on a product/cylinder sigma algebra when the finite-dimensional laws are permutation- and marginalization-consistent. Extra path regularity—continuous, differentiable, bounded-variation, or Sobolev samples—does not follow from consistency alone. Kolmogorov's continuity criterion and related tightness/regularity arguments require quantitative increment bounds.

    Three Consistency Questions¤

    1. Permutation consistency: reordering a finite query presentation only reorders the joint law.
    2. Marginal or projective consistency: deleting query coordinates from a larger law recovers the smaller law.
    3. Conditioning consistency: treating a predicted point as newly observed and re-running the conditional model agrees with conditioning the earlier joint predictive law.

    The first two define compatibility of an unconditional family. The third is a coherence requirement for learned conditional prediction maps. A factorized CNP can be marginally consistent over its targets for fixed context while still failing conditioning consistency because its context encoder changes when a target becomes context. On the Conditioning Consistency Gap in Conditional Neural Processes, published in TMLR in April 2026, quantifies this gap under bounded-encoder and Lipschitz-decoder assumptions. Its \(O(n^{-2})\) context-size result is assumption-specific and does not say every NP is coherent in the few-shot regime.

    Read the upper diamond before the lower one. Agreement under restriction is a law-level statement; smooth-looking samples on two grids do not establish it. Even exact projective agreement does not by itself establish continuous paths.

    Context and Target Sets¤

    A task supplies context observations

    \[ C=\{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n_c} \]

    and asks for a predictive distribution at target inputs \(X_T\). The desired object is

    \[ p(y_T\mid X_T,C). \]

    Context order should not matter. Target predictions should be coherent jointly, not only accurate pointwise. When contexts are noisy, repeated, missing not at random, or selected adaptively, the observation model and selection mechanism must be specified.

    The latent function and observations are distinct random objects. A standard model is

    \[ f\sim P, \qquad y_i\mid f,x_i\sim p_{\text{obs}}(y_i\mid f(x_i),x_i). \]

    Noise-free interpolation assumes a Dirac observation model. Heteroscedastic measurement noise, censoring, or informative sensor placement changes the posterior. Treating repeated noisy measurements as duplicate set elements is not equivalent to observing the latent function once.

    Interpolation, Extrapolation, and Adaptation¤

    • Interpolation: targets lie within context support.
    • Extrapolation: targets lie beyond observed locations or times.
    • Adaptation: the model uses a small context from a new task/function.
    • Imputation: missing values are predicted at known coordinates.
    • Forecasting: target coordinates are later than context under a temporal domain.

    These are different distribution shifts. Random context/target splits dominated by interpolation do not establish forecasting ability.

    A further distinction is new coordinates on the same task versus a new function drawn from a shifted task distribution. Neural Processes meta-learn across tasks; good within-task interpolation can coexist with poor held-out-family adaptation. Forecasting also relies on a directional information set: using future points in the encoder converts forecasting into smoothing.

    Worked Consistency Failure¤

    Suppose a model predicts independent \(Y_i\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) when queried at one point, but for any pair predicts \((Y_1,Y_2)\) with each marginal \(\mathcal N(1,1)\). Both outputs are normalized, yet marginalizing the pair gives \(\mathcal N(1,1)\) rather than the one-point law. No stochastic process has these finite-dimensional distributions.

    Worked Gaussian Compatibility Check¤

    Let the law at \(X=(x_1,x_2)\) be

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}Y_1\\Y_2\end{bmatrix} \sim\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1&\rho\\\rho&4\end{bmatrix} \right). \]

    Its one-point restrictions must be \(Y_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(Y_2\sim\mathcal N(0,4)\). The covariance is valid only when \(|\rho|\le2\). A separately emitted one-point variance of 3 at \(x_2\) violates projective consistency even though all individual covariance matrices are positive. Positive semidefiniteness is required within each query set; compatibility is additionally required across sets.

    Function Samples Versus Marginal Error Bars¤

    Independent predictive Gaussians at each \(x\) provide uncertainty bands but not coherent function samples. A function posterior must specify cross-input dependence. This distinction is critical for maxima, integrals, threshold crossings, control, and active learning.

    For example, if ten temperatures each have a calibrated 95% interval but are sampled independently, the probability and duration of a heatwave can be badly wrong. Pointwise calibration does not determine the joint law of extrema or threshold runs.

    Chapter 1 retrieval Questions¤

    1. Which consistency conditions enter the Kolmogorov extension construction?
    2. Why is conditioning consistency separate from marginal target consistency?
    3. Does a consistent family guarantee continuous sample paths?
    Complete solutions
    1. Permutation and marginal/projective compatibility of every finite-dimensional law.
    2. A conditional model can change its representation when an observed point is added; that update need not equal probabilistic conditioning of its earlier joint prediction.
    3. No. Path regularity needs further increment/tightness assumptions or an explicit regular function space.

    Chapter 1 Synthesis¤

    Function-space generation requires compatible laws across arbitrary query sets. Permutation symmetry and marginal consistency are semantic requirements, not architectural decoration. Chapter 2 studies Gaussian processes, where both are explicit.

    If a model accepts any number of query points, is it automatically a stochastic process?

    No. Its outputs must also be permutation-consistent and marginally compatible across every finite query set.

    Continue continuously, or use the Chapter 1 stopping-point view.

    2. Gaussian Processes as the Canonical Function Prior¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and multivariate Gaussians. By the end, you should be able to construct a GP from mean and positive-semidefinite kernel, derive finite posterior prediction and marginal likelihood, distinguish prior, latent-function, and noisy-observation uncertainty, analyze kernel and inducing- point approximations, and identify where stationarity, smoothness, and Gaussian assumptions constrain generated functions.

    Prerequisites. Chapter 1, multivariate Gaussian conditioning, and linear algebra.

    Numbered learning outcomes. After completing the chapter, you can:

    1. O1 — Construct compatible GP laws. Verify kernel positive semidefiniteness and explain why one mean/kernel pair gives permutation- and projectively consistent finite Gaussians.
    2. O2 — Derive GP updating. Compute posterior mean, covariance, sequential conditioning, and marginal likelihood without treating a numerical inverse as an algorithm.
    3. O3 — Separate uncertainty layers. Distinguish latent-function covariance, observation noise, jitter, hyperparameter uncertainty, and non-Gaussian likelihood approximation.
    4. O4 — Audit scalable inference. Identify whether a method changes the data, kernel/prior, posterior family, conditional approximation, or numerical solve.
    5. O5 — Connect without conflating. Separate GP draws from RKHS functions and distinguish NNGP, NTK, deep-kernel, and deep-GP constructions.
    6. O6 — Diagnose model boundaries. Evaluate stationarity, boundary behavior, learned geometry, extrapolation, calibration, and empirical-prior shift.

    A Gaussian process (GP)

    \[ f\sim\mathcal{GP}(m,k) \]

    means every finite vector \(f_X=(f(x_1),\ldots,f(x_n))\) is Gaussian:

    \[ f_X\sim\mathcal N(m_X,K_{XX}), \qquad K_{ij}=k(x_i,x_j). \]

    A valid kernel is positive semidefinite for every finite input set. It determines covariance, smoothness, stationarity, periodicity, and symmetry assumptions.

    Positive semidefinite means

    \[ \sum_{i,j=1}^n a_i a_j k(x_i,x_j)\ge0 \]

    for every finite \(X\) and coefficient vector \(a\). Symmetry alone is insufficient. Kernel sums and nonnegative scalar products remain kernels; multiplying kernels also preserves validity under standard closure results. Input-dependent neural features can define \(k_\phi(x,x')=\langle\phi(x),\phi(x')\rangle\), but their learned extrapolation geometry must be audited.

    From One Kernel to a Random Function¤

    A GP is not defined by fitting one covariance matrix. One pair of functions \((m,k)\) supplies a law for every finite ordered query collection \(X\):

    \[ P_X=\mathcal N(m_X,K_{XX}). \]

    These laws are automatically compatible. If a permutation matrix \(\Pi\) reorders \(X\), then the new mean and covariance are \(\Pi m_X\) and \(\Pi K_{XX}\Pi^\top\). If \(X\subset X'\), marginalizing the Gaussian law at \(X'\) to the coordinates in \(X\) selects the corresponding entries of \(m_{X'}\) and principal submatrix of \(K_{X'X'}\), which are exactly \(m_X\) and \(K_{XX}\). The kernel therefore supplies the permutation and projective consistency required by the extension construction in Chapter 1.

    For example, with the unit-variance RBF kernel \(k(x,x')=\exp(-(x-x')^2/2)\), the laws on \(X=(0,1)\) and \(X'=(0,1,2)\) use

    \[ K_X= \begin{bmatrix} 1&e^{-1/2}\\ e^{-1/2}&1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad K_{X'}= \begin{bmatrix} 1&e^{-1/2}&e^{-2}\\ e^{-1/2}&1&e^{-1/2}\\ e^{-2}&e^{-1/2}&1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    The upper-left block of \(K_{X'}\) is \(K_X\), so integrating out \(f(2)\) recovers the two-input law exactly. This statement holds for every nested pair, not only these grids. The consistent family defines an abstract random function on the product sigma algebra; continuity or membership in \(C(\mathcal X)\), an RKHS, or a Sobolev space needs additional kernel and path-regularity results.

    Repeated query locations create another useful boundary. If \(x_i=x_j\), then \(f(x_i)=f(x_j)\) almost surely and the finite covariance is singular. The Gaussian law still exists, but it has no ordinary Lebesgue density on the full coordinate space. Positive semidefiniteness is the general kernel requirement; strict definiteness is an optional condition for nonsingular densities on distinct inputs.

    Sampling on one finite grid produces a random vector, not a literal stored value at every point of an uncountable domain. Samples on two grids have the correct marginal laws even if drawn independently, but they represent the same realized function only when they are coupled through one process draw. This distinction matters when comparing a coarse sample with a refined version of that same field.

    A Kernel Is a Covariance Contract, Not Merely a Similarity Score¤

    For a GP, \(k(x,x')\) has units of output squared and specifies covariance before any data are observed. Calling it a similarity can build intuition, but it hides three facts:

    1. \(k(x,x)\) is a prior variance, not a self-similarity normalized to one;
    2. negative covariance is allowed; and
    3. the complete Gram matrix must remain positive semidefinite for every finite input set.

    On a Euclidean input space, a stationary kernel depends on the displacement \(x-x'\). An isotropic kernel depends only on its length \(r=\lVert x-x'\rVert\). Every isotropic kernel is stationary, but a stationary kernel can distinguish direction. Neither assumption is automatic for time, geography, boundaries, graphs, or manifolds.

    The following vocabulary connects a formula to the functions it favors. These are prior implications, not facts learned from the observations.

    Squared exponential / RBF¤

    \[ k(r)=\sigma_f^2\exp\!\left(-\frac{r^2}{2\ell^2}\right). \]

    One length scale produces extremely smooth local variation. It can be too smooth for abrupt or rough phenomena.

    Matérn¤

    \[ k(r)=\sigma_f^2 M_\nu(r/\ell), \]

    where \(M_\nu\) is the Matérn correlation. The parameter \(\nu\) controls mean-square differentiability and \(\ell\) controls range. A sample-path differentiability claim still needs a precise theorem and domain assumptions.

    Periodic¤

    With \(\Delta=t-t'\),

    \[ k(t,t')=\sigma_f^2 \exp\!\left[ -\frac{2\sin^2(\pi\Delta/p)}{\ell^2} \right]. \]

    This kernel states exact repetition with period \(p\), which is stronger than a recurring but evolving seasonal pattern.

    Linear¤

    \[ k(x,x')=\sigma_b^2 +\sigma_v^2(x-c)^T(x'-c). \]

    This is a prior over random affine functions around origin \(c\). It is nonstationary: prior variance grows with distance from \(c\).

    White-noise covariance¤

    \[ k(x,x')=\sigma^2\mathbf 1\{x=x'\}. \]

    Values at distinct inputs are independent. On a continuum, the construction need not have ordinary function-valued samples.

    For the common anisotropic RBF kernel, define its squared scaled distance by

    \[ d_\ell^2(x,x')= \sum_{d=1}^{D} \frac{(x_d-x'_d)^2}{\ell_d^2} \]

    and then set

    \[ k(x,x')=\sigma_f^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac12d_\ell^2(x,x')\right). \]

    A large fitted \(\ell_d\) makes changes along coordinate \(d\) weak under this kernel and parameterization. The label automatic relevance determination is historical: a fitted length scale is not a causal relevance certificate, and correlated coordinates, scaling, local optima, priors, and kernel misspecification can make individual length scales non-identifiable or unstable.

    Positive-semidefinite covariance kernels and Mercer expansions are related but not synonymous. A positive-semidefinite kernel is enough to define the compatible finite Gaussian laws above. A Mercer eigenfunction expansion additionally invokes conditions on a domain, measure, and kernel—for example, a continuous kernel on a compact domain with the associated integral operator. The phrase “Mercer kernel” should not be used to smuggle those extra conditions into every GP.

    Building Kernels Without Misreading the Construction¤

    If \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are independent zero-mean GPs with kernels \(k_1\) and \(k_2\), then \(f=f_1+f_2\) is a GP with kernel \(k_1+k_2\). This gives a literal additive component interpretation. Nonnegative scaling, sums, and products of valid kernels preserve positive semidefiniteness, but the interpretations differ:

    • \(k_1+k_2\) can represent a sum of independent latent functions;
    • \(k_1k_2\) represents the conjunction of two covariance structures; and
    • \(f_1(x)f_2(x)\) is generally not Gaussian, so it is not the GP whose kernel is \(k_1k_2\).

    That last distinction is easy to miss. At one input, the product of two independent standard-normal GP values has a product-normal distribution, not a Gaussian distribution, even though its covariance across inputs is \(k_1k_2\). A covariance function does not determine a non-Gaussian process law.

    Input warping and learned features also preserve validity when they are used consistently. If \(g_\phi\) maps raw inputs into a feature space and \(k_0\) is valid there, then

    \[ k_\phi(x,x')=k_0\!\left(g_\phi(x),g_\phi(x')\right) \]

    is a valid kernel for fixed \(\phi\). Learning \(\phi\) changes the geometry of extrapolation. It does not make uncertainty about \(\phi\) disappear; a plug-in estimate conditions on one learned geometry.

    Worked Kernel Design: Trend, Evolving Seasonality, and Noise¤

    Suppose a monthly environmental series has a smooth trend, a seasonal pattern that changes gradually, and independent measurement noise. Let \(\Delta=t-t'\). One coherent covariance decomposition for the observed series is

    \[ k(t,t')=k_{\mathrm{trend}} +k_{\mathrm{season}} +k_{\mathrm{noise}}, \]

    with

    \[ k_{\mathrm{trend}}= \sigma_{\mathrm{trend}}^2 \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta^2}{2\ell_{\mathrm{trend}}^2}\right), \]
    \[ \begin{aligned} k_{\mathrm{period}}&= \exp\!\left[ -\frac{2\sin^2(\pi\Delta/12)} {\ell_{\mathrm{period}}^2} \right],\\ k_{\mathrm{drift}}&= \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta^2}{2\ell_{\mathrm{drift}}^2}\right), \end{aligned} \]
    \[ k_{\mathrm{season}}= \sigma_{\mathrm{season}}^2 k_{\mathrm{period}}k_{\mathrm{drift}}, \]

    and

    \[ k_{\mathrm{noise}}= \sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}^2\mathbf 1\{t=t'\}. \]

    The first summand is a long-range trend. The product in the second line says points must be close both in seasonal phase and across years; when \(\ell_{\mathrm{drift}}\) is finite, the seasonal shape can evolve instead of repeating forever. If the final diagonal term belongs in the likelihood, the latent kernel is \(k_{\mathrm{trend}}+k_{\mathrm{season}}\) and the observation covariance includes the independent noise term. If the diagonal term is placed in the latent-function kernel instead, it declares a latent process with independent pointwise variation. The same matrix expression can therefore encode different scientific objects depending on which random variable it belongs to.

    This design is a hypothesis, not a discovery. Posterior checks should test residual seasonality, boundary behavior, long-horizon calibration, and sensitivity to alternative decompositions. Several component combinations can explain the same finite observations, so a visually plausible decomposition need not be identifiable.

    Conditioning¤

    For noisy observations \(y_C=f_C+\epsilon\), \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I)\), the target posterior is

    \[ \mu_T=m_T+K_{TC}(K_{CC}+\sigma^2I)^{-1}(y_C-m_C), \]
    \[ \Sigma_T=K_{TT}-K_{TC}(K_{CC}+\sigma^2I)^{-1}K_{CT}. \]

    The covariance couples all targets. Adding a target point and then marginalizing it exactly recovers the smaller posterior.

    These formulas are the Schur-complement conditional of one joint Gaussian:

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}y_C\\f_T\end{bmatrix} \sim\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}m_C\\m_T\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}K_{CC}+\sigma^2I&K_{CT}\\K_{TC}&K_{TT}\end{bmatrix} \right). \]

    Never form the matrix inverse explicitly. Solve a Cholesky system (with declared jitter if needed), and distinguish observation noise \(\sigma^2\) from numerical jitter. Jitter changes the computed covariance; it is not automatically part of the statistical likelihood.

    The posterior mean and covariance are two outputs of the same conditional law. A pipeline that reports only the mean, silently identifies jitter with noise, or changes hyperparameters between updates has changed this diagram's contract.

    Worked Two-Point Posterior¤

    Let prior mean be zero, variance one, and correlation \(k(x_1,x_2)=\rho\). The complete two-input prior is

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}f_1\\f_2\end{bmatrix} \sim\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1&\rho\\\rho&1\end{bmatrix} \right). \]

    After observing noiseless \(f(x_1)=a\), Gaussian conditioning gives

    \[ f(x_2)\mid f(x_1)=a \sim\mathcal N(\rho a,1-\rho^2). \]

    For \(\rho=0\), the observation gives no information. As \(|\rho|\to1\), posterior uncertainty vanishes. The kernel controls both adaptation and extrapolation.

    For \(a=2\) and \(\rho=0.75\), the posterior is \(\mathcal N(1.5,0.4375)\). A second noisy observation would not reduce variance to zero even at the observed input. This is a complete distributional statement; reporting only the posterior mean discards the feature that makes a GP a function-space generator.

    The same calculation is also a constructive sampler for the prior:

    \[ f_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad f_2\mid f_1\sim\mathcal N(\rho f_1,1-\rho^2). \]

    Indeed, \(\mathbb E[f_2]=0\), \(\operatorname{Var}(f_2)=\rho^2+(1-\rho^2)=1\), and \(\operatorname{Cov}(f_1,f_2)=\rho\). Thus ancestral conditional sampling and a joint two-dimensional Gaussian draw produce the same finite law. Using an independently resampled \(f_1\) inside the second equation would destroy that coupling.

    Exact Conditioning Is an Associativity Property¤

    For disjoint input sets \(A,B,T\), a GP posterior may be obtained by conditioning the prior jointly on observations at \(A\cup B\), or by first conditioning on \(A\) and then updating that posterior with \(B\). In exact arithmetic, both routes are conditionals of the same joint Gaussian and therefore give the same \(p(f_T\mid y_A,y_B)\). Implementations can disagree if they refit hyperparameters between updates, change the observation model, replace either posterior by an approximation, or accumulate numerical error.

    A three-input example makes the claim concrete. For \(|\rho|<1\), take

    \[ \begin{bmatrix}f_1\\f_2\\f_3\end{bmatrix} \sim\mathcal N\!\left(0, \begin{bmatrix} 1&\rho&\rho^2\\ \rho&1&\rho\\ \rho^2&\rho&1 \end{bmatrix} \right). \]

    This is the covariance of a stationary Gaussian Markov chain. Conditioning directly on \(f_1=a,f_2=b\) gives

    \[ f_3\mid f_1=a,f_2=b\sim\mathcal N(\rho b,1-\rho^2). \]

    Sequentially updating first with \(a\) and then with \(b\) gives the same law. By contrast, adding \((x_2,b)\) to a learned context encoder and calling the network again need not equal conditioning its earlier joint predictive distribution. That is the conditioning-consistency distinction used to evaluate Neural Processes.

    Marginal Likelihood and Kernel Learning¤

    The GP log marginal likelihood is

    \[ \log p(y\mid X)= -\frac12(y-m)^TK_y^{-1}(y-m) -\frac12\log|K_y| -\frac n2\log2\pi, \]

    where \(K_y=K+\sigma^2I\). The data-fit and log-determinant terms trade fit against volume/complexity. Hyperparameter optimization can have local optima and can underestimate uncertainty when the kernel family is misspecified.

    The log determinant is sometimes called an Occam factor, but it is not a universal guarantee against overfitting. Optimizing hyperparameters on one dataset and then treating them as known ignores hyperparameter uncertainty. Fully Bayesian inference integrates them; empirical Bayes plugs in an estimate. Both retain conditional Gaussian algebra but express different uncertainty.

    The objective is usually nonconvex in length scales, noise, amplitudes, inducing locations, and learned features. Report parameter transforms, priors, initialization, restarts, optimizer, stopping rule, and whether the displayed uncertainty conditions on a point estimate or integrates hyperparameters. Marginal likelihood compares models only inside the declared likelihood and prior family; it cannot diagnose a missing boundary condition or an inappropriate observation law by itself.

    RKHS Functions and GP Draws Are Different Objects¤

    Every positive-semidefinite kernel \(k\) determines a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space \(\mathcal H_k\) satisfying

    \[ \langle f,k(x,\cdot)\rangle_{\mathcal H_k}=f(x). \]

    The representer theorem makes this space computationally useful. For many regularized empirical-risk problems, a minimizer has the finite form

    \[ \widehat f(\cdot)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\alpha_i k(x_i,\cdot). \]

    With squared loss and a matched regularization/noise convention, kernel ridge regression produces the same point prediction as the posterior mean of GP regression. It does not produce the GP posterior covariance, hyperparameter posterior, or function samples. Equality of one estimator is not equality of probabilistic models.

    There is a deeper apparent paradox. \(\mathcal H_k\) describes finite-energy shifts of the Gaussian measure—its Cameron–Martin geometry—but in the usual infinite-dimensional, non-degenerate setting, a draw from \(\mathcal{GP}(0,k)\) lies outside \(\mathcal H_k\) with probability one. Brownian motion makes this concrete: the associated Cameron–Martin functions are absolutely continuous with a square-integrable derivative and a fixed starting value, while Brownian paths are almost surely nowhere differentiable. The RKHS controls directions of plausible shifts and regularized estimators; it is not generally the set in which random draws live. Finite-rank GPs are an important exception because their draws remain in the finite feature span.

    The Observation Model Controls Posterior Tractability¤

    The GP prior specifies a law for a latent function. A complete model also declares how observations arise:

    \[ f\sim\mathcal{GP}(m,k), \qquad y_i\mid f\sim p(y_i\mid f(x_i)). \]

    Independent Gaussian noise is conjugate, which is why the conditioning equations above are exact. Bernoulli or categorical likelihoods create GP classifiers; Poisson and point-process likelihoods create count and event models; Student-\(t\) likelihoods can reduce sensitivity to large residuals. For these non-Gaussian likelihoods, the posterior over \(f\) is generally not a GP. Laplace, expectation propagation, variational inference, MCMC, and augmentation methods approximate or sample different objects and have different failure modes.

    A link function evaluated at the posterior mean is not the posterior predictive probability. For binary logistic observations,

    \[ p(y_*=1\mid x_*,D) =\int \sigma(f_*)\,p(f_*\mid x_*,D)\,df_*, \]

    which generally differs from \(\sigma(\mathbb E[f_*\mid D])\). The integral carries latent-function uncertainty through the nonlinear link.

    Worked log-Gaussian Cox process boundary¤

    For event locations \(S=\{s_1,\ldots,s_N\}\) in a window \(W\), let

    \[ f\sim\mathcal{GP}(m,k), \qquad \lambda(s)=\exp(f(s)). \]

    Conditional on the complete intensity function, the point-pattern log likelihood is, up to the chosen reference-measure constant,

    \[ \begin{aligned} \log p(S\mid f)={}& \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(s_i)\\ &-\int_W \exp(f(s))\,ds. \end{aligned} \]

    The integral supplies the probability of no additional events in the rest of the window. Evaluating \(f\) only at observed event locations and multiplying \(\lambda(s_i)\) omits this normalizer and does not define the point-process likelihood. Gridding or quadrature approximates the integral and therefore becomes part of the realized inference protocol. The Point Processes and Random Finite Sets volume develops the associated cardinality, empty-space, and event-set semantics.

    Learning a GP Prior Across Related Functions¤

    When training data contain independent functions \(f^{(1)},\ldots,f^{(S)}\) from related tasks, the mean and covariance themselves can be learned across tasks. With complete noiseless functions, the elementary empirical construction is

    \[ \widehat m_S(x)=\frac1S\sum_{s=1}^S f^{(s)}(x), \]
    \[ \widehat k_S(x,x')= \frac1S\sum_{s=1}^S \bigl(f^{(s)}(x)-\widehat m_S(x)\bigr) \bigl(f^{(s)}(x')-\widehat m_S(x')\bigr). \]

    For any common finite query set, \(\widehat k_S\) is positive semidefinite because its Gram matrix is an empirical covariance. It may be low rank when the number of functions is small. Partial, noisy, and heterogeneous observation locations require latent-function inference; simply filling missing values and computing a covariance can confound imputation error with prior variation.

    Empirical Gaussian Processes, published at ICML 2026, develops this multi-task prior-learning view with an EM construction for heterogeneous observations and a function-space convergence result under the paper's assumptions. It still fits the closest Gaussian-process prior under its criterion; it does not make Gaussian finite-dimensional laws universally adequate for multimodal or heavy-tailed function populations. Once the mean and kernel are fitted, GP conditioning is exact for that fitted model. Finite-task estimation error, task-distribution shift, and ignored uncertainty in the learned prior remain separate issues.

    Scalability¤

    Dense Cholesky inference for a generic \(n\times n\) covariance costs \(O(n^3)\) time and \(O(n^2)\) memory. Those are costs of one standard algorithm for an unstructured matrix, not a lower bound for every GP. A scalability claim is incomplete until it states which object was approximated.

    Let \(Z=(z_1,\ldots,z_M)\) be inducing locations, \(u=f_Z\), and

    \[ Q_{AB}=K_{AZ}K_{ZZ}^{-1}K_{ZB}. \]

    This low-rank covariance appears in several methods that should not be conflated:

    • Subset of data

      Changes: discards observations, then performs exact inference on the retained subset.

      Report: selection rule and omitted-data sensitivity.

    • Random features / Nyström

      Changes: replaces the kernel or its feature representation by finite rank.

      Report: feature count, sampling or landmarks, kernel error, and randomness.

    • SoR / deterministic inducing conditional

      Changes: replaces train and test covariance by a degenerate \(Q\) construction.

      Report: rank collapse and variance behavior away from \(Z\).

    • DTC / FITC-style conditionals

      Changes: use different residual-covariance approximations at training and prediction points.

      Report: the precise train/test conditional convention; “sparse GP” is insufficient.

    • Sparse variational GP

      Changes: keeps the declared GP model but optimizes a variational posterior and bound using \(u\).

      Report: inducing-variable definition, \(M\), ELBO, variational family, minibatching, and gap diagnostics.

    • Structured kernel interpolation

      Changes: approximates kernel matrix–vector products through an inducing-grid and interpolation structure.

      Report: grid, interpolation rule, dimensional scaling, and boundary effects.

    • Conjugate gradients and stochastic Lanczos quadrature

      Changes: approximate solves and log determinants for the chosen finite kernel matrix.

      Report: residual tolerance, preconditioner, iterations, probe count, and stochastic error.

    • State-space conversion

      Changes: exploits a finite Markov representation of a one-dimensional kernel.

      Report: whether conversion is exact, state dimension, and discretization or parallel-scan details.

    • Local / Vecchia construction

      Changes: factors or approximates conditionals using neighborhoods.

      Report: ordering, neighbor sets, induced joint law, and long-range error.

    The classical variational construction uses

    \[ q(f,u)=p(f\mid u)q(u) \]

    and maximizes

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L &=\mathbb E_{q(f)}[\log p(y\mid f)]\\ &\quad-D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q(u)\,\|\,p(u)\right). \end{aligned} \]

    The inducing variables are variational summaries, not necessarily observed data and not necessarily point evaluations; inter-domain linear functionals are also possible. With point inducing variables and common dense algebra, batch computation often scales as \(O(nM^2+M^3)\) and minibatch steps as \(O(BM^2+M^3)\), but prediction, output dimension, whitening, structure, and implementation change the realized cost. Titsias (2009) derives the variational inducing-point view. Newer sparse-GP bounds relax the conditional variational factor in a particular construction to tighten the training bound; this is a change in approximation family, not evidence that every inducing method targets the same posterior.

    Matrix-free computation presents a different trade-off. Conjugate gradients may retain the selected kernel matrix while solving its linear system only to a tolerance; stochastic Lanczos quadrature estimates the log determinant with random probes. Calling the result “exact GP inference” without solver and estimator error is therefore misleading. Conversely, an approximation can be highly accurate without preserving the original model exactly. Model error and numerical error need separate ledgers.

    For one-dimensional inputs such as time, kernels with suitable rational spectral representations admit finite-dimensional linear state-space forms and Kalman inference. Matérn kernels with common half-integer smoothness values are central examples. The squared-exponential kernel does not have an exact finite-dimensional rational state-space representation, although controlled approximations are available. Regular grids, Kronecker products, Toeplitz structure, compact support, or sparsity provide other accelerations only when the inputs and covariance actually have that structure.

    Neural Processes make a different exchange: they amortize prediction over a task distribution rather than solving one GP posterior for every new task. Their cost and approximation error depend on meta-training, context size, target factorization, and architecture; they are not simply inducing-point GPs implemented by a neural network.

    Four Different GP-Neural Connections¤

    The phrase “a neural network is a Gaussian process” collapses several non-equivalent constructions:

    • Neural-network GP (NNGP)

      Object: random network weights at initialization.

      GP statement: under architecture, scaling, independence, activation, and infinite-width assumptions, finite output vectors converge to Gaussian laws with a recursive kernel.

      Boundary: a generic finite trained network does not thereby have a GP posterior.

    • Neural tangent kernel (NTK)

      Object: parameter Jacobian along gradient training.

      GP statement: in the specified infinite-width and lazy-training regime, the limiting kernel governs function evolution and can remain constant.

      Boundary: the NTK is not the NNGP covariance and does not describe unrestricted feature learning.

    • Deep kernel learning (DKL)

      Object: deterministic learned feature map inside \(k_0(g_\phi(x),g_\phi(x'))\).

      GP statement: conditional on fitted \(\phi\), this is one GP with a data-adapted covariance geometry.

      Boundary: marginal likelihood does not automatically prevent feature collapse or integrate uncertainty in \(\phi\).

    • Deep Gaussian process (DGP)

      Object: composition of random functions \(f_L\circ\cdots\circ f_1\).

      GP statement: every layer has a GP conditional prior.

      Boundary: the marginal output process is generally not Gaussian, and posterior inference is generally not analytic.

    Jacot, Gabriel, and Hongler establish the NTK result in its asymptotic training regime. Wilson et al. construct deep kernels, while Ober, Rasmussen, and van der Wilk exhibit overfitting pathologies for highly parameterized plug-in DKL and study a Bayesian treatment. These results support a component-level distinction: an expressive covariance, a posterior over its parameters, and calibrated extrapolation are three different claims.

    Infinite-width limits also extend beyond vector-input multilayer perceptrons. Infinite Neural Operators, published at NeurIPS 2025, derives function-valued GP limits for specified neural-operator parameterizations under its assumptions. That result characterizes those prior limits; it does not turn every finite trained neural operator into a calibrated stochastic operator.

    Vector Fields and Symmetry¤

    For vector-valued outputs, matrix-valued kernels encode correlations among components. Translation, rotation, reflection, divergence-free, or curl-free structure can be built into the kernel. Equivariant stochastic-field work connects these GP constraints to steerable Neural Processes.

    GP Limitations¤

    Gaussian finite-dimensional marginals can be too restrictive for multimodal function posteriors. Kernel selection may be difficult, dense generic scaling is poor, and stationary kernels extrapolate according to prior assumptions rather than learned semantic reasoning. A flexible learned kernel can distort distances, collapse features, or overfit the marginal likelihood; “the GP layer supplies uncertainty” is not a calibration theorem. Non-Gaussian likelihoods, hyperparameter fitting, approximate inference, and numerical solvers add distinct approximation layers.

    Counterexample: Stationarity at a Boundary¤

    A stationary kernel depends only on \(x-x'\). On a bounded physical domain, two points equally separated near and far from a boundary receive the same prior covariance, even when boundary conditions make their behavior different. Coordinate features, nonstationary kernels, or physics-informed constructions may be needed. Translation equivariance is a modeling assumption, not a free form of generalization.

    Conditional Neural Processes encode each context pair, aggregate with a permutation-invariant operation, and decode at targets:

    \[ r_i=h_\theta(x_i,y_i), \qquad r_C=\operatorname{Agg}_{i\in C}r_i, \]
    \[ p_\theta(y_T\mid X_T,C) =\prod_{j\in T}p_\theta(y_j\mid x_j,r_C). \]

    Mean/sum aggregation makes context order irrelevant. The common factorized decoder predicts marginal uncertainty but makes target outputs conditionally independent given \(r_C\), limiting coherent global samples.

    Permutation invariance is necessary but not sufficient. Mean aggregation loses context cardinality unless count is supplied; sum aggregation exposes cardinality but can change scale outside training ranges. Deep Sets give broad representation results under assumptions, yet a finite bottleneck can still discard local detail or conflicting evidence.

    The CNP objective usually samples a task/function, a context subset, and targets, then maximizes conditional target log probability. It learns a conditional prediction map directly; it is not generally exact Bayesian conditioning under an explicit learned prior process. The 2026 conditioning-gap result in Chapter 1 makes this boundary quantitative for a restricted class.

    Latent Neural Processes¤

    A global latent variable \(z\) induces shared uncertainty:

    \[ p(y_T\mid X_T,C) =\int p(z\mid C) \prod_{j\in T}p(y_j\mid x_j,z,C)dz. \]

    Training commonly uses a variational bound with a target-informed posterior approximation and a context prior. Sampling one \(z\) for all targets produces correlated function realizations. Posterior collapse, amortization gaps, and latent identifiability issues are inherited from VAEs.

    A common training bound is

    \[ \mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid C,T)} \left[\sum_{j\in T}\log p_\theta(y_j\mid x_j,z,C)\right] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid C,T)\|q_\phi(z\mid C)\right). \]

    Here the context encoder supplies a learned conditional prior and the target-informed encoder supplies a training-time posterior. At test time only \(q_\phi(z\mid C)\) is available. A loose bound or mismatch between those encoders can produce poor predictive uncertainty even if reconstruction is strong.

    One global latent captures task-level ambiguity such as amplitude, phase, or regime. It may not express fine local stochasticity without local latents, correlated decoder noise, autoregressive targets, flows, or diffusion. Conversely, independent per-target latents do not automatically create a coherent function because their coupling across query sets must be specified.

    Attention¤

    Pooling all context into one vector creates an information bottleneck. Cross-attention lets each target retrieve relevant context values; self-attention models context interactions. Attentive NPs improve local adaptation at quadratic or structured-attention cost.

    Attention must remain permutation-equivariant over context/target sets unless coordinate order is meaningful. Positional coordinates describe the domain; arbitrary list position should not become a hidden signal.

    Cross-attention makes the prediction at \(x_j\) depend on nearby or relevant observations instead of one global summary. Self-attention can represent dependencies among targets, but target–target attention changes computational and probabilistic semantics: parallel deterministic outputs, masked autoregressive conditionals, and jointly parameterized covariance are different models.

    Convolutional and Equivariant NPs¤

    Convolutional CNPs map irregular observations to a continuous representation and use translation-equivariant convolutions. Equivariance encodes how posterior predictions transform with the domain. Translation-Equivariant Transformer NPs combine transformer set functions with stationarity-related translation symmetry.

    Symmetry is an assumption. A stationary/translation-equivariant model can fail under boundaries, trends, changing regimes, or nonstationary sensors.

    Transformer Neural Processes¤

    TNPs use transformer architectures to approximate posterior prediction maps. They can represent rich dependencies but do not inherit GP consistency merely by using attention. Masking scheme, output factorization, and joint target modeling determine whether they form coherent stochastic-process approximations.

    Transformer Neural Processes cast meta-learning as sequence modeling and include variants with different likelihood factorizations. An autoregressive target model defines

    \[ p(y_T\mid X_T,C)=\prod_{j=1}^{n_T} p(y_{\pi(j)}\mid x_{\pi(j)},C,y_{\pi(<j)},X_T), \]

    for an ordering \(\pi\). It can express rich joint dependence, but sampling becomes sequential and the resulting set law must be invariant to, averaged over, or canonically tied to the ordering. Randomly permuting targets during training does not by itself prove exact permutation consistency.

    Meta-Training Distribution¤

    An NP learns across tasks/functions \(f^{(1)},\ldots,f^{(M)}\). Generalization depends on whether the new function and context-selection mechanism resemble meta-training. Within-task held-out targets test interpolation; held-out task families test meta-generalization.

    Worked Aggregation Example¤

    Let two contexts encode to \(r_1=(1,0)\) and \(r_2=(0,1)\). Mean aggregation gives \((0.5,0.5)\) in either order. Duplicating the first observation changes the mean to \((2/3,1/3)\); set aggregation is permutation invariant but not necessarily invariant to repeated evidence. Repeated measurements should be handled according to the noise model, not silently deduplicated.

    Worked Shared-Latent Correlation¤

    Let \(z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and \(y_j=z+\epsilon_j\) with independent \(\epsilon_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2)\). Then

    \[ \operatorname{Cov}(y_i,y_j)=1\quad(i\ne j), \qquad \operatorname{Var}(y_j)=1+\sigma^2. \]

    Sampling one \(z\) jointly creates coherent vertical shifts. Sampling a fresh \(z_j\) per target instead makes cross-target covariance zero and produces jagged draws. The marginal distribution at every point remains identical, so pointwise NLL or calibration cannot distinguish the two.

    The fork through one \(z\) is the source of global sample coherence in the basic latent NP. Matching each target marginal does not make the independent-latent branch equivalent to the shared-latent branch.

    Discrete State-Space Models and Continuous Transition Laws¤

    At observation times \(t_0<t_1<\cdots<t_n\), a latent process generator can be written

    \[ p(z_0)\prod_{i=1}^{n} K_{\Delta_i}(z_i\mid z_{i-1},u_i) p(y_i\mid z_i), \qquad \Delta_i=t_i-t_{i-1}. \]

    The transition kernel \(K_{\Delta}\) is the common interface between discrete and continuous time. In an HMM it is a transition matrix, often with a fixed step. In a linear-Gaussian model it is a Gaussian transition. For an SDE, it is the Markov transition law induced over elapsed time \(\Delta\). A numerical solver samples an approximation to that kernel; it is not the kernel itself.

    This same object supports three roles:

    • as a generator, sample \(z_0\), transitions, and emissions;
    • as a forecast model, condition the starting distribution on observed context and propagate it forward;
    • as an inference model, combine transitions with emissions to estimate latent paths.

    Changing the inference algorithm does not change the declared generative law. Conversely, changing a solver, time discretization, or learned transition can change the effective law even if the inference network is held fixed. The finite-state construction in Classical Generative Models and the controlled construction in World Models instantiate the same separation.

    The discrete-time inference algorithms are developed systematically in Filtering and Smoothing for State-Space Generators. That volume owns Kalman, RTS, nonlinear Gaussian, mixture, ensemble, robust, particle, and learned filter comparisons; this chapter owns continuous-time path semantics and solver-induced transition error.

    The arrows define the generative law; the filter and smoother are different queries over that law. Replacing the filter does not replace the dynamics, while changing the elapsed-gap transition or its solver can change the effective law.

    For temporal processes, a latent state may follow

    \[ dz_t=f_\theta(z_t,t)dt+g_\theta(z_t,t)dW_t, \]

    with observation model

    \[ y_i\sim p_\theta(y_i\mid z_{t_i}). \]

    This defines a path distribution together with an initial law and SDE assumptions. Irregular observation times enter the solver/transition rather than requiring artificial equal spacing.

    A complete model declares \(p(z_0)\), drift, diffusion, driving-noise dimension, Itô versus Stratonovich interpretation, observation process, and boundary/explosion behavior. Local Lipschitz and growth conditions are common sufficient conditions for a unique nonexplosive strong solution; neural coefficients do not satisfy them merely because they are differentiable in software.

    The latent path law and observation law differ. Even when \(z_t\) is Markov, noisy partial observations \(y_i\) need not be Markov. A generator of observations must sample both latent dynamics and emissions at the requested times.

    Filtering, Smoothing, and Generation¤

    • filtering estimates \(p(z_t\mid y_{t_i\le t})\);
    • smoothing uses past and future observations;
    • forecasting samples future paths given context;
    • unconditional generation samples initial state, driving noise, and observations.

    An encoder over the full series performs smoothing, not online filtering. Evaluation must match the deployment information set.

    For a missing interval inside an observed trajectory, bidirectional context supports smoothing/interpolation. For future targets, using any later observation leaks information. Irregular forecasting benchmarks should state the cutoff time, available covariates, event-time mechanism, and whether the next observation time is known or itself random.

    Particle Filtering Without Hiding the Proposal¤

    The complete family-independent derivation appears in Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods. This section retains the stochastic-process specialization: irregular observation times, transition kernels induced by discretized or exact process dynamics, and path-space evaluation.

    Suppose particles approximate \(p(z_{i-1}\mid y_{1:i-1})\). After resampling ancestors \(a_j\), draw

    \[ z_i^{(j)}\sim q_i(z_i\mid z_{i-1}^{(a_j)},y_i), \]

    and assign incremental weight

    \[ \widetilde w_i^{(j)} \propto \frac{p(y_i\mid z_i^{(j)}) K_{\Delta_i}(z_i^{(j)}\mid z_{i-1}^{(a_j)})} {q_i(z_i^{(j)}\mid z_{i-1}^{(a_j)},y_i)}. \]

    The bootstrap filter chooses the transition itself as \(q_i\), so the transition terms cancel and the new weights are observation likelihoods. This makes the method simple, but it can be inefficient when the new observation is surprising under most transition samples. An observation-aware proposal can reduce weight variance, provided its density and support make the importance ratio valid.

    If resampling is skipped at this step, the displayed incremental factor must also be multiplied by the selected ancestor's previous normalized weight. Omitting that factor silently changes the particle approximation.

    Resampling controls current weight collapse by duplicating high-weight particles and discarding low-weight ones. It also reduces diversity among ancestral paths. Consequently, a particle cloud that is adequate for \(p(z_i\mid y_{1:i})\) can give a poor approximation to old smoothed states. Report particle count, proposal, resampling rule and threshold, effective sample size, repeated-run variability, and the separate smoothing procedure. “Used a particle filter” is not a complete inference protocol.

    Variational Latent SDEs¤

    A recognition process approximates the posterior path. Continuous-time ELBOs often contain observation log likelihood and a path-space KL between posterior and prior drifts, evaluated with stochastic solvers. Girsanov-based expressions require compatible diffusion coefficients and absolute-continuity conditions.

    When prior and recognition SDEs share diffusion \(g\) and their drift difference lies in its range, a typical path KL contains

    \[ \frac12\mathbb E_q\int_0^T \left\|g(z_t,t)^{\dagger} \bigl(f_q(z_t,t)-f_p(z_t,t)\bigr)\right\|^2dt, \]

    plus any initial-law KL, under the required Novikov/integrability and absolute-continuity conditions. If posterior and prior diffusions differ arbitrarily, their path measures may be singular and this formula is invalid. A discrete Gaussian-transition KL is not automatically the same infinite-dimensional path KL.

    Solver discretization, stochastic-gradient variance, posterior approximation, and observation misspecification all contribute error. A smaller step size reduces numerical error but not model or inference error.

    Training also requires a derivative convention: reverse differentiation through the realized numerical steps and a continuous stochastic adjoint are not the same finite program. The generative-process gradient chapter separates Brownian-path coupling, solver error, path sampling, and adjoint error.

    Euler–Maruyama has strong order \(1/2\) under standard conditions; higher-order methods require additional regularity and sometimes derivatives or iterated stochastic integrals. Adaptive solvers must preserve the stochastic interpretation and random-number coupling. Report tolerance/step size, NFE, Brownian construction, and convergence under refinement.

    Neural SDEs as Implicit Path Generators¤

    Neural SDEs as infinite-dimensional GANs train path generators adversarially using neural controlled differential-equation critics. This is an implicit distribution over paths: the driving Brownian motion supplies randomness, while pathwise density is not usually evaluated.

    The discriminator/critic observes interpolated paths through a controlled differential equation, so path representation and interpolation influence the learned discrepancy. Adversarial success on a sampled grid is not proof of equality of continuous path measures.

    State-Space and Operator Acceleration¤

    Continuous recurrent units exploit linear-SDE/Kalman structure for closed-form updates. LS4 combines latent state-space dynamics with structured state-space computation for long sequences. Fast convolutional evaluation changes computational structure; it does not automatically establish a coherent law at arbitrary continuous times.

    Deep Bayesian Filter, published at ICML 2025, learns a structured latent space intended to retain analytic Gaussian updates for its specified nonlinear data-assimilation setting. Its closed-form update is a property of those learned structural restrictions, not evidence that arbitrary neural state-space posteriors are Gaussian. Parallel variational Monte Carlo, published at ICML 2026, instead constructs an importance-smoothing training objective with temporal parallelization and reports results on its named state-estimation and generative benchmarks. It is a bounded systems result: smoothing during training does not turn an online filter into a smoother, and benchmark speedups do not remove importance-weight or model-misspecification error.

    Worked Ornstein–Uhlenbeck Transition¤

    For

    \[ dz_t=-\lambda z_tdt+\sigma dW_t, \]

    the transition over interval \(\Delta\) is

    \[ z_{t+\Delta}\mid z_t \sim\mathcal N\left( e^{-\lambda\Delta}z_t, \frac{\sigma^2}{2\lambda}(1-e^{-2\lambda\Delta}) \right). \]

    Irregular gaps change both predictive mean decay and uncertainty. Reusing a fixed discrete-time transition for every gap would be incorrect.

    For \(\lambda=1\), \(\sigma=2\), \(z_t=3\), and \(\Delta=0.5\), the conditional mean is \(3e^{-0.5}\approx1.82\) and variance is \(2(1-e^{-1})\approx1.26\). At \(\Delta=2\), the mean is about \(0.41\) and variance about \(1.96\), close to the stationary variance \(\sigma^2/(2\lambda)=2\). This separates uncertainty from elapsed dynamics, not just from the count of observations.

    Pathwise Evaluation¤

    Marginal histograms can match while temporal dependence fails. Evaluate autocorrelation, cross-spectra, event durations, hitting times, increments at multiple lags, path signatures, calibration, and downstream decisions. Forecasting scores should separate aleatoric path uncertainty from observation noise.

    Counterexample: Correct One-Time Marginals, Wrong Paths¤

    Let \(X_t\) be a stationary OU process and let \(Y_t\) be independent draws from the OU stationary Gaussian at every queried time. For each \(t\), \(X_t\) and \(Y_t\) have the same marginal. But \(X\) has exponentially decaying autocorrelation and continuous paths, whereas \(Y\) has no temporal correlation and no ordinary continuous-time version with independent values at all times. Single-time histograms cannot validate a path generator.

    Diffusing Functions¤

    Instead of adding independent noise to a fixed vector, define a noise process \(\epsilon(\cdot)\), for example a GP, and corrupt an entire function:

    \[ f_t(x)=a_t f_0(x)+b_t\epsilon(x). \]

    For every finite query set this induces multivariate Gaussian corruption with covariance inherited from the noise kernel. Stochastic Process Diffusion applies this view to temporal data as continuous functions.

    If functions live in a separable Hilbert space \(H\), a Gaussian reference \(\mathcal N(0,Q)\) is an \(H\)-valued random element only under appropriate covariance conditions, commonly that \(Q\) is positive, self-adjoint, and trace class. A formal identity-covariance Gaussian generally does not exist as an \(H\)-valued measure in infinite dimensions. “Standard Gaussian noise at every coordinate” can therefore be a generalized random distribution rather than a sample in the desired function space.

    Diffusion Generative Models in Infinite Dimensions develops Gaussian-measure constructions in Hilbert/Sobolev spaces. Neural Diffusion Processes instead learns rich function distributions through finite marginals with exchangeability-aware attention. These are related but not identical claims: an architecture operating on arbitrary finite sets is not automatically a measure on a specified Sobolev space.

    White noise, smooth GP noise, and operator-adapted noise define different function-space geometries. Independent noise at sampled points may fail to extend to regular sample paths.

    Worked Finite-Set Score Calculation¤

    For \(f_t(X)=a_tf_0(X)+b_t\epsilon(X)\) with \(\epsilon(X)\sim\mathcal N(0,K_X)\) and fixed clean \(f_0\), the conditional score is

    \[ \nabla_{f_t(X)}\log p(f_t(X)\mid f_0(X)) =-\frac1{b_t^2}K_X^{-1} \bigl(f_t(X)-a_tf_0(X)\bigr). \]

    The kernel inverse couples coordinates. Setting \(K_X=I\) at every sampled grid gives simple coordinate noise but says nothing about continuity between points. Under grid refinement, noise covariance and any quadrature-weighted norm must approximate the same function-space object.

    Scores in Function Space¤

    On a finite query set, the score is a vector gradient of the joint log density. In an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, one needs a reference Gaussian measure, Fréchet/Cameron–Martin derivatives, and domain assumptions. Training only on random grids may learn finite-dimensional scores without guaranteeing cross-grid consistency.

    There is usually no Lebesgue measure on an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space, so the finite-dimensional phrase “gradient of log density” must be replaced by a derivative relative to a reference measure or an integration-by-parts object. Formulae are construction-specific; copying the Euclidean score while dropping covariance operators can change both units and target.

    Resolution and Discretization Consistency¤

    A resolution-independent claim should test:

    1. train and evaluate on different query densities;
    2. restrict a fine-grid sample to a coarse grid and compare with direct coarse sampling;
    3. verify convergence as grid spacing and solver tolerance change;
    4. preserve physical units and noise scaling under refinement;
    5. test irregular, nonnested query sets.

    Neural operators map functions to functions, but a deterministic operator is not a stochastic-process generator until supplied with a coherent random input and probabilistic semantics.

    Functional Flow Matching defines a path of measures from a Gaussian reference to data in function space and learns a function-valued vector field. It is a continuous transport family, not a Neural Process conditional predictor by default. Its finite implementation inherits approximation from basis/grid representation, operator architecture, and ODE integration.

    Operator Flow Matching instead emphasizes learning a stochastic-process prior by transporting a GP reference with a neural operator, then evaluating finite-query densities and performing functional regression. The paper derives a function-space construction, but its implementation replaces a supremum over every finite query collection with sampled expectations and uses numerical ODE integration, stochastic trace estimation, and SGLD for posterior sampling. “Likelihood available” therefore does not mean every reported finite-grid log density or posterior draw is numerically exact.

    A clean discretization test uses restriction operators \(R_{h\leftarrow h'}\). If \(G_h\) is the generated law on grid \(h\), consistency asks whether

    \[ (R_{h\leftarrow h'})_\#G_{h'}\approx G_h \]

    as the fine grid \(h'\) is restricted to \(h\), and whether both converge toward one limiting law. Similar performance at two resolutions without this distributional comparison is weaker evidence.

    The two branches must reuse the same declared process-level construction, not merely the same tensor shape. A low error at each resolution is compatible with a large distributional gap between the restricted and directly generated laws.

    SPDEs and Random Solution Operators¤

    A stochastic PDE may be written schematically

    \[ du_t=\mathcal A(u_t)dt+\mathcal G(u_t)dW_t, \]

    where \(u_t\) is field-valued. Generative tasks include sampling solution fields, learning uncertain dynamics, or emulating the map from forcing/noise to solution. Boundary conditions, mesh geometry, and driving-noise discretization are part of the law.

    An operator surrogate may condition on coefficients, forcing, initial/boundary data, or a low-fidelity solution. Stochasticity can represent random forcing, uncertain inputs, unresolved scales, posterior uncertainty, or model error; these meanings should not be collapsed. A deterministic PDE with unknown inputs induces a different uncertainty problem from an SPDE with intrinsic process noise.

    Diffusion Bridges and Conditional Processes¤

    Conditioning a process on observations or endpoints yields a bridge measure. Score-based bridge methods and stochastic control can generate posterior paths without reducing the target to one fixed vector. Exact bridge formulas are rare; learned controls and discretization introduce approximation.

    Functional Adjoint Sampler, accepted at ICML 2026, develops a stochastic-control sampler for Gibbs-type targets on infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces. This is peer-reviewed research evidence for a specified functional representation and target class, not a generic guarantee that arbitrary neural path constraints are sampled exactly.

    Neural-Operator Research¤

    Expanding the Chaos, accepted at ICML 2026, uses Wiener-chaos features and neural operators to learn SDE/SPDE solution behavior and reconstruct trajectories from driving noise. This is peer-reviewed research evidence for stochastic solution operators. It is not a general theorem that one forward pass reproduces every SPDE path law across discretizations.

    FLORAL, published as an ICLR 2026 conference paper, learns operator-valued flow-matching corrections from low- to high-fidelity PDE solutions. Its cross-resolution and uncertainty results are tied to the named advection, Burgers, and Darcy benchmarks, discretizations, low-fidelity solvers, and Fourier-operator architecture.

    FNOPE, published at NeurIPS 2025, uses an FNO-parameterized flow-matching field for simulation-based posterior inference over function-valued parameters. Its ability to evaluate requested discretizations is an inference-system property under a declared function-valued prior and the paper's training grids and simulator benchmarks; it does not turn every FNO into a calibrated stochastic process.

    Split by independent functions/tasks before splitting coordinates; otherwise observations from the same latent function can leak across train and test. Report the number of tasks, observations per task, context-size distribution, query mechanism, spatial/temporal resolution, normalization units, and whether missingness is simulated or real.

    Compute reporting includes parameters, active parameters, training function evaluations, solver NFE, memory, wall clock/hardware, sampling batch size, and cost per queried coordinate or field. For amortized methods, separate one-time training cost from per-task adaptation and sampling.

    Failure Modes¤

    • correct marginals but incoherent target covariance;
    • context aggregation bottlenecks;
    • latent collapse and underdispersed samples;
    • meta-training leakage between tasks/functions;
    • interpolation-heavy evaluation presented as forecasting;
    • grid-specific noise scaling;
    • neural operator violating boundary or conservation constraints;
    • SDE solver error mistaken for stochastic uncertainty;

    • symmetry assumptions failing under nonstationarity.

    • learned conditioning updates that disagree with the model's earlier joint law;

    • an identity-noise discretization presented as an \(H\)-valued Gaussian;
    • function-space claims without a named norm/reference measure;
    • test tasks sharing latent trajectories or PDE simulations with training;
    • stochasticity that conflates observation noise, parameter uncertainty, and intrinsic forcing.

    Cumulative Worked Study: Irregular Climate Fields¤

    Design a conditional generator for daily temperature fields from sparse stations and uncertain forcing.

    1. Object: a field \(f(t,s)\) with declared temporal interval, spatial domain, units, and regularity.
    2. Context: station observations with sensor-specific noise and a documented placement/missingness process.
    3. Conditioning: past observations imply forecasting; observations on both sides imply smoothing; forcing scenarios are separate covariates.
    4. Family: a GP is an interpretable small-data baseline; an equivariant NP amortizes across days/regions; a latent SDE handles temporal paths; function diffusion/operator flow represents non-Gaussian fields.
    5. Law: specify whether randomness represents weather variability, measurement error, parameter uncertainty, or unresolved physics.
    6. Evaluation: hold out whole weather systems/years, test station permutations and nested grids, check spectra/extremes/spell duration, calibrate regional integrals, and verify boundary/physical units.
    7. Evidence: compare at equal data and compute, report independent-task uncertainty, and avoid calling reanalysis agreement an independent physical validation.

    The “best” family depends on which of these semantics and workloads matter. A lower pointwise RMSE does not compensate for incoherent extreme-event probabilities.

    Source-Tracing Checklist¤

    For every result, record the paper version and evidence status; process/function space and norm; finite-grid or infinite-dimensional claim; context/target and task split; observation/noise semantics; architecture and objective; query/grid resolution; solver and NFE; calibration/joint/path metrics; and independent reproduction status.

    Cumulative retrieval: stochastic-process generators
    1. What consistency condition distinguishes a process model from unrelated predictors for each query grid?
    2. Which uncertainty sources should a latent stochastic-dynamics model separate?
    3. Why can low fixed-grid error coexist with incoherent refinement behavior?
    4. What evaluation supports a claim of function-space rather than discretization-specific generation?

    Solutions. (1) Marginals on overlapping finite query sets must agree under restriction, up to explicitly bounded approximation. (2) Latent initial state, process/dynamics noise, parameter or posterior uncertainty, and observation noise. (3) The network can learn grid-index correlations without defining compatible laws when points are inserted, removed, or moved. (4) Held-out locations, irregular and refined grids, restriction consistency, calibrated joint samples, boundary/physics tests, and comparison across discretizations with the same underlying functions.

    Final Synthesis¤

    Stochastic-process generators model random functions rather than fixed vectors. GPs guarantee coherent finite-dimensional laws analytically; Neural Processes amortize context-conditioned prediction across tasks; latent variables and attention improve global uncertainty and local retrieval; neural SDEs generate path measures; function diffusion and stochastic operators extend denoising and learned solution maps to fields. The central acceptance test is cross-query and cross-resolution coherence, not merely the ability to accept variable-size tensors.
