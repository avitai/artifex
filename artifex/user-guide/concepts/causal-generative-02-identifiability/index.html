    2. Identifiability and Causal Representation Learning¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapter 1, latent-variable identifiability, and equivalence relations.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Order identification before estimation: distinguish a causal estimand, identifying functional or bound, support conditions, estimator, and numerical algorithm.
    2. O2 — Prove query-specific identification: show why the running model's ATE is identified while its factual-unit coupling is only bounded.
    3. O3 — Use graphical identification: recognize adjustment and front-door structures and explain what do-calculus and the ID algorithm add.
    4. O4 — Construct non-identifiability witnesses: produce two compatible models or representations that disagree on the requested causal query.
    5. O5 — Audit causal-representation theorems: record observation-map, environment, coverage, ambiguity, and guarantee assumptions.
    6. O6 — Transfer only invariant claims: determine which causal queries survive the residual equivalence class of a learned representation.

    Estimation begins only after identification¤

    A causal quantity is identifiable when all causal models compatible with the stated assumptions and observed distribution agree on that quantity. An estimator can converge perfectly and still estimate the wrong target if identification fails. More neural capacity cannot repair missing causal information.

    Identification is query-specific. A graph may be only partially oriented while one average effect is still identified; conversely, knowing the DAG may not identify a unit counterfactual when latent response coupling is unspecified. Distinguish:

    • graph/Markov-equivalence identification;
    • interventional-distribution identification;
    • counterfactual-functional identification;
    • latent representation identification up to a named transformation group;
    • finite-sample estimation and computation.

    Identification-before-estimation decision sequence¤

    The order of work is a correctness condition, not editorial preference:

    An estimand is the mathematical target; an identification formula rewrites it using available distributions; an estimator is a finite-sample algorithm for that formula. If step 4 fails, a larger generator or lower optimization loss estimates an assumption-chosen completion, not the missing causal information. If step 6 fails, a population formula may exist while the available data provide no stable support for it.

    From adjustment to graphical identification¤

    Back-door adjustment is one identification pattern, not the definition of identification. With latent confounding, no observed set may block every back-door path, yet a causal effect can still be identified through other observed mechanisms. In the classical front-door graph, an observed mediator \(M\) intercepts every directed path from \(X\) to \(Y\); there is no unblocked back-door path from \(X\) to \(M\); and \(X\) blocks every back-door path from \(M\) to \(Y\). Under consistency, positivity, and those graphical conditions,

    \[ p(y\mid do(x)) =\sum_m p(m\mid x) \sum_{x'} p(y\mid m,x')p(x'). \]

    The inner sum estimates the causal effect of changing \(M\) after blocking its back-door paths with \(X\); the outer sum transports that response through the mediator distribution induced by \(X\). Conditioning on \(M\) alone would generally open or retain bias and is not the formula above.

    For general acyclic latent-variable models, observed margins can be represented by acyclic directed mixed graphs (ADMGs). The complete identification algorithms for the causal hierarchy determine when an interventional or counterfactual query can be reduced to available lower-level distributions and return a graphical obstruction when it cannot. Do-calculus is complete for the corresponding nonparametric effect-identification problem; this does not say that every query is identifiable, that the returned functional has good finite-sample support, or that high-dimensional estimation is easy.

    An expert workflow therefore has three possible outputs before model fitting: an observed-data functional, a sharp or assumption-indexed bound, or a non-identification certificate. A neural generator is an estimator or assumption-chosen completion downstream of that output.

    Return to the finite SCM: one identified effect, one unidentified coupling¤

    Return to the running finite SCM. Its graph has observed back-door set \(C\), and every \(P(T=t\mid C=c)\) is either \(1/4\) or \(3/4\), so positivity holds. Define \(m_t(c)=P(Y=1\mid T=t,C=c)\) and \(p_t^{\mathrm{do}}=P(Y=1\mid do(T=t))\). Under causal sufficiency, consistency, and the declared graph,

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_t^{\mathrm{do}} &=\sum_{c=0}^{1}m_t(c)P(C=c). \end{aligned} \]

    The intervention probabilities \(p_0^{\mathrm{do}}=1/4\) and \(p_1^{\mathrm{do}}=3/4\), and hence the ATE \(1/2\), are identified from the observational joint. Estimation begins only after this equality and its assumptions have been recorded.

    The unit counterfactual uses more information. Within \(C=1\), both the observational and interventional laws determine only

    \[ \begin{aligned} P(Y_0=1\mid C=1)&=\frac38,\\ P(Y_1=1\mid C=1)&=\frac78. \end{aligned} \]

    They do not determine how \(Y_0\) and \(Y_1\) are paired within a unit. Two response-type couplings illustrate the ambiguity:

    Table CAUSAL.7. Return to the finite SCM: one identified effect, one unidentified coupling
    Response type \((Y_0,Y_1)\) Shared-threshold SCM A SCM B with a harm type
    \((0,0)\) \(1/8\) \(0\)
    \((0,1)\) \(4/8\) \(5/8\)
    \((1,0)\) \(0\) \(1/8\)
    \((1,1)\) \(3/8\) \(2/8\)

    Keep SCM B identical to SCM A for \(C=0\) and for the treatment mechanism. For \(C=1\), SCM B can be implemented with \(U_Y\sim\operatorname{Uniform}\{0,\ldots,7\}\) by assigning \(U_Y=0,1\) to \((1,1)\), \(U_Y=2\) to \((1,0)\), and \(U_Y=3,\ldots,7\) to \((0,1)\), then setting \(Y=Y_T(U_Y)\). Both SCMs reproduce every displayed \(P(Y\mid T,C)\) and therefore the same observational and interventional distributions. Let \(E=\{C=1,T=0,Y=0\}\) denote the factual untreated failure. Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} P_A(Y_1=1\mid E)&=\frac{4/8}{5/8}=\frac45,\\ P_B(Y_1=1\mid E)&=\frac{5/8}{5/8}=1. \end{aligned} \]

    The shared-threshold construction imposes monotonicity: no unit has response type \((1,0)\). That structural restriction identifies \(4/5\) within the restricted class; it is not learned merely by fitting the two outcome marginals.

    Sensitivity as a response-coupling calculation¤

    Let

    \[ q_{11}=P(Y_0=1,Y_1=1\mid C=1). \]

    The Fréchet bounds from the two known marginals are

    \[ \max\!\left(0,\frac38+\frac78-1\right) \le q_{11}\le \min\!\left(\frac38,\frac78\right), \qquad \frac28\le q_{11}\le\frac38. \]

    Consequently,

    \[ P(Y_1=1\mid Y_0=0,C=1) =\frac{7/8-q_{11}}{5/8} \in\left[\frac45,1\right]. \]

    Reporting this sharp finite-model range is more informative than silently selecting one neural coupling. Peer-reviewed research. Partial counterfactual identification generalizes this principle by optimizing bounds over canonical finite SCMs for discrete observed domains. Positive identification results add explicit structure: exogenous isomorphism studies bijective and triangular-monotone SCM classes, while dynamic optimal transport gives a flow-based construction under its monotonicity, rank-preservation, and regularity conditions. Preprint. A non-monotone triangular-SCM result proposes a broader assumption set based on mechanism-wise invertibility and context-independent inverse transport. These are class-specific routes that reduce the compatible coupling set; none erases the identification step for an unrestricted generator.

    The diagram below makes the logical object explicit: data and assumptions shrink a compatible model set; a query may be constant on that set, vary over a bounded range, or remain too weakly constrained for a useful answer.

    Finite-SCM identification and sensitivity
    1. Which assumptions identify the ATE in the running model?
    2. Why do SCMs A and B agree on every intervention marginal but disagree after conditioning on factual failure?
    3. What changes if \(P(T=1\mid C=1)\) becomes one?

    Solutions. (1) The declared causal graph and sufficiency of \(C\), consistency, and positivity justify back-door adjustment. (2) Marginals determine the row and column sums of the response-type table, not its within-unit cells; factual failure selects cells using that unresolved coupling. (3) Positivity fails for untreated units in the \(C=1\) stratum, so the observational data no longer identify \(P(Y\mid T=0,C=1)\) without extrapolation or other regimes.

    For a two-variable observational distribution with no extra assumptions, both \(X\to Y\) and \(Y\to X\) can often represent the data. Conditional-independence methods usually recover a Markov equivalence class rather than a uniquely oriented DAG. Non-Gaussianity, additive-noise restrictions, temporal ordering, known interventions, multiple environments, or background knowledge can shrink the equivalence class—but every gain comes from an assumption or an additional data regime.

    Latent variables make the problem harder¤

    Suppose high-dimensional observations are generated from latent causal variables:

    \[ Z_i=f_i(Z_{\operatorname{pa}(i)},U_i), \qquad X=g(Z,\epsilon). \]

    Causal representation learning seeks a representation \(h(X)\) aligned, up to an explicitly characterized ambiguity, with the causal variables \(Z\). Unsupervised disentanglement from a single IID observational distribution is generally non-identifiable: many invertible transformations of \(Z\) induce the same \(p(X)\).

    Even recovering latents up to component-wise invertible transformations may be sufficient for some graph or intervention queries and insufficient for others. A theorem should state its equivalence class—permutation, component-wise transformation, scaling, affine map, or a broader graph-preserving transformation—and the curriculum should evaluate only invariant claims.

    The useful research question is therefore not “did the model discover the true factors?” but:

    • what equivalence class is identifiable;
    • from which observations, interventions, views, time structure, or environments;
    • under which assumptions on \(g\), the mechanisms, noise, sparsity, or interventions;
    • and which downstream causal queries remain invariant within that class.

    Auxiliary information and nonlinear ICA¤

    Identifiable VAE / nonlinear ICA results show how auxiliary variables can restore identifiability under conditional exponential-family and mixing assumptions. Schematically,

    \[ p(z\mid e)=\prod_i p(z_i\mid e), \qquad x=g(z), \]

    where environment or auxiliary variable \(e\) changes latent distributions in a sufficiently rich way. The recovery guarantee is not “a VAE always identifies causes”; it is a theorem tied to the assumed conditional family, variation in \(e\), invertibility, and model specification.

    Multiple distributions and environments can similarly expose mechanism changes. General-environment work studies recovery beyond neatly labeled single-node interventions, while multiple-distribution CRL formalizes broader environment structure. These are assumption-dependent identifiability results, not universal recipes.

    General Identifiability and Achievability for CRL proves recovery in a nonparametric latent causal/mixing setting using specified hard intervention coverage, including two uncoupled interventions per node in its main result. Multi-node intervention work replaces the single-target convention with coverage/diversity and linear-mixing assumptions. Grouping-based CRL obtains an observational result by assuming observed variables are organized into groups related to latent causal variables. “No intervention” in the last result does not mean “no structural information”: grouping is the additional information.

    Assumption Ledger for a CRL Theorem¤

    Table CAUSAL.8. Assumption Ledger for a CRL Theorem
    Question Examples that change the theorem
    observation map invertible/noninvertible, linear/nonlinear, dimension, noise
    latent mechanisms DAG/cycle, additive noise, exponential family, sparsity
    environments observational, hard/soft interventions, known targets, paired/uncoupled
    coverage how many interventions, which nodes, variation/rank conditions
    ambiguity permutation, component-wise transforms, scaling, equivalence class
    guarantee population identifiability, constructive algorithm, finite-sample rate

    A result is portable only when the new application satisfies the same ledger or proves a replacement argument.

    Interventional pairs and sparse changes¤

    Pairs before and after an action provide a particularly informative signal. If an intervention changes only a sparse subset of causal variables while leaving the scene and other mechanisms fixed, the difference can reveal intervention targets and invariant content. Causal delta embeddings represent interventions through sparse, scene-invariant latent differences. This is peer-reviewed research evidence: the reported out-of-distribution gains do not establish that arbitrary image differences correspond to causal actions.

    Worked non-identifiability example¤

    Let \(Z=(Z_1,Z_2)\) have a continuous density and observations be \(X=Z\). Define an invertible mixing

    \[ \tilde Z_1=Z_1, \qquad \tilde Z_2=Z_2+Z_1^2. \]

    A decoder using \(\tilde Z\) can reconstruct exactly as well because \(Z_2=\tilde Z_2-\tilde Z_1^2\). Reconstruction, likelihood, and sample quality alone cannot select between the coordinate systems. If the true intervention modifies \(Z_2\) while holding \(Z_1\) fixed, an intervention described as “change only the second coordinate” has different meaning in the two systems. Intervention or environment information is needed to privilege a causal coordinate system.

    The example also shows why sparsity is coordinate-dependent: an intervention sparse in \(Z\) can become dense after a generic invertible mixing. Penalizing sparse latent changes introduces a preference for a coordinate system; whether that preference identifies causal variables depends on a theorem and intervention coverage.

    Disentanglement is not causality¤

    Independent or visually interpretable coordinates may be statistically useful without being causal variables. Conversely, causal variables can be dependent because mechanisms connect them. Marginal independence penalties can destroy causal dependence rather than reveal it. A causal representation must be evaluated under interventions, mechanism shifts, or identified causal queries—not only with latent traversal images.

    Measurement and abstraction¤

    Real causal variables are often not uniquely defined at pixel level. Temperature, cell type, intent, and object identity are abstractions with measurement error and domain conventions. A high-level causal model should approximately commute with low-level interventions: performing a permitted low-level intervention and then abstracting should agree with intervening in the high-level model. Accepted ICLR 2026 nonlinear causal-reduction work studies interventional consistency for policy explanations; its guarantees apply to the modeled reduction class, not all learned abstractions.

    If \(\tau\) maps low-level states to high-level variables and \(\omega\) maps allowed low-level interventions to high-level interventions, a causal abstraction seeks a commuting relation of the form

    \[ \tau_\#P_L^{\,i}\approx P_H^{\,\omega(i)}. \]

    The approximation metric, intervention set, and level of aggregation matter. Matching observational abstractions alone does not validate this relation.

    Identification checklist¤

    Before fitting a causal generator, record:

    1. the exact query—association, average intervention, conditional effect, mediation, or unit counterfactual;
    2. observed and latent variables, temporal order, and possible interference;
    3. the graph or model class and its equivalence class;
    4. available regimes—IID observation, environments, known/unknown interventions, temporal data, or paired counterfactual-like observations;
    5. assumptions—causal sufficiency, positivity, consistency, faithfulness, noise form, invertibility, sparsity, and selection;
    6. a graphical or algebraic identification argument;
    7. sensitivity analysis for assumptions that cannot be tested from the observed data.

    Chapter 2 retrieval Questions¤

    1. Can an effect be identifiable when the full DAG is not?
    2. What extra information does an observational grouping-based CRL theorem use?
    3. Why must a representation result name its residual ambiguity?
    Complete solutions
    1. Yes. All graphs in the compatible equivalence class can agree on a particular adjustment or effect functional.
    2. It assumes a grouping of observed variables linked to latent causal structure; this is structural information even without interventions.
    3. Only queries invariant to that ambiguity are justified; a latent coordinate value is meaningless if an allowed transformation changes it.

    Independent practice and assessment map: identification and representation¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. Identification answers must quantify over the full compatible model class, not merely describe one fitted model.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: identify the quantity. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. In the running finite SCM, predict whether the ATE and \(P(Y_1=1\mid C=1,T=0,Y=0)\) have the same identification status from the observational joint. Derive the identified quantity and name the unresolved object.
    2. P1.2 — Reproduce the sharp coupling bound. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. From \(P(Y_0=1\mid C=1)=3/8\) and \(P(Y_1=1\mid C=1)=7/8\), derive the Fréchet range of \(q_{11}\) and the resulting range of \(P(Y_1=1\mid Y_0=0,C=1)\).
    3. P1.3 — Compute a front-door functional. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Assume the front-door conditions hold, \(P(X=0)=P(X=1)=1/2\), \(P(M=1\mid X=1)=3/4\), \(P(M=1\mid X=0)=1/4\), and \(P(Y=1\mid M=m,X=x)\) equals \(0.1,0.3,0.6,0.8\) for \((m,x)=(0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1)\). Compute \(P(Y=1\mid do(X=1))\), \(P(Y=1\mid do(X=0))\), and their difference.
    4. P1.4 — Construct a representation witness. Type: explain · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. For \(X=(Z_1,Z_2)\), use \(\tilde Z_1=Z_1\) and \(\tilde Z_2=Z_2+Z_1^2\) to show exact reconstruction equivalence and explain why “change only coordinate two” is not representation-invariant.
    5. P1.5 — Audit a theorem ledger. Type: research · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. A CRL theorem assumes an invertible mixing map, population distributions, two hard uncoupled interventions per latent node, and recovery up to a named permutation/component-wise ambiguity. Fill every row of the chapter's assumption ledger and list three claims the theorem still does not justify.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer an ambiguity correctly. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 17 min. A medical-image representation is recovered only up to permutation and component-wise invertible transforms. Specify one structural claim that can be transported after relabeling, one numeric intervention claim that cannot be copied unchanged, and the additional calibration needed to make that intervention meaningful.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The graph, sufficiency of \(C\), consistency, and positivity identify \(p_t^{do}=\sum_cP(Y=1\mid T=t,C=c)P(C=c)\), giving \(p_0^{do}=1/4\), \(p_1^{do}=3/4\), and ATE \(1/2\). The observational joint fixes only the marginals of \((Y_0,Y_1)\), not their within-unit coupling, so the factual-unit query is bounded rather than point identified. Check. Estimating both outcome regressions perfectly would not reveal the missing joint cell.
    2. P1.2. \(q_{11}\in[\max(0,3/8+7/8-1),\min(3/8,7/8)]=[2/8,3/8]\). Since \(P(Y_0=0\mid C=1)=5/8\), the target is \((7/8-q_{11})/(5/8)\) and therefore lies in \([4/5,1]\). Check. The lower target value uses the upper common-success cell.
    3. P1.3. Averaging over \(X\) gives \(r_0=(0.1+0.3)/2=0.2\) and \(r_1=(0.6+0.8)/2=0.7\). Thus \(P(Y=1\mid do(X=1))=(1/4)(0.2)+(3/4)(0.7)=23/40=0.575\), while \(P(Y=1\mid do(X=0))=(3/4)(0.2)+(1/4)(0.7)=13/40=0.325\). The difference is \(10/40=1/4\). Check. The outcome conditionals were first standardized over \(X\), then weighted by the intervention-specific mediator law.
    4. P1.4. The map is invertible because \(Z_1=\tilde Z_1\) and \(Z_2=\tilde Z_2-\tilde Z_1^2\), so either coordinate system reconstructs \(X\) exactly. Holding \(Z_1\) fixed and changing \(Z_2\) maps to a change in \(\tilde Z_2\), but an arbitrary “change coordinate two while preserving coordinate one” in a learned system has no causal meaning until the learned transform is related to the true intervention. Generic mixings make sparsity still more coordinate-dependent. Check. Reconstruction identifies an observation map inverse, not intervention semantics.
    5. P1.5. The ledger records: invertible observation map; the theorem's nonparametric latent causal class; hard intervention environments; two uncoupled interventions per node as coverage; permutation/component-wise residual ambiguity; and a population identifiability/achievability guarantee. It does not by itself justify finite-sample accuracy, robustness to approximate or mislabeled interventions, or a numeric semantic name/value for each recovered coordinate. Check. Computational convergence and real-world measurement validity are additional claims.
    6. P1.6. After resolving permutation, graph adjacency or conditional-independence structure preserved by the allowed component maps can be transported. The claim \(do(Z_i=2)\) cannot: the value two changes under an allowed invertible reparameterization. It needs a measurement model or paired intervention that anchors the recovered coordinate to a physical construct and maps physical action levels through the learned transform. Rubric. Full credit names an invariant, a non-invariant, and an explicit calibration bridge.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Identification determines what the data and assumptions can reveal; optimization only estimates within that boundary. Causal representations are recoverable up to assumption-specific ambiguities, and useful evaluation must match those ambiguities. Chapter 3 shows how deep generative families can parameterize an SCM without changing these rules.

    Retrieval solution

    Why can a perfectly reconstructing autoencoder fail at causal representation learning? Any invertible reparameterization can preserve reconstruction while changing which coordinate an intervention should modify.

    Continue continuously, or use the Chapter 2 stopping-point view.
