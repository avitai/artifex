    Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) learn a stochastic constructive policy whose terminal objects are sampled with probability proportional to a nonnegative reward:

    \[ P(X=x)=\frac{R(x)}{Z}, \qquad Z=\sum_{x\in\mathcal X}R(x). \]

    They are designed for combinatorial spaces where many action sequences can construct the same object and where diverse high-reward samples matter more than returning one maximizer. The “flow” is conserved probability mass on a state graph—not an invertible normalizing flow or an ODE velocity field.

    How to read this book

    Read continuously to derive every balance objective from flow conservation. Numbered pages are exact projections.
    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–3.
    Practitioner: add Chapters 4 and 6.
    Research: all chapters, including partition-function and posterior interpretations.

    Throughout Chapters 1–3, the default setting is a finite directed acyclic graph, a unique source, finite terminating trajectories, and positive reward on the modeled terminal support. Later sections state what changes for continuous spaces, stochastic environments, zero rewards, or incomplete data. A theorem about this ideal graph is not automatically a convergence theorem for a neural optimizer.

    1. Constructive State Graphs and Reward-Proportional Sampling¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes conditional probability, finite directed graphs, and ancestral sampling. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. distinguish semantic terminal objects from states, actions, and their possibly many construction trajectories;
    2. derive state and edge flow from trajectory flow and prove source-to-terminal conservation on a finite constructive DAG;
    3. derive forward and backward kernels and the reward-proportional terminal law, including the Markovian-flow assumption or Markovianization step that justifies a product policy;
    4. audit explicit-stop, dead-end, reachability, and duplicate-terminal conventions before treating the graph as a probabilistic model;
    5. predict how scaling, tempering, clipping, shifting, or noisily estimating reward changes the target or its estimator; and
    6. transfer the construction to a new combinatorial object while separating path multiplicity, semantic identity, reward validity, and optimization evidence.

    Let \(\mathcal G=(\mathcal S,\mathcal E)\) be a directed acyclic graph with initial state \(s_0\). A state is a partially constructed object; an edge \(s\to s'\) is a valid action; a terminal state \(x\in\mathcal X\) is a completed object. A trajectory is

    \[ \tau=(s_0\to s_1\to\cdots\to s_n=x). \]

    Different trajectories may end at the same \(x\). Adding atoms in different orders can yield the same molecule; adding edges in different orders can yield the same graph. A correct object probability must sum over all such trajectories.

    The graph is part of the probabilistic model. It specifies which partial objects exist, which actions are valid, when construction stops, and which histories count as alternative routes to the same terminal. Two implementations with the same reward function but different state graphs need not induce the same optimization problem or even the same terminal sample space.

    Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations applies this distinction to partial derivations, expansion-order multiplicity, executable programs, and the difference between terminal reward, grammar probability, and observation likelihood.

    This construction graph is not necessarily the relational graph being generated. When terminal objects are themselves graphs, Random Graphs and Relational Generators supplies the additional invariance, automorphism, sparsity, and graph-evaluation contracts; the GFlowNet supplies a reward-proportional constructive sampler.

    Stops, Terminals, and Complete Objects¤

    There are two clean conventions:

    1. a state \(x\) is terminal and has no outgoing action; or
    2. a completed-but-not-yet-terminal state takes an explicit STOP edge to a terminal sink representing the object.

    Either convention works, but mixing them can count one object twice or let probability leak past completion. If variable-length strings may stop at every prefix, STOP is a genuine action whose probability affects length. A dead end that is not a valid terminal is not an implicit stop: it is a graph-construction bug unless an explicit failure terminal receives the flow.

    Trajectory, Edge, and State Flow¤

    Assign nonnegative flow \(F(\tau)\) to complete trajectories. Derived quantities are

    \[ F(s\to s')=\sum_{\tau\ni(s\to s')}F(\tau), \]
    \[ F(s)=\sum_{\tau\ni s}F(\tau). \]

    Because the graph is acyclic, a trajectory visits a state or edge at most once. In cyclic constructions, the notation \(\tau\ni s\) must count visits or the state must be augmented with construction time; the simple equations below otherwise lose information.

    For a nonterminal state, conservation requires

    \[ \sum_{p\in\operatorname{Pa}(s)}F(p\to s) =F(s) =\sum_{c\in\operatorname{Ch}(s)}F(s\to c). \]

    At a terminal state, incoming flow is matched to reward:

    \[ F(x)=R(x). \]

    The source flow is the partition function:

    \[ F(s_0)=Z=\sum_xR(x). \]

    To see why, sum conservation over every internal state. Each internal edge appears once as outgoing and once as incoming and cancels. Only source outflow and terminal inflow remain. Anchoring terminal flow to \(R(x)\) therefore forces source flow to equal the total reward.

    Forward and Backward Policies¤

    Normalize outgoing and incoming edge flows:

    \[ P_F(s'\mid s)=\frac{F(s\to s')}{F(s)}, \qquad P_B(s\mid s')=\frac{F(s\to s')}{F(s')}. \]

    Sampling \(P_F\) from \(s_0\) to termination gives terminal probability \(R(x)/Z\) when the balance constraints hold. \(P_B\) is a training factorization over construction histories; it need not be the physical reverse of an environment.

    There is a precise factorization boundary here. An arbitrary trajectory flow can remember its earlier path, so its normalized trajectory measure need not equal a product of state-only transition kernels. A Markovian flow is one for which the next-action distribution depends only on the current state; then both the forward and backward products below reproduce the same trajectory law. If one starts only from compatible conserved edge flows on a DAG, normalizing them constructs a Markovian flow with those state and edge marginals. This is a Markovianization step, not a proof that every history-dependent trajectory flow was already Markovian.

    Formally, the probability of a complete trajectory is

    \[ P_F(\tau)=\prod_{t=0}^{n-1}P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t), \]

    whereas the probability of the terminal object is the many-to-one pushforward

    \[ P_F(X=x)=\sum_{\tau:s_n=x}P_F(\tau). \]

    Using \(P_F(s'\mid s)=F(s\to s')/F(s)\) and summing the induced trajectory flows gives

    \[ P_F(X=x)=\frac{F(x)}{F(s_0)}=\frac{R(x)}{Z}. \]

    This short derivation contains the central idea of the family: distribute a terminal mass across paths, conserve it through partial states, then normalize only once at the source.

    Worked Diamond Graph¤

    Suppose \(s_0\) branches to \(a\) and \(b\), both lead to terminal \(x\), and a separate branch leads to terminal \(y\). Let \(R(x)=3\), \(R(y)=1\). Total source flow is \(Z=4\), so terminal probabilities must be \(P(x)=3/4\), \(P(y)=1/4\).

    The flow of 3 into \(x\) may be split between paths through \(a\) and \(b\) in many ways. Object probability is fixed while trajectory probability is not. This degree of freedom lets a backward policy select how credit is allocated among construction orders.

    Make the numbers explicit. Give the two \(x\) paths flows 1 and 2 and the \(y\) path flow 1. Then

    \[ P_F(a\mid s_0)=\tfrac14, \quad P_F(b\mid s_0)=\tfrac24, \quad P_F(y\mid s_0)=\tfrac14, \]

    and both \(a\) and \(b\) deterministically finish at \(x\). Hence \(P_F(x)=1/4+2/4=3/4\). At \(x\), the backward policy is \(P_B(a\mid x)=1/3\) and \(P_B(b\mid x)=2/3\). Changing the split to \(3/2\) and \(3/2\) changes the trajectory law and backward policy but leaves the object law unchanged.

    Read the converging arrows as a sum, not as duplicate objects. The two routes carry different trajectory probabilities, but the terminal receives one total flow of 3. This is the visual invariant to retain when a graph, molecule, set, or program admits several construction orders.

    Counterexample: Path Multiplicity Is Not Reward¤

    Suppose objects \(x\) and \(y\) both have reward 1, but the implementation represents two orderings for \(x\) as two distinct terminals while \(y\) has one. If it assigns reward 1 to each encoding, the represented terminal space has total mass 3 and the semantic object probability becomes \(P(x)=2/3\), not the intended \(1/2\). The repair is to canonicalize terminal objects, aggregate all paths into one terminal, or divide/aggregate rewards according to a rigorously declared quotient representation. “The network will learn to ignore duplicates” is not a valid repair.

    Reward Is an Unnormalized Density¤

    If \(R(x)=e^{-E(x)/T}\), a GFlowNet amortizes sampling from an energy-based target. If \(R(x)\) is an acquisition function, it samples candidates in proportion to acquisition value. Raising reward to power \(\beta\) changes the target:

    \[ P_\beta(x)\propto R(x)^\beta. \]

    Reward clipping, adding \(\epsilon\), or rank transforms also change the target and must not be described as mere numerical stabilization.

    Rewards need not be normalized, differentiable, or available for partial states. They must, however, be evaluable on sampled terminals for the basic objectives. Negative utility cannot be used directly as flow: choose a nonnegative mapping and acknowledge the resulting distribution. If only an unbiased noisy reward estimate is available, putting that estimate inside a logarithm generally introduces bias; the noise model becomes part of the estimator design.

    What the Population Result Does—and Does Not—Say¤

    Established. On the specified graph, exact nonnegative conserved flow with terminal boundary \(F(x)=R(x)\) produces \(P_F(x)=R(x)/Z\).

    It does not say that stochastic gradient descent finds that flow, that a finite neural network represents it, that unvisited modes are corrected, that the reward is scientifically valid, or that a sampled object is novel. Those are approximation, exploration, and external-validity questions developed in Chapter 6.

    Chapter 1 retrieval Questions¤

    1. Why can two correct GFlowNets have different trajectory probabilities but the same object distribution?
    2. What changes if a reward is replaced by \(R^2\)?
    3. Why must a variable-length stop decision be represented explicitly?
    Complete solutions
    1. Conservation fixes total flow reaching each terminal, but that total may be split among multiple construction histories in many ways.
    2. The target becomes \(P(x)\propto R(x)^2\); it is more concentrated and is not a stabilization-equivalent target.
    3. Stop determines which prefix is a terminal and therefore contributes to both the trajectory probability and the length distribution.

    Chapter 1 Synthesis¤

    GFlowNets conserve flow through a constructive DAG and terminate with flow equal to reward. The induced object law is reward-proportional even when many paths reach one object. Chapter 2 derives local training constraints.

    Why not multiply the probabilities of one chosen construction path and call that the object probability?

    Because the object probability is the sum over every path that constructs it. Ignoring multiplicity biases objects with many or few construction orders.

    Independent practice and assessment map: constructive flow¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. In the diamond, keep \(R(x)=3\) and \(R(y)=1\) but move all of \(x\)'s flow to the path through \(a\). Predict which of the object law, trajectory law, forward policy, and backward policy change; then calculate them.
    2. P1.2 — Conservation derivation. A source sends flows 2 and 5 into two partial states that merge at terminal \(u\), and flow 3 directly to terminal \(v\). Enumerate state, edge, terminal, and source flows; normalize \(P_F\); and prove the two terminal probabilities both by path summation and by \(R/Z\).
    3. P1.3 — Markovianity audit. Two histories merge at state \(s\). A history-dependent controller chooses left with probability \(0.9\) after the first history and \(0.1\) after the second. Explain why one state-only \(P_F(\cdot\mid s)\) cannot reproduce both conditional trajectory laws, and give two valid repairs.
    4. P1.4 — Stop and quotient counterexample. Variable-length sets may stop after one or two insertions, and insertion order is retained in terminal IDs. Construct the smallest example in which one semantic set receives too much mass. Repair both the stop semantics and the semantic quotient.
    5. P1.5 — Reward intervention. For rewards \((1,4,9)\), compute the target before and after multiplying all rewards by 10, squaring them, and adding 1. State which interventions preserve terminal probabilities and how \(Z\) changes. Then explain why an unbiased random reward cannot generally be inserted unbiasedly into \(log R\).
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Design a constructive graph for unlabeled three-node graphs. Specify state identity, valid action, stop rule, terminal canonicalization, nonnegative reward, and a toy exact-enumeration test that distinguishes path coverage from semantic-object coverage.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. The object law remains \((P(x),P(y))=(3/4,1/4)\) because terminal flows remain \((3,1)\). The \(x\) path law changes from a positive split across \(a,b\) to probability one through \(a\) conditional on reaching \(x\). At the source, \(P_F(a\mid s_0)=3/4\), \(P_F(b\mid s_0)=0\), and \(P_F(y\mid s_0)=1/4\). At \(x\), \(P_B(a\mid x)=1\) and \(P_B(b\mid x)=0\). Thus object probability is invariant to a valid reallocation of terminal flow, but both path kernels change.
    2. The two incoming edge flows to \(u\) are 2 and 5, so \(F(u)=R(u)=7\); \(F(v)=R(v)=3\); and \(F(s_0)=Z=10\). Source action probabilities are \(2/10,5/10,3/10\). Summing the first two complete-path probabilities gives \(P_F(u)=7/10\), while the direct path gives \(P_F(v)=3/10\). The boundary calculation gives the same values: \(R(u)/Z=7/10\) and \(R(v)/Z=3/10\).
    3. Conditioning on the visible state \(s\) loses which incoming history occurred, yet the stipulated next-action laws differ. One state-only kernel would have to be both \((0.9,0.1)\) and \((0.1,0.9)\). Either augment the state with the relevant history so the two cases no longer merge, or replace the history-dependent trajectory flow by a declared Markovianized flow that preserves chosen state/edge marginals while accepting a different full trajectory law.
    4. Let elements be \(a,b\). If ordered terminals [a,b] and [b,a] each receive reward 1 while singleton [a] receives reward 1, semantic set \(\{a,b\}\) gets mass \(2/3\) rather than \(1/2\). Use one explicit STOP decision at each valid set size and map both insertion orders to the same canonical terminal \(\{a,b\}\), with total terminal reward assigned once. Alternatively, a formally declared quotient can divide and aggregate encoding rewards, but its induced object law must be proved.
    5. Initially \(Z=14\) and \(p=(1,4,9)/14\). Multiplication by 10 gives \(Z'=140\) and the same \(p\). Squaring gives \(Z'=98\) and \(p'=(1,16,81)/98\); adding 1 gives \(Z'=17\) and \(p'=(2,5,10)/17\). Only common positive scaling preserves normalized probabilities. For a positive unbiased estimate \(\widehat R\), Jensen's inequality generally gives \(\mathbb E[\log\widehat R]\ne\log R\); zeros can make the log undefined as well.
    6. Use isomorphism classes of partial simple graphs as semantic states, add one missing edge as an action, and allow one explicit stop after the declared minimum size. Canonical graph labeling supplies state and terminal IDs; invalid duplicate edges are masked. Assign reward once per terminal isomorphism class. For three vertices, enumerate every labeled action path, map paths to canonical graph IDs, and compare both path visitation and aggregated terminal frequencies with exact \(R/Z\). The first diagnoses exploration; the second diagnoses semantic proportionality.

    2. Flow Matching and Detailed Balance¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and stable log-domain arithmetic. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. write flow-matching conservation for terminal-without-stop and explicit-stop graph conventions without double-counting reward;
    2. derive detailed balance from compatible state flow and forward/backward kernels and telescope it along a complete trajectory;
    3. distinguish what FM, DB, and terminal boundary constraints each parameterize and establish at zero residual;
    4. implement positive-flow sums, masks, empty-set handling, and source/terminal constraints stably in log space;
    5. diagnose reachability, co-reachability, reverse-support, and sampled-coverage failures that a low empirical loss can hide; and
    6. transfer local balance to a new construction graph while specifying its parent-enumeration and state-flow costs.

    The original GFlowNet construction converts conservation equations into learnable residuals, analogous to temporal-difference learning.

    Flow Matching¤

    Parameterize edge or state flows. For each noninitial state \(s\), compare total incoming flow with total outgoing flow plus terminal reward when applicable:

    \[ \sum_{p}F_\theta(p\to s) =R(s)\mathbf1_{s\in\mathcal X} +\sum_cF_\theta(s\to c). \]

    A stable loss commonly squares a log-domain residual. Exact form, reward placement, and stop-action representation must be declared. Summing exponentially large parent/child sets may itself require structured parameterization.

    For a graph in which terminal states have no outgoing edges, one common positive-flow residual is

    \[ \Delta_{\mathrm{FM}}(s)= \log\!\left(\sum_{p\in\operatorname{Pa}(s)}F_\theta(p\to s)\right) - \log\!\left(R(s)\mathbf 1_{s\in\mathcal X} +\sum_{c\in\operatorname{Ch}(s)}F_\theta(s\to c)\right). \]

    Training minimizes a sampled average of \(\Delta_{\mathrm{FM}}(s)^2\). Implement both sums with logsumexp, mask invalid edges before normalization, and define what happens when a sum is empty. A source constraint is handled separately; there is no incoming parent flow to match there.

    With an explicit stop edge, an object-bearing state can have both continuation edges and a stop edge. The reward is attached to the stop/terminal boundary rather than added again to ordinary outgoing flow. These two formulations are equivalent only after the state graph and boundary variables are mapped consistently.

    Detailed Balance¤

    With learned state flow \(F_\theta(s)\) and policies, impose for each edge

    \[ F_\theta(s)P_F(s'\mid s) =F_\theta(s')P_B(s\mid s'). \]

    At terminal states set \(F_\theta(x)=R(x)\). This edge-local equation ensures the same flow is seen from both directions. It resembles MCMC detailed balance algebraically, but the interpretation differs: a GFlowNet constructs acyclic trajectories rather than running a stationary reversible chain among terminal objects.

    The scopes differ. Flow matching may require a full parent/child sum at one state; detailed balance checks one sampled edge but carries an extra learned state flow and backward kernel. Multiplying compatible DB equations changes the scope to a path—it does not make the three parameterizations identical.

    Why Detailed Balance Is Sufficient¤

    Multiply detailed-balance ratios along a complete trajectory:

    \[ \prod_{t=0}^{n-1} \frac{P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t)}{P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1})} = \prod_{t=0}^{n-1}\frac{F(s_{t+1})}{F(s_t)} =\frac{R(x)}{F(s_0)}. \]

    The state-flow ratios telescope. Rearranging yields the trajectory-balance identity introduced in Chapter 3. Summing over all backward paths then yields reward-proportional terminal sampling, provided \(P_B\) is a normalized kernel whose paths return to the source and the relevant graph support is covered.

    The converse needs care. A set of full-trajectory TB equations can determine the correct terminal law without uniquely determining every internal state flow. Detailed balance specifies a stronger edge-local factorization when its state-flow variables exist. Thus “same terminal population optimum” is not “identical parameterization or optimization landscape.”

    Worked example: a numerical edge check¤

    If \(F(s)=10\), \(P_F(s'\mid s)=0.3\), and \(F(s')=6\), then balance requires

    \[ P_B(s\mid s')=\frac{10(0.3)}6=0.5. \]

    The remaining backward probability at \(s'\) must be allocated among other parents. If the computed ratio exceeds one, the proposed flows/policies cannot satisfy the local constraint.

    For the Chapter 1 diamond, let \(F(s_0)=4\), \(F(a)=1\), \(F(b)=2\), and \(F(x)=3\). Edge balance on \(a\to x\) gives \(1(1)=3P_B(a\mid x)\), hence \(P_B(a\mid x)=1/3\); the \(b\) edge gives \(2/3\). The backward probabilities normalize because the two incoming edge flows sum to the state flow at \(x\).

    Boundary Conditions Matter¤

    Local edge balance alone does not select the desired target unless terminal flows are anchored to rewards and the source/graph are connected appropriately. Invalid actions, premature stop, duplicate terminal encodings, and unreachable states alter the state graph.

    Three conditions are easy to miss:

    • Reachability: every positive-reward terminal must be reachable from \(s_0\).
    • Co-reachability: a partial state visited by the policy must be able to terminate or route to an explicit failure sink.
    • Kernel support: if \(P_F(s'\mid s)>0\), the reverse edge required by \(P_B(s\mid s')\) must be represented for DB/TB evaluation, and vice versa for the theorem being used.

    An edge residual of zero on one connected component says nothing about a missing positive-reward component. Boundary conditions select the target only over the graph actually modeled.

    Credit Propagation¤

    Local residuals propagate sparse terminal rewards backward over many updates. Long trajectories and large branching factors can make this slow. Replay, backward sampling, reward shaping that preserves the target, and longer-balance objectives address optimization—not the population conservation theorem.

    “Reward shaping that preserves the target” is narrower than ordinary RL shaping. A potential can be incorporated into a reparameterized flow/objective when the terminal boundary remains exactly \(R(x)\); replacing terminal rewards or tempering them changes the target. Every shaping proposal must show the invariance rather than relying on analogy.

    Parent Enumeration and State-Flow Models¤

    Flow matching may require enumerating every parent and child of a sampled state. That is cheap on a small grid but difficult when a graph has combinatorially many inverse actions. Detailed balance evaluates sampled edges and can avoid full parent sums if \(P_F\), \(P_B\), and \(F(s)\) are separately normalized/parameterized. The trade-off is that the model must learn mutually compatible state flow and policies.

    A common parameterization emits log_F(s) and forward/backward action logits. These are distinct outputs: a softmax normalizes action probabilities but does not enforce the scalar flow equation. A successful unit test checks masks, normalization, edge residuals, terminal anchoring, and exact enumeration on a toy graph.

    Counterexample: Small Sampled Loss, Wrong Global Law¤

    Return to two disjoint branches with rewards 1 and 100. If the behavior policy visits only the first branch, every sampled local residual can become zero while the high-reward terminal remains absent. The empirical loss is then a statement about the behavior distribution, not a certificate over the full DAG. This is why exploration belongs to correctness in practice.

    3. Trajectory and Subtrajectory Balance¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–2, log probabilities, and importance-sampling support intuition. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. derive trajectory balance from compatible flow ratios and prove reward proportionality by summing normalized backward paths;
    2. explain how the backward kernel allocates one object's reward among histories without changing the exact terminal target;
    3. derive the log-squared TB residual, learned-normalizer scale test, and positive-reward support requirement;
    4. derive SubTB on arbitrary partial trajectories and distinguish its weighting/normalization conventions from its zero-residual identities;
    5. compare DB, TB, and SubTB credit-assignment and gradient bias–variance behavior without turning empirical observations into universal theorems; and
    6. design on-policy, exploratory, replay, or offline training with explicit coverage and identifiability limits.

    Trajectory balance imposes one constraint on every complete trajectory \(\tau\) ending at \(x\):

    \[ Z_\theta\prod_{t=0}^{n-1}P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) =R(x)\prod_{t=0}^{n-1}P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \tag{GFN.1}\label{eq:gflownet-trajectory-balance} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:gflownet-trajectory-balance}\) compares the complete forward and backward trajectory probabilities after accounting for the source normalizer and terminal reward. The standard log-squared loss is

    \[ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{TB}}(\tau)= \left[ \log Z_\theta +\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}\log P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) -\log R(x) -\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}\log P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}) \right]^2. \]

    At zero loss for all relevant trajectories under the theorem's assumptions, the forward terminal distribution is proportional to reward and \(Z_\theta\) equals the partition function.

    The phrase “all relevant trajectories” is essential. A neural training run observes a finite sample and optimizes a nonconvex empirical objective. Zero residual on a minibatch is neither zero residual on the graph nor proof that all positive-reward terminals have been reached.

    Why the Backward Product Appears¤

    The forward product is trajectory probability. Multiplying terminal reward by the backward trajectory probability assigns the object's reward among its construction histories. Summing the TB identity over all backward paths to \(x\) gives \(ZP_F(x)=R(x)\) because backward path probabilities sum to one.

    More explicitly, define

    \[ P_F(\tau)=\prod_tP_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t), \qquad P_B(\tau\mid x)=\prod_tP_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \]

    Then exact TB says \(P_F(\tau)=R(x)P_B(\tau\mid x)/Z\). Summing over \(\tau\to x\) gives

    \[ P_F(x)=\frac{R(x)}Z \underbrace{\sum_{\tau\to x}P_B(\tau\mid x)}_{1}. \]

    The backward kernel therefore supplies a normalized allocation of object reward over histories. It can be fixed—for example, uniform over valid parents—or learned. A learned \(P_B\) may reduce variance or represent asymmetric construction histories, but it also introduces another model whose normalization, support, and compensation effects require diagnosis.

    Worked TB Calculation¤

    In the diamond graph, take \(Z=4\), \(P_F(a\mid s_0)=1/4\), \(P_F(x\mid a)=1\), and \(P_B(a\mid x)=1/3\). For trajectory \(s_0\to a\to x\),

    \[ 4\left(\frac14\right)(1) =3\left(\frac13\right)(1)=1. \]

    For the path through \(b\), \(P_F(b\mid s_0)=1/2\) and \(P_B(b\mid x)=2/3\), so both sides equal 2. TB does not demand equal probability for paths to the same object; it demands agreement with the backward allocation.

    Learning the Normalizer¤

    TB often treats \(\log Z_\theta\) as a free scalar, or as a context-dependent network output in conditional models. It receives gradients on every complete trajectory. If all rewards are multiplied by a constant \(c\), the correct policy is unchanged while the correct normalizer becomes \(cZ\). This scale test is a useful implementation invariant: terminal frequencies should remain stable and learned \(\log Z\) should shift by \(\log c\).

    Subtrajectory Balance¤

    For any partial trajectory \(s_m\to\cdots\to s_n\),

    \[ F(s_m)\prod_{t=m}^{n-1}P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) =F(s_n)\prod_{t=m}^{n-1}P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \]

    Length-one subtrajectories recover detailed balance; full source-to-terminal subtrajectories recover trajectory balance. SubTB(\(\lambda\)) weights multiple subtrajectory lengths, trading local bootstrapping against long-range credit.

    One generic log residual for \(s_m\to\cdots\to s_n\) is

    \[ \delta_{m:n}= \log F_\theta(s_m) +\sum_{t=m}^{n-1}\log P_F(s_{t+1}\mid s_t) -\log F_\theta(s_n) -\sum_{t=m}^{n-1}\log P_B(s_t\mid s_{t+1}). \]

    A SubTB objective averages \(\delta_{m:n}^2\) with declared weights, often depending on length through a hyperparameter \(\lambda\). Papers and libraries use different normalization conventions—per trajectory, per subtrajectory, or per length—so writing only “SubTB(\(\lambda\))” is not enough to reproduce a result.

    In the original SubTB(\(\lambda\)) convention for one length-\(T\) episode, each nonempty subtrajectory \(i{:}j\) receives unnormalized weight \(\lambda^{j-i}\) and the weighted losses are divided by the sum of those weights. Under that convention, \(\lambda\to0^+\) retains the averaged length-one DB terms, \(lambda=1\) weights all subtrajectories uniformly, and \(\lambda\to\infty\) retains the full-trajectory TB term. Batch-global versus per-episode normalization is a further declared choice. These limits describe that estimator convention; they do not say finite-\(\lambda\) gradients equal a convex combination of already-optimized DB and TB models.

    At exact satisfaction:

    • length-one SubTB constraints are DB constraints;
    • every-subtrajectory balance implies full TB by choosing \(m=0,n=T\);
    • full TB alone fixes terminal path ratios but need not identify a unique intermediate state-flow parameterization.

    Constraint length changes where credit enters and which auxiliary quantities are learned. It does not change the exact terminal target when the respective global constraints and support assumptions are satisfied.

    Bias–Variance and Optimization¤

    Long constraints communicate terminal reward directly but combine many log probabilities and can have high variance. Local constraints provide dense updates but bootstrap through inaccurate state flows. Subtrajectory objectives interpolate. These are empirical/optimization trade-offs; all exact balance families target the same reward-proportional terminal law when their constraints are satisfied.

    Trajectory length can also change numerical scale. A sum of 100 log probabilities has different variance and gradient geometry from a sum of 5. Normalizing the residual by length changes the optimization objective and may change which trajectories dominate; it should be reported, not silently treated as algebraically equivalent TB. PowerFlow proposes a specific length-aware TB construction for autoregressive language-model distribution matching. That is peer-reviewed research evidence for one formulation, not a universal fix for length bias.

    Zero Rewards¤

    TB contains \(\log R(x)\), so zero reward is undefined. Restricting support to positive-reward terminals, using an explicit failure sink, or adding \(\epsilon\) are different modeling choices. Adding \(\epsilon\) changes \(P(x)\) by giving formerly impossible objects nonzero mass.

    Off-Policy Training¤

    Trajectories may come from exploratory or replay behavior rather than the current \(P_F\). Balance losses can be evaluated off-policy, but sampling distribution affects which constraints receive updates and the variance/bias of stochastic optimization. Full support over important transitions is essential.

    TB's pointwise residual can be computed on an off-policy trajectory without the ordinary policy-gradient requirement that the trajectory came from \(P_F\). That does not mean arbitrary logged data identify the global solution. If logged prefixes omit actions, multiple forward policies may fit every observed SubTB equation while allocating different mass to unsupported branches. Conservative GFlowNets for offline recommendation, accepted at ICML 2026, formalizes such non-identifiability for a dataset-induced token-prefix DAG and distinguishes flow overestimation, forward leakage, and backward compensation. Its correction is domain/formulation-specific; the general lesson is to state what support the data constrain.

    Importance weights can change which expectation is estimated, but cannot manufacture information about transitions of zero behavior probability. Large ratios can also make variance unacceptable. Replay contents, behavior policy, priority rule, and any correction weights are part of the algorithm specification.

    This distinction is easier to maintain with the gradient contract: the gradient of a sampled balance residual, a policy gradient of expected terminal reward, and an importance-corrected off-policy estimator have different objectives or averaging laws even when they reuse the same trajectories.

    Divergence Views¤

    Balance losses are squared log-ratio objectives, not automatically a named \(f\)-divergence between terminal distributions. Under specified forward/backward trajectory measures, one can derive variational or divergence relationships. On Divergence Measures for Training GFlowNets extends the hierarchical variational-inference view and studies high-variance stochastic divergence gradients with control variates. Its results compare estimators and finite tasks; they do not make TB literally reverse KL in every parameterization.

    4. Partition Functions, Conditionals, and Bayesian Inference¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–3, Bayes' rule, marginalization, and variational-inference basics. After completing it, you should be able to:

    1. identify source flow with a partition function only under the corresponding reward, balance, graph, and coverage assumptions;
    2. construct conditional GFlowNets with context-specific rewards, masks, policies, and normalizers \(Z(c)\);
    3. formulate Bayesian posterior sampling as reward \(p(D\mid h)p(h)\) and separate sampler fidelity from likelihood, prior, and identifiability validity;
    4. interpret partial-state flow using backward-path weighting rather than an unqualified sum over completions;
    5. distinguish reward-landscape diversity, sampling entropy, epistemic uncertainty, and calibrated conditional generalization; and
    6. choose among or compose GFlowNets, MCMC, SMC, variational inference, and autoregressive likelihood for a declared workload.

    Learning \(Z\)¤

    Trajectory balance commonly learns a scalar \(\log Z_\theta\). If the reward is an unnormalized posterior or energy, this estimates evidence/free energy. The estimate is reliable only insofar as balance constraints and exploration cover the target. Compare with exact enumeration, dynamic programming, AIS, or held-out identities on small problems.

    Do not infer a trustworthy normalizer merely because \(\log Z_\theta\) stops moving. Jointly scaling the policy errors and \(Z_\theta\) can make optimization appear stable while modes remain absent. Useful checks include reward-rescaling equivariance, agreement across seeds/objectives, exact small instances, and importance-style identities evaluated with a proposal of adequate support.

    Conditional GFlowNets¤

    For context \(c\), define reward \(R(x\mid c)\) and target

    \[ P(x\mid c)=\frac{R(x\mid c)}{Z(c)}. \]

    Condition the policies and possibly \(Z_\theta(c)\) on \(c\). Training over contexts amortizes a family of samplers. Generalization to new contexts is a neural generalization claim, not guaranteed by flow conservation on training contexts.

    Each context has its own normalizer

    \[ Z(c)=\sum_xR(x\mid c). \]

    A single global scalar is correct only when those normalizers are equal or the objective has been reformulated to absorb their ratios. Conditional TB commonly learns \(\log Z_\theta(c)\) alongside \(P_F(\cdot\mid s,c)\) and \(P_B(\cdot\mid s',c)\). If \(c\) is a preference vector, temperature, dataset, or partial observation, the training distribution over \(c\) determines which conditional laws are constrained.

    Worked Conditional Example¤

    Let \(x\in\{A,B\}\) and context \(c\in\{0,1\}\). Suppose

    \[ R(A\mid0)=1,\ R(B\mid0)=3, \qquad R(A\mid1)=8,\ R(B\mid1)=2. \]

    Then \(Z(0)=4\) and \(P(A\mid0)=1/4\), while \(Z(1)=10\) and \(P(A\mid1)=4/5\). A context-conditioned policy must learn both a reversal of preference and different source flows. Reusing \(Z=4\) for \(c=1\) cannot satisfy the TB boundary equations.

    The three evidence branches answer different questions. Exact conditional balance on trained contexts does not certify a misspecified Bayesian score, calibrate a scientific oracle, or guarantee generalization to unseen contexts.

    Bayesian Posterior Sampling¤

    If

    \[ R(x)=p(D\mid x)p(x), \]

    then terminal reward is an unnormalized posterior and \(Z=p(D)\) is the evidence. DAG-GFlowNet constructs Bayesian-network structures edge by edge to approximate a posterior over DAGs.

    Sampling a distribution over structures is not causal discovery by itself. Observational scores often identify only an equivalence class without extra assumptions or interventions.

    For a Bayesian parameter or structure \(h\),

    \[ p(h\mid D)=\frac{p(D\mid h)p(h)}{p(D)}, \]

    so choose \(R_D(h)=p(D\mid h)p(h)\). The construction graph determines how \(h\) is assembled, while the reward determines posterior mass. A correct GFlowNet samples the score-defined posterior; it does not repair a misspecified likelihood or prior.

    In Bayesian network structure learning, observational data may make several DAGs Markov equivalent. A posterior over those DAGs expresses uncertainty under the score and assumptions; it is not proof that sampled edge directions are causally identified. Interventions, functional restrictions, temporal knowledge, or other assumptions are needed for stronger claims.

    Latent-Variable Learning and Streaming Updates¤

    GFlowNet-EM uses a GFlowNet to approximate the difficult E-step posterior over discrete compositional latents, then updates model parameters in an EM-style loop. This is a hybrid inference-and-learning system: approximation error in the sampler affects the parameter update, and changing parameters changes the posterior reward.

    Streaming Bayes GFlowNets updates a discrete posterior sampler as new data chunks arrive. It is peer-reviewed research evidence on named preference-learning and phylogenetic tasks, not a general theorem that GFlowNets preserve exact sequential Bayesian coherence under function approximation.

    Marginals and Partial Objects¤

    State flow \(F(s)\) sums terminal rewards over all completions reachable from \(s\) when the graph representation has the required semantics:

    \[ F(s)=\sum_{x\succeq s}R(x)\,P_B(s\mid x), \]

    with the precise weighting determined by backward paths. This supports conditional completion and uncertainty over supersets. Duplicate representations and noncanonical construction histories must be included consistently.

    The \(P_B(s\mid x)\) term is the probability that a reverse construction from \(x\) passes through \(s\); it prevents blindly counting every path to every completion. Under graph conventions in which \(s\) is a mandatory, uniquely represented prefix of every completion \(x\succeq s\), that factor may reduce to one. In a multi-path graph it generally does not.

    For example, if terminal \(x\) has reward 6 and two reverse histories pass through \(a\) and \(b\) with probabilities \(1/3\) and \(2/3\), its contribution to \(F(a)\) is 2 and to \(F(b)\) is 4. Both are meaningful partial-object flows; neither alone equals the full completion reward.

    Entropy and Diversity¤

    Reward-proportional sampling balances quality and diversity according to the full reward landscape. It does not maximize diversity independently. Low temperature concentrates on high rewards; high temperature flattens. Entropy estimates can be derived from trajectory/state quantities under suitable flow parameterizations, but empirical unique-count metrics are strongly sample-size dependent.

    Multiple samples are correlated through a shared learned model even if ancestral draws use independent random seeds. Uncertainty about the target, reward oracle, or model parameters is different from diversity of terminal draws. A GFlowNet trained against one deterministic reward produces aleatoric sampling over its normalized landscape; it does not automatically quantify epistemic uncertainty in that reward.

    6. classify cross-family claims as algebraic equivalence, distributional equivalence under assumptions, population correspondence, shared estimator, analogy, or hybrid composition.

    Reinforcement Learning¤

    Standard RL maximizes expected return and may concentrate on one optimal trajectory. A GFlowNet targets an entire terminal distribution proportional to reward. Entropy-regularized RL can yield related policies under specific state/reward formulations, but objectives, boundary conditions, and trajectory multiplicity must be aligned before claiming equivalence.

    GFlowNet state flows resemble value functions and local losses resemble TD residuals. The learned quantity is conserved probability flow, not expected cumulative reward.

    Under carefully chosen terminal rewards, entropy regularization, state augmentation, and path corrections, soft-RL and GFlowNet objectives can be transformed into one another. Generative Flow Networks as Entropy-Regularized RL and Discrete Probabilistic Inference as Control in Multi-path Environments make such correspondences explicit. The assumptions matter most in multi-path graphs: ordinary maximum-entropy RL assigns entropy to trajectories, so an object with more paths can receive more aggregate mass unless path multiplicity/backward terms correct it.

    Counterexample. If \(x\) and \(y\) have equal terminal utility but \(x\) has ten action sequences and \(y\) has one, a uniformly maximum-entropy trajectory policy can prefer semantic object \(x\) ten-to-one. A reward-proportional GFlowNet with one unit of object reward each targets one-to-one. The methods coincide only after aligning which distribution—trajectory or object—is regularized.

    Energy-Based Models¤

    Setting \(R(x)=e^{-E(x)}\) makes the target the normalized EBM distribution. A GFlowNet becomes an amortized constructive sampler for that energy. The EBM defines relative terminal probability; the GFlowNet adds a state graph and policy. This is hybrid composition, not an identity between arbitrary EBMs and GFlowNets.

    The composition can be trained jointly: GFlowNets for Discrete Probabilistic Modeling couples a learned energy with a GFlowNet sampler. Then two approximations interact—the energy is learned from data and the sampler is learned from the energy. Negative samples from an imperfect sampler can bias or destabilize the energy update; low GFlowNet balance loss alone cannot validate the learned data distribution.

    Energy shifts illustrate the division of labor. Replacing \(E(x)\) by \(E(x)+c\) multiplies all rewards by \(e^{-c}\), changes \(Z\), and leaves normalized terminal probabilities unchanged. A correct GFlowNet policy should be invariant while its learned source flow rescales.

    Normalizing and Probability Flows¤

    Normalizing flows transport continuous density through bijections; flow matching learns continuous vector fields; GFlowNets conserve scalar mass on constructive edges. The shared word “flow” refers to conservation but the state spaces, equations, and likelihoods differ.

    Autoregressive Models¤

    Both sample sequential actions. An autoregressive model is trained from observed terminal sequences/objects by normalized conditional likelihood. A GFlowNet can train from terminal rewards without target samples and corrects for multiple construction paths using backward flow. If every object has one canonical trajectory and reward equals a known data probability, the forward policy resembles an AR factorization.

    For a unique-path prefix tree, choose \(R(x)=p_{\text{data}}(x)\) and \(Z=1\). The exact forward policy satisfies

    \[ P_F(x_t\mid x_{<t})= \frac{\sum_{x_{t+1:T}}p_{\text{data}}(x_{1:T})} {\sum_{x_{t:T}}p_{\text{data}}(x_{1:T})} =\frac{p_{\text{data}}(x_{1:t})}{p_{\text{data}}(x_{<t})}, \]

    the usual conditional factorization. This is an algebraic equivalence at the target distribution, not a claim that reward-based TB has the same gradient estimator, data access, or finite-sample behavior as maximum likelihood.

    When several token/action sequences denote the same object, AR likelihood normally models the sequences present in data. A GFlowNet can instead model an object-level reward and sum across constructions. Conversely, if the desired output really is the full sequence including order, there is no multiplicity to correct.

    Diffusion and Continuous-State GFlowNets¤

    GFlowNet balance ideas extend to continuous and diffusion trajectories. Relative trajectory balance for diffusion posteriors trains an amortized sampler for a reweighted diffusion prior. This is a research hybrid; it does not turn every diffusion loss into ordinary discrete TB.

    Continuous and Hybrid State Spaces¤

    Replacing each sum by an integral is insufficient because points in a continuous space have zero probability and densities depend on a reference measure. A Theory of Continuous Generative Flow Networks formulates flows as measures and transition kernels on measurable spaces, covering discrete, continuous, and hybrid cases under explicit assumptions. Conservation becomes equality of measures over sets, while Radon–Nikodym derivatives provide density-like local relations when they exist.

    A continuous construction may append a real-valued coordinate, place a particle, or grow a hybrid object with discrete type and continuous geometry. The implementation must state:

    • the state and action reference measures;
    • whether transition kernels have densities and on which support;
    • the terminal measure associated with reward density;
    • termination and finite-trajectory assumptions;
    • how sampled log densities include Jacobians or action-volume terms.

    Ignoring an action-space volume factor is the continuous analogue of ignoring path multiplicity. A coordinate reparameterization can then change the learned law even though the semantic reward appears unchanged.

    Diffusion paths add stochastic continuous-time dynamics rather than merely continuous actions. Relative TB methods use a pretrained diffusion/path measure as a reference and reweight terminal outcomes. Their backward/forward likelihood ratios and discretization belong to that construction. Equality of terminal targets does not make their finite-step solver, score loss, or path law identical to a DAG GFlowNet.

    A Six-Label Audit¤

    1. Algebraic equivalence: DB telescopes to a TB identity when one compatible state flow and the same policies are used.
    2. Distributional equivalence under assumptions: a unique-path GFlowNet and an exact AR factorization can define the same terminal law.
    3. Population-optimum correspondence: FM, DB, TB, and SubTB have the reward-proportional terminal solution when their respective global constraints and support assumptions hold.
    4. Shared estimator or objective: particular variational/divergence GFlowNet formulations may share score-function estimators with hierarchical VI.
    5. Implementation analogy: node residuals resemble TD residuals; graph flow resembles conservation in other flow families.
    6. Hybrid system composition: an EBM supplies reward to a GFlowNet; a diffusion prior supplies a reference path measure to relative TB.

    Worked example: one two-object target, four different path laws¤

    Exploration Is Part of Correctness¤

    Balance losses only constrain visited states and trajectories. Sparse high-reward modes may never be discovered. Useful strategies include exploratory behavior policies, replay buffers, backward trajectory sampling, tempered rewards, curriculum/pretraining, and active-learning loops. Each changes data collection; reward transformations may also change the target.

    Exploration has two phases. Discovery reaches previously unknown rewarding terminals; distribution fitting revisits enough of the graph to allocate probability in proportion to their rewards. A method can excel at the first and still miscalibrate the second. Conversely, a sampler can fit known modes well and never discover a separated mode.

    Common mechanisms have different semantics:

    • mixing \(P_F\) with a uniform valid-action policy changes the behavior distribution but can leave the evaluated target unchanged;
    • prioritizing replay toward high residual or high reward changes which constraints receive gradient updates;
    • sampling backward from known terminals improves credit coverage but cannot discover unknown terminals by itself;
    • pretraining on cheaper rewards changes initialization and may import reward-oracle bias;
    • sampling from \(R^\beta\) changes the target unless an explicit correction restores the original law.

    Discovery and fitting share data but have different success criteria. The target-policy evaluation branch must disable exploratory sampling, while the external-evidence branch tests the reward or scientific claim rather than the balance equations.

    Record the behavior policy, exploration coefficient/schedule, replay capacity and priorities, backward sampling rule, and reward evaluations. “Same number of updates” is not a controlled comparison when methods consume different oracle budgets.

    Worked example: exact proportionality beats rank correlation¤

    Let terminal rewards be \((1,2,3)\). The exact target is

    \[ p^*=\left(\frac16,\frac13,\frac12\right). \]

    Sampler A returns exactly \(p^*\). Sampler B returns the tempered law proportional to \(R^2\),

    \[ q=\left(\frac1{14},\frac4{14},\frac9{14}\right). \]

    Both samplers have perfect reward–frequency rank correlation, and sampler B has higher expected reward. Yet its total variation error is

    \[ D_{\mathrm{TV}}(p^*,q) =\frac12\sum_i|p_i^*-q_i| =\frac17. \]

    Thus rank correlation and best/mean reward can prefer a sampler with the wrong distribution. On an enumerated graph, direct probability error is the primary proportionality evidence; optimization and discovery metrics answer additional questions.

    On a finite test graph with exact target \(p^*(x)=R(x)/Z\), report at least total variation or KL where support permits, expected reward, effective sample size/entropy, and the error of \(\hat Z\). For large spaces, use reward-stratified calibration: bin objects by predicted or oracle reward and compare empirical terminal frequency with the mass implied by the target. Correlation between reward and frequency is necessary but not sufficient—a sampler proportional to \(R^{10}\) can have excellent rank correlation and the wrong distribution.

    Mode counts require a declared mode threshold or clustering rule and confidence intervals. Novelty is relative to a declared reference corpus. Diversity metrics should deduplicate semantic objects, not just construction strings. Scientific claims additionally need oracle calibration and, when possible, prospective physical or experimental validation.

    Before scaling, construct several enumerated environments:

    1. a chain with one path per terminal;
    2. a diamond with unequal path splits;
    3. equal-reward objects with unequal path multiplicity;
    4. a disconnected positive-reward mode that exploration must discover;
    5. variable-length paths with explicit stop;
    6. zero-reward failure terminals.

    Enumerate every trajectory and verify policy normalization, backward normalization, conservation, terminal frequencies, reward-rescaling behavior, learned \(Z\), and agreement among FM/DB/TB population solutions. These tests find semantic bugs that a large benchmark score cannot localize.

    Implementation Invariants¤

    1. The state graph must be acyclic or include an augmented construction-time state that makes trajectories finite.
    2. Forward and backward action masks must be mutually consistent.
    3. Terminal rewards must be nonnegative; log objectives require positive support handling.
    4. Stop actions and terminal states must be represented exactly once.
    5. Duplicate encodings of one object must be canonicalized or summed correctly.
    6. Log probabilities use stable normalization over valid actions only.

    7. Replay/exploration must cover target-relevant transitions.

    8. A semantic object ID must be separated from its trajectory/serialization ID.

    9. Context-specific normalizers and masks must use the same context as the reward.
    10. Evaluation sampling must declare whether exploration noise or target temperature remains enabled.

    sample a finite valid trajectory to an explicit terminal
    evaluate a nonnegative terminal reward and record oracle cost
    store states, masks, actions, context, reward, and behavior metadata
    choose on-policy and/or replay trajectories
    compute the declared FM, DB, TB, or SubTB residual in log space
    update models; monitor support, residuals by length/reward, and log-Z
periodically:
    sample with the target forward policy, not the exploratory behavior policy
    run exact toy tests and distributional/domain-specific evaluation

    This pseudocode deliberately leaves the neural architecture open. The core correctness risks are graph semantics, support, objectives, and evaluation—not whether the policy uses a Transformer, message-passing network, or MLP.

    Applications¤

    • molecular and material design with expensive property rewards;
    • Bayesian network and phylogenetic structure posteriors;
    • program, sequence, set, graph, and combinatorial-object generation;
    • multi-objective Pareto-conditioned discovery;
    • active learning where diverse batches are evaluated by an oracle;
    • amortized posterior inference under an unnormalized target.

    Multi-Objective GFlowNets condition on scalarizations or acquisition objectives. Atomic GFlowNet pretraining is a peer-reviewed 2025 example of using cheaper molecular signals before expensive reward optimization. Benchmark gains remain protocol-specific and should not be generalized across reward oracles.

    Genetic-guided GFlowNets combine evolutionary search signals with GFlowNet training for biological sequence design. The evidence is peer-reviewed research: better exploration on the reported oracles is not laboratory confirmation of biological function. Stochastic GFlowNets augment the framework for stochastic environments by separating agent actions from environment outcomes; ordinary deterministic-DAG equations should not be reused without that augmentation.

    A GFlowNet is the inference engine, not the experiment¤

    In a scientific-design system, a GFlowNet occupies one layer of a larger five-component decomposition:

    Table GFN.6. A GFlowNet is the inference engine, not the experiment
    Component Constructive-design realization
    1. reference and support semantic object definition, building blocks, action graph, validity rules, optional prior \(g_0(x\mid c)\)
    2. predictive model learned property surrogate, simulator, docking score, synthesis model, or calibrated ensemble
    3. target construction nonnegative reward, acquisition function, constraint indicator, scalarization, or posterior tilt
    4. inference engine the GFlowNet policy, backward policy, balance objective, exploration behavior, and replay system
    5. evidence and update high-fidelity simulation, synthesis, assay, field test, posterior/model update, and the next design round

    For example, a posterior-style design target can use

    \[ R_t(x\mid c)=g_{0,t}(x\mid c)\,w_t(x,c), \qquad P_{F,t}(x\mid c)=\frac{R_t(x\mid c)}{Z_t(c)}. \]

    If \(w_t(x,c)\) is a learned success probability, exact balance makes the terminal policy proportional to the learned success-weighted reference law. It does not make \(w_t\) calibrated outside its training support. If the reward is changed to \(R_t=w_t\), the reference factor is removed and the target distribution changes. If an acquisition function replaces success probability, uncertainty and information value become part of component 3; they are not uncertainty automatically produced by reward-proportional sampling.

    The three-candidate tilt makes the boundary executable. A GFlowNet could reproduce \((4/13,6/13,3/13)\) perfectly and still over-sample the candidate whose predicted success is \(0.9\) but prospective success is \(0.1\). Flow error and oracle error are orthogonal:

    \[ \underbrace{D\!\left(P_F,\frac{R}{Z}\right)}_{\text{sampler fidelity}} \quad\text{and}\quad \underbrace{\mathbb E_{X\sim P_F} \left[\hat u(X)-u_{\mathrm{real}}(X)\right]}_{\text{selection-conditioned oracle gap}} \]

    answer different questions. Low balance residual or low terminal divergence cannot upper-bound the second quantity without assumptions connecting the learned score to real outcomes on the selected distribution.

    “Oracle” is therefore a role, not an evidence grade. A cheap neural score, a docking program, a density-functional calculation, an expert review, and a prospective assay have different error mechanisms and costs. Record which one supplies training reward, which one selects candidates, and which one is withheld for validation.

    Evidence from complete design loops¤

    • Conservative Objective Models provide peer-reviewed research evidence that optimizing a learned offline objective can drive selection toward erroneously overestimated out-of-distribution inputs. Their conservative construction is one defense, not a universal calibration guarantee.
    • The Practical Molecular Optimization benchmark compares 25 methods on 23 tasks and shows that conclusions change under a fixed 10,000-call computational-oracle budget. It evaluates optimisation protocols, not wet-lab discovery.
    • SynGFN is peer-reviewed research for reaction-based constructive support, hierarchical policy pretraining, and multifidelity acquisition in its reported molecular protocols. Its computational design results do not turn every reward evaluation into experimental evidence.
    • A 2026 closed-loop molecular active-learning study feeds higher-fidelity density-functional calculations back into property models and reports large selected-region prediction improvements. This supports the value of component 5 while remaining simulation-based evidence.
    • SyntheMol synthesized 58 generated molecules and reported antibacterial activity for six. This is prospective experimental evidence for that pipeline and assay protocol, not proof that its computational reward is generally calibrated.

    These examples differ in model family, support, target, inference, and evidence level. Their common lesson is architectural: compare complete loops at matched real-oracle budget, not generators at unlimited surrogate calls.

    Retrieval practice: localize the design-system claim

    1. What does exact GFlowNet balance establish in a design loop?

    2. Where should epistemic uncertainty enter if the desired target is an acquisition distribution?

    3. Can a prospective assay validate reward proportionality?

    4. Can reward proportionality validate biological activity?

    Solutions

    1. Fidelity of the covered terminal law to the declared nonnegative reward, subject to the graph, support, and optimization assumptions.

    2. In the predictive model and target construction; for example, an ensemble supplies uncertainty and an acquisition rule converts it into nonnegative reward.

    3. Not by itself. Assays test external utility; proportionality requires terminal-frequency evidence against \(R/Z\) or another target-specific diagnostic.

    4. No. A sampler can match a misspecified computational reward exactly. Activity requires an appropriate external experiment.

    Scientific-Discovery Capstone¤

    Suppose the goal is to propose a diverse batch of molecules for an expensive assay.

    1. Define the semantic object. A molecular graph, including stereochemistry and charge—not a construction string.
    2. Define the graph. Valid atom/bond actions, canonical terminal identity, explicit stop, and failure states.
    3. Define reward. A nonnegative combination of predicted activity, uncertainty/acquisition value, synthesizability, and constraints. State whether scalarization changes across contexts.
    4. Separate oracle levels. Cheap learned predictor, higher-fidelity simulator, synthesis feasibility, and physical assay are not interchangeable evidence.
    5. Train with coverage controls. Track unique semantic terminals, replay age, behavior support, oracle calls, residuals by length and reward, and \(Z\) stability.
    6. Evaluate distribution and utility. Use an enumerated proxy for proportionality, compare baselines at equal oracle/compute budget, and report novelty, diversity, calibration, and prospective hit rate.
    7. Close the loop. When assays return, update the reward/posterior and disclose whether the sampler was retrained, conditioned, or evaluated out of distribution.

    The terminal target is only as meaningful as the reward. A perfectly trained GFlowNet can faithfully amplify a biased predictor; the five-component audit makes that failure attributable rather than calling the entire pipeline a bad generator.

    Failure Modes¤

    • undiscovered modes and replay self-reinforcement;
    • reward hacking or simulator exploitation;
    • trajectory-length bias from incorrect backward policies;
    • duplicate-object multiplicity errors;
    • unstable learned \(Z\) and log-reward scale;
    • invalid actions or dead-end partial states;
    • high reward but low real-world feasibility;

    • conditional sampler failure outside trained reward/context ranges.

    • offline non-identifiability when logged prefixes omit actions;

    • continuous-action density errors from missing reference-measure or Jacobian terms;
    • confusing discovery of high reward with proportional fitting;
    • reporting forward-policy samples generated with exploration enabled as target samples.

    Exploration Research¤

    Avoid What You Know: Divergent Trajectory Balance, accepted at ICML 2026, introduces adaptive complementary exploration aimed at undersampled high-reward regions. It is peer-reviewed research evidence that exploration remains a first-order training bottleneck. It does not change the population reward-proportional target or remove the need to evaluate proportionality and oracle coverage outside the reported benchmarks.

    Evaluation Balance, accepted as an ICLR 2026 poster, derives a partial-episode evaluator for policy-based GFlowNet training and studies parameterized backward policies and offline data. It should be read as an optimisation/evaluation contribution: exact population balance and target coverage assumptions remain the reference semantics.

    Flow of Spans (FoSS), accepted as an ICLR 2026 poster, constructs a DAG over alternative retrieved-span segmentations so that multiple construction paths for the same text are represented explicitly. Its reported MAUVE and knowledge-task gains are specific to its GPT-2/BERT, retrieval, reward-model, and datastore protocols. FoSS illustrates why the state graph matters; it does not imply that every language model benefits from a GFlowNet objective or that MAUVE alone establishes reward proportionality.

    Conservative GFlowNets for LLM-based recommendation, accepted at ICML 2026, identifies non-identifiability of naive SubTB on partially supported logged token-prefix DAGs and proposes CFlower. This is especially important negative evidence: an objective valid under online coverage can be underdetermined offline. The paper's accuracy–exposure results remain specific to its Amazon recommendation protocols.

    PowerFlow, accepted at ICML 2026, frames unsupervised LLM fine-tuning as matching an \(\alpha\)-power distribution and introduces a length-aware TB objective. Its sharpening/flattening interpretation and reported reasoning/creativity trade-offs are peer-reviewed research evidence for the named models and evaluations, not a general demonstration that a power target improves both properties in every language model.

    Together, these papers broaden exploration, evaluation, offline learning, structured language DAGs, and length-aware distribution matching. None removes the established contract: declare the target, construction graph, support, objective, and evaluation distribution separately.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. The behavior law is \(0.8(0.9,0.1)+0.2(0.5,0.5)=(0.82,0.18)\), not the target. Those frequencies validate only the mixed behavior sampler. Disable the exploration mixture, sample from the normalized target forward policy with declared target temperature, and report the behavior run separately as training/coverage evidence.
    2. \(Z=6\) and \(p^*=(1/6,1/3,1/2)\). As derived above, \(D_{\mathrm{TV}}=1/7\). Expected reward is \(7/3\approx2.333\) under \(p^*\) and \(36/14=18/7\approx2.571\) under \(q\). Both assign positive mass to both threshold modes, so catalog coverage is complete, although their masses are miscalibrated. TV measures proportionality, expected reward measures utility tilt, and mode coverage ignores within-mode calibration.
    3. The chain checks normalization and one-path products; the diamond checks path aggregation and backward allocation; unequal multiplicity checks semantic canonicalization; the disconnected mode checks exploration/coverage; explicit stop checks length and single terminal accounting; zero reward checks positive-log support or failure-sink policy. Across all applicable cases, multiplying rewards by a constant must leave terminal frequencies unchanged while shifting \(Z\), and trajectory IDs must aggregate to canonical semantic IDs.
    4. Support is the molecular representation, building blocks, validity graph, and synthesizability restrictions. The docking surrogate is the predictive model; its nonnegative transform is the target; the GFlowNet is the inference engine; synthesis and assay are external evidence. Exact reward matching rules out sampler-fidelity error on covered support, not docking misspecification or selection-conditioned extrapolation. Add assay outcomes to a calibrated model/update loop, preserve a held-out prospective test, redefine the reward transparently, and retrain or recondition before the next round.
    5. Top-\(k\) benchmark results support optimization under that benchmark and budget but not proportional fitting or wet-lab validity. Exact toy tests support implementation semantics and population-law recovery on the enumerated graph but not scaling. A fixed-budget computational loop supports sample/decision efficiency against that simulator and protocol but not physical discovery. Prospective synthesis and assay support external outcomes for selected candidates and assay conditions but do not by themselves prove that all terminal frequencies follow \(R/Z\).
    6. Define catalysts up to graph/geometry symmetries, with discrete composition/bond actions and continuous coordinates relative to declared base measures and Jacobians. Separate agent actions from stochastic simulator outcomes and use a stochastic-GFlowNet formulation rather than deterministic DAG equations. Audit logged transitions for unsupported actions; combine online exploration or conservative restrictions with explicit scope. Use a calibrated ensemble to turn uncertainty and predicted utility into a nonnegative acquisition reward. Train a declared DB/TB/SubTB objective, validate on enumerated coarse catalysts and coordinate-reparameterization tests, and compare to MCMC/SMC/search at matched simulator calls, amortization, and wall time. Finally fabricate and test a preregistered held-out batch, update the predictor from returned outcomes, and keep sampler proportionality and experimental success as separate endpoints.

    Final Synthesis¤

    GFlowNets turn an unnormalized terminal reward into an amortized constructive sampler. Conservation connects trajectory, edge, state, and terminal flows; local, trajectory, and subtrajectory balance provide complementary training constraints. The backward policy allocates reward among multiple construction histories, while source flow estimates the partition function. Their promise—diverse sampling in combinatorial scientific spaces—depends on exploration, correct state-graph semantics, and evaluation of proportionality rather than only best reward.
