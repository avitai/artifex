    VAE Variants¤

    VAE Variants: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the rate–distortion interpretation of the ELBO and the reduction audit from Chapter 2. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. identify exactly which term β-VAE, β-TCVAE, FactorVAE, InfoVAE, MMD-VAE, AAE, CVAE, and VQ-VAE modify;
    2. distinguish conditional factorization, aggregate-posterior independence, and semantic disentanglement;
    3. distinguish a conditional VAE from a joint multimodal latent-variable model;
    4. derive Gaussian product-of-experts updates for any observed-modality subset without counting the prior twice;
    5. write labeled and unlabeled bounds for a semi-supervised VAE;
    6. derive the VQ straight-through/codebook/commitment updates and diagnose codebook underuse;
    7. separate posterior collapse from a valid low-rate optimum;
    8. explain why matching \(q_\phi(z)\) to \(p(z)\) does not determine mutual information or per-example inference; and
    9. select interventions through controlled ablations rather than modality-wide hyperparameter rules.

    β-VAE: Disentangled Representations¤

    β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017) modifies the objective to encourage disentanglement, where individual latent dimensions capture independent factors of variation:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\beta} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]

    Effect of β:

    • β = 1: Standard VAE (no additional emphasis on disentanglement)
    • β > 1: Stronger rate penalty often reduces encoded information and can improve some disentanglement metrics on selected factorized datasets; neither independence nor semantic axes are guaranteed
    • β < 1: Weaker rate penalty often improves reconstruction and latent usage, but moves away from the ordinary ELBO; its effect on semantic structure is empirical

    Use \(\beta=1\) as the ordinary-ELBO baseline, then sweep values on a declared grid against reconstruction, rate, prior sampling, and the downstream representation metric. Values reported for dSprites or a text decoder are historical protocol points, not transferable image/text defaults. Burgess et al. (2018) analyse capacity control and propose annealing a target capacity \(C\); this changes the optimization path and still requires identifiability assumptions.

    Applications:

    • Interpretable representations for analysis and visualization
    • Fairness research only when leakage, downstream harms, and affected-group metrics are explicitly tested; factorization does not remove sensitive information by construction
    • Controllable generation by manipulating specific latent factors

    Beta-TCVAE and FactorVAE: Targeting Total Correlation¤

    The expected per-example KL decomposes into mutual information, aggregate total correlation, and dimension-wise prior mismatch under the stated factorized prior. These components affect rate and representation differently; total correlation targets aggregate dependence, but no one term alone guarantees semantic disentanglement. Two variants emphasize it:

    • FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih, 2018) trains a discriminator to estimate the total correlation \(\mathrm{TC}(z) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\,\|\,\prod_j q_\phi(z_j))\) and adds a \(\gamma\cdot\mathrm{TC}\) penalty on top of the standard ELBO.
    • β-TCVAE (Chen et al., 2018) decomposes the KL term in closed form into mutual information, total correlation, and dimension-wise KL, and re-weights only the TC component:
    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)) = \underbrace{I_q(x;z)}_{\text{MI}} + \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z)\|\textstyle\prod_j q_\phi(z_j))}_{\text{TC}} + \underbrace{\sum_j D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z_j)\|p(z_j))}_{\text{dim-wise KL}} \]

    β-TCVAE reported favorable disentanglement–reconstruction trade-offs relative to its evaluated baselines, and introduced the Mutual Information Gap (MIG). The estimator, dataset factors, hyperparameters, and random seeds are part of that empirical result.

    Aggregate-Posterior Objectives: InfoVAE, MMD-VAE, and AAE¤

    The ordinary expected prior KL charges two quantities at once:

    \[ \begin{aligned} &\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)\right)\\ &\quad=I_q(X;Z) +D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)\right). \end{aligned} \]

    An objective may therefore change the amount of information carried about each record without changing how strongly the aggregate code distribution is matched to the prior. InfoVAE makes these controls explicit. Define the population reconstruction, expected conditional KL, and aggregate discrepancy as

    \[ \begin{aligned} R&:=\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)} [\log p_\theta(x\mid z)],\\ K&:=\mathbb E_{p_{\mathcal D}(x)} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x)\|p(z)\right),\\ M&:=D\!\left(q_\phi(z)\|p(z)\right). \end{aligned} \]

    One parameterization of the InfoVAE objective is then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal L_{\mathrm{InfoVAE}} ={}&R-(1-\alpha)K\\ &-(\alpha+\lambda-1)M. \end{aligned} \]

    Here \(D\) is a chosen aggregate-distribution discrepancy. Setting \(\alpha=0\) and \(\lambda=1\) recovers the ordinary ELBO because the coefficient of \(D\) becomes zero. Other coefficients generally define a different objective, not another bound on \(\log p_\theta(x)\). The population proposition in the InfoVAE paper assumes a strict divergence, \(\alpha<1\), \(\lambda>0\), a fixed bounded mutual information, and sufficiently expressive distributions. It does not guarantee the behavior of a finite discriminator, kernel estimator, neural family, or optimizer.

    MMD-VAE: sample the distribution you claim to match¤

    MMD-VAE replaces the intractable aggregate KL by maximum mean discrepancy. Given \(m\) aggregate-posterior samples \(z_i\sim q_\phi(z)\) and \(m\) independent prior samples \(\tilde z_i\sim p(z)\), an unbiased squared-MMD estimator is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat{\operatorname{MMD}}_{u}^{\,2} ={}&\frac{1}{m(m-1)}\\[-2pt] &\quad\times\sum_{i\ne j}k(z_i,z_j)\\ &+\frac{1}{m(m-1)}\\[-2pt] &\quad\times\sum_{i\ne j}k(\tilde z_i,\tilde z_j)\\ &-\frac{2}{m^2} \sum_{i,j}k(z_i,\tilde z_j). \end{aligned} \]

    To sample \(q_\phi(z)\), first sample a record and then sample \(z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)\). Substituting posterior means \(\mu_\phi(x)\) matches the distribution of deterministic means instead; it is a different target unless \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) is itself deterministic. A characteristic kernel makes the population MMD identify equality of the two distributions. Kernel choice, bandwidth, finite-batch estimator, and optimization still determine what signal the model receives. In particular, this unbiased finite-sample estimator can be negative even though population \(\operatorname{MMD}^2\) is nonnegative.

    AAE: an adversarial aggregate-matching game¤

    Adversarial Autoencoders combine a reconstruction objective with a discriminator trained to distinguish samples from \(q_\phi(z)\) and \(p(z)\). Under the original logistic game, an unrestricted population-optimal discriminator corresponds to Jensen–Shannon matching. A finite neural discriminator and alternating optimizer provide only an estimated training signal. An AAE can also use a deterministic encoder, so its name does not imply a Gaussian variational posterior or an ordinary ELBO. The required contract is the encoder distribution, reconstruction likelihood or distortion, adversarial loss, prior sampler, and update schedule.

    Worked finite counterexample: the same aggregate, different information¤

    Let \(X\) and the prior \(Z\) both be uniform on \(\{0,1\}\). Compare two encoders:

    1. Record-preserving: \(q_A(z\mid x)=\mathbf 1[z=x]\).
    2. Record-ignoring: \(q_B(z\mid x)=1/2\) for both values of \(z\) and every \(x\).

    Both have the exact aggregate \(q_A(z)=q_B(z)=p(z)\), so every strict aggregate discrepancy is zero. But

    \[ I_{q_A}(X;Z)=\log 2, \qquad I_{q_B}(X;Z)=0. \]

    Encoder A supports zero reconstruction NLL with the deterministic decoder \(x=z\). Under encoder B, the best decoder for this balanced population remains \(p(x\mid z)=1/2\) and pays \(\log2\) nats per record. Aggregate matching is thus neither a collapse cure nor a semantic-representation guarantee. It must be read together with reconstruction, mutual information, inference accuracy, and the intended downstream query.

    Retrieval practice: aggregate-posterior objectives
    1. Can \(D(q_\phi(z)\|p(z))=0\) prove that \(Z\) contains information about \(X\)?
    2. Why is an MMD computed on posterior means generally not an estimator of MMD between \(q_\phi(z)\) and \(p(z)\)?
    3. When does the InfoVAE parameterization displayed above reduce to the ordinary ELBO?
    4. Does adversarial aggregate matching make an AAE a maximum-likelihood VAE?

    Solutions. (1) No; the two binary encoders above have the same perfectly matched aggregate but mutual information \(\log2\) and zero. (2) The aggregate posterior includes conditional sampling noise; posterior means define a different pushforward distribution unless the encoder is deterministic. (3) At \(\alpha=0\) and \(\lambda=1\); the aggregate-discrepancy coefficient is then zero. (4) No. The reconstruction term, discriminator game, variational family, and finite optimization must be stated; ordinary ELBO and likelihood identities do not follow from the AAE label.

    Conditional VAE (CVAE)¤

    Conditional VAEs (Sohn et al., 2015) incorporate additional information \(y\) (class labels, attributes, text descriptions) to enable controlled generation:

    \[ \begin{align} q_\phi(z|x, y) &= \mathcal{N}(z; \mu_\phi(x, y), \sigma^2_\phi(x, y) \mathbf{I}) \\ p_\theta(x|z, y) &= \mathcal{N}(x; \mu_\theta(z, y), \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}) \end{align} \]

    How conditioning works:

    • Concatenation: Append \(y\) to the input before encoding and to \(z\) before decoding — simple but less expressive than feature-wise modulation.
    • Conditional Batch Normalization (De Vries et al., 2017; Dumoulin et al., 2017): Replace BN's affine parameters with \(y\)-dependent values.
    • FiLM (Feature-wise Linear Modulation) (Perez et al., 2018): Scale and shift each feature channel by \(y\)-dependent \((\gamma, \beta)\). Used pervasively in modern conditional generators.
    • Cross-attention: For sequence/text conditions, attend from feature maps onto an encoded \(y\) — the technique that powers Stable Diffusion's text-to-image conditioning.

    Applications:

    • Class-conditional generation: Generate specific digit classes in MNIST or specific ImageNet classes (Mirza & Osindero, 2014).
    • Attribute manipulation: Change hair color, age, expression in face images.
    • Multimodal conditional decoding: use a learned condition in encoder/decoder distributions. DALL·E 1's autoregressive prior over discrete image tokens and latent-diffusion systems are related hybrid pipelines, not examples of the basic CVAE objective.

    Conditional generation is not joint multimodal modeling¤

    A CVAE normally treats \(y\) as supplied side information and models \(p_\theta(x\mid y)\). It need not define \(p(y)\), so it cannot by itself answer joint-density questions about \((x,y)\) or generate \(y\) unconditionally. A multimodal latent-variable model instead defines a joint law over all modalities. A semi-supervised model goes one step further: the same variable \(y\) is observed in some records and latent in others.

    Table VAE.4. Conditional generation is not joint multimodal modeling
    Goal Probabilistic object What must be available at generation or inference time?
    Controlled generation \(p_\theta(x\mid y)\) the condition \(y\)
    Joint multimodal generation \(p_\theta(x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(M)})\) no modality is necessarily supplied
    Cross-modal generation \(p_\theta(x_T\mid x_S)\) an observed subset \(x_S\); targets \(T\) are generated
    Semi-supervised classification and generation \(p_\theta(x,y)\) \(y\) is observed for labeled records and inferred for unlabeled records

    Calling every one of these systems a “conditional VAE” hides different model, inference, and data contracts.

    Multimodal VAEs and Missing Modalities¤

    Let \(X=(X^{(1)},\ldots,X^{(M)})\) denote \(M\) modalities and let \(S\subseteq\{1,\ldots,M\}\) index the modalities observed for one query. A shared-latent multimodal VAE commonly assumes

    \[ p_\theta(z,x^{(1:M)}) =p(z)\prod_{m=1}^{M}p_{\theta_m}(x^{(m)}\mid z). \]

    The factorization says that the modalities are conditionally independent given the declared latent \(Z\). It does not say that raw modalities are marginally independent, nor does it guarantee that one shared latent captures modality-private variation. When private information matters, the model may need modality-specific latents \(u_m\) in addition to \(z\).

    For observed modalities \(x_S\) and target modalities \(T\subseteq\{1,\ldots,M\}\setminus S\), the model's cross-modal predictive distribution is

    \[ p_\theta(x_T\mid x_S) =\int\left[\prod_{m\in T}p_{\theta_m}(x^{(m)}\mid z)\right] p_\theta(z\mid x_S)\,dz. \]

    This is a distribution, not a single filled-in value. Its spread should retain ambiguity left unresolved by the observed modalities.

    Which ELBO is being optimized?¤

    Let \(q_{\phi,S}(z\mid x_S)\) be an inference distribution that consumes only the observed subset. If a training record contains scored values for a set \(A\) with \(S\subseteq A\), then

    \[ \mathcal B_{S\rightarrow A}(x_A) =\mathbb E_{q_{\phi,S}(z\mid x_S)} \left[\sum_{m\in A}\log p_{\theta_m}(x^{(m)}\mid z)\right] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_{\phi,S}(z\mid x_S)\,\|\,p(z)\right). \]
    • With \(A=S\), this is an ELBO for the observed-subset marginal \(\log p_\theta(x_S)\).
    • With a fully paired record and \(A=\{1,\ldots,M\}\), it is a joint-data ELBO whose proposal is restricted to use \(x_S\). Training several such directions teaches cross-generation.
    • A missing target value cannot contribute its reconstruction likelihood merely because a decoder could predict it; the value is unavailable for scoring.
    • Modality weights \(\lambda_m\) can balance gradients or encode utilities, but arbitrary weights generally define a reweighted objective rather than the ordinary joint-likelihood ELBO. Report them as part of the estimand.

    The MVAE construction uses subset-based training so the encoders are exercised on incomplete inputs. An architecture that can syntactically omit an encoder is not thereby trained for every omission pattern.

    Gaussian likelihood messages: the product update¤

    Suppose the shared latent is \(d\)-dimensional, with \(p(z)=\mathcal N(z;\mu_0,\Lambda_0^{-1})\). Let modality \(m\) contribute the Gaussian-shaped likelihood message

    \[ e_m(z;x^{(m)}) \propto \exp\!\left[-\frac12(z-r_m)^\top\Delta\Lambda_m(z-r_m)\right], \qquad \Delta\Lambda_m\succeq0. \]

    Under the shared-coordinate, conditional-independence, Gaussian-message, and positive-semidefinite-precision assumptions, the subset posterior

    \[ q_S(z)\propto p(z)\prod_{m\in S}e_m(z;x^{(m)}) \]

    has natural parameters

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Lambda_S &=\Lambda_0+\sum_{m\in S}\Delta\Lambda_m,\\ \eta_S &=\Lambda_0\mu_0+ \sum_{m\in S}\Delta\Lambda_m r_m,\\ \Sigma_S&=\Lambda_S^{-1}, &\mu_S&=\Sigma_S\eta_S. \end{aligned} \]

    Omitting a missing modality means omitting its likelihood message. It does not mean inserting a zero observation. With no observed modalities, the result is the prior.

    Worked two-modality Gaussian model¤

    Consider the scalar model

    \[ \begin{aligned} Z&\sim\mathcal N(0,1),\\ X^{(1)}\mid Z=z&\sim\mathcal N(z,1),\\ X^{(2)}\mid Z=z&\sim\mathcal N(z,4). \end{aligned} \]

    Suppose the realized observations are \(x^{(1)}=1\) and \(x^{(2)}=-1\). The prior precision is \(1\). Modality 1 contributes precision \(1\) and natural parameter \(1\); modality 2 contributes precision \(1/4\) and natural parameter \(-1/4\). The four possible observation patterns therefore give:

    Table VAE.5. Worked two-modality Gaussian model
    \(S\) \(\Lambda_S\) \(\mu_S\) \(\Sigma_S\)
    \(\varnothing\) \(1\) \(0\) \(1\)
    \(\{1\}\) \(2\) \(1/2\) \(1/2\)
    \(\{2\}\) \(5/4\) \(-1/5\) \(4/5\)
    \(\{1,2\}\) \(9/4\) \(1/3\) \(4/9\)

    The more precise first modality outweighs the conflicting second modality; the mean is not an unweighted average. Missing-modality prediction preserves both latent and decoder uncertainty:

    \[ \begin{aligned} X^{(2)}\mid X^{(1)}=1 &\sim\mathcal N\!\left(\frac12,\frac92\right),\\ X^{(1)}\mid X^{(2)}=-1 &\sim\mathcal N\!\left(-\frac15,\frac95\right). \end{aligned} \]

    A point imputation would conceal this uncertainty and could make a many-valued cross-modal query appear deterministic.

    Do not count the prior twice¤

    The preceding \(e_m\) are likelihood messages, not normalized unimodal posteriors. For exact single-modality posteriors under the same prior, Bayes' rule gives the identity

    \[ p(z\mid x_S) \propto p(z)^{1-|S|}\prod_{m\in S}p(z\mid x^{(m)}). \]

    If encoders instead output \(q_m(z\mid x^{(m)})\approx p(z\mid x^{(m)})\), the corresponding approximate aggregator is

    \[ \widetilde q_S(z) \propto p(z)^{1-|S|}\prod_{m\in S}q_m(z\mid x^{(m)}). \]

    For Gaussian posterior experts \(q_m=\mathcal N(\mu_m,\Lambda_m^{-1})\), this becomes

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Lambda_S &=\sum_{m\in S}\Lambda_m-(|S|-1)\Lambda_0,\\ \eta_S &=\sum_{m\in S}\Lambda_m\mu_m -(|S|-1)\Lambda_0\mu_0. \end{aligned} \]

    The resulting precision must remain positive definite. In the scalar example, the unimodal posteriors are \(q_1=\mathcal N(1/2,1/2)\) and \(q_2=\mathcal N(-1/5,4/5)\). The corrected rule has precision \(2+5/4-1=9/4\) and mean \(1/3\), agreeing with exact Bayes. The naive product \(p(z)q_1(z)q_2(z)\) has precision \(17/4\) and mean \(3/17\); it is spuriously overconfident because it includes the same prior three times.

    Name the expert parameterization

    A formula of the form \(p(z)\prod_m q_m(z\mid x^{(m)})\) is valid when the learned factors are intended as likelihood-like messages. If each factor is advertised as a complete unimodal posterior, the prior correction above is required. The symbol \(q_m\) alone does not settle which object was learned.

    PoE, MoE, mixtures of products, and learned aggregation¤

    There is no universally superior aggregation rule. Each encodes a different geometry and failure mode.

    Table VAE.6. PoE, MoE, mixtures of products, and learned aggregation
    Aggregator Distributional effect Useful behavior Characteristic risk
    Product of likelihood experts Intersects evidence; Gaussian precision accumulates Confident agreement and modular subset inference One overconfident or contradictory expert can dominate; dependence can be counted as independent evidence
    Mixture of posterior experts Forms a weighted union \(\sum_m\pi_mq_m\) Retains alternative modes and weak experts Does not perform Bayesian evidence accumulation; a sample may follow only one expert
    Mixture of subset products Mixes PoE posteriors over modality subsets Trades conjunction against mode coverage All \(2^M\) subsets are expensive without sampling or structure
    Learned set/dependence-aware aggregator Learns interactions and can be permutation invariant Can represent correlated, heterogeneous evidence Needs mask and dependence coverage; loses the closed-form Bayesian interpretation

    MMVAE studies mixture aggregation, while MoPoE-VAE mixes subset-specific products. Later work makes the assumptions more explicit: CoDE-VAE models dependence among experts, and HELVAE places PoE and MoE inside a broader opinion-pooling view. These are peer-reviewed research results, not proofs that one aggregator wins for every modality, mask distribution, or query.

    The observation mask is part of the problem¤

    Let \(R\in\{0,1\}^M\) record which modalities are observed. Three issues must not be conflated:

    Table VAE.7. The observation mask is part of the problem
    Situation Statistical statement Consequence for a generator
    Designed mask or modality dropout \(R\) is chosen by the training protocol Robustness is learned only for masks and frequencies actually sampled
    Missing at random The mechanism can depend on observed values, but not on missing values after conditioning on what is observed Ignoring the mask mechanism in likelihood/Bayesian inference additionally requires the usual parameter-distinctness conditions
    Missing not at random \(R\) still depends on an unobserved value or latent cause after conditioning on observed data Modeling only \(p(x)\) can give biased conditionals; model or sensitivity-analyze \(p(R\mid x,z)\)

    This is the generative-model consequence of the classical missing-data conditions in Rubin (1976). A zero, empty sequence, mask token, and unavailable sensor are different observations. The model must receive an unambiguous mask, and evaluation must preserve the deployment missingness mechanism rather than randomly deleting modalities by default.

    Missing-modality robustness is trained, not inherited

    Training only \(q(z\mid x^{(1)},x^{(2)})\) does not validate \(q(z\mid x^{(1)})\) or \(q(z\mid x^{(2)})\). Exercise the intended subset encoders or mask-aware set encoder during training, or perform query-adaptive inference against the decoder at test time. Even then, extrapolation to unseen masks or a changed missingness mechanism remains an empirical claim.

    The query-adaptive inference approach is an important alternative: optimize a posterior for the particular observed query using the trained decoder instead of assuming a fixed amortized encoder generalizes to every mask. It trades test-time optimization for less dependence on the encoder's training-mask support.

    Training and evaluation contract¤

    For a model intended to support arbitrary modality subsets:

    1. define the modeled joint, any shared/private latents, and the conditional- independence assumptions;
    2. specify whether each encoder emits a likelihood message, posterior expert, mixture component, or learned feature;
    3. sample training masks from a declared distribution, retaining full and single-modality anchor cases when those queries matter;
    4. score only target values actually present in the training record and report every modality likelihood scale or reweighting coefficient;
    5. separate paired, weakly paired, and unpaired records—marginal datasets alone do not identify their coupling without additional assumptions;
    6. evaluate unconditional joint generation, reconstruction, every required cross-generation direction, conditional diversity, calibration, and performance by mask; and
    7. test contradictory inputs and modality ablations so a strong expert cannot silently erase weaker modalities.

    Multimodal ELBOs can also suffer modality collapse, where optimization serves an easy or high-gradient modality while neglecting another. This is distinct from posterior collapse and should be diagnosed with per-modality likelihoods, gradient contributions, cross-generation, and intervention tests; see Javaloy et al. (2022).

    Semi-Supervised VAE: One Label, Two Observation States¤

    Semi-supervised learning is not just a CVAE with some label vectors replaced by zeros. In the M2 model of Kingma et al. (2014), the label is a random variable in the generative model:

    \[ p_\theta(x,y,z)=p(y)p(z)p_\theta(x\mid y,z). \]

    Labeled observation state. Both \(x\) and \(y\) are observed, while \(z\) is latent.

    Unlabeled observation state. Only \(x\) is observed; inference must represent uncertainty about both \(y\) and \(z\).

    Solid arrows show the generative graph; dotted arrows show variational inference. For a labeled pair \((x,y)\), use \(q_\phi(z\mid x,y)\) and the bound

    \[ \mathcal B_L(x,y) =\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x,y)}[\log p_\theta(x\mid y,z)] -D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z\mid x,y)\,\|\,p(z)\right) +\log p(y). \]

    For an unlabeled \(x\), introduce \(q_\phi(y,z\mid x)=q_\phi(y\mid x)q_\phi(z\mid x,y)\). When \(Y\) is a small discrete label set, marginalize it exactly:

    \[ \mathcal B_U(x) =\sum_y q_\phi(y\mid x)\mathcal B_L(x,y) +H\!\left[q_\phi(y\mid x)\right]. \]

    The entropy term is not an optional confidence bonus; it comes from \(-\mathbb E_q\log q_\phi(y\mid x)\) in the unlabeled ELBO. An auxiliary supervised term \(\alpha\log q_\phi(y\mid x)\) is commonly added on labeled records because the generative bound alone may train the classifier weakly. Its coefficient changes the combined objective and must be reported.

    Two misconception checks follow directly:

    • Replacing an unobserved label by \(\arg\max_y q(y\mid x)\) is hard pseudo- labeling, not exact marginalization; it discards class uncertainty and changes the estimator.
    • If the probability that a label is observed depends on the unobserved class after conditioning on \(x\), the ordinary M2 objective does not repair that selection mechanism. The label-observation process must be modeled or sensitivity-analyzed.

    Vector Quantized VAE (VQ-VAE)¤

    VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) turns each continuous encoder vector into a finite index and its associated embedding. This creates two related but different latent objects:

    • the index \(C_j\in\{1,\ldots,K\}\), which a categorical prior models; and
    • the embedding \(E_{C_j}\in\mathbb R^d\), which the decoder consumes.

    For position \(j\), let \(A_j=E_\phi(x)_j\) be the encoder output and let \(\mathcal C=\{e_1,\ldots,e_K\}\) be the codebook. Squared-Euclidean nearest- neighbour quantization gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} c_j(x) &=\arg\min_{k\in\{1,\ldots,K\}} \lVert A_j-e_k\rVert_2^2,\\ Z_{q,j}(x)&=e_{c_j(x)},\\ q_\phi(C_j=k\mid x)&=\mathbf 1\{k=c_j(x)\}. \end{aligned} \]

    The tie rule, distance metric, feature normalization, and search approximation are part of this encoder. An index is not itself a point in Euclidean space: renaming every index while permuting the codebook leaves the codec unchanged, but can invalidate a separately trained prior that expects the old names.

    A probabilistic statement and an optimization recipe¤

    If every position has a uniform categorical prior \(p(C_j=k)=1/K\) and the posterior above is deterministic, then

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(C_j\mid x)\,\|\,p(C_j)\right) =\log K. \]

    For \(T\) positions with a factorized uniform prior, the KL is \(T\log K\) and does not train the encoder. The reconstruction term is therefore the only data-dependent term in that elementary ELBO. The codebook and commitment terms below are auxiliary optimization terms; they should not be silently described as likelihood or KL terms.

    Define the straight-through decoder input

    \[ Z_{q,j}^{\mathrm{ST}} =A_j+\operatorname{sg}\!\left(e_{c_j}-A_j\right), \]

    where \(\operatorname{sg}\) is identity in the forward pass and has zero derivative. The decoder sees \(e_{c_j}\), while automatic differentiation uses \(\partial Z_{q,j}^{\mathrm{ST}}/\partial A_j=I\) and \(\partial Z_{q,j}^{\mathrm{ST}}/\partial e_{c_j}=0\). A common loss is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal J_{\mathrm{codec}} =&-\log p_\theta\!\left(x\mid Z_q^{\mathrm{ST}}\right)\\ &+\alpha\sum_j \left\lVert\operatorname{sg}(A_j)-e_{c_j}\right\rVert_2^2 +\beta\sum_j \left\lVert A_j-\operatorname{sg}(e_{c_j})\right\rVert_2^2. \end{aligned} \]

    The first line may be a declared negative log likelihood or a reconstruction, perceptual, adversarial, or task loss. Only a normalized observation model makes it a likelihood. The second line contains, respectively, the codebook and commitment losses. Their coefficients depend on reduction axes, feature scale, and the other loss terms; values from one implementation are not universal.

    The dashed arrows are backward/update routes, not additional forward edges. Under this construction, reconstruction gradients update the decoder and are copied to the encoder; they do not update the codebook. The codebook loss or an EMA rule supplies the codebook update, and the commitment loss supplies an additional encoder update. A system that routes gradients differently is a different estimator and must say so.

    EMA codebook updates are online clustering¤

    The codebook-loss gradient pulls a selected vector toward the mean of its assigned encoder outputs. An exponential-moving-average implementation tracks the same sufficient statistics. Let \(n_k^{(t)}\) and \(s_k^{(t)}\) be the current minibatch count and sum for code \(k\):

    \[ n_k^{(t)}=\sum_j\mathbf 1\{c_j=k\}, \qquad s_k^{(t)}=\sum_{j:c_j=k}A_j. \]

    With decay \(\gamma\in[0,1)\), update

    \[ \begin{aligned} N_k^{(t)}&=\gamma N_k^{(t-1)}+(1-\gamma)n_k^{(t)},\\ M_k^{(t)}&=\gamma M_k^{(t-1)}+(1-\gamma)s_k^{(t)},\\ e_k^{(t)}&=\frac{M_k^{(t)}}{N_k^{(t)}}. \end{aligned} \]

    Implementations often smooth the denominator, bias-correct early iterates, or use the opposite convention for the number called “decay.” State the exact recurrence. In distributed training, counts and sums must be aggregated across replicas before every replica applies the same update.

    Worked two-code example. Start with scalar codes \(e_1=0\) and \(e_2=4\), statistics \((N_1,M_1)=(2,0)\) and \((N_2,M_2)=(1,4)\), and decay \(\gamma=1/2\). A minibatch produces encoder values \(0.5,1.5,5.0\). Nearest-code assignment sends the first two values to code 1 and the last to code 2, so

    Table VAE.8. EMA codebook updates are online clustering
    Code \(n_k\) \(s_k\) New \(N_k\) New \(M_k\) New \(e_k\)
    1 \(2\) \(2\) \(2\) \(1\) \(0.5\)
    2 \(1\) \(5\) \(1\) \(4.5\) \(4.5\)

    The update retains history: code 1 does not jump to the current batch mean \(1.0\). If a code receives no assignment, both its count and sum decay; their ratio does not move toward new data. EMA alone therefore does not revive a dead code. Reinitialization, split/merge rules, usage regularization, changed geometry, or a different quantizer must be evaluated as separate interventions.

    # Pseudocode: synchronized EMA update
assignment = nearest_code(encoder_vectors, codebook)
batch_count = count_by_code(assignment)
batch_sum = sum_vectors_by_code(encoder_vectors, assignment)
batch_count = all_reduce_sum(batch_count)
batch_sum = all_reduce_sum(batch_sum)
running_count = decay * running_count + (1 - decay) * batch_count
running_sum = decay * running_sum + (1 - decay) * batch_sum
codebook = running_sum / smoothed(running_count)

    The general discrete-gradient problem¤

    VQ uses deterministic nearest-neighbour assignments, but a VAE may instead use a stochastic categorical posterior. Let

    \[ J(\phi)=\mathbb E_{C\sim q_\phi}[F_\phi(C)] =\sum_{c}q_\phi(c)F_\phi(c). \]

    Whenever differentiation and summation may be interchanged,

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E_q\!\left[ \nabla_\phi F_\phi(C) +F_\phi(C)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(C) \right]. \]

    This identity exposes the choices rather than claiming that discrete variables cannot be optimized with gradients.

    Exact finite example. Let three categories have \(q=(0.2,0.5,0.3)\) and losses \(F=(4,1,2)\). Then \(J=1.9\). If \(a\) are the categorical logits, softmax differentiation gives

    \[ \frac{\partial J}{\partial a_k}=q_k(F_k-J), \qquad \nabla_aJ=(0.42,-0.45,0.03). \]

    These exact values are a useful unit-test oracle. Enumeration requires three decoder evaluations here, but a joint state of \(L\) unrestricted categorical variables has \(K^L\) configurations. Dynamic programming or local enumeration can still be exact when the factorization permits it.

    For sampled hard categories, the score-function estimator uses

    \[ \widehat g_{\mathrm{SF}} =\nabla_\phi F_\phi(C) +\bigl(F_\phi(C)-b\bigr)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(C), \qquad C\sim q_\phi. \]

    A baseline \(b\) that does not depend on the sampled category, conditional on the information already observed, leaves the expectation unchanged and can reduce variance. Learned baselines, leave-one-out baselines, Rao–Blackwellization, antithetic coupling, and relaxation-based control variates differ in cost and assumptions. NVIL, VIMCO, REBAR, and RELAX are representative constructions. “Unbiased” describes the named gradient target under its conditions; it does not mean low variance or low finite-compute error.

    Gumbel–Softmax and hard straight-through samples¤

    For categorical probabilities \(\pi_1,\ldots,\pi_K\), draw independent \(U_k\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and set \(G_k=-\log[-\log U_k]\). The Gumbel-max rule

    \[ H=\operatorname{onehot}\!\left( \arg\max_k\{\log\pi_k+G_k\} \right) \]

    is an exact categorical sample. The hard argmax remains nondifferentiable. Gumbel–Softmax and the Concrete distribution replace it by a simplex-valued sample

    \[ Y_{\tau,k} =\frac{\exp[(\log\pi_k+G_k)/\tau]} {\sum_{r=1}^{K}\exp[(\log\pi_r+G_r)/\tau]}, \qquad \tau>0. \]

    The decoder may consume the mixture \(\sum_kY_{\tau,k}e_k\), which permits a pathwise gradient. At finite temperature, however, the decoder is trained on interior simplex points rather than categorical vertices. It therefore optimizes a relaxed objective, not the original hard-discrete expectation. As \(\tau\) decreases, samples concentrate near vertices, while derivatives carry a \(1/\tau\) scale and saturate away from close competitions. Low temperature is not a free route to an unbiased, low-variance gradient.

    A hard-forward, soft-backward construction uses

    \[ Y_{\mathrm{ST}} =\operatorname{sg}(H-Y_\tau)+Y_\tau. \]

    The forward value is \(H\) and the backward Jacobian is that of \(Y_\tau\). This removes the decoder's soft-input mismatch in the forward pass but does not make the gradient unbiased. Likewise, copying an identity derivative through a hard binary threshold is only a surrogate: if \(C\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(\sigma(a))\) and \(F(C)=C\), the true derivative is \(\sigma(a)[1-\sigma(a)]\), whereas an identity straight-through rule returns \(1\). A straight-through estimator can work well without being the derivative of the declared discrete objective.

    Estimator comparisons must match the objective, number of function evaluations, batch size, random-number coupling, wall-clock cost, and whether model and inference parameters use the same estimator. Lower training loss under a relaxed objective is not evidence of a better hard-discrete likelihood.

    Learning the code prior changes the model¤

    After fitting a codec, encode the training set into index sequences \(c(x)\) and fit a prior by minimizing

    \[ \mathcal J_{\mathrm{prior}}(\psi) =-\mathbb E_{x\sim\mathcal D}\log p_\psi(c(x)). \]

    The resulting probabilistic generator is

    \[ p_{\psi,\theta}(x) =\sum_c p_\psi(c)\,p_\theta(x\mid e_c), \]

    when the decoder defines a normalized conditional distribution. With a deterministic renderer it is instead a pushforward sampler unless an observation model is added. An autoregressive, masked, diffusion, flow, or other categorical model can serve as \(p_\psi\); the family name does not follow from the codec.

    Training this prior after freezing the codec does not retroactively turn the first-stage reconstruction-plus-auxiliary loss into the joint ELBO for the learned prior. Joint fine-tuning is possible, but the prior loss reaches the encoder only through an explicitly declared estimator, and a moving tokenizer changes the prior's target data. Checkpoint compatibility therefore includes encoder, codebook, index ordering, latent shape, preprocessing, and decoder.

    For empirical code probabilities \(\widehat p_k=n_k/\sum_r n_r\), code perplexity is

    \[ \operatorname{PPL}_{\mathrm{code}} =\exp\!\left(-\sum_{k:\widehat p_k>0} \widehat p_k\log\widehat p_k\right). \]

    It lies between \(1\) and \(K\), but high perplexity alone is not quality: an encoder can use every code for noise, and a prior can still model the resulting sequence poorly. Conversely, a low perplexity can reflect genuinely low source entropy or harmful collapse. Compare it with reconstruction, conditional information, prior NLL, and interventions.

    Alternatives and current research boundaries¤

    • Finite Scalar Quantization (Peer-reviewed research) constructs an implicit product codebook from fixed scalar levels and removes learned nearest-neighbour code vectors. Its reported robustness does not imply that every factorized code geometry is suitable for every source or downstream prior.
    • ReinMax and Beyond ReinMax (Peer-reviewed research, with the latter accepted by TMLR in 2026) study increasingly accurate or variance-controlled straight-through-family estimators for stochastic discrete latents. Their benchmark results do not make one estimator uniformly best across support size, hierarchy, and compute.
    • VQ-Transplant (ICLR 2026) and IVQ (ICML 2026) are peer-reviewed research on efficient module replacement and structured codebooks, respectively. They address codebook construction and adaptation, not the general discrete-gradient problem.
    • Diffusion-based categorical reparameterization (Gourevitch et al., 2026) is a workshop paper. It belongs in the evidence map as a developing relaxation, not as an established replacement for enumeration, score-function, or Gumbel-based estimators.

    Applications¤

    • DALL·E 1 (Ramesh et al., 2021) uses a transformer prior over discrete image codes. Later DALL·E systems should not be inferred to use the same model from the shared product name.
    • Jukebox (Dhariwal et al., 2020) uses hierarchical VQ representations for raw audio and separate priors over their code streams.
    • VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) combines quantization with perceptual and adversarial codec losses and a transformer prior. This is a hybrid objective, not evidence that quantization itself makes reconstructions sharp.
    • Neural audio codecs use residual or grouped codebooks whose order, bandwidth, and delayed generation patterns become part of the downstream prior's factorization.

    Training Dynamics and Common Challenges¤

    Posterior Collapse¤

    What is it?

    The encoder learns to ignore the input, producing latent codes that are essentially identical to the prior \(q_\phi(z|x) \approx p(z)\). The decoder learns to generate data without using latent information, defeating the purpose of the model.

    Candidate diagnostics:

    • per-group KL and active-unit profiles across data rather than only a total scalar
    • decoder sensitivity to replacing \(z\) with a prior draw or posterior mean
    • estimates of \(I_q(X;Z)\) with estimator and bias limitations stated
    • reconstruction and conditional-generation changes under latent interventions

    Why does it happen?

    Powerful autoregressive decoders (especially in text VAEs) can model \(p(x)\) without needing latent information. The KL term drives encodings toward the prior, and if the decoder doesn't need \(z\), the KL term wins.

    Interventions to test with controlled ablations: no intervention has a universal effectiveness ranking. Its effect depends on the decoder, data, likelihood, latent grouping, optimiser, and evaluation target.

    1. KL Annealing: Start with \(\beta=0\) and ramp toward the intended objective so the encoder can become informative before receiving the full KL pressure.
    2. Linear (Bowman et al., 2015): beta = min(1.0, step / warmup_steps). The warmup fraction is a protocol choice, not a transferable constant.
    3. Cyclical (Fu et al., 2019): repeat the 0→1 ramp. It improved the evaluated NLP settings in the original paper, but it changes the optimisation path and is not guaranteed to dominate a well-tuned monotonic schedule.

    4. Sigmoid / monotonic schedules are also common. Report the complete schedule and compare against training the target objective without annealing.

    5. Free bits (Kingma et al., 2016): make the objective insensitive to reducing a latent group's average KL below a chosen budget \(\lambda_g\):

      \[ \widetilde{\mathcal{L}}_{\mathrm{KL}} =\sum_g\max\!\left( \lambda_g, \mathbb E_{x\sim\mathcal D} D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(q_\phi(z_g\mid x)\|p(z_g)\right) \right). \]

      A minibatch average usually estimates the data expectation. The floor removes the objective's incentive to shrink an already-small group KL; it does not force KL upward, guarantee \(\lambda_g\) nats of mutual information \(I_q(X;Z_g)\), or guarantee that each coordinate is active. Report the unclamped KL and an information/active-unit diagnostic separately.

    6. δ-VAE (Razavi et al., 2019): replace the standard normal prior with a constrained family (e.g., AR-1 latent dynamics) that has a strict lower bound on KL, eliminating the collapsed solution from the optimisation landscape entirely.

    7. \(\beta\)-VAE with \(\beta<1\): Reducing KL pressure can increase latent usage, but changes the rate–distortion operating point and no longer optimises the ordinary ELBO. Tune \(\beta\) against a declared downstream objective; there is no modality-independent recommended range.

    8. Word dropout (for text): Randomly replace some decoder inputs so the decoder cannot rely exclusively on autoregressive shortcuts. The corruption rate changes the conditional model and must be tuned and reported rather than copied as a universal percentage.

    9. Changing decoder information access: reduce receptive field/capacity, add latent connections, or corrupt teacher-forced inputs. This can force latent use while worsening the observation model, so compare likelihood and downstream utility rather than calling weaker automatically healthier.

    Blurry Reconstructions¤

    Why it happens:

    With fixed-variance Gaussian likelihood and a deterministic mean decoder, squared-error risk is minimized by the conditional mean. When \(z\) leaves unresolved multimodal detail, that mean can average incompatible outcomes. Blur is therefore linked to the observation model, information bottleneck, architecture, and data—not to “VAEs” as an unavoidable family property.

    Solutions:

    1. Perceptual Loss (LPIPS — Zhang et al., 2018): Replace pixel-wise MSE with VGG/AlexNet feature matching.
    2. Significantly improves sharpness while maintaining structure.

    3. Used in DFC-VAE (Hou et al., 2017) and inside the SD-VAE training recipe.

    4. Adversarial Training: Add a discriminator to penalise unrealistic outputs.

    5. VAE-GAN (Larsen et al., 2016) is the canonical recipe.
    6. The Stable Diffusion VAE adds a PatchGAN (Isola et al., 2017) discriminator on top of LPIPS + L1.

    7. Combines reconstruction, KL, and adversarial losses.

    8. Multi-scale SSIM (Wang et al., 2003): Structural-similarity loss across multiple resolutions, better correlated with perceptual quality than MSE.

    9. VQ-VAE / VQGAN (van den Oord et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021): Discrete latents and adversarial training together produce sharper outputs.

    10. Learned Variance: Let the decoder predict per-pixel variance \(\sigma^2_\theta(z)\) instead of using a fixed σ² — recovers a proper Gaussian likelihood and lets the model express its own uncertainty.

    Optimization Challenges¤

    NaN losses:

    • Match the decoder output parameterisation to the observation model: a Bernoulli likelihood uses logits; a bounded continuous or discretised likelihood needs its own valid parameterisation. Pixel scaling alone does not imply a sigmoid decoder.
    • If clipping is justified, clip the norm of the complete gradient PyTree with optax.clip_by_global_norm; coordinate-wise jnp.clip changes gradient direction and is a different intervention.
    • Parameterize either unconstrained log_var directly (with optional clipping) or a positive scale, sigma = nnx.softplus(sigma_raw) + 1e-6, followed by log_var = 2 * jnp.log(sigma). Do not apply softplus to log_var itself: that unnecessarily forces the variance above one.
    • Reduce learning rate if gradients explode

    Loss not decreasing:

    • Verify loss signs: minimize negative ELBO
    • Check that data range/support matches the declared observation distribution and decoder parameterization; there is no universal [0,1] or [-1,1] rule
    • Ensure encoder-decoder dimension matching
    • Monitor gradient norms and non-finite values relative to model size, optimiser, precision, and clipping rule. No universal healthy raw-norm interval transfers across architectures.

    Imbalanced loss terms:

    • Reconstruction loss sums over many pixels; KL sums over few latent dimensions
    • Choose and report a consistent reduction and objective weighting; changing from sums to means or introducing \(\beta\) changes the rate–distortion target

    Worked diagnosis: collapse or legitimate zero rate?¤

    Suppose a conditional VAE predicts \(x\) from a condition \(y\) that already determines \(x\) almost exactly. The decoder can achieve high conditional likelihood without \(z\), and the optimum may have \(q(z\mid x,y)=p(z\mid y)\) with zero conditional rate. Calling this “posterior collapse” describes latent non-use but does not prove optimization failure. If the goal is diverse residual uncertainty given \(y\), evaluate multiple valid \(x\) per condition, conditional coverage, and sensitivity to \(z\). If the data contain no residual variation, forcing positive KL only encodes nuisance information.

    Independent practice and assessment map: variants¤

    Treat each named variant as a change to a probability object, objective, estimator, or optimization route. A family name without that decomposition is not a complete answer.

    P1 — Design and falsify a missing-modality discrete-latent VAE¤

    Consider two scalar sensors with prior \(z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and likelihoods \(x_1\mid z\sim\mathcal N(z,1)\) and \(x_2\mid z\sim\mathcal N(z,4)\). A second design quantizes an encoder output into a four-entry codebook and learns an autoregressive prior over code indices.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict which sensor dominates the joint Gaussian posterior, whether exact aggregate matching \(q(z)=p(z)\) fixes \(I(X;Z)\), and whether replacing the Gaussian latent by four codes eliminates latent underuse. Give a counterexample or missing condition for each claim.
    2. P1.2 — Objective taxonomy. For \(\beta\)-VAE, \(\beta\)-TCVAE, FactorVAE, InfoVAE/MMD-VAE, AAE, CVAE, and VQ-VAE, identify the changed term, probability object, estimator, and intended diagnostic. Mark which objectives remain the ordinary ELBO and which semantic claims require extra assumptions.
    3. P1.3 — Product-of-experts calculation. For observations \((x_1,x_2)=(1,2)\), calculate the exact Gaussian posterior precision, mean, and variance for each singleton subset and for both sensors. Derive the prior-correction formula when multiplying complete unimodal posteriors and show numerically what goes wrong if the prior is counted twice.
    4. P1.4 — Semi-supervised bounds. Write separate labeled and unlabeled objectives for a model \(p(y)p(z)p_\theta(x\mid y,z)\) with \(q_\phi(y\mid x)q_\phi(z\mid x,y)\). Identify where entropy/classification terms enter and why treating an unobserved label as a zero-valued observed label changes the model.
    5. P1.5 — Discrete-update audit. For one encoder vector and four code entries, write nearest-code assignment, straight-through reconstruction, codebook, and commitment updates. Route gradients to encoder, decoder, and selected embedding; then design utilization, perplexity, dead-code, and learned-prior tests that distinguish codec failure from prior failure.
    6. P1.6 — Collapse and intervention study. Construct matched ablations for KL annealing, free bits, weaker decoder information access, \(\beta<1\), and a changed posterior family. Report unclamped rate, conditional coverage, prior samples, reconstructions, active units, and held-out bounds, and state which observation would support a legitimate zero-rate optimum.
    7. P1.7 — Transfer. Move the two-sensor design to paired protein sequence and structure observations with informative missingness. Specify the joint versus conditional query, subset-training mask distribution, equivariance requirement, missingness sensitivity analysis, and evidence needed before claiming cross-modal robustness.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    VAE variants change a specific probabilistic or optimization component: objective weights, aggregate-dependence penalties, conditioning, subset inference, label observability, or the latent alphabet. Aggregate-prior matching does not determine per-record information; InfoVAE coefficients, MMD kernels, and adversarial games state different estimator and objective contracts. A conditional model and a joint multimodal model answer different queries. Product aggregation is Bayesian only under its declared message and independence assumptions, while missing-modality robustness depends on the training-mask distribution. None of these variants supplies semantic factors or sharp output by name. Collapse is goal-relative, codebooks have their own utilization failures, and every intervention changes a rate, support, estimator, or decoder assumption that must be reported. For discrete latents, exact marginalization, score-function gradients, continuous relaxations, and straight-through rules target different quantities; codebook optimization and prior learning add further, separate contracts.

    Retrieval practice: VAE variants and failures
    1. Which three terms appear in the aggregate KL decomposition used by β-TCVAE?
    2. Does VQ eliminate every form of latent collapse?
    3. Why can zero KL be optimal in a well-specified conditional model?
    4. What does a larger \(\beta\) directly penalize?
    5. Why is \(p(z)\prod_m q_m(z\mid x^{(m)})\) generally wrong when each \(q_m\) is already a complete unimodal posterior under \(p(z)\)?
    6. In the worked Gaussian model, why does modality 1 pull the joint posterior more strongly than modality 2?
    7. What changes about \(Y\) between a labeled and an unlabeled M2 record?
    8. Does random modality dropout establish robustness to informative sensor failure?
    9. Which VQ loss routes update the encoder, decoder, and selected codebook entry under the stated straight-through construction?
    10. Why does an EMA update fail to revive a code that never receives assignments?
    11. Does taking Gumbel–Softmax temperature toward zero guarantee an unbiased, low-variance gradient for the hard categorical objective?
    12. Why must codec reconstruction and the learned code prior be evaluated separately?
    13. Why can exact aggregate-prior matching coexist with either one bit or zero bits of information about an input?

    Solutions. (1) Mutual information, aggregate total correlation, and dimension-wise prior mismatch. (2) No; codebook underuse and decoder insensitivity remain possible. (3) The condition or decoder may already explain all predictable variation, leaving no useful residual information for \(z\). (4) Expected posterior-to-prior KL/rate, not semantic entanglement directly. (5) Each unimodal posterior already contains one copy of the prior, so their product needs the correction \(p(z)^{1-|S|}\); multiplying by another prior double-counts it. (6) Its observation noise variance is smaller, so its likelihood message contributes precision \(1\) rather than \(1/4\). (7) \(Y\) is observed and conditioned on in the labeled bound, but latent and marginalized through \(q(y\mid x)\) in the unlabeled bound. (8) No; it supports only the simulated mask distribution. Informative missingness can depend on unseen values or causes and may require an explicit mechanism or sensitivity analysis. (9) Reconstruction updates the decoder and is copied to the encoder; commitment updates the encoder; the codebook loss or EMA updates selected embeddings. (10) Its moving count and sum decay together, so their ratio receives no information about new regions; an explicit reset or changed assignment mechanism is required. (11) No. The relaxed sample approaches a vertex, but derivatives become ill-conditioned and the finite-temperature objective differs from the hard objective. (12) A codec can discard information even if the prior is perfect, while a strong codec can be paired with a poor prior; only separate measurements localize the failure.

    Solution 13. Aggregate matching fixes only the marginal law of \(Z\); it does not fix the conditional coupling between \(X\) and \(Z\), which determines mutual information.
