    4. 11. Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods

    Sequential Monte Carlo and Particle Methods¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    • Latent Variables and Inference
    • Filtering and Smoothing for State-Space Generators
    • Sampling and Continuous-Time Generative Mathematics

    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) approximates a sequence of related target laws with a population of weighted samples. Mutation moves particles into new states, weighting corrects the proposal, and resampling reallocates finite computation toward particles with large current weight. This common mechanism supports online filtering, path smoothing, static Bayesian inference, normalizing-constant estimation, rare-event simulation, approximate Bayesian computation, and conditional generation. The target sequence and returned query—not the word particle—determine which algorithm has been specified.

    This volume is the canonical family-independent treatment of particle algorithms. Filtering and Smoothing owns the state-space queries, Sampling and Continuous-Time Mathematics owns invariant mutation kernels and Monte Carlo uncertainty, and Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants owns comparisons among evidence estimators. Family volumes retain their specialized applications and point here for the reusable SMC construction.

    Reading routes

    • Entry after prerequisites: read Chapters 1–3, reproduce the two-state filter, and draw every ancestor arrow by hand.
    • Practitioner: continue through Chapters 4–6, implement at least two resampling schemes, and use the reporting ledger before trusting a result.
    • Research: read continuously, derive the path-space weights and normalizer identity, compare particle and genealogical diagnostics under matched compute, and trace each research method to its primary source.

    Reader orientation¤

    Chapter map¤

    The complete volume is one continuous argument. Six exact projections provide optional stopping points:

    1. Sequential Targets and Feynman–Kac Semantics — targets, paths, proposals, potentials, weighted empirical laws, and returned objects;
    2. Particle Filtering, Resampling, and Degeneracy — SIS, bootstrap filtering, normalizer estimation, resampling schemes, ESS, and ancestry;
    3. Proposal Design and Auxiliary Particles — observation-aware proposals, fully adapted limits, auxiliary filters, and support failures;
    4. Smoothing, Rao–Blackwellization, and Particle MCMC — path recovery, conditional exact submodels, resample–move, PMCMC, SMC², and nested particles;
    5. Static Targets, Tempering, Rare Events, and ABC — general SMC samplers, backward kernels, adaptive paths, rare-event sequences, and simulator-based targets; and
    6. Learning, Twisting, Evaluation, and Research — differentiable resampling, learned proposals and targets, generative-model control, validation, and bounded research directions.

    Notation and execution contract¤

    At stage \(t\), let \(x_{0:t}\) denote a path, although the state can instead be a fixed-dimensional parameter. An unnormalized target is \(\gamma_t\) with normalizer \(Z_t\) and normalized law \(\pi_t=\gamma_t/Z_t\). The particle count is \(N\). Unnormalized and normalized weights are \(w_t^i\) and \(W_t^i=w_t^i/\sum_jw_t^j\). Ancestor index \(A_t^i\) identifies the parent of particle \(i\) at stage \(t\). A proposal kernel is \(M_t\) and an optional backward kernel is \(L_{t-1}\). All densities are relative to declared base measures.

    Unless a subsection says otherwise, the execution order is:

    1. resample ancestors using the previous normalized weights when the declared trigger fires;
    2. mutate each selected ancestor with \(M_t\);
    3. compute the new incremental and cumulative weights; and
    4. normalize for the current empirical approximation.

    Other valid conventions resample after weighting. Formulae must follow the chosen convention; code that silently combines one convention's indices with another convention's weights is not a harmless reordering.

    The diagram describes a computational skeleton, not a guarantee. Validity still depends on support, proper weighting, integrability, resampling, mutation, and the query extracted from the finite particle system.

    1. Sequential Targets and Feynman–Kac Semantics¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Importance sampling, conditional probability, Markov kernels, and normalized versus unnormalized densities.

    Learning objectives. Specify an SMC algorithm through its target sequence, proposal, potential, resampling rule, and returned query; derive the generic importance weight; distinguish path-space and marginal targets; and explain why a weighted cloud is an approximation rather than a new model family.

    Begin with the sequence of laws¤

    Ordinary importance sampling corrects one proposal toward one target. SMC inserts intermediate targets so that no single correction must bridge the entire discrepancy. There are two common geometries.

    In a growing-space construction, stage \(t\) targets a longer object:

    \[ \pi_t(x_{0:t})=\frac{\gamma_t(x_{0:t})}{Z_t}. \]

    A state-space posterior \(p(x_{0:t}\mid y_{1:t})\) is the canonical example. In a fixed-space construction, every target lives on the same state space:

    \[ \pi_t(x)=\frac{\gamma_t(x)}{Z_t}, \qquad t=0,\ldots,T. \]

    Tempering from a prior to a posterior is a canonical example. These two cases share weighted-population machinery but not necessarily the same weight, mutation, memory, or returned path.

    The Feynman–Kac construction¤

    A useful general representation starts with an initial law \(M_0(dx_0)\), forward kernels \(M_t(x_{t-1},dx_t)\), and nonnegative potentials \(G_t(x_{0:t})\). They recursively define an unnormalized path measure

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Gamma_0(dx_0)&=M_0(dx_0)G_0(x_0),\\ \Gamma_t(dx_{0:t}) &=\Gamma_{t-1}(dx_{0:t-1})\\ &\quad\times M_t(x_{t-1},dx_t)G_t(x_{0:t}). \end{aligned} \]

    Repeated substitution yields the corresponding product over stages. Its mass \(Z_t=\Gamma_t(1)\) and normalized law \(\Pi_t=\Gamma_t/Z_t\) determine the mathematical target. The potential may be an observation likelihood, an annealing increment, a constraint-survival factor, or a ratio induced by a backward kernel. It is not automatically a physical energy or a learned reward.

    This representation separates three layers:

    Table SMC.1. The Feynman–Kac construction
    Layer Object Question
    model or target \(\gamma_t\), \(\pi_t\), or \(\Pi_t\) Which law or path measure is intended?
    particle algorithm proposals, potentials, resampling, mutation How is finite computation allocated?
    estimator weighted average, normalizer product, path draw, gradient Which query is returned, and with what error contract?

    Generic growing-space weight¤

    Suppose a proposal factorizes as

    \[ q_t(x_{0:t})=q_0(x_0)\prod_{s=1}^{t} q_s(x_s\mid x_{0:s-1}). \]

    Before resampling, ordinary importance sampling gives

    \[ w_t(x_{0:t})=\frac{\gamma_t(x_{0:t})}{q_t(x_{0:t})}. \]

    The recursive form is

    \[ \begin{aligned} w_t(x_{0:t}) &=w_{t-1}(x_{0:t-1})\alpha_t(x_{0:t}),\\ \alpha_t(x_{0:t}) &=\frac{\gamma_t(x_{0:t})}{\gamma_{t-1}(x_{0:t-1})} \frac{1}{q_t(x_t\mid x_{0:t-1})}. \end{aligned} \]

    The incremental weight \(\alpha_t\) is the exact correction associated with the declared targets and proposal. After resampling at \(t-1\), the inherited weights are equal and only \(\alpha_t\) need be stored. Without resampling, cumulative weights retain all previous increments.

    Weighted empirical laws and their limits¤

    For an integrable function \(f\), the normalized particle estimate is

    \[ \widehat\pi_t^N(f)=\sum_{i=1}^{N}W_t^i f(X_t^i). \]

    Under the construction's support, moment, and regularity conditions, this converges to \(\pi_t(f)\) as \(N\to\infty\). At finite \(N\), self-normalization is generally biased even when the unnormalized numerator and denominator have useful unbiasedness properties. Consistency is an asymptotic statement; it does not say that a particular particle cloud found every important region.

    For a path-space target, the same formula with \(f(X_{0:t}^i)\) estimates a path functional. A good current-state marginal does not imply a good old-state or whole-path approximation because resampling changes genealogy.

    Worked finite target sequence¤

    Let the state space be \(\{a,b,c\}\). Take a uniform initial law and two unnormalized targets

    \[ \gamma_1=(1,2,1), \qquad \gamma_2=(1,6,3). \]

    Their normalizers are \(Z_1=4\) and \(Z_2=10\). If particles at stage 1 are exactly distributed as \(\pi_1=(1/4,1/2,1/4)\) and mutation is the identity, the stage-2 incremental weights are

    \[ G_2=\frac{\gamma_2}{\gamma_1}=(1,3,3). \]

    Reweighting gives masses proportional to

    \[ \begin{aligned} &(1/4,1/2,1/4)\odot(1,3,3)\\ &\qquad=(1/4,3/2,3/4), \end{aligned} \]

    which normalize to \((1/10,6/10,3/10)=\pi_2\). The average incremental factor under \(\pi_1\) is \(10/4=Z_2/Z_1\). With finitely sampled particles, the identity holds in expectation under the proper construction, not exactly for every realized cloud.

    Five different returned objects¤

    An SMC run can return any of the following, and a report must name which one:

    1. a weighted approximation to the current marginal;
    2. a weighted approximation to the full path law;
    3. one or more trajectories selected from the particle genealogy;
    4. an estimator of \(Z_t\) or \(Z_t/Z_0\); or
    5. a stochastic objective or gradient used to learn model, proposal, or twist parameters.

    The guarantees do not transfer automatically. In particular, an unbiased normalizing-constant estimator does not make its logarithm unbiased, does not make a normalized posterior expectation unbiased, and does not make a gradient formed after stopping resampling derivatives exact.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    SMC is importance sampling over a designed sequence of targets. Feynman–Kac notation exposes the path law, mutation kernels, potentials, and normalizer. The finite particle system approximates a declared query; it does not redefine the target. Chapter 2 specializes the construction to filtering and shows why resampling manages current weights while damaging ancestral diversity.

    Chapter 1 retrieval practice¤

    1. What distinguishes growing-space from fixed-space SMC?
    2. Why is a potential not automatically an observation likelihood?
    3. Which finite-particle estimate is generally biased: the standard normalizer estimate or a normalized particle expectation?
    4. Why can the current marginal be adequate while a path functional is poor?
    Complete solutions
    1. A growing-space target adds variables or decisions with stage, whereas fixed-space SMC changes the target law on one common state space.
    2. A potential is any nonnegative factor in the declared Feynman–Kac path measure; its semantics depend on the construction.
    3. Under proper standard constructions the normalizer estimate can be unbiased, while the self-normalized expectation is generally finite-\(N\) biased.
    4. Resampling reallocates present particles but makes ancestral lineages coalesce, so old path diversity can vanish.

    Continue continuously, or use the Chapter 1 stopping-point view.

    2. Particle Filtering, Resampling, and Degeneracy¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapter 1, state-space filtering, importance weights, and conditional independence.

    Learning objectives. Derive sequential importance sampling and the bootstrap particle filter; compute the likelihood estimator; implement and compare resampling schemes; distinguish current-weight, particle, and genealogical degeneracy; and use ESS as a trigger rather than an accuracy certificate.

    From exact Bayes recursion to particles¤

    For the state-space law

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(x_{0:T},y_{1:T}) &=p(x_0)\prod_{t=1}^{T}f_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1})\\ &\qquad\times g_t(y_t\mid x_t), \end{aligned} \]

    the filtering target is \(p(x_t\mid y_{1:t})\) and the growing path target is \(p(x_{0:t}\mid y_{1:t})\). A proposal \(q_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1},y_{1:t})\) produces the incremental weight

    \[ \alpha_t^i= \frac{g_t(y_t\mid X_t^i)f_t(X_t^i\mid X_{t-1}^{A_t^i})} {q_t(X_t^i\mid X_{t-1}^{A_t^i},y_{1:t})}. \]

    The bootstrap filter chooses the transition prior as proposal, \(q_t=f_t\), leaving \(\alpha_t^i=g_t(y_t\mid X_t^i)\). This simplicity can be expensive: particles are propagated without seeing the current observation and may all land where its likelihood is negligible.

    Sequential importance sampling and resampling¤

    Without resampling, cumulative weights multiply every incremental likelihood ratio. Their dispersion typically grows with time. Sequential importance resampling instead selects parents according to \(W_{t-1}\) and resets inherited weights before mutation. Resampling removes particles with small current weights and duplicates particles with large current weights. It does not create states outside the current support and does not recover a mode that no particle reached.

    The last population has four particles but only two stage-1 parents and two stage-0 lineages. Current sample count and ancestral diversity are therefore different quantities.

    Normalizing-constant estimate¤

    Under resampling at every stage and the convention used here, define

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat Z_t &=\widehat Z_{t-1} \left(\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^{N}\alpha_t^i\right),\\ \widehat Z_0&=Z_0. \end{aligned} \]

    For a bootstrap filter with normalized initial law, this is the product of average observation weights and estimates \(p(y_{1:t})\). Under standard proper weighting and unbiased resampling conditions, \(\widehat Z_t\) is unbiased for \(Z_t\) even though \(\log\widehat Z_t\) is downward biased by Jensen's inequality unless the estimator has zero variance. Adaptive resampling can preserve the normalizer result when the stopping rule and weight bookkeeping satisfy the corresponding SMC construction; it should not be assumed for arbitrary code.

    Resampling is an offspring distribution¤

    Given normalized weights \(W^{1:N}\), let \(N_i\) be the number of children of particle \(i\). A valid unbiased resampling rule satisfies

    \[ \begin{aligned} \sum_iN_i&=N,\\ \mathbb E[N_i\mid W^{1:N}]&=NW^i. \end{aligned} \]

    Common schemes include:

    Table SMC.2. Resampling is an offspring distribution
    Scheme Randomness Useful property Qualification
    multinomial \(N\) independent categorical draws simple and permutation-insensitive largest conditional offspring variance among the basic comparisons
    residual deterministically assigns \(\lfloor NW^i\rfloor\), samples the remainder reduces avoidable offspring randomness still duplicates lineages and requires a rule for the residual part
    stratified one uniform draw per equal CDF stratum lower conditional variance than multinomial for standard estimates particle ordering can affect particular realized couplings
    systematic one random offset for an evenly spaced grid fast, one uniform draw, often effective dependent offspring; no universal variance dominance over multinomial without additional conditions

    The final qualification matters. A simple counterexample rules out the common claim that systematic resampling always has smaller variance for every ordered particle system. Report the particle order or permutation policy when it is algorithmically meaningful.

    Worked offspring calculation¤

    Take \(N=4\) and weights \(W=(0.55,0.25,0.15,0.05)\). Multinomial resampling has

    \[ \mathbb E[N_i]=4W_i=(2.2,1.0,0.6,0.2), \]

    and

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(N_i\mid W)=4W_i(1-W_i). \]

    For the first particle this is \(0.99\). Residual resampling first assigns \((2,1,0,0)\), leaving one draw from residual probabilities \((0.2,0,0.6,0.2)\). Its first-particle offspring variance is therefore \(0.2(0.8)=0.16\), but this smaller one-step variance says nothing by itself about long-horizon genealogy or the quality of mutation after selection.

    Effective sample size: three names, three objects¤

    The importance-weight diagnostic

    \[ \operatorname{ESS}_{\mathrm{IS}} =\frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^{N}(W_i)^2} \]

    ranges from \(1\) to \(N\). It summarizes current weight concentration. It is not:

    • the observable-specific MCMC ESS derived from autocorrelation;
    • conditional ESS used to choose a tempering increment; or
    • a certificate of support coverage, path diversity, or estimator accuracy.

    For the worked weights, \(\operatorname{ESS}_{\mathrm{IS}}\approx2.56\). Resampling makes all immediate weights \(1/4\), so the displayed formula jumps to \(4\) while the number of distinct parents can fall. This is why ESS must be read beside unique-ancestor counts and query-specific error.

    Three degeneracies¤

    1. Weight degeneracy: a small number of particles carry most current weight.
    2. Particle impoverishment: resampling produces duplicates that mutation does not sufficiently separate.
    3. Genealogical or path degeneracy: many current particles descend from few early ancestors.

    Resampling directly manages the first and can worsen the latter two. A claim that particle filters “do not suffer weight degeneracy” confuses a corrective operation with elimination of the failure.

    Worked exact-versus-particle filter¤

    Let \(X_t\in\{0,1\}\), with transition matrix

    \[ F=\begin{pmatrix}0.8&0.2\\0.3&0.7\end{pmatrix}, \]

    and binary observation accuracy \(0.9\): \(p(Y_t=X_t\mid X_t)=0.9\). If \(p(X_0=1)=0.5\) and \(Y_1=1\), exact prediction gives \(p(X_1=1)=0.45\). The two correction masses are

    \[ \begin{aligned} r_1&=0.9(0.45)=0.405,\\ r_0&=0.1(0.55)=0.055. \end{aligned} \]

    Let \(p_1=p(X_1=1\mid Y_1=1)\). Therefore

    \[ p_1=\frac{r_1}{r_1+r_0}\approx0.8804. \]

    A four-particle bootstrap realization that propagates to \((0,1,1,1)\) receives weights \((0.1,0.9,0.9,0.9)\) and estimates \(0.9643\) before resampling. The algorithm is valid, but the small realized cloud is inaccurate. Repeating the run exposes Monte Carlo variation that one cloud cannot.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Particle filtering is a particular growing-space SMC construction. Resampling controls present weight allocation but creates dependence and ancestry loss. The normalizer, normalized expectation, and genealogical outputs have different finite-\(N\) properties. Chapter 3 reduces weight variance by letting proposals use the current observation without abandoning importance correction.

    Chapter 2 retrieval practice¤

    1. Why can ESS equal \(N\) immediately after resampling while path diversity is poor?
    2. What makes the bootstrap proposal easy and sometimes inefficient?
    3. Which resampling variance ordering is unsafe to state universally?
    4. Why must \(\widehat Z\) and \(\log\widehat Z\) be discussed separately?
    Complete solutions
    1. Resampling resets current weights to equality but can duplicate a small set of ancestors; the two diagnostics measure different objects.
    2. It samples from the transition already supplied by the model, so the ratio cancels, but it ignores the current observation before proposing.
    3. Systematic resampling does not universally dominate multinomial resampling for every ordered system and estimator.
    4. An unbiased positive \(\widehat Z\) generally has a downward-biased logarithm by concavity.

    Continue continuously, or use the Chapter 2 stopping-point view.

    3. Proposal Design and Auxiliary Particles¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–2, conditional probability, and support-aware importance sampling.

    Learning objectives. Derive the locally optimal state-space proposal; separate proposal adaptation from target correctness; explain auxiliary look-ahead weights; compare analytic, approximate, and learned proposals; and diagnose support failure before interpreting ESS.

    Proposals allocate effort before correction¤

    The bootstrap filter proposes from \(f_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1})\). When \(y_t\) is informative, a better proposal uses it:

    \[ q_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1},y_t). \]

    The importance ratio preserves the same filtering target as long as proposal support covers every target-relevant state. Proposal design changes variance and compute; it does not change the intended posterior unless the correction is removed or approximated.

    The locally optimal proposal¤

    Conditional on one selected ancestor \(x_{t-1}\) and current observation \(y_t\), the proposal minimizing the conditional variance of the next incremental weight is

    \[ r_t(x_t)=g_t(y_t\mid x_t)f_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1}). \]

    Suppressing the fixed ancestor and observation in the proposal's left-hand side gives

    \[ q_t^\star(x_t)=\frac{r_t(x_t)}{p(y_t\mid x_{t-1})}. \]

    Thus \(q_t^\star=p(x_t\mid x_{t-1},y_t)\).

    Substitution leaves the weight

    \[ \alpha_t^\star(x_{t-1})=p(y_t\mid x_{t-1}), \]

    which is constant across proposed children of the same ancestor but generally varies across ancestors. “Optimal” is therefore local to this conditional variance criterion; it is not a global theorem about path diversity, smoothing, runtime, or parameter learning. It may also require the intractable integral that the filter was meant to avoid.

    Worked binary proposal¤

    Use the binary model from Chapter 2. For an ancestor \(X_0=0\) and observation \(Y_1=1\),

    \[ \begin{aligned} r_0&=0.8(0.1)+0.2(0.9)\\ &=0.08+0.18=0.26, \end{aligned} \]
    \[ \begin{aligned} p_1&=p(X_1=1\mid X_0=0,Y_1=1)\\ &=\frac{0.18}{0.26}\approx0.6923. \end{aligned} \]

    For ancestor \(X_0=1\), the predictive observation probability is \(0.3(0.1)+0.7(0.9)=0.66\), and the proposed probability of \(X_1=1\) is \(0.7(0.9)/0.66\approx0.9545\). The child weight is \(0.26\) or \(0.66\) according to its parent, independent of the sampled child. The current observation has already guided the proposal.

    Approximate observation-aware proposals¤

    When \(q_t^\star\) is unavailable, common approximations include:

    • local linearization or Laplace proposals around an observation-informed mode;
    • extended, sigma-point, or quadrature Gaussian updates;
    • conditional mixtures or normalizing-flow proposals;
    • amortized neural proposals trained across sequences; and
    • inner Monte Carlo or nested SMC approximations.

    Each method must expose the density or proper-weighting correction required by the outer particle algorithm. A proposal can make particles visually plausible while supplying an incorrect likelihood estimate if its density, Jacobian, normalizer, or random inner estimate is mishandled.

    Auxiliary particle filtering¤

    Standard resampling ranks ancestors by their previous weights before seeing how well they predict \(y_t\). An auxiliary particle filter introduces a positive look-ahead score \(m_t^i\), often an approximation to \(p(y_t\mid X_{t-1}^i)\). It first selects ancestors with probabilities proportional to \(W_{t-1}^i m_t^i\), proposes a child, and corrects by

    \[ \alpha_t^i =\frac{g_t(y_t\mid X_t^i)f_t(X_t^i\mid X_{t-1}^{A_t^i})} {m_t^{A_t^i}q_t(X_t^i\mid X_{t-1}^{A_t^i},y_t)}. \]

    The auxiliary score changes allocation, not the target, because it also appears in the denominator. Omitting this correction silently changes the particle law. If \(m_t\) is a poor predictor, the extra look-ahead can increase cost or variance.

    Support cannot be learned away¤

    For every relevant ancestor, a safe proposal condition is

    \[ \begin{aligned} &g_t(y_t\mid x_t)f_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1})>0\\ &\qquad\Longrightarrow q_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1},y_t)>0. \end{aligned} \]

    A neural proposal trained on typical sequences can assign zero or numerically negligible mass to a rare but posterior-relevant transition. Importance weights cannot correct an event that is never proposed. Clipping the log weight may avoid overflow but changes the estimator unless the altered target is stated.

    Proposal comparison protocol¤

    Compare proposals with matched target, particle count, resampling policy, random-number budget, and wall-clock hardware. Report:

    • current weighted ESS before resampling;
    • variance and log-bias of the likelihood estimate across independent runs;
    • unique ancestors by lag and smoothing error;
    • proposal density and evaluation cost;
    • support failures, nonfinite weights, and clipping; and
    • query-specific error against an exact or high-accuracy reference.

    No single ESS curve determines the winner. A costly guided proposal can reduce particles needed per step yet lose at equal wall-clock time; a proposal with excellent current weights can still produce poor old-state trajectories.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    The proposal is the particle algorithm's allocation policy. The exact ratio retains the target while allowing analytic, approximate, or learned guidance. The fully adapted proposal removes child-level weight variation only under a local criterion, and auxiliary filtering adds ancestor look-ahead with an explicit correction. Chapter 4 turns from current filtering to path recovery, conditional exact computation, and combinations with MCMC.

    4. Smoothing, Rao–Blackwellization, and Particle MCMC¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–3, backward smoothing, conditional expectation, and Metropolis–Hastings invariance.

    Learning objectives. Explain why storing ancestral paths is a weak default smoother; derive backward trajectory sampling; apply Rao–Blackwellization to a valid conditional submodel; distinguish resample–move from particle MCMC; and state what PMMH, particle Gibbs, SMC², and nested SMC target.

    Filtering particles are not automatically smoothing particles¤

    Tracing a final particle through stored ancestors produces a draw from the finite particle genealogy. Repeated resampling makes early ancestry coalesce, so this path-space smoother can have little old-state diversity even when the final filter is accurate.

    A forward-filtering backward-sampling particle smoother instead samples \(X_T\) from final weights and then chooses earlier particles with probabilities

    \[ \begin{aligned} &\Pr(B_t=i\mid B_{t+1}=j,\mathcal P)\\ &\qquad\propto W_t^i f_{t+1}(X_{t+1}^j\mid X_t^i), \end{aligned} \]

    where \(\mathcal P\) denotes the stored particle system. Backward simulation reconnects compatible states across the cloud instead of accepting only the recorded parent. It improves path diversity but remains conditional on the finite forward particle approximation.

    Worked backward choice¤

    Suppose two stage-\(t\) particles have weights \((0.6,0.4)\), and their transition densities into the selected next state are \((0.2,0.9)\). The backward probabilities are proportional to \((0.12,0.36)\) and therefore equal \((0.25,0.75)\). The second particle is preferred despite its smaller filtering weight because it better explains the selected future state. Following only the recorded ancestor would ignore this alternative compatible connection.

    Rao–Blackwellized particle filters¤

    Partition the latent state as \((R_t,S_t)\) such that, conditional on the sampled trajectory \(R_{0:t}\), the law of \(S_{0:t}\) admits exact tractable filtering—for example, a linear-Gaussian submodel. Particles represent \(R_{0:t}\), while each particle carries analytic sufficient statistics for \(S_t\). For any query \(h\),

    \[ \begin{aligned} &\mathbb E[h(R,S)\mid y]\\ &\qquad=\mathbb E\!\left[ \mathbb E[h(R,S)\mid R,y]\mid y \right]. \end{aligned} \]

    Replacing sampled conditional variation by the inner expectation cannot increase variance for the same outer samples. This is a conditional Rao–Blackwell statement, not a claim that the sampled variables or finite particle approximation become exact. Switching linear dynamical systems and mapping/localization decompositions are common applications.

    Resample–move rejuvenates the current target¤

    After resampling, apply a Markov kernel \(K_t\) invariant to the current target \(\pi_t\). The mutation can separate duplicates and explore directions not provided by the forward proposal. Its validity follows from invariance; its finite usefulness depends on mixing and cost. A single weak move does not reverse lost genealogy, and a kernel invariant to the wrong marginal cannot repair a path-space query.

    Particle Markov chain Monte Carlo¤

    Particle MCMC uses an SMC system inside an outer MCMC construction. The key result is an extended-space target whose marginal is the desired posterior. The particle approximation is part of the proposal or auxiliary state rather than a replacement posterior.

    Table SMC.3. Particle Markov chain Monte Carlo
    Method SMC role Outer target and correction Main finite-compute risk
    particle marginal MH (PMMH) supplies a nonnegative unbiased likelihood estimate and a path proposal pseudo-marginal MH targets the exact parameter posterior under its assumptions noisy log-likelihood estimates make the chain sticky
    particle Gibbs conditional SMC updates a trajectory while retaining a reference path Gibbs on an extended target has the exact trajectory posterior as a marginal path degeneracy can make successive trajectories nearly identical
    particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling reconnects the retained path through alternative ancestors preserves the conditional-SMC target under its construction gains depend on model, proposal, and particle count

    PMMH does not require an unbiased log likelihood. It requires the nonnegative likelihood estimator itself to be unbiased under the pseudo-marginal construction. Replacing it with \(\exp\) of an unbiased log estimate does not in general satisfy that condition.

    Parameter particles and nested particle systems¤

    SMC² maintains parameter particles and runs a state particle filter within each one so sequential data update parameter uncertainty. Particle rejuvenation uses PMCMC moves. The two particle counts control different approximations and costs.

    Nested SMC places a properly weighted inner SMC algorithm inside an outer SMC step when the proposal or incremental weight is itself intractable. Correct composition requires the inner output to satisfy the proper-weighting identity; calling the inner result “approximately normalized” is insufficient.

    Particle belief propagation¤

    Particle messages can approximate continuous or hybrid messages in factor graphs. This is an implementation composition between message passing and weighted samples. On a loopy graph it inherits both particle error and the fixed-point or convergence limitations of loopy message passing. It is not the same algorithm as time-indexed particle filtering merely because both use weighted samples.

    Diagnostics for smoothing and hybrids¤

    Record current-state error separately from path error. Useful quantities include unique ancestors at each lag, most-recent-common-ancestor time, backward-sampling diversity, fixed-lag bias, likelihood-estimator variance, outer-chain ESS and \(\widehat R\) for PMCMC, and compute split between inner and outer particle systems. Validate on a finite-state or linear-Gaussian model where exact smoothing is available before using only application metrics.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Smoothing asks for a path posterior, not merely a final weighted cloud. Backward simulation, conditional analytic integration, invariant rejuvenation, and particle-MCMC embeddings solve different problems. Their guarantees depend on different extended targets. Chapter 5 uses the same population machinery on fixed-dimensional targets, likelihood levels, and simulator discrepancies.

    5. Static Targets, Tempering, Rare Events, and ABC¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–4, invariant MCMC kernels, annealing paths, normalizing constants, and simulator-based inference.

    Learning objectives. Derive the forward/backward-kernel SMC sampler weight; design likelihood- and data-tempering sequences; distinguish particle ESS from conditional ESS; formulate rare-event and ABC target sequences; and compare SMC with AIS, parallel tempering, nested sampling, and optimization by output.

    Fixed-dimensional SMC samplers¤

    Let \(\pi_t(x)=\gamma_t(x)/Z_t\) be targets on one space. Augment a forward move \(M_t(x_{t-1},x_t)\) with a backward kernel \(L_{t-1}(x_t,x_{t-1})\). The incremental weight is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \alpha_t(x_{t-1},x_t) &=\frac{\gamma_t(x_t)}{\gamma_{t-1}(x_{t-1})}\\ &\quad\times \frac{L_{t-1}(x_t,x_{t-1})}{M_t(x_{t-1},x_t)}. \end{aligned} \]

    This is ordinary importance sampling on an artificial path whose final marginal is \(\pi_t\). The backward kernel is a variance-design object; it need not describe a physical reverse process.

    If \(M_t\) is invariant to \(\pi_t\) and the standard reversal choice is available, the weight can simplify to a ratio evaluated at the pre-move state. The exact form depends on the declared move–weight order. Implement the general identity first and prove any simplification rather than memorizing a formula detached from its convention.

    Likelihood tempering¤

    For prior \(p(x)\) and likelihood \(\ell(x)\), a common path is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \gamma_t(x)&=p(x)\ell(x)^{\beta_t},\\ 0&=\beta_0<\cdots<\beta_T=1. \end{aligned} \]

    Before mutation, the incremental weight for an exponent change is

    \[ u_i(\beta)=\ell(X_{t-1}^i)^{\beta-\beta_{t-1}}. \]

    Large exponent jumps can collapse weights; tiny jumps increase stage count and mutation cost. An adaptive schedule can choose the largest next \(\beta\) whose conditional effective sample size exceeds a threshold. One common normalized form is

    \[ \operatorname{CESS}(\beta) =N\frac{\left(\sum_i W_{t-1}^i u_i(\beta)\right)^2} {\sum_i W_{t-1}^i u_i(\beta)^2}. \]

    This predicts the effect of the proposed incremental reweighting conditional on the current cloud. It is not MCMC ESS and does not prove that mutation mixed.

    Worked adaptive step¤

    With equal current weights and likelihood values \((1,4,16,64)\), a proposed \(\Delta\beta=1/2\) gives incremental weights \((1,2,4,8)\). Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{CESS} &=\frac{(1+2+4+8)^2}{1^2+2^2+4^2+8^2}\\ &=\frac{225}{85}\approx2.65. \end{aligned} \]

    For \(N=4\), a threshold of \(0.8N=3.2\) rejects this jump and chooses a smaller one. After selection, invariant mutation must still explore the new target; the CESS calculation cannot diagnose a multimodal kernel trapped within each particle's local basin.

    Data tempering and sequential Bayes¤

    Instead of changing a power, assimilate observations or factors in batches:

    \[ \gamma_t(x)=p(x)\prod_{s=1}^{t}p(y_s\mid x). \]

    This is static-parameter sequential Bayes. The data order can affect finite variance and compute even though the final exact posterior is order-invariant. For exchangeable data, an order chosen after inspecting particle performance becomes part of the adaptive algorithm and must be reported.

    Rare-event SMC¤

    To estimate \(P(X\in A)\) when \(A\) is rare, construct nested events

    \[ A_0\supset A_1\supset\cdots\supset A_T=A \]

    or smooth potentials approaching \(\mathbf1_A\). Selection retains particles that reach the next level, and mutation explores within or near that level. The probability decomposes as

    \[ P(A)=P(A_1)\prod_{t=2}^{T}P(A_t\mid A_{t-1}). \]

    If a level is so hard that no particle reaches it, the estimate is zero and later resampling cannot recover. Adaptive quantile levels reduce this risk but make the estimator's exact finite-sample properties depend on the full adaptive construction.

    Worked rare-event sequence¤

    Suppose \(P(A_1)=0.1\), \(P(A_2\mid A_1)=0.2\), and \(P(A_3\mid A_2)=0.25\). Then \(P(A_3)=0.005\). Estimating three moderate conditional probabilities can be far more stable than waiting for five events per thousand direct samples, but dependence from selection and mutation must be included in uncertainty. Multiplying three point estimates without replicated runs or particle variance analysis is not a confidence interval.

    SMC for approximate Bayesian computation¤

    When a simulator \(Y\sim p(\cdot\mid\theta)\) is available but its likelihood is not, an ABC target can use a kernel or event based on summary discrepancy:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \pi_{\epsilon}(\theta,y) &\propto p(\theta)p(y\mid\theta)\\ &\quad\times K_{\epsilon}\!\left( \rho(s(y),s(y_{\mathrm{obs}})) \right). \end{aligned} \]

    SMC decreases \(\epsilon\) through a sequence of approximate targets. Particle weights correct parameter proposals; simulated datasets are auxiliary variables. The final result remains conditional on the summary \(s\), discrepancy \(\rho\), kernel, tolerance, simulator, and prior. More particles do not remove summary insufficiency or simulator misspecification.

    Do not collapse neighboring algorithms¤

    Table SMC.4. Do not collapse neighboring algorithms
    Method Population mechanism Returned object
    AIS one or more weighted annealed trajectories, normally without population resampling normalizer ratio and weighted endpoint
    static SMC interacting weighted population with optional resampling and mutation target approximation plus normalizer ratio
    parallel tempering MCMC on a product of simultaneous tempered replicas correlated draws, usually from the cold replica
    nested sampling likelihood-constrained prior-mass progression and quadrature evidence and posterior-weighted points under its construction
    simulated annealing changing-temperature search approximate optimizer, not generally a posterior sample

    Shared schedules create an implementation analogy or hybrid composition, not algebraic equivalence among their finite algorithms.

    Static-target SMC turns a difficult fixed law into a sequence of corrected population moves. Backward kernels define the artificial path correction; tempering and data assimilation define useful target sequences; rare-event and ABC applications define different potentials and approximation boundaries.     6. Learning, Twisting, Evaluation, and Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Chapters 1–5, gradient estimators, variational objectives, optimal transport, conditional generation, and reproducible evaluation.

    Learning objectives. Distinguish objectives for model, proposal, and target learning; explain the resampling-gradient problem; place relaxed, marginalized, and transport resampling correctly; derive a twist as a change of path measure; and design a query-specific particle-method evaluation through the current research literature.

    An SMC computation can train several objects¤

    Let \(\widehat Z_N(\theta,\phi,\psi)\) be an SMC normalizer estimator with model parameters \(\theta\), proposal parameters \(\phi\), and optional target-twisting parameters \(\psi\). The objective

    \[ \mathcal L_N=\mathbb E[\log\widehat Z_N] \le \log Z \]

    is an evidence lower bound when \(\widehat Z_N\) is nonnegative and unbiased for \(Z\) under the stated construction. This idea underlies filtering and auxiliary SMC objectives such as FIVO, AESMC, and variational SMC. A tighter value of the bound does not guarantee a lower-variance gradient, a better smoothing posterior, or better generated samples under a different query.

    Model, proposal, and twist updates follow different objectives:

    • model learning changes the generative joint law and its evidence;
    • proposal learning changes how finite particles cover a fixed target;
    • twist learning changes intermediate targets while preserving a declared endpoint after correction; and
    • recognition learning may target inclusive, exclusive, wake, sleep, or smoothing-oriented divergences.

    Sharing one particle system does not make these gradients identical.

    Why resampling complicates differentiation¤

    Categorical ancestor sampling is discontinuous in its weights. Several strategies have distinct contracts:

    Table SMC.5. Why resampling complicates differentiation
    Strategy Mechanism Boundary
    stop-gradient through ancestors differentiate weights and mutations on the realized genealogy biased for the full discrete-resampling derivative in general
    score-function estimator differentiate the probability of ancestor choices unbiased under regularity but often high variance
    continuous relaxation or soft resampling replace or mix categorical selection with a differentiable operation changes the finite particle algorithm and can bias the target approximation
    marginalize discrete ancestry analytically sum selected ancestor variables when tractable cost can grow and trajectory information may change
    entropy-regularized optimal-transport resampling deterministically transport weighted particles toward equal mass differentiable with convergence results under its construction; regularization and transport cost remain

    “End-to-end differentiable” names a derivative program, not a theorem that the gradient equals the exact log-likelihood gradient.

    Twisting uses future information in intermediate targets¤

    Filtering targets use observations only through the current time. A positive twist \(h_t(x_{0:t})\) defines

    \[ \widetilde\gamma_t(x_{0:t}) =\gamma_t(x_{0:t})h_t(x_{0:t}). \]

    With compatible terminal condition and incremental corrections, the twist can steer particles toward states likely under future observations or a terminal constraint while retaining the desired endpoint. An ideal twist is related to a backward predictive value such as the probability of future evidence. It is usually intractable and therefore approximated or learned.

    The relation to a Doob transform is a distributional or dynamical correspondence under assumptions: both use a positive backward value to alter path dynamics toward an endpoint event. A learned twist without exact correction defines an approximation, not automatic conditional exactness.

    Worked terminal twist¤

    Consider a two-step Markov chain with terminal event \(E\). Define

    \[ h_t(x_t)=P(E\mid X_t=x_t). \]

    For a transition \(f(x_{t+1}\mid x_t)\), the ideal conditioned transition is

    \[ \begin{aligned} f^E(x_{t+1}\mid x_t) &=f(x_{t+1}\mid x_t)\\ &\quad\times\frac{h_{t+1}(x_{t+1})}{h_t(x_t)}. \end{aligned} \]

    Summing over \(x_{t+1}\) gives one because \(h_t(x_t)=\sum_{x_{t+1}}f(x_{t+1}\mid x_t)h_{t+1}(x_{t+1})\). If a network \(\widehat h_t\) violates this backward identity, the resulting transition is not automatically normalized or exact; importance correction, normalization, or an explicit approximation statement is required.

    SMC inside modern generators¤

    Particle methods increasingly act at inference time around a pretrained generator:

    • autoregressive particles reweight partial sequences by constraints or rewards;
    • diffusion particles use likelihoods, terminal rewards, or learned twists along denoising paths;
    • discrete diffusion particles allocate multiple refinement trajectories;
    • energy and Bayesian systems use tempering populations for difficult targets; and
    • scientific generators use particles for posterior or rare-event conditioning.

    These are hybrid system compositions. The base generator supplies a proposal path; SMC supplies selection and correction toward a declared target. Finite performance depends on particle count, proposal/path discretization, potential quality, resampling, target support, and compute. It is unsafe to say that SMC makes an arbitrary approximate guide exact at a fixed particle count.

    Evaluation and reporting contract¤

    Every particle-method result should report:

    1. Target: normalized or unnormalized law, path space, conditioning event, and support.
    2. Sequence: intermediate targets, data order, temperatures, tolerances, or twists.
    3. Proposal and mutation: densities, invariant kernels, inner approximations, and step counts.
    4. Selection: resampling scheme, timing, trigger, particle ordering, and randomization.
    5. Compute: particles, stages, likelihood or model evaluations, active parameters, wall clock, memory, and hardware.
    6. Returned query: current marginal, path, normalizer, gradient, event probability, or generated candidate.
    7. Diagnostics: weight ESS before selection, CESS where used, unique ancestors by lag, nonfinite weights, likelihood variance, repeated-run variability, and query error.
    8. Evidence: exact finite oracle, independent reproduction, ablations, uncertainty, and failure cases.

    Compare at matched target and cost. Increasing \(N\) while reducing mutation, changing a guidance scale, or using a different model-evaluation budget is not a controlled particle-method comparison.

    Research problems¤

    1. High-dimensional stability. Identify model structure, localization, factorization, or transport that prevents exponential weight collapse without changing the intended target.
    2. Genealogical control. Design selection and smoothing mechanisms that preserve old-path diversity under bounded memory and parallel depth.
    3. Gradient validity. Quantify bias and variance jointly for resampling derivatives, proposal learning, and model learning rather than reporting only bound tightness.
    4. Twist verification. Measure how twist error propagates into finite-particle conditional generation and when correction restores endpoint validity.
    5. Adaptive compute. Allocate particles, stages, mutation steps, and model evaluations from uncertainty signals whose calibration is itself tested.
    6. Generator composition. Establish cost-matched comparisons among SMC, best-of-\(N\), beam search, MCMC correction, and direct conditional training for the same target.
    7. Parallelism. Separate parallel work within a stage from sequential depth across stages, and include accelerator communication in the compute model.

    Cumulative worked audit¤

    For a binary state-space model, a defensible study would:

    1. enumerate exact filtering, smoothing, and likelihood values;
    2. compare bootstrap and fully adapted proposals at particle counts \(4,16,64\);
    3. compare multinomial, stratified, and systematic resampling under declared particle order;
    4. record pre-resampling ESS, unique ancestors, filtering error, smoothing error, and log-likelihood variance over independent runs;
    5. add backward simulation and a valid invariant resample–move kernel;
    6. learn a proposal only after the analytic baselines are understood; and
    7. repeat under a rare observation for which proposal support and numerical weights are stressed.

    This ladder moves from exact algebra to finite computation, learned components, and distribution shift without changing all variables at once.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Learned proposals, differentiable resampling, smoothing twists, and generator control extend the same target–proposal–weight–selection contract. Their derivative and endpoint guarantees must be stated separately. The complete SMC workflow begins with the returned query, validates on exact finite models, reports current weights and genealogy together, and treats recent generator applications as bounded system compositions rather than a new model family.
