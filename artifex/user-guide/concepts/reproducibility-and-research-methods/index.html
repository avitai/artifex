    Reproducibility and Generative-Model Research Methods¤

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    Research needs more than a new score. It needs a falsifiable question, a controlled estimand, uncertainty, mechanism-revealing ablations, reproducible artifacts, bounded conclusions, evidence maturity, and maintenance as the literature changes.

    This volume provides an end-to-end method for selecting questions, designing experiments, reporting negative results, packaging reproductions, distinguishing publication from replication, maintaining claim ledgers, and reviewing research evidence.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–2 for questions, hypotheses, and uncertainty.
    Practitioner: Chapters 1–4 for experimental and artifact practice.
    Research: all chapters, especially evidence/reproduction labels and release-time evidence maintenance.

    1. Selecting Research Questions and Claims¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes familiarity with generative-model components and evaluation. By the end, you should be able to convert a broad idea into a falsifiable question, name the model/system boundary and estimand, distinguish descriptive, predictive, causal, mechanistic, and theoretical claims, choose baselines that could refute the contribution, and write explicit success, failure, and scope criteria before experimentation.

    Start from a decision or missing explanation¤

    Strong questions arise when evidence could change a model choice, theory, safety decision, or scientific explanation. Examples:

    • Does an objective improve conditional coverage at matched training/inference compute?
    • Is a claimed speedup caused by fewer model evaluations or a different codec/hardware path?
    • Which representation information predicts downstream failure?
    • Does an apparent unification preserve the same distribution, only the same population optimum, or merely implementation structure?
    • Under what shift does a method stop working?

    “Can we add module X?” is an engineering prompt. A research question names the comparison, mechanism, population, and failure criterion.

    Define the estimand¤

    Let \(Y(m,u)\) be an outcome for method/configuration \(m\) on evaluation unit \(u\). A method-effect estimand might be

    \[ \Delta=\mathbb E_{u\sim\mathcal U} [Y(m_1,u)-Y(m_0,u)] \]

    under matched data, architecture, budget, and protocol. If those differ, \(\Delta\) is a system-bundle difference. State the target population \(\mathcal U\): prompts, datasets, tasks, domains, users, or hardware workloads.

    Hypotheses and predictions¤

    A mechanism hypothesis should predict more than the headline metric. If better token utilization causes improvement, predict code entropy, per-region errors, and failure under controlled code collapse. If a solver reduces curvature error, predict where/when trajectories diverge—not only lower FID.

    Predefine observations that would weaken the hypothesis. A result that can only confirm the story is not a strong test.

    Scope ladder¤

    Write claims at the narrowest supported level:

    1. implementation behaves on a unit test;
    2. method improves one benchmark/protocol;
    3. effect repeats across tasks/seeds/scales;
    4. mechanism survives targeted interventions;
    5. theory proves a property under assumptions;
    6. independent evidence generalizes beyond the original setup.

    Do not jump from level 2 to “general solution.”

    Baseline hierarchy¤

    Include:

    • identity/no-change or random baseline;
    • strongest simple baseline;
    • current relevant method under retuning;
    • parameter/compute/data-matched baseline;
    • oracle or upper/lower bound where possible;
    • ablated and alternative-mechanism controls.

    A new component must beat spending its extra compute/data on a simpler existing method.

    Worked question refinement¤

    Weak: “Are discrete tokenizers better?” Refined: “At matched bitrate, decoder compute, training images, and downstream prior FLOPs, does tokenizer A improve compositional prompt accuracy without reducing reconstruction text accuracy or conditional diversity on held-out sources?” The refined question defines resources, outcomes, trade-offs, and population.

    Retrieval question: what separates a method claim from a system claim?

    A method claim isolates the method while controlling other causes. A system claim compares complete configurations and must not attribute the bundle difference to one component.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A research question becomes testable through a declared estimand and counter-predictions. Chapter 2 designs sampling, uncertainty, and confirmatory analysis around it.

    2. Experimental Design, Uncertainty, and Significance¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes elementary statistics. By the end, you should be able to identify the sampling and intervention units, randomize or block appropriate nuisance factors, distinguish seed variation from dataset and evaluator variation, select intervals or tests for the estimand, correct multiplicity when needed, and interpret practical effect size separately from thresholded significance.

    Identify independent units¤

    Prompts, dataset examples, training seeds, stochastic samples, raters, tasks, and checkpoints form a hierarchy. Repeated generations from one prompt are not independent prompt draws; repeated metrics on one checkpoint are not training replicates.

    For paired prompt differences \(d_i=Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}\),

    \[ \bar d=\frac1n\sum_i d_i, \qquad SE(\bar d)=\frac{s_d}{\sqrt n} \]

    under independent prompt units and standard assumptions. When prompts/tasks are clustered or heavy-tailed, use hierarchical models or cluster bootstrap. Training-seed uncertainty requires independent training runs.

    Report distributions and intervals¤

    Provide per-task/per-prompt distributions, median/quantiles or robust aggregates, and intervals—not only mean ± one run's sample standard deviation. Statistical Precipice demonstrates how few-run aggregate point estimates can support unstable rankings and motivates interval/performance-profile reporting.

    What a p-value is not¤

    A \(p\)-value is not the probability the null is true, the probability the result replicates, or effect importance. Report effect size and interval. A tiny effect can be significant with huge \(n\); a useful effect can remain uncertain with small \(n\).

    For Bayesian analysis report priors, posterior quantity, sensitivity, and decision threshold. A credible interval and frequentist confidence interval have different interpretations.

    Multiple comparisons and optional stopping¤

    Trying many metrics, datasets, prompts, seeds, checkpoints, and hyperparameters then reporting the best inflates false discoveries. Predeclare primary outcomes; separate exploratory from confirmatory analyses; adjust or model multiplicity; and use a fresh confirmation set after selection.

    Do not stop collecting runs when significance appears unless using a valid sequential design. Keep an experiment registry including failed/neutral runs.

    Power and precision¤

    Choose sample size from the smallest effect worth detecting, expected variance/correlation, desired interval width or power, and attrition/failure. Simulation-based power is often easier for hierarchical generative studies. Post-hoc “observed power” adds little beyond the interval.

    Seeds and determinism¤

    Seeds sample initialization, data order, augmentation, dropout, sampler noise, and environment stochasticity. Report the selection policy and all completed runs. Hardware kernels/distributed reductions can remain nondeterministic despite fixed seeds; record determinism settings and tolerances.

    Stratification¤

    Aggregate scores can hide difficulty, language, modality, demographic, source, or tail-event differences. Predefine meaningful strata and interactions. Predictability-Aligned Evaluation, ICML 2026, is Peer-reviewed research evidence in time-series forecasting that average error can confound model performance with changing instance difficulty; its spectral framework is domain-specific, while stratified difficulty analysis is broadly useful.

    Retrieval question: which unit should a bootstrap resample?

    The highest sampled unit to which the conclusion generalizes—often prompts/tasks and, separately, training runs—not every correlated generation or rater click as independent.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    Uncertainty follows the sampling hierarchy, not the number of logged rows. Chapter 3 designs ablations and negative-result analyses that test mechanisms instead of decorating a positive result.

    3. Ablations, Mechanisms, and Negative Results¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapters 1–2 and controlled-comparison design. By the end, you should be able to design necessity, sufficiency, dose-response, replacement, and interaction ablations; distinguish component attribution from mechanistic explanation; identify compensating changes and hyperparameter unfairness; and turn a negative result into a bounded, reproducible update rather than a universal failure claim.

    Ablation is an intervention¤

    An ablation changes a component while keeping the rest of the causal comparison stable. Removing a training component only at inference may create an out-of-distribution system rather than test its training contribution. Retrain when the claim concerns learned adaptation.

    Use factorial designs for interactions. With components \(A,B\), estimate

    \[ I_{AB}= [Y_{11}-Y_{01}]-[Y_{10}-Y_{00}]. \]

    If \(I_{AB}\ne0\), “the effect of A” depends on B. One-at-a-time tables cannot reveal this.

    Mechanism tests¤

    • intervene on the proposed mediator;
    • measure predicted intermediate quantities;
    • create synthetic cases where alternatives disagree;
    • test invariances/equivariances claimed by theory;
    • vary scale/horizon/noise where the mechanism predicts a curve;
    • use negative controls that should not respond;
    • test an alternative mechanism with equal capacity/compute.

    Attention visualization, latent clustering, or correlation can suggest hypotheses but does not establish causal mechanism.

    Null and negative results¤

    A useful negative result specifies:

    1. the claim tested and smallest meaningful effect;
    2. faithful implementation and baseline validation;
    3. search/tuning budget and diagnostics;
    4. uncertainty and power/precision;
    5. settings where failure holds and does not hold;
    6. plausible explanations separated from evidence;
    7. artifacts that make further testing possible.

    “It did not work” after one seed is anecdote; a bounded failure after competent testing is knowledge.

    Debug before interpreting¤

    Verify shapes, dtypes, masks, signs, schedules, gradient paths, train/eval modes, preprocessing, metric code, data overlap, and a small solvable case. Reproduce a known baseline. Check loss/gradient scale and overfit a tiny dataset where appropriate. Only then interpret algorithmic failure.

    Sensitivity and robustness¤

    Report response surfaces over important hyperparameters rather than the proposed method's optimum against baseline defaults. Include data size, compute, model scale, guidance, NFE, resolution, corruption, and shift. A method useful only in a narrow stable region may still matter, but the region is part of the result.

    Publication bias and outcome switching¤

    Maintain preregistered or timestamped hypotheses when feasible, publish neutral/negative ablations, and distinguish planned from post-hoc stories. Do not rename a failed primary endpoint as “diagnostic” and promote a secondary metric without disclosure.

    The official NeurIPS 2026 MLRC call explicitly treats careful negative and partial failures to reproduce as valuable contributions—Official venue policy, not empirical evidence about any method.

    Worked apparent mechanism¤

    A new loss improves FID but also doubles guidance scale. Factorial runs (old/new loss × low/high guidance) show both losses gain equally from guidance and no loss effect at matched guidance. The original bundle result was real; the attributed mechanism was not.

    Retrieval question: when must an ablation retrain?

    When the removed/changed component affects the learned parameters or training distribution. Inference-only removal then tests robustness to disabling a component, not its training contribution.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Ablations are controlled interventions; negative results need validated implementations and precision. Chapter 4 makes those claims executable by another researcher.

    4. Reproducible Artifacts and Independent Replication¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes a runnable research codebase. By the end, you should be able to package data lineage, environment locks, configurations, seeds, commands, checkpoints, and expected outputs; distinguish repeatability, computational reproducibility, robustness, and independent replication; design smoke and full-scale verification; and document deviations without silently changing the claim.

    Local terminology¤

    Terminology varies across disciplines. This curriculum uses:

    • Repeatability: same team, code, data, and environment reruns the experiment.
    • Artifact reproduction: another team runs provided code/data/configs and obtains compatible results.
    • Independent replication: another team independently implements or recollects key pieces and tests the claim.
    • Generalization study: tests the claim on new models/data/domains/scales.

    State the definition rather than relying on the word alone. Pineau et al. documents the NeurIPS reproducibility program and checklist/artifact practices.

    Artifact bundle¤

    Include:

    • exact code commit and patch state;
    • environment/lockfile, compiler/driver/runtime versions;
    • data manifest, hashes, licenses, preprocessing, splits;
    • model initialization/checkpoint hashes;
    • every configuration and random seed;
    • commands/workflow DAG from raw data to tables/figures;
    • raw per-example/per-seed outputs, not only aggregates;
    • metric code/version/reference statistics;
    • hardware/topology/precision and resource logs;
    • expected runtime/cost and small smoke path;
    • known nondeterminism, tolerances, and failure recovery;
    • artifact license and access restrictions.

    Containers help but do not preserve unavailable data/services, hardware semantics, or external APIs indefinitely. Archive source and metadata; pin mutable model/dataset revisions; provide checksums.

    Reproduction compatibility¤

    Define acceptance before rerun: effect direction, interval overlap, absolute tolerance, or claim-specific threshold. Exact floating-point equality is rarely appropriate; “looks similar” is too weak.

    If reproduction fails, compare data, preprocessing, model checkpoint, optimizer, random seeds, library kernels, evaluation, and hidden defaults. Contact authors where possible without allowing post-hoc changes to erase the original specification.

    Restricted artifacts¤

    Privacy, licenses, safety, or commercial constraints may prevent public data/weights. Provide synthetic/minimal examples, schemas, hashes, access procedure, secure evaluation, detailed pseudocode, and aggregate diagnostics. “Cannot release” does not justify omitting enough information to assess the claim.

    Artifact availability versus scientific validity¤

    Perfectly runnable code can reproduce a confounded or contaminated experiment. Independent replication can reveal hidden dependencies. Conversely, a sound theorem or result can have imperfect packaging. Assess artifact quality, experimental validity, and evidence maturity separately.

    Worked acceptance rule¤

    Original paired improvement is 2.0 points with 95% CI [1.2,2.8]. Before rerun, define reproduction success as positive improvement with CI excluding zero and estimate within a scientifically chosen tolerance; partial success as positive but imprecise or smaller beyond tolerance. Do not define success after seeing the result.

    Retrieval question: what does a container fail to preserve?

    Mutable/unavailable data and APIs, external services, hardware behavior, licenses/credentials, random nondeterminism, and long-term image availability unless separately archived.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Artifacts make a claim inspectable; independent replication tests hidden assumptions. Chapter 5 records both publication maturity and reproduction status without conflating them.

    5. Evidence Labels, Reproduction Levels, and Claim Ledgers¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the claim taxonomy and artifact workflow. By the end, you should be able to attach point-of-use evidence to an atomic claim, separate peer-reviewed, official, reproduced, and preliminary support, score a reproduction level without overstating it, maintain a claim/evidence ledger, and identify when a citation is relevant but does not entail the authored statement.

    Standard evidence labels¤

    Use exactly one maturity label per claim source:

    Table REPRO.1. Standard evidence labels
    Label Meaning
    Established mature result supported across substantial theory/evidence
    Peer-reviewed research accepted archival result; still protocol-bound
    Preprint public manuscript without verified archival acceptance
    Technical report organization/author report, possibly detailed but not archival peer review
    Vendor claim product/organization assertion or demonstration without sufficient independent evidence
    Interpretive synthesis curriculum inference connecting sources; not directly asserted by one source

    Peer review is a filter, not proof. “Established” requires more than age or citation count. Vendor claims can later gain stronger evidence; record versions rather than silently relabeling history.

    Reproduction status¤

    Track independently from maturity:

    1. Author result — evidence reported by originating authors/team.
    2. Independent reproduction — independent team obtains a compatible claim under declared acceptance criteria.
    3. Partial reproduction — some components/settings reproduce or effect is materially weaker/different.
    4. Unverified claim — no qualifying reproduction found in the audit.

    A peer-reviewed paper may remain Author result; a preprint may have an Independent reproduction.

    Claim ledger schema¤

    For each benchmark/research claim store:

    • stable claim ID and exact bounded wording;
    • evidence label and reproduction status;
    • source URL/DOI, authors, venue/status, version/date;
    • dataset/split/modality/resolution;
    • conditional/unconditional and guidance/decoding/sampler;
    • total/active parameters, training data/compute;
    • NFE/candidates/wall-clock/hardware;
    • metric implementation and uncertainty;
    • comparable baselines and known confounders;
    • supporting table/figure/appendix location;
    • verification date and reviewer;
    • superseding/contradicting evidence;
    • curriculum pages using the claim.

    Relationship labels¤

    Unification claims additionally use the six curriculum relationships:

    1. algebraic equivalence;
    2. distributional/dynamical equivalence under assumptions;
    3. population-optimum correspondence;
    4. shared estimator/objective;
    5. implementation analogy;
    6. hybrid system composition.

    The claim ledger must state assumptions and finite-estimation/sampling gaps. Shared architecture never upgrades an analogy to distributional equivalence.

    Source hierarchy¤

    Use primary papers for technical claims, official specifications/reports for their own standards/products, and surveys to discover/organize literature. Verify benchmark claims in the cited table/appendix and code, not abstract alone. Record withdrawn/rejected status when relevant; acceptance can change between preprint versions.

    Worked evidence matrix¤

    “Method X is SOTA” becomes: “X reports metric M on dataset split S at resolution R with guidance G and NFE K in paper version V, verified date D; author result; training-data comparability unresolved.” The longer sentence is scientifically useful because it can be falsified and updated.

    Retrieval question: why are evidence maturity and reproduction separate axes?

    Peer review evaluates a submission; reproduction tests whether the result can be obtained again. Each can exist without the other and supports a different inference.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Claim ledgers make evidence status, protocol, and reuse explicit. Chapter 6 defines how to refresh the evidence base without treating publication recency as evidence maturity.

    6. Evidence Review and Maintenance¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes Chapters 1–5. By the end, you should be able to run a release-time evidence review, preserve a reproducible literature-search log, detect stale, retracted, superseded, or scope-shifted evidence, triage errata by claim impact, and design maintenance ownership and cadence so a living technical book can update without rewriting its historical record.

    Release-Time Evidence Review¤

    For every module with last_verified:

    1. enumerate claim IDs and search terms;
    2. search primary venue/proceeding indexes and author/project records through cutoff;
    3. check whether cited preprints were accepted, revised, withdrawn, or contradicted;
    4. compare paper versions and benchmark protocols;
    5. inspect tables/appendices/code for headline numbers;
    6. search independent reproductions and negative evidence;
    7. update evidence/reproduction labels and qualifiers;
    8. record reviewer, queries, cutoff, and unresolved items;
    9. run links/contracts/build and render inspection.

    A stale verification date produces a report and review queue. It does not automatically delete historical content or replace it with an unsupported “latest” claim.

    Literature-search log¤

    Record databases/venues, exact queries, date window, status filters, inclusion/exclusion criteria, duplicates, and reasons for excluding submissions/workshops/vendor posts. Search by method aliases and citations; title search alone misses renamed work.

    Peer-Reviewed Research Checkpoints¤

    • The Generalization Spectrum, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for separating memorization, near transfer, and farther transfer in controlled competitive-programming variants; distances and conclusions are benchmark-specific.
    • Predictability-Aligned Evaluation, ICML 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for stratifying forecast error by spectral predictability in its domain.
    • Train Once, Answer All, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research for multiplexing controlled pretraining experiments while explicitly testing interactions in the reported setup.
    • MedEvidence, ICLR 2026 — Peer-reviewed research benchmark evidence that tested models lacked expert-like skepticism toward low-quality findings; it warns against automated literature synthesis, not against all research assistance.
    • The NeurIPS 2026 MLRC is Official venue policy recognizing reproduction, generalization, meta-reproducibility, AI-assisted reproducibility audits, and negative/partial results.

    Research exercises¤

    1. Convert a vague “better model” idea into an estimand, mechanism predictions, and falsifiers.
    2. Simulate hierarchical prompt/seed data and compare naive versus cluster-bootstrap intervals.
    3. Design a factorial ablation that separates architecture, objective, and guidance interaction.
    4. Reproduce one result from provided artifacts, then independently implement its key algorithm.
    5. Create a claim ledger for five research statements and audit their exact tables/status.
    6. Write a bounded negative-result report with power, diagnostics, and released artifacts.
    7. Run a release-time review that discovers a cited preprint was withdrawn; update maturity without erasing historically useful method discussion.
    8. Stress-test an AI literature assistant against source versions, retractions, benchmark qualifiers, and fabricated citations.
    Exercise 7 solution criterion

    Preserve the method as historical/preprint material if pedagogically relevant, mark the withdrawal and verification date, remove unsupported peer-reviewed/SOTA language, add stronger replacement evidence if available, and update every dependent claim through the ledger.

    Cumulative retrieval: reproducible research
    1. What must be fixed before a comparison can support a causal component claim?
    2. Why are several random seeds not the same as independent replication?
    3. What information turns a link list into a claim-evidence ledger?
    4. Which events should trigger an evidence-maintenance review?

    Solutions. (1) The estimand, intervention/component change, system boundary, data, budget, tuning, evaluation, and relevant nuisance controls. (2) Seeds repeat one implementation, team, data pipeline, and interpretation; independent replication recreates the test with meaningful organizational or implementation independence. (3) Atomic claim identity, exact supporting source and location, entailment/scope, evidence maturity, verification/review state, and update history. (4) Releases, material claim edits, new contradictory or stronger evidence, retractions/corrections, broken or moved sources, benchmark changes, and reported errata.

    Final synthesis¤

    Durable research starts with a bounded question and ends with maintained evidence. Sampling hierarchies determine uncertainty; ablations test mechanisms; negative results map failure regions; artifacts enable reproduction; independent replications test hidden assumptions; claim ledgers keep maturity, protocol, and reproduction distinct. Conceptual coverage and executable library support are separate evidence claims and must be documented as such.
