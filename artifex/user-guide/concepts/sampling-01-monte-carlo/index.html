    Sampling 1 — Monte Carlo Queries and Variance¤

    Learning path

    Required knowledge

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    • Sampling 2 — Markov Kernels, Metropolis, and Gibbs

    This chapter begins from one target and four computational routes, then develops Monte Carlo estimands, uncertainty, direct and weighted sampling, and variance-reduction designs. For uninterrupted reading, use the continuous view.

    One Target, Four Ways to Reach It¤

    Sampling methods differ in what they can access and what they return. Keep one target law \(\pi\) fixed while comparing the routes:

    The output boxes are deliberately different. A weighted proposal is not an independent target draw; a chain with the right invariant law may not have mixed; and an ODE endpoint inherits learned-field and solver error.

    • Target error

      Does the mathematical procedure target the intended probability law?

    • Finite-compute error

      Are proposals degenerate, chains unmixed, or particles impoverished?

    • Discretization error

      Does a finite step size change the stationary law or endpoint path?

    • Numerical error

      Do precision, tolerances, unstable ratios, or implementation details alter the calculation?

    Learning Objectives¤

    • distinguish independent sampling, importance weighting, and Markov-chain sampling;
    • separate a target law, a query, a random estimator, and its reported uncertainty;
    • derive the iid Monte Carlo variance and state the conditions behind a normal error interval;
    • compare Rao–Blackwellization, control variates, antithetic coupling, common random numbers, randomized quasi-Monte Carlo, and multilevel coupling;
    • identify whether a procedure returns target draws, weighted particles, an expectation estimate, or a normalizing-constant estimate;
    • distinguish time homogeneity, invariance, uniqueness, distributional convergence, path-average convergence, and finite mixing;
    • derive Metropolis–Hastings acceptance and state its support requirements;
    • construct Gibbs, blocked, collapsed, slice, and Metropolis-within-Gibbs transitions without confusing acceptance with exploration;
    • distinguish warmup, adaptation, initialization bias, retained sampling, and a theoretical mixing time;
    • derive HMC with one mass-matrix convention and interpret rank-normalized split \(\widehat R\), bulk/tail ESS, MCSE, and divergences;
    • separate exact-gradient MCMC from fixed-step and stochastic-gradient approximations;
    • explain dimension matching in reversible-jump MCMC and distinguish simulated annealing from parallel tempering;
    • explain why ULA has no acceptance rate;
    • separate target-distribution error from discretization and mixing error;
    • derive continuity and Fokker–Planck equations at a conceptual level;
    • explain probability-flow ODEs, stochastic transport, and numerical NFE;
    • identify which diagnostics apply to which sampler.

    Chapter Map and Canonical Continuations¤

    This chapter is the mathematical spine for sampling in the book. It owns the family-independent objects: a target law, a statistical query, a random or deterministic estimator, a transition kernel, a continuous-time process, and an error budget. Later chapters do not replace these definitions. They specialize them to a model family, state space, conditioning mechanism, or experimental claim.

    That division prevents two opposite mistakes. Copying the same foundation into every family chapter invites notation drift and contradictory assumptions. Moving every detail into Foundations would erase the model-specific questions that make a method useful. The rule is therefore:

    Derive a reusable mathematical contract here; derive its consequences again where a particular generative model changes the available objects, approximation, or failure mode.

    A first pass needs only five questions

    If sampling is new, leave the continuation map closed. For each method ask: What distribution is the target? What does the procedure return? Which query will be estimated? What makes the answer exact or asymptotically valid? What can go wrong at finite compute? Follow the Beginner reading route at the top of the page, work the finite examples, and return to the map after Section 10.

    Monte Carlo Begins with a Query¤

    A collection of random draws is not yet a statistical answer. Begin with the quantity to be computed. For a probability law \(\pi\) and an integrable observable \(f\), the query

    \[ \mu=\mathbb E_{X\sim\pi}[f(X)] \]

    might represent a generated image's mean intensity, the probability that a sample satisfies a constraint, a posterior moment, an ELBO term, or a metric averaged over generator noise. If \(X_1,\ldots,X_N\) are independent draws from \(\pi\), the ordinary Monte Carlo estimator is

    \[ \widehat\mu_N=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N f(X_i). \]

    When \(\sigma_f^2=\operatorname{Var}_\pi[f(X)]<\infty\),

    \[ \mathbb E[\widehat\mu_N]=\mu, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_N)=\frac{\sigma_f^2}{N}. \]

    The \(N^{-1/2}\) root-mean-square rate is a statement about the number of independent evaluations. It is not a dimension-free difficulty theorem: \(\sigma_f^2\), evaluation cost, tail behavior, and the number of samples needed to discover a rare region can all deteriorate with dimension.

    What an ordinary Monte Carlo error bar assumes¤

    The iid central limit theorem gives

    \[ \sqrt N\,(\widehat\mu_N-\mu) \xrightarrow{d}\mathcal N(0,\sigma_f^2) \]

    under finite-variance regularity. Estimate the variance with Bessel's correction,

    \[ s_f^2=\frac1{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^N \left(f(X_i)-\widehat\mu_N\right)^2, \qquad \widehat{\operatorname{SE}}(\widehat\mu_N)=\frac{s_f}{\sqrt N}. \]

    The familiar interval

    \[ \widehat\mu_N\ \pm\ z_{1-\alpha/2}\frac{s_f}{\sqrt N} \]

    is therefore asymptotic, not an exact finite-sample confidence interval. It can be badly calibrated for a small \(N\), infinite or very large variance, rare-event indicators, an adaptively chosen stopping time, or dependent samples treated as iid. For MCMC, use a dependence-aware long-run variance or effective sample size; for repeated randomized quasi-Monte Carlo, use independent randomizations; for a deterministic quadrature rule, there is no ordinary sampling variance to estimate.

    Three errors that one standard error cannot combine

    Monte Carlo error is variation caused by finite random computation under a fixed mathematical target and observable. It does not measure model misspecification, learned-parameter uncertainty, dataset sampling error, discretization bias, or floating-point error. A defensible experiment names each error source and reports an estimator appropriate to it.

    Worked finite example: an exact answer can occur by accident¤

    Let \(X\) take values \(-1,0,2\) with probabilities \(1/4,1/2,1/4\), and let \(f(X)=X^2\). Exact summation gives

    \[ \mu=\frac14(1)+\frac12(0)+\frac14(4)=\frac54, \]

    and

    \[ \sigma_f^2 =\left[\frac14(1)+\frac14(16)\right]-\left(\frac54\right)^2 =\frac{43}{16}. \]

    Suppose four independent draws happen to be \((2,0,-1,0)\). Their transformed values are \((4,0,1,0)\), so \(\widehat\mu_4=5/4\), exactly the true answer. But the estimated variance is \(s_f^2=43/12\) and the estimated standard error is \(\sqrt{43/48}\approx0.946\). The point estimate's equality is a coincidence; the realized sample does not justify zero uncertainty. Repeating the experiment with fresh random streams tests Monte Carlo variability, while comparing with the finite exact sum tests correctness.

    1. Direct, Rejection, and Importance Sampling¤

    Direct sampling is a pushforward construction¤

    Direct ancestral sampling follows a normalized factorization. More generally, if \(U\) has a tractable base law and \(T\) is a measurable map, then \(X=T(U)\) has the pushforward law \(T_\#p_U\). The map need not be invertible and need not provide a density. The generalized inverse-CDF construction is the scalar canonical example.

    For independent \(U_1,U_2\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), the Box–Muller construction

    \[ \begin{aligned} R&=\sqrt{-2\log U_1}, & \Theta&=2\pi U_2,\\ Z_1&=R\cos\Theta, & Z_2&=R\sin\Theta \end{aligned} \]

    returns two independent standard Gaussian variables. If \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\) and \(LL^T=\Sigma\), then \(X=\mu+LZ\) has law \(\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)\). These constructions explain the probability map; a production random-number library may use a different Gaussian algorithm and must separately control uniform-generator quality, endpoint handling, precision, and factorization error.

    Rejection sampling exposes its normalizer¤

    Let the normalized target be \(\pi(x)=\gamma(x)/Z\), where \(\gamma\) can be evaluated and \(Z\) may be unknown. If a normalized proposal \(q\) and finite constant \(M\) satisfy

    \[ \gamma(x)\le Mq(x) \]

    everywhere, propose \(X\sim q\), draw \(U\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\), and accept when

    \[ U\le\frac{\gamma(X)}{Mq(X)}. \]

    The accepted state has density \(\pi\), and the mean acceptance probability is

    \[ \mathbb P(\text{accept}) =\int q(x)\frac{\gamma(x)}{Mq(x)}\,dx =\frac ZM. \]

    The frequently quoted value \(1/M\) applies only when the envelope was written for an already normalized target, so that \(Z=1\). An invalid bound creates a biased sampler; silently clipping an acceptance probability above one does not repair it.

    Worked failure case: a harmless scale mismatch becomes exponential¤

    Take \(\pi=\mathcal N(0,I_d)\) and \(q=\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I_d)\) with \(\sigma>1\). Their density ratio is

    \[ \frac{\pi(x)}{q(x)} =\sigma^d\exp\!\left[-\frac12\left(1-\sigma^{-2}\right) \lVert x\rVert^2\right], \]

    so the tight rejection constant is \(M=\sigma^d\) and the acceptance probability is \(\sigma^{-d}\). A one-percent scale mismatch gives \(1.01^{-1000}\approx4.77\times10^{-5}\) in one thousand dimensions: about one accepted draw per twenty-one thousand proposals. The algebraic \(N^{-1/2}\) Monte Carlo rate has not disappeared; the cost of obtaining each target draw has exploded.

    Importance sampling changes the output object¤

    Importance sampling estimates

    \[ \begin{aligned} w(x)&=\frac{\tilde p(x)}{q(x)},\\ \mathbb E_p[f(X)] &=\frac{\mathbb E_q[w(X)f(X)]} {\mathbb E_q[w(X)]}, \end{aligned} \]

    where \(\tilde p\) may be unnormalized. Self-normalization introduces finite-sample bias but removes the unknown normalizer. Weight degeneracy is measured through effective sample size, not merely proposal count.

    Coverage Is Non-Negotiable¤

    If \(q(x)=0\) in a region where \(p(x)|f(x)|>0\), importance sampling cannot recover that contribution. Increasing the number of samples does not fix missing support. Even with full support, heavy-tailed weights may have infinite variance, making ordinary error bars unreliable.

    For normalized weights \(\bar w_i=w_i/\sum_jw_j\), the heuristic

    \[ \operatorname{ESS}=\frac{1}{\sum_i\bar w_i^2} \]

    summarizes concentration. It does not prove accurate tail probabilities or proposal coverage. Inspect log weights and repeat the estimate across independent runs.

    Rejection sampling returns independent exact draws when the envelope is valid. Importance sampling returns weighted proposals rather than independent target draws unless followed by resampling, which introduces dependence and duplication.

    The output object constrains the query¤

    Sampling procedures that use the same target density need not return interchangeable objects:

    Table SAMP.1. The output object constrains the query
    Procedure Immediate output Directly supports Does not provide automatically
    Direct or valid rejection sampling Independent target draws Sample statistics and expectations with ordinary Monte Carlo error Density values or an evidence estimate
    Ordinary importance sampling Independent proposal draws with unnormalized weights Unnormalized integrals and, when applicable, an unbiased normalizer estimate Independent target draws
    Self-normalized importance sampling Proposal draws with weights summing to one A finite weighted approximation to normalized expectations An unbiased finite-sample ratio estimator
    MCMC One or more correlated state paths Posterior expectations after initialization and mixing are controlled The target normalizer, independent draws, or a universal burn-in length
    SMC Weighted particles, ancestor indices, and incremental normalizer estimates Filtering, path-space expectations, and normalizer ratios under the chosen construction Recovery of a mode absent from every particle

    Resampling weighted proposals produces an unweighted empirical population, but finite resampling can duplicate high-weight states and discard low-weight ones. It is an approximation to the target population, not a conversion into exact independent draws. The query-first inference map compares these outputs with exact inference and VI.

    For the full estimator algebra—ordinary and self-normalized importance sampling, proposal divergences, multiple proposals, AIS/SMC normalizer ratios, log-scale bias, and diagnostic contracts—continue to Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants.

    Randomized optimization supplies another output class. Independent Gumbel noise for every complete finite configuration followed by an exact argmax gives an exact categorical sample, and matched local Gaussian perturbations followed by an exact quadratic solve give an exact Gaussian sample. Unary or factorwise Gumbels, relaxed argmax operations, truncated max-product, and finite-tolerance linear solves generally change that guarantee. The randomized-optimization derivation states the perturbation field, dependence, solver, and correction contract.

    When the target is created from a base generator by a likelihood, reward, constraint, or verifier, the conditioning and steering volume derives the returned laws of rejection, Doob guidance, SMC twisting, projection, and best-of-\(N\) selection. Those mechanisms share sampling tools but do not share one finite output law.

    When the state is a random event configuration, the output contract must also include cardinality, empty windows, locations or times, marks, and stopping. Point Processes and Random Finite Sets derives count–location sampling, inverse compensators, dominated thinning, branching, spectral DPP sampling, and configuration MCMC. These are distinct uses of the sampling foundations developed here.

    Variance Reduction and Randomness Design¤

    Variance reduction changes how a fixed expectation is estimated. It does not change the target distribution, create a new model family, or excuse an invalid sampler. The basic contract is

    \[ \mathbb E[\widehat\mu_{\mathrm{new}}]=\mu \]

    when unbiasedness is claimed, followed by a cost-matched comparison of variance, bias, and wall-clock work.

    Table SAMP.2. Variance Reduction and Randomness Design
    Method Construction What must be checked
    Rao–Blackwellization Replace a random quantity by its conditional expectation given retained information The conditional expectation is exact or its approximation error is declared
    Control variate Subtract a correlated zero-mean quantity Its expectation is known and coefficient fitting does not reuse data in a way ignored by the error analysis
    Antithetic variables Couple correct marginals to induce negative covariance The transformed outputs, not merely the base noises, are negatively correlated
    Common random numbers Reuse base randomness across two systems It improves a difference only when the induced covariance has the helpful sign
    Stratification or RQMC Cover base-noise space more evenly Smoothness, effective dimension, transformation, randomization, and uncertainty protocol
    Fixed randomness / SAA Optimize one reusable finite-sample objective Convergence to that finite objective is not convergence to the population objective
    Multilevel coupling Telescope across resolutions and couple adjacent levels Cost, bias, and variance decay across levels, plus the finest-level target

    Conditional expectation cannot increase variance¤

    For a square-integrable estimator \(Y\) and retained information \(S\), the law of total variance gives

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(Y) =\mathbb E[\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid S)] +\operatorname{Var}(\mathbb E[Y\mid S]). \]

    Therefore \(\mathbb E[Y\mid S]\) has the same expectation as \(Y\) and no larger variance. Marginalizing a tractable discrete latent variable instead of sampling it, or summing over a local graphical-model state inside a larger estimator, are generative-model examples. Approximate marginalization does not inherit the exact guarantee.

    Control variates make correlation explicit¤

    Suppose \(H\) has known expectation \(\eta\). For any fixed coefficient \(c\),

    \[ Y_c=f(X)-c\bigl(H(X)-\eta\bigr) \]

    has expectation \(\mu\). Its variance is minimized by

    \[ c^*=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(f(X),H(X))} {\operatorname{Var}(H(X))}, \]

    provided \(\operatorname{Var}(H)>0\). A coefficient estimated on the same finite sample can alter small-sample bias and uncertainty; fit on independent data, cross-fit, or analyze the joint estimator. Baselines for score-function gradients are control variates specialized to a derivative query, as developed in Gradients and Geometry.

    Antithetic pairs and common random numbers answer different questions¤

    If \(Y^+\) and \(Y^-\) each have mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\), the paired average has

    \[ \operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{Y^++Y^-}{2}\right) =\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\bigl(1+\rho\bigr), \qquad \rho=\operatorname{Corr}(Y^+,Y^-). \]

    The pair helps only when \(\rho<0\) and is worse than two independent draws when \(\rho>0\). A symmetric base law makes \(Z\) and \(-Z\) valid marginals, but it does not prove that a nonlinear generator statistic \(f(G(Z))\) and \(f(G(-Z))\) are negatively correlated.

    Common random numbers instead compare two methods. If \(\widehat\mu_A\) and \(\widehat\mu_B\) share base randomness,

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_A-\widehat\mu_B) =\operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_A) +\operatorname{Var}(\widehat\mu_B) -2\operatorname{Cov}(\widehat\mu_A,\widehat\mu_B). \]

    A positive covariance sharpens the paired difference even if neither marginal estimate becomes less variable. Report paired and unpaired designs explicitly.

    Worked example: one symmetry, two variance-reduction views¤

    Let \(X\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and estimate \(\mu=\mathbb E[X^2]=1/3\). Ordinary sampling has

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X^2)=\frac15-\frac19=\frac4{45}. \]

    Because \(1-X\) is also uniform, use the antithetic pair

    \[ A(X)=\frac{X^2+(1-X)^2}{2}=X^2-X+\frac12. \]

    Direct integration gives \(\mathbb E[A]=1/3\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(A)=1/180\). By comparison, the average of two independent \(X^2\) values has variance \(2/45\), eight times larger.

    The same estimator follows from the control variate \(H(X)=X\) with known mean \(1/2\). Since

    \[ c^*=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X^2,X)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)}=1, \]

    the optimal controlled value is \(X^2-(X-1/2)=A(X)\). Antithetic coupling and a control variate are different constructions, but here a symmetry makes their estimators coincide.

    Deterministic QMC, randomized QMC, and uncertainty¤

    Write a target expectation as an integral over base noise,

    \[ \mu=\int_{[0,1]^d}h(u)\,du. \]

    Quasi-Monte Carlo (QMC) evaluates \(h\) on a deterministic low-discrepancy point set \(u_{1:N}\). Its error can improve on ordinary Monte Carlo for suitable smoothness, variation, and effective dimension, but the deterministic estimate has no ordinary sampling standard error. Ambient dimension alone is not a complete predictor; the coordinate transformation and the dimensions that dominate variation matter.

    Randomized QMC (RQMC) applies a valid randomization that keeps each point marginally uniform while retaining low-discrepancy structure. With \(R\) independent randomizations, compute one estimate \(\widehat\mu_N^{(r)}\) per randomization and use variation across the \(R\) independent estimates for an error assessment. Treating the \(N\) dependent points within one scramble as iid would be incorrect. Favorable RQMC rates require regularity and are not a universal promise for discontinuous constraints, heavy tails, or high effective dimension.

    QMC has been applied to both score- and pathwise-gradient VI (Buchholz, Wenzel, and Mandt, 2018), while randomized QMC has been combined with quasi-Newton optimization (Liu and Owen, 2021). These results establish method-specific improvements under their assumptions, not that low-discrepancy points dominate iid sampling for every generative objective.

    Fixed randomness creates a finite optimization problem¤

    Let a population objective be

    \[ L(\theta)=\mathbb E_{\epsilon}[\ell(\theta;\epsilon)]. \]

    Sample-average approximation (SAA) fixes draws \(\epsilon_{1:N}\) and optimizes

    \[ \widehat L_N(\theta)=\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N \ell(\theta;\epsilon_i). \]

    The resulting objective is deterministic conditional on those draws, so line searches, stopping tests, Hessian-vector products, and quasi-Newton methods can be meaningful for the executed finite problem. A tiny optimization residual for \(\widehat L_N\) does not show that \(\theta\) optimizes \(L\). Validate with fresh random draws or increasing \(N\). Deterministic ADVI applies this idea to a fixed Monte Carlo approximation of a mean-field variational objective; its favorable low-sample results have explicit posterior-family boundaries.

    Multilevel estimators couple resolutions¤

    When \(Y_0,\ldots,Y_L\) approximate the same query at increasing fidelity,

    \[ \mathbb E[Y_L] =\mathbb E[Y_0] +\sum_{\ell=1}^L \mathbb E[Y_\ell-Y_{\ell-1}]. \]

    Multilevel Monte Carlo spends many cheap samples on coarse levels and fewer expensive samples on fine corrections. The identity is elementary; efficiency comes from coupling adjacent levels so that \(Y_\ell-Y_{\ell-1}\) has small variance. In generative modeling, levels can represent solver grids, discretized stochastic paths, or nested inference computations. Estimating \(\mathbb E[Y_L]\) without bias does not remove the gap between the finite \(Y_L\) and a continuum target. Multilevel VI variants instead use related couplings to reduce gradient-estimation cost; their guarantee is tied to the declared hierarchy and optimization assumptions.

    Current generative applications, with bounded claims¤

    Evidence labels and scope

    • Peer-reviewed research — ICLR 2026: Quasi-Monte Carlo Methods Enable Extremely Low-Dimensional Deep Generative Models uses randomized lattice integration to approximate marginal likelihood in specialized one-, two-, and three-dimensional deep latent-variable models. Its reported advantage is a result for that low-dimensional regime; the method is compute-intensive and is not a replacement for high-dimensional latent inference.
    • Peer-reviewed research — ICLR 2026: Antithetic Noise in Diffusion Models reports negative correlation for paired initial noises \(z\) and \(-z\) across studied generators and uses it for uncertainty estimation. Marginal validity follows from Gaussian symmetry; variance reduction for a chosen statistic still depends on its measured output covariance. The paper's approximate score-symmetry explanation remains a conjectural interpretation, not a universal theorem about trained generators.
    • Peer-reviewed research — ICLR 2026: multilevel control functionals combine learned Stein control variates with multiple fidelities and include variational-inference experiments. Their faster-rate analysis assumes smoothness and a regime whose dimension is not too high.

    These examples show why randomness design belongs in a generative-model book: it can change the cost and uncertainty of training, inference, evaluation, and scientific queries without changing the model family itself.

    Monte Carlo retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: direct, rejection, importance, and variance reduction
    1. What must be declared before saying samples from an algorithm are “accurate”?
    2. Why does rejection sampling require a global envelope rather than good average proposal fit?
    3. What support condition is required for importance sampling?
    4. How do common random numbers reduce variance without changing either marginal estimator?

    Solutions. (1) The target, query/estimand, approximation and randomness sources, budget, and error criterion. (2) Acceptance validity requires \(p(x)\le Mq(x)\) everywhere relevant; one missed region invalidates the bound. (3) The proposal must be positive wherever the target-times-integrand contributes. (4) They correlate paired estimates so shared fluctuations cancel in their difference while preserving each run's marginal randomness.

    Monte Carlo synthesis¤

    Sampling begins with a target and query, not an algorithm name. Direct, rejection, and importance methods trade tractability, support coverage, and weight or acceptance variance. Variance reduction redesigns randomness or uses known structure; it must be evaluated for the actual estimator rather than only for visually plausible samples.
