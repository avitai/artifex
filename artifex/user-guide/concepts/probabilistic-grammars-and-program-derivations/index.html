    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    A grammar does more than declare which objects are legal. Once its alternatives carry probabilities or nonnegative weights, it defines a law over constructive histories: parse trees, abstract-syntax trees, graph-rewrite derivations, or programs assembled from reusable primitives. Executing a derivation or reading its frontier produces an observation. Because several derivations can produce the same observation, the probability of the observation is usually a sum over hidden structures.

    That simple idea reaches from probabilistic context-free grammars (PCFGs) and inside–outside inference to molecular graph grammars, Bayesian program learning, grammar-constrained language models, and GFlowNets over partial derivations. It also creates obligations that a purely syntactic account can hide. Recursive rule probabilities may leave mass on infinite derivations; grammar ambiguity changes the observation likelihood; an executable program may fail, diverge, or be semantically equivalent to many other programs; and a decoder that forbids invalid tokens need not sample the original model conditioned on validity.

    This volume develops the family from those probability semantics. It treats a grammar, neural network, parser, constrained sampler, and execution engine as different components. The family-defining object is the distribution over derivations and their yields or executions—not the particular architecture used to score the next rule.

    Curriculum position

    Probability and Generative Modeling supplies marginalization and conditional probability. Classical Generative Models supplies latent-variable and dynamic-programming precedents. Autoregressive Models, Probabilistic Circuits, and Generative Flow Networks become adjacent families once a derivation order, finite chart, or constructive state graph is made explicit. The concise Extended Generative Landscape remains the orientation route.

    1. Derivations, Yields, and Probability Mass¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes discrete probability, trees, and finite sums. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Identify the random object: distinguish a grammar's language, a law over derivations, and the induced law over observed yields or executions.
    2. O2 — Compute tree and yield mass: multiply local rule probabilities for a named finite derivation and sum compatible derivations for an observation.
    3. O3 — Treat ambiguity as latent structure: separate recognition, Viterbi decoding, marginal likelihood, and the posterior over derivations.
    4. O4 — Audit recursive consistency: solve a finite-termination fixed point and distinguish almost-sure termination from finite expected size.
    5. O5 — Normalize weighted grammars: distinguish local rule normalization, a finite global partition function, and probability lost to infinite paths.
    6. O6 — Transfer the semantic contract: specify the derivation, observation map, normalization, termination, and ambiguity policy for a new domain.

    From a context-free grammar to a PCFG¤

    A context-free grammar rewrites one nonterminal at a time. A PCFG associates a probability \(\theta_{A\to\beta}\) with each production and normally requires

    \[ \sum_{r:\operatorname{lhs}(r)=A}\theta_r=1 \qquad\text{for every productive nonterminal }A. \]

    For a finite derivation tree \(t\), the standard context-free independence assumption gives

    \[ \boxed{p(t)=\prod_{r\in t}\theta_r,} \]

    where repeated uses of a rule contribute repeated factors. This is a probability law over trees only if recursive expansion terminates almost surely. The grammar's string law is the pushforward through the yield map:

    \[ \boxed{ p(x)=\sum_{t:\operatorname{yield}(t)=x}p(t). } \]

    The tree is therefore a discrete latent variable. If the grammar is ambiguous, replacing the sum by the most probable tree changes the model.

    Ambiguity is probability, not parser noise¤

    Two trees may have the same terminal frontier because productions associate in different ways, use different latent categories, or construct different hidden objects that serialize identically. Ambiguity can be intentional: it lets the model represent alternative explanations. It can also make parameters weakly identified and inference expensive.

    Keep four quantities separate:

    1. recognition, whether any derivation yields \(x\);
    2. the probability of one named derivation \(p(t)\);
    3. the marginal yield probability \(p(x)\); and
    4. the posterior over derivations \(p(t\mid x)=p(t)/p(x)\) for compatible \(t\).

    The maximum-probability derivation need not yield the maximum-probability string, because a string supported by many moderate-probability trees can have more total mass than one supported by a single high-probability tree.

    Local rule normalization does not prove global consistency¤

    Consider the one-nonterminal grammar

    \[ S\to SS\quad\text{with probability }\rho, \qquad S\to a\quad\text{with probability }1-\rho. \]

    Every expansion chooses a locally normalized alternative. Yet the number of child nonterminals has mean \(2\rho\). The finite-termination probability \(q\) is the smallest solution in \([0,1]\) of

    \[ q=(1-\rho)+\rho q^2. \]

    Hence

    \[ q= \begin{cases} 1, & \rho\le \tfrac12,\\[2mm] \dfrac{1-\rho}{\rho}, & \rho>\tfrac12. \end{cases} \]

    At \(\rho=0.6\), only \(q=2/3\) of the mass reaches a finite string; the remaining \(1/3\) belongs to infinite derivations. At the critical value \(\rho=1/2\), termination still occurs almost surely, but expected tree size is infinite. This branching-process boundary is why a maximum length or emergency stop in an implementation is not merely an engineering detail: it changes the sampled law unless the model already conditions on that event.

    For a multitype PCFG, termination probabilities form a system of polynomial fixed-point equations. Spectral-radius tests on the mean progeny matrix give a standard consistency criterion under the corresponding accessibility and branching-process assumptions. The exact assumptions must accompany the test. Booth and Thompson's consistency theory and later algorithmic consistency work make this global requirement explicit.

    The following audit keeps three statements separate. Local normalization is a property of one expansion decision; almost-sure termination is a property of the infinite recursive process; finite expected cost is stronger still.

    Weighted grammars and partition functions¤

    A weighted context-free grammar assigns \(w_r\ge0\) without requiring local sums of one. A tree has weight \(w(t)=\prod_{r\in t}w_r\). If the total finite weight

    \[ Z_S=\sum_{t\text{ finite from }S}w(t) \]

    is finite and positive, then \(p(t)=w(t)/Z_S\). For a grammar without problematic cycles, the subtree normalizers satisfy formal fixed-point equations

    \[ Z_A=\sum_{A\to\beta}w_{A\to\beta} \prod_{B\in\beta\cap\mathcal N}Z_B, \]

    with one factor per nonterminal occurrence. A finite solution is not guaranteed. PCFGs and weighted grammars are therefore related but not interchangeable: local normalization, global normalization, and almost-sure termination are three separate claims.

    Complete worked example: an ambiguous finite yield¤

    Set \(\rho=1/3\) in the binary grammar above. A tree yielding aa uses one binary rule and two terminal rules, so

    \[ p(\texttt{aa})=\frac13\left(\frac23\right)^2=\frac4{27}. \]

    The string aaa has two binary tree shapes. Each uses two binary rules and three terminal rules:

    \[ p(t_1)=p(t_2)= \left(\frac13\right)^2\left(\frac23\right)^3 =\frac8{243}. \]

    Consequently,

    \[ p(\texttt{aaa})=p(t_1)+p(t_2)=\frac{16}{243}, \qquad p(t_1\mid\texttt{aaa})=p(t_2\mid\texttt{aaa})=\frac12. \]

    Returning \(8/243\) as the string probability would silently replace marginalization by Viterbi decoding.

Full credit names all six requested layers and does not equate parseability with chemistry.

    Counterexamples and misconception checks¤

    • “A CFG is a generative probability model.” A CFG defines support. It needs weights, a normalization contract, and a sampling process to define a law.
    • “Rule probabilities sum to one, so the grammar is normalized.” Recursive mass can escape to infinite derivations.
    • “One string has one parse.” Ambiguous grammars deliberately permit many derivations with the same yield.
    • “Syntactic validity implies semantic validity.” A parseable program can still be ill typed, raise an exception, diverge, or violate a domain law.
    Retrieval question: where is the latent variable?

    The derivation tree is latent when only its yield or execution result is observed. The observation likelihood sums the probabilities of every compatible finite derivation; selecting one parse answers a different query.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    A probabilistic grammar assigns mass first to constructive derivations and then pushes that mass through a many-to-one observation map. Ambiguity requires a sum, and recursion requires a termination proof. Chapter 2 turns those sums into dynamic programs for likelihoods, posteriors, constraints, and exact samples.

    2. Dynamic Programming, Conditioning, and Sampling¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Chapter 1 and basic dynamic programming. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Derive inside inference: construct the lexical base cases and the binary span recurrence for a finite PCFG chart.
    2. O2 — Use outside mass: compute posterior span or rule events and explain why their marginals are not independent tree decisions.
    3. O3 — Select the query algebra: distinguish recognition, marginalization, Viterbi decoding, stable log computation, and expectation calculations.
    4. O4 — Sample the declared law: use chart masses to sample posterior trees and distinguish that procedure from ancestral or best-tree generation.
    5. O5 — Condition globally: augment states for length or formal constraints and state when rejection, masking, and product construction are exact.
    6. O6 — Connect charts to circuits: identify the finite compilation and transfer the recurrence to a new structured query without erasing cycles or complexity assumptions.

    Inside probabilities¤

    Let \(x=x_0\ldots x_{n-1}\). For a binary PCFG, define

    \[ \alpha[A,i,j] =p(A\Rightarrow^*x_i\ldots x_{j-1}). \]

    Lexical rules initialize spans of length one:

    \[ \alpha[A,i,i+1]=\theta_{A\to x_i}. \]

    For a longer span,

    \[ \boxed{ \alpha[A,i,j] =\sum_{A\to BC}\theta_{A\to BC} \sum_{k=i+1}^{j-1} \alpha[B,i,k]\alpha[C,k,j]. } \]

    The marginal likelihood is \(p(x)=\alpha[S,0,n]\). A dense implementation has cubic dependence on sequence length; its dependence on the number of symbols and rules depends on rule sparsity and factorization. Unary and nullable cycles require closure or fixed-point treatment rather than insertion into this acyclic recurrence.

    Outside probabilities and posterior events¤

    The outside value \(\beta[A,i,j]\) collects the mass of every derivation context from the start symbol down to an occurrence of \(A\) spanning \(i{:}j\), excluding the subtree below that occurrence. Inside and outside values give posterior span marginals:

    \[ p(A\text{ spans }i{:}j\mid x) =\frac{\alpha[A,i,j]\beta[A,i,j]}{p(x)}. \]

    Combining the parent outside value, a rule probability, and the sibling inside value gives expected rule-use counts. These expectations power EM, uncertainty visualization, and posterior sampling. They are not independent probabilities: one tree contains mutually constrained spans and rules.

    The classical inside–outside estimator is dynamic-programming EM for PCFGs, not a different model family.

    expectation semiring probability plus sufficient statistics
    k-best or forest algebra packed alternatives

    Semiring parsing formalizes this reuse. It does not mean every algebra supports every optimization or that numerical properties are identical.

    Complete worked example: inside versus Viterbi¤

    Return to \(S\to SS\) with probability \(1/3\) and \(S\to a\) with probability \(2/3\). For aaa, the lexical cells are \(2/3\). A length-two cell is

    \[ \alpha[S,0,2]=\frac13\frac23\frac23=\frac4{27}. \]

    The length-three cell has two split points:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \alpha[S,0,3] &=\frac13\left[ \alpha[S,0,1]\alpha[S,1,3] +\alpha[S,0,2]\alpha[S,2,3] \right]\\ &=\frac13\left[ \frac23\frac4{27}+\frac4{27}\frac23 \right] =\frac{16}{243}. \end{aligned} \]

    A max-product chart returns one tree with mass \(8/243\). The sum-product chart returns the string probability \(16/243\). Both are correct answers to different queries.

    Sampling trees, strings, and posterior parses¤

    Unconditional ancestral sampling expands nonterminals according to rule probabilities. It is exact only when the grammar is consistent and the expansion policy does not alter rule probabilities. To sample a parse from \(p(t\mid x)\), start from the root chart item and recursively sample a rule and split with probability proportional to

    \[ \theta_{A\to BC}\alpha[B,i,k]\alpha[C,k,j]. \]

    To condition on length \(n\), build an inside chart indexed by generated length and sample rule decompositions from the resulting conditional masses. Rejecting unwanted lengths is exact in principle but can be arbitrarily inefficient; hard truncation without renormalization is not exact conditioning.

    Constraints by product construction¤

    Suppose a grammar-generated string must also belong to a regular language recognized by a finite automaton. Chart states can be augmented with automaton start and end states. The resulting product dynamic program computes

    \[ P_G(X\in L) \]

    and can sample from \(p_G(x\mid x\in L)\) when this probability is positive. This is global conditioning because the completion mass is retained. It differs from merely masking locally invalid next tokens. More general grammar intersections can be expensive or fall outside context-free closure.

    Work on SCFG probabilities of regular languages studies the numerical fixed-point problem and its assumptions.

    Packed forests and circuit compilation¤

    For a fixed observation, the chart is a directed acyclic sum-product graph: alternative rules and split points are sum nodes, while compatible child items are products. This is an algebraic equivalence to a probabilistic circuit for that instantiated parse forest. The grammar remains a recursive reusable specification across all observations; the chart is one finite compilation.

    3. Estimation, Neural Parameterization, and Grammar Induction¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes likelihood, EM, and variational inference. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Estimate observed-tree rules: derive locally normalized maximum- likelihood or smoothed rule probabilities from declared counts.
    2. O2 — Perform soft latent-tree learning: compute posterior expected counts and distinguish inside–outside EM from hard-parse training.
    3. O3 — Audit neural parameterization: explain which rule contractions stay exact and which expressivity or complexity choices a neural score introduces.
    4. O4 — Locate approximation: separate exact conditional tree sums from the approximate continuous-latent integral in compound grammars.
    5. O5 — Diagnose identifiability: test symbol duplication, collapse, annotation dependence, and the mismatch between likelihood and tree quality.
    6. O6 — Design and transfer an evaluation: match density, structure, symbol use, and compute evidence to a newly learned grammar formalism.

    Observed derivations: count and normalize¤

    If every training tree is observed, the PCFG log likelihood is

    \[ \ell(\theta)=\sum_r c_r\log\theta_r, \]

    where \(c_r\) is the total rule count. Subject to local normalization, maximum likelihood gives

    \[ \widehat\theta_{A\to\beta} =\frac{c_{A\to\beta}} {\sum_{r:\operatorname{lhs}(r)=A}c_r}. \]

    Dirichlet priors provide smoothing. Rule-count estimation is simple because the tree exposes every local choice. It does not determine whether the grammar structure, nonterminal inventory, or observation encoding is appropriate.

    Hidden derivations: inside–outside EM¤

    With yields but no trees, the log likelihood contains a log of a sum over derivations. EM alternates:

    1. E-step: use inside–outside to compute posterior expected rule counts;
    2. M-step: normalize those expected counts for each left-hand side.

    EM does not decrease the data likelihood when its E- and M-steps are exact, but it can converge to local optima or saddle points. Unused rules can remain dead; large latent symbol sets can duplicate one another; and a better likelihood can correspond to a less linguistically meaningful tree inventory.

    Complete worked example: one ambiguous E-step¤

    Consider

    \[ \begin{array}{lll} S\to AB & (0.5), & S\to BA\quad(0.5),\\ A\to a & (0.9), & A\to b\quad(0.1),\\ B\to a & (0.2), & B\to b\quad(0.8). \end{array} \]

    For the observed string ab, the two tree probabilities are

    \[ p(t_{AB},\texttt{ab})=0.5(0.9)(0.8)=0.36, \]

    and

    \[ p(t_{BA},\texttt{ab})=0.5(0.2)(0.1)=0.01. \]

    Therefore \(p(\texttt{ab})=0.37\) and

    \[ p(t_{AB}\mid\texttt{ab})=\frac{36}{37}, \qquad p(t_{BA}\mid\texttt{ab})=\frac1{37}. \]

    The E-step assigns fractional expected counts, not a hard parse. For example, the expected count of \(S\to AB\) is \(36/37\). A hard-EM update that assigns it count one discards posterior uncertainty and optimizes a different objective.

    Neural PCFGs¤

    A neural PCFG can embed symbols and score rules before normalizing them:

    \[ \theta_{A\to BC} =\frac{\exp s_\phi(A,B,C)} {\sum_{B',C'}\exp s_\phi(A,B',C')}. \]

    The chart still enforces context-free composition and exactly marginalizes trees for a fixed set of rule probabilities. The neural network shares statistical strength and changes the parameterization; it does not turn the inside algorithm into an approximation.

    Low-rank tensor factorization can reduce the symbol-dependent cost of binary rule contractions. Tensor-decomposed neural PCFGs demonstrate this trade-off. Rank is both a computational and expressivity choice, so a speed claim must state symbol count, rank, sentence length, and implementation.

    Global latents and conditional tractability¤

    A compound PCFG introduces a sentence-level latent \(z\):

    \[ z\sim p(z), \qquad t\mid z\sim\operatorname{PCFG}(\theta(z)), \qquad x=\operatorname{yield}(t). \]

    For fixed \(z\), the tree sum remains an exact inside computation. Marginally,

    \[ p(x)=\int p(z)\sum_{t:\operatorname{yield}(t)=x}p(t\mid z)\,dz, \]

    and the integral generally needs variational or Monte Carlo approximation. Compound PCFGs use amortized variational inference for \(z\) while collapsing the discrete trees conditionally. “Exact tree marginalization” must not be inflated into “exact model inference.”

    Grammar induction and identifiability¤

    Grammar induction learns hidden categories and structures from strings or weak supervision. Several obstacles recur:

    • renaming latent symbols leaves the observed law unchanged;
    • splitting one category into duplicate categories can preserve or nearly preserve the yield distribution;
    • different grammars can define the same language or yield probabilities;
    • likelihood can favor structural explanations that disagree with a chosen linguistic annotation scheme; and
    • neural parameterizations may underuse or collapse latent symbols.

    The 2025 EMNLP analysis of probability-distribution collapse in compact neural grammars is evidence about particular parameterizations and multilingual parsing protocols, not a theorem that all neural PCFGs collapse.

    Report held-out likelihood and structural measures separately. Unlabeled span F1 depends on a treebank convention and does not establish recovery of a unique true grammar.

    Beyond context-free rules¤

    Lexicalized PCFGs, tree-substitution grammars, adaptor grammars, synchronous grammars, tree-adjoining grammars, hyperedge-replacement grammars, and attribute grammars weaken different independence assumptions or change the generated object. More expressivity can increase inference cost or require approximation. The formalism, probability normalization, and tractable queries must be named; “grammar-based” is too broad to predict any of them.

    4. Program Distributions and Execution Semantics¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes latent-variable models and basic programming-language ideas. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Define a program law: specify a normalized distribution over finite abstract-syntax trees and its domain-specific language.
    2. O2 — Push mass through execution: compute an induced output probability by summing or integrating over programs and execution randomness.
    3. O3 — Separate validity layers: distinguish syntax, static semantics, dynamic safety, functional behavior, and scientific adequacy.
    4. O4 — Combine priors, likelihoods, and search: identify which component defines the target law and which only proposes or verifies candidates.
    5. O5 — Audit termination and equivalence: distinguish construction from execution termination and aggregate mass over declared behavioral classes.
    6. O6 — Transfer the program contract: design a resource-accounted program generator whose posterior and evaluation object are unambiguous.

    A grammar over programs¤

    Let a typed domain-specific language (DSL) provide primitives and production rules. Sampling a derivation produces an abstract syntax tree \(P\):

    \[ P\sim p_\theta(P). \]

    Execution maps the syntax tree and an input or random seed \(u\) to an output:

    \[ y=\operatorname{Exec}(P,u). \]

    If execution is stochastic with \(u\sim p(u\mid P)\), the induced observation law is

    \[ \boxed{ p(y)=\sum_P p_\theta(P) \int p(u\mid P) \mathbf 1\!\left\{\operatorname{Exec}(P,u)=y\right\}\,du. } \]

    For continuous outputs, the indicator is replaced by the appropriate density or pushforward measure. This sum is usually harder than parsing a string because execution can be many-to-one, discontinuous, expensive, or partial.

    Syntax, static semantics, and behavior¤

    Program validity has layers:

    Table GRAM.4. Syntax, static semantics, and behavior
    Layer Example question Typical mechanism
    lexical/syntactic does the token sequence parse? CFG or parser
    static semantics are variables scoped and types compatible? attributes, types, symbol table
    dynamic safety does execution avoid exceptions and resource violations? interpreter, sandbox, abstract interpretation
    functional behavior does it satisfy examples or a specification? tests, theorem prover, constraint solver
    scientific adequacy is the program a plausible explanation? posterior, predictive checks, domain review

    A context-free grammar usually guarantees only the first layer. Attribute or type-directed generation can enforce some static constraints. No finite syntax mask proves total functional correctness for an unrestricted programming language.

    Complete worked example: many programs, one result¤

    Consider the expression DSL

    \[ E\to\operatorname{add}(E,E)\quad(0.4), \qquad E\to 1\quad(0.6). \]

    Execution returns the integer sum of all leaves. The program 1 returns one with probability \(0.6\). The only program returning two is add(1,1), with probability

    \[ 0.4(0.6)^2=0.144. \]

    Two syntax trees return three:

    add(1, add(1, 1))
add(add(1, 1), 1)

    Each has probability \(0.4^2 0.6^3=0.03456\), so

    \[ p(Y=3)=2(0.03456)=0.06912. \]

    The posterior over programs given \(Y=3\) is uniform over these two trees. If the DSL later adds identities such as add(E,0) or multiplication by one, many more semantically equivalent programs may support the same result.

    Priors, likelihoods, and Bayesian program learning¤

    A common posterior has the form

    \[ p(P\mid D)\propto p(P)\,p(D\mid P). \]

    The prior can favor short descriptions, reusable library components, typed productions, or domain-specific motifs. The likelihood may be deterministic agreement with examples, a noisy observation model, or a differentiable renderer. Shorter programs are not automatically truer; description length depends on the chosen language and library.

    Human-level concept learning through probabilistic program induction and memoised wake--sleep illustrate different domains and inference systems. Their empirical results are not universal sample-efficiency guarantees for arbitrary DSLs.

    Search, amortization, and library learning¤

    Exact enumeration is possible only in small, strongly bounded DSLs. Practical systems combine enumerative search, beam search, SMC, MCMC, constraint solving, neural proposals, or cached candidate sets. A recognition model \(q_\phi(P\mid D)\) can prioritize promising programs without becoming the prior \(p(P)\) itself.

    Library learning adds reusable abstractions and therefore changes the language, prior, and program lengths. Bayesian program learning by decompiling amortized knowledge reports an ICML 2024 DreamCoder extension that uses a learned search policy to propose library components. Comparisons must include search budget, timeout, DSL, library-update schedule, and the number of solutions retained.

    Termination and resource-bounded semantics¤

    There are two termination questions:

    1. does the grammar finish constructing a finite program? and
    2. does the constructed program finish executing?

    A subcritical construction grammar can still generate a looping program. A strongly normalizing DSL can guarantee execution termination while its recursive program generator remains inconsistent. Timeouts convert execution into a resource-bounded partial observation map; timeout must be recorded as an outcome or explicitly conditioned away.

    Program equivalence and posterior multiplicity¤

    Many programs can compute the same function. Commutativity, dead code, variable renaming, refactoring, and library identities create equivalence classes. In a general expressive language, exact semantic equivalence is not a tractable generic deduplication operation. Bounded finite DSLs can admit canonical forms or exhaustive behavioral signatures, but those are additional assumptions.

    Posterior mass over functions is

    \[ p(f\mid D)=\sum_{P:\llbracket P\rrbracket=f}p(P\mid D), \]

    not the probability of one shortest representative. Evaluation must therefore say whether diversity is measured over strings, syntax trees, canonical forms, or behavior.

    Grammar-aware latent-variable models¤

    A grammar VAE encodes an observation or parse tree into \(z\) and decodes a rule sequence while masking productions that cannot expand the current nonterminal. The grammar provides syntactic support; the VAE provides a learned latent prior and amortized posterior. Grammar VAE demonstrated this construction for expressions and molecules.

    Attribute-guided decoders can propagate inherited and synthesized information while constructing a tree. Syntax-Directed VAE uses this idea to enforce a declared collection of syntax-directed constraints. Such constraints are stronger than CFG validity but remain bounded by the attributes and checks actually encoded.

    Hypergraph grammars can preserve graph-specific invariants during construction. Molecular Hypergraph Grammar reports method-specific molecular validity and optimization results. Foundation Molecular Grammar, ICML 2025, uses multimodal foundation models in grammar induction. Neither result means an arbitrary learned graph grammar captures all chemical feasibility or synthesis constraints.

    Autoregressive yield models plus formal constraints¤

    Let a base autoregressive model assign \(p_\theta(v\mid x_{<i})\). A completion checker returns the next tokens \(V_L(x_{<i})\) that keep at least one path to the formal language \(L\). A common constrained decoder samples from

    \[ q(v\mid x_{<i})= \frac{p_\theta(v\mid x_{<i})\mathbf1\{v\in V_L(x_{<i})\}} {\sum_{v'\in V_L(x_{<i})}p_\theta(v'\mid x_{<i})}. \]

    This guarantees syntactic support when tokenization, parser state, end-of-string handling, and numerical implementation are correct. In general it does not equal the globally conditioned law

    \[ p_\theta(x\mid x\in L) =\frac{p_\theta(x)\mathbf1\{x\in L\}} {P_\theta(X\in L)}. \]

    Global conditioning weights each prefix by its total valid completion mass. Local renormalization ignores that mass unless the procedure computes an exact lookahead or equivalent Doob transform.

    Complete worked example: local masks change valid-string ratios¤

    Suppose a two-token base model has

    \[ P(X_1=a)=P(X_1=b)=0.5, \]

    with

    \[ P(a\mid a)=0.9, \quad P(b\mid a)=0.1, \quad P(a\mid b)=P(b\mid b)=0.5. \]

    Thus

    \[ q(\texttt{aa})=0.5, \qquad q(\texttt{ba})=q(\texttt{bb})=0.25. \]

    The base model conditioned globally on validity instead gives

    \[ p(\texttt{aa}\mid L)=\frac{0.45}{0.95}, \qquad p(\texttt{ba}\mid L)=p(\texttt{bb}\mid L)=\frac{0.25}{0.95}. \]

    Both laws generate only valid strings, but they are different distributions. Grammar-constrained decoding and GRAMMAR-LLM provide particular algorithms and task evidence; their support guarantees should not be misreported as preservation of every base-model probability ratio.

    Constraints for non-left-to-right generation¤

    Masked diffusion generates tokens in arbitrary orders, so a prefix parser is insufficient. The 2026 ICLR work on constrained decoding of diffusion LLMs with context-free grammars formulates an additive-infilling feasibility problem and reduces it to intersection with a regular language. It reports near-perfect syntactic correctness on named code and structured-extraction tasks with its stated solver. This is a Peer-reviewed method result, not a proof that every diffusion sampler can enforce arbitrary semantic constraints cheaply or preserve its unconstrained conditional law.

    Grammar charts and probabilistic circuits¤

    An inside chart for a fixed input is a sum-product circuit. Reusing low-rank rule tensors also resembles circuit factorization. The correspondence is exact for the compiled computation under a named semiring. It does not make every probabilistic circuit recursively generative or every grammar query tractable: cycles, unbounded length, and grammar form still determine compilation size.

    Grammars and GFlowNets¤

    A partial derivation can be a GFlowNet state; applying a production is an action; a complete tree or program is terminal. With terminal reward \(R(t)\), a correctly trained GFlowNet targets

    \[ P_T(t)=\frac{R(t)}{\sum_{t'}R(t')}. \]

    If several action orders reach the same tree or several trees execute to the same object, trajectory multiplicity must be handled by flow conservation and the chosen terminal identity. Assigning reward to a program and then reporting an object distribution again requires summing over equivalent programs.

    A canonical leftmost expansion turns each ordered tree into one construction trace and yields an autoregressive rule policy. Allowing any expandable node creates multiple traces to the same tree. These are different state graphs even if their terminal support is identical.

    Program priors, neural proposals, and verifiers¤

    Neural-guided program search commonly composes:

    1. a grammar or DSL defining candidate programs;
    2. a prior or learned production policy;
    3. a recognition proposal conditioned on the task;
    4. an executor or verifier;
    5. a search algorithm and resource budget; and
    6. an optional library-learning step.

    Only the first two define the unconditioned program law. A verifier used for rejection, reward, or posterior likelihood induces three different systems. PHOG conditions code productions on learned higher-order AST context; it is not a context-free PCFG despite retaining grammar-based construction.

    Likelihood and structural evaluation¤

    For observed yields, report the fully marginalized log likelihood

    \[ \sum_{i=1}^{N}\log p(x^{(i)}), \]

    with tokenization, vocabulary, length treatment, grammar pruning, and numerical precision. If comparing models with different support, record how impossible or out-of-vocabulary observations are handled.

    For hidden trees, structural scores can include labeled or unlabeled span precision/recall, rule recovery on synthetic data, posterior entropy, and calibration of span probabilities. A treebank is one annotation convention; agreement with it is not equivalent to recovering the only valid latent analysis.

    Validity has layers¤

    Measure at least:

    • finite-construction and finite-execution rates;
    • parser acceptance;
    • type and static-check success;
    • domain-constraint success;
    • exception and timeout rates;
    • test or specification success; and
    • robustness under size, depth, input, and environment shifts.

    Conditional reporting can hide failures. “Ninety percent correct among programs that compiled” must also state the compilation rate and total attempt count.

    Diversity, equivalence, and coverage¤

    Token novelty, unique syntax trees, canonicalized programs, and distinct behaviors are different diversity measures. Report which equivalence relation was used. For program synthesis, pass@\(k\) depends on sampling without or with replacement, number of candidates, filtering, temperature, and test leakage. For molecule or graph grammars, validity, uniqueness, novelty, property distribution, scaffold coverage, and synthesizability remain separate.

    Compute and termination accounting¤

    Report grammar size, active rules, symbol count, chart complexity, pruning, maximum length/depth, rejected or timed-out samples, parser/executor versions, neural parameter counts, search expansions, wall-clock time, memory, and hardware. A grammar can reduce invalid branching while adding parser overhead; the relevant comparison is end-to-end at a matched success criterion.

    Complete worked protocol: a symbolic-expression generator¤

    Suppose two systems generate arithmetic expressions from the same operator and constant vocabulary. One is a grammar VAE; the other is an autoregressive model with a grammar mask. A controlled comparison should:

    1. use identical train/validation/test expressions and canonicalization rules;
    2. declare whether likelihood is over serialized expressions, parse trees, or execution results;
    3. verify normalization or estimate mass lost to truncation;
    4. report parse, type, execution, and held-out-input correctness separately;
    5. measure unique syntax trees and unique behaviors after canonicalization;
    6. stratify by expression depth and length;
    7. match the number of attempted samples and record rejected/time-out cases;
    8. report encoder/decoder/search compute and wall-clock hardware; and
    9. inspect memorization by structural and behavioral nearest-neighbor tests.

    If the masked autoregressive system has 100% parse validity but shifts valid-expression probabilities, report both facts. If the VAE reconstructs well but samples invalid latent regions, separate posterior reconstruction from prior generation.

    Applications and what the grammar contributes¤

    • Natural language: latent syntax, controllable structure, parsing, and language modeling; semantic adequacy remains outside bare CFG support.
    • Code and formal data: AST support, schemas, types, and executable tests; security and functional correctness require stronger checks.
    • Molecules and graphs: graph-rewrite invariants and reusable motifs; chemical feasibility and target properties require domain evaluation.
    • Equations and scientific models: dimensional or operator constraints and interpretable programs; identifiability and extrapolation remain empirical.
    • Scenes, graphics, and shapes: reusable parts, loops, symmetry, and rendering; inverse graphics introduces severe program equivalence.

    Current research, verified through 2026-07-19¤

    The evidence supports several bounded developments:

    • Foundation Molecular Grammar, ICML 2025, uses multimodal foundation models to induce an interpretable molecular graph language and reports synthesizability, diversity, and data-efficiency results on its molecular protocols.
    • Probability Distribution Collapse, EMNLP 2025, identifies and mitigates a compact-neural-PCFG parameterization failure across its named multilingual grammar-induction experiments.
    • GRAMMAR-LLM, ACL Findings 2025, studies an LL(1)-equivalent constraint class and task-specific decoding results; it does not cover arbitrary context-sensitive constraints.
    • Constrained Decoding of Diffusion LLMs with Context-Free Grammars, ICLR 2026, extends formal-language feasibility to arbitrary-order masked infilling under its additive-infilling construction.
    • Inside--Outside for Probabilistic Product-Free Lambek Categorial Grammar, COLING 2025, derives dynamic programming for a different grammar formalism; it is evidence that tractable marginalization can extend beyond PCFGs under stated structural assumptions, not that all richer grammars are tractable.

    These are Peer-reviewed method results. None establishes a universally best grammar formalism, a general semantic-correctness guarantee, or a zero-overhead constraint mechanism.

    Open research questions¤

    1. How can recursive neural grammars remain expressive, compact, identifiable, and almost surely terminating at once?
    2. Which global-conditioning algorithms preserve a base generator's valid-object ratios while remaining practical for large grammars and vocabularies?
    3. How should posterior mass be aggregated across semantically equivalent programs without requiring an impossible universal equivalence oracle?
    4. Can learned grammars extrapolate in depth, size, and composition without losing their declared invariants?
    5. How should grammar induction incorporate domain experts or foundation models while measuring annotation leakage and false constraints?
    6. When does compilation into circuits, automata, or GFlowNet state graphs reduce total computation rather than merely relocate it?

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The correct count is \(0.9(800)=720\), so unconditional success is \(720/1000=72\%\), not above 80%. Compilation coverage is 80%, and the remaining 20% cannot disappear from an all-attempt system claim. Check. Conditional and unconditional rates answer different questions.
    2. P1.2. A compiles \(0.9(2000)=1800\) and succeeds on \(0.8(1800)=1440\). B compiles \(0.75(2000)=1500\) and succeeds on \(0.95(1500)=1425\). B wins conditional correctness, \(95\%\) versus \(80\%\); A wins total successes, 1440 versus 1425, and unconditional success, \(72\%\) versus \(71.25\%\). Check. The conclusion can change with the operational cost assigned to noncompilation.
    3. P1.3. pass@\(5=1-0.8^5=1-0.32768=0.67232\). Real candidates may be correlated, sampled without replacement from a finite set, filtered, generated at changing temperatures, or evaluated with hidden test leakage; any of these breaks the simple model. Check. pass@\(k\) is a protocol statistic, not an intrinsic property of one program.
    4. P1.4. Report 100 distinct serialized strings, 80 formatting-normalized ASTs, 40 algebraic canonical forms, and 25 distinct behaviors. The last count is behavioral coverage on this exhaustive finite domain. Check. It is not automatically whole-function equivalence outside that declared domain.
    5. P1.5. A defensible claim is: “On the named JSON benchmark, tokenizer, model, decoding settings, hardware, and sample budget, this method produced syntax-valid outputs for every evaluated attempt and had lower measured latency than the specified rejection baseline.” Base-law fidelity needs probabilities or distortion against exact conditioning; semantic validity needs schema, type, and task checks; general compute superiority needs matched implementations, solver failures, memory, throughput, scale curves, and additional grammars/hardware. Check. One benchmark cannot establish a universal constraint or speed claim.
    6. P1.6. A complete protocol declares whether probability is over rewrite traces, labeled graphs, or isomorphism classes; reports exact likelihood, a named bound, or an explicit absence of density evaluation; checks finite construction, valence, sanitization, synthesizability, and timeout rates; measures uniqueness and coverage after graph-isomorphism or scaffold canonicalization; evaluates calibrated target properties and held-out distribution shift; retains every attempt and failure; and matches parameter counts, sample budgets, grammar induction, parser/search costs, hardware, wall time, and memory. Rubric. Full credit covers all requested layers and does not use chemical validity as a substitute for target-law fidelity.

    Chapter 6 failure cases and misconception checks¤

    • One hundred percent syntax is not one hundred percent task success. Report every validity layer.
    • Best parser is not best generator. Tree F1, yield likelihood, and sample utility answer different questions.
    • Unique text is not unique behavior. Canonicalize or execute when the task concerns programs.
    • Recent does not mean general. Every modern result remains bounded by its formalism, dataset, constraints, sampler, and compute protocol.
    Retrieval question: what is the minimum constrained-generation report?

    Name the base model, formal language, tokenizer/parser interface, constraint algorithm, target law, validity level, probability distortion or correction, decoding settings, failure rate, compute overhead, and task metric. Validity alone is insufficient.

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Probabilistic grammars and program derivations are best evaluated as layered systems: derivation law, observation map, inference, constraint mechanism, execution, and task protocol. Their distinctive strength is explicit recursive construction with marginalizable or executable structure; their distinctive risks are ambiguity, escaped mass, semantic multiplicity, and hidden distributional changes introduced by search or constraints.
