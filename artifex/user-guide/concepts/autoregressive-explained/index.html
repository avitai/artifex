    An autoregressive probabilistic model chooses an ordering of an object and represents its joint law as normalized conditional distributions, one coordinate or block at a time. The factorization determines which complete-data likelihoods and ancestral samples are tractable; the architecture used to parameterize the conditionals is a separate choice.

    • Sequential Generation

      Generate data one element at a time, predicting each based on all previous elements

    • Tractable Likelihood

      Evaluate the model likelihood exactly when every conditional is normalized and tractable

    • Architecture-Independent Factorization

      Use any architecture (RNNs, CNNs, Transformers) that respects the autoregressive property

    • Multiple State Spaces

      Underpin leading language systems and competitive image, audio, scientific, and multimodal models under specific protocols

    Curriculum position

    The Generative Modeling Landscape classifies autoregressive models relative to VAEs, GANs, diffusion, flows, EBMs, and the book's additional families. This volume develops the autoregressive family in full.

    Overview¤

    Definition and scope¤

    For an ordered object \(x_{1:n}\), an autoregressive model specifies normalized conditionals \(p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{<i})\) whose product defines a normalized joint distribution. The chain-rule identity itself is architecture independent. Neural parameterizations include recurrent networks, masked density estimators and convolutions, Transformers, state-space models, and hybrids. Representative milestones include neural language models (Bengio et al., 2003 — JMLR), the Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), and large-scale autoregressive language modeling (Radford et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020). The same definition also covers continuous, image, audio, scientific, graph, set, and multimodal representations once their ordering and conditional family are declared.

    Overview: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should be comfortable with joint and conditional probability, logarithms, expectations, and categorical distributions. If any of those are unfamiliar, first read Probability and Generative Modeling. The notation \(x_{<i}\) means the ordered prefix \((x_1,\ldots,x_{i-1})\); \(p\) denotes a distribution or density and \(p_\theta\) its parameterized approximation.

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. derive the chain-rule factorization for any chosen ordering;
    2. distinguish exact joint likelihood from marginal, conditional, and MAP inference;
    3. calculate a finite autoregressive joint probability and verify normalization;
    4. distinguish a Markov assumption from a recurrent or masked parameterization;
    5. explain how ordering and masking change the learning problem without changing the probability identity; and
    6. detect target leakage and the common one-position shift error; and
    7. distinguish direct conditional training from joint prefix modeling.

    Consequences of the factorization¤

    The chain-rule construction has several consequences that should be stated separately:

    • Direct likelihood computation - exact model likelihood when each normalized conditional is tractable
    • Simple training - standard maximum likelihood with cross-entropy loss
    • Broad applicability - can model discrete symbols or continuous variables once an ordering, conditional family, and support are specified
    • Flexible expressiveness - from simple next-token prediction to complex long-range dependencies
    • Scalability depends on the parameterization and system - sparse experts, state-space or hybrid backbones, long-context mechanisms, multi-token objectives, and caching change compute without changing the probability identity

    The ordering is part of the model specification. It determines the prefixes presented to each conditional and the sequence used by ancestral sampling. Tractable maximum-likelihood training and exact evaluation of a complete ordered observation do not make arbitrary conditional inference, marginalisation, MAP search, or constrained generation exact.

    Constructive interpretation: building an ordered object¤

    An ancestral sampler implements the factorization as a concrete procedure:

    1. Initialize the order. Draw the first element from the normalized initial conditional \(p_\theta(x_1)\).

    2. Evaluate a prefix conditional. At position \(i\), compute \(p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{<i})\) from the elements already sampled.

    3. Sample and extend. Draw \(x_i\) from that conditional and append it to the prefix. Local conditionals compose into a global joint law through their product.

    4. Terminate or invoke a separate inference procedure. Plain left-to-right ancestral sampling does not revise an emitted element. Infilling, reranking, editing, and blocked sampling require appropriate conditionals, proposals, or search procedures beyond the basic ancestral pass.

    This construction makes complete-data likelihood evaluation parallel across observed positions during teacher-forced training, while ancestral generation remains ordered because each newly sampled value becomes part of the next input.

    Mathematical Foundation¤

    The Chain Rule Factorization¤

    The chain rule of probability is the cornerstone of all autoregressive models. Any joint distribution can be factored as:

    \[ p(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) = p(x_1) \prod_{i=2}^{n} p(x_i \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1}) \tag{AR.1}\label{eq:autoregressive-chain-rule} \]

    Equation \(\eqref{eq:autoregressive-chain-rule}\) is an identity for every ordered joint law; an autoregressive model then parameterizes each conditional \(p(x_i \mid x_{<i})\) with a neural network:

    \[ p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i}) = f_\theta(x_i; x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1}) \]

    where \(\theta\) are learnable parameters and \(x_{<i} = (x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1})\) denotes all previous elements.

    Example - Image with 3 pixels:

    \[ p(x_1, x_2, x_3) = p(x_1) \cdot p(x_2 \mid x_1) \cdot p(x_3 \mid x_1, x_2) \]

    For a 256×256 RGB image with discrete pixel values \(\{0, 1, \ldots, 255\}\):

    \[ p(\text{image}) = \prod_{i=1}^{256 \times 256 \times 3} p(x_i \mid x_{<i}) \]

    The chain rule replaces one flat table with 196,608 conditional modeling problems. This is a useful structural decomposition, but it does not mean the problem now has only 196,608 scalar probabilities: each conditional is a function of a potentially enormous prefix, and its representation and generalization remain the central modeling challenge.

    Direct conditioning and joint prefix modeling are different objectives¤

    Let \(C\) be observed context and \(Y_{1:T}\) the object to generate. A directly conditional autoregressive model specifies

    \[ p_\theta(y_{1:T}\mid c) = \prod_{t=1}^{T}p_\theta(y_t\mid c,y_{<t}). \]

    A joint model with the context first instead specifies

    \[ p_\phi(c,y_{1:T}) =p_\phi(c) \prod_{t=1}^{T}p_\phi(y_t\mid c,y_{<t}). \]

    For that ordering, conditioning the normalized joint on an observed \(c\) cancels the prefix factor \(p_\phi(c)\) and leaves the displayed product over \(Y\). Consequently, one decoder-only architecture can support two different training objectives:

    • applying loss to both context and target tokens fits a joint model of the serialized pair; and
    • masking the context-token losses while exposing those tokens as inputs fits the conditional factors needed for \(p_\theta(y\mid c)\).

    The second objective need not learn the context marginal. This can be desirable when prompts are supplied rather than generated, but it means that its loss is not the joint log-likelihood of the concatenated record. Conversely, a joint objective spends capacity and data on \(p(c)\) even when deployment asks only for \(p(y\mid c)\). Padding, separators, modality tags, and the boundary at which loss begins are therefore parts of the probability specification, not formatting details.

    Worked binary example: the same conditional generator, different joints¤

    Suppose

    \[ p(C=0)=0.75, \qquad p(Y=1\mid C=0)=0.20, \qquad p(Y=1\mid C=1)=0.80. \]

    The resulting joint table is

    Table AR.2. Worked binary example: the same conditional generator, different joints
    \(Y=0\) \(Y=1\) context marginal
    \(C=0\) \(0.60\) \(0.15\) \(0.75\)
    \(C=1\) \(0.05\) \(0.20\) \(0.25\)

    A direct conditional model needs only the two Bernoulli probabilities \(0.20\) and \(0.80\). Replacing the context marginal by \(p'(C=0)=0.5\) while retaining those conditionals leaves every requested generation law \(p'(Y\mid C=c)\) unchanged, but changes all four joint masses. Conditional generation therefore does not identify how often contexts occur.

    Ordering matters to query access as well. An exact reverse factorization \(p(y)p(c\mid y)\) represents the same joint distribution, but obtaining \(p(y\mid c)\) from it generally requires normalizing or otherwise inferring over \(y\); one cannot merely prefix \(c\) to a \(Y\)-first ancestral sampler. Neither factorization is universally easier to learn, and generation order alone does not establish causal direction.

    Prefix Length, Statistical Restriction, and Computation¤

    The argument list of \(p(x_t\mid x_{<t})\) grows with \(t\), but argument count is not the same as intrinsic function complexity. A long prefix may contain much irrelevant information, or it may admit a small sufficient summary. Conversely, a short window can omit the one old variable that determines the next value. The chain rule alone does not decide which case holds.

    The naïve tabular construction exposes the scaling problem. Suppose \(T\) variables share an alphabet of size \(K\). At position \(t\), there are \(K^{t-1}\) prefixes, and a normalized categorical distribution needs \(K-1\) free probabilities at each prefix. Separate unrestricted tables therefore require

    \[ \sum_{t=1}^{T} K^{t-1}(K-1)=K^T-1 \]

    free parameters—the same number as a saturated joint table. For four binary variables, the count is \(1+2+4+8=15\). The chain rule makes normalization and ancestral sampling simple; it does not, by itself, solve statistical estimation. Generalization comes from a justified restriction, parameter sharing, or both.

    Four ways to represent the same prediction interface¤

    For \((X_1,X_2,X_3,X_4)\), compare four constructions. The last two still expose a conditional distribution at every position, but share computation across histories.

    Table AR.3. Four ways to represent the same prediction interface
    Construction Four-variable form What it assumes or shares Known-sequence evaluation Ordinary ancestral state
    Full-prefix tables \(p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)p(x_3\mid x_1,x_2)p(x_4\mid x_1,x_2,x_3)\) No conditional-independence restriction; separate value for every prefix Four table lookups; storage grows as \(2^4-1\) here and \(K^T-1\) generally Store the generated prefix, or enough information to address its table entry
    First-order Markov \(p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)p(x_3\mid x_2)p(x_4\mid x_3)\) \(X_t\perp X_{<t-1}\mid X_{t-1}\); position-specific tables use seven binary parameters, while a tied transition uses three including \(p(x_1)\) Four local lookups; all targets are known during scoring Retain the latest value
    Deterministic recurrent state \(h_t=f_\theta(h_{t-1},x_t)\) and \(p_\theta(x_{t+1}\mid h_t)\) Shares an update and output map; the state is a learned summary rather than a declared finite observation window A general nonlinear recurrence has a length-\(T\) dependency path; some structured recurrences admit parallel scans Retain \(h_t\); its dimension may be fixed even though the represented history is not a fixed-order Markov window
    Direct causal masked network \((\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_T)=F_\theta(x_{1:T})\), with \(\eta_t\) forbidden from using \(x_{t:T}\) Shares one network across positions; a full causal receptive field permits every prefix dependency Teacher forcing exposes all conditional parameters in one masked pass; arithmetic depends on the architecture Retain the prefix or an architecture-specific cache; generation still has \(T\) dependent draws in the basic factorization

    Here \(\eta_t\) denotes logits or other normalized conditional parameters. A causal mask is an information-flow constraint: it removes the present target and future from the input to \(\eta_t\). With a full causal receptive field, it does not assert that any earlier variable is irrelevant. A local convolutional mask, finite attention window, or bounded cache adds a stronger restriction.

    The diagram is an audit of the four prototypes, not a claim that real systems must select exactly one branch. A hybrid can, for example, combine recurrent state with local masked attention; its distributional restrictions are determined by the complete information path.

    Assumption versus architecture¤

    The distinction is easiest to audit by asking whether a choice removes distributions from the model family before any neural-network capacity limit is considered.

    Table AR.4. Assumption versus architecture
    Choice Distributional restriction? What can still go wrong?
    Full chain-rule conditionals No: this is a probability identity Separate tables have exponential storage and sparse context counts
    Order-\(k\) Markov conditionals Yes: histories with the same last \(k\) values must have the same next-value law Bias persists with infinite data when an older value matters
    Fixed-dimensional recurrent state Architecture-dependent: all influence must pass through \(h_t\), but this is not the same as an observed order-\(k\) assumption Finite precision, update design, optimization, and length generalization can prevent the state from retaining the needed property
    Full causal attention or a full-receptive-field mask No additional conditional independence beyond causality, before finite-capacity limits Compute, memory, positional representation, and optimization can still limit effective context use
    Local mask, finite window, or truncated cache Yes at that layer or system boundary unless other paths restore the missing dependency A relevant distant variable becomes structurally unreachable

    Calling every recurrent model “Markov” hides an important level distinction. The augmented computation is first-order in its state, \(h_t=f(h_{t-1},x_t)\), but the observable conditional may depend on the entire prefix through \(h_t\). Likewise, calling a masked network “fully non-Markov” is too strong when its actual receptive field is finite.

    Worked four-bit example: long dependence, one-bit state¤

    Let \(X_1,X_2,X_3\) be independent fair bits and set

    \[ X_4=X_1\mathbin{\oplus}X_2\mathbin{\oplus}X_3, \]

    where \(\oplus\) is exclusive OR. The last conditional is deterministic:

    Table AR.5. Worked four-bit example: long dependence, one-bit state
    Prefix \((x_1x_2x_3)\) 000 001 010 011 100 101 110 111
    \(p(X_4=1\mid x_1,x_2,x_3)\) 0 1 1 0 1 0 0 1

    A first-order Markov model cannot reproduce this joint. Given either value of \(X_3\), the parity of the two independent earlier bits is still fair, so

    \[ p(X_4=1\mid X_3=0)=p(X_4=1\mid X_3=1)=\frac12. \]

    The Markov approximation therefore discards a deterministic relationship even with unlimited data. Yet a recurrent model needs only one bit of state:

    \[ h_0=0,\qquad h_t=h_{t-1}\mathbin{\oplus}X_t\quad(t=1,2,3), \qquad p(X_4=1\mid h_3)=h_3. \]

    A full-prefix table and a full causal masked network can also represent the rule. This counterexample makes both lessons concrete: dependence on every prefix variable need not require a large sufficient state, and a small recent window is not interchangeable with a small recurrent state.

    Evidence boundary: recurrent state is not one capability class

    The one-bit construction proves that some recurrence can represent parity; it does not prove that every gated RNN or state-space layer will learn or even express the same length-generalizing update under its parameter and precision constraints. Sarrof, Veitsman & Hahn (NeurIPS 2024) derive formal-language boundaries for specified modern SSM classes. Mamba-3 (ICLR 2026 Oral) changes the recurrent dynamics and reports different state-tracking and parity behavior under its experimental protocol. These are architecture-specific results, not revisions to the chain rule.

    Worked example: a normalized three-bit model¤

    Let \(x_1,x_2,x_3\in\{0,1\}\). Specify the Bernoulli probability of 1 at every reachable prefix:

    Table AR.7. Worked example: a normalized three-bit model
    Conditional Probability of 1
    \(p(x_1=1)\) \(0.6\)
    \(p(x_2=1\mid x_1=0)\), \(p(x_2=1\mid x_1=1)\) \(0.2,\ 0.7\)
    \(p(x_3=1\mid 00),p(x_3=1\mid 01),p(x_3=1\mid 10),p(x_3=1\mid 11)\) \(0.1,\ 0.4,\ 0.5,\ 0.9\)

    The probability of the event \((1,1,1)\) is

    \[ p(1,1,1)=0.6\times0.7\times0.9=0.378. \]

    For any prefix, the probabilities of the two next values sum to one. Therefore the full joint is normalized:

    \[ \sum_{x_1,x_2,x_3}p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)p(x_3\mid x_1,x_2) =\sum_{x_1}p(x_1)\sum_{x_2}p(x_2\mid x_1)\sum_{x_3}p(x_3\mid x_1,x_2)=1. \]

    This telescoping argument is the essential reason locally normalized conditionals define a normalized joint. Reordering the variables—for example, \(x_3,x_1,x_2\)—gives another exact factorization of the same true joint. With finite data and a restricted neural architecture, however, different orderings induce different approximation and optimization problems.

    Log-Likelihood and Training¤

    The log-likelihood decomposes additively:

    \[ \log p_\theta(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i}) \]

    This makes maximum likelihood training straightforward:

    \[ \max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i})\right] \]

    Equivalently, minimize the negative log-likelihood (cross-entropy):

    \[ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p_\theta(x_i^{(j)} \mid x_{<i}^{(j)}) \]

    where \(N\) is the dataset size.

    With base-two logarithms and discrete normalized conditionals, the same sum is the sequence's ideal lossless code length under the model. An arithmetic, range, or ANS coder can consume the predicted conditional tables in order; model mismatch adds expected redundancy equal to a forward KL. This interpretation requires a concrete discrete alphabet and coding protocol—the four-direction prefix-code example develops the finite case before extending it to sequences.

    What is exact—and what is not

    For discrete observations with normalized categorical conditionals, teacher-forced training evaluates the exact model log-likelihood as a sum of cross-entropies. For continuous observations, each conditional is a normalized density rather than a vocabulary classification. Exact evaluation of a complete ordered observation does not automatically make missing-data marginalization, arbitrary conditional sampling, constrained decoding, or MAP search tractable.

    Ordering and Masking¤

    Choosing an ordering is crucial. Different orderings lead to different models:

    Text (Natural Sequential Order):

    \[ p(\text{"hello"}) = p(\text{h}) \cdot p(\text{e}|\text{h}) \cdot p(\text{l}|\text{he}) \cdot p(\text{l}|\text{hel}) \cdot p(\text{o}|\text{hell}) \]

    Images (Raster Scan):

    Pixels generated left-to-right, top-to-bottom:

    \[ p(\text{image}) = \prod_{h=1}^{H} \prod_{w=1}^{W} \prod_{c=1}^{C} p(x_{h,w,c} \mid x_{<h}, x_{h,<w}, x_{h,w,<c}) \]

    where \(x_{<h}\) denotes all rows above, \(x_{h,<w}\) denotes pixels to the left in current row, and \(x_{h,w,<c}\) denotes previous channels.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    An autoregressive model is first a probability factorization, not a particular neural architecture. Local normalization gives a normalized joint and makes the log-likelihood of a complete ordered observation directly evaluable. Direct conditional training and joint prefix modeling can use the same causal architecture while fitting different probability objects. The chain rule does not reduce the parameter count of a saturated joint: useful models gain statistical strength from justified conditional independence or shared computation. A Markov window changes the distribution family; recurrent state and causal masking describe how allowed prefix information is represented unless their capacity or receptive field imposes an additional restriction. The price remains an imposed order: architecture and representation must enforce it, and ordinary ancestral sampling follows it one decision at a time. “Exact likelihood” must never be expanded into a claim of exact arbitrary inference.

    NADE and MADE: normalized density from shared computation¤

    The chain rule becomes a useful density estimator only after its conditionals are both normalized and computable. Two historically important constructions make that point unusually concrete:

    • the Neural Autoregressive Distribution Estimator (NADE) shares a recursively updated hidden preactivation across binary conditionals (Larochelle & Murray, AISTATS 2011); and
    • the Masked Autoencoder for Distribution Estimation (MADE) removes forbidden network paths so all conditionals of a known vector can be evaluated by one masked feed-forward network (Germain et al., ICML 2015).

    These are not merely autoencoders used for feature learning. Their wiring and output distributions define normalized joints. They also expose three ideas reused throughout modern generative modeling: parameter sharing across conditionals, structural masking, and the difference between parallel scoring and sequential ancestral sampling.

    Binary NADE construction¤

    Let \(x=(x_1,\ldots,x_D)\in\{0,1\}^D\) and let \(o=(o_1,\ldots,o_D)\) be a permutation of the coordinate indices. A one-hidden-layer binary NADE can be written as

    \[ \begin{aligned} a_d &= c+\sum_{j<d}W_{:,o_j}x_{o_j},\\ h_d &= \phi(a_d),\\ q_{o_d} &= \sigma\!\left(b_{o_d}+V_{o_d,:}h_d\right),\\ p_\theta^o(x) &= \prod_{d=1}^{D} q_{o_d}^{x_{o_d}}(1-q_{o_d})^{1-x_{o_d}}. \end{aligned} \]

    Here \(c\in\mathbb R^H\), \(W\in\mathbb R^{H\times D}\), \(V\in\mathbb R^{D\times H}\), and \(b\in\mathbb R^D\). The hidden nonlinearity \(\phi\) is commonly logistic in the original binary construction, while \(\sigma\) ensures that each \(q_i\) is a Bernoulli probability. The coordinate-specific output rows of \(V\) let different conditionals read the shared prefix representation differently.

    The useful recurrence is

    \[ a_{d+1}=a_d+W_{:,o_d}x_{o_d}. \]

    It reuses the prefix computation rather than rebuilding a separate network from scratch for every \(d\). With \(H\) hidden units, the elementary one-layer construction scores a vector in \(O(DH)\) arithmetic. It is still sequential across coordinates because \(a_{d+1}\) depends on the observed or sampled value \(x_{o_d}\).

    The joint is normalized without a global partition function. For every prefix, summing the next Bernoulli factor over \(x_{o_d}\in\{0,1\}\) gives one; eliminating variables from \(o_D\) back to \(o_1\) therefore gives

    \[ \sum_{x\in\{0,1\}^D}p_\theta^o(x)=1. \]

    This proof depends on local normalization, not on the neural network being an autoencoder or on the chosen ordering being natural.

    Continuous conditionals: normalization is an output-law decision¤

    Autoregression does not require a discrete alphabet. An RNADE-style model emits a normalized mixture density for each real-valued coordinate (Uria, Murray & Larochelle):

    All parameters in the following display are functions of the known prefix \(x_{<d}\); suppressing that repeated argument keeps the normalized output law readable:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \pi_{d,1:K} &=\operatorname{softmax}(a_d),\\ \sigma_{d,k} &=\operatorname{softplus}(s_{d,k})+\varepsilon,\\ q_{d,k}(x_d) &=\mathcal N\!\left(x_d;\mu_{d,k},\sigma_{d,k}^2\right),\\ p_\theta(x_d\mid x_{<d}) &=\sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi_{d,k}q_{d,k}(x_d). \end{aligned} \]

    Positive scales and simplex-valued mixture weights make every conditional a proper density. Their product is therefore a normalized joint density without a global partition function. Sampling draws a mixture component and then a value from its Gaussian; evaluating a known vector uses a stable log-sum-exp over components. The architecture may share prefix computation as in NADE or expose all known-prefix parameters through masks as in MADE—the probability argument depends on the output density, not on which network emits its parameters.

    Let

    \[ X_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1) \]

    \[ \begin{aligned} q_+(x_2\mid x_1) &=\mathcal N(x_2;x_1,1/4),\\ q_-(x_2\mid x_1) &=\mathcal N(x_2;-x_1,1),\\ p(x_2\mid x_1) &=\frac14q_++\frac34q_-. \end{aligned} \]

    The joint \(p(x_1,x_2)=p(x_1)p(x_2\mid x_1)\) integrates to one by iterated integration. At \(x_1=2\), the conditional mean is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb E[X_2\mid X_1=2] &=\frac14(2)+\frac34(-2)\\ &=-1. \end{aligned} \]

    while the conditional variance is

    \[ \operatorname{Var}(X_2\mid X_1=2) =\frac{13}{16}+3 =\frac{61}{16}. \]

    The second term is uncertainty between the two component means; replacing the mixture by one Gaussian with only the average component variance would lose it. For quantized pixels, the observation is discrete: a continuous density must be integrated over quantization bins, or a categorical mass must be used. Merely evaluating a density at the integer bin center does not define normalized pixel probabilities.

    MADE construction: a mask is a dependency proof¤

    NADE reuses a prefix state sequentially. MADE instead begins with a feed-forward network and assigns an integer degree to every input and hidden unit. A connection is retained only when its degrees cannot create a path from a coordinate into its own conditional or into an earlier conditional.

    For one hidden layer, let \(m_x(j)\) be the position of input \(x_j\) in the chosen order and let \(m_h(k)\in\{1,\ldots,D-1\}\) be the degree of hidden unit \(h_k\). With rows as receiving units and columns as sending units, use

    \[ (M_{\mathrm{in}})_{k,j}=\mathbf 1\!\left[m_h(k)\ge m_x(j)\right], \qquad (M_{\mathrm{out}})_{i,k}=\mathbf 1\!\left[m_x(i)>m_h(k)\right]. \]

    The strict inequality at the output is essential: it prevents the output for \(x_i\) from seeing \(x_i\). Hidden-to-hidden masks in a deeper MADE use a non-strict degree rule, and the final output mask again uses the strict rule.

    Fixed order, order-agnostic training, and order ensembles¤

    “Orderless” is used loosely in the literature, but three different objects must remain separate.

    1. One fixed-order density. For a declared permutation \(o\),

    \[ p_\theta^o(x)=\prod_{d=1}^{D}p_\theta(x_{o_d}\mid x_{o_{<d}},o) \]

    is one normalized joint distribution. Training and deployment may use the same fixed order. Sampling follows that order, and its likelihood is the sum of the corresponding conditional log-probabilities.

    2. An order-agnostic training objective. Deep NADE and MADE can share parameters across many orders by sampling an order, or a prefix/mask that represents one, during training (Uria, Murray & Larochelle, ICML 2014; Uria et al., JMLR 2016). With an order distribution \(\pi\),

    \[ \mathcal L_{\mathrm{OA}}(\theta) =\mathbb E_{x\sim p_{\mathrm{data}}} \mathbb E_{o\sim\pi}\left[-\log p_\theta^o(x)\right]. \]

    This is an average of normalized-model negative log-likelihoods. It is a training objective, not by itself the likelihood of a single normalized joint. The shared parameters also do not force different orderings to agree on the probability of every \(x\).

    3. A normalized order ensemble. If deployment retains \(K\) orders \(o_1,\ldots,o_K\) with weights \(\pi_k\ge0\) and \(\sum_k\pi_k=1\), then

    \[ p_{\mathrm{mix}}(x)=\sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi_k p_\theta^{o_k}(x) \]

    is a valid mixture. Exact sampling first draws \(k\sim\operatorname{Cat}(\pi)\) and then ancestrally samples from \(p_\theta^{o_k}\). Exact scoring requires a log-sum-exp over component log-densities and costs roughly \(K\) component evaluations unless additional structure is exploited.

    The orderless objective and mixture likelihood are related by Jensen's inequality,

    \[ \mathbb E_{o\sim\pi}\log p_\theta^o(x) \le \log\mathbb E_{o\sim\pi}p_\theta^o(x) =\log p_{\mathrm{mix}}(x), \]

    but they are not equal unless the component probabilities agree at \(x\) (or the order distribution is degenerate).

    Exponentiating the average log-probability gives a geometric pool. Its mass here is \(0.6\), so it is not a normalized distribution. It could be normalized by an additional partition function, but that would define a different model and could destroy the tractability that motivated autoregressive factorization.

    Order-agnostic is not permutation-invariant

    Sampling many coordinate orders during training does not erase coordinate identity, make a model invariant to relabeling, or license switching orders halfway through a sample. A deployed density must declare one factorization or a normalized mixture over complete factorization-specific models. For arbitrary conditioning, an order trained with observed coordinates first can make the desired query tractable, but different orders may still give different answers unless cross-order consistency is separately enforced.

    Terminology boundary: any-order generation is a larger design space

    A generator may also sample or learn which unfilled coordinates to reveal next. That introduces a distribution or policy over generation paths, possibly with blockwise or iterative updates; it is not automatically the fixed-order mixture \(p_{\mathrm{mix}}\) above. Autoregressive Diffusion Models (ICLR 2022) connect order-agnostic autoregression to absorbing discrete diffusion under stated assumptions, and a 2026 GenBio workshop poster uses uniform-permutation decoding for conditional RNA inverse folding. These establish two particular constructions, not a universal equivalence among orderless training, random-order decoding, permutation invariance, and discrete diffusion. The Discrete Iterative Generation guide develops masked states, transition kernels, schedules, and parallel refinement explicitly.

    The same mask in MADE, MAF, and IAF¤

    MADE is a dependency-enforcing network pattern. What its outputs mean determines the probabilistic model.

    • In binary MADE, outputs are Bernoulli probabilities for a discrete autoregressive joint.

    • In a Masked Autoregressive Flow (MAF), outputs are continuous transformation parameters computed from the observed data prefix:

      \[ z_i=(x_i-\mu_i(x_{<i}))\exp[-\alpha_i(x_{<i})]. \]

    Because all of \(x\) is known while scoring, the data-to-base map and its triangular-Jacobian diagonal are exposed in one masked-network evaluation per layer. Sampling must solve for \(x_1,x_2,\ldots\) sequentially.

    • In an Inverse Autoregressive Flow (IAF), outputs depend on the already known base-noise prefix:

      \[ x_i=\mu_i(z_{<i})+z_i\exp[\alpha_i(z_{<i})]. \]

    The base vector \(z\) is available at once, so base-to-sample evaluation is parallel within a layer. Mapping an externally supplied \(x\) back to \(z\) is sequential.

    Misconception check: what exactly did the mask buy?

    Claim: “MADE removes sequential dependence from an autoregressive model.”

    Correction: MADE removes sequential network evaluation while scoring a fully observed vector. Its masks prove that every output ignores its own and later coordinates. Ancestral sampling still has a dependency chain because those earlier coordinates must first be generated. MAF and IAF reuse the triangular dependency pattern but choose opposite known directions.

    2. Masked Convolutional Networks (PixelCNN)¤

    PixelCNN (van den Oord, Kalchbrenner & Kavukcuoglu, 2016 — ICML; Gated PixelCNN — van den Oord et al., 2016) uses masked convolutions for autoregressive image generation:

    Key idea: Apply convolution with a spatial mask ensuring pixel \((i,j)\) only depends on pixels above and to the left.

    Masked Convolution:

    # Shape-oriented pseudocode: a complete implementation must apply
# `masked_kernel` with the convolution's stride, padding, dilation, and bias.
class MaskedConv2D(nnx.Module):
    def __init__(self, in_channels, out_channels, kernel_size, mask_type, *, rngs):
        super().__init__()
        self.in_channels = in_channels
        self.out_channels = out_channels
        self.conv = nnx.Conv(in_channels, out_channels,
                            kernel_size=kernel_size, padding="SAME", rngs=rngs)
        self.mask = self._create_mask(kernel_size, mask_type)

    def _create_mask(self, kernel_size, mask_type):
        """Create autoregressive mask for convolution."""
        kh, kw = kernel_size
        mask = jnp.ones((kh, kw, self.in_channels, self.out_channels))

        center_h, center_w = kh // 2, kw // 2

        # Mask future pixels (below and to the right)
        mask = mask.at[center_h + 1:, :, :, :].set(0)
        mask = mask.at[center_h, center_w + 1:, :, :].set(0)

        # For mask type A (first layer), also mask center
        if mask_type == "A":
            mask = mask.at[center_h, center_w, :, :].set(0)

        return mask

    def __call__(self, x):
        masked_kernel = self.conv.kernel * self.mask
        # Apply masked convolution
        ...

    Architecture:

    1. First layer: Masked Conv with type A (masks center pixel)
    2. Hidden layers: Masked Conv with type B (includes center pixel)
    3. Residual blocks: Stack masked convolutions with skip connections
    4. Output: Per-pixel categorical distribution over pixel values

    The PixelCNN name covers several probability and architecture choices that should not be collapsed into one recipe:

    • PixelRNN uses two-dimensional recurrent layers to communicate image context, while PixelCNN uses masked convolutions so known-image conditionals can be trained in parallel.
    • Gated PixelCNN introduces vertical and horizontal stacks to remove a blind spot and permits conditioning on labels, embeddings, or other declared context.
    • PixelCNN++ changes the observation law from a 256-way subpixel softmax to a discretized logistic mixture, models whole-pixel channel dependence through mixture parameters, and adds multi-resolution computation. The bin-integrated likelihood—not a logistic density evaluated at an integer—is the normalized mass assigned to a pixel.
    • Subscale Pixel Networks change the image ordering to a sequence of interleaved slices and use multidimensional upscaling. This gives later slices image-wide lower-resolution context; it does not remove the need to declare the resulting order.

    These variants illustrate three independent design axes: the network carrying prefix information, the order in which image variables or blocks are exposed, and the normalized conditional observation law. A likelihood comparison is meaningless unless its quantization, channel convention, and units are matched.

    Advantages:

    • Parallel training: All pixels computed simultaneously
    • Spatial inductive bias: Local patterns learned efficiently
    • Exact model likelihood for the declared discrete conditionals and ordering

    Disadvantages:

    • Slow generation: Sequential pixel-by-pixel (196,608 steps for 256×256×3 image)
    • Blind spot: Standard PixelCNN misses dependencies due to receptive field limitations (fixed in Gated PixelCNN)
    • Limited long-range dependencies: Receptive field grows linearly with depth

    When to use: Image generation when exact likelihood matters, density estimation on images, image inpainting.

    3. Transformer-Based Autoregressive Models¤

    Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017 — NeurIPS) use self-attention with causal masking for autoregressive modeling:

    Self-Attention:

    \[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}} + M\right) V \]

    where \(M\) is a causal mask:

    \[ M_{ij} = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } j \leq i \\ -\infty & \text{if } j > i \end{cases} \]

    This ensures position \(i\) only attends to positions \(\leq i\).

    class CausalSelfAttention(nnx.Module):
    def __init__(self, hidden_dim, num_heads, *, rngs):
        super().__init__()
        self.num_heads = num_heads
        self.head_dim = hidden_dim // num_heads

        self.qkv = nnx.Linear(hidden_dim, 3 * hidden_dim, rngs=rngs)
        self.output = nnx.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim, rngs=rngs)

    def __call__(self, x):
        # x: [batch, seq_len, hidden_dim]
        batch_size, seq_len, _ = x.shape

        # Compute Q, K, V
        qkv = self.qkv(x)  # [batch, seq_len, 3 * hidden_dim]
        q, k, v = jnp.split(qkv, 3, axis=-1)

        # Reshape for multi-head attention
        q = q.reshape(batch_size, seq_len, self.num_heads, self.head_dim)
        k = k.reshape(batch_size, seq_len, self.num_heads, self.head_dim)
        v = v.reshape(batch_size, seq_len, self.num_heads, self.head_dim)

        # Compute attention scores
        scores = jnp.einsum('bqhd,bkhd->bhqk', q, k) / jnp.sqrt(self.head_dim)

        # Apply causal mask
        mask = jnp.tril(jnp.ones((seq_len, seq_len), dtype=jnp.bool_))
        scores = jnp.where(mask, scores, -1e9)

        # Attention weights and output
        attn_weights = nnx.softmax(scores, axis=-1)
        attn_output = jnp.einsum('bhqk,bkhd->bqhd', attn_weights, v)

        # Concatenate heads and project
        attn_output = attn_output.reshape(batch_size, seq_len, -1)
        output = self.output(attn_output)

        return output

    1. Token Embedding + Positional Embedding
    2. Stack of Transformer Blocks:
    3. Causal Self-Attention
    4. Layer Normalization
    5. Feed-Forward Network (2-layer MLP)
    6. Residual connections
    7. Output projection to vocabulary

    Advantages:

    • Parallel training: All positions computed simultaneously
    • Long-range dependencies: Direct connections via attention
    • Scalability: Powers models with billions of parameters — GPT-3 (175B, Brown et al., 2020), DeepSeek-V3 (671B sparse / 37B active, DeepSeek-AI, 2024), and Llama 3.1 (405B, Meta, 2024). Closed-model parameter counts that are not disclosed by their developers are rumors, not reproducible model specifications, and are intentionally excluded here.
    • Strong empirical scaling: decoder-only Transformers are widely used for text and code and can be competitive for suitably tokenized image generation; this is a protocol-dependent empirical result, not a theorem about attention

    Disadvantages:

    • Quadratic complexity: \(O(n^2)\) in sequence length for self-attention
    • Memory intensive: Storing attention matrices
    • Sequential generation: Still generate one token at a time

    When to use: Text generation (GPT, LLaMA), code generation, any task requiring long-range dependencies.

    4. WaveNet: Autoregressive Audio Generation¤

    WaveNet (van den Oord et al., 2016 — DeepMind) is a deep autoregressive model for raw audio waveforms:

    Key innovation: Dilated causal convolutions for exponentially large receptive fields.

    Dilated Convolution:

    \[ y_t = \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} w_k \cdot x_{t - d \cdot k} \]

    where \(d\) is the dilation factor. Stacking layers with dilations \(1, 2, 4, 8, \ldots, 512\) achieves receptive field of 1024 time steps with only \(\log_2(1024) = 10\) layers.

    Gated activation units:

    \[ z = \tanh(W_{f} * x) \odot \sigma(W_g * x) \]

    where \(*\) denotes convolution, \(\odot\) is element-wise product, and \(W_f\), \(W_g\) are filter and gate weights.

    Residual and skip connections: Connect all layers to output for deep architectures (30-40 layers).

    Advantages:

    • Raw waveform modeling: No hand-crafted features
    • Historically high-quality audio: the original work established a strong raw-waveform baseline; modern audio systems use several autoregressive and non-autoregressive families
    • Large receptive field: Captures long-term dependencies efficiently

    Disadvantages:

    • Extremely slow generation: 16kHz audio requires 16,000 sequential steps per second
    • Specialized for audio: Architecture designed for 1D temporal data

    When to use: Text-to-speech, audio generation, music synthesis.

    5. Multiscale Visual Autoregressive Modeling (VAR)¤

    VAR (Tian et al., 2024 — NeurIPS Best Paper) applies GPT-style autoregressive modeling to images via next-scale prediction:

    Key innovation: Instead of predicting pixels in raster scan order, predict image tokens at progressively finer scales.

    Multi-scale tokenization:

    1. Encode image into tokens at multiple resolutions: \(16 \times 16\), \(32 \times 32\), \(64 \times 64\), etc.
    2. Autoregressively predict tokens at scale \(s+1\) conditioned on all tokens at scales \(\leq s\)
    3. Use Transformer to model \(p(\text{tokens}_{s+1} \mid \text{tokens}_{\leq s})\)

    Advantages over pixel-level AR:

    • Faster generation: Fewer sequential steps (sum of tokens across scales vs. total pixels)
    • Better quality: Multi-scale structure matches image hierarchies
    • Scalable: The authors report near-linear log-log scaling trends, with fitted linear correlation coefficients close to \(-0.998\) between validation loss and model compute/size—not a negative \(R^2\) (Tian et al., 2024).

    Reported result: Under the paper's class-conditional ImageNet 256×256 protocol, VAR reported FID 1.73 and compared favorably with the listed diffusion-transformer baselines. The claim does not transfer automatically to text-to-image generation, different data, other resolutions, or matched-compute studies.

    When to use: High-quality image generation, scaling autoregressive models to large datasets.

    6. State-space and hybrid causal backbones¤

    A causal state-space model compresses the prefix into a recurrent state. In its simplest linear time-invariant form,

    \[ h_t=A h_{t-1}+B x_t,\qquad y_t=C h_t+D x_t. \]

    The recurrence supports streaming, while an equivalent convolution or structured scan can expose training parallelism. Modern selective state-space models make some coefficients input-dependent. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) introduced a hardware-aware selective scan; Mamba-2 derived a state-space duality that relates a structured state-space computation to attention-like matrix forms (Dao & Gu, ICML 2024). Mamba-3 (Lahoti et al., ICLR 2026 Oral) adds more expressive discretized dynamics, complex-valued state updates, and multi-input/multi-output variants, and reports improved retrieval and state tracking under its protocols. These are architecture-level relationships and results. They do not make an SSM probability distribution identical to a Transformer distribution unless the conditional functions themselves coincide, and they do not make every fixed-state recurrence equally expressive.

    Pure recurrent state offers favorable asymptotic sequence scaling but may create an information bottleneck; global attention retrieves particular prior tokens directly but incurs a growing KV cache and, without sparse or kernel methods, quadratic prefill attention. Hybrids interleave the two so local or compressed dynamics and explicit retrieval can complement each other. Actual latency depends on kernels, batch size, sequence length, device, cache layout, and model quality at matched compute—big-\(O\) notation alone cannot select a production backbone.

    Worked architecture trace: four tokens, one prediction¤

    Consider inputs \((x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)\) and an output used to predict \(x_5\).

    • An RNN applies its cell four times; the prediction reads the final state \(h_4\).
    • A width-3 causal convolution with one layer can directly see only a three-token receptive field; stacking or dilation expands it.
    • A single causal self-attention layer at position 4 can directly weight all four keys, subject to the causal mask.
    • An SSM scan updates one state per token; the final state is a learned compression of the prefix.

    All four can parameterize the same formal conditional \(p_\theta(x_5\mid x_{1:4})\). Their differences concern function class, optimization, hardware execution, and how information travels—not the chain rule.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    “Autoregressive” constrains information flow, while NADE, MADE, RNN, CNN, Transformer, WaveNet, visual hierarchy, and SSM name computational designs. NADE shares a recurrent prefix computation; MADE certifies dependencies by removing paths. Discrete softmaxes, continuous mixtures, and bin-integrated logistic mixtures are different normalized output laws. A fixed ordering defines one normalized joint, an order-agnostic objective averages log-likelihoods across joints, and an order ensemble averages their probabilities. Parallel teacher-forced evaluation does not imply parallel ancestral generation. Conversely, sequential recurrence at inference does not always imply slow execution: cache size, kernel fusion, and hardware utilization matter. Architecture comparisons are meaningful only with representation, observation law, quality, context length, batch size, and compute held sufficiently clear.

    Retrieval practice: architectures
    1. Why can a Transformer train all sequence positions in parallel but not ordinarily sample all tokens independently?
    2. What is the distinction between a receptive field and an effective ability to use distant context?
    3. What information does a fixed-size recurrent state trade away relative to a full KV cache?
    4. Does state-space duality establish equality of every Mamba and attention model?
    5. Why does \(M_{\mathrm{out}}M_{\mathrm{in}}\) provide a structural audit of a one-hidden-layer MADE?
    6. Why is \(\mathbb E_o\log p^o(x)\) not the log-likelihood of the order mixture?
    7. Which direction is parallel for MAF, and which direction is parallel for IAF?
    8. Why is a Gaussian-mixture density evaluated at pixel integers not automatically a normalized pixel likelihood?

    Solutions. (1) Teacher forcing supplies the full known prefix for every target, whereas generation has not yet produced future prefix tokens. (2) A receptive field identifies inputs that can influence an output structurally; optimization and learned weights determine whether the model uses them effectively. (3) The state compresses the prefix instead of retaining a separately addressable key/value entry for every token. (4) No. It relates structured computations under stated parameterizations; equality of full conditional distributions requires stronger parameter and function equality. (5) Its entries count permitted input-to-output paths; zeros therefore certify that a conditional cannot see its own or a later coordinate. (6) The expectation is outside the logarithm. It is the log of a geometric pool, whereas a mixture uses \(\log\mathbb E_op^o(x)\) and is normalized by arithmetic averaging. (7) MAF parallelizes arbitrary-data scoring \(x\to z\) and samples sequentially; IAF parallelizes \(z\to x\) for sampling and evaluates an externally supplied \(x\) sequentially. (8) A density assigns probability to intervals, not point masses. Quantized observations require integrating over bins or declaring a discrete mass function.

    Training Autoregressive Models: Prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand log-likelihood and the shifted prediction convention. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. derive token cross-entropy from maximum likelihood;
    2. implement the input/target shift without leaking the answer;
    3. distinguish sequence NLL, mean token NLL, perplexity, and bits per dimension;

    Maximum Likelihood Training¤

    Autoregressive models are trained via maximum likelihood estimation using teacher forcing:

    Teacher Forcing: During training, use ground truth previous tokens as input (not model's own predictions).

    Training loop:

    def train_step(model, batch, optimizer):
    # batch['sequences']: [batch_size, seq_len] ground truth sequences

    def loss_fn(model):
        # Forward pass with ground truth input
        outputs = model(batch['sequences'])
        logits = outputs['logits']  # [batch_size, seq_len, vocab_size]

        # Shift targets: predict x_i given x_<i
        shifted_logits = logits[:, :-1, :]  # Remove last position
        shifted_targets = batch['sequences'][:, 1:]  # Remove first position
        vocab_size = shifted_logits.shape[-1]

        # Cross-entropy loss
        log_probs = nnx.log_softmax(shifted_logits, axis=-1)
        one_hot_targets = nnx.one_hot(shifted_targets, vocab_size)
        loss = -jnp.mean(jnp.sum(log_probs * one_hot_targets, axis=-1))

        return loss

    loss, grads = nnx.value_and_grad(loss_fn)(model)
    optimizer.update(model, grads)  # NNX 0.11.0+ API

    return loss

    Why teacher forcing?

    • Stable training: Prevents error accumulation from model's mistakes
    • Faster convergence: Model sees correct context
    • Direct pathwise differentiation of the log-likelihood: no sampling estimator is required for ordinary observed discrete targets

    Train–generation context mismatch: at generation time, the model conditions on prefixes sampled from its own distribution rather than only data prefixes. It is often called exposure bias, but that label does not identify a unique error or imply that teacher-forced MLE is statistically inconsistent. Candidate interventions include:

    • Scheduled sampling: Gradually mix model predictions into training prefixes. The resulting objective is generally not maximum likelihood and can be statistically inconsistent; compare it with sequence-level training and stronger MLE baselines rather than treating it as a default fix (Huszár, 2015).
    • Sequence-level or on-policy objectives: Optimise a declared sequence metric or reward, accepting the estimator variance, exploration, and alignment trade-offs.
    • Robust MLE and data coverage: Better models and broader prefix coverage can reduce practical errors without changing the population MLE objective, but scale is not a guarantee against compounding mistakes.

    Loss Functions and Metrics¤

    Primary loss: Negative log-likelihood (NLL) / Cross-entropy:

    \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}} = -\frac{1}{N \cdot T} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(x_t^{(n)} \mid x_{<t}^{(n)}) \]

    Perplexity: Exponentiated cross-entropy (lower is better):

    \[ \text{PPL} = \exp(\mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}}) \]

    Bits per dimension (BPD): for an ordered observation with \(D\) scalar dimensions,

    \[ \text{BPD} =-\frac{1}{N D\log 2} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{D} \log p_\theta(x_i^{(n)}\mid x_{<i}^{(n)}). \]

    If \(\mathcal L_{\mathrm{NLL}}\) has already been averaged over the \(D\) dimensions in nats, then \(\mathrm{BPD}=\mathcal L_{\mathrm{NLL}}/\log 2\); do not divide by \(D\) twice. Discrete-image comparisons must also state preprocessing/dequantisation and the unit of each modeled symbol.

    Accuracy: Token-level prediction accuracy (for discrete data):

    \[ \text{Acc} = \frac{1}{N \cdot T} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{1}[\arg\max p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}) = x_t] \]

    Token accuracy discards the probability assigned to every non-argmax outcome. A model can therefore improve NLL and calibration without changing accuracy, or preserve accuracy while becoming severely overconfident. For variable-length batches, all sums and denominators must exclude padding tokens.

    Worked example: from conditionals to NLL and perplexity¤

    Suppose a three-target sequence receives probabilities \(0.8\), \(0.5\), and \(0.25\) on its observed next tokens. Its sequence probability under the model is

    \[ p_\theta(x_{1:3})=0.8\times0.5\times0.25=0.1. \]

    The sequence NLL is \(-\log 0.1\approx2.303\) nats. The mean token NLL is \(2.303/3\approx0.768\) nats, so token-level perplexity is

    \[ \exp(0.768)\approx2.15. \]

    Exponentiating the sequence NLL would instead give 10. Both numbers are mathematically meaningful but answer different questions; a reported “perplexity” conventionally exponentiates mean token NLL. Comparisons also require a compatible tokenizer because changing the unit called a token changes the denominator.

    Numerical Stability and Best Practices¤

    Log-space computation: Always work in log-space to prevent underflow:

    # WRONG: Can underflow
probs = softmax(logits)
loss = -jnp.mean(jnp.log(probs[targets]))

# CORRECT: Numerically stable
log_probs = nnx.log_softmax(logits)
target_log_probs = jnp.take_along_axis(
    log_probs, targets[..., None], axis=-1
).squeeze(-1)
loss = -jnp.mean(target_log_probs)

    Gradient clipping: Prevent exploding gradients in deep models:

    # Clip the norm of the complete gradient PyTree, preserving its direction.
clipper = optax.clip_by_global_norm(clip_value)
clip_state = clipper.init(grads)
grads, _ = clipper.update(grads, clip_state)

    Element-wise jnp.clip is a different operation: it clips every coordinate independently and can rotate the gradient substantially. Global-norm clipping rescales the whole PyTree only when its joint norm exceeds the threshold.

    Learning rate schedules: Use warmup + decay for Transformers:

    def lr_schedule(step, warmup_steps=4000, d_model=512):
    step = jnp.maximum(step, 1)  # Avoid division by zero
    arg1 = step ** -0.5
    arg2 = step * (warmup_steps ** -1.5)
    return (d_model ** -0.5) * jnp.minimum(arg1, arg2)

    Label smoothing: Reduce overconfidence:

    def label_smoothing(one_hot_labels, smoothing=0.1):
    num_classes = one_hot_labels.shape[-1]
    smooth_labels = one_hot_labels * (1 - smoothing)
    smooth_labels += smoothing / num_classes
    return smooth_labels

    Label smoothing replaces the empirical one-hot target with a mixture distribution. It is therefore not exact MLE for the original empirical distribution. It can regularize training, but its effect on probability calibration, rare tokens, and downstream decoding must be measured rather than inferred from the name.

    Independent practice and assessment map: training¤

    Greedy Decoding¤

    Select the most likely token at each step:

    \[ x_t = \arg\max_{x} p_\theta(x \mid x_{<t}) \]
    def greedy_generation(model, max_length, start_token_id, *, rngs):
    sequence = jnp.zeros((1, max_length), dtype=jnp.int32)
    sequence = sequence.at[:, 0].set(start_token_id)

    for t in range(1, max_length):
        outputs = model(sequence, rngs=rngs)
        # Training uses logits at t-1 to predict the token at t.
        logits = outputs['logits'][:, t - 1, :]  # [1, vocab_size]

        next_token = jnp.argmax(logits, axis=-1)
        sequence = sequence.at[:, t].set(next_token)

    return sequence

    Pros: Deterministic, fast

    Cons: Repetitive, lacks diversity, not true sampling from \(p_\theta\)

    Sampling with Temperature¤

    Temperature \(\tau\) controls randomness:

    \[ p_\tau(x_t \mid x_{<t}) = \frac{\exp(f_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}) / \tau)}{\sum_{x'} \exp(f_\theta(x' \mid x_{<t}) / \tau)} \]
    • \(\tau \to 0\): Greedy (deterministic)
    • \(\tau = 1\): Sample from model distribution
    • \(\tau > 1\): More uniform (random)

    For every finite \(\tau>0\), tokens with finite logits retain nonzero probability. Greedy decoding is the limiting operation as \(\tau\to0^+\), with tie behavior requiring an explicit rule. Implementations must reject \(\tau\leq0\) rather than divide by it.

    def temperature_sampling(
    model, max_length, start_token_id, temperature=1.0, *, rngs
):
    sequence = jnp.zeros((1, max_length), dtype=jnp.int32)
    sequence = sequence.at[:, 0].set(start_token_id)
    sample_key = rngs.sample()

    for t in range(1, max_length):
        outputs = model(sequence, rngs=rngs)
        logits = outputs['logits'][:, t - 1, :] / temperature

        sample_key, subkey = jax.random.split(sample_key)
        next_token = jax.random.categorical(subkey, logits, axis=-1)
        sequence = sequence.at[:, t].set(next_token)

    return sequence

    Top-k Sampling¤

    Restrict sampling to the \(k\) most likely tokens:

    1. Find top-k logits: \(\text{top}_k(f_\theta(x \mid x_{<t}))\)
    2. Set all other logits to \(-\infty\)
    3. Sample from renormalized distribution
    def top_k_sampling(
    model, max_length, start_token_id, k=40, temperature=1.0, *, rngs
):
    sequence = jnp.zeros((1, max_length), dtype=jnp.int32)
    sequence = sequence.at[:, 0].set(start_token_id)
    sample_key = rngs.sample()

    for t in range(1, max_length):
        outputs = model(sequence, rngs=rngs)
        logits = outputs['logits'][:, t - 1, :] / temperature

        # Get top-k
        top_k_logits, top_k_indices = jax.lax.top_k(logits, k)

        # Mask non-top-k
        masked_logits = jnp.full_like(logits, -1e9)
        masked_logits = masked_logits.at[0, top_k_indices[0]].set(top_k_logits[0])

        # Sample
        sample_key, subkey = jax.random.split(sample_key)
        next_token = jax.random.categorical(subkey, masked_logits, axis=-1)
        sequence = sequence.at[:, t].set(next_token)

    return sequence

    Values such as \(k=40\) are historical text-generation starting points, not universal defaults. Tune jointly with temperature and evaluate truncation-induced calibration and diversity changes.

    Top-p (Nucleus) Sampling¤

    Sample from the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds \(p\):

    1. Sort tokens by probability in descending order
    2. Find cutoff where cumulative probability \(\geq p\)
    3. Sample from this subset
    def top_p_sampling(
    model, max_length, start_token_id, p=0.9, temperature=1.0, *, rngs
):
    sequence = jnp.zeros((1, max_length), dtype=jnp.int32)
    sequence = sequence.at[:, 0].set(start_token_id)
    sample_key = rngs.sample()

    for t in range(1, max_length):
        outputs = model(sequence, rngs=rngs)
        logits = outputs['logits'][:, t - 1, :] / temperature

        # Sort by probability
        probs = nnx.softmax(logits, axis=-1)
        sorted_indices = jnp.argsort(-probs, axis=-1)
        sorted_probs = jnp.take_along_axis(probs, sorted_indices, axis=-1)

        # Cumulative probabilities
        cumulative_probs = jnp.cumsum(sorted_probs, axis=-1)

        # Remove tokens *after* the first token that crosses p. Shifting the
        # threshold mask keeps that boundary token, so retained mass is >= p.
        remove_mask = cumulative_probs > p
        remove_mask = remove_mask.at[:, 1:].set(remove_mask[:, :-1])
        remove_mask = remove_mask.at[:, 0].set(False)
        cutoff_mask = ~remove_mask

        # Mask and renormalize
        masked_probs = jnp.where(cutoff_mask, sorted_probs, 0.0)
        masked_probs /= jnp.sum(masked_probs, axis=-1, keepdims=True)

        # Sample
        sample_key, subkey = jax.random.split(sample_key)
        sampled_idx = jax.random.categorical(subkey, jnp.log(masked_probs), axis=-1)
        next_token = sorted_indices[0, sampled_idx[0]]
        sequence = sequence.at[:, t].set(next_token)

    return sequence

    Values such as \(p=0.9\) or \(0.95\) are common starting points. The same value can retain very different vocabulary sizes across positions, models, and domains.

    Advantages: Adapts to probability distribution shape (varies nucleus size).

    Worked example: the top-p boundary¤

    Let the sorted next-token probabilities be \((0.40,0.30,0.15,0.10,0.05)\) and choose \(p=0.8\). The cumulative masses are \((0.40,0.70,0.85,0.95,1.00)\). The smallest prefix reaching the threshold contains the first three tokens, including the token that crosses \(0.8\). After renormalization their probabilities become approximately

    \[ (0.4706,\ 0.3529,\ 0.1765). \]

    Dropping the crossing token would retain mass \(0.70<p\) and is the classic boundary error. Keeping every token after it would no longer be nucleus truncation.

    Beam Search¤

    Maintain top-\(B\) most likely sequences:

    1. At each step, expand each of the \(B\) sequences with all possible next tokens
    2. Score all \(B \times V\) candidates (where \(V\) is vocab size)
    3. Keep top-\(B\) by cumulative log-probability
    4. Return highest-scoring sequence at the end
    def beam_search(model, max_length, start_token_id, beam_size=5, *, rngs):
    # Initialize with start token
    sequences = jnp.zeros((beam_size, max_length), dtype=jnp.int32)
    sequences = sequences.at[:, 0].set(start_token_id)
    scores = jnp.zeros(beam_size)
    scores = scores.at[1:].set(-1e9)  # Only first beam is active initially

    for t in range(1, max_length):
        outputs = model(sequences, rngs=rngs)
        logits = outputs['logits'][:, t - 1, :]  # [beam_size, vocab_size]
        log_probs = nnx.log_softmax(logits, axis=-1)
        vocab_size = logits.shape[-1]

        # Expand: [beam_size, vocab_size]
        candidate_scores = scores[:, None] + log_probs

        # Flatten and get top beam_size
        flat_scores = candidate_scores.reshape(-1)
        top_indices = jnp.argsort(-flat_scores)[:beam_size]

        # Decode indices to (beam_idx, token_idx)
        beam_indices = top_indices // vocab_size
        token_indices = top_indices % vocab_size

        # Update sequences and scores
        sequences = sequences[beam_indices]
        sequences = sequences.at[:, t].set(token_indices)
        scores = flat_scores[top_indices]

    # Return best sequence
    best_idx = jnp.argmax(scores)
    return sequences[best_idx:best_idx+1]

    The sketches above generate a fixed length and keep the BOS token in the returned sequence. Production decoders also carry prompt length, padding masks, EOS/stop conditions, per-example completion state, and often length or coverage penalties. For fixed scoring and length, a wider beam searches more candidates but costs more memory/compute; it need not improve calibration, diversity, or a downstream task metric.

    Use cases: Machine translation, caption generation (prefer high likelihood over diversity).

    Reranking changes the deployed autoregressive system¤

    Reranking is neither ordinary sampling nor beam search. It draws complete candidates from the deployed AR sampler—including its temperature and truncation—and then applies another model or score. The resulting output law depends jointly on the candidate law, score, candidate count, dependence, and tie rule. Exact model likelihood or calibration under the candidate law does not automatically describe the selected outputs.

    For example, an image system may sample visual tokens from \(p_\theta(z\mid c)\), decode \(x\sim p_\psi(x\mid z)\), and return the highest CLIP-scoring image. The deployed generator is then the composition of the AR prior, codec, candidate sampler, scorer, and selection rule—not the AR prior alone. Increasing the pool can amplify genuine preference signal or exploitable scorer error without changing \(\theta\).

    The conditioning, steering, and constraints volume owns the order-statistic density derivation, finite best-of-three example, reward-hacking boundary, and reporting contract. In an AR evaluation, report candidate count, sampler, codec, score, tie rule, compute, and both score-based and independent outcomes.

    Constrained decoding¤

    A constraint maps each prefix to a set of legal next tokens \(A(x_{<t})\). Hard masking defines

    \[ \tilde p(x_t\mid x_{<t})= \frac{p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})\,\mathbb 1[x_t\in A(x_{<t})]} {\sum_{v\in A(x_{<t})}p_\theta(v\mid x_{<t})}. \]

    This is exact sampling from the prefixwise masked law defined by the displayed conditionals when every legal set is correct and non-empty. It is not a sample from the original unconstrained model. Nor is it generally the original model conditioned on eventual validity: exact global conditioning weights each legal token by the remaining valid-completion probability \(p(F\mid x_{<t},x_t)\), whereas a binary grammar mask records only whether at least one completion exists. The laws coincide only under additional conditions, such as equal valid-suffix mass among surviving tokens, or when that suffix mass is computed explicitly. JSON grammars, finite-state lexical constraints, type systems, and safety filters differ in how much state is required; tokenization can make a human-readable constraint span multiple tokens. A robust decoder defines behavior for an empty legal set and tests that every emitted prefix remains extendable to a valid completion. The hard-constraint chapter derives a finite counterexample and compares masks with rejection, projection, and constrained Monte Carlo.

    The complete Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations volume derives the same boundary from derivation and yield semantics, then connects prefix constraints to inside algorithms, executable programs, diffusion infilling, circuits, and GFlowNet state graphs.

    Speculative decoding and multi-token proposals¤

    Speculative decoding uses a cheaper draft distribution \(q\) to propose tokens and the target model \(p\) to verify several positions in parallel (Leviathan, Kalman & Matias, 2023). For a one-step proposal \(y\sim q\), accepting with probability

    \[ \min\!\left(1,\frac{p(y)}{q(y)}\right) \]

    and, after rejection, sampling from the normalized positive residual \((p-q)_+\) is a rejection-correction construction. Its blockwise extension preserves the target model's sampling distribution when implemented exactly. Approximate verification, different truncation between draft and target, numerical shortcuts, or a changed acceptance rule can forfeit that guarantee.

    Multi-token prediction heads can provide draft candidates or auxiliary training signals, but predicting several future tokens in one forward pass does not by itself remove their statistical dependence. A verifier or a separately defined joint block distribution is needed before multiple proposals can be accepted as exact target samples. Speedup depends on draft cost, acceptance length, target batch efficiency, and memory traffic—not only the number of proposed tokens.

    Autoregressive factorization versus tensor-network contraction¤

    An exactly contractible tensor-network law can be sampled sequentially by forming \(p(x_i\mid x_{<i})\) from prefix and suffix environments.     Masked Autoregressive Flows (MAF)¤

    MAF uses autoregressive transformations as invertible flow layers:

    \[ z_i = (x_i - \mu_i(x_{<i})) \cdot \exp(-\alpha_i(x_{<i})) \]

    where \(\mu_i\) and \(\alpha_i\) are outputs of a MADE (Masked Autoencoder) network.

    Jacobian is triangular:

    \[ \frac{\partial z_i}{\partial x_j}=0\quad\text{for }j>i, \qquad \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right) =\left(\exp[-\alpha_i(x_{<i})]\right)_{i=1}^{D}. \]

    Log-determinant:

    \[ \log \left| \det \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} \right| = -\sum_i \alpha_i(x_{<i}) \]

    Trade-offs:

    • Density estimation: one masked-network evaluation exposes all transformation parameters in parallel, although arithmetic still scales with dimension
    • Sampling: \(D\) sequential inverse dependencies in the basic construction

    IAF (Inverse Autoregressive Flow) reverses the dependency attachment: its conditioner reads the already available base prefix, so base-to-sample evaluation is parallel within a layer while scoring arbitrary external data requires sequential inversion. See the full MAF/IAF direction audit.

    Autoregressive Energy-Based Models¤

    Autoregressive and energy-based modelling can be combined by parameterising each conditional with an energy function:

    \[ p(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\exp(-E_i(x_i \mid x_{<i}))}{Z_i(x_{<i})} \]

    For a finite vocabulary, each prefix-dependent partition function \(Z_i(x_{<i})=\sum_{v}\exp[-E_i(v\mid x_{<i})]\) is just a local softmax normalizer, so ordinary cross-entropy is available; contrastive divergence is neither required nor generally desirable. For continuous or enormous output spaces, local normalization can again become intractable and alternative estimators may be needed.

    The 2026 preprint Autoregressive Language Models are Secretly Energy-Based Models develops a function-space correspondence under its stated construction and connects it to a soft Bellman relation in maximum-entropy reinforcement learning. Treat this as a Preprint result, not the unrestricted statement that generic globally normalized EBMs admit tractable AR conditionals or that every preference objective is literally the same algorithm as energy shaping. See the EBM explainer for the assumption boundary.

    Sparse Transformers and Efficient Attention¤

    Problem: Standard self-attention is \(O(n^2)\) in sequence length.

    Sparse Transformers (Child et al., 2019) use sparse attention patterns:

    • Strided attention: Attend to every \(k\)-th position
    • Fixed attention: Attend to fixed positions (e.g., beginning of sequence)
    • Local + global: Combine local windows with global tokens

    Complexity: \(O(n \sqrt{n})\) or \(O(n \log n)\) depending on pattern.

    Linear Transformers (Katharopoulos et al., 2020 — ICML; Performer — Choromanski et al., 2021) approximate attention with kernels:

    \[ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) \approx \phi(Q) (\phi(K)^T V) \]

    achieving \(O(n)\) complexity.

    Autoregressive for Protein and Scientific Data¤

    ProtGPT2 (Ferruz et al., 2022): Autoregressive Transformer for protein sequences

    • Generates novel, functional proteins
    • 50M parameters, trained on UniRef50

    AlphaFold 2 is an important non-example. It predicts and refines all residue coordinates jointly through the Evoformer, structure module, and recycling; recycling is iterative refinement, not a chain-rule factorization or token-by-token structure decoder (Jumper et al., 2021). Keeping this distinction prevents the common mistake of calling every sequential computation “autoregressive.” Autoregressive protein sequence models such as ProtGPT2 do satisfy the factorization; joint structure predictors need not.

    Recent peer-reviewed scientific systems make the representation boundary more explicit:

    • InertialAR (ICML 2026) serializes atom identities and continuous coordinates after an inertial-frame canonicalization. The resulting generator is autoregressive; the claimed geometric invariance depends on how axis degeneracies, signs, chirality, and atom-order ties are resolved.
    • Autoregressive Boltzmann Generators (ICML 2026 Spotlight) use coordinatewise generation and tractable model likelihood inside equilibrium-sampling and importance-correction procedures. The correction and effective sample size belong to the full sampler, not to autoregressive factorization alone.
    • Protein Autoregressive Modeling (ICML 2026 Spotlight) combines coarse-to-fine next-scale prediction with a flow-based backbone decoder. This is a hybrid composition: the outer scale order and inner continuous conditional generator must both be reported.

    Applications: Drug design, enzyme engineering, materials discovery.

    Sparse, Recurrent-State, and Deliberative Autoregressive Systems¤

    The transitions and few-step guide explains continuous autoregressive factorization, Transition Matching's internal generative heads, full-history variants, and why causal generation order does not imply causal-inference semantics.

    The geometric and equivariant generation guide analyzes order dependence for sets, graph edits, internal coordinates, torsions, and local-frame autoregression.

    Large-scale autoregressive modelling is best organized along five independent axes—architecture (MoE, state-space, hybrids), objective (multi-token prediction and post-training), inference (test-time compute and speculative decoding), modality (visual, audio, and multimodal generation), and alternatives or hybrids (continuous-token AR and diffusion language models). The Transformer and GPT-3 are historically important instances, not boundaries around the framework. Primary papers support the technical claims below; the survey table is a secondary map for broader reading.

    Mixture-of-Experts at Production Scale¤

    Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) (Shazeer et al., 2017 — Outrageously Large MoE; Switch Transformer — Fedus et al., 2022) routes each token through only a small subset of expert subnetworks, partially decoupling total parameter capacity from per-token active compute. Sparse MoE appears in several open and closed model families alongside competitive dense models:

    • Mixtral 8×7B (Jiang et al., 2024) — 46.7B total and 12.9B active parameters per token; its report compares favorably with Llama 2 70B on a declared benchmark suite. This is not a parameter-matched universal quality ordering.
    • DeepSeek-V3 (DeepSeek-AI, 2024) — 671B total / 37B active parameters and a reported 2.788M H800 GPU-hours for full training. A commonly repeated dollar conversion assumes an hourly rental price and excludes prior research, ablations, data, and staff, so it is not a complete reproducible training-cost figure. The report describes auxiliary-loss-free routing bias and Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) for KV compression.
    • Qwen3 (Technical report, 2025), gpt-oss (Model card, 2025), and Granite 4.0 (Vendor technical announcement, 2025) are documented sparse-MoE releases; Qwen3 includes both dense and MoE variants, while Granite combines Mamba-2, attention, and MoE. Their existence does not establish MoE as a universal flagship default. Report total/active parameters, routing, communication, and hardware, and preserve the evidence label of each source.

    No single stack is a universal default. A useful modern design vocabulary is “Transformer or hybrid backbone + dense or sparse feed-forward layers + MHA/GQA/MLA attention where present + a positional scheme + a normalization and gated MLP.” The Llama 3 report documents one influential dense Transformer recipe; DeepSeek-V3 documents a different sparse recipe.

    State-Space Models and Hybrid Backbones¤

    For very long contexts, quadratic self-attention becomes the dominant cost. The 2024 wave of selective state-space models (SSMs) offers \(O(N)\) alternatives:

    • Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024 — COLM) — a selective SSM whose parameters depend on input; the paper reports up to 5× inference throughput over selected Transformer comparisons and linear sequence-length scaling for its recurrent computation. Hardware, kernels, quality, and comparison setup bound the number.
    • Mamba-2 (Dao & Gu, 2024 — ICML) — derives state-space duality and reports faster core-layer execution than Mamba under its experiments. It is not an architecture-independent equivalence or quality guarantee.
    • Hybrid backbones — pure Mamba models (Codestral Mamba) coexist with hybrid stacks like Jamba (Lieber et al., 2024), IBM Granite-4, and Samba (Microsoft, 2024) that interleave Mamba blocks with sparse softmax-attention layers (typically 3:1 SSM:attention).
    • Large hybrid systems have also been described in technical reports and conference expo material. Such presentations are useful system evidence but do not substitute for an archival paper with reproducible training and evaluation details.

    There is no architecture-independent winner. Pure SSM, hybrid SSM-attention, sparse/local attention, and dense Transformer results depend on parameter and active-parameter count, training tokens, context-length distribution, recall versus reasoning task, kernel maturity, and hardware. Compare quality and wall-clock under a controlled protocol rather than inferring a consensus from model labels.

    Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)¤

    Instead of predicting only the next token, MTP trains the model to predict the next \(k\) tokens at each position:

    • Better & Faster Large Language Models via Multi-Token Prediction (Gloeckle et al., 2024) reports benefits concentrated in parts of its model-size and task sweep and up to roughly 3× self-speculative speedup in its setup. The auxiliary heads, loss weights, and verification algorithm are part of the result.
    • DeepSeek-V3 MTP (DeepSeek-AI, 2024) uses sequential prediction modules and reports acceptance and throughput improvements in its own serving configuration. It does not establish MTP as a universal training or serving optimum.
    • FastMTP (2025) — aligns multi-step draft quality with inference patterns.
    • Parallel Token Prediction (Draxler et al., ICLR 2026) introduces random inputs so a single call can represent dependent token blocks and reports a 2.4× speedup on its speculative-decoding protocol. Representing a block proposal is not the same as drawing exact samples from another target AR model; target preservation still requires the declared training or verification relationship.

    Speculative Decoding¤

    Speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023 — ICML; Chen et al., 2023 — DeepMind) decouples proposing tokens (cheap draft model) from verifying them (expensive target model), restoring batch parallelism to autoregressive inference:

    Modern variants include EAGLE-3 (Li et al., 2025) and Medusa (Cai et al., 2024). Reported speedups depend on model pair, hardware, batch size, proposal length, acceptance, and baseline. Correct rejection-corrected stochastic algorithms preserve the target distribution, which is not the same as bit-identical samples under arbitrary random-number coupling; deterministic greedy variants can reproduce the target's greedy sequence.

    Test-Time Compute and Reasoning Models¤

    One major direction is scaling inference compute rather than only parameters:

    • OpenAI o1 (2024 — technical report) reports improvements with train-time RL and additional test-time computation. Its benchmark numbers are Vendor claims under the published protocol, not independent family-level evidence.
    • DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI, 2025) reports reinforcement learning with automatically checked rewards on math and code. R1-Zero is evidence that the paper's RL setup elicited extended reasoning behavior without an SFT stage; “pure RL suffices” should not be generalized to all capabilities, data, or safety requirements.

    At least two compute axes matter: train-time compute and test-time compute such as generated reasoning tokens, parallel candidates, search, or verifier calls. The gains and costs of test-time scaling depend on task, base model, stopping policy, verifier quality, and accounting convention.

    Continuous-Token Autoregression (CALM, MAR)¤

    One research thread decouples autoregression from discrete tokens:

    • CALM — Continuous Autoregressive Language Models (Preprint, 2025) compresses blocks of discrete tokens into continuous vectors and models those vectors autoregressively. This changes the conditional density and decoder problem; any throughput benefit must include codec cost and reconstruction error.
    • MAR — Autoregressive Image Generation without Vector Quantization (Li et al., 2024) autoregresses over continuous image tokens with a diffusion loss for each next-token conditional. It is a concrete hybrid system composition, not proof that categorical AR and diffusion are the same family.

    Visual and Multimodal Autoregression¤

    VAR (covered above) is one visual milestone. Follow-up work separates tokenization, auxiliary consistency, variable compute allocation, and shared multimodal context:

    • MAGVIT-v2 (Yu et al., 2024) — discrete-token AR over video VQ codes; competitive with diffusion on short-clip generation.
    • reAR (ICLR 2026) adds token-wise embedding-recovery and consistency regularization to reduce mismatch between AR-generated codes and the visual tokenizer's decoder. It changes the objective, not the chain-rule family or inference order.
    • DPAR (CVPR 2026) uses prediction entropy from a lightweight model to merge visual tokens into variable-size patches. Its reported token, FLOP, convergence, and FID changes belong to the paper's ImageNet resolutions and baselines; dynamic patchification is a representation and compute-allocation method.
    • UniAR (ICML 2026) studies one discrete visual tokenizer shared by understanding and generation so generated codes can re-enter the same context. A shared tokenizer is a system interface, not proof that understanding and generation have identical sufficient statistics or evaluation criteria.
    • Multimodal AR systems may interleave modality tokens, use modality-specific encoders and decoders, cross-attention, or combine several patterns. Closed-system interfaces do not reveal enough architecture detail to infer a single shared token stream.

    Diffusion Language Models: an AR Alternative¤

    Discrete diffusion language modelling provides a non-autoregressive alternative whose parallel refinement, likelihood semantics, and quality–latency trade-offs differ from left-to-right decoding:

    • LLaDA (Nie et al., 2025) is an 8B masked-diffusion model trained from scratch. Its paper reports selected comparisons with similarly sized AR systems; tokenizer, data, post-training, number of refinement steps, and benchmark protocol remain necessary qualifiers.
    • Mercury (Technical report, 2025) reports low-latency parallel decoding. Latency numbers from the developer are Vendor claims unless hardware, batch, output length, quality matching, and independent reproduction are controlled.

    The AR–EBM preprint discussed above supplies a specific function-space correspondence. Its assumptions and evidence label should travel with the result; it does not collapse all AR, EBM, RLHF, and DPO algorithms into one implementation.

    Sparse, Recurrent-State, and Deliberative Autoregressive Systems: Post-Training: RLHF → DPO → RLVR¤

    • RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022 — InstructGPT) — supervised fine-tuning + reward model + PPO; a common recipe for instruction-following chat systems.

    Long-Context: From 2k to 10M Tokens¤

    Some modern AR systems advertise 128k–1M-token input windows. Maximum accepted length, accuracy at distance, memory use, and useful task performance are different quantities:

    • YARN, NTK-aware RoPE — scale RoPE frequencies for context-length extrapolation.
    • Ring Attention (Liu et al., 2024) distributes blockwise attention and communication across devices; feasible length remains bounded by aggregate memory, compute, topology, and runtime.
    • InfiniAttention, Compressive Memory — bounded-memory long-context attention.
    • Native long-context training uses long examples during training rather than only post-hoc position scaling; public disclosures vary, especially for closed models.
    • Needle-in-a-haystack and RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024) — widely used long-context stress tests that should be complemented with natural tasks, contamination controls, position/length sweeps, and effective-context analysis.

    Worked relationship audit¤

    Consider MAR's outer autoregressive factorization with a diffusion model for each continuous next-token conditional.

    1. The outer chain rule is an autoregressive probabilistic construction.
    2. The inner denoiser and iterative sampler form a diffusion conditional generator.
    3. Their relation is hybrid system composition, not algebraic equivalence.
    4. Exact outer likelihood is unavailable unless each diffusion conditional's normalized density is itself evaluated under a valid likelihood procedure.

    The same audit prevents a Transformer architecture from being mislabeled as an autoregressive model when it uses bidirectional masked refinement, and prevents an autoregressive factorization from being mislabeled as a Transformer when it uses an RNN or SSM.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Family comparisons become reliable when they compare the same layer of abstraction and state the protocol. Autoregression provides normalized factorization; it does not guarantee perceptual quality, coverage, stability, or speed. Modern work increasingly composes representations, conditional heads, objectives, architectures, and samplers from several traditions. These are productive hybrids precisely because the components remain distinguishable.

    Retrieval practice: connections and research
    1. Why is a finite-vocabulary energy parameterization of each AR conditional tractable to normalize?
    2. In basic MAF, which operation is sequential: density evaluation or sampling?
    3. Is MAR an algebraic equivalence between AR and diffusion?
    4. Why must total and active parameters both be reported for an MoE?
    5. What evidence label belongs on a developer's closed-system latency number without independent reproduction?
    6. Classify reAR, DPAR, and UniAR by modeling layer rather than calling each a new family.
    7. Why is Protein Autoregressive Modeling a hybrid system?

    Solutions. (1) Its local partition function is a finite sum over the vocabulary, identical to a softmax normalizer. (2) Sampling requires the sequential inverse; density evaluation exposes all parameters in one masked-network evaluation. (3) No; it is hybrid composition with diffusion-generated continuous conditionals. (4) Total parameters describe capacity and storage, while active parameters more closely—but not completely—describe per-token arithmetic; routing communication also matters. (5) Vendor claim. (6) reAR adds auxiliary consistency objectives, DPAR changes tokenization and compute allocation, and UniAR defines a shared representation and system interface. Each retains an autoregressive probability construction. (7) It uses autoregressive next-scale planning outside a flow-based continuous backbone decoder; neither component alone describes the full generator.

    Evaluation Metrics¤

    Density-Estimation Metrics¤

    • Cross-entropy loss / NLL — the training objective. Reported per-token for text, per-pixel for images.
    • Perplexity \(\mathrm{PPL} = \exp(\mathrm{NLL})\) — the standard text metric.
    • Bits per dimension (BPD) is total NLL in nats divided by \(D\log2\). If NLL is already a mean over dimensions, divide only by \(\log2\).
    • Coding interpretation — an ideal code matched to \(p_\theta\) assigns information content \(-\log_2p_\theta(x)\) bits to an event. Real arithmetic or range coders add finite-precision, termination, blocking, and metadata overhead; report measured compressed size rather than assuming a universal percentage gap. The lossless-coding chapter supplies the Kraft, source-coding, tokenizer, and full-file accounting needed to make this operational.

    For VAR, MAGVIT-v2, and audio AR (WaveNet, Mamba audio):

    • None of these metrics alone establishes prompt adherence, temporal coherence, memorization safety, or human preference.

    Inference / Throughput Metrics¤

    • Tokens per second and time to first token — useful serving metrics only when hardware, precision, batch size, prompt/output lengths, speculative setup, and concurrency are reported.
    • Time-to-first-token (TTFT) vs time-per-output-token (TPOT) — separates prefill from decode.
    • Acceptance rate for speculative decoding — fraction of draft tokens accepted by the verifier.

    Worked accounting example: speculative throughput is a ratio of complete costs¤

    If an exact speculative run proposes four tokens per target verification but accepts 2.5 draft tokens on average, its gross accepted output per verification is not automatically a 2.5× speedup. If the draft costs 0.3 target-pass equivalents and verification costs 1.0, an idealized compute-only ratio is approximately \(2.5/(1.0+0.3)=1.92\). Synchronization, memory traffic, rejected-tail resampling, and batching reduce or sometimes improve the realized wall-clock ratio. Measure it.

    Quantisation¤

    • BF16 / FP16 are standard deployment formats, but not universally safe. Validate normalization, softmax, reductions, logits, and long-context attention in higher precision when overflow, underflow, or accumulated error appears; quantization and mixed-precision choices are model- and hardware-dependent.
    • INT8 / FP8 can improve throughput or capacity on supporting hardware, but quality loss depends on calibration data, kernel, scaling granularity, outliers, and which tensors remain high precision.
    • GPTQ / AWQ and related weight-only methods enable lower-bit deployment in selected settings. Report perplexity and task regressions for the exact checkpoint and quantizer; no fixed “less than 2%” rule transfers across models.
    • MoE quantisation adds expert-frequency and routing-distribution concerns. Per-expert or grouped calibration is a design option, not an architecture-independent requirement or guarantee.

    • SGLang (Zheng et al., 2024) — provides structured generation and prefix/KV reuse mechanisms.
    • TensorRT-LLM — an NVIDIA-optimized stack with hardware-specific quantization and parallel execution options.
    • llama.cpp and MLX — representative local and device-oriented ecosystems. Suitability depends on model format, hardware, kernels, and operational requirements.

    Common Production Pitfalls¤

    • KV-cache growth at long contexts — GQA, MQA, MLA, sliding windows, recurrence, compression, paging, and eviction trade memory against quality, compatibility, or compute in different ways.
    • Unsupported factual output is not diagnosed by temperature alone. Data, objective, prompt, retrieval, context use, calibration, and verification all matter; low temperature can make a wrong high-probability answer more repeatable.
    • Repetition loops may respond to truncation, repetition penalties, contrastive objectives, or better training, but each intervention changes behavior and can damage legitimate repetition. Evaluate by domain.
    • Speculative-decoding mis-acceptance changes the target distribution. Exactness tests should compare empirical output laws on small categorical models, not merely compare a few strings.

    Ethical / Safety Considerations¤

    • Risk assessment follows deployment and affected people, not the model-family label. Analyze misuse, representation harms, privacy leakage, memorization, automation bias, access controls, incident response, and domain-specific regulation.
    • Statistical watermarking (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023) biases a compatible sampling procedure and can be added without retraining in some deployments. Detectability, text quality, secret-key management, edits, translation, paraphrase, short samples, and incompatible decoding limit any “works on every AR model” claim.
    • Provenance metadata such as C2PA can record signed origin and edit information when the generation and publishing pipeline preserves it. Metadata is not proof that unmarked content is synthetic or that marked content is truthful.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    Autoregressive models decompose joint distributions via the chain rule, enabling exact model-likelihood evaluation when every declared conditional is normalized. Maximum-likelihood training is direct; ancestral generation follows the chosen order. Architecture, representation, post-training, and decoding determine the actual quality and systems behavior.

    Core Principles¤

    • Chain Rule Factorization

      Decompose \(p(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \prod_i p(x_i \mid x_{<i})\) for tractable training

    • Autoregressive Property

      Element \(i\) depends only on elements \(< i\), enforced by masking

    • Exact Likelihood

      Exact model log-likelihood for normalized conditionals; data fit and arbitrary inference remain separate questions

    • Simple Training

      Discrete MLE uses standard cross-entropy; continuous conditionals require their declared density loss

    When to Use Autoregressive Models¤

    Strong candidates when:

    • Text generation (GPT, LLaMA, code models)
    • Exact likelihood tasks (compression, density estimation)
    • Sequential data with natural ordering (time series, audio)
    • Long-range dependencies (via Transformers)
    • A single-objective likelihood baseline is valuable even if another family will later be used

    Question or combine with another family when:

    • The chosen unit creates too many serial decisions for the latency budget
    • An explicit global latent representation, bidirectional revision, or arbitrary conditioning is central
    • The data are sets, graphs, or geometric objects and an arbitrary order would violate required symmetry—unless the order is marginalized, randomized, canonicalized with justification, or combined with an equivariant construction

    For graph-valued data, Random Graphs and Relational Generators derives how an ordering-dependent trajectory law relates to labeled and unlabeled terminal-graph probabilities.

    Open Research Directions¤

    • Interpretive synthesis—test-time compute as a first-class scaling axis: reasoning-token budgets, parallel sampling, search, and verifiers form an active research area. Their cost-adjusted scaling, faithfulness, and generalisation beyond verifiable domains remain open.
    • Interpretive synthesis—sparse capacity versus communication: MoE systems motivate better reporting of total/active parameters, routing stability, expert specialization, and network cost. Whether sparse or dense models dominate a future regime is open.
    • Interpretive synthesis—hybrid memory: SSM-attention systems test when compressed recurrence should be supplemented by explicit token retrieval. Effective context, not advertised window alone, is the key outcome.
    • Interpretive synthesis—verified block generation: multi-token heads, learned drafts, and tree proposals may reduce serial target calls. Exactness, acceptance, memory traffic, and latency under load remain joint constraints.
    • Peer-reviewed research and preprints—iterative language generation: discrete diffusion provides a serious alternative factorization and sampler for some language tasks; controlled data/compute and end-to-end latency comparisons remain incomplete.
    • Preprint and peer-reviewed research—continuous representations: MAR and CALM-style systems show that autoregressive units need not be discrete, while exposing new density, codec, reconstruction, and evaluation questions.
    • Interpretive synthesis—multimodal composition: future systems may interleave tokens, use shared latent spaces, or retain modality-specific components. Public interface behavior alone cannot identify the internal factorization.

    Cumulative design exercise¤

    You must generate molecular graphs with permutation-invariant evaluation, hard valence constraints, calibrated uncertainty, and an interactive latency budget. Write a defensible first design rather than naming a fashionable model.

    Worked solution: one complete design

    Represent construction as a sequence of graph-edit actions whose legal set enforces valence. Because node order is artificial, train with randomized valid orderings or define and justify a canonical order; evaluate sensitivity to that choice. Use locally normalized action conditionals to retain tractable trajectory likelihood, but recognize that several action sequences may yield the same terminal graph, so terminal likelihood requires summing trajectories unless the construction is unique. For latency, propose several edits with a draft policy and verify them against the target only if the constraint state and correction preserve the declared distribution. Report validity, uniqueness, novelty, property calibration, likelihood or trajectory likelihood, wall-clock latency, active parameters, and order-ablation results. Compare with a permutation-equivariant graph diffusion or GFlowNet baseline under matched data and compute. This answer composes autoregression with constraints; it does not claim the arbitrary action order is physically meaningful.

    Final misconception check¤

    • Exact model likelihood is not exact knowledge of the data distribution.
    • A causal Transformer is an architecture; an autoregressive model is a factorization.
    • Parallel training does not imply parallel ancestral sampling.
    • Low perplexity does not guarantee factuality, reasoning, diversity, or preference.
    • A larger beam does not sample better and need not improve task quality.
    • A long accepted context window does not prove effective use of distant information.
    • Hybrid composition is not algebraic equivalence.
    Retrieval practice: cumulative synthesis
    1. State the minimal ingredients needed to define an autoregressive model over continuous vectors.
    2. Why can terminal-object likelihood differ from trajectory likelihood in constructive graph generation?
    3. Which comparison would test whether an architectural speedup survives at matched quality?
    4. What is the most important first question when someone says two modern families are “the same”?

    Solutions. (1) An ordering, a normalized conditional density with the correct support for every step, parameters, and a base/first-element conditional; practical use also needs a sampler. (2) Several action orders can reach the same terminal object, so its probability is the sum over their trajectory probabilities unless the construction is unique. (3) Match data, quality target, workload, precision, hardware, and batch/concurrency, then report end-to-end latency and memory. (4) Ask which relationship is meant: algebraic equivalence, distributional equivalence under assumptions, population-optimum correspondence, shared objective/estimator, implementation analogy, or hybrid composition.
