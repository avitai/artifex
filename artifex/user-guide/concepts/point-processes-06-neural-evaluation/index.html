    6. Neural Models, Evaluation, and Current Research¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the complete preceding volume and the evidence labels used throughout this book.

    Numbered learning outcomes. After completing the chapter, you can:

    1. O1 — Classify neural interfaces. Identify whether a network parameterizes intensity, cumulative hazard, next-event density, a latent field, a configuration transition, or amortized inference.
    2. O2 — Track normalization obligations. State which integrals, determinants, stopping probabilities, or reverse-transition arguments remain after neural parameterization.
    3. O3 — Diagnose temporal calibration. Apply time rescaling with its regularity, censoring, sample-size, marginal, and independence conditions.
    4. O4 — Evaluate complete configurations. Test cardinality, empty space, temporal and spatial dependence, marks, rollouts, privacy, and computation rather than one next-event score.
    5. O5 — Design controlled comparisons. Match windows, conditioning, reference measures, numerical accuracy, baselines, budgets, and leakage controls.
    6. O6 — Bound research conclusions. Distinguish a peer-reviewed result, preprint claim, benchmark counterexample, and general family-level conclusion.

    Neural parameterizations by probabilistic interface¤

    “Neural point process” is an umbrella for several different constructions.

    Table POINT.5. Neural parameterizations by probabilistic interface
    Interface What the network parameterizes Normalization burden Examples
    Conditional intensity nonnegative \(\lambda(t,m\mid\mathcal H_t)\) compensator integral RMTPP, Neural Hawkes, self-attentive/Transformer intensities
    Cumulative hazard monotone \(\Lambda(t\mid\mathcal H)\) derivative must be nonnegative; endpoint behavior matters fully neural cumulative-hazard models
    Next-event density or flow normalized gap/mark conditional terminal survival and stopping still required Point Process Flows, conditional normalizing flows
    Latent intensity random field or latent state driving events posterior marginalization neural Cox and latent-state point processes
    Configuration transition add, thin, birth, death, move, or score on sets reversal/matching and finite-window law Add and Thin and related configuration generators
    Amortized estimator maps observed configurations to process parameters or functions uncertainty and family coverage foundation inference models for point processes

    Intensity-based sequence models¤

    RMTPP uses a recurrent history embedding to parameterize timing and marks. Neural Hawkes uses a continuous-time LSTM state with event-driven jumps and continuous decay. Self-Attentive Hawkes Process replaces recurrent history aggregation with self-attention and time-aware encoding. These are architectural choices inside a declared intensity model; their names do not guarantee Hawkes branching semantics.

    Fully Neural Network Based Models for General Temporal Point Processes parameterize a monotone cumulative hazard so its derivative supplies the intensity and the compensator is evaluated directly. Monotonicity, positivity, differentiability, and tail behavior are structural constraints, not cosmetic activation choices.

    Intensity-free conditional distributions¤

    Point Process Flows parameterizes conditional inter-event distributions using normalizing flows rather than choosing an intensity form. This can give normalized gap densities and reparameterized samples, but the model still needs a coherent mark law, observation-window convention, and terminal survival probability. “Intensity free” means the implementation does not use intensity as its primary parameterization; it does not remove point-process probability semantics.

    For space–time events, Neural Spatio-Temporal Point Processes combine continuous-time neural dynamics and conditional spatial flows. Factorizing time and location can ease normalization, but it imposes an ordering of conditionals whose adequacy must be tested on joint spatial–temporal structure.

    Configuration-valued models and a score limitation¤

    Configuration transition models operate on the whole random finite set and can express parallel births, deaths, or moves. Their advantage over next-event factorization depends on cardinality accuracy, interaction recovery, simulation cost, and finite-step approximation—not parallelism alone.

    Preprint. Score Matching for Estimating Finite Point Processes formalizes scores relative to Janossy measures and shows an important boundary: a spatial score alone need not identify a general nonparametric finite point-process law because it can omit cardinality or survival information. The paper augments score matching with a survival-classification term. This is current preprint evidence, not a settled universal impossibility theorem for every restricted family or reference convention.

    In-context inference and constraint-aware generation¤

    Peer-reviewed research. FIM-PP, accepted at ICLR 2026, amortizes inference of temporal point-process intensity functions from example sequences. It changes the inference interface: a pretrained estimator can adapt in context without per-dataset optimization. It does not change the underlying distinction among Poisson, Hawkes, renewal, or other target laws, and its uncertainty must be evaluated under family shift.

    Peer-reviewed research. Forward-Chaining Temporal Point Process, published as an ICML 2026 regular paper, combines continuous-time event generation with a latent symbolic state and differentiable multi-step rules for constraint-aware generation. Separate non-archival workshop versions also exist, so the venue claim here refers specifically to the linked regular-paper record. Constraint satisfaction, event-law calibration, and the validity of the rule layer are separate evaluation axes.

    Peer-reviewed research. Towards Reliable Detection of Empty Space, accepted at ICLR 2026, treats object detections as a marked spatial point process and emphasizes empty-space probabilities. It illustrates why evaluating only matched detected objects can ignore the probability assigned to regions containing no object.

    Preprint/submission. Asynchronous diffusion and speculative multi-event proposals have also been proposed for temporal point processes. Until venue decisions, assumptions, and independent comparisons are established, they remain provisional compositions rather than canonical replacements for intensity, flow, or thinning methods.

    Likelihood is necessary but not sufficient¤

    Held-out point-process log likelihood must report:

    • the observation window and boundary history;
    • continuous-time or discretized reference measure;
    • whether the terminal survival term is included;
    • mark factorization and missing-mark treatment;
    • compensator approximation and tolerance;
    • normalization per event, per sequence, per unit exposure, or per area;
    • treatment of empty sequences and zero-event windows.

    Per-event NLL can hide count errors by excluding empty windows. Per-window NLL can be dominated by exposure length. Report both when they answer different questions.

    Time rescaling and residual analysis¤

    For a correctly specified simple temporal point process under the standard regularity, completeness, and non-termination assumptions, transformed inter-event compensator increments

    \[ z_i=\int_{t_{i-1}}^{t_i} \lambda(s\mid\mathcal H_s)\,ds \]

    are iid \(\operatorname{Exp}(1)\), and

    \[ u_i=1-e^{-z_i} \]

    are iid uniform. The classical diagnostic is developed by Brown et al. (2002). A 2025 generalized time-rescaling theorem makes clear that terminating processes and incomplete realizations need additional treatment rather than automatic application of the standard theorem.

    Complete worked diagnostic¤

    Suppose a fitted model yields compensator increments

    \[ z=(0.2,\ 1.0,\ 2.0). \]

    Then

    \[ u=1-e^{-z}\approx(0.1813,\ 0.6321,\ 0.8647). \]

    These three values are consistent with the transformation but far too few for a useful uniformity or independence judgment. A responsible analysis pools comparable held-out sequences only when their conditioning and censoring conventions match, plots transformed residuals, tests serial dependence as well as marginal uniformity, and checks the terminal censored interval separately.

    Time rescaling is not an omnibus proof of correctness. A test can have low power, parameter fitting affects null distributions, and passing temporal residuals does not validate marks or spatial structure.

    A configuration-level evaluation matrix¤

    Table POINT.6. A configuration-level evaluation matrix
    Target property Suitable checks Common blind spot
    Cardinality count histogram, proper count score, empty-set probability conditioning on nonempty windows
    Temporal calibration time-rescaled residuals, interval coverage, count calibration over horizons testing only next-event mean error
    Spatial first order intensity maps, held-out regional counts confusing intensity with interaction
    Clustering or repulsion pair correlation, Ripley's \(K/L\), empty-space and nearest-neighbor functions matching only average density
    Marks proper scoring rules, class calibration, conditional mark diagnostics ignoring rare or missing marks
    Joint rollouts multi-horizon counts, burst/quiet-period duration, constraint validity teacher-forced evaluation only
    Memorization/privacy nearest-neighbor and membership analyses with exposure controls treating novel timestamps as novel sequences
    Computation training cost, likelihood quadrature, proposals, accepted events, wall time, memory reporting only network parameters

    Spatial summary statistics are not interchangeable. Ripley's \(K\) compares expected neighbor counts across radii, pair correlation is a local second-order quantity, and empty-space/nearest-neighbor functions probe different aspects of gaps. Use simulation envelopes or calibrated tests under the fitted composite null, not isolated curves interpreted without uncertainty.

    From a score to a defensible claim¤

    Moving downward adds a different falsification opportunity. No single rung can be substituted for all later ones: good NLL does not prove empty-space calibration, and one benchmark reversal does not rank model families universally.

    Controlled comparison protocol¤

    A fair family comparison matches:

    1. training windows, exposure, missingness, and marks;
    2. boundary histories and censoring treatment;
    3. conditioning information and external covariates;
    4. likelihood/reference conventions;
    5. parameter, active-compute, and sampling budgets;
    6. numerical accuracy for compensators, determinants, or chains;
    7. domain baselines and leakage controls;
    8. both predictive and generative metrics.

    EarthquakeNPP, published in TMLR in 2026, provides useful counterevidence: after correcting data leakage and evaluation issues, a classical ETAS baseline remained competitive with or better than evaluated neural point-process systems in that setting. The correct conclusion is bounded to its protocol and domain. It supports retaining strong mechanistic baselines, not declaring neural point processes generally ineffective.

    Independent practice and assessment map: neural models and evaluation¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. Use the success criterion to decide whether to revise before reading the complete solution.

    1. P1.1 — Classify before comparing. Type: compare · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 10 min. For a model that outputs a monotone cumulative hazard, a conditional gap normalizing flow, and an add–thin reverse kernel, name the probabilistic interface and the remaining normalization or correctness obligation for each.
    2. P1.2 — Predict before computing. Type: predict · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 8 min. If two complete inter-event compensator increments are \((\log2,\log4)\), first predict which uniform transform is larger, then compute both exponential-to-uniform transforms. State two properties these values should satisfy across a sufficiently large correctly handled sample.
    3. P1.3 — Expose an empty-window blind spot. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. Two unit windows contain zero and one event. Model A uses rate one in both; model B uses rate four in the empty window and rate one in the event window. Compute total log likelihood for each and show why a per-event score that discards empty windows cannot distinguish them.
    4. P1.4 — Diagnose a false residual pass. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 12 min. A paper reports three transformed residuals near \((0.2,0.6,0.9)\) and declares the model calibrated because they span \([0,1]\). Identify at least four missing checks or assumptions.
    5. P1.5 — Design a controlled benchmark. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Compare a classical Hawkes/ETAS model, a neural intensity, and an intensity-free flow on marked space–time data. Specify splits, histories, likelihood units, numerical tolerances, generative diagnostics, compute budgets, and leakage controls.
    6. P1.6 — Research transfer. Type: research · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Using the linked FIM-PP, Forward-Chaining TPP, empty-space detector, score-matching preprint, and EarthquakeNPP records, write one supported sentence per source and one tempting conclusion that the combined evidence still does not justify.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The monotone network parameterizes cumulative hazard; its derivative must be nonnegative and its endpoint/tail behavior must define a valid event-or-survival law. The flow parameterizes a normalized next-gap density; marks and terminal stopping still need definitions. Add–thin parameterizes configuration transitions; the forward/reverse construction, endpoint law, and finite-step approximation must justify the sampler. Check. None of the three labels alone supplies every component of a finite-window law.
    2. P1.2. The transforms are \(1-1/2=0.5\) and \(1-1/4=0.75\). Across a sufficiently large sample handled under the theorem's conditions, transformed values should be marginally uniform and mutually independent. Check. Exact arithmetic for two values does not itself test either property.
    3. P1.3. Model A gives \(-1+(\log1-1)=-2\). Model B gives \(-4+(\log1-1)=-5\). If the score drops empty windows and averages NLL only over the one observed event, both report \(1\). Check. The three-log-unit difference is entirely the empty-window survival penalty.
    4. P1.4. Three values provide almost no power; visual span is not a uniformity test; independence/serial dependence was not checked; terminal censoring and possible process termination were ignored; fitting on the same data changes the null; and marks or spatial structure remain untested. Rubric. Full credit requires at least four distinct issues, including one theorem assumption and one untested law component.
    5. P1.5. Freeze chronological and spatially separated train/validation/test windows, pre-window histories, exposure, marks, missingness, and covariates. Use the same continuous reference and report per-window plus per-event likelihood with quadrature/flow tolerances. Evaluate counts, empty space, time-rescaled dependence, spatial pairs, marks, and free rollouts; match parameter/active-compute/sampling budgets and report wall time. Deduplicate sequences, audit future and geographic leakage, and tune only on validation data. Rubric. Full credit requires every requested control and at least one strong domain baseline diagnostic.
    6. P1.6. FIM-PP supports in-context amortized estimation within its evaluated process distribution; the regular ICML Forward-Chaining paper proposes a symbolic-state constraint interface; the ICLR empty-space paper shows a marked point-process treatment of undetected regions; the score-matching preprint identifies a Janossy/cardinality-related nonidentifiability and proposes survival classification; EarthquakeNPP reports that five tested neural systems did not beat ETAS in its leakage-corrected earthquake protocol. Together they do not prove that one neural interface is universally best—or that neural point processes universally fail. Rubric. Each sentence must preserve venue, scope, and empirical/theoretical status.

    Selection guide¤

    Table POINT.7. Selection guide
    Need Begin with Escalate when
    Independent events with varying exposure nonhomogeneous Poisson residual interaction remains
    Gap-based regularity or duration effects renewal or self-correcting model full history or marks matter
    Triggering and interpretable excitation linear marked Hawkes nonlinear history improves held-out law and calibration
    Latent environmental clustering Cox/LGCP posterior field cost or non-Gaussian structure dominates
    Explicit spatial inhibition Gibbs or DPP cardinality/geometry exceeds tractable kernel or energy form
    Unknown object count and tracking identity RFS/multi-object Bayes model learned representations improve matched, leakage-free protocols
    Flexible event history neural intensity or conditional flow a simpler mechanistic baseline fails controlled diagnostics
    Parallel configuration generation add–thin or configuration transitions demonstrated cardinality and interaction gains justify added complexity
    Rapid adaptation across process families amortized/in-context inference coverage and uncertainty under family shift are validated

    Research questions that remain open¤

    • How can configuration generators preserve exact cardinality and empty-space semantics while reducing sequential sampling cost?
    • Which neural parameterizations remain identifiable under missing events, latent exposure, and dependent censoring?
    • How should uncertainty be calibrated for amortized point-process inference outside its training family?
    • Can spatial and temporal residual tests be made powerful in high-dimensional marked spaces without learning the judge on contaminated data?
    • Which invariances, conservation laws, and causal mechanisms should be built into scientific event generators?
    • How can benchmarks prevent leakage while retaining realistic long histories, rare bursts, and domain-specific baselines?

    Chapter 6 misconception checks¤

    If transformed residuals look uniform, is the fitted model correct?

    No. Uniformity is one necessary implication under stated assumptions. Independence, terminal censoring, marks, spatial structure, test power, parameter estimation, and alternative misspecifications still matter.

    Does an in-context point-process model define a new generated-object family?

    No. It is an inference architecture over a collection of point-process laws. The generated object remains a configuration; the family and reference conventions must still be stated.

    Chapter 6 retrieval questions¤

    1. What distinguishes intensity, cumulative-hazard, and intensity-free neural models?
    2. Why can spatial score matching miss part of a finite point-process law?
    3. What two properties should be checked after time rescaling, in addition to terminal censoring?
    4. What is the bounded lesson of the EarthquakeNPP comparison?
    Complete solutions
    1. They directly parameterize different normalized interfaces: a local hazard requiring integration, a monotone integral whose derivative is the hazard, or a normalized next-gap distribution. Each still needs marks, stopping, and observation-window semantics.
    2. A location score can be invariant to cardinality-dependent factors and therefore fail to identify count or survival mass without an additional term or restricted family.
    3. The transformed increments should have the correct marginal distribution and be independent.
    4. Under its leakage-corrected earthquake protocol, the classical ETAS baseline remained essential and could outperform evaluated neural alternatives; this does not establish a universal ranking.

    Synthesis and next steps¤

    Neural point-process methods differ by the probabilistic interface they parameterize: intensity, cumulative hazard, conditional density, latent field, configuration transition, or amortized estimator. Evaluation must return to the complete generated object—counts, empty space, times, locations, marks, dependence, boundaries, and computation. Continue to Autoregressive Models for sequential factorization, General Markov Generators for configuration-valued jump dynamics, Likelihood and Normalizer Estimation for difficult evidence calculations, and Generative Model Evaluation for protocol design.

    • Brown et al. (2002), The Time-Rescaling Theorem and Its Application to Neural Spike Train Data Analysis — temporal goodness-of-fit diagnostics.

    Book-length routes
