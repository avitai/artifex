    4. Stochastic Interpolants and Schrödinger Bridges¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes stochastic dynamics, KL divergence, and the path distinctions from Chapter 1. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Build a stochastic interpolant: identify the endpoint coupling, interpolation map, latent noise schedule, induced marginal path, and learned targets.
    2. O2 — Define a Schrödinger bridge: formulate path-space relative-entropy projection with a declared reference process and two endpoint constraints.
    3. O3 — Connect static and dynamic views: relate endpoint matrix scaling, reference bridges, Doob transforms, and controlled stochastic dynamics.
    4. O4 — Separate bridges from OT: state what finite reference noise changes and what assumptions are required in a zero-noise limit.
    5. O5 — Diagnose identifiability and support: determine what unpaired endpoint marginals cannot specify and when reference zeros make a bridge infeasible.
    6. O6 — Transfer to a bridge experiment: design reference, endpoint, discretization, and semantic-coupling ablations with calibrated evidence.

    A stochastic interpolant constructs

    \[ X_t=I(t,X_0,X_1)+\gamma(t)Z, \]

    where \(Z\) supplies controllable noise and \(\gamma(0)=\gamma(1)=0\). Stochastic Interpolants derive velocity and score-based dynamics from this shared construction. Deterministic interpolation appears when \(\gamma=0\); diffusion-like paths arise for suitable schedules and couplings.

    The framework is a unifying language, not a claim that all choices are identical. \(I\), \(\gamma\), endpoint coupling, learned target, and sampler determine the actual algorithm.

    For \(t\in(0,1)\), the score of the Gaussian-latent interpolant obeys

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_t(x) =-\gamma(t)^{-1}\mathbb E[Z\mid X_t=x] \]

    under the framework's regularity assumptions. Velocity and score objectives therefore arise from the same declared interpolant, but their finite losses and endpoint conditioning differ. The stochastic-interpolant source also derives a family of forward and backward Fokker--Planck dynamics with adjustable diffusion; these dynamics share the constructed marginal path, not one common path law.

    Schrödinger bridge¤

    Given a reference path measure \(R\)—often Brownian diffusion—the Schrödinger bridge solves

    \[ \min_{P:\,P_0=p_0,\,P_1=p_1} D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|R). \]

    It selects the path law closest in relative entropy to the reference while matching both endpoint marginals. This differs from merely learning any transport between endpoints. Diffusion Schrödinger Bridge uses iterative forward/backward fitting for generative modeling.

    Static endpoint projection and dynamic lift¤

    Let \(R_{01}\) be the joint law of \((X_0,X_1)\) under the reference. The static Schrödinger problem is

    \[ \min_{\pi\in\Pi(p_0,p_1)}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi\|R_{01}). \]

    Under standard positivity conditions, the solution has scaling form \(\pi^*(dx_0,dx_1)=a(x_0)R_{01}(dx_0,dx_1)b(x_1)\). The full path law then keeps the reference conditional bridge between endpoints:

    \[ P^*(d\omega) =\int R(d\omega\mid x_0,x_1)\,\pi^*(dx_0,dx_1). \]

    This factorization separates two roles: endpoint potentials choose how much mass each reference bridge receives, while the reference process specifies the conditional temporal fluctuations. Iterative proportional fitting or Sinkhorn scaling alternately enforces the two marginals in finite settings.

    For a reference diffusion with drift \(b_R\) and noise scale \(\sqrt\varepsilon\), the dynamic control view penalizes deviation from the reference:

    \[ \min_u\ \mathbb E\!\left[\frac{1}{2\varepsilon} \int_0^1\|u_t(X_t)\|^2dt\right], \quad dX_t=[b_R(t,X_t)+u_t(X_t)]dt+\sqrt\varepsilon\,dW_t, \]

    subject to both endpoint marginals. The exact coefficient depends on convention, but the invariant is stable: changing \(R\) or \(\varepsilon\) changes the optimization problem.

    Relation to optimal transport¤

    For Brownian reference with noise level tending to zero, entropic transport can converge toward dynamic optimal transport under suitable assumptions. At nonzero noise, the Schrödinger bridge is a stochastic entropy-regularized transport with different path law and cost. “SB equals OT” without the limiting regime is false.

    The stochastic-control treatment makes the relationship precise for controlled references and studies zero-noise limits. One must specify the scaling of the reference noise, topology of convergence, cost, endpoint regularity, and whether the statement concerns endpoint couplings, marginal paths, or controlled path laws. A finite-\(\varepsilon\) entropic plan is often smoother and computationally convenient; that is a different object, not a defective OT solution.

    Paired translation and inverse problems¤

    Bridges can connect two nontrivial data distributions, making them useful for translation, restoration, and trajectory inference. If only unpaired endpoints are available, the bridge is selected relative to the reference process—not necessarily the semantic pairing a user intends. Conditioning observations or boundary constraints changes the bridge problem and identification assumptions.

    Worked discrete bridge intuition¤

    On a finite Markov chain, choose a reference transition matrix and endpoint distributions. Reweighting reference paths by endpoint potentials can satisfy both marginals while minimally changing path KL. Changing the reference transition graph changes which paths are possible and therefore changes the bridge, even with identical endpoints.

    For an explicit one-step example, let the reference endpoint joint be uniform,

    \[ R_{01}=\begin{pmatrix}1/4&1/4\\1/4&1/4\end{pmatrix}, \]

    and require \(p_0=(3/4,1/4)\) and \(p_1=(2/5,3/5)\). Row scaling first gives \(\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}3/8&3/8\\1/8&1/8\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\); column scaling then gives

    \[ \pi^*=p_0p_1^\top =\begin{pmatrix}3/10&9/20\\1/10&3/20\end{pmatrix}. \]

    Because the reference endpoints are independent, the KL projection is the independent coupling with the required marginals. A non-product reference would retain some reference dependence after scaling.

    Brownian bridge, Schrödinger bridge, and diffusion bridge¤

    A Brownian bridge usually conditions a reference process on fixed endpoint values. A Schrödinger bridge constrains endpoint distributions and selects a new path law by KL projection. “Diffusion bridge” may refer broadly to conditioned diffusions or to learned generative bridge methods. Always ask which endpoints are fixed, which are distributions, and which reference law is being conditioned or controlled.

    Retrieval practice: interpolants and bridges
    1. Which object makes a Schrödinger bridge relative rather than an absolute shortest path between endpoints?
    2. What happens to the bridge problem when the reference noise or dynamics changes?
    3. Why are deterministic optimal transport and a finite-noise Schrödinger bridge not the same coupling?
    4. What information can paired data add that endpoint marginals alone cannot identify?

    Solutions. (1) The declared reference path measure. (2) The path-space KL objective and therefore the optimal controlled process change. (3) The bridge trades endpoint matching against relative entropy to a stochastic reference; optimal transport is recovered only in specific limiting formulations. (4) A semantic or observational coupling between source and target records.

    Misconception checks: interpolants and bridges¤

    • “The endpoints identify the bridge.” They identify constraints; the reference path law selects among feasible paths.
    • “A Brownian bridge and a Schrödinger bridge are synonyms.” One commonly pins endpoint values; the other imposes endpoint marginals through a KL projection.
    • “Sinkhorn scaling proves a learned diffusion bridge is exact.” Matrix scaling solves a discretized static projection; learning, time discretization, and state approximation remain.
    • “Small noise can simply be set to zero.” A convergence theorem and scaling regime are required; finite-noise formulas may become singular.

    Independent practice and assessment map: interpolants and bridges¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: endpoint scaling. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. For the displayed uniform \(R_{01}\) and required marginals, predict whether the KL-minimizing coupling is dependent. Verify the row and column sums of \(\pi^*\).
    2. P1.2 — Execute two IPF updates. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Starting from \(R_{01}\), perform the displayed row scaling and column scaling, state why a second row update is unnecessary here, and identify what property of the reference makes this happen.
    3. P1.3 — Diagnose reference support failure. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Replace \(R_{01}\) by a diagonal matrix with entries \(1/2\). Can any finite-KL coupling have endpoint relation \(X_1=1-X_0\) with both endpoints uniform? Justify using absolute continuity.
    4. P1.4 — Compare bridge meanings. Type: compare · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 9 min. Classify fixed-value Brownian conditioning, distribution-constrained Schrödinger bridging, and deterministic quadratic-cost OT by endpoint object, stochastic reference, and optimized quantity.
    5. P1.5 — Audit a zero-noise claim. Type: evaluate · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. A paper says “our bridge is OT because \(\varepsilon\) is small.” List the minimum mathematical and finite-compute evidence needed to support an approximation claim, and reject the stronger identity claim.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to unpaired translation. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design a bridge study between two unpaired image domains. Declare reference dynamics, endpoint constraints, semantic side information, support, fitting algorithm, finite solver, baselines, and a test for reference-induced rather than semantic pairing.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The minimizing coupling is independent because projecting a uniform product reference onto fixed marginals preserves product form. Its rows sum to \((3/10+9/20,1/10+3/20)=(3/4,1/4)\) and columns to \((3/10+1/10,9/20+3/20)=(2/5,3/5)\). Check. All entries are positive, so the KL is finite.
    2. P1.2. Row factors three-halves and one-half give rows \((3/8,3/8)\) and \((1/8,1/8)\). Their columns are both \(1/2\); multiplying columns by \((2/5)/(1/2)=4/5\) and \((3/5)/(1/2)=6/5\) gives \(\pi^*\). Because both rows had identical conditional column proportions, column scaling preserves the row totals. Check. A general dependent reference requires further alternating updates.
    3. P1.3. No. Every finite-KL coupling must be absolutely continuous with respect to the diagonal reference, hence can place mass only on \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\). The relation \(X_1=1-X_0\) requires antidiagonal mass, where \(R_{01}=0\), producing infinite KL. Check. Matching endpoint marginals alone does not guarantee feasible path support.
    4. P1.4. A Brownian point bridge conditions a stochastic reference on \(X_0=x_0,X_1=x_1\). A Schrödinger bridge constrains \(P_0=p_0,P_1=p_1\) and minimizes path-space KL to a reference. Deterministic quadratic OT chooses a coupling or continuity-equation flow minimizing squared displacement or kinetic action, with no finite stochastic reference in the basic formulation. Check. All can share endpoint locations in a special case while optimizing different objects.
    5. P1.5. A defensible approximation claim states the reference-noise scaling, cost, endpoint assumptions, topology and quantity of convergence, and cites or proves the relevant limit. Finite experiments sweep \(\varepsilon\), discretization, and solver tolerance against an OT coupling/action oracle with uncertainty. A single small nonzero value remains a finite-noise entropic bridge, so identity with OT is unsupported. Rubric. Distinguish theorem assumptions from empirical closeness.
    6. P1.6. Declare a Brownian, latent, or domain-informed reference; empirical endpoint marginals; any labels, paired subset, observation operator, or cycle constraint; feasible support; IPF/forward-backward learning; and solver schedule. Compare references and OT/diffusion/paired baselines at matched cost, evaluating endpoint realism, paired holdout task accuracy, diversity, reversibility, path cost, and sensitivity. If pair semantics change substantially across references while endpoint metrics remain fixed, the pairing is reference-induced rather than identified by marginals. Rubric. Do not call cycle consistency alone proof of the true semantic coupling.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Stochastic interpolants expose a broad design space; Schrödinger bridges choose a stochastic path by relative entropy to a reference. Their relationship to flow matching and OT depends on construction and limiting assumptions. Chapter 5 adds augmented-space and discrete transports, then states what shared marginals do not guarantee.
