    • Probabilistic Circuits 2 — Structural Properties

    This chapter constructs probability distributions from tractable leaves, mixture sums, factorized products, scopes, and shared subcircuits. For uninterrupted reading, use the complete guide.

    1. From Arithmetic Expression to Probability Model¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    You should understand finite mixtures, factorized distributions, DAGs, and probability masses/densities with respect to a declared base measure. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. evaluate a sum/product circuit bottom-up and compute every node's scope;
    2. state sufficient conditions under which the root is normalized;
    3. interpret sum choices as discrete latent variables without expanding the DAG into a mixture tree;
    4. distinguish circuit evaluation, probabilistic validity, and query tractability; and
    5. construct and normalize a complete finite-state example.

    Consider variables \(X=(X_1,\ldots,X_D)\). A circuit is a DAG. Every node \(n\) has a scope \(\operatorname{sc}(n)\): the variables on which its output depends.

    • A leaf computes a tractable univariate or small multivariate mass/density, such as a Bernoulli, categorical, Gaussian, or histogram.
    • A product node multiplies child values.
    • A sum node computes a nonnegative weighted sum of child values.

    For input \(x\), write

    \[ v_n(x)= \begin{cases} p_n(x_{\operatorname{sc}(n)}) & n\text{ is a leaf},\\ \prod_{c\in\operatorname{ch}(n)}v_c(x) & n\text{ is a product},\\ \sum_{c\in\operatorname{ch}(n)}w_{nc}v_c(x) & n\text{ is a sum}. \end{cases} \]

    When leaves are normalized, sum weights are nonnegative and normalized, and the graph satisfies the required scope constraints, the root computes a normalized distribution. Products express independence factorizations; sums express mixtures. Sharing subcircuits lets the DAG reuse distributions rather than expand into an exponentially large mixture tree.

    More precisely, a standard sufficient construction is smooth and decomposable: children of each sum have the same scope, and children of each product have disjoint scopes. If every leaf integrates to one over its scope and each sum's outgoing weights sum to one, induction over the DAG proves every node integrates to one over its scope. Without those conditions, a positive arithmetic circuit may still define a nonnegative function, but its partition function need not be one or tractable.

    The reference measure matters. Bernoulli and categorical leaves use counting measure; Gaussian leaves use Lebesgue measure. Mixing a mass and a density over the same variable without an explicit mixed measure is not a well-defined shortcut. Likewise, a missing-value sentinel is an observed category only if the model says so; otherwise missingness is a query operation that marginalizes an unobserved variable.

    Three different uses of “tractable”¤

    Keep three complexity claims separate:

    1. Evaluation tractability: given a circuit and a complete assignment, one bottom-up pass evaluates its arithmetic function in \(O(|E|)\) scalar node operations plus leaf cost.
    2. Query tractability: a particular marginal, maximization, expectation, or circuit operation has a polynomial algorithm under stated properties.
    3. Representation tractability: the target distribution admits a compact circuit satisfying those properties.

    The first can hold while the second fails, and both can hold while the third requires exponential circuit size. Learning adds a fourth question: whether data and optimization can discover the compact representation when one exists.

    A conceptual evaluator is short:

    for node in topological_order_from_leaves:
    if leaf(node): value[node] = leaf_value(node, observation_or_query)
    if product(node): value[node] = product(value[child] for child in children(node))
    if sum(node): value[node] = sum(weight[node, child] * value[child]
                                     for child in children(node))
return value[root]

    Changing leaf_value from point evaluation to a leaf marginal turns the same graph into a marginal algorithm only when the structural proof in Chapter 2 applies.

    A Running Four-Variable Circuit¤

    Let \(X_1,X_2,X_3,X_4\) be binary. Build two alternative decompositions of the same full scope:

    \[ C_A(X)=C_{12}^{A}(X_1,X_2)C_{34}^{A}(X_3,X_4), \]
    \[ C_B(X)=C_{12}^{B}(X_1,X_2)C_{34}^{B}(X_3,X_4), \]

    and mix them:

    \[ C(X)=\lambda C_A(X)+(1-\lambda)C_B(X). \]

    Inside either product branch, the two variable groups are independent. After mixing over the unobserved branch, they need not be marginally independent. This is the same “mixture of factorizations” mechanism that makes mixture models more expressive than any component.

    Introduce a latent branch variable \(H\in\{A,B\}\). Then

    \[ p(x)=\sum_h p(h)\,p(x_{1:2}\mid h)p(x_{3:4}\mid h). \]

    Nested sum nodes introduce several latent choices. Expanding all choices produces a mixture whose number of components can grow exponentially with depth, while shared subcircuits keep the representation and evaluation graph compact. This latent-variable view is useful for EM, but it does not imply every combination of local choices is reachable: DAG sharing and deterministic support constraints can couple which traces represent valid assignments.

    Circuits, SPNs, and Compiled Models¤

    The terminology overlaps several traditions:

    • sum-product networks emphasize learned deep sum/product DAGs;
    • arithmetic circuits may be compiled from Bayesian networks or logical theories;
    • probabilistic sentential decision diagrams combine logical and probabilistic structure;
    • Einsum Networks organize regular circuit computations into dense tensor operations.

    Probabilistic circuit is the umbrella representation. The graph is not merely a neural architecture: its structural constraints determine which probabilistic queries remain exact.

    A positive sum/product network is not automatically tractable for every query

    Evaluating one complete assignment can be easy while marginal, MAP, entropy, or circuit-combination queries remain hard. State the circuit properties and query together.

    Worked example: normalize and evaluate a two-variable circuit¤

    Let \(X,Y\in\{0,1\}\) and

    \[ C(x,y)=0.6\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.8)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.7) +0.4\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.2)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.1). \]

    Each product node is decomposable because one child has scope \(\{X\}\) and the other \(\{Y\}\). The root sum is smooth because both products have scope \(\{X,Y\}\). Therefore

    \[ \sum_{x,y}C(x,y)=0.6(1)(1)+0.4(1)(1)=1. \]

    For \((x,y)=(1,1)\), the child products are \(0.8\cdot0.7=0.56\) and \(0.2\cdot0.1=0.02\), so \(C(1,1)=0.344\). The arithmetic evaluation costs time linear in the circuit edges. That statement concerns this represented function; finding a compact circuit from data is a separate learning problem.

    Counterexample: normalized parts, invalid product

    Let two normalized Bernoulli leaves both have scope \(\{X\}\) and multiply them. The result is \(p_1(x)p_2(x)\), whose sum over \(x\) is generally not one. Normalized children do not make a product normalized when scopes overlap.

    Independent practice and assessment map: probabilistic-circuit semantics¤

    Work from scopes, leaf measures, and local normalization rather than from the graph's appearance. For every claimed probability or complexity result, show the induction or finite enumeration that makes it true.

    P1 — Build and falsify a finite probabilistic circuit¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether each statement is necessarily true: every nonnegative sum/product DAG is normalized; normalized leaves and normalized sum weights suffice even when product scopes overlap; linear-time point evaluation implies linear-time arbitrary marginalization; a smooth sum is a mixture over one common scope; DAG sharing preserves the represented function while reducing repeated computation; and an unobserved missing-value sentinel should always be assigned probability mass. For every false statement, name the missing structural, measure, or query assumption.
    2. P1.2 — Scope and normalization ledger. Let \(C(x,y)=0.3\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.9)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.2)+0.7\,\operatorname{Bern}(x;0.4)\operatorname{Bern}(y;0.8)\). Draw the leaf, product, and sum DAG; label every scope; evaluate all four assignments; verify their masses sum to one; and compute the root value at \((1,0)\) in two independent ways. Give one overlapping-scope edit that keeps point evaluation easy but destroys the standard normalization proof.
    3. P1.3 — Measure and missingness contract. Design a two-variable circuit for a nonnegative count and a positive continuous measurement. Declare counting and Lebesgue reference measures, leaf supports, units, and any support transform. Compare treating a missingness flag as an observed Bernoulli variable with marginalizing an unobserved measurement. Show why mixing a point mass and density on the same coordinate without a mixed reference measure is not a valid shortcut.
    4. P1.4 — Latent traces and sharing. Interpret the root of P1.2 as a latent branch \(H\). Then construct \(0.4\,S(X,Y)p_A(Z)+0.6\,S(X,Y)p_B(Z)\) with a shared normalized subcircuit \(S\). Expand it as a mixture tree and compare nodes, edges, values, and latent traces with the shared DAG. Generalize the trace count for \(L\) nested binary sums and identify which combinations can disappear under deterministic support constraints.
    5. P1.5 — Separate three tractability claims. Give one circuit that is easy to evaluate but not yet proved normalized, one normalized smooth/decomposable circuit with expensive multivariate leaf marginals, and one compact-query circuit class whose representation of a chosen target requires a large compiled graph. For each, state input size, query, leaf oracle, and complexity measure; reject any conclusion that moves from evaluation to validity, query tractability, representation size, or learnability without a proof.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer. Transfer P1.2 to either zero-inflated event counts with exposure or circular sensor angles. Redefine support, base/reference measure, leaves, scopes, normalized sums, and required queries. Provide a finite or quadrature normalization oracle and explain which Bernoulli-circuit arguments survive unchanged and which must be replaced.

    Chapter 1 Synthesis¤

    A PC composes tractable leaf distributions with mixture and factorization operations. Normalization follows from explicit local conditions and an induction over scopes—not from drawing a sum/product DAG. The next chapter asks which additional properties preserve particular queries.

    Retrieval practice: circuit semantics
    1. What does a sum node mean probabilistically when it is normalized and smooth?
    2. Why must a normalized product's child scopes be disjoint in the standard construction?
    3. Does linear-time evaluation prove the circuit is a normalized distribution?
    4. What does DAG sharing save compared with expanding every latent trace?

    Solutions. (1) A mixture over child distributions on the same variables. (2) Disjoint scopes let the integral factor into the product of child integrals without integrating the same variable twice. (3) No. Any arithmetic DAG can be evaluated; validity requires parameter, leaf, scope, and measure conditions. (4) It reuses identical subcomputations/subdistributions and can avoid an exponentially larger mixture tree.

    Bridge. Semantics tells us what function the graph computes. Chapter 2 turns structural properties into a query-by-query guarantee map. Continue continuously or open Chapter 2.
