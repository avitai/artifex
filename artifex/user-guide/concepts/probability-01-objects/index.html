    • Probability 2 — Factorizations and Generative Queries

    This chapter starts from four generative queries, then separates probability laws, random variables, mass functions, densities, joints, marginals, and conditionals. For uninterrupted reading, use the continuous view.

    Begin with Four Questions¤

    Before introducing notation, imagine a table containing two binary attributes for each observation. A generative model of that table may be asked four different questions. The same probability law can answer all four, but each answer requires a different operation.

    • What could happen?

      Draw a new pair \(X=(X_1,X_2)\) from the joint law.

    • How plausible is this pair?

      Evaluate its probability mass—or a density relative to a named measure.

    • What follows from what I saw?

      Condition on \(X_1\) and compute or sample \(X_2\mid X_1\).

    • What was hidden?

      Introduce a latent \(Z\) and infer \(p(Z\mid X)\) rather than following the generative direction.

    The chapter builds the vocabulary needed to state these questions without ambiguity. Keep the questions in view: a formula matters because it enables an operation, not because it adds another symbol to memorize. The complete numeric version appears in the two-bit worked example.

    Learning Objectives¤

    After this chapter you should be able to:

    1. distinguish random variables, probability measures, PMFs, and densities;
    2. move between joint, marginal, and conditional distributions;
    3. derive the chain rule and Bayes' rule rather than memorize them;
    4. explain why a density value is not itself a probability for continuous data;
    5. distinguish likelihood evaluation, density estimation, sampling, conditional generation, representation learning, and structured prediction;
    6. convert discrete negative log probability into ideal code length and explain the cross-entropy penalty of a mismatched model;
    7. identify the reference measure and support assumptions behind a generative-model equation;
    8. separate a conditional predictive law from a loss-dependent point decision;
    9. identify when a constructive generator has no declared output probability law;
    10. recognize discrete, continuous, and mixed laws and choose a distribution whose support matches the generated object;
    11. distinguish event conditioning from a conditional law evaluated at a continuous value; and
    12. transform a law through bijective and many-to-one maps and repair the probability integral transform when the law has atoms.

    1. Probability Laws, PMFs, and Densities¤

    Picture first: probability is assigned to regions

    On a finite grid, a PMF places mass directly on cells. On a continuous line, a density specifies mass per unit length, so the probability of an interval is its area under the density. This is why the height of a density can exceed one while the probability of every region remains at most one.

    A random variable \(X\) is a measurable map from an underlying probability space \((\Omega,\mathcal F,P)\) to a measurable state space \((\mathcal X,\mathcal A)\). Its law is the pushforward measure

    \[ P_X=P\circ X^{-1}, \]

    so for every measurable region \(A\in\mathcal A\),

    \[ P_X(A)=P\!\left(X^{-1}(A)\right)=P(X\in A). \]

    Here \(X^{-1}(A)\) means the set of outcomes mapped into \(A\); it does not require the function \(X\) to have an inverse. This distinction matters for implicit and many-to-one generators: a measurable map always induces an output law once its input is random, even when no output density or inverse map exists.

    For a discrete state space, the probability mass function

    \[ p_X(x)=P(X=x) \]

    satisfies \(p_X(x)\ge 0\) and \(\sum_x p_X(x)=1\). For a continuous variable, a density is defined relative to a reference measure—usually Lebesgue measure—and probabilities are integrals:

    \[ P_X(A)=\int_A p_X(x)\,dx, \qquad p_X(x)\ge0, \qquad \int_{\mathcal X}p_X(x)\,dx=1. \]

    A continuous density can exceed one; only its integral over a region must be at most one. Changing coordinates changes the density through a Jacobian even though the underlying probability law is unchanged. This distinction becomes central for normalizing flows, likelihood comparisons, and manifold-valued data.

    Not Every Law Is Discrete or Continuously Densitized¤

    The PMF-versus-PDF split is useful but incomplete. A law may contain atoms and continuous mass at the same time. Let \(B\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(0.3)\) and \(U\sim\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) be independent, and define

    \[ X= \begin{cases} 0,&B=1,\\ U,&B=0. \end{cases} \]

    Then

    \[ P_X=0.3\,\delta_0+0.7\,\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1). \]

    Consequently, \(P(X=0)=0.3\), while \(P(a<X<b)=0.7(b-a)\) for \(0\le a<b\le1\). No ordinary Lebesgue density can represent the atom: the integral of any finite density over the single point \(\{0\}\) is zero. There is also no PMF over the uncountable continuous component. The law itself is well defined; only the attempted single-density representation fails.

    A common dominating measure restores a density when one is useful. Relative to \(\mu=\delta_0+\lambda|_{(0,1)}\), where \(\lambda\) is Lebesgue measure, the same law has Radon--Nikodym derivative \(dP_X/d\mu=0.3\) at the atom and \(0.7\) on the open interval. This is why measure-level definitions are safer than silently assuming that every model distribution has a PDF with respect to ambient volume.

    Reference Measures Matter¤

    Writing \(p(x)\) suppresses the measure relative to which the density is defined. A Bernoulli PMF is a density relative to counting measure; a Gaussian PDF is a density relative to Lebesgue measure. On a sphere or another manifold, the natural volume measure differs from ambient Euclidean volume. Comparing log densities defined against different reference measures is meaningless unless the conversion is included.

    This is why a continuous density evaluated on raw discrete pixels is not yet a valid discrete-image likelihood. One must integrate density over each quantization bin or define a dequantization distribution and account for it in the objective.

    Support¤

    The support is the region to which a distribution assigns nonzero probability in the relevant measure-theoretic sense. Support mismatch explains several practical phenomena:

    • maximum-likelihood objectives heavily penalize missing observed regions;
    • the original GAN discriminator can become perfect when real and generated supports are separated;
    • a continuous bijection cannot map a connected base support to a disconnected target support exactly;
    • adding Gaussian noise gives a distribution full support in Euclidean space, making scores better behaved away from the data manifold.

    Support has topological as well as measure-theoretic consequences. A continuous bijection maps connected sets to connected sets, so an ordinary flow from a connected Gaussian base cannot exactly represent a target supported on two disconnected components without augmentation, discontinuity, or density in the space between. A decoder with observation noise can assign a full-dimensional density around a lower-dimensional manifold, but the choice of noise model changes the likelihood semantics.

    A Distribution Atlas Organized by the Generated Object¤

    Distribution names are shorthand for support, tail, dependence, and sampling assumptions. Choose them by asking what object is generated and which events must receive nonzero probability.

    Binary labels, categories, and counts¤

    • A Bernoulli law generates one bit. A categorical law generates one label from \(K\) named alternatives. Their parameters are probabilities, not unconstrained neural logits; a softmax or another normalization maps logits to the simplex.
    • Binomial and multinomial laws generate aggregate counts from a fixed number of conditionally IID trials. A multinomial count vector sums to the declared trial count. It is not a model for an arbitrary nonnegative vector.
    • A Poisson law generates a nonnegative count with equal mean and variance. A negative-binomial construction can represent overdispersion, but its probability, mean, and dispersion parameterizations differ across sources and software. State which convention is used before comparing parameters.

    Real-valued observations¤

    • A Gaussian law supplies a location and a positive scale, has thin tails, and is closed under linear conditioning and marginalization. Those algebraic advantages do not make Gaussian observation noise appropriate for every modality.
    • A Laplace law has a sharper central cusp and exponential tails. Its negative log density produces an absolute-error term only when its scale is fixed and the normalization is handled consistently.
    • A Student-\(t\) law can be derived as a Gaussian scale mixture and provides polynomial tails. Its degrees of freedom control more than appearance: the mean is undefined for \(\nu\le1\) and the variance is infinite for \(\nu\le2\). The Cauchy law is the \(\nu=1\) case, so reporting its sample mean as an estimate of a population mean has no target.

    Positive, bounded, and simplex-valued quantities¤

    • Exponential and gamma laws live on the positive half-line. The exponential law is memoryless; a gamma shape parameter permits increasing, decreasing, or unimodal densities. Rate and scale are reciprocals and must not be interchanged silently.
    • Log-normal and Pareto laws are also positive but encode different tail behavior. A log-normal arises by exponentiating a Gaussian; a Pareto model makes a power-law tail assumption. Finite samples rarely identify extreme tails without substantial uncertainty.
    • A beta law lives on \((0,1)\) and a Dirichlet law lives on a simplex. If \(\Pi\sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_K)\) and \(\alpha_0=\sum_j\alpha_j\), then \(\mathbb E[\Pi_j]=\alpha_j/\alpha_0\). Holding these ratios fixed while increasing \(\alpha_0\) concentrates the law; it does not add more categories.

    For categorical counts \(n_1,\ldots,n_K\), a Dirichlet prior gives posterior \(\operatorname{Dirichlet}(\alpha_1+n_1,\ldots,\alpha_K+n_K)\). Three point summaries that are often confused are

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat\pi_j^{\mathrm{MLE}} &=\frac{n_j}{N},\\ \mathbb E[\Pi_j\mid n] &=\frac{n_j+\alpha_j}{N+\alpha_0},\\ \widehat\pi_j^{\mathrm{MAP}} &=\frac{n_j+\alpha_j-1}{N+\alpha_0-K}, \end{aligned} \]

    where the displayed interior MAP formula requires every \(n_j+\alpha_j>1\). Otherwise a mode may lie on a simplex boundary and the interior formula is invalid. With a uniform \(\operatorname{Dirichlet}(1)\) prior, adding one to each count gives the posterior mean and one-step posterior predictive probability—not the MAP estimate.

    Vectors, matrices, directions, and manifolds¤

    • A multivariate Gaussian uses a positive-definite covariance, while a multivariate Student law adds heavy-tailed radial behavior. Diagonal covariance asserts conditional coordinate independence within that one distribution; it does not identify semantic factors.
    • A von Mises--Fisher law models directions on a sphere relative to its surface measure. Its location parameter is a mean direction, whereas the expectation vector also contains a concentration-dependent length.
    • Matrix-normal, Wishart, and inverse-Wishart laws model matrices or positive-definite matrices under declared row/column and scale conventions. Parameterizations vary, and convenient conjugacy does not make a covariance prior weakly informative in high dimension.
    • Sets, graphs, point configurations, functions, and manifold-valued objects need laws on their actual state spaces. Flattening them into a Euclidean vector may discard permutation, topology, or geometry that the model should preserve.

    The atlas is a starting point, not a selection theorem. Mixtures, truncations, hierarchies, transformations, stochastic processes, and neural conditional parameters create richer laws, but every construction still owes the reader a support, reference measure when a density is used, and a valid sampling story.

    2. Joint, Marginal, and Conditional Distributions¤

    The joint distribution is the complete ledger. Marginalization folds that ledger along one axis; conditioning selects a slice and renormalizes it. These are two different operations even when both begin from the same table.

    For random variables \(X\) and \(Y\), the product rule is

    \[ p(x,y)=p(x\mid y)p(y)=p(y\mid x)p(x). \]

    Marginalization removes an unobserved variable:

    \[ p(x)=\sum_y p(x,y) \quad\text{or}\quad p(x)=\int p(x,z)\,dz. \]

    Conditioning renormalizes the joint distribution on the event or value observed:

    \[ p(x\mid y)=\frac{p(x,y)}{p(y)}, \qquad p(y)>0. \]

    Combining the two product-rule factorizations yields Bayes' rule:

    \[ p(z\mid x)=\frac{p(x\mid z)p(z)}{p(x)}, \qquad p(x)=\int p(x\mid z)p(z)\,dz. \]

    In a latent-variable model, \(p(z)\) is a prior, \(p(x\mid z)\) is a likelihood or decoder distribution, \(p(x)\) is the marginal evidence, and \(p(z\mid x)\) is the posterior. The evidence integral is often the first intractable quantity encountered in deep generative modeling.

    Read Bayes' rule as a workflow

    Start with a prior belief about \(z\), score how each \(z\) could have produced the observation, and normalize across all possibilities. The numerator ranks explanations; the evidence makes their total probability one.

    Conditioning on an Event and Conditioning at a Value¤

    The elementary ratio \(P(A\mid B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)\) applies when \(P(B)>0\). For a continuous observation, however, \(P(Y=y)=0\) at every ordinary point, so the conditional density formula must not be read as a ratio of point probabilities. Where joint and marginal densities exist, it defines a density for almost every \(y\) with \(p_Y(y)>0\):

    \[ p_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)=\frac{p_{X,Y}(x,y)}{p_Y(y)}. \]

    The measure-level object is a probability kernel \(K(y,A)=P(X\in A\mid Y=y)\). On standard Borel state spaces, regular conditional distributions exist, but two valid versions may disagree on a set of \(y\) values having \(P_Y\)-measure zero. A model can choose a continuous or otherwise structured version; the joint law alone determines it only almost everywhere.

    For example, if \(Y=X\) and \(X\) has a continuous law, the natural conditional kernel is

    \[ P(X\in A\mid Y=y)=\delta_y(A). \]

    It says that observing \(Y=y\) reveals \(X=y\), even though the event \(\{Y=y\}\) has probability zero. Writing \(P(X=x,Y=y)/P(Y=y)\) would instead produce the meaningless ratio \(0/0\). In generative modeling this distinction appears whenever a conditional model accepts a real-valued context, a diffusion conditions on an exact measurement, or an inverse problem imposes a noiseless constraint.

    Exchangeability, Sequences, and Sets¤

    A joint distribution over a set is invariant to permutations; a joint distribution over a sequence generally is not. The chain rule can factor either distribution in any chosen order, but an architecture may inject ordering information that violates the intended symmetry. For exchangeable data, symmetrization or an invariant/equivariant architecture is part of the model specification, not a cosmetic implementation choice.

    Conditioning Is Not Intervention¤

    Observational conditioning uses \(p(y\mid x)\). A causal intervention \(p(y\mid\operatorname{do}(x))\) replaces the structural mechanism that produced \(x\). Confounding can make the two disagree. Conditional text-to-image generation, class guidance, or attribute editing does not establish counterfactual validity without a causal model and identifiability assumptions.

    Probability-object retrieval practice¤

    Retrieval practice: laws, densities, marginals, and conditionals
    1. What must be named before writing a density \(p(x)\)?
    2. How do a PMF, a density, and a probability law differ?
    3. What operation obtains a marginal from a joint law, and what extra event obtains a conditional law?
    4. Why is a model's sampled tensor representation not necessarily the semantic sample space?

    Solutions. (1) The sample space/support and reference measure or coordinate convention. (2) A law assigns probability to events; a PMF is its mass function on a countable space; a density is a Radon–Nikodym derivative relative to a measure and can exceed one. (3) Sum/integrate out unwanted variables; conditioning additionally restricts and renormalizes on observed information. (4) Padding, ordering, tokenization, quotient symmetries, or a lossy codec can give several tensors one meaning or exclude semantic objects.

    Probability-object synthesis¤

    Generative modeling begins with the object, sample space, law, observation mechanism, and query. PMFs and densities are representations relative to a measure; joint, marginal, and conditional distributions are different operations on one law. These distinctions determine what later likelihoods, samplers, and evaluations can mean.
