    4. Superposition and Multimodal State Spaces¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes product probability spaces and generator notation. By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Distinguish four constructions: separate generator sums, convex same-path combinations, random switching, and mixtures of whole path laws.
    2. O2 — Verify valid superposition: check common state space/domain, nonnegative clocks, covariance addition, conservativity, and non-explosion.
    3. O3 — Preserve a marginal path deliberately: use adjoint linearity and identify when an added mechanism changes only paths versus marginal time.
    4. O4 — Couple product spaces: encode cross-modal dependence through joint coefficients, shared events, and state-transforming jumps.
    5. O5 — Quantify splitting error: relate noncommuting generators to Lie and Strang approximations and distinguish local error from invalid transitions.
    6. O6 — Transfer composition safely: design an auditable hybrid generator for a new multimodal or variable-structure task.

    Adding generators¤

    If \(\mathcal L_1\) and \(\mathcal L_2\) are valid generators on a common domain, then under suitable well-posedness

    \[ \mathcal L=\alpha_1\mathcal L_1+\alpha_2\mathcal L_2, \qquad \alpha_i\ge0, \]

    represents superposed infinitesimal mechanisms. Nonnegative scaling changes clocks or strengths. Negative coefficients generally destroy Markov positivity and are not “subtractive generation.”

    “Under suitable well-posedness” matters. For finite CTMCs, nonnegative sums of rate matrices are again rate matrices. For regular SDE components, drifts and covariances can be combined and the resulting SDE or martingale problem checked. For unbounded operators, a common dense domain is only the starting point: closure and perturbation conditions are needed to know that the formal sum generates a semigroup. A simulator that alternates two individually valid mechanisms does not repair an ill-posed continuous sum.

    Validity of the sum is not the same as preservation of a chosen probability path. If \(\mathcal L_1\) and \(\mathcal L_2\) each generate the same \(p_t\), then the convex combination

    \[ \mathcal L_t=\lambda_t\mathcal L_{1,t}+(1-\lambda_t)\mathcal L_{2,t}, \qquad 0\le\lambda_t\le1, \]

    also generates that path because the weak forward equation is linear. An arbitrary positive sum usually changes the marginal evolution unless its extra component has zero adjoint action on \(p_t\).

    If each component individually satisfies \(\mathcal L_{i,t}^*p_t=\partial_tp_t\), their unnormalized sum satisfies \((\mathcal L_{1,t}+\mathcal L_{2,t})^*p_t=2\partial_tp_t\): it generally doubles the marginal clock rather than preserving the same scheduled path. A convex combination retains the derivative. By contrast, if every component is \(p\)-stationary, so \(\mathcal L_i^*p=0\), any nonnegative sum remains \(p\)-stationary. These two cases are often conflated.

    For independent jump mechanisms, rates add. For independent diffusion noises, covariance matrices add, not arbitrary signed noise matrices. Drift fields add directly.

    For two independent jump clocks with exit rates \(\lambda_1(x)\) and \(\lambda_2(x)\), the next event time is exponential with total rate \(\lambda_1+\lambda_2\); conditional on an event, mechanism \(i\) is selected with probability \(\lambda_i/(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)\). This competing-clock construction realizes the sum exactly when the rates are constant between events or the corresponding integrated-hazard procedure is used. It is not a mixture that selects one mechanism once per sample.

    Product state spaces¤

    Let \(X=(C,Y)\) contain discrete category \(C\) and continuous value \(Y\). A generator may combine:

    \[ \mathcal L\varphi(c,y) =b(c,y)^T\nabla_y\varphi +\frac12\operatorname{Tr}(a(c,y)\nabla_y^2\varphi) +\sum_{c'\ne c}q(c,c';y) [\varphi(c',T_{c\to c'}y)-\varphi(c,y)]. \]

    The jump can transform continuous coordinates through \(T\). This models a label switch, bond edit, birth/death event, or modality transition without pretending all variables share a Euclidean representation.

    Multimodal coupling¤

    Independent modality generators produce a product distribution and cannot learn cross-modal alignment. Coupling enters through coefficients conditioned on the joint state, shared events, or joint conditional targets. A text-token jump rate can depend on an image latent; an image drift can depend on text state.

    Missing modalities require a defined state and inference procedure, not a zero-filled tensor by default. Observed-only, imputed, masked, and nonexistent variables have different semantics.

    If the joint generator separates as \(\mathcal L=\mathcal L_C+\mathcal L_Y\), with the \(C\) coefficients independent of \(Y\) and the \(Y\) coefficients independent of \(C\), then an initially factorized law stays factorized. Joint training data alone do not create alignment in such dynamics. Dependence requires at least one of:

    • coefficients conditioned on the other component;
    • a joint event that changes several components;
    • a shared latent variable retained in the state;
    • a conditional target whose posterior couples the coefficient regressions.

    Appending an observed/missing indicator is often necessary but not sufficient: the reference distribution and transition rules for that indicator must also be declared.

    Worked superposition¤

    Suppose a molecule has continuous coordinates \(R\) and discrete bonds \(B\). Coordinate diffusion alone cannot change graph topology; bond jumps alone cannot relax geometry. A hybrid generator uses

    \[ \mathcal L=\mathcal L_{\mathrm{coordinate\ diffusion}} +\mathcal L_{\mathrm{bond\ jumps}}. \]

    If bond rates depend on invariant geometric features and coordinate dynamics are equivariant, the joint process can respect rigid motions. Alternating two simulators approximates the superposition but introduces operator-splitting error because the components need not commute.

    Worked noncommuting two-state split¤

    Let

    \[ Q_1=\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad Q_2=\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\2&-2\end{pmatrix}. \]

    \(Q_1\) activates \(0\to1\) and \(Q_2\) activates \(1\to0\). Both and their sum are valid rate matrices, but

    \[ [Q_1,Q_2]=Q_1Q_2-Q_2Q_1 =\begin{pmatrix}2&-2\\2&-2\end{pmatrix}\ne0. \]

    Lie splitting uses \(e^{hQ_1}e^{hQ_2}\) or the reverse order and has a leading local discrepancy proportional to \(h^2[Q_1,Q_2]\). Strang splitting \(e^{hQ_1/2}e^{hQ_2}e^{hQ_1/2}\) cancels that second-order term and has local error of order \(h^3\) under the required regularity. Each factor is stochastic, so these particular finite products remain valid transition matrices even while they approximate the wrong finite-time law. Higher-order compositions with negative substeps may lose Markov positivity.

    Composition versus mixture¤

    Adding generators makes mechanisms act within the same path. Mixing path laws first samples which entire process to run. These are not the same:

    \[ e^{t(\mathcal L_1+\mathcal L_2)} \ne \pi e^{t\mathcal L_1}+(1-\pi)e^{t\mathcal L_2} \]

    in general. Operator splitting, random switching, and mixture-of-experts each define different processes.

    Table MARKOV.14. Composition versus mixture
    Construction What happens along one sample Finite-time object
    generator sum both mechanisms act through competing infinitesimal effects \(e^{t(L_1+L_2)}\) when time homogeneous
    path-law mixture choose a latent component once, then keep it \(\pi e^{tL_1}+(1-\pi)e^{tL_2}\)
    Markov switching a mode process changes which mechanism is active semigroup on an augmented state \((X,M)\)
    operator splitting apply sub-solvers sequentially each step product of approximate or exact sub-semigroups

    Misconception checks: composition¤

    • “Two generators of the same path can simply be added.” Their convex combination preserves the scheduled derivative; their sum generally changes its speed.
    • “Independent modality noise learns dependence.” Factorized coefficients preserve factorization unless coupling enters elsewhere.
    • “A stochastic split step is exact because every factor is stochastic.” Valid transition matrices can still approximate the wrong semigroup.
    • “Mixture weights and clock rates play the same role.” One chooses an entire latent process; the other changes infinitesimal event competition.
    Retrieval practice: superposition and multimodal generators
    1. What process does \(L=L_1+L_2\) describe when both components act on the same state?
    2. Why is generator addition different from sampling one of two generators once from a mixture?
    3. What is missing if image and text components evolve independently but the target requires alignment?
    4. Which compatibility condition must hold before adding two formal generators?

    Solutions. (1) Interleaved infinitesimal mechanisms whose rates or fluxes add. (2) A mixture selects a global latent component, whereas a sum allows both mechanisms to act during one trajectory. (3) Cross-component drift, jump, conditioning, or shared latent coupling. (4) They must share a well-defined state space and a common domain on which the sum generates a conservative, non-explosive process.

    Independent practice and assessment map: composition¤

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: competing clocks. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 8 min. Two independent event mechanisms have rates \(1\) and \(3\) while the state is fixed. Predict the mean next-event time and the more likely mechanism, then compute both and compare with choosing either mechanism once with probability \(1/2\).
    2. P1.2 — Preserve or accelerate a path. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 10 min. Suppose \(L_1^*p_t=L_2^*p_t=\dot p_t\). Compute the adjoint action of \(\lambda L_1+(1-\lambda)L_2\) and of \(L_1+L_2\). Repeat when both components leave a fixed \(p\) stationary.
    3. P1.3 — Add independent diffusion noise correctly. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 9 min. In two dimensions, one mechanism has noise vector \((1,0)^T\) and another \((0,2)^T\), driven independently. Compute the joint covariance and give one valid matrix square root. Explain why adding the two noise vectors would be wrong.
    4. P1.4 — Measure noncommutation. Type: derive · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Multiply the displayed \(Q_1,Q_2\), recover their commutator, and state the leading local orders of Lie and Strang splitting. Which conclusion is about stochastic validity and which about accuracy?
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose a factorized multimodal model. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 14 min. Image state \(Y\) and token state \(C\) start independently, and each generator's coefficients depend only on its own modality. Prove from the product semigroup or forward equation that factorization persists. Name two minimal coupling repairs.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to topology-and-geometry generation. Type: transfer · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 20 min. Design a coupled coordinate-diffusion/bond-jump generator. Declare equivariance, rate validity, coordinate transforms at jumps, explosion control, and a split-versus-joint-clock validation.

    Feedback contract: composition¤

    Table MARKOV.15. Feedback contract: composition — table 1
    Task Evidence of success
    P1.1 Total rate four, mean waiting time \(1/4\), and mechanism probabilities \(1/4,3/4\) are distinguished from a path mixture.
    P1.2 Convex same-path preservation, doubled derivative under the sum, and stationary closure under sums are all correct.
    P1.3 Covariances rather than noise vectors are added and the proposed factor reproduces the covariance.
    P1.4 The exact commutator and local orders are recovered while positivity is kept separate from finite-time exactness.
    P1.5 Factorization is derived and both repairs introduce actual cross-modal dependence.
    P1.6 Every operator and simulator condition is tied to the joint state rather than checked modality by modality only.
    Graduated hints for P1
    Table MARKOV.16. Feedback contract: composition — table 2
    Task Hint 1 — Direction Hint 2 — Setup
    P1.1 The minimum of independent exponential clocks adds rates. Conditional event type is proportional to its rate.
    P1.2 Apply the adjoint linearly. For stationarity replace \(\dot p_t\) by zero.
    P1.3 Independent Brownian contributions add outer products. Use \(a=v_1v_1^T+v_2v_2^T\).
    P1.4 Compute \(Q_1Q_2-Q_2Q_1\). Lie has a commutator term at order \(h^2\); symmetry cancels it for Strang.
    P1.5 The product of two independent semigroups acts separately. Let a token rate depend on image state or add a shared event.
    P1.6 A bond event may need to transform or reinitialize coordinates. Compare against a small system where \(e^{hQ}\) or a joint event simulator is available.

    Outcome-to-task alignment: composition¤

    Table MARKOV.17. Outcome-to-task alignment: composition
    Learning objective Independently assessed by
    O1 — Distinguish four constructions P1.1, P1.4
    O2 — Verify valid superposition P1.1, P1.3, P1.6
    O3 — Preserve a marginal path deliberately P1.2

    O5 — Quantify splitting error P1.4, P1.6
    O6 — Transfer composition safely P1.6
    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The combined waiting time is \(\operatorname{Exp}(4)\) with mean \(1/4\). Mechanisms one and two are selected with probabilities \(1/4\) and \(3/4\). A half-half path mixture instead first selects a process; conditional waiting times then have means \(1\) or \(1/3\), and its survival is \(\tfrac12e^{-t}+\tfrac12e^{-3t}\) rather than \(e^{-4t}\). Check. Mixture and competing clocks differ before the first event.
    2. P1.2. \([\lambda L_1+(1-\lambda)L_2]^*p_t=\lambda\dot p_t+(1-\lambda)\dot p_t=\dot p_t\), whereas \((L_1+L_2)^*p_t=2\dot p_t\). If \(L_i^*p=0\), both the convex combination and any nonnegative sum give zero. Check. A stationary path has no scheduled derivative to double.
    3. P1.3. The covariance is \(\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0\\0&4\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\); \(\sigma=\operatorname{diag}(1,2)\) is one square root. Adding vectors gives \((1,2)^T(1,2)=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\), which introduces spurious correlation. Check. Independent noise coordinates are separate columns.
    4. P1.4. \(Q_1Q_2=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}2&-2\\0&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\) and \(Q_2Q_1=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&0\\-2&2\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\), so the displayed commutator follows. Lie local error begins at \(h^2\) and Strang at \(h^3\). Products of the exact positive-time sub-semigroups are stochastic, but nonzero error means they are not the exact sum semigroup. Check. Validity does not imply accuracy.
    5. P1.5. With \(P_t=P_t^C\otimes P_t^Y\), \((p_C\otimes p_Y)P_t=(p_CP_t^C)\otimes(p_YP_t^Y)\), so factorization persists. Make token rates depend on image state, make image drift depend on tokens, or add a joint/shared event. Check. Merely concatenating independent embeddings without coefficient dependence changes no transition law.
    6. P1.6. Use an equivariant tangent coordinate drift/covariance conditioned on bonds and nonnegative bond rates conditioned on invariant geometry. Each bond event declares a map on coordinates, the rate diagonal is the negative exit sum, and total rates satisfy a bound or Lyapunov condition. Compare joint-clock simulation with Lie/Strang orders on a small enumerated graph-coordinate benchmark and report constraint error. Rubric. Independent ablations are insufficient unless the coupled coefficient paths and split error are also tested.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Generator superposition rigorously combines compatible infinitesimal mechanisms and supports mixed discrete/continuous systems. It does not make independent modalities aligned or remove splitting error. Chapter 5 connects each operator to its finite simulator and time-reversal requirements.
