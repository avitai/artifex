    6. Compilation, Differentiation, and Learned Inference¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes exact and approximate message passing and basic automatic differentiation. By the end, you should be able to:

    • derive marginals from derivatives of a log partition function;
    • distinguish compiled exact reuse from learned amortization;
    • explain why integrated nested Laplace approximation is specialized to a latent Gaussian model with sparse structure and low-dimensional hyperparameters;
    • identify what remains exact when messages are unrolled and differentiated;
    • distinguish exact configuration-level Gumbel-Max sampling, approximate low-dimensional perturbations, and exact Gaussian perturb-and-solve;
    • separate probabilistic BP from a general GNN or MPNN; and
    • design an evaluation protocol for a learned inference system.

    Differentiate a correct normalizer to obtain moments¤

    Write an exponential-family factorization as

    \[ p_\eta(x)=\exp\{\eta^\top T(x)-A(\eta)\}, \qquad A(\eta)=\log Z(\eta). \]

    Whenever differentiation and summation or integration may be exchanged,

    \[ \nabla_\eta A(\eta)=\mathbb E_{p_\eta}[T(X)]. \]

    For a tabular factor entry parameterized by its log value, this becomes

    \[ \frac{\partial\log Z}{\partial\log f_a(x_a)} =p_\eta(X_a=x_a). \]

    Thus an exact differentiable program for \(\log Z\) can return all represented factor marginals in one reverse-mode pass. The gradient is exact only to the extent that the forward normalizer computation and numerical differentiation are exact.

    Worked example: differentiate the running chain¤

    In the running model, scale \(u_A(0)\) by \(e^\theta\). Differentiating at \(\theta=0\) gives

    \[ \left.\frac{\partial\log Z(\theta)}{\partial\theta}\right|_{\theta=0} =p(A=0)=\frac{54}{87}=\frac{18}{29}. \]

    Likewise, differentiating with respect to \(\log f_{AB}(0,0)\) returns

    \[ p(A=0,B=0)=\frac{12+24}{87}=\frac{12}{29}. \]

    These values agree with enumeration and calibrated messages. Automatic differentiation changed how the marginals were extracted, not the probability law or the sum being computed.

    Four ways to reuse inference¤

    Table MSG.9. Four ways to reuse inference
    Strategy What is reused Guarantee New failure mode
    cached tree/junction messages partial exact contractions exact for supported queries and unchanged assumptions invalid cache after factor/evidence changes
    arithmetic or probabilistic circuit compiled sum-product DAG exact for queries justified by circuit properties compilation size and representation mismatch
    differentiable unrolling a fixed number of solver iterations exact only if the unrolled algorithm reaches an exact result under its schedule truncation bias, unstable gradients, memory cost
    amortized neural solver parameters learned across query instances empirical approximation under the training distribution generalization, calibration, and shortcut learning

    Compilation and amortization both move work before a query, but only compilation preserves a proved symbolic computation by construction. A learned solver may be faster or handle intractable factors, yet its answer needs empirical validation.

    Randomized optimization: when a MAP oracle becomes a sampler¤

    Optimization returns a high-scoring state; sampling must reproduce an entire law. Random perturbations connect the two only under a declared construction. For a finite configuration space \(\mathcal X\) with unnormalized log mass \(a(x)\), draw one independent standard Gumbel variable \(G_x\) for every complete configuration. Then

    \[ X^*=\arg\max_{x\in\mathcal X}\{a(x)+G_x\} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \Pr(X^*=x)=\frac{e^{a(x)}}{\sum_{x'}e^{a(x')}}. \]

    For weights \((1,2,5)\), the returned configuration therefore has probabilities \((1/8,2/8,5/8)\). This is an exact sample, not a temperature heuristic. The difficulty is computational: a structured state with exponentially many configurations appears to require exponentially many independent perturbations and a global argmax.

    Low-dimensional perturb-and-MAP replaces that field with noise on unary or local factors so a structured MAP solver can be reused. It is generally a different output law. For example, if an equality constraint leaves only \(00\) and \(11\), full-configuration Gumbel-Max compares one difference of two Gumbels, which is logistic with variance \(\pi^2/3\). Independently perturbing the two unary factors compares a sum of two independent logistic differences, whose variance is \(2\pi^2/3\). The selection law therefore cannot agree for all score gaps. Factorwise perturbation can be useful, but exactness needs a separate theorem, correction, or special factorization.

    This boundary explains the difference between exact Gumbel-process search such as A* sampling, low-dimensional methods with exact or approximate regimes (Hazan, Maji, and Jaakkola, 2013), and perturb-and-max-product for discrete EBMs, which is presented as approximate sampling. A continuous relaxation of the argmax supplies a differentiable surrogate; it does not preserve the discrete sample law automatically.

    Gaussian quadratic models provide an important exact local construction. If

    \[ p(x)\propto \prod_{\ell=1}^{L} \mathcal N(F_\ell x;\mu_\ell,\Sigma_\ell), \]

    define

    \[ J=\sum_\ell F_\ell^\top\Sigma_\ell^{-1}F_\ell, \qquad h=\sum_\ell F_\ell^\top\Sigma_\ell^{-1}\mu_\ell. \]

    Draw \(\widetilde\mu_\ell\sim \mathcal N(\mu_\ell,\Sigma_\ell)\) independently and solve

    \[ \widetilde x =J^{-1}\sum_\ell F_\ell^\top\Sigma_\ell^{-1}\widetilde\mu_\ell. \]

    Then \(\mathbb E[\widetilde x]=J^{-1}h\) and \(\operatorname{Cov}(\widetilde x)=J^{-1}\), so the solve returns an exact Gaussian sample when \(J\) is nonsingular and the factor perturbations and solve are exact. This Gaussian local-perturbation construction can exploit sparse or matrix-free solvers. Finite solver tolerance changes the sample unless its error is controlled or corrected.

    Keep a randomized-optimization audit with:

    1. the target score and temperature;
    2. the unit being perturbed—configuration, factor, variable, or continuous field;
    3. the noise law and dependence structure;
    4. whether the argmax or linear solve is exact, bounded, relaxed, or truncated;
    5. the theorem or correction connecting returned states to the target law; and
    6. small-state frequency, moment, constraint, and independence checks.

    Gumbel noise can also be used as latent randomness in a learned or distilled generator. For example, Gumbel Distillation for Parallel Text Generation (Preprint, 2026) trains a parallel model using a Gumbel-coupled autoregressive teacher. That is a distillation construction with empirical claims, not evidence that arbitrary local perturb-and-MAP samples the teacher exactly. The few-step and distillation volume provides the corresponding teacher–student evaluation contract.

    Differentiating through messages¤

    Unrolling \(L\) iterations creates a feed-forward computation graph. Gradients can train factor parameters, damping, schedules, or downstream objectives. On a tree with a dependency-respecting complete schedule, the exact result needs finitely many messages; differentiating that exact computation is principled. On a loopy graph, differentiating \(L\) iterations optimizes the behavior of that truncated approximate solver.

    Implicit differentiation treats a converged fixed point \(m^*=F_\theta(m^*)\) as a constraint. It requires a sufficiently regular fixed point and solving a linear system involving \(I-\partial F/\partial m\). Nonconvergence, nonuniqueness, singular Jacobians, or switching between fixed points can invalidate a naive gradient. “No need to store every iterate” is a memory statement, not a correctness theorem.

    Integrated nested Laplace approximation: structure-specific deterministic inference¤

    Some graphical models expose a more specialized deterministic route. A latent Gaussian model has a latent field \(x\) whose conditional prior is Gaussian with sparse precision \(Q(\theta)\), a comparatively low-dimensional hyperparameter \(\theta\), and observations that are conditionally independent given components or linear projections of \(x\):

    Define the conditionally factorized observation likelihood

    \[ L(y\mid x,\theta) =\prod_{i=1}^{n}p(y_i\mid\eta_i(x),\theta). \]

    Then the joint model is

    \[ p(y,x,\theta)=p(\theta)p(x\mid\theta)L(y\mid x,\theta), \]

    with sparse latent Gaussian law

    \[ p(x\mid\theta) =\mathcal N\!\left(x;m(\theta),Q(\theta)^{-1}\right). \]

    The posterior is generally non-Gaussian when the observation laws are, for example, Bernoulli or Poisson. Integrated nested Laplace approximation (INLA) targets marginal posteriors without constructing an MCMC chain. Its two integrations make the name literal:

    \[ \widetilde p(x_i\mid y) =\int \widetilde p(x_i\mid\theta,y) \widetilde p(\theta\mid y)\,d\theta. \]

    With quadrature points \(\theta_k\) and area weights \(\Delta_k\), write

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widetilde p(x_i\mid y) &\approx\sum_k w_k\widetilde p(x_i\mid\theta_k,y),\\ w_k&=\widetilde p(\theta_k\mid y)\Delta_k. \end{aligned} \]

    First, a Gaussian approximation to \(p(x\mid\theta,y)\) around its conditional mode supplies a Laplace approximation to \(p(\theta\mid y)\). Second, Gaussian, simplified-Laplace, or full-Laplace approximations estimate \(p(x_i\mid\theta,y)\) at selected \(\theta_k\). Numerical integration over those points yields the marginal. Sparse Cholesky operations exploit the GMRF precision structure; the low dimension of \(\theta\) makes its numerical integration plausible.

    This is not a theorem that every graphical model has fast, globally accurate Laplace inference. The contract can fail or degrade when the latent field is not conditionally Gaussian, the precision lacks useful sparsity, the hyperparameter dimension is too large for numerical integration, relevant posterior mass is strongly multimodal, or local quadratic/skewness corrections are inadequate. A mode-finding routine also finds a stationary point or local mode under its numerical conditions—not an unconditional global maximum. Check approximation variants against exact small cases, importance sampling, or well-diagnosed MCMC whenever the scientific conclusion is sensitive to tails or joint rather than marginal behavior.

    The original INLA construction (Established / peer-reviewed) explicitly assumes a latent Gaussian field, uses sparsity and few hyperparameters for speed, and studies Gaussian, simplified-Laplace, and Laplace marginal approximations. It therefore belongs beside graphical compilation and differentiation as a structure-aware inference method, not as a universal alternative to Monte Carlo.

    Probabilistic messages, neural messages, and architecture analogies¤

    Keep four designs separate:

    1. Learned factors, exact messages. A network outputs local factor values; a tree or tractable circuit still computes an exact query for the resulting model.
    2. Parameterized BP corrections. Learned operators adjust damping or messages while preserving some BP invariances or constraints.
    3. Unrolled approximate inference. A fixed number of recognizable BP, mean-field, EP, or optimization steps is trained end to end.
    4. General MPNN/GNN. Hidden vectors are exchanged and aggregated on a graph, but they need not be log probabilities, normalized beliefs, or messages for any factorized law.

    Belief Propagation Neural Networks (Peer-reviewed research, NeurIPS 2020) parameterize operators on factor graphs and study retained BP properties. Factor Graph Neural Networks (Peer-reviewed research, NeurIPS 2020) show that a factor-graph neural architecture can represent max-product BP. Neither result makes every trained GNN a calibrated posterior solver.

    Current generative connections¤

    Message structure continues to enter generative modeling in several distinct ways:

    • a score, energy, or decoder can be parameterized by an equivariant MPNN while sampling is performed by diffusion, a flow, MCMC, or autoregression;
    • exact or approximate messages can infer latent variables inside a structured generator;
    • a learned message system can amortize inference across related factor graphs; and
    • graph structure itself can be optimized for a downstream iterative decoder.

    Factored Score Matching on Graphical Models (Workshop paper / preprint, 2026) studies exact score computation by messages on trees and contractive approximations on loopy Markov random fields, then uses the structure in a score-based generator. Its stated graph and contraction assumptions bound the theory; the reported molecular results do not establish a general superiority of factorized score networks.

    Factor Graph Optimization for Belief Propagation Decoding (Preprint / conference submission, 2026 record) differentiates through a representation of BP to optimize code graphs. It is evidence for learned graph-and-algorithm co-design in a particular decoding setting, not an established generic inference guarantee.

    These examples belong in this chapter because they connect a model factorization, inference computation, or generative score to learned message structure. A generic graph architecture with no declared generative object or inference query belongs in a graph-learning text instead.

    Evaluation contract for learned inference¤

    Use instances small enough for exact enumeration or junction-tree answers before scaling. Then report:

    • the model/query distribution used for training and testing, including graph sizes and out-of-distribution structures;
    • whether ground truth is exact, bounded, Monte Carlo estimated, or unavailable;
    • marginal error, calibration, normalizer error, MAP objective gap, or sample discrepancy as appropriate;
    • constraint violations and local/global consistency, not only task accuracy;
    • iterations, messages, active parameters, peak memory, wall-clock hardware, and compilation/training cost;
    • sensitivity to initialization, factor scale, graph diameter, degree, loops, and evidence strength; and
    • baselines matched by target query and compute, including exact methods wherever feasible.

    A solver trained only to predict the correct MAP label may produce excellent decisions and poor posterior probabilities. Conversely, small marginal KL does not prove accurate rare-event probabilities, joint samples, or marginal MAP. Match the metric to the exposed query.

    Method selection¤

    Chapter 6 synthesis¤

    Exact inference can be cached, compiled, differentiated, and—in special randomized constructions—reused for sampling. Approximate inference can be specialized to latent Gaussian structure, randomized through lower-dimensional perturbations, unrolled, implicitly differentiated, or amortized. Neural message passing becomes probabilistic inference only when its target, semantics, and guarantees are stated. Across all variants, model structure, query, algorithm, numerical implementation, and empirical evaluation remain separate layers.

    Retrieval practice: compilation and learning
    1. What does \(\partial\log Z/\partial\log f_a(x_a)\) return for a tabular positive factor?
    2. Why is an arithmetic circuit not the same as an amortized encoder?
    3. When is differentiating through a finite message schedule differentiating an exact answer?
    4. Which structural conditions make INLA a plausible inference strategy?
    5. What must be shown before a learned GNN output can be called a posterior marginal?
    6. Why does adding independent Gumbels to unary factors not generally implement exact Gumbel-Max over complete structured states?

    Solutions. (1) The factor-scope marginal probability \(p(X_a=x_a)\). (2) The circuit stores a symbolic sum-product computation; the encoder learns a predictive approximation across instances. (3) When the schedule completes an exact algorithm under its model and numerical assumptions, as on a finite tree. (4) A latent Gaussian field with useful sparse precision, conditionally factorized observations, and sufficiently few hyperparameters for numerical integration; approximation quality still requires diagnostics. (5) A declared probabilistic target, output semantics and normalization, and accuracy/calibration evidence under the relevant instance distribution. (6) Exact Gumbel-Max requires the joint perturbation field induced by independent noise for every complete configuration. A sum of reusable local Gumbels has a different dependence and difference distribution except under special constructions.
