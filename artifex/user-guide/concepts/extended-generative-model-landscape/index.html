    Readiness check: can you explain the central object and reproduce one worked example from each required chapter? If not, follow its link before continuing.

    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    The best-known neural families do not exhaust generative modeling. Copulas isolate dependence from marginals; generative stochastic networks specify a stationary Markov chain; probabilistic programs compose random choices with arbitrary computation; tensor and Born machines factorize a probability amplitude or table; quantum generators sample measurements of parameterized states; grammars, programs, and cellular systems generate through constructive rules. Random measures place distributions over distributions or unbounded latent structures, point processes generate random event configurations, and random-graph models generate relational structures while confronting labeling, exchangeability, and sparsity.

    This routing volume gives each family enough formal and practical depth to place it correctly. It does not claim equal deployment maturity, and placement never depends on whether Artifex implements the family. The decision criteria are the generative object, state space, probability semantics, and tractable queries—not architectural novelty.

    Reading routes

    Entry after prerequisites: Chapters 1–3, then Chapters 7–9, for dependence, transition-defined laws, model/inference composition, random measures, events, and graphs.

    Practitioner: add Chapters 4 and 6 when compact discrete structure or constructive support matters.

    Research: read all chapters, with particular attention to contraction complexity, hardware-aware quantum evidence, nonparametric inference, point-process calibration, and graph exchangeability.

    1. Copulas: Modeling Dependence Separately¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes probability integral transforms and multivariate distributions. By the end, you should be able to separate marginals from a copula dependence law, state the continuous-marginal conditions behind Sklar's representation, compare Gaussian, Archimedean, vine, and neural copulas, diagnose tail-dependence mismatch, and identify how discrete atoms complicate uniqueness and likelihood.

    Complete family volume

    This chapter is the concise orientation. Use Copula Models for the complete six-chapter volume covering Sklar semantics, classical and tail families, estimation and conditioning, vines, discontinuous observations, neural validity mechanisms, and dependence-specific evaluation.

    Sklar factorization¤

    For a random vector \(X=(X_1,\ldots,X_d)\) with marginal CDFs \(F_i\), Sklar's theorem represents the joint CDF as

    \[ F(x_1,\ldots,x_d) =C(F_1(x_1),\ldots,F_d(x_d)), \]

    where \(C\) is a copula: a multivariate distribution with uniform marginals. When every marginal is continuous, \(C\) is unique. If densities exist,

    \[ f(x)=c(u_1,\ldots,u_d)\prod_{i=1}^d f_i(x_i), \qquad u_i=F_i(x_i). \]

    This is an algebraic decomposition of a joint distribution, not a neural architecture. It lets a practitioner change marginal models while preserving an estimated dependence structure—provided the probability-integral transforms and support assumptions remain valid.

    Sampling and conditioning¤

    To sample:

    1. draw \(u\sim C\);
    2. set \(x_i=F_i^{-1}(u_i)\).

    Conditional queries require conditional copula distributions, often computed analytically for simple families or numerically for vines/neural copulas. Tail dependence matters in finance, hydrology, reliability, and extremes: two copulas with similar central correlation can imply very different joint-tail risk.

    Parametric and structured copulas¤

    • Gaussian copulas express dependence through a correlation matrix but have no nontrivial upper/lower tail dependence away from degenerate correlation.
    • Student-\(t\) copulas add symmetric tail dependence.
    • Archimedean copulas offer low-dimensional interpretable dependence but can be restrictive.
    • Vine copulas factor high-dimensional density into ordered pair copulas; flexibility depends on structure selection and truncation.
    • Neural, flow, and diffusion copulas increase expressivity but must preserve uniform marginals.

    Diffusion and Flow-based Copulas, ICLR 2026, is Peer-reviewed research evidence for marginal-preserving processes that forget and learn to restore dependence. Its empirical results do not make arbitrary diffusion processes valid copulas; marginal preservation is the essential construction.

    Discrete and mixed marginals¤

    For discontinuous marginals the copula is generally not unique. Randomized distributional transforms, latent continuous constructions, or explicit rectangle probabilities are needed. Applying a rank transform and treating the result as a uniquely identified copula silently changes the claim.

    Worked binary dependence¤

    Let \(X,Y\) be fair Bernoulli variables. Independence gives \(p(1,1)=1/4\); perfect agreement gives \(p(1,1)=p(0,0)=1/2\). The marginals are identical in both models, so all information distinguishing them is dependence. Because the marginals are discrete, many continuous copulas can induce the same \(2\times2\) table—an immediate counterexample to uniqueness.

    When to use copulas¤

    Use them when domain-specific marginals are trustworthy, dependence itself is the scientific target, or tail-risk interpretation matters. Prefer a joint latent/flow/diffusion model when marginal/dependence separation adds no useful constraint and high-dimensional conditional generation dominates.

    Retrieval question: what must a neural copula preserve?

    Its generated \(u_i\) must each be uniform on \([0,1]\), not merely lie in that interval. Otherwise it is a generic bounded joint model, not a copula with the advertised marginals.

    Chapter 1 synthesis¤

    Copulas are a probability-level separation of marginal behavior and dependence. Continue to Copula Models for the full treatment. Chapter 2 shifts from explicit joint factorization to a distribution defined by a learned stationary transition.

    2. Generative Stochastic Networks and Learned Stationary Chains¤

    Chapter 2 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes Markov chains and denoising objectives. By the end, you should be able to define a GSN through corruption and reconstruction transitions, state conditions connecting local denoising to a stationary data law, distinguish transition learning from direct likelihood, analyze mixing and spurious modes, and relate GSNs to diffusion and modern iterative generators without claiming finite-algorithm identity.

    Distribution by transition operator¤

    A generative stochastic network (GSN) learns a Markov transition \(T_\theta(x'\mid x)\) whose stationary distribution should approximate data. A common denoising construction corrupts \(x\) into \(\tilde x\) and reconstructs:

    \[ T_\theta(x'\mid x) =\int C(\tilde x\mid x) P_\theta(x'\mid\tilde x)d\tilde x. \]

    Sampling alternates corruption and reconstruction. Deep GSNs formalized conditions under which consistent learned conditionals and an ergodic chain recover the data distribution. The target is implicit through stationarity; one-step reconstruction quality alone is not the stationary-law guarantee.

    Denoising and scores¤

    For small Gaussian corruption, the optimal denoising vector points toward higher data density and is related to the score. This connects denoising autoencoders, score matching, Langevin dynamics, and diffusion. The relationship is assumption-specific: a generic GSN need not use Gaussian noise, a prescribed time-indexed forward process, or a reverse diffusion parameterization.

    Ergodicity, mixing, and spurious states¤

    The chain needs access between relevant modes, an invariant distribution, and sufficient mixing. Failure cases include:

    • nearly disconnected modes and initialization dependence;
    • absorbing artifacts created by the reconstructor;
    • local conditionals accurate near data but arbitrary off manifold;
    • periodic or non-ergodic dynamics;
    • train/test mismatch between short corruption steps and long generated chains.

    These are MCMC diagnostics, not only neural-training diagnostics. Run multiple chains, test autocorrelation/effective sample size where meaningful, inspect mode transitions, and compare short versus stationary-looking samples.

    Conditioning and missing variables¤

    Clamping observed components while updating missing components can produce conditional samples if the transition is compatible with the intended joint and remains ergodic on the clamped subspace. Arbitrarily overwriting coordinates after each transition does not automatically yield the correct conditional.

    Finite-state example¤

    For states \(\{0,1\}\) let

    \[ T=\begin{bmatrix}1-a&a\\b&1-b\end{bmatrix}. \]

    When \(a,b>0\), the stationary law is \(\pi(1)=a/(a+b)\). Many transitions share the same stationary law but mix at different rates. As \(a+b\to0\), the target remains unchanged while transitions between states become rare. This separates distribution correctness from sampling efficiency.

    Placement in the modern landscape¤

    GSNs are historically important because they define generation through a learned Markov operator. Diffusion and general generator matching provide more structured paths and objectives; EBMs provide a stationary target plus generic MCMC. GSNs remain useful conceptually for dependency networks, denoising chains, and arbitrary conditional updates.

    Retrieval question: what two properties are distinct for a GSN?

    Having the desired invariant distribution and reaching it efficiently are distinct. Stationarity does not imply rapid mixing, and visually fast movement does not prove the invariant law is correct.

    Chapter 2 synthesis¤

    GSNs compose a transition and a sampler. Chapter 3 generalizes composition further: probabilistic programs specify arbitrary random computations while inference remains a separate algorithm.

    3. Probabilistic Programming¤

    Chapter 3 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes graphical models and posterior inference. By the end, you should be able to distinguish a probabilistic program's generative semantics from its inference algorithm, trace addresses and control flow, explain how conditioning and stochastic recursion affect measure, compare universal and specialized inference, and identify when an implementation defines an unnormalized score rather than an executable normalized model.

    Programs as generative models¤

    A probabilistic program is ordinary computation augmented with random choices and conditioning. One execution creates a trace

    \[ \tau=\{(a_j,z_j,p_j)\}_{j=1}^{N(\tau)}, \]

    where \(a_j\) is an address, \(z_j\) a sampled value, and \(p_j\) its distribution. Control flow can make the number and identity of choices dynamic. observe(y, likelihood) multiplies trace weight by the observation likelihood.

    For a fixed trace structure, the unnormalized posterior is

    \[ \tilde p(\tau\mid y) =\prod_{j\in sample}p_j(z_j\mid\tau_{<j}) \prod_{k\in observe}p_k(y_k\mid\tau_{<k}). \]

    Marginal likelihood sums or integrates over all compatible traces. Arbitrary branching and recursion make that sum expressive and often intractable.

    Model–inference composition¤

    The program specifies a model; an inference engine answers queries. Choices include exact enumeration/variable elimination, importance sampling, SMC, MCMC over traces, HMC for differentiable fixed-dimensional programs, variational inference, and amortized proposals. Inference compilation learns proposals for sequential importance sampling. Pyro combines deep networks with stochastic variational inference; Gen exposes programmable inference.

    Inference is not semantics-free. HMC cannot cross arbitrary discrete branches; mean-field VI can miss posterior dependence; SMC degenerates without useful proposals; trace MCMC needs reversible moves across changing program structure.

    Worked mixture program¤

    k ~ Categorical(pi)
mu ~ Normal(m[k], s[k])
x ~ Normal(mu, sigma)
observe(x = x_obs)

    Forward execution generates \(x\). Posterior inference asks about \((k,\mu)\) after observing \(x_{obs}\). Enumerating \(k\) and integrating or sampling \(\mu\) is different from unconditional generation. A neural amortized guide can accelerate repeated queries but introduces an amortization gap.

    Simulators and likelihood-free inference¤

    A simulator can be expressed as a probabilistic program even when its likelihood is unavailable. Approximate Bayesian computation, synthetic likelihoods, neural posterior estimation, and likelihood-ratio estimation then connect simulation-based inference with modern generative models. Identifiability and simulator misspecification remain; a flexible inference network cannot recover information the simulator/data do not contain.

    Differentiability and discontinuity¤

    Pathwise gradients work through reparameterizable continuous choices. Score-function estimators handle more general stochastic choices with higher variance. Discrete control flow, rejection loops, and external simulators require specialized estimators. “Written in a differentiable framework” does not make the entire probability program differentiable.

    When to use a PPL¤

    Use probabilistic programming when scientific structure, heterogeneous latent variables, missingness, explicit priors, and multiple posterior queries matter more than pure generation throughput. A PPL is a composition framework, not one additional neural model family.

    Retrieval question: why can one program need several inference algorithms?

    Different queries and substructures expose discrete choices, continuous geometry, long sequences, or black-box simulators. No inference method is uniformly efficient across all of them; programmable or hybrid inference exploits structure.

    Chapter 3 synthesis¤

    Probabilistic programs trade restricted architecture for arbitrary composition, moving difficulty into inference. Chapter 4 examines tensor factorizations that make a narrower class of discrete probability queries tractable by contraction.

    4. Tensor Networks and Born Machines¤

    Chapter 4 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes linear algebra and discrete probability. By the end, you should be able to interpret tensor-network factorization as a tractability and inductive-bias choice, derive Born probabilities from squared amplitudes and a normalizer, distinguish classical nonnegative from complex-amplitude models, relate bond dimension to expressivity/contraction cost, and avoid equating tensor notation with quantum hardware.

    Complete family volume

    This chapter is the concise orientation. Use Tensor-Network and Born Probability Models for the complete six-chapter treatment of nonnegative and Born semantics, MPS ranks and gauges, exact contraction queries, learning, continuous feature maps, family relationships, and evaluation.

    Probability tensors¤

    For \(d\) discrete variables with \(K\) states, a full joint table has \(K^d\) entries. A matrix product state (MPS) represents an amplitude or nonnegative score through contracted cores:

    \[ \Psi(x_1,\ldots,x_d) =G^{(1)}[x_1]G^{(2)}[x_2]\cdots G^{(d)}[x_d], \]

    where adjacent bond dimensions are at most \(R\). Parameter count is roughly \(O(dK R^2)\) for uniform internal rank instead of \(O(K^d)\), but only distributions compatible with the chosen rank and ordering are compact.

    Two probability constructions¤

    1. Nonnegative tensor model: constrain the contracted score nonnegative and normalize, \(p(x)=\Psi(x)/Z\).
    2. Born machine: allow real or complex amplitudes and apply the Born rule,
    \[ p(x)=\frac{|\Psi(x)|^2}{\sum_{x'}|\Psi(x')|^2}. \]

    A Born machine implemented as a classical tensor network is quantum-inspired, not a quantum computer. Squaring amplitudes can encode interference-like cancellation before the square, while normalization and marginalization use doubled-network contractions.

    Exact queries and their boundary¤

    For an MPS, normalization, sequential sampling, many marginals, and conditionals are polynomial in sequence length and bond dimension. General tensor-network contraction can still be #P-hard; tractability depends on topology/treewidth and contraction order. Increasing \(R\) improves expressivity but raises time/memory polynomially—often cubically in common MPS contractions.

    Tensor Networks for Probabilistic Sequence Modeling develops uniform MPS models and exact sequence likelihood/sampling. The expressive-power analysis distinguishes nonnegative, Born, and locally purified tensor factorizations.

    Ordering and entanglement analogy¤

    MPS compactness depends on cutting the variable order: strongly dependent variables separated across a cut can require larger bond dimension. “Entanglement” here measures amplitude correlations across tensor partitions; it should not be conflated with physical quantum entanglement unless the tensor represents an actual quantum state.

    Worked rank-one case¤

    With bond dimension \(R=1\),

    \[ \Psi(x)=\prod_i g_i(x_i), \]

    so both nonnegative and squared-amplitude constructions factorize into independent marginals after normalization. Dependence requires \(R>1\). This is the tensor-network analogue of increasing latent state size in an HMM.

    Relationship to circuits and graphical models¤

    Tensor networks and probabilistic circuits both exploit distributive structure for exact contraction. Nonnegative MPS relate closely to weighted automata/HMM-like sequence models; circuit decomposability and tensor topology offer different languages for the same tractability question. The appropriate guide is Probabilistic Circuits when arbitrary marginal queries and circuit structure are central, and tensor networks when low-rank multilinear structure is the useful inductive bias.

    Evidence status¤

    “Efficient Learning of Probabilistic Tensor Networks” (arXiv 2510.00382) reports stabilized training and scaling results, but its ICLR 2026 submission was rejected. It therefore remains Preprint evidence rather than a peer-reviewed result. By contrast, the orthogonality construction in How to Square Tensor Networks and Circuits Without Squaring Them was accepted at ICLR 2026; its tractability result applies under the paper's stated circuit properties.

    Retrieval question: what controls MPS tractability and fidelity?

    Topology/order and bond dimension. Low rank makes contraction cheap but may underfit long-range dependence; higher rank expands expressivity while increasing compute and memory.

    Chapter 4 synthesis¤

    Tensor networks are compact classical factorizations, and Born machines need not use quantum hardware. Continue to the complete family volume for the full derivations and evaluation protocol. Chapter 5 isolates actual quantum generative models and the evidence required for an advantage claim.

    5. Quantum Generative Models¤

    Chapter 5 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the Born rule and basic circuit language introduced here. By the end, you should be able to distinguish quantum data, quantum parameterization, and quantum execution; write the measurement-induced sample law of a circuit model; compare basis, latent, adversarial, and annealing-style constructions; account for shot noise and hardware error; and evaluate advantage claims against classical simulation and matched-resource baselines.

    Quantum circuit Born machines¤

    An \(n\)-qubit parameterized circuit prepares

    \[ |\psi_\theta\rangle=U_\theta|0^n\rangle, \]

    and computational-basis measurement produces

    \[ p_\theta(x)=|\langle x|\psi_\theta\rangle|^2, \qquad x\in\{0,1\}^n. \]

    This quantum circuit Born machine (QCBM) gives direct samples as hardware shots. Benedetti et al. (2019) studied shallow circuits as trainable generative models and benchmarking tools.

    Training objectives¤

    Exact likelihood needs probabilities that hardware sampling does not directly reveal for every bit string. Practical objectives include MMD, adversarial losses, moment matching, Sinkhorn/OT variants, and approximate likelihoods. Gradients may use parameter-shift rules or stochastic estimators, multiplying circuit executions by parameters, observables, and shots.

    Finite-shot variance, readout error, decoherence, circuit depth, and barren plateaus affect training. Error mitigation changes compute and statistical uncertainty and must be included in comparisons.

    Quantum GANs and hybrid models¤

    Quantum generative adversarial learning proposes adversarial games where generator, discriminator, or data may be quantum. Hybrid models can use classical preprocessing and a quantum latent generator. The phrase “quantum GAN” therefore does not specify which component is quantum or whether outputs are classical or quantum states.

    Data loading and output bottlenecks¤

    For classical data, encoding \(d\) real features into amplitudes can itself be expensive; for classical samples, measurement emits at most \(n\) bits per shot. An end-to-end advantage claim must include data preparation, circuit compilation, shots, mitigation, classical optimization, and comparison with strong classical samplers at matched accuracy.

    Expressivity is not advantage¤

    A circuit family may represent distributions hard for a restricted classical family yet still be classically simulable at tested sizes. Conversely, worst-case hardness does not guarantee useful trainability, sample quality, or advantage on natural data. Establishing quantum advantage needs a task, scaling regime, error tolerance, hardware model, and credible classical baselines.

    Worked two-qubit distribution¤

    Apply a Hadamard to qubit 1 and a CNOT from qubit 1 to 2:

    \[ |00\rangle\mapsto (|00\rangle+|11\rangle)/\sqrt2. \]

    Measurement yields 00 or 11 with probability \(1/2\). This is a dependent classical sample distribution, but a two-state classical latent variable represents it trivially. Quantum state entanglement in a small demonstration is not evidence of classical generative advantage.

    Evidence Posture¤

    The evidence audit found no accepted result that justifies a blanket quantum advantage for practical classical-data generation. Peer-reviewed work studies circuit expressivity and continuous-distribution constructions, but claims remain hardware-, scale-, and baseline-specific. The correct curriculum label is research family, not production replacement.

    Retrieval question: what is the minimum quantum-advantage accounting?

    Include data loading, state preparation, circuit depth/compilation, shots, error mitigation, optimization, output extraction, accuracy, and the best relevant classical baseline as problem size scales.

    Chapter 5 synthesis¤

    Quantum generators define valid Born distributions, but representation and sampling do not by themselves establish practical advantage. Chapter 6 returns to classical constructive generators whose inductive bias is local growth, grammar, or executable program structure.

    6. Grammars, Programs, and Neural Cellular Generators¤

    Chapter 6 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes discrete probability and constructive generation. By the end, you should be able to locate randomness in probabilistic grammars, programs, and cellular update rules; distinguish derivation probability from terminal- object probability; explain ambiguity and many-to-one construction paths; analyze local-rule/global-constraint tension; and route each constructive family within the book's probability, inference, and evaluation taxonomy.

    Continue into the complete grammar volume

    This chapter remains the concise orientation across grammars, executable programs, and neural cellular automata. The complete treatment of probabilistic grammars and program-derivation laws is Probabilistic Grammars and Program Derivations: six chapters covering derivation probability, ambiguity, termination, inside–outside inference, grammar induction, execution semantics, neural hybrids, constrained sampling, and evaluation.

    Probabilistic grammars¤

    A probabilistic context-free grammar assigns probabilities to productions \(A\to\beta\). For a derivation tree \(t\),

    \[ p(t)=\prod_{r\in t}p(r\mid lhs(r)), \qquad p(x)=\sum_{t:\,yield(t)=x}p(t). \]

    Inside dynamic programming marginalizes parse trees for context-free grammars. Neural or latent-variable grammars increase context sensitivity but can sacrifice exact structure. Compound PCFGs add a sentence-level continuous latent while marginalizing trees conditionally.

    Grammars impose hard syntactic support and offer derivations; they do not guarantee semantic validity. A molecule grammar can generate syntactically valid strings that violate chemistry unless semantic constraints are encoded or checked.

    Programs as constructive generators¤

    A program generator samples code \(P\sim p_\theta(P)\) and executes

    \[ x=Execute(P,u), \]

    where \(u\) may contain random seeds or inputs. Programs provide reuse, loops, symmetry, recursion, and exact constraints. The induced observation likelihood sums over programs and execution randomness and is usually intractable. Search must handle equivalence—many programs produce the same output—plus nontermination and runtime limits.

    DreamCoder is a Preprint / research system using wake–sleep library learning and neural-guided program search. Its learned abstractions show the appeal of programmatic composition, but its domain results do not imply a general-purpose density model.

    Neural cellular automata¤

    On a grid or graph, a neural cellular automaton (NCA) applies a shared local rule:

    \[ h_i^{t+1}=h_i^t+m_i^t\odot f_\theta\big(h_i^t,\{h_j^t:j\in\mathcal N(i)\}\big), \]

    where \(m_i^t\) can randomly select updates. Generation starts from a seed or random field and iterates the rule. Shared locality gives translation-compatible growth and can produce self-organization, persistence, and regeneration.

    Growing Neural Cellular Automata is an Established technical demonstration of differentiable morphogenesis; Variational NCA, ICLR 2022, turns the process into an explicit latent generative construction while reporting a gap to image-generation leaders. A 2026 TMLR review and reference implementation is Peer-reviewed synthesis, not a new state-of-the-art generative result.

    Where randomness lives¤

    Randomness may enter the initial seed, per-cell update masks, latent variables, or stochastic local transitions. A deterministic NCA from one fixed seed produces one trajectory, not a distribution. Stochastic updates create a distribution, but likelihood is generally implicit and long-run stability must be tested across step counts and damage patterns.

    Local rules and global constraints¤

    Locality enables scaling and repair but makes exact global constraints difficult. Training at one grid size or rollout length may not extrapolate. Boundary conditions, synchronous versus asynchronous updates, neighborhood, hidden-channel count, and stopping rule are part of the model and must be reported.

    Routing decisions¤

    • Choose grammars for hard hierarchical syntax and tractable parsing.
    • Choose program generators when reusable executable structure and constraints dominate.
    • Choose NCAs for decentralized growth, morphogenesis, textures, or repair.
    • Choose probabilistic programs when arbitrary stochastic composition and posterior queries dominate.
    • Choose AR/diffusion/flow models when scalable perceptual likelihood or sample quality matters more than explicit constructive semantics.

    Family placement¤

    Copula models, probabilistic grammars, and tensor/Born probability models define distinct generative objects and query contracts. Probabilistic programming is instead a composition framework: the program may contain many different model families and inference algorithms. “Quantum generative model” names a computational substrate unless the probability construction is specified—for example, a circuit Born machine or a quantum component in an adversarial game. An NCA is a transition architecture until its seed, update randomness, stopping rule, and observation map define a generative law.

    Research exercise solution: make an NCA genuinely generative

    Define a distribution over seeds or latent codes and, if used, update masks; specify a fixed or random stopping time; run the shared transition; and define the observation decoder. Then test diversity, stability across step counts, sensitivity to update schedules, and whether distinct latents collapse to the same attractor.

    Retrieval practice: constructive generators
    1. Why can a normalized distribution over derivations fail to give the same probability to equivalent terminal objects?
    2. What must a probabilistic program declare when control flow changes the set of random choices?
    3. Why do local cellular rules not guarantee a desired global constraint?
    4. Which query distinguishes sampling a construction trace from evaluating terminal-object likelihood?

    Solutions. (1) Ambiguous grammars or programs can have several traces yielding one object, so terminal mass is their sum. (2) Trace addresses, branching/termination semantics, and the reference measure or probability of each execution path. (3) Local validity need not compose into connectivity, conservation, topology, or other long-range properties. (4) The likelihood query must marginalize all traces for the terminal object, whereas trace sampling follows one constructive path.

    7. Bayesian Nonparametric Random-Measure Models¤

    Chapter 7 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes the mixture-model, posterior-inference, and exchangeability ideas introduced in Classical Generative Models and Latent Variables and Inference. By the end, you should be able to define a random probability measure through its finite-dimensional laws, distinguish partitions from feature allocations, separate an infinite-dimensional prior from finite posterior computation, and explain why density consistency need not imply recovery of a scientific cluster count.

    Continue into the complete family volume

    This landscape chapter provides the orientation and preserves its original public anchors. The complete treatment is now Bayesian Nonparametric Random-Measure Models: six chapters covering random measures, partitions and mixtures, feature and trait allocations, dependent hierarchies, posterior computation, evaluation, and current research through the recorded verification date.

    The generative object¤

    “Nonparametric” does not mean “parameter free.” A Bayesian nonparametric model places a prior on an object that is not restricted to one fixed finite-dimensional parameter vector: a probability measure, function, partition, feature allocation, graph, or transition law. Any finite dataset still activates only finite information, and every implementation uses finite computation.

    A Dirichlet process (DP) is a canonical prior on probability measures. Write

    \[ G\sim \operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H). \]

    For every measurable partition \((A_1,\ldots,A_K)\) of the sample space,

    \[ \bigl(G(A_1),\ldots,G(A_K)\bigr) \sim \operatorname{Dirichlet} \bigl(\alpha H(A_1),\ldots,\alpha H(A_K)\bigr). \]

    Here \(H\) is the base probability measure and \(\alpha>0\) is a concentration parameter. This finite-partition contract, not an informal clustering analogy, defines the process.

    From a random measure to a density model¤

    A Dirichlet-process mixture separates the random mixing measure from the component likelihood:

    \[ G\sim\operatorname{DP}(\alpha,H),\qquad \theta_i\mid G\sim G,\qquad x_i\mid\theta_i\sim p(x_i\mid\theta_i). \]

    A draw \(G\) is almost surely discrete, so several \(\theta_i\) values coincide and induce a random partition of the observations. The mixture density can nevertheless be continuous because \(p(x\mid\theta)\) smooths the atoms. Density estimation, clustering, and inference about the number of occupied components are therefore different queries.

    The stick-breaking representation makes the countably infinite mixture explicit:

    \[ v_k\sim\operatorname{Beta}(1,\alpha),\qquad \pi_k=v_k\prod_{j<k}(1-v_j),\qquad G=\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\pi_k\delta_{\theta_k}, \quad \theta_k\sim H. \]

    Truncating this series is an inference or approximation choice; it does not turn the underlying model into a finite mixture.

    Partitions are not feature allocations¤

    After marginalizing \(G\), the DP predictive rule assigns observation \(n+1\) to occupied cluster \(k\) with probability \(n_k/(n+\alpha)\) and to a new cluster with probability \(\alpha/(n+\alpha)\). This is an exchangeable partition law. By contrast, the Indian buffet process defines a distribution over binary feature-allocation matrices: one object may use several latent features. Treating clusters and features as interchangeable changes the latent object.

    Hierarchical and dependent random measures share atoms or statistical strength across groups. Covariate-dependent hierarchical Dirichlet processes provide Peer-reviewed research evidence through 2026 for covariate-indexed group structure; they do not make every covariate-dependent mixture identifiable or computationally easy.

    Inference is a separate contract¤

    Collapsed Gibbs samplers integrate out a conjugate random measure; slice and retrospective samplers instantiate only the components needed by a finite computation; SMC propagates partitions; variational methods truncate or otherwise approximate the posterior; amortized methods learn reusable posterior proposals. Neural Clustering Processes amortize posterior sampling over partitions, but the neural inference network is not the DP prior itself.

    Report the random-measure prior, likelihood, posterior approximation, truncation or slice rule, chain diagnostics, and posterior predictive query separately. A small ELBO gap or stable cluster count under one initialization is not a posterior-convergence proof.

    Worked predictive step¤

    Let \(\alpha=1\) and suppose two observations currently occupy the same cluster. The next observation joins it with probability

    \[ \frac{2}{2+1}=\frac{2}{3}, \]

    and starts a new cluster with probability \(1/(2+1)=1/3\). The probabilities concern a partition before seeing the next datum's likelihood. Once \(x_3\) is observed, posterior assignment also depends on the existing-cluster and new-cluster predictive densities.

    Failure boundary: density consistency is not cluster-count consistency¤

    The phrase “lets the data determine the number of clusters” is too strong. Miller and Harrison prove that broad classes of Dirichlet- and Pitman–Yor-process mixtures are inconsistent for the number of finite-mixture components even when they remain useful density models. The posterior number of occupied clusters is a model-dependent random quantity, not automatically the true number of scientific populations.

    Retrieval question: what remains finite?

    For a finite dataset, the number of occupied clusters or used features is finite, as is any computation. The prior can still have countably infinite support or define an infinite-dimensional random object.

    8. Point Processes and Random Finite Sets¤

    Chapter 8 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes basic probability, conditional likelihood, and the hazard and continuous-time ideas in Sampling and Continuous-Time Mathematics. By the end, you should be able to distinguish a random event configuration from a random function or token sequence, derive the event and survival terms in an intensity likelihood, select a valid simulation mechanism, and evaluate count, time, location, and mark behavior without making an unsupported causal claim.

    Orientation and complete treatment

    This chapter is the concise landscape orientation. Use Point Processes and Random Finite Sets for the complete six-chapter volume covering Janossy and set-density conventions, temporal and spatial constructions, random finite sets, latent inference, exact and approximate simulation, neural methods, and configuration-level evaluation.

    Events as a random counting measure¤

    A point process on a space \(\mathcal X\) is a random locally finite counting measure

    \[ N=\sum_i \delta_{x_i}, \]

    so every bounded region contains finitely many points almost surely. Marks can attach event types, sizes, or other attributes: \(x_i=(t_i,m_i)\) for a marked temporal process. This object differs from a random function. An intensity may parameterize the law, but sampling an intensity function is not the same as sampling the event configuration it governs.

    For a Poisson process with intensity measure \(\Lambda\), disjoint-region counts are independent and

    \[ N(B)\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(\Lambda(B)). \]

    Hawkes processes introduce history-dependent excitation; self-correcting processes can inhibit events; Cox processes first sample a random intensity; determinantal point processes (DPPs) produce repulsive configurations.

    Conditional intensity and likelihood¤

    For a simple marked temporal point process on \([0,T]\) with history \(\mathcal H_t\) and predictable intensities \(\lambda_m(t\mid\mathcal H_t)\), the log likelihood relative to the standard event reference measure is

    \[ \log p\bigl(\{(t_i,m_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}\bigr) =\sum_{i=1}^{n}\log \lambda_{m_i}(t_i\mid\mathcal H_{t_i}) -\int_0^T\sum_m\lambda_m(s\mid\mathcal H_s)\,ds. \]

    The survival integral is essential. Optimizing only the intensity at observed events rewards arbitrarily large intensities and does not define the correct likelihood.

    The Neural Hawkes Process parameterizes history-dependent marked intensities with continuous-time recurrent state. Intensity-free models instead parameterize conditional inter-event distributions. Both are autoregressive factorizations over event histories, but the point-process semantics supply continuous time, random cardinality, and survival probabilities.

    Static random sets and repulsion¤

    On a finite ground set, an \(L\)-ensemble DPP assigns

    \[ \Pr(Y)=\frac{\det(L_Y)}{\det(I+L)}, \]

    for positive semidefinite \(L\). Determinants reduce the mass of subsets containing similar, nearly dependent items, while the eigenstructure supports exact sampling. DPP normalization and marginal queries are special tractability results, not generic properties of neural set generators. A 2026 TMLR study of DPP kernel estimation shows that even this tractable probability family can have nonconvex, ill-posed parameter learning without suitable conditions or regularization.

    Simulation mechanisms¤

    • Inverse integrated hazards sample the next event when the cumulative conditional intensity is invertible.
    • Thinning proposes from an upper-bound process and accepts events according to the intensity ratio; an invalid upper bound invalidates the sampler.
    • Superposition combines independent processes by adding their counting measures.
    • Spectral algorithms sample many DPPs through eigenvectors and conditional item selection.
    • Add/thin transitions can noise and denoise whole event configurations. Add and Thin is a Peer-reviewed research diffusion construction for temporal point processes, not ordinary Gaussian diffusion applied to a padded event vector.

    Worked homogeneous process¤

    Let events arrive at constant rate \(\lambda=2\) on an interval of length one. Then

    \[ \Pr[N([0,1])=2]=e^{-2}\frac{2^2}{2!}=2e^{-2}. \]

    Conditional on exactly two events, their unordered locations are IID uniform and their ordered times are the corresponding order statistics. The count law and conditional location law are both required; predicting two plausible timestamps without the correct count distribution does not reproduce the process.

    Evaluation and causal restraint¤

    Evaluate held-out log likelihood or proper scores, count calibration, time-rescaling residuals, mark calibration, long-horizon event statistics, and domain-specific operational forecasts. An intensity describes observational conditional risk, not an intervention effect. Self-excitation estimated from event histories can also reflect latent common causes or measurement artifacts.

    Domain evidence reinforces the need for established baselines. EarthquakeNPP, accepted by TMLR in 2026, reports that five evaluated neural point-process systems did not outperform ETAS under its leakage-corrected earthquake protocol. In-context point-process inference, ICLR 2026, is a distinct amortized inference result; it does not remove point-process identifiability or calibration requirements.

    Retrieval question: why is an event sequence not just an ordinary token sequence?

    Event times live in continuous time, the number of events in a window is random, and the probability of no event contributes through a survival term. Tokenizing time can be useful, but it changes or approximates these semantics.

    9. Random Graph and Relational Generators¤

    Chapter 9 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    This chapter assumes discrete probability, latent-variable models, and the symmetry concepts in Geometric and Equivariant Generative Modeling. By the end, you should be able to state whether a graph law is labeled or unlabeled, compute an isomorphism-class probability, distinguish vertex exchangeability from equivariant parameterization, explain why dense graphons do not automatically model sparse growing graphs, and place a deep graph sampler as a composition rather than a replacement for graph probability semantics.

    A probability law on relational structures¤

    A graph generator needs more than a matrix-shaped output. It specifies a law for node count, node and edge attributes, topology, and—when labels have no semantic meaning—equivalence under relabeling. Classical families include Erdős–Rényi graphs, stochastic block models (SBMs), latent-position models, exponential random graph models, graphons, and sparse exchangeable random-measure constructions.

    An undirected SBM first samples latent classes and then conditionally independent edges:

    \[ z_i\sim\operatorname{Categorical}(\pi),\qquad A_{ij}\mid z_i,z_j\sim \operatorname{Bernoulli}(B_{z_i z_j}),\quad i<j. \]

    The model separates community uncertainty from within/between-community connection probabilities. Posterior community recovery, graph generation, and prediction of missing edges are related but different queries.

    Labeling and graph probability¤

    If node labels are arbitrary, a probability assigned to one adjacency ordering is not yet the mass of the unlabeled graph. For an isomorphism class \([G]\),

    \[ p([G])=\sum_{H\in[G]}p(H). \]

    The class has \(n!/|\operatorname{Aut}(G)|\) distinct labeled representatives. Autoregressive graph generators therefore need an ordering convention, order marginalization, or an explicitly invariant construction. A permutation-equivariant network alone does not prove that the induced graph law is invariant.

    Exchangeability, graphons, and sparsity¤

    A common vertex-exchangeable construction samples \(u_i\stackrel{iid}{\sim}\operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\) and

    \[ A_{ij}\mid u_i,u_j\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(W(u_i,u_j)). \]

    SBMs are step-function graphons. Different graphons related by measure-preserving transformations define the same exchangeable graph distribution, so the latent coordinates are not generally identifiable. Projective dense graphon models also do not describe sparse growing networks without changing the sampling or exchangeability construction. Caron and Fox use exchangeable random measures to obtain sparse graph processes; the change of object and sampling scheme is mathematically consequential.

    Modern samplers are family compositions¤

    GraphRNN uses an autoregressive node/edge ordering. DiGress applies discrete denoising diffusion to categorical node and edge states. Flow, VAE, EBM, GAN, and GFlowNet systems can likewise generate graphs. These are hybrid compositions: the graph state space and invariance contract do not turn every sampler into one new “graph architecture” family.

    HOG-Diff, ICLR 2026, supplies Peer-reviewed research on higher-order guidance for diffusion graph generation. Its reported benchmark improvements support that particular topology-aware diffusion construction; they do not supersede classical random-graph semantics or identify one universally sufficient graph metric.

    Worked three-node example¤

    For \(G\sim G(3,p)\), a labeled graph with \(m\) edges has probability

    \[ p^m(1-p)^{3-m}. \]

    There are three labeled two-edge paths on three nodes, so the probability of the unlabeled two-edge-path class is

    \[ 3p^2(1-p), \]

    not \(p^2(1-p)\). This multiplicity is the smallest example of why graph likelihood depends on the labeled-versus-unlabeled query.

    Evaluation and failure cases¤

    Degree, clustering, orbit, motif, spectral, and learned-embedding summaries expose different graph properties; matching a finite list does not identify the graph distribution. Report whether training uses many IID graphs or subgraphs from one network, whether node identities persist, how node count is modeled, which validity constraints apply, and whether metrics are invariant to relabeling. For molecules and proteins, structural validity, property predictors, and experimental function remain separate evidence layers.

    Do not confuse a generated relational graph with a causal graph. A graph generator can reproduce observational network motifs without representing interventions, structural equations, or causal direction.

    Retrieval question: what must be declared before reporting graph likelihood?

    Declare whether graphs are labeled, which node order or permutation marginal is used, how node count and attributes are modeled, and whether the reported probability is for one adjacency representation or an isomorphism class.

Deep graph generators compose those contracts with autoregressive, latent, flow, energy, adversarial, or diffusion machinery.     Final synthesis¤

    The extended landscape is organized by explicit generative semantics rather than neural-network shape. Copulas separate dependence from marginals; GSNs define stationary transitions; probabilistic programs distribute over traces; tensor/Born machines contract structured tables or amplitudes; quantum circuits generate measurement laws; constructive systems execute derivations or local rules; Bayesian nonparametric models randomize distributions and latent structure; point processes generate event configurations; and random-graph models generate relational structures. Each exposes different queries and failure modes.
