    Evaluation Metrics¤

    Capstone objectives¤

    By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

    1. evaluate normalization estimates, sample quality, mixing, OOD scoring, robustness, calibration, compute, and deployment precision without treating any one axis as a proxy for all others; and
    2. produce an evidence-bounded research report for a new energy system.

    EBMs are evaluated on density-estimation, sample-quality, out-of-distribution-detection, and representation-quality axes:

    Density-Estimation Metrics¤

    The partition function \(Z_\theta\) makes exact likelihood intractable, so EBMs use bounds and ratios:

    The algorithms and uncertainty semantics are owned by Estimating Likelihoods and Normalizing Constants. This EBM section records only the consequences for energy-model evaluation.

    • Annealed Importance Sampling (AIS) — estimates \(Z_\theta\) using a sequence of intermediate distributions. Accuracy depends on schedule, transitions, chains, and mixing; repeat runs and compare schedules rather than treating one estimate as ground truth.
    • Reverse AIS and bidirectional Monte Carlo — reverse procedures have different guarantees. Stochastic upper bounds on a marginal likelihood require reverse runs initialized from an exact target/posterior sample, as in Grosse, Ghahramani & Adams (2015). RAISE instead gives a conservative stochastic lower bound on the log-likelihood of an approximation to the model (Burda, Grosse & Salakhutdinov, 2015). Generic “reverse AIS” does not automatically bracket \(Z\).
    • NCE log-Z estimate — when training with NCE, the learned \(c = \log Z\) parameter is itself an estimate.
    • Energy-gap reporting — state the subtraction order. If \(\Delta_E=\mathbb{E}[E_\theta(x_{\mathrm{sample}})]-\mathbb{E}[E_\theta(x_{\mathrm{data}})]\), the desired ordering gives a positive value. The reverse definition is negative. Neither gap estimates \(Z_\theta\) or proves good mixing.

    Sample-Quality Metrics¤

    EBM sampling is unconditional by default; the standard image-generation metrics apply:

    • FID (Heusel et al., 2017), Inception Score, CMMD (Jayasumana et al., 2024).
    • Protocol-qualified reference points from Energy–Flow Constructions and Implicit EBMs include the EqM preprint's author-reported FID 1.90 on class-conditional ImageNet 256² (Wang & Du, 2025) and CDRL's comparisons on CIFAR-10. Reproduce the cited evaluation configuration before comparing either number with another family.
    • For language, EDLM (NVIDIA, 2025) reports perplexity competitive with similar-scale autoregressive LMs.

    Out-of-Distribution Detection¤

    Energy is a useful candidate OOD score, not a reliable OOD detector by construction. An energy function is learned for a particular objective; low energy can reflect background statistics, model misspecification, or unseen high-confidence regions rather than semantic in-distribution membership:

    • Report AUROC, AUPR, and FPR at a declared TPR on named in/out datasets, corruption severities, and tuning splits. Do not transfer a threshold or an aggregate result to a new domain without validation.
    • Likelihood-ratio scores (Ren et al., 2019) — divide by the energy of a "background" model to fix the well-known counter-intuitive higher-likelihood-on-OOD problem (cf. Nalisnick et al., 2019).

    Representation Quality (JEM, conditional EBMs)¤

    • Linear probing on top of the JEM classifier head.
    • Adversarial robustness under a declared threat model and strong attacks such as PGD/AutoAttack. Some JEM successors report improvements over named baselines; re-evaluate adaptive attacks against the released model.
    • Calibration using ECE plus proper scoring rules and reliability plots. Joint-energy training does not guarantee calibration under distribution shift.

    Production Considerations¤

    Inference Cost¤

    EBM inference cost is determined by the sampler, energy-network architecture, state dimension, and convergence criterion. The research landscape contains several qualitatively different regimes:

    Table EBM.8. Inference Cost
    Sampling regime Per-step work Main limitation
    ULA / SGLD One energy gradient per step Discretization bias, autocorrelation, and potentially long burn-in
    MALA / HMC Energy gradients plus accept/reject bookkeeping More work per transition; tuning and diagnostics remain essential
    Tempered / recovery-likelihood ladders Multiple energy or recovery evaluations across noise levels Additional states improve movement but increase cost
    Learned or equilibrium-style optimizers Method-specific iterative updates Report the actual algorithm, stopping rule, NFE, hardware, and benchmark; there is no universal latency

    Equilibrium-style methods explore adaptive optimization as a sampler, but that does not create a generic “one-step distilled EBM” category. Any such claim must name a concrete trained sampler and distinguish approximate optimization toward low energy from sampling the normalized Boltzmann distribution.

    Quantisation and Deployment¤

    • FP16 / BF16 can work for the energy network, but are not universally safe: energy differences and input gradients can be more sensitive than class logits. Validate chains, gradients, and acceptance statistics against an FP32 reference.
    • INT8 PTQ is risky when the sampler differentiates the quantized energy, because small energy/gradient errors can change trajectories or acceptance decisions. Mixed-precision or quantization-aware designs need sampler-level validation; “quantize only the forward pass” is not a coherent rule when the backward input gradient is computed through that same forward graph.
    • Distillation of EBMs into a single-step student is an active research area (see Equilibrium Matching's appendix on adaptive-NFE sampling).

    Common Pitfalls in Production¤

    • Long-run sample collapse — if chains drift to a narrow basin, diagnose model landscape, temperature, transition kernel, and initialization. Restarts or equilibrium-style optimization change behavior but do not prove the normalized target is represented.
    • Energy explosion during fine-tuning — absolute energy has an additive gauge, but nonfinite values and gradients are real failures. Centering, an explicitly scaled energy regularizer, optimizer changes, or architectural confinement should be tested with their effect on the objective disclosed.
    • OOD detection drift — the AUROC on a held-out OOD set should be tracked as a training metric, not just a final-evaluation one.

    For the broader cross-family picture and how EBMs fit alongside diffusion / flow / VAE / GAN / AR systems, see The Generative Modeling Landscape.

    Summary and Key Takeaways¤

    Energy-Based Models provide a principled framework for learning probability distributions through energy functions. Their practical behavior depends jointly on the energy parameterization, training objective, negative-sample mechanism, and sampler; contrastive, score-based, regression-based, and hybrid methods make different trade-offs rather than forming a single progression.

    Core Principles¤

    • Energy Landscape

      Model assigns scalar energy to configurations—low energy = high probability

    • Boltzmann Distribution

      \(p(x) \propto e^{-E(x)}\) connects energy to probability via exponential

    • MCMC Sampling

      Generate samples by traversing energy landscape with Langevin dynamics

    • Flexible Architecture

      Broad scalar networks are possible, but normalized models still require finite \(Z\), declared support, geometry, and stable derivatives

    Training Methods¤

    Table EBM.9. Training Methods
    Method Sampling During Training Pros Cons
    Contrastive Divergence Short MCMC chains (k=1-60) Practical, widely used Biased gradient estimates
    Persistent CD Persistent buffer + MCMC Longer chain history Biased if chains fail to track the changing model
    Score Matching None Avoids negative-phase model sampling Exact trace can be expensive; alternatives change estimator/noise choices
    NCE Noise samples only Simple, no MCMC Requires good noise distribution

    When to Use EBMs¤

    Consider EBMs when:

    • Flexible unnormalized density modeling where energies, ratios, constraints, or compositional structure matter more than cheap exact normalized likelihood
    • OOD or anomaly scoring experiments when representative anomalies, held-out shifts, calibration data, and failure costs are available; neither \(p(x)\) nor \(E(x)\) alone certifies safety
    • Hybrid tasks combining generation and classification (JEM)
    • Structured prediction with complex dependencies

    Prefer another or hybrid approach when the validated sampler misses the latency budget, normalized likelihood is required but \(Z\) cannot be estimated adequately, or available data/compute and domain evidence strongly favor another family. These are operating-point decisions; VAEs and diffusion models do not have a universal sample-efficiency advantage.

    Open Research Directions¤

    Interpretive synthesis: the following are research hypotheses, not established forecasts.

    • Energy-flow connections — Energy Matching (Balcerak et al., 2025) and the EqM preprint (Wang & Du, 2025) motivate scalar-potential or equilibrium-field alternatives to time-dependent flows. Whether large production generators adopt explicit energy heads remains open.
    • EBM dual of language models — the ARM–EBM preprint provides one rigorous function-space lens for lookahead and distillation. Whether it yields better post-training algorithms than policy or probabilistic views is open.
    • Energy-based discrete diffusion for text — EDLM reports reduced gaps to selected autoregressive baselines on named datasets. Extension to multimodal systems is a hypothesis, not an established trajectory.
    • One model, many tasks — EqM reports generation, OOD scoring, denoising, and composition experiments; future work must test whether one fitted model remains competitive under controlled multi-task protocols.
    • Scientific applications — scalar energies are attractive for constraints, composition, and physical priors in protein, molecular, materials, and physics problems. They compete and hybridise with diffusion and flow methods; no family naturally dominates across those domains.

    Cumulative worked example: audit a scientific EBM¤

    Suppose a molecular model outputs an invariant scalar energy and uses clipped unadjusted Langevin steps to generate conformations. A complete audit should:

    1. declare coordinates, quotient symmetries, chirality handling, base measure, temperature, and whether the energy is normalizable;
    2. verify force equivariance and finite-difference input gradients;
    3. validate ULA or MALA on tractable harmonic wells in the same units;
    4. compare multiple initializations, step sizes, chain lengths, adjusted and tempered kernels, and effective sample sizes;
    5. measure structural validity, energy/observable marginals, coverage, memorization, and held-out thermodynamic quantities;
    6. distinguish clipped optimization samples from the claimed Boltzmann distribution; and
    7. compare diffusion, flow, and autoregressive baselines under matched data, compute, constraints, and wall-clock.

    Independent practice and assessment map: cumulative synthesis¤

    Complete the capstone for a different domain from the molecular worked audit. No individual metric may stand in for normalization, mixing, scientific validity, robustness, or deployment feasibility.

    P1 — Produce an evidence-bounded EBM decision record¤

    Design and audit an EBM for spatial climate fields conditioned on season and coarse boundary conditions, with rare extremes and a fixed serving budget.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing. Predict whether low held-out energy, an AIS estimate with low apparent variance, attractive samples, high OOD AUROC, adversarial robustness, calibrated class probabilities, or low-precision speed independently proves the other properties. Classify each as a candidate measurement and name at least one counterexample or missing control.
    2. P1.2 — Normalization and sampling evaluation. Specify the state space, base measure, energy gauge, conditional law, and sampler. Define a tractable reduced problem with exact \(Z\); an AIS or alternative normalizer protocol with schedules, transitions, repeated chains, uncertainty, and sensitivity runs; and multi-chain mixing, autocorrelation, effective-sample, initialization, step-size, and long-run diagnostics.
    3. P1.3 — Multi-axis system evaluation. Define extreme-event fidelity and coverage, spatial/physical constraints, OOD datasets and threshold selection, calibration and proper scores, robustness threat model, memorization/privacy, subgroup or region behavior, FP32-versus-deployed-precision agreement, energy use, memory, and measured end-to-end latency. Give a decision rule that prevents one favorable axis from overriding a failed safety or scientific constraint.
    4. P1.4 — Transfer, evidence ledger, and rerouting. Produce an evidence-bounded report separating identities, theorem assumptions, reproduced paper results, new experiment results, interpretations, and hypotheses. Compare matched diffusion/flow, autoregressive, and non-neural structured baselines; decide whether the energy is the primary generator, a scorer or constraint in a hybrid, or unsuitable; and route every unresolved claim to a derivation, executable test, primary source, or new experiment.

    Retrieval practice: cumulative EBM synthesis
    1. Why can low energy and high likelihood rank inputs differently across models?
    2. What must an AIS report include before its \(Z\) estimate is credible?
    3. Why is energy alone an unsafe OOD certificate?
    4. Which distinction is violated by calling EqM gradient descent exact Boltzmann sampling?

    Solutions. (1) Absolute energies have an additive gauge and normalized likelihood also depends on \(Z\) and base measure. (2) Intermediate path/schedule, transition kernels, chain count, mixing diagnostics, uncertainty and sensitivity runs, plus a tractable validation where possible. (3) The learned objective may reward nuisance statistics or assign unseen regions low energy; thresholds and shifts require validation. (4) Mode/equilibrium optimization versus distributional sampling with a stationary target.

    Where to continue. Study Density, Latent, and Energy Relationships, General Markov Generators, GFlowNets, and the geometric guide. Return to the finite-state and Gaussian checks whenever a new “energy” method changes its objective or sampler.
