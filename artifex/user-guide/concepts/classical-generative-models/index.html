    Modern neural generators did not invent latent variables, sequential factorization, energy functions, approximate inference, or structured conditional independence. This chapter develops the classical models that make those ideas explicit and computationally inspectable.

    Reading routes

    Beginner: follow the latent-variable grammar → mixtures → factor models → directed models → HMM example → misconception checks.
    Practitioner: add numerical stability, model selection, matrix factorization, message passing, and failure diagnostics.
    Research: derive the EM bound, implement forward–backward in log space, and compare exact inference in a linear model with approximate inference after replacing its decoder by a neural network.

    A Visual Grammar for the Chapter¤

    Classical models are valuable because the picture, joint distribution, and inference algorithm can be placed side by side. We will repeatedly ask:

    • Structure

      Which variables exist, which are observed, and which conditional dependencies does the model declare?

    • Factors

      What probability distribution parameterizes each local relationship?

    • Query

      Are we sampling, scoring the joint, marginalizing, conditioning, or learning parameters?

    • Computation

      Which sums, integrals, messages, or approximations answer that query?

    This separation prevents a common category error: changing a factor from a table to a neural network does not by itself change the graph or perform global inference.

    How to Read Plate Notation¤

    Plate notation compresses repeated graphical-model structure. The convention used throughout this book follows the graphical-model notation systematized by Buntine (1994):

    Table CLASS.1. How to Read Plate Notation
    Visual role Meaning in this book
    open circle latent random variable or random measure
    filled circle observed random variable
    rectangle fixed parameter, input, or deterministic quantity
    enclosing plate repeat every enclosed node and edge over the shown index
    directed arrow the child distribution is conditioned on the parent; not automatically a causal claim

    The smallest useful example has one local latent variable for every observed item and shared fixed parameters. The plate replaces \(N\) copies of the same two nodes:

    The diagram represents

    \[ p_\theta(x_{1:N},z_{1:N}) =\prod_{i=1}^{N}p_\theta(z_i)p_\theta(x_i\mid z_i). \]

    Read the diagram and equation together. The picture exposes replication and conditional structure; the equation specifies the factors. Neither specifies an inference algorithm. When parameters are random, they become open random nodes with their own prior rather than fixed rectangles.

    What a plate does not say

    A plate is not a tensor-shape diagram, a neural-network architecture, a temporal order, or proof of conditional independence beyond the graph it encloses. Arrows describe a probabilistic factorization. Causal meaning requires the additional structural assumptions developed in the causal volume.

    Learning Objectives¤

    You should be able to:

    • derive maximum-likelihood updates for a Gaussian mixture;
    • distinguish generation, joint scoring, marginal likelihood, posterior inference, and parameter learning in one latent model;
    • derive the marginal and posterior of a small linear-Gaussian factor model;
    • distinguish factor analysis, probabilistic PCA, deterministic matrix factorization, and probabilistic matrix factorization;
    • explain Gaussian scale mixtures, local factor mixtures, paired-view factors, and exponential-family observation models;
    • derive the ICA change-of-variables likelihood and state its identification equivalences;
    • distinguish sparse-code MAP inference, dictionary learning, and marginal-likelihood learning;
    • explain why nonlinear ICA needs additional information or structural assumptions;
    • read conditional independence from a directed or undirected graphical model;
    • distinguish a random graph, a dependency graph, a causal graph, and a learned computation graph;
    • recover the conditional-independence graph of a small Gaussian model from its precision matrix;
    • explain how regularization, hidden variables, and equivalence classes limit structure recovery;
    • distinguish hidden Markov, state-space, topic, and Boltzmann models;
    • carry one state-space model through generation, likelihood evaluation, filtering, smoothing, and forecasting;
    • distinguish normalized emission models from discriminative state scores;
    • derive the Baum–Welch transition update from smoothed expected counts;
    • explain which duration, factorial, coupled, and switching structures an ordinary HMM cannot express compactly;
    • test the stability of a linear latent process and compute its stationary covariance when one exists;
    • construct a structural probabilistic forecast without turning temporal order into a causal claim;
    • explain why exact HMM and Kalman recursions become approximate in nonlinear or non-Gaussian models;
    • explain what EM, message passing, and dynamic programming compute;
    • distinguish regular, minimal, and overcomplete exponential-family representations;
    • state when moment matching gives a finite maximum-likelihood estimate and diagnose boundary data for which it does not;
    • translate a normalized Gaussian between moment and information form without confusing a distribution with an unnormalized potential;
    • identify which classical tractability assumptions deep models relax.

    1. Exponential Families¤

    Why begin here?

    Exponential families package a whole dataset into sufficient statistics. For Bernoulli data, the relevant statistic is a count; for Gaussian data, sums and sums of squares appear. Conjugacy works because the likelihood updates the prior using objects of the same algebraic form.

    Fix a state space, support, and base measure \(\nu\). An exponential-family law has density with respect to \(\nu\)

    \[ \begin{aligned} p_\eta(x) &=h(x)\exp\!\bigl(\eta^\top T(x)-A(\eta)\bigr),\\ A(\eta) &=\log\int h(x)e^{\eta^\top T(x)}\,\nu(dx). \end{aligned} \]

    The natural parameter space is the set of \(\eta\) for which \(A(\eta)\) is finite. The carrier \(h\) and base measure are part of the family: changing them can change support and can admit or forbid hard constraints. The statistic \(T(x)\) and natural parameter \(\eta\) determine the parameter-dependent factor, while \(A\) supplies exact normalization.

    At interior points where differentiation can be exchanged with integration, derivatives of the log-partition function produce moments:

    \[ \nabla_\eta A(\eta)=\mathbb E_\eta[T(X)], \qquad \nabla_\eta^2A(\eta)=\operatorname{Cov}_\eta[T(X)]. \]

    Bernoulli, categorical, Gaussian, Poisson, and many conjugate Bayesian components belong here. Neural networks often output their natural or mean parameters; the observation likelihood remains an exponential-family modeling choice even when the parameter network is deep.

    Minimality, Regularity, and Identifiable Coordinates¤

    An exponential-family representation is minimal when there is no nonzero vector \(a\) for which \(a^\top T(x)\) is constant \(\nu\)-almost everywhere. If such a combination exists, the representation is overcomplete: different natural-parameter vectors can name the same probability law. Minimality is a statement about parameter coordinates, not about whether the distribution is a good model of the data.

    The family is regular when its natural parameter space is open. In a regular minimal family, \(A\) is strictly convex and its gradient maps natural parameters one-to-one onto the interior of the realizable mean-parameter set. Without minimality, the Hessian \(\operatorname{Cov}_\eta[T(X)]\) can be singular along redundant directions, so concavity of a log likelihood does not imply a unique parameter vector. Without an interior empirical statistic, a finite maximizer may not exist at all.

    This vocabulary prevents three different claims from collapsing into “exponential families are convex”:

    1. \(A\) is convex whenever it is well defined;
    2. strict convexity in the chosen coordinates needs minimality; and
    3. existence of a finite MLE needs the empirical sufficient statistic to lie in the appropriate interior, not merely a concave objective.

    Conjugacy and Sufficient Statistics¤

    A conjugate prior produces a posterior in the same family after observing data. For a Bernoulli likelihood and Beta prior,

    \[ p(\pi)=\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha,\beta),\qquad x_n\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(\pi), \]

    the posterior is \(\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha+\sum_nx_n,\beta+N-\sum_nx_n)\). The dataset enters only through the count \(\sum_nx_n\), a sufficient statistic. Formally, sufficiency is the factorization

    \[ p_\eta(x_{1:N})=g_\eta\!\left(T_N(x_{1:N})\right)r(x_{1:N}), \]

    where \(r\) is independent of \(\eta\). It is not the assertion \(p(x_{1:N}\mid\eta)=p(T_N\mid\eta)\): the two sides generally live on different state spaces and omit the parameter-free multiplicity of datasets sharing the same statistic. Classical inference exploits the correct factorization exactly; deep amortized inference trades analytic structure for flexible learned mappings.

    The log-partition Hessian is positive semidefinite, so \(A(\eta)\) is convex. This links exponential families to convex duality, moment matching, maximum entropy, and the geometry behind natural gradients.

    Moment Matching Has an Existence Boundary¤

    For IID data and empirical statistic \(\bar T=N^{-1}\sum_nT(x_n)\), the average log likelihood is, up to a term independent of \(\eta\),

    \[ \ell(\eta)=\eta^\top\bar T-A(\eta). \]

    An attained interior optimum satisfies \(\nabla A(\widehat\eta)=\bar T\). That equation is a first-order condition, not a promise that every dataset has a finite solution.

    For Bernoulli observations, let \(k=\sum_nx_n\). With natural parameter \(\eta=\log(\pi/(1-\pi))\),

    \[ \begin{aligned} \ell(\eta) &=\frac{k}{N}\eta-\log(1+e^\eta),\\ \ell'(\eta) &=\frac{k}{N}-\sigma(\eta). \end{aligned} \]

    If \(0<k<N\), the finite solution is \(\widehat\eta=\operatorname{logit}(k/N)\). If \(k=0\), likelihood increases as \(\eta\to-\infty\); if \(k=N\), it increases as \(\eta\to+\infty\). The limiting Bernoulli law exists on the boundary, but there is no finite MLE in the open natural parameter space. A prior, penalty, or explicit closed-family parameterization changes the estimation problem rather than proving that the original finite MLE existed.

    Maximum Entropy Is a Constrained Result¤

    Exponential form also arises from maximizing entropy subject to declared expectation constraints. The statement needs a fixed support and reference measure, feasible constraint values, an attained optimum, and the relevant integrability conditions. Under those conditions the Lagrange multipliers are natural parameters and the optimum matches the constraints. Changing support, base measure, or constraints changes the result. There is no unconditional theorem that one exponential family is the unique maximum-entropy model for an informal property such as “known scale.”

    Gaussian Moment Form, Information Form, and Potentials¤

    A nonsingular Gaussian distribution can be written in moment coordinates \((\mu,\Sigma)\) or information coordinates

    \[ \Lambda=\Sigma^{-1}, \qquad h=\Lambda\mu. \]

    Ignoring only constants independent of \(x\), its log density is

    \[ \log p(x)=-\frac12x^\top\Lambda x+h^\top x+\text{constant}. \]

    If two Gaussian potentials concern the same variable, multiplication adds their information parameters:

    \[ \widetilde\Lambda=\Lambda_1+\Lambda_2, \qquad \widetilde h=h_1+h_2. \]

    The product becomes a normalized Gaussian distribution only after checking \(\widetilde\Lambda\succ0\) and restoring its normalizer. A positive semidefinite or indefinite quadratic factor may be a valid message or likelihood potential without being a standalone probability density.

    Marginalization and conditioning are also different Gaussian operations. A block covariance gives a conditional covariance by a Schur complement of the conditioned block; eliminating variables from a canonical potential gives a Schur complement in the precision parameters. Similar-looking matrix formulas therefore do not license swapping covariance and precision. The scalar Kalman worked example later in the continuous view shows information from a prior and measurement combining into one posterior.

    2. The Latent-Variable Grammar¤

    The plate above gives the shape of the model. This section now assigns probabilities to that shape and separates the five operations one can perform with it.

    A latent-variable model introduces an unobserved random variable \(z\) and declares a joint law

    \[ p_\theta(x,z)=p_\theta(z)p_\theta(x\mid z). \]

    This compact expression defines several computations that must remain distinct:

    1. Generation: draw \(z\sim p_\theta(z)\) and then \(x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z)\).
    2. Complete-data scoring: evaluate \(p_\theta(x,z)\) for a proposed pair.
    3. Observed-data scoring: marginalize the hidden state, \(p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz\).
    4. Posterior inference: condition in the opposite direction, \(p_\theta(z\mid x)=p_\theta(x,z)/p_\theta(x)\).
    5. Learning: choose or infer \(\theta\) from data using the observed-data likelihood, a bound, a posterior over parameters, or another declared objective.

    The first two operations can be easy while the next two are hard. A decoder that cheaply evaluates \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\) does not perform the integral over \(z\). Conversely, a model may admit an analytic marginal and posterior even when its latent dimension is not small—for example, a linear-Gaussian factor model.

    For independent observations with one local latent per item and a fixed parameter \(\theta\),

    \[ p_\theta(x_{1:N},z_{1:N}) =\prod_{n=1}^{N}p_\theta(z_n)p_\theta(x_n\mid z_n). \]

    If the parameters are themselves uncertain, a Bayesian model instead includes a global random variable:

    \[ p(\theta,z_{1:N},x_{1:N}) =p(\theta)\prod_{n=1}^{N}p(z_n\mid\theta)p(x_n\mid z_n,\theta). \]

    Treating \(\theta\) as a point to optimize and treating it as a random variable to integrate are different statistical models. EM usually does the former. A Bayesian mixture, topic model, or state-space model may do the latter using conjugacy, variational inference, or Monte Carlo.

    Five Structures Built from the Same Operations¤

    The following map compares dependency structure. It does not claim that the latent variables have the same state space, semantics, or inference cost.

    Table CLASS.2. Five Structures Built from the Same Operations
    Model Representative latent structure Observed marginal Typical posterior route What a neural parameterization changes
    Finite mixture \(z_n\) is one categorical assignment \(\sum_k\pi_kp(x_n\mid k)\) Exact enumeration over \(k\) for fixed parameters Component distributions or mixing weights can be predicted by networks; the finite sum remains
    Factor analysis \(z_n\in\mathbb R^L\) generates correlated features Analytic Gaussian for a linear-Gaussian decoder Exact Gaussian conditioning A nonlinear decoder increases expressivity but generally removes analytic marginalization
    Topic model document proportions \(\theta_d\) and token assignments \(z_{dn}\) Sum/integral over coupled assignments and proportions Collapsed sampling, VI, or specialized message passing An inference network can amortize document-level inference; simplex/count semantics remain modeling choices
    State-space model \(z_{1:T}\) is a Markov chain with local emissions Sum/integral over latent paths Forward–backward, Kalman smoothing, particles, or structured VI Neural transitions/emissions change factor functions, not filtering versus smoothing semantics
    VAE or deep latent model one or more latent layers with neural conditionals Usually an intractable integral Amortized VI, importance methods, or hybrids The decoder and inference model become flexible learned maps; neither flexibility nor amortization implies exactness

    The table reveals the bridge used throughout this book: change the latent state, graph, or conditional family and the same marginalization problem takes a new form; change only the neural architecture and the probabilistic query remains the same.

    Retrieval practice: one law, five operations
    1. Why can ancestral generation be easy when observed-data likelihood is hard?
    2. Does adding an inference network change \(p_\theta(x,z)\)?
    3. When does a point-estimated parameter become a global latent variable?

    Solutions. (1) Generation follows normalized conditionals forward, whereas likelihood must sum or integrate over every compatible latent state. (2) No. It adds an algorithm or approximation for queries about the existing model unless the model definition itself is changed. (3) When the probability model assigns that parameter a prior and includes it among the random variables to be inferred or integrated.

    3. Mixture Models and EM¤

    A \(K\)-component mixture introduces a discrete latent assignment \(z\):

    \[ p(x)=\sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi_k p(x\mid z=k), \qquad \pi_k\ge0, \quad\sum_k\pi_k=1. \]

    The corresponding plate makes two facts immediate: each item has exactly one assignment \(z_i\), while the component weights and component parameters are shared across all items.

    Generation follows the arrows. Responsibility inference reverses the question: after observing \(x_i\), how much posterior mass belongs to each possible \(z_i\)?

    For a Gaussian mixture, the posterior responsibility is

    \[ r_{nk}=p(z_n=k\mid x_n) =\frac{\pi_k\mathcal N(x_n;\mu_k,\Sigma_k)} {\sum_j\pi_j\mathcal N(x_n;\mu_j,\Sigma_j)}. \]

    Expectation–maximization alternates:

    1. E-step: compute \(r_{nk}\) under current parameters;
    2. M-step: maximize the expected complete-data log-likelihood.

    For full-covariance GMMs,

    \[ N_k=\sum_n r_{nk},\quad \pi_k=\frac{N_k}{N},\quad \mu_k=\frac1{N_k}\sum_n r_{nk}x_n, \]
    \[ \Sigma_k=\frac1{N_k}\sum_n r_{nk}(x_n-\mu_k)(x_n-\mu_k)^\top. \]

    EM monotonically improves the data likelihood but can converge to a local optimum or a singular covariance. Variational inference generalizes the same lower-bound logic when the exact E-step is unavailable.

    Why EM Improves the Likelihood¤

    For any distribution \(q(z)\),

    \[ \log p_\theta(x) =\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q(z)] +D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(z)\|p_\theta(z\mid x)). \]

    The E-step sets \(q\) to the current exact posterior, making the bound tight. The M-step increases the first term while holding \(q\) fixed. The new likelihood cannot be below that improved bound, so the procedure is monotone. Monotone does not mean globally optimal: component permutations, poor initialization, overlapping components, and covariance collapse remain.

    Mixture Identifiability and Model Selection¤

    Permuting component labels leaves the mixture density unchanged. Additional non-identifiabilities arise when components coincide or weights vanish. Choosing \(K\) by training likelihood alone always favors added flexibility; held-out likelihood, Bayesian evidence, BIC under its regularity assumptions, or task-specific predictive checks are more informative. Mixture models are singular statistical models, so textbook asymptotic approximations can fail near redundant components.

    The Bayesian Nonparametric Random-Measure Models volume replaces fixed \(K\) with priors over random measures and their induced partitions or feature allocations. It does not remove the need to declare a component likelihood, an inference algorithm, or what a recovered cluster is supposed to mean scientifically.

    Gaussian Scale Mixtures and Adaptive Tails¤

    A mixture need not choose among a finite set of locations. A Gaussian scale mixture first samples a positive precision and then a Gaussian observation:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \lambda &\sim\operatorname{Gamma}\!\left(\frac{\nu}{2},\frac{\nu}{2}\right),\\ x\mid\lambda &\sim\mathcal N\!\left(\mu,\frac{\Sigma}{\lambda}\right), \end{aligned} \]

    where the Gamma distribution uses shape and rate. Integrating \(\lambda\) gives a multivariate Student-\(t\) law with \(\nu\) degrees of freedom. This is a continuous latent-variable model, not a finite Gaussian mixture. Conditional on \(\lambda\) the coordinates are Gaussian; after marginalization the shared random scale couples their tail behavior.

    For a scalar standardized observation, conjugacy gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} a_x&=\frac{\nu+1}{2},\\ b_x&=\frac{\nu+(x-\mu)^2/\Sigma}{2},\\ \lambda\mid x&\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(a_x,b_x). \end{aligned} \]

    With \(\nu=3\), \(\mu=0\), \(\Sigma=1\), and \(x=2\), this is \(\operatorname{Gamma}(2,7/2)\) and has posterior mean \(4/7\). At \(x=0\) the posterior mean is \(4/3\). The model explains a large residual partly by lowering its latent precision, which is why Student-\(t\) observation models can be more robust than a fixed-variance Gaussian. It does not make every outlier harmless: the tail family, degrees of freedom, dependence structure, and contamination mechanism still need checking. The more general normal scale-mixture question and its exact representability conditions are developed by Andrews and Mallows (1974).

    Classical latent-foundation synthesis¤

    Exponential families separate sufficient statistics, natural parameters, and normalization; latent-variable models add unobserved structure whose meaning is limited by identifiability; mixture likelihoods sum over assignments; and EM alternates exact or approximate posterior expectations with parameter updates. Plate notation records repetition and dependence but never substitutes for the actual factorization or observation model.

    4. Factor Analysis and Matrix Factorization¤

    Factor and source-model learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes latent-variable likelihoods and linear algebra. By the end, you should be able to derive the factor-analysis covariance and posterior, identify rotation/scale ambiguities, distinguish probabilistic PCA from generic matrix factorization, compare mixture/local factor models and probabilistic CCA, and state the assumptions that identify linear or auxiliary-variable nonlinear ICA rather than treating every latent coordinate as a source.

    A finite mixture uses a discrete latent to choose among component distributions. A factor model uses a continuous latent vector to induce dependence among observed coordinates. In factor analysis,

    \[ z\sim\mathcal N(0,I_L), \qquad x\mid z\sim\mathcal N(Wz+\mu,\Psi), \]

    where \(W\in\mathbb R^{D\times L}\) and \(\Psi\) is usually diagonal. Marginalizing \(z\) gives

    \[ p(x)=\mathcal N\!\left(x;\mu,WW^\top+\Psi\right). \]

    The graph is almost identical to the mixture plate; the state space and factor family changed. The assignment is now a continuous vector and the observation factor is linear Gaussian:

    This visual similarity isolates the mathematical change responsible for analytic Gaussian marginalization instead of making factor analysis look like an unrelated list of formulas.

    The low-rank term \(WW^\top\) explains covariance shared across features; the diagonal term \(\Psi\) represents feature-specific residual variation. The model therefore uses \(O(DL+D)\) covariance parameters rather than the \(O(D^2)\) entries of an unrestricted covariance matrix when \(L\ll D\).

    The posterior is also Gaussian. With \(M=I_L+W^\top\Psi^{-1}W\),

    \[ p(z\mid x)=\mathcal N\!\left( z; M^{-1}W^\top\Psi^{-1}(x-\mu), M^{-1} \right). \]

    This exactness comes from linear-Gaussian closure. Replacing \(Wz+\mu\) by a nonlinear neural decoder preserves the directed graph \(z\to x\) but usually destroys the analytic marginal and posterior. That replacement leads toward the generative model used by a VAE; adding \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) supplies an approximate inference model rather than changing the latent joint by itself (Kingma and Welling, 2014).

    Worked Two-Dimensional Factor Model¤

    Let \(L=1\), \(D=2\), \(W=(1,1)^\top\), \(\mu=0\), and \(\Psi=I_2\). Then

    \[ z\sim\mathcal N(0,1), \qquad x\mid z\sim\mathcal N\!\left( \begin{bmatrix}z\\z\end{bmatrix},I_2 \right), \]

    so the observed covariance is

    \[ WW^\top+I_2= \begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The two coordinates are correlated even though their observation noises are independent: both inherit the same latent factor. For the observation \(x=(2,0)^\top\),

    \[ M=1+W^\top W=3, \qquad p(z\mid x)=\mathcal N\!\left(z;\frac23,\frac13\right). \]

    The value \(2/3\) is a posterior mean, not a deterministically recovered code. Its variance \(1/3\) records uncertainty left after seeing the noisy coordinates. Generation runs in the other direction: sample one \(z\), then add an independent noise draw to each coordinate.

    Probabilistic PCA and Identifiability¤

    Probabilistic PCA restricts \(\Psi=\sigma^2I\). Maximum-likelihood loading directions span the principal subspace, while the probabilistic model also supplies a noise scale, a density, missing-data handling, and posterior uncertainty (Tipping and Bishop, 1999). It is not identical to applying deterministic PCA and then declaring its coordinates random.

    Factor coordinates are not unique. For any orthogonal \(R\),

    \[ (WR)(WR)^\top=WW^\top, \]

    so rotating \(W\) and the latent coordinate leaves the observed law unchanged under an isotropic latent prior. Sparsity, non-Gaussian independent components, supervision, temporal structure, or other constraints can reduce the equivalence class, but a named axis is not automatically an identified concept.

    Matrix Factorization Needs a Probability Model¤

    A deterministic low-rank approximation writes a data matrix as \(X\approx UV^\top\) and minimizes a reconstruction loss. Non-negative matrix factorization additionally constrains the factors. These are useful representation or optimization methods, but neither is a generative probability model until a likelihood, priors or parameter constraints, and a sampling procedure are declared.

    For example, probabilistic matrix factorization may specify

    \[ u_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\lambda_u^{-1}I), \quad v_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\lambda_v^{-1}I), \quad x_{ij}\mid u_i,v_j\sim\mathcal N(u_i^\top v_j,\sigma^2) \]

    for observed entries. This model has global collections of row and column latents; posterior inference couples them through the observed matrix. A Poisson or multinomial likelihood is often more appropriate for counts, and simplex or non-negativity constraints change both semantics and inference. Topic models are one structured count-data branch of this idea. Deep decoders replace bilinear maps with networks, but they still require a declared observation law if likelihood or posterior claims are made.

    A probabilistic non-negative factorization for counts can instead use Gamma-distributed non-negative factors and a Poisson observation law, for example

    \[ u_{ik}\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(a_u,b_u), \quad v_{jk}\sim\operatorname{Gamma}(a_v,b_v), \quad x_{ij}\mid u_i,v_j \sim\operatorname{Poisson}\!\left(\sum_k u_{ik}v_{jk}\right). \]

    Now prior sampling, count generation, posterior inference, and held-out predictive likelihood are defined. Gamma–Poisson conjugacy can support coordinate or augmented-variable updates, but integrating both factor matrices is not thereby trivial. Different NMF losses correspond to different observation/noise assumptions when a probabilistic interpretation exists; non-negativity alone does not select one. Cemgil (2009) develops the Poisson/Gamma construction and separates maximum-likelihood NMF updates from full Bayesian inference.

    Counterexample: a reconstruction factorization is not yet a generator

    An algorithm that returns matrices \(U,V\) minimizing \(\|X-UV^\top\|_F^2\) has not specified how to sample a new row, how missing entries are distributed, what noise measure the squared loss represents, or whether \(U\) and \(V\) are parameters or random variables. Calling it “generative” before those choices are made hides the probability space.

    Mixtures of Local Factor Models¤

    One linear subspace may be too rigid for curved or heterogeneous data. A mixture of factor analyzers introduces a component \(c\) and local factor \(z\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} c &\sim \operatorname{Categorical}(\pi),\\ z\mid c=k &\sim \mathcal N(0,I),\\ x\mid z,c=k &\sim \mathcal N(W_kz+\mu_k,\Psi_k). \end{aligned} \]

    After marginalizing \(z\), component \(k\) has covariance \(W_kW_k^\top+\Psi_k\). The model is therefore a constrained Gaussian mixture whose local low-rank covariance needs \(O(DL)\) rather than \(O(D^2)\) parameters per component when \(L\ll D\). It can approximate a curved distribution using several local subspaces, but component labels, local rotations, and redundant components remain non-identifiable. A finite mixture of local linear maps is not one globally invertible flow. The original exact-EM construction makes this local-density interpretation explicit (Ghahramani and Hinton, 1996).

    Paired Views and Probabilistic CCA¤

    For paired observations \((x,y)\), a shared latent model writes

    \[ z\sim\mathcal N(0,I),\qquad x\mid z\sim\mathcal N(W_xz+\mu_x,\Psi_x),\qquad y\mid z\sim\mathcal N(W_yz+\mu_y,\Psi_y). \]

    The views are conditionally independent given \(z\), and their cross-covariance is \(W_xW_y^\top\). Under the probabilistic CCA restrictions, maximum likelihood recovers the classical canonical subspaces—not unique named coordinates. View-private latent variables are needed when each view contains structured variation absent from the other; otherwise the shared code is forced to explain both common and private variation. More than two views, nonlinear decoders, or non-Gaussian observation laws change the model and usually the inference problem, even when the training objective still contains a correlation term. The Gaussian latent-variable equivalence to classical CCA is an assumption-specific maximum-likelihood result (Bach and Jordan, 2005).

    Exponential-Family Factors and Nonlinear Decoders¤

    Factor analysis extends beyond real-valued Gaussian observations. A conditional exponential-family model can use

    \[ p(x_d\mid z) = h_d(x_d)\exp\!\left\{ T_d(x_d)^\top\eta_d(z)-A_d(\eta_d(z)) \right\}, \]

    with a linear natural parameter \(\eta_d(z)=W_dz+b_d\) for Bernoulli, count, or categorical data. The likelihood remains normalized, but a Gaussian prior is generally no longer conjugate to this observation law. A deterministic "exponential-family PCA" point estimate of every \(z_n\) is therefore not the same procedure as integrating those latents or maintaining a non-degenerate variational posterior.

    Collins, Dasgupta, and Schapire (2001) derive the point-estimation and Bregman-loss construction. The normalized conditional model above is a probabilistic extension; it should not be attributed every property of that deterministic objective without an inference and parameter-learning specification.

    Replacing \(W_dz+b_d\) by a neural network gives the decoder side of a VAE-like latent model; adding an encoder specifies an inference algorithm. Replacing it by a Gaussian-process function gives a GP latent-variable model. In the classical GP-LVM, latent inputs for the training cases are often optimized rather than produced by an amortized encoder. Sampling a new observation still requires a declared latent prior and the appropriate integration over the GP function or posterior. A nonlinear map increases flexibility; it does not by itself identify the latent coordinates (Lawrence, 2005).

    Independent Components and the Source-Separation Contract¤

    Independent component analysis (ICA) changes the latent assumption rather than merely rotating PCA after the fact. In the square noiseless model,

    \[ z_j\ \text{independent}, \qquad x=Az, \qquad B=A^{-1}, \]

    so a continuous density transforms as

    \[ p_X(x) = \left|\det B\right| \prod_{j=1}^{D}p_j\!\left((Bx)_j\right). \]

    The determinant is essential: optimizing only independent-looking recovered coordinates is not maximum likelihood for a general unmixing matrix. In practice, centering and whitening can reduce the remaining search to an orthogonal rotation, after which likelihood, non-Gaussian contrast functions, or independence objectives estimate the axes.

    Worked finite example: six observations, one exact unmixing rule¤

    Let \(Z_1\) be uniform on \(\{-1,+1\}\), let \(Z_2\) have probabilities \((1/4,1/2,1/4)\) on \(\{-2,0,+2\}\), and make the two sources independent. Mix them with

    \[ \begin{aligned} A&= \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix},\\[4pt] B=A^{-1} &=\frac12 \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}. \end{aligned} \]

    For example, \(z=(1,2)^\top\) produces \(x=(3,-1)^\top\), and \(Bx=(1,2)^\top\) recovers the source exactly. Enumerating the \(2\times3\) latent states gives six observation locations with the corresponding product masses. The observed coordinates are mixtures; the recovered coordinates are independent by construction.

    This example does not prove that a learning algorithm will recover \(B\) from finite samples. Classical linear ICA identification additionally requires an appropriate non-degenerate mixing model and non-Gaussian independent sources, with at most one Gaussian source under the standard theorem. Even then, permutation and nonzero scaling (including sign) are unavoidable equivalences (Comon, 1994). If instead \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)\), every orthogonal \(R\) gives \(RZ\overset{d}{=}Z\); replacing \(A\) by \(AR^\top\) preserves the observed law, so whitening cannot choose a unique rotation.

    Sparse Coding and Dictionary Learning¤

    Sparse coding permits a noisy, rectangular, even overcomplete dictionary:

    \[ \begin{aligned} z_k&\overset{\mathrm{iid}}\sim\operatorname{Laplace}(0,1/\lambda),\\ x\mid z,W&\sim\mathcal N(Wz,\sigma^2I). \end{aligned} \]

    For fixed \(W\), MAP inference is the Lasso-shaped problem

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal J(z) &=\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|x-Wz\|_2^2\\ &\phantom{{}={}}+\lambda\|z\|_1,\\ \hat z_{\mathrm{MAP}}&=\arg\min_z\mathcal J(z). \end{aligned} \]

    With

    \[ W= \begin{bmatrix}1&0&1\\0&1&1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad x=\begin{bmatrix}3\\1\end{bmatrix}, \]

    both \(z=(3,1,0)^\top\) and \(z=(2,0,1)^\top\) reconstruct \(x\) exactly, but their \(\ell_1\) norms are \(4\) and \(3\). The MAP objective prefers the latter when the residuals tie. This is estimator behavior under a declared prior—not proof that the selected atoms are unique physical causes.

    Learning \(W\) while integrating \(z\) maximizes a marginal likelihood. Alternating between point estimates of \(z_n\) and updates of \(W\) instead optimizes a joint or profile objective. These procedures can be useful but are not interchangeable. Dictionary-column norms must also be constrained: replacing one column by \(cw_k\) and its coefficient by \(z_k/c\) preserves reconstruction while shrinking the coefficient penalty as \(c\) grows. Modern sparse autoencoders inherit these scaling, overcompleteness, stability, and identification questions even when the encoder makes inference fast (Mairal et al., 2010).

    Nonlinear ICA: Expressivity Removes the Linear Theorem¤

    An unrestricted invertible nonlinear mixing \(x=f(z)\) is not identified from one observational distribution merely because the coordinates of \(z\) are independent. Flexible measure-preserving transformations can produce different independent coordinates with the same observed law. Positive nonlinear-ICA results therefore add information or structure: temporal nonstationarity, multiple environments, paired views, an auxiliary variable, restricted mixing maps, sparsity, or interventions.

    Hyvärinen, Sasaki, and Turner (2019) derive one auxiliary-variable route using generalized contrastive learning. Khemakhem et al. (2020) obtain identification for a conditional, factorized exponential-family prior under their stated assumptions; an ordinary unconditional VAE does not inherit that theorem. More specific results extend particular identification routes to multi-domain linear mixtures with dependent components and study sparse-autoencoder stability through dictionary-learning conditions. These results enlarge the assumption–conclusion map. They do not make arbitrary nonlinear latents, ICA axes, or sparse-autoencoder features uniquely meaningful.

    Independence is a model claim, not a visual impression

    Low sample correlation establishes neither mutual independence nor source recovery. State the allowed equivalence class, test held-out density or independence with uncertainty, rerun estimation, perturb preprocessing and dimension, and validate any semantic interpretation using external variables or interventions.

    Factor and source-model synthesis¤

    Factor models become generative only after their latent, loading, and noise laws are declared. Their coordinates are generally identified only up to a symmetry. Multiple views, sparsity, temporal structure, or auxiliary variables can reduce ambiguity, but each conclusion inherits the assumptions that supplied it.

    5. Directed Graphical Models¤

    Directed and state-space learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes conditional factorization and latent-variable models. By the end, you should be able to read a DAG, plate, or factor graph as a joint law; separate graph structure from tabular or neural factor parameterization; derive HMM generation, filtering, smoothing, and forecasting queries; execute the scalar Kalman update; and diagnose duration, stability, switching, observation, and latent-coordinate assumptions in state-space models.

    A Bayesian network is a directed acyclic graph with factorization

    \[ p(x_1,\ldots,x_D)=\prod_{i=1}^{D}p(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}). \]

    The graph encodes conditional independences through d-separation. An autoregressive model is a dense ordered DAG; a VAE is a latent DAG \(z\to x\) plus an auxiliary inference model; a causal model adds semantics about interventions that an ordinary Bayesian network does not obtain from factorization alone.

    Graph Structure Is Not Factor Parameterization¤

    A directed graph specifies parent sets and therefore a factorization. It does not prescribe how each conditional distribution is stored or computed. For a node \(X_i\), the same conditional

    \[ p_\theta(x_i\mid x_{\operatorname{pa}(i)}) \]

    may be represented by a conditional-probability table, a generalized linear model, a decision tree, a spline, a probabilistic circuit, or a neural network that outputs normalized distribution parameters. Replacing a table with a network changes the factor's function class, parameter sharing, evaluation cost, and learning behavior. It does not by itself change the DAG or the conditional-independence statements encoded by that DAG. Neural estimators have been used inside Bayesian-network conditionals for decades (Monti and Cooper, 1996); the graph and the estimator remain distinct objects.

    and

    The complete-data likelihood needs two local factor evaluations, and ancestral sampling draws \(A\) before \(B\). The marginal and posterior additionally sum over an unobserved value of \(A\). Nothing about that sum changes when the conditional table is replaced by a neural network.

    What changes as the model grows¤

    For a binary child with \(d\) binary parents, an unrestricted conditional table stores \(2^d\) Bernoulli probabilities. A neural conditional can share parameters across parent configurations and may be much smaller, but compact representation does not guarantee accurate learning or cheap probabilistic inference. Exact variable elimination still depends on variable cardinalities, elimination order, and the width of the induced factors. Bounded treewidth can make exact inference tractable; unrestricted Bayesian-network inference is hard in general (Korhonen and Parviainen, 2013). Parametric structure such as determinism or context-specific independence can sometimes reduce costs beyond a crude treewidth bound, so treewidth is an important control variable rather than the only possible explanation.

    Plates, Factor Graphs, and Separation¤

    The plate-notation key repeats variables over data or time. Factor graphs separate variable nodes from factor nodes and make message passing explicit. In a directed graph, d-separation determines which paths are blocked after conditioning; a collider \(X\to Z\leftarrow Y\) blocks association until \(Z\) or a descendant is observed. Conditioning can therefore create dependence rather than remove it.

    This collider behavior matters in generative evaluation: selecting only examples that pass a filter can induce correlations absent in the source population. It also underlies explaining-away effects in latent-cause models.

    Hidden Markov Models¤

    An HMM factorizes as

    \[ p(z_{1:T},x_{1:T}) =p(z_1)\prod_{t=2}^{T}p(z_t\mid z_{t-1}) \prod_{t=1}^{T}p(x_t\mid z_t). \]

    A plate cannot show a time chain with one generic repeated arrow unambiguously, so sequence models are often unrolled for a few steps. The repeated visual motif below is the factorization above:

    Filtering moves left to right using observations available so far; smoothing sends information back from later observed nodes. The graph stays fixed while the information set changes.

    The forward algorithm computes the marginal likelihood in \(O(TK^2)\), forward–backward computes state marginals, Viterbi computes the most probable state path, and Baum–Welch applies EM. Neural state-space and world models replace transition/emission tables with networks but inherit filtering, smoothing, rollout, and identifiability questions.

    The same finite HMM observation law can be written as a constrained nonnegative matrix product state: hidden-state values become bond indices, and transition/emission tables become site tensors. This is an algebraic correspondence under the HMM's positivity and stochastic constraints, not a claim that every signed or Born MPS of the same rank is an HMM. The complete tensor/Born volume develops that boundary and the effect of variable order.

    Define the scaled forward message

    \[ \alpha_t(j)=p(x_{1:t},z_t=j) =p(x_t\mid z_t=j)\sum_i\alpha_{t-1}(i)A_{ij}. \]

    Multiplying many probabilities underflows, so implementations normalize messages at each step or operate with log-sum-exp. The normalization constants recover the sequence log likelihood. Viterbi replaces the sum with a maximum; its most probable path is not the sequence of independently most probable marginal states.

    Emission Models: Normalized Factors, Not Arbitrary Scores¤

    The HMM graph does not require a categorical observation. Its local emission \(B_j(x)=p(x\mid z_t=j)\) may be categorical for symbols, Gaussian or a mixture for continuous vectors, Poisson or negative binomial for counts, or an autoregressive density for a structured observation. A neural network can parameterize any of these distributions. In every case, however, \(B_j\) must be a normalized probability mass or density over the declared observation space if the model is to support ancestral generation and sequence likelihoods.

    This requirement separates an emission model from a classifier. Bayes' rule gives

    \[ p(x\mid z=j) =\frac{p(z=j\mid x)\,p(x)}{p(z=j)}. \]

    Consequently, calibrated classifier probabilities and compatible state priors can provide relative state evidence: the common factor \(p(x)\) cancels when a filter normalizes across \(j\). They do not by themselves specify the missing observation marginal \(p(x)\), a sampler for \(x\mid z\), or a normalized sequence likelihood. Raw logits have a weaker interpretation still. Calling every state-dependent score an "emission probability" silently changes which model queries are valid.

    The same network, two different probabilistic roles

    A network outputting a Gaussian mean and positive-definite covariance defines \(p(x_t\mid z_t)\) and can generate an observation. A network outputting a softmax over states defines \(p(z_t\mid x_t)\) after calibration; it can help recognize a state, but it does not tell us how to sample \(x_t\) from that state. Architecture alone does not determine the direction of the conditional.

    Learning HMM Parameters: One Model, Four Estimators¤

    For a sequence \(x_{1:T}\), forward–backward produces the smoothed one- and two-slice probabilities

    \[ \begin{aligned} \gamma_t(i) &=p(z_t=i\mid x_{1:T}),\\ \xi_t(i,j) &=p(z_t=i,z_{t+1}=j\mid x_{1:T}). \end{aligned} \]

    Baum–Welch is exact EM for a finite HMM. Its transition M-step normalizes expected transition counts:

    \[ A_{ij}^{\mathrm{new}} =\frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\xi_t(i,j)} {\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}\gamma_t(i)}. \]

    Categorical emission counts are normalized in the same way; Gaussian emissions use responsibility-weighted means and covariances. For example, if the expected transition-count matrix is

    \[ N=\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\2&4\end{bmatrix}, \]

    then row normalization gives

    \[ A^{\mathrm{new}} =\begin{bmatrix}3/4&1/4\\1/3&2/3\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The expected complete-data log likelihood \(Q\) is only one term in the EM lower-bound identity; the posterior entropy completes the ELBO. With an exact E-step and a proper M-step, EM does not decrease the observed-data likelihood. It does not guarantee the global maximum, and its limiting behavior still depends on regularity, initialization, boundaries, and nonidentifiability.

    Durations and Structured Discrete States¤

    An ordinary HMM implies a geometric dwell time. Write the self-transition probability of state \(j\) as \(A_{jj}\). The duration \(D_j\) of one visit satisfies

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(D_j=d) &=A_{jj}^{d-1}(1-A_{jj}),\\ \mathbb E[D_j] &=\frac{1}{1-A_{jj}}. \end{aligned} \]

    For \(A_{jj}=0.8\), a five-step visit has probability \(0.8^4(0.2)=0.08192\) and the mean duration is \(5\). The geometric law is memoryless: after any number of steps in the state, the probability of leaving at the next step remains \(0.2\). A hidden semi-Markov model (HSMM) instead draws an explicit state-dependent duration, permitting peaked, heavy-tailed, bounded, or covariate-dependent dwell-time laws. The expanded state must include the remaining duration or run length, so exact messages cost more than ordinary HMM messages and depend on the supported duration range.

    State-Space Queries: Generation, Filtering, Smoothing, and Forecasting¤

    The HMM is the finite-state member of a larger state-space family. With optional inputs \(u_t\), a controlled state-space model has joint law

    \[ p(z_{1:T},x_{1:T}\mid u_{1:T}) =p(z_1\mid u_1) \prod_{t=2}^{T}p(z_t\mid z_{t-1},u_t) \prod_{t=1}^{T}p(x_t\mid z_t,u_t). \]

    This one probability law supports several different computations. They should not all be called “prediction.”

    Table CLASS.8. State-Space Queries: Generation, Filtering, Smoothing, and Forecasting
    Query Distribution or operation Information available
    unconditional generation sample \(z_1\), then each transition and emission model and any declared inputs
    sequence likelihood \(p(x_{1:T}\mid u_{1:T})\), integrating or summing over every latent path complete observed sequence

    For a \(K\)-state HMM, let \(b_{t-1}(i)=p(z_{t-1}=i\mid x_{1:t-1})\), \(A_{ij}=p(z_t=j\mid z_{t-1}=i)\), and \(B_j(x)=p(x\mid z_t=j)\). The normalized forward filter is the predict–score–update recursion

    \[ \pi_t(j)=\sum_i b_{t-1}(i)A_{ij},\qquad c_t=\sum_j B_j(x_t)\pi_t(j),\qquad b_t(j)=\frac{B_j(x_t)\pi_t(j)}{c_t}. \]

    Here \(c_t=p(x_t\mid x_{1:t-1})\) is not a disposable normalization constant. It gives

    \[ \log p(x_{1:T})=\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log c_t. \]

    Forecasting propagates \(b_t\) through transitions without conditioning on future observations. Smoothing instead sends information backward from observations after \(t\). A recognition network that reads the complete sequence is therefore a smoother, even if its output is called an “encoded state.”

    Linear Gaussian State-Space Models¤

    \[ z_t=Az_{t-1}+\xi_t, \qquad x_t=Cz_t+\epsilon_t, \]

    with Gaussian noise. The Kalman filter gives exact recursive posterior means and covariances; the Rauch–Tung–Striebel smoother conditions on future observations. These are the tractable ancestors of deep latent dynamical models.

    The filter alternates a linear prediction with a Bayesian correction. The Kalman gain balances predicted uncertainty against observation noise. If the transition or emission is nonlinear, extended and unscented Kalman filters approximate these operations; particle filters replace the Gaussian belief with weighted samples. Learned world models inherit the same distinction between filtering from past observations and smoothing with future context.

    For the linear-Gaussian model \(z_t=Az_{t-1}+q_t\), \(x_t=Cz_t+r_t\), with \(q_t\sim\mathcal N(0,Q)\) and \(r_t\sim\mathcal N(0,R)\), a Gaussian filtered belief remains Gaussian. Writing its mean and covariance as \((m_{t-1},P_{t-1})\), the exact update is

    \[ \begin{aligned} m_t^-&=Am_{t-1}, & P_t^-&=AP_{t-1}A^\top+Q,\\ S_t&=CP_t^-C^\top+R, & K_t&=P_t^-C^\top S_t^{-1},\\ m_t&=m_t^-+K_t(x_t-Cm_t^-), \end{aligned} \]

    with posterior covariance computable in the numerically robust Joseph form

    \[ P_t=(I-K_tC)P_t^-(I-K_tC)^\top+K_tRK_t^\top. \]

    Implementations solve a linear system involving \(S_t\) rather than forming \(S_t^{-1}\). Exactness comes from closure of Gaussian distributions under linear conditioning, not from the words “state-space model.” Neural transitions, nonlinear emissions, discrete–continuous switching, and non-Gaussian noise generally destroy that closure.

    Worked Scalar Kalman Update¤

    Suppose the current belief is \(z_0\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\), the transition is \(z_1=z_0+q_1\) with \(q_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1/4)\), and the observation is \(x_1=z_1+r_1\) with \(r_1\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\). Before observing \(x_1\), the predictive state is \(\mathcal N(0,5/4)\) and the predictive observation variance is \(9/4\). For \(x_1=2\),

    \[ K_1=\frac{5/4}{9/4}=\frac59,\qquad m_1=\frac59(2)=\frac{10}{9},\qquad P_1=\left(1-\frac59\right)\frac54=\frac59. \]

    The measurement moves the state estimate toward \(2\) and reduces its variance. A deep state-space model may replace the linear mean maps by neural networks, but it must then choose an approximate filtering or smoothing representation; a network outputting one vector does not by itself preserve this posterior calculation.

    System Identification and Stability¤

    Inference estimates latent states under fixed parameters; system identification estimates the transition, emission, and noise parameters from sequences. If states are observed, linear dynamics reduce to regression. If states are hidden, linear-Gaussian EM uses Kalman smoothing to obtain expected states, cross-products, and lagged cross-products before updating the matrices. Direct likelihood gradients differentiate through the Kalman recursion. Subspace methods instead estimate a finite-dimensional predictive realization from structured covariance or Hankel matrices. These routes optimize or match different objects and need not return the same finite-sample parameters.

    For the unforced process \(z_t=Az_{t-1}+q_t\) with finite noise covariance \(Q\), the standard discrete-time stationarity condition is

    \[ \rho(A)=\max_k|\lambda_k(A)|<1. \]

    When it holds, the stationary covariance is the unique finite solution of the discrete Lyapunov equation

    \[ P_\infty=AP_\infty A^\top+Q. \]

    For the scalar process \(z_t=0.8z_{t-1}+q_t\) with \(\operatorname{Var}(q_t)=0.36\), the solution is \(P_\infty=0.36/(1-0.8^2)=1\). Replacing \(0.8\) by \(1.05\) makes the autonomous latent dynamics unstable; adding process noise increases uncertainty and does not stabilize the transition. Filtering-error stability can hold under broader detectability and stabilizability conditions, so it should not be conflated with stationarity of the unforced latent process.

    A negative eigenvalue can still be stable

    The criterion concerns magnitudes. A scalar transition \(A=-0.9\) is stable even though trajectories alternate sign; \(A=1.01\) is unstable even though the numerical eigenvalue is only slightly greater than one.

    Switching, Nonlinear, and Non-Gaussian Systems¤

    A switching linear dynamical system combines a discrete mode \(s_t\) with a continuous state \(z_t\):

    \[ \begin{aligned} s_t &\sim p(s_t\mid s_{t-1}),\\ z_t &\sim\mathcal N(A_{s_t}z_{t-1},Q_{s_t}),\\ x_t &\sim\mathcal N(C_{s_t}z_t,R_{s_t}). \end{aligned} \]

    The unrolled graph shows why this is not merely an HMM attached to an unrelated Kalman filter. Each regime selects the local continuous dynamics, the continuous state carries information forward, and the observation is emitted from that state:

    Conditioning on a regime path fixes the upper chain and leaves one linear-Gaussian calculation below it. Marginalizing regimes retains every compatible upper-chain path unless an approximation merges or discards them.

    Conditioned on one complete mode path, Kalman inference remains exact. After summing over paths, however, the filtering law is a Gaussian mixture. With two modes, its unreduced number of path-indexed components can double at every step: \(2,4,8,\ldots,2^t\). Generic exact inference is therefore exponential before identical or approximated components are merged. Variational methods, Gaussian-sum pruning, interacting multiple-model filters, particles, and MCMC make different approximations; none inherits exact Kalman closure merely because every local regime is linear Gaussian.

    For nonlinear transitions \(f_\theta\) or emissions \(g_\theta\), an extended Kalman filter linearizes locally, an unscented filter propagates selected sigma points, a particle filter represents the belief by weighted samples, and structured variational methods optimize an approximate path posterior. For non-Gaussian count, binary, censored, or heavy-tailed observations, even a linear transition usually loses Gaussian posterior closure. Each method should therefore report the posterior representation it preserves and the approximation it introduces.

    Structural Probabilistic Time-Series Models¤

    A structural time-series model builds observations from interpretable latent components such as local level, trend, seasonality, regression effects, and intervention terms. These components form one state-space model, so posterior uncertainty propagates through forecasts rather than being appended after a point predictor.

    The local-level model is the smallest example:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mu_t &=\mu_{t-1}+\eta_t, &\eta_t &\sim\mathcal N(0,q),\\ y_t &=\mu_t+\epsilon_t, &\epsilon_t &\sim\mathcal N(0,r). \end{aligned} \]

    If filtering gives \(\mu_t\mid y_{1:t}\sim\mathcal N(m_t,P_t)\), then

    \[ y_{t+h}\mid y_{1:t} \sim\mathcal N\!\left(m_t,\;P_t+hq+r\right). \]

    With \(P_t=0.5\), \(q=0.2\), \(r=0.3\), and \(h=4\), the predictive variance is \(0.5+4(0.2)+0.3=1.6\). The growing uncertainty is a consequence of repeatedly integrating future process noise, not an arbitrary confidence-band rule. Seasonal and regression components enlarge the state and may preserve exact Kalman inference when the complete model remains linear Gaussian.

    Forecasting does not become causal because state arrows point forward in time. A counterfactual-impact analysis additionally needs a well-defined intervention, unaffected comparison information or a defensible structural model, stability of the relevant mechanisms, and diagnostics for pre-treatment fit and sensitivity. Without those assumptions, the model estimates a conditional predictive distribution under observed temporal associations, not an interventional or counterfactual distribution.

    Latent Coordinates Are Not Automatically Identified¤

    Even exact inference is inference about a model coordinate. HMM labels can be permuted without changing the observation law. In a linear state-space model, any invertible coordinate change \(z_t'=Tz_t\) produces observationally equivalent parameters

    \[ A'=TAT^{-1},\qquad C'=CT^{-1},\qquad Q'=TQT^\top, \qquad P_0'=TP_0T^\top. \]

    The initial mean transforms as \(m_0'=Tm_0\). Nonlinear latent models admit still broader reparameterizations. Calling a learned coordinate “velocity,” “object,” or “health” therefore needs supervision, structural constraints, interventions, or an identification argument. Results for switching dynamical systems, for example, obtain stronger identification only under declared model assumptions and up to a stated transformation class. Predictive adequacy alone does not assign physical meaning.

    Directed and state-space synthesis¤

    Directed graphs organize normalized conditional factors, while inference depends on their algebra and graph width. HMMs and state-space models distinguish path generation, filtering, smoothing, and forecasting. Neural factors can increase expressivity without changing those query definitions, and latent coordinates remain non-identifiable unless additional structure supports them.

    6. Undirected Models and Energies¤

    Undirected and dependency-structure learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes exponential families, conditional independence, and matrix algebra. By the end, you should be able to distinguish an energy from its normalized undirected law, derive RBM conditional structure, relate zeros in a Gaussian precision matrix to conditional independence, work the marginal- association counterexample, and compare neighborhood selection, score-based DAG search, equivalence classes, and latent-induced dependence.

    An undirected graphical model has

    \[ p(x)=\frac1Z\prod_{c\in\mathcal C}\psi_c(x_c) =\frac{e^{-E(x)}}Z. \]

    Local factors make constraints and symmetric relations easy to express, but the partition function couples all configurations. Conditional random fields apply the same structure to \(p(y\mid x)\).

    For a pairwise Markov random field,

    \[ p(x)\propto\prod_i\psi_i(x_i)\prod_{(i,j)\in E}\psi_{ij}(x_i,x_j). \]

    On a tree, messages from \(i\) to \(j\) take the form

    \[ m_{i\to j}(x_j)=\sum_{x_i}\psi_i(x_i)\psi_{ij}(x_i,x_j) \prod_{k\in N(i)\setminus j}m_{k\to i}(x_i). \]

    After two passes, local products of incoming messages yield exact node marginals. On graphs with loops, the same updates become loopy belief propagation: often useful, but no longer guaranteed to converge or return exact marginals.

    Restricted Boltzmann Machines¤

    An RBM uses visible \(v\) and hidden \(h\) units with bipartite energy

    \[ E(v,h)=-b^\top v-c^\top h-v^\top Wh. \]

    The bipartite graph makes \(p(h\mid v)\) and \(p(v\mid h)\) factorize, enabling block Gibbs updates. The canonical Gibbs and blocking contract explains why acceptance is one without implying rapid mixing. The marginal likelihood still contains an intractable partition function, motivating contrastive divergence. Deep belief networks stacked RBMs historically before end-to-end VAEs and GANs became dominant.

    Contrastive divergence initializes a short Markov chain at data and uses its endpoint as an approximate negative-phase sample. It is not the exact likelihood gradient unless the chain reaches equilibrium. Persistent contrastive divergence keeps chains across updates to reduce repeated burn-in, but changing parameters mean the chain target also moves. These limitations reappear in modern neural EBMs.

    A deep belief network is a historical hybrid with an undirected top pair and directed lower layers; it is not the generic name for any deep Bayesian network. A deep Boltzmann machine remains undirected across layers and requires approximate posterior inference.

    Learning Dependency Graphs from Data¤

    So far the graph has been part of the model specification. In many applications, however, the graph is unknown and must be selected from repeated observations. Before choosing an algorithm, identify which graph is being learned:

    These objects can share an adjacency-matrix representation while answering different questions. This section concerns the first object: selecting the factorization or conditional-independence structure of a distribution over a fixed collection of variables. The random-graph volume owns graph-valued observations, the causal volume owns intervention semantics, and the graphical-inference model volume owns the factorization, separation, and graph-semantics contract, while the graphical-inference volume owns queries once the factors have been declared.

    Gaussian graphical models: zeros in the precision matrix¤

    Let \(X\in\mathbb R^D\) have a nonsingular Gaussian law

    \[ X\sim\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma), \qquad \Theta=\Sigma^{-1}. \]

    For distinct coordinates \(i\) and \(j\),

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Theta_{ij}=0 &\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad X_i\perp X_j\mid R_{ij},\\ R_{ij} &=X_{\{1,\ldots,D\}\setminus\{i,j\}}. \end{aligned} \]

    Thus the off-diagonal support of the precision matrix, not the covariance matrix, defines the undirected conditional-independence graph. The equivalence uses the nonsingular multivariate-Gaussian model. Zero covariance does not encode conditional independence for arbitrary non-Gaussian distributions, and a nonzero covariance between two Gaussian coordinates can coexist with their conditional independence given the remaining coordinates.

    For centered IID observations \(x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(N)}\) with sample covariance \(S=N^{-1}\sum_n x^{(n)}x^{(n)\top}\), the average Gaussian log likelihood, up to constants independent of \(\Theta\), is

    \[ \begin{aligned} \ell(\Theta) &=\frac12\bigl[ \log\det\Theta-\operatorname{tr}(S\Theta) \bigr],\\ &\hspace{7em}\Theta\succ0. \end{aligned} \]

    When \(D\) is not small relative to \(N\), the unregularized sample covariance may be noisy or singular. One graphical-lasso convention defines

    \[ \widehat\Theta_\lambda =\underset{\Theta\succ0}{\arg\min}\;\mathcal J_\lambda(\Theta), \]

    where

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal J_\lambda(\Theta) &=-\log\det\Theta+\operatorname{tr}(S\Theta)\\ &\quad+\lambda\sum_{i\ne j}|\Theta_{ij}|. \end{aligned} \]

    Here the diagonal is not penalized; implementations that use \(\lVert\Theta\rVert_1\) instead must state whether diagonal entries are included. The penalty estimates parameters and selects edges simultaneously. It does not make \(\lambda\) a property of nature: changing \(\lambda\) changes the selected graph. Cross-validated held-out likelihood, stability under resampling, domain constraints, and error control answer different selection questions and need not choose the same graph.

    Worked example: marginal association without an edge¤

    Consider the precision matrix

    \[ \Theta= \begin{bmatrix} 2&-1&0\\ -1&2&-1\\ 0&-1&2 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Its inverse is

    \[ \Sigma=\Theta^{-1} =\frac14 \begin{bmatrix} 3&2&1\\ 2&4&2\\ 1&2&3 \end{bmatrix}. \]

    The covariance \(\Sigma_{13}=1/4\) is nonzero, so \(X_1\) and \(X_3\) are marginally associated. Yet \(\Theta_{13}=0\). Direct Gaussian conditioning gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} C_{13\mid2} &\equiv\operatorname{Cov}(X_1,X_3\mid X_2),\\ C_{13\mid2} &=\Sigma_{13}-\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{23},\\ &=\frac14-\frac12(1)\frac12=0. \end{aligned} \]

    The chain \(1-2-3\) therefore explains the endpoint association through the middle variable. Thresholding the covariance matrix would add the wrong edge; thresholding an estimated precision matrix without uncertainty analysis can still add or delete edges because the estimate is noisy.

    Discrete neighborhoods and tractable special cases¤

    For binary spins \(x_i\in\{-1,+1\}\), a pairwise Ising model has

    \[ \begin{aligned} p(x)&=\frac1Z\exp\bigl(U(x)\bigr),\\ U(x)&=\sum_i h_i x_i+\sum_{i<j}J_{ij}x_ix_j. \end{aligned} \]

    Under this strictly positive pairwise model, \(J_{ij}=0\) removes the direct conditional dependence between \(X_i\) and \(X_j\). The global normalizer is usually expensive, but each node conditional is logistic. Neighborhood selection fits one sparse conditional regression per node; a symmetric graph then needs an explicit AND or OR reconciliation rule. Pseudolikelihood multiplies such node conditionals and avoids evaluating \(Z\), but it is not the joint likelihood. Its statistical guarantees require the stated sparsity, signal, regularity, sample-size, and model-correctness assumptions.

    Trees provide an important exact boundary. For a discrete distribution, the Chow--Liu construction selects the maximum-weight spanning tree using empirical pairwise mutual informations. At the population level it gives the tree-structured distribution minimizing forward KL divergence to the target; with finite samples, mutual-information estimation and small edge gaps create structure uncertainty. A tractable tree approximation is not evidence that the data-generating graph is truly a tree.

    Hidden variables turn sparse structure into dense dependence¤

    A sparse graph on observed and hidden variables need not remain sparse after the hidden variables are marginalized. Partition a joint Gaussian precision matrix between observed variables \(O\) and latent variables \(H\). The marginal observed precision is the Schur complement

    \[ \begin{aligned} \Theta_{O,\mathrm{marg}} &=K_{OO}-K_{OH}K_{HH}^{-1}K_{HO}\\ &=S-L. \end{aligned} \]

    where \(S=K_{OO}\) may be sparse and \(L=K_{OH}K_{HH}^{-1}K_{HO}\succeq0\) has rank no greater than the number of latent coordinates. One hidden common neighbor can therefore induce many nonzero observed precision entries. Sparse-plus-low-rank estimators attempt to separate direct observed interactions from latent common variation, but that decomposition is not automatic: recovery needs sufficient samples, signal, rank and sparsity control, and geometric conditions that keep the sparse and low-rank components distinguishable.

    For example, take

    \[ \begin{aligned} K_{OO}&=2I_2,\\ K_{OH}&=\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix},\\ K_{HH}&=2. \end{aligned} \]

    The low-rank correction is

    \[ L=\frac12 \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}. \]

    There is no direct observed-to-observed precision edge in \(K_{OO}\), yet

    \[ \Theta_{O,\mathrm{marg}} =2I_2-L =\begin{bmatrix}3/2&-1/2\\-1/2&3/2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The induced off-diagonal entry is a concrete warning: an observed dependency edge may summarize an unobserved common cause rather than a direct relation.

    For a Bayesian network, structure learning searches over DAG factorizations rather than precision-matrix supports. Common strategies are:

    A selected adjacency is not yet a generator¤

    An adjacency matrix by itself specifies neither local probability factors nor a normalized joint law. After selecting structure, one must fit conditional distributions or potentials, declare the support and base measure, and establish the relevant normalizer, inference, and sampling procedures. Gaussian graphical models are a favorable case: a positive-definite fitted precision matrix immediately defines a normalized Gaussian law. A collection of unreconciled neighborhood regressions or attention weights does not.

    Graph learning inside a GNN supplies another instructive boundary. Variational graph structure learning by marginal likelihood learns a distribution over adjacencies jointly with a GNN for a declared node-prediction task. Its adjacency is conditioned on that task, architecture, graph prior, variational family, and approximation. It is not automatically the Markov graph of a joint distribution over measured variables, the law of a random graph, or a causal graph. Conversely, results on learning Bayes nets with a fixed chordal skeleton and bounded indegree are distribution-learning results under that structural regime, not generic evidence that unrestricted DAG search is easy.

    Counterexample: predictive edges need not recover dependencies

    Suppose a label is predictable from either of two strongly correlated features. A task-trained graph can retain one route and suppress the other with no loss in prediction, even when both variables participate in the observational dependency law. Predictive adequacy, conditional-independence recovery, and causal recovery use different targets.

    Undirected energies specify local compatibility only after a base measure and normalizer are supplied. Sparse precision or neighborhood structure represents conditional rather than marginal independence, and hidden variables can make marginals dense. Learning an adjacency or equivalence class is therefore a model-selection result, not yet a complete generator.

    7. Topic Models¤

    Sequence, function, and exact-inference learning objectives¤

    This projection assumes directed latent models and conditional probability. By the end, you should be able to state the exchangeability and count assumptions of topic models, construct and condition a Gaussian-process prior, identify when message passing is exact from graph structure, execute the two-state HMM generation/filtering/smoothing/forecasting example, and explain what classical tractability teaches about modern structured generators.

    Latent Dirichlet allocation (Blei, Ng & Jordan, 2003) models each document with topic proportions \(\theta_d\) and each word with a latent topic:

    \[ \theta_d\sim\operatorname{Dirichlet}(\alpha), \quad z_{dn}\sim\operatorname{Categorical}(\theta_d), \quad w_{dn}\sim\operatorname{Categorical}(\beta_{z_{dn}}). \]

    LDA needs nested plates because documents repeat, and words repeat inside each document:

    The outer plate says each document receives one topic-mixture vector. The inner plate says every token receives a topic assignment and an observed word. The shared \(\beta\) remains outside both plates because every document uses the same topic-word catalog.

    Exact posterior inference couples topic assignments through \(\theta_d\), so variational inference or collapsed Gibbs sampling is used. LDA demonstrates a recurring lesson: interpretable latent names do not guarantee identified human concepts; priors, data, and posterior symmetries matter.

    Collapsed Gibbs sampling integrates out Dirichlet-distributed proportions and samples each topic assignment from counts involving document–topic and topic–word frequencies. Collapsing reduces dimension and can improve mixing, but large corpora still require sparse data structures, parallel approximations, or amortized alternatives. The foundation chapter separates collapsing the chain from Rao–Blackwellizing an estimator and states the parallel-update boundary. Topic coherence metrics can reward semantically familiar word lists without establishing predictive likelihood or causal themes.

    8. Gaussian Processes¤

    A Gaussian process defines a distribution over functions:

    \[ f\sim\mathcal{GP}(m,k), \]

    meaning every finite vector \((f(x_1),\ldots,f(x_N))\) is jointly Gaussian. Conditioning gives closed-form prediction for Gaussian observation noise. The kernel encodes smoothness, periodicity, and invariances; exact inference costs \(O(N^3)\) time and \(O(N^2)\) memory, motivating sparse approximations and Neural Processes.

    For training inputs \(X\), targets \(y\), kernel matrix \(K\), and noise variance \(\sigma^2\), the posterior at test inputs \(X_*\) is Gaussian with

    \[ \mu_*=K_{*X}(K_{XX}+\sigma^2I)^{-1}y, \]
    \[ \Sigma_*=K_{**}-K_{*X}(K_{XX}+\sigma^2I)^{-1}K_{X*}. \]

    The same solve appears in the marginal likelihood used to learn kernel hyperparameters. A GP is a distribution over functions with consistent finite-dimensional marginals; independently predicting a Gaussian at each input does not provide that joint coherence.

    9. Exact Inference, Message Passing, and Tractability¤

    Variable elimination rearranges sums/products using distributivity. On trees, sum–product message passing computes exact marginals; max–product computes MAP assignments. Complexity is exponential in induced treewidth, not simply variable count. Probabilistic circuits make tractability a construction invariant by compiling compatible sums and products into a circuit.

    The complete derivation now lives in Graphical Inference and Message Passing: one finite factor graph is solved by enumeration, elimination, two-pass sum-product, junction-tree clustering, loopy approximation, and log-normalizer differentiation. This section retains the classical-model orientation and HMM application.

    What Classical Tractability Teaches¤

    Exact inference usually comes from at least one of four sources:

    1. conjugate algebra closes posterior updates;
    2. conditional independence reduces a global sum to local messages;
    3. restricted graph structure keeps treewidth small;
    4. circuit constraints make distributive computations reusable.

    Deep models relax those restrictions by using universal function approximators, but the missing computation does not disappear—it becomes amortized inference, Monte Carlo estimation, variational optimization, MCMC, or an implicit objective. Classical models remain the cleanest test cases for verifying those approximations.

    Worked Example: Forward Filtering in a Two-State HMM¤

    Let transition matrix

    \[ A=\begin{bmatrix}0.8&0.2\\0.3&0.7\end{bmatrix}, \]

    initial state probability \((0.6,0.4)\), and emission probability for observation \(x_1=1\) equal to \((0.1,0.8)\). The unnormalized filtered state is

    \[ \tilde\alpha_1=(0.6,0.4)\odot(0.1,0.8)=(0.06,0.32), \]

    so \(p(x_1=1)=0.38\) and

    \[ p(z_1\mid x_1=1)=(0.1579,0.8421). \]

    This same finite model now exposes all of the state-space queries.

    1. Generate a Joint Path¤

    Let \(p(x_t=0\mid z_t)=(0.9,0.2)\). One possible latent and observed path is \((z_1,z_2)=(2,1)\) and \((x_1,x_2)=(1,0)\). Its joint probability is

    \[ p(z_1=2,x_1=1,z_2=1,x_2=0) =0.4\times0.8\times0.3\times0.9=0.0864. \]

    Generation samples one such path. Likelihood evaluation sums the probabilities of all latent paths compatible with the observations.

    2. Filter After the Second Observation¤

    Before observing \(x_2\), the joint forward mass over \(z_2\) is

    \[ (0.06,0.32)A=(0.144,0.236). \]

    After observing \(x_2=0\), the unnormalized mass is

    \[ (0.144,0.236)\odot(0.9,0.2)=(0.1296,0.0472). \]

    Thus \(p(x_1=1,x_2=0)=0.1768\) and

    \[ p(z_2\mid x_1=1,x_2=0)=(0.7330,0.2670). \]

    Equivalently, \(p(x_2=0\mid x_1=1)=0.1768/0.38\approx0.4653\). The two evidence factors \(0.38\) and \(0.4653\) multiply to the same sequence likelihood.

    3. Smooth the Earlier State¤

    The later observation also changes the belief about \(z_1\). Its backward likelihoods are

    \[ p(x_2=0\mid z_1=1)=0.8(0.9)+0.2(0.2)=0.76, \]
    \[ p(x_2=0\mid z_1=2)=0.3(0.9)+0.7(0.2)=0.41. \]

    Combining these with the time-one forward masses gives

    \[ p(z_1\mid x_1=1,x_2=0) =\frac{(0.06,0.32)\odot(0.76,0.41)}{0.1768} =(0.2579,0.7421). \]

    The filtered belief at time one was \((0.1579,0.8421)\); smoothing assigns more mass to state 1 because the later zero is more compatible with it. This is legitimate offline information, but it would be future leakage in an online filter.

    4. Forecast Without Future Evidence¤

    Propagating the time-two filtered belief through \(A\) gives

    \[ p(z_3\mid x_{1:2})=(0.6665,0.3335). \]

    Therefore

    \[ p(x_3=1\mid x_{1:2})=0.6665(0.1)+0.3335(0.8)\approx0.3334. \]

    This calculation begins from a distribution over the current state, not from its most likely value. Replacing \((0.7330,0.2670)\) by state 1 would discard uncertainty before the rollout begins. The same mistake appears in neural world models when one encoded vector is treated as if it were a calibrated belief.

    Cumulative Worked Example: A Bernoulli Mixture from Generation to EM¤

    Classical-model capstone learning objectives¤

    This capstone assumes all preceding classical chapters. By the end, you should be able to generate and score complete and observed Bernoulli-mixture records, infer responsibilities, perform an exact EM update, sample from the updated model, identify its label and data-identifiability limits, and use the diagnostic checklist to connect mixture failures to factor, graph, state-space, and dependency-structure models.

    This finite model carries one set of numbers through the operations introduced in Section 2. It is deliberately small enough to enumerate—and deliberately non-identifiable enough to show why a latent explanation is not automatically learned from observations.

    Let \(Z\in\{1,2\}\) choose one of two Bernoulli components:

    \[ p(Z=1)=p(Z=2)=\frac12, \qquad p(X=1\mid Z=1)=\mu_1=0.2, \qquad p(X=1\mid Z=2)=\mu_2=0.8. \]

    1. Generate and Score a Complete Pair¤

    Generation first samples \(Z\), then samples \(X\) from the selected component. For example,

    \[ p(X=1,Z=2)=p(Z=2)p(X=1\mid Z=2)=0.5(0.8)=0.4. \]

    The four joint masses are

    Table CLASS.11. 1. Generate and Score a Complete Pair
    \(X=0\) \(X=1\)
    \(Z=1\) \(0.4\) \(0.1\)
    \(Z=2\) \(0.1\) \(0.4\)

    They sum to one, so they can be checked without numerical integration.

    2. Marginalize and Infer Responsibilities¤

    Summing down each column gives

    \[ p(X=0)=p(X=1)=0.5. \]

    Conditioning reverses the generative direction. For an arbitrary observation \(x_n\), the responsibility of component 2 is

    \[ r_{n2}= \frac{0.5\,\mu_2^{x_n}(1-\mu_2)^{1-x_n}} {0.5\,\mu_1^{x_n}(1-\mu_1)^{1-x_n} +0.5\,\mu_2^{x_n}(1-\mu_2)^{1-x_n}}. \]

    Thus

    \[ p(Z=2\mid X=1)=0.8, \qquad p(Z=2\mid X=0)=0.2. \]

    These are posterior probabilities under the current parameters, not observed labels.

    3. Evaluate the Observed-Data Likelihood¤

    For the dataset \((x_1,x_2,x_3)=(1,1,0)\), the initial observed-data likelihood is

    \[ p(x_{1:3})=(0.5)^3=\frac18, \qquad \log p(x_{1:3})=-3\log2\approx-2.07944. \]

    Multiplying the most likely complete-data assignments would answer a different question; the observed likelihood sums over both assignments for every item.

    4. Perform One Exact E-Step and M-Step¤

    For the two ones, the component-two responsibilities are \(r_{12}=r_{22}=0.8\); for the zero, \(r_{32}=0.2\). The effective counts are therefore \(N_2=1.8\) and \(N_1=1.2\). The M-step gives

    \[ \pi_2'=\frac{1.8}{3}=0.6, \qquad \mu_2'=\frac{0.8+0.8}{1.8}=0.888\ldots, \]

    while

    \[ \pi_1'=0.4, \qquad \mu_1'=\frac{0.2+0.2}{1.2}=\frac13. \]

    Soft assignments let every observation contribute to each component. The updated marginal success probability is

    \[ p_{\theta'}(X=1) =0.4\left(\frac13\right)+0.6\left(\frac89\right) =\frac23. \]

    Consequently,

    \[ p_{\theta'}(x_{1:3}) =\left(\frac23\right)^2\left(\frac13\right) =\frac4{27}, \qquad \log p_{\theta'}(x_{1:3})\approx-1.90954. \]

    The likelihood rose from \(1/8\) to \(4/27\), exactly as the EM lower-bound argument predicts. This is one monotone update, not a proof of a global optimum.

    5. Sample from the Updated Model¤

    To draw a new observation, sample \(Z=2\) with probability \(0.6\) (otherwise \(Z=1\)), then sample

    \[ X\mid Z=2\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(8/9), \qquad X\mid Z=1\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(1/3). \]

    Marginally, the new draw is Bernoulli with success probability \(2/3\). Sampling the component exposes a latent explanation; sampling only \(X\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(2/3)\) produces the same observed law.

    6. Diagnose What the Data Cannot Identify¤

    A mixture of univariate Bernoulli distributions is itself Bernoulli:

    \[ p(X=1)=\pi_1\mu_1+\pi_2\mu_2. \]

    Infinitely many triples \((\pi_2,\mu_1,\mu_2)\) yield \(2/3\), so observations of one binary coordinate cannot identify the two components. The responsibilities are internally consistent with the fitted parameters but are not uniquely determined by the observed law. Multiple binary features with conditional structure, repeated measurements, informative priors, or other assumptions can make a mixture more informative, yet label permutation still remains.

    With only three points, even the identifiable observed Bernoulli rate is uncertain. The purpose of the example is to expose generation, joint scoring, marginalization, posterior responsibilities, EM learning, and sampling—not to claim a reliable population decomposition.

    Failure and Diagnostic Checklist¤

    • Mixtures: inspect tiny component weights, covariance eigenvalues, label stability, and multiple initializations.
    • Factor and matrix models: inspect residual covariance, held-out likelihood, posterior uncertainty, rank sensitivity, and invariance under rotations or rescalings.
    • ICA/source separation: test held-out likelihood and residual dependence, repeat whitening and initialization, report the equivalence class, and validate sources against external information.
    • Sparse dictionaries: normalize columns, vary sparsity and width, compare code stability across runs, and separate MAP reconstruction from marginal predictive evidence.
    • HMMs/state-space models: verify that emissions are normalized in the declared direction, compare filtered and smoothed beliefs, inspect transition entropy and dwell-time fit, test stability, and evaluate long-rollout calibration.
    • State estimators: declare the information set, posterior representation, approximation family, and whether future observations entered training or evaluation.
    • Undirected models: assess chain mixing and partition-function estimators rather than relying on reconstruction alone.
    • Learned dependency graphs: declare the graph object and assumptions; vary regularization, resample data, report edge stability or posterior uncertainty, test held-out joint queries, and examine latent-variable sensitivity.
    • Topic models: report held-out likelihood and stability/coherence under multiple seeds; do not name topics as if labels were identified.
    • Gaussian processes: check kernel assumptions, numerical conditioning, posterior calibration, and extrapolation behavior.
    • Graphical inference: record whether results are exact, variational, loopy-message, or Monte Carlo approximations.

    Misconception Checks¤

    A graphical factorization is not automatically causal

    A DAG can encode conditional independence without making intervention or counterfactual claims. Causal semantics require structural assumptions and an intervention model.

    EM is not generic gradient descent

    EM alternates an exact or approximate posterior expectation with parameter maximization of a lower bound. Generalized EM may only improve the M-step objective, but the latent-variable decomposition remains essential.

    A state score is not automatically an emission density

    A discriminative score can rank states for one observation without defining a normalized law over observations. Generation and sequence likelihood require the latter.

    An exact E-step is not Bayesian parameter inference

    Standard mixture EM computes exact assignment responsibilities conditional on current point parameters and then optimizes those parameters. It does not integrate parameter uncertainty, guarantee a global optimum, or identify a unique latent interpretation.

    A named latent component is not identified

    Mixture labels can permute, topic meanings can rotate or split, and latent factors can be observationally equivalent.

    Non-Gaussian does not mean semantically identified

    Classical ICA can reduce linear rotational ambiguity under its source and mixing assumptions. It still permits permutation and scaling, and it supplies no domain meaning for an axis. Nonlinear models require stronger assumptions still.

    Local linearity does not restore exact global inference

    A switching or nonlinear model may have tractable local conditionals while its path-marginal posterior is an exponentially growing mixture or a non-Gaussian law.

    Temporal precedence is not an intervention

    A state-space graph orders variables in time. Counterfactual claims additionally require intervention semantics, identification assumptions, and an appropriate comparison distribution.

    An estimated edge does not have one universal meaning

    A nonzero Gaussian precision entry, a Bayesian-network parent, a causal arrow, a sampled random-graph edge, and a learned GNN connection answer different questions. Shared adjacency notation does not transfer their semantics or guarantees.

    Sparsity does not remove hidden-variable bias

    A sparse estimator can select a compact observed graph even when latent common variables induced the observed dependencies. Direct-interaction language requires a latent-variable argument, not merely a sparse picture.
