    Newly unlocked in the default route

    No immediate successor names this page as its final required prerequisite. Continue through the book navigation or the chapter's cross-links.

    Generative modeling uses the word gradient for several mathematical objects. A parameter gradient trains a neural likelihood. A score is a derivative with respect to a generated state. A pathwise or score-function rule estimates the derivative of an expectation. A solver adjoint propagates sensitivity through a continuous-time computation. A policy gradient assigns reward to a sampled trajectory. An adversarial gradient belongs to a coupled vector field. A functional gradient moves a probability measure or a particle map. A natural gradient is steepest descent under a statistical metric. These objects can appear in one system without being interchangeable.

    The Optimization and Learning Dynamics foundation develops derivative programs, stochastic estimators, optimizer state, game dynamics, and transport. This later volume organizes their applications across model families. Its governing questions are: gradient of what, with respect to what, estimated under which law, interpreted in which geometry, and used by which finite algorithm?

    How to read this volume

    • Read continuously for the complete argument.
    • Entry after prerequisites: review the foundation, then read Chapters 1--4 and the decision table in Chapter 10.
    • Practitioner: add Chapters 5--9 and follow the application route for the family you use.
    • Research: read all ten chapters, especially estimator target changes, discrete and multi-sample estimators, singular models, solver sensitivities, game and bilevel derivatives, functional gradients, and evidence boundaries.

    Book-length routes into the subject¤

    No single text covers statistical information geometry, derivative programs, Monte Carlo sensitivity, transport geometry, and modern generative-model applications at equal depth. Use these routes as complements:

    • Probabilistic-machine-learning route: Murphy's Probabilistic Machine Learning: Advanced Topics supplies the broad probabilistic context and repeatedly connects variational, Monte Carlo, graphical, and deep-learning methods. It is the best bridge into this volume when the model families are familiar but their gradient objects are not yet separated.
    • Information-geometry route: Amari's Information Geometry and Its Applications begins with divergence-induced dual geometry, then develops statistical inference, hidden variables, machine learning, and natural-gradient dynamics, including singular regions. Use it for the mathematical foundations behind Chapters 2--3 and 5--7.
    • Derivative-program route: Griewank and Walther's Evaluating Derivatives treats forward/reverse automatic differentiation, adjoints, sparsity, checkpointing, and higher derivatives as properties of implemented numerical programs. Use it for Chapters 5 and 8.
    • Stochastic-sensitivity route: Glasserman's Monte Carlo Methods in Financial Engineering develops variance reduction, path simulation, discretization, and sensitivity estimation in a domain where estimator bias and numerical error cannot be hidden. Pair it with the accessible Monte Carlo gradient survey for Chapter 4.
    • Optimization route: Boyd and Vandenberghe's open Convex Optimization is the supporting reference for local models, duality, constrained updates, Newton/trust-region reasoning, and sensitivity. It does not make the neural problems convex; it clarifies which finite subproblem is actually solved.
    • Distributional-geometry route: Peyré and Cuturi's open Computational Optimal Transport emphasizes scalable numerical OT, while Santambrogio's Optimal Transport for Applied Mathematicians develops Wasserstein spaces, functionals on probability measures, and gradient flows with proofs. Use both for Chapter 9.

    These sources use the same words—gradient, metric, natural, flow, adjoint—for objects living in different spaces. Translate every result into this volume's contract before transferring it: objective, differentiation variable, averaging law, geometry, estimator or solver, and finite update.

    Reader orientation¤

    Learning contract¤

    By the end, you should be able to:

    • classify a gradient by its objective, differentiation variable, expectation, geometry, and finite estimator;
    • name the probability law whose Fisher information is being used;
    • derive natural directions in regular and singular finite-dimensional models;
    • exploit exponential-family dual coordinates in maximum likelihood and variational inference;
    • choose and audit gradient estimators for discrete latents, multi-sample bounds, stochastic trajectories, and non-differentiable rewards;
    • implement a damped matrix-free natural step without calling an empirical gradient outer product “the Fisher”;
    • decide what natural-gradient claim is meaningful for AR models, VAEs, EBMs, GANs, diffusion models, flows, and search distributions; and
    • distinguish parameter, state-score, game, implicit, functional, and distributional gradients as well as Fisher--Rao, Wasserstein, Sinkhorn, latent-space, and data-manifold geometries.

    The order is contractual. Starting with an optimizer or sampler name and guessing what derivative it represents reverses the scientific argument.

    1. A Taxonomy of Gradient Objects¤

    Chapter 1 prerequisites and learning objectives¤

    Prerequisites. Multivariable derivatives, probability densities, and expected objectives.

    By the end, you should be able to:

    1. O1 — Type a gradient claim: name its objective, variable, averaging law, geometry, and estimator or solver.
    2. O2 — Separate vector spaces: distinguish parameter, state, game, implicit, functional, and search gradients by domain and codomain.
    3. O3 — Separate estimator from geometry: identify when two estimators target one Euclidean derivative and when a metric transforms that derivative.
    4. O4 — Audit pullbacks and shapes: verify the Jacobian or adjoint needed to transport a state-space field to parameters.
    5. O5 — Detect target changes: recognize relaxation, truncation, detachment, and straight-through rules that alter or replace a declared derivative.
    6. O6 — Transfer the contract: specify all five fields for a new generative-model training or sampling update.

    The five-field gradient contract¤

    Write a gradient claim as

    \[ \mathsf G =\bigl( \text{objective}, \text{differentiation variable}, \text{averaging law}, \text{geometry}, \text{estimator or solver} \bigr). \]

    For example, the pathwise VAE encoder gradient may be described as:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathsf G_{\mathrm{VAE}} =\bigl(&-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x),\ \phi,\ \varepsilon\sim p(\varepsilon),\\ &\text{Euclidean parameter metric},\ \text{reparameterized Monte Carlo and reverse AD}\bigr). \end{aligned} \]

    Changing any field can change the mathematical object. Using the same neural architecture, automatic-differentiation system, or optimizer name does not restore equality.

    “Stochastic gradient” can mean a minibatch estimator of a deterministic parameter gradient or a score-function estimator through a random variable. “Score gradient” can mean the data score \(\nabla_x\log p(x)\) or an outer-product curvature built from parameter scores \(\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x)\). The subscripts are not optional decoration; they identify different vector spaces.

    The map below is a practical type checker. A familiar word such as “score” is not enough: follow the variable first, then ask whether another map is required before the resulting vector can be used elsewhere.

    Estimators are not geometries¤

    Suppose

    \[ J(\theta)=\mathbb E_{Z\sim q_\theta}[f_\theta(Z)]. \]

    The score-function and pathwise identities can estimate the same Euclidean parameter derivative under their respective regularity conditions. They do not define different population objectives merely because their sample formulas differ. Conversely, a finite-temperature relaxation or a straight-through rule may estimate the derivative of a relaxed objective or a custom surrogate rather than \(\nabla_\theta J\).

    Once an estimator \(\widehat g\) is available, a natural, mirror, or other preconditioned method may transform it. Thus

    \[ \widehat d=(\widehat G+\lambda I)^{-1}\widehat g \]

    contains at least two approximation problems: whether \(\widehat g\) estimates the intended gradient and whether \(\widehat G\) represents the intended metric.

    Training gradients versus sampling gradients¤

    An EBM makes the distinction concrete. Its parameter likelihood gradient is

    \[ \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x), \]

    whereas Langevin sampling uses the state score

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_\theta(x)=-\nabla_xE_\theta(x). \]

    The first changes the model; the second moves a sample under a fixed model. Differentiating through several sampler steps introduces a third object: a total derivative of a downstream loss through the numerical chain. Its validity depends on truncation, random-number treatment, and the role of the sampler.

    Worked scalar separation¤

    Let \(E_\theta(x)=\tfrac12\theta x^2\) with \(\theta>0\). The normalized model is \(\mathcal N(0,1/\theta)\). For one observed \(x\), the parameter score is

    \[ \partial_\theta\log p_\theta(x) =\frac{1}{2\theta}-\frac{x^2}{2}. \]

    The state score used by Langevin dynamics is

    \[ \partial_x\log p_\theta(x)=-\theta x. \]

    At \(\theta=2\) and \(x=1\), these are \(-1/4\) and \(-2\). They differ in value, units, dimension, and purpose. Calling both “the model gradient” loses the information required to implement either correctly.

    The noun after gradient matters

    Parameter, state, functional, game, and hypergradients live in different spaces. Before comparing two methods, verify that their vectors can even be added without a pullback, projection, or other declared conversion.

    Independent practice and assessment map: typed gradient objects¤

    Attempt each task before opening its hints. The typical time is a planning aid, not a speed target. A correct answer must type the mathematical object before discussing implementation quality.

    1. P1.1 — Predict before computing: two meanings of score. Type: predict · Difficulty: foundation · Typical time: 7 min. For \(E_\theta(x)=\tfrac12\theta x^2\), predict whether the parameter score and state score at \(\theta=3,x=2\) have the same sign, units, and purpose. Then compute both for the normalized model.
    2. P1.2 — Complete two five-field contracts. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 11 min. Fill the objective, variable, averaging law, geometry, and estimator or solver for (a) a pathwise VAE encoder update and (b) Langevin sampling from a fixed EBM. State when “objective” is a state-space sampling target rather than a training loss.
    3. P1.3 — Separate estimator from geometry. Type: compare · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 9 min. A score-function estimator and a reparameterized estimator both target \(\nabla_\phi J\). A colleague calls the first “Euclidean” and the second “natural.” Diagnose the claim and write the additional operation that would create a natural direction.
    4. P1.4 — Audit a generator pullback. Type: derive · Difficulty: core · Typical time: 8 min. Let \(g_\theta:\mathbb R^m\to\mathbb R^n\) have \(p\) parameters and let \(v(x)\in\mathbb R^n\) be a teacher state field. Give the shapes of \(J_\theta g_\theta(z)\), \(v\), and the parameter pullback. Explain why \(v\) cannot be added directly to a parameter gradient when \(n=p\) happens numerically.
    5. P1.5 — Diagnose a silent target change. Type: diagnose · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 12 min. A discrete latent model samples a hard category in the forward pass and backpropagates through a softmax probability in the backward pass. Identify the exact derivative that has not yet been justified and list two experiments that would reveal the surrogate's effect.
    6. P1.6 — Transfer to guided diffusion. Type: design · Difficulty: stretch · Typical time: 16 min. Type a classifier-guided diffusion sampling update. Name the state variable, unconditional and classifier fields, law being approximated, geometry, time weighting or guidance scale, and finite solver. Then name the distinct parameter gradient used to train the classifier.

    Complete solutions to P1
    1. P1.1. The normalized model is \(\mathcal N(0,1/\theta)\). Its parameter score is \(1/(2\theta)-x^2/2=1/6-2=-11/6\); its state score is \(-\theta x=-6\). Both are negative here, but agreement of sign is accidental. The first has parameter-covector units and changes model precision during training; the second has inverse-state units and moves a sample under fixed \(\theta\). Check. Changing \(x\) can reverse or change their relative magnitudes without making the objects equal.
    2. P1.2. For a pathwise VAE encoder update: objective \(-\mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x)\); variable \(\phi\); law \(x\sim p_D\), \(\varepsilon\sim p(\varepsilon)\) with \(z=T_\phi(x,\varepsilon)\); Euclidean parameter geometry unless another metric is declared; reparameterized Monte Carlo plus reverse AD. For Langevin: target \(p_\theta(x)\propto e^{-E_\theta(x)}\) with fixed \(\theta\); variable \(x\); law is the current sampler state plus Gaussian increments; Euclidean state geometry; Euler--Maruyama or another declared integrator using \(-\nabla_xE_\theta\). The “objective” field describes the sampling target or free energy, not an encoder loss. Check. Only the first update changes learned parameters.
    3. P1.3. Under their conditions, score-function and pathwise formulas are alternative estimators of the same Euclidean coordinate derivative. Neither is natural merely because of how randomness is differentiated. A natural direction requires \(d=G^\dagger\nabla_\phi J\) or an approximate solve \((\widehat G+\lambda I)d=\widehat g\) for a declared metric. Check. Estimator variance and metric approximation are separate error axes.
    4. P1.4. With output-by-parameter convention, \(J_\theta g\in\mathbb R^{n\times p}\), \(v\in\mathbb R^n\), and \(J_\theta g^\top v\in\mathbb R^p\). Equal integer dimensions do not identify vector spaces: changing sample units and changing parameters have different meanings and coordinate transformations. Check. The transpose is the pullback from a state covector to parameter coordinates.
    5. P1.5. The backward softmax rule has not established the derivative of the expected hard-category objective; it defines a straight-through surrogate and may not be integrable. Enumerate a tiny support to compare its expectation with the exact gradient or a score estimator, and compare bias, variance, optimization, and final discrete metrics against a declared continuous relaxation across temperatures. Check. Good downstream performance does not retroactively make the surrogate unbiased.
    6. P1.6.     A tangent vector changes a law¤

    Consider a differentiable family

    \[ \mathcal M=\{p_\theta:\theta\in\Theta\subseteq\mathbb R^d\}. \]

    A parameter-space direction \(v\) induces the tangent score

    \[ u_v(x)=v^\top\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x). \]

    For a regular family with parameter-independent support, \(\mathbb E_{p_\theta}[u_v(X)]=0\). The Fisher--Rao inner product between two tangent directions is

    \[ g_\theta(v,w) =\mathbb E_{p_\theta}[u_v(X)u_w(X)] =v^\top F(\theta)w. \]

    This construction measures first-order changes in the represented probability law. It is not a metric on the semantic quality of samples, the Euclidean distance between neural weights, or the geometry of individual latent codes.

    From a local discrepancy to a natural direction¤

    Under the regularity conditions stated in the optimization foundation,

    \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\theta\|p_{\theta+\Delta}) =\frac12\Delta^\top F(\theta)\Delta +o(\lVert\Delta\rVert^2). \]

    For a scalar objective \(J(\theta)\) with Euclidean coordinate gradient \(g=\nabla_\theta J\), the infinitesimal steepest descent vector under this metric solves

    \[ F(\theta)v_{\mathrm{nat}}=g, \qquad \dot\theta=-v_{\mathrm{nat}}. \]

    If \(F\) is singular, the equation is interpreted on the identifiable tangent space. The Moore--Penrose solution \(F^\dagger g\) is meaningful only when the objective gradient is compatible with that quotient geometry. A numerical inverse cannot manufacture statistical identifiability.

    What invariance does and does not say¤

    Let \(\theta=h(\psi)\) be a smooth locally invertible coordinate change with Jacobian \(J_h\). Then

    \[ F_\psi=J_h^\top F_\theta J_h, \qquad \nabla_\psi J=J_h^\top\nabla_\theta J. \]

    The exact infinitesimal natural vector transforms as a tangent vector, so both coordinates describe the same first-order motion of the law. This statement does not make the following objects invariant automatically:

    • a finite Euler step;
    • damping by \(\lambda I\) in whichever coordinates happen to be used;
    • diagonal, block-diagonal, Kronecker, or low-rank approximations;
    • momentum or adaptive state stored in parameter coordinates;
    • clipping and per-parameter learning-rate schedules; or
    • a non-invertible map between overparameterized descriptions.

    Martens gives a careful account of these boundaries. The original efficiency argument is due to Amari.

    Worked categorical law: logits versus probabilities¤

    Let a three-category model have probabilities

    \[ p=(0.8,0.1,0.1) \]

    and let the target frequencies be \(r=(0.6,0.2,0.2)\). For softmax logits \(\theta\), the cross-entropy gradient is

    \[ g_\theta=p-r=(0.2,-0.1,-0.1), \]

    and the Fisher matrix is

    \[ F_\theta=\operatorname{diag}(p)-pp^\top. \]

    It is singular because adding one constant to every logit does not change \(p\). Any natural logit direction satisfying \(F_\theta v=g_\theta\) is defined only up to that null direction. Nevertheless, the induced probability motion is unique:

    \[ \dot p=-F_\theta v=-(p-r). \]

    With a small time step \(0.1\), the first-order update is \(p^+=(0.78,0.11,0.11)\). The calculation shows what invariance preserves: the motion of the categorical law, not a unique motion of redundant logits.

    Metric, divergence, and optimizer are three objects

    KL supplies the local Fisher metric under regularity. A finite natural-gradient optimizer additionally chooses a solve, damping, step rule, stochastic estimator, and state update. Two implementations that cite the same metric can therefore follow measurably different trajectories.

    Dual coordinates¤

    For a minimal regular exponential family

    \[ q_\eta(z)=h(z)\exp\{\eta^\top T(z)-A(\eta)\}, \]

    the mean parameter and Fisher information are

    \[ \mu=\mathbb E_{q_\eta}[T(Z)]=\nabla_\eta A(\eta), \qquad F_\eta=\nabla_\eta^2A(\eta) =\operatorname{Cov}_{q_\eta}[T(Z)]. \]

    If a differentiable objective is written as \(L(\eta)=\widetilde L(\mu(\eta))\), then the chain rule gives

    \[ \nabla_\eta L=F_\eta\nabla_\mu\widetilde L, \qquad \widetilde\nabla_\eta L =F_\eta^{-1}\nabla_\eta L =\nabla_\mu\widetilde L. \]

    Natural and mean coordinates are dual; they are not numerically identical arrays with interchangeable semantics. This duality connects natural-gradient steps, Bregman proximal updates generated by \(A\), and mirror descent. The equivalence must name its coordinate, objective, and finite-step convention.

    Maximum likelihood as moment motion¤

    For fully observed data with empirical statistic \(\bar T\), the average negative log likelihood is

    \[ J(\eta)=A(\eta)-\eta^\top\bar T+\text{constant}, \]

    so \(\nabla_\eta J=\mu-\bar T\). Infinitesimally, a natural-gradient flow produces

    \[ \dot\mu=-F_\eta F_\eta^{-1}(\mu-\bar T) =\bar T-\mu. \]

    The mean parameter moves directly toward the empirical moment. A finite Euler step in \(\eta\) is not necessarily the same as an exact convex interpolation in \(\mu\), because the coordinate map is nonlinear.

    Conjugate SVI: when the update becomes simple¤

    In the conjugate models treated by Hoffman et al., let \(\lambda\) be a global variational natural parameter and let \(\widehat\lambda\) be the complete-data conjugate update formed from prior hyperparameters plus expected sufficient statistics. Under the paper's factorization and unbiased minibatch construction, the natural gradient of the ELBO with respect to \(\lambda\) has the form

    \[ \widetilde\nabla_\lambda\mathcal L =\widehat\lambda-\lambda. \]

    The stochastic update is therefore

    \[ \lambda_{t+1} =(1-\rho_t)\lambda_t+\rho_t\widehat\lambda_t. \]

    This is not a universal identity for arbitrary amortized neural posteriors. It depends on conjugate exponential-family structure and the chosen global variational coordinate.

    Worked Beta--Bernoulli minibatch update¤

    Suppose a Bernoulli probability has prior \(\operatorname{Beta}(2,2)\). There are \(N=100\) observations. A uniformly sampled minibatch of size \(10\) contains seven successes and three failures. Scaling the minibatch counts by \(N/10\) gives the stochastic full-data target. Here we use the concentration pair \(\lambda=(\alpha,\beta)\), which is an affine shift of the canonical natural coordinate \((\alpha-1,\beta-1)\) and therefore obeys the same convex update:

    \[ \widehat\lambda=(2+70,\ 2+30)=(72,32). \]

    Starting from \(\lambda_t=(12,8)\) and using \(\rho_t=0.1\) gives

    \[ \lambda_{t+1} =0.9(12,8)+0.1(72,32) =(18,10.4). \]

    The minibatch target is noisy but unbiased under uniform sampling. Reusing a nonuniform data stream without importance correction changes that claim.

    VAEs and amortization change the geometric object¤

    A VAE contains at least two parameterized distribution families:

    1. the generative joint \(p_\theta(x,z)\); and
    2. the variational conditional \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\).

    A natural gradient for \(q_\phi\) can use the conditional Fisher

    \[ F_\phi^{(q)} =\mathbb E_{X\sim p_D} \mathbb E_{Z\sim q_\phi(\cdot\mid X)} \left[s_\phi(Z\mid X)s_\phi(Z\mid X)^\top\right]. \]

    That is not the Fisher of the decoder distribution. Moreover, shared amortized parameters couple the posterior updates for every record, so one neural step cannot generally reproduce independent coordinate-optimal updates for all \(q(z\mid x)\).

    Ay, van Oostrum, and Datar analyze the Fisher--Rao gradient of the ELBO and identify conditions under which ELBO and target-KL geometry align on a constrained model. The result is a geometric characterization, not permission to treat every finite VAE optimizer as exact posterior natural-gradient descent. Natural-gradient VI also extends beyond minimal single-family approximations, but mixture and structured cases need their own representations and algorithms, as shown by Lin, Khan, and Schmidt.

    One derivative, several estimator families¤

    For

    \[ J(\phi)=\mathbb E_{Z\sim q_\phi}[f_\phi(Z)], \]

    the likelihood-ratio identity is

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E_{q_\phi} \left[ f_\phi(Z)\nabla_\phi\log q_\phi(Z) +\nabla_\phi f_\phi(Z) \right]. \]

    If \(Z=T_\phi(\varepsilon)\) for parameter-free noise, the pathwise identity is

    \[ \nabla_\phi J =\mathbb E \left[ \nabla_z f_\phi(Z)\,\nabla_\phi T_\phi(\varepsilon) +\nabla_\phi f_\phi(Z) \right]. \]

    Under their regularity conditions, these are two representations of the same population derivative. Their Monte Carlo variance can differ greatly. Exact marginalization, Rao--Blackwellization, antithetic coupling, common random numbers, and control variates reduce randomness without defining a new objective. A continuous relaxation, straight-through rule, or truncated stochastic graph may instead change the differentiated target.

    The third classical family is a weak or measure-valued derivative. It represents a derivative of the sampling law as a signed measure, often as a scaled difference between two probability laws. For the smallest exact case, \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(q)\) and a function \(f\) that does not otherwise depend on \(q\),

    \[ \frac{d}{dq}\mathbb E_q[f(Z)] =f(1)-f(0). \]

    If \(q=q_\phi\), the parameter derivative is \((f(1)-f(0))\,dq_\phi/d\phi\). This is neither a score sample nor a continuous relaxation: it is an exact signed-measure difference. In larger systems a weak derivative may require paired perturbation laws, coupling, and two or more function evaluations. Those costs and the validity of the signed-measure decomposition are part of the estimator contract. The Monte Carlo gradient survey develops score-function, pathwise, and measure-valued estimators in one common framework.

    Worked Bernoulli estimator: exact, score-function, and baseline¤

    Let \(Z\sim\operatorname{Bernoulli}(q)\) with \(q=\sigma(\phi)\), and let \(f(0)=0.2\), \(f(1)=1.4\). Exact summation gives

    \[ \begin{aligned} J(\phi)&=0.2+1.2q,\\ \frac{dJ}{d\phi}&=1.2q(1-q). \end{aligned} \]

    At \(\phi=0\), \(q=1/2\) and the exact derivative is \(0.3\). Since \(\partial_\phi\log q_\phi(Z)=Z-q\), a one-sample score estimator is

    \[ \widehat g=f(Z)(Z-q). \]

    It equals \(0.7\) when \(Z=1\) and \(-0.1\) when \(Z=0\), so its expectation is \(0.3\) but it has sampling variance. Subtracting a baseline that does not depend on the sampled value preserves the expectation:

    \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat g_b&=(f(Z)-b)(Z-q),\\ \mathbb E[b(Z-q)]&=0. \end{aligned} \]

    Here \(b=0.8\) makes both outcomes equal \(0.3\), eliminating variance in this tiny problem. In a stochastic computation graph, a learned baseline may depend on parents of \(Z\) but must not receive an unintended derivative through the score-surrogate term. Detachment is part of the estimator definition.

    Exact enumeration is preferable when the support is small. For a large structured latent space, sum out any tractable subset first and sample only the remainder. This is Rao--Blackwellization: it is a mathematical variance reduction, not merely a larger batch.

    Multi-sample bounds couple objective and estimator¤

    For importance weights

    \[ w_k=\frac{p_\theta(x,z_k)}{q_\phi(z_k\mid x)}, \qquad z_k\sim q_\phi(\cdot\mid x), \]

    the importance-weighted bound is

    \[ \mathcal L_K =\mathbb E \left[ \log\left(\frac1K\sum_{k=1}^K w_k\right) \right]. \]

    Increasing \(K\) changes the objective and the dependence among normalized weights. A tighter bound need not produce a higher-signal inference-network gradient: the signal-to-noise ratio of the usual reparameterized estimator can deteriorate even while the generative-parameter estimate improves (Rainforth et al.).

    Doubly reparameterized gradients analytically transform remaining score terms and yield a lower-variance unbiased inference-network estimator for the supported continuous multi-sample objectives (Tucker et al.). VIMCO uses sample-specific leave-one-out control variates when latents are discrete. Reweighted wake--sleep uses a different wake or sleep update. These methods may share samples and weights while optimizing different population quantities.

    Tighter bound, better estimator, and better model are separate claims

    Bound tightness concerns the scalar population objective. Bias and variance concern a derivative estimator. Generative quality concerns the learned solution and finite optimizer. None follows from either of the others without additional analysis.

    Hierarchies and stochastic computation graphs¤

    For a hierarchy

    \[ q_\phi(z_{1:L}\mid x) =\prod_{\ell=1}^L q_\phi(z_\ell\mid z_{<\ell},x), \]

    each stochastic node has its own score term and downstream cost. A baseline for one node may condition on variables that precede it but not on an uncorrected realization of that node. Local expectation gradients, critics, and conditional Rao--Blackwellization assign credit to smaller subgraphs. Generalized DReG derivations show that apparently pathwise hierarchical objectives can retain score terms that require separate treatment (Bauer and Mnih).

    Repeated automatic differentiation of an ordinary first-order score surrogate can omit higher-order terms because detachment was chosen to reproduce only the first derivative. DiCE constructs a stochastic objective whose repeated differentiation produces the intended higher-order score estimators (Foerster et al.). This is relevant to meta-inference, learned inner optimizers, and games in which one player differentiates through another player's stochastic update.

    Never materialize a giant Fisher without a reason¤

    For score \(s_\theta(X)=\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(X)\),

    \[ Fv =\mathbb E_{X\sim p_\theta} \left[s_\theta(X)\bigl(s_\theta(X)^\top v\bigr)\right]. \]

    This identity permits stochastic Fisher-vector products. In neural conditional models, generalized Gauss--Newton products may be obtained by JVP/VJP compositions without explicitly forming scores. The two products coincide only under the likelihood and loss conditions established for that model.

    An empirical average at observed outcomes is often easier to compute, but it is the empirical Fisher, not automatically an unbiased model-Fisher estimator. Kunstner, Balles, and Hennig demonstrate why that substitution can fail as curvature.

    Validate the derivative program before the metric¤

    A curvature routine composes derivative programs, so a low linear-solver residual cannot expose a wrong JVP, VJP, sign, detach, or batching convention. For a differentiable map \(y=f(\theta)\) with Jacobian \(J\), test the adjoint identity on random directions \(v\) and cotangents \(u\):

    \[ \langle u,Jv\rangle =\langle J^\top u,v\rangle. \]

    Then compare the implemented JVP against a central directional difference,

    \[ Jv \approx \frac{f(\theta+h v)-f(\theta-h v)}{2h}, \]

    over a range of \(h\). Equivalently, a first-order Taylor remainder should initially decrease quadratically as \(h\) shrinks, before floating-point error dominates. Run these checks on the exact precision, parameter pytree, masks, random seeds, and batch reduction used by the Fisher or GGN routine. The automatic-differentiation survey provides the computational-graph foundations; the JAX autodiff cookbook demonstrates JVP/VJP constructions and numerical checking.

    These tests validate the derivative of the implemented program. They do not prove that the program represents the intended population objective, metric, or continuous-time equation. That separate semantic check is why both program and law-level diagnostics are required.

    A matrix-free trust-region step¤

    A defensible finite procedure makes each approximation visible:

    1. estimate a gradient \(g\) for the declared scalar objective;
    2. define a routine for \(v\mapsto \widehat Fv\) using samples from the declared law;
    3. approximately solve \((\widehat F+\lambda I)d=g\), for example by conjugate gradients;
    4. measure the undamped predicted law movement \(\widehat\delta=\tfrac12d^\top\widehat Fd\);
    5. choose \(\alpha\leq1\) so \(\alpha^2\widehat\delta\leq\epsilon\);
    6. propose \(\theta^+=\theta-\alpha d\); and
    7. verify the actual objective, divergence proxy, and held-out generative behavior before accepting a large step.
    g = stochastic_objective_gradient(theta)
Fv = model_fisher_vector_product(theta, declared_model_samples)
d = conjugate_gradient(v -> Fv(v) + damping * v, g)
predicted_kl = 0.5 * dot(d, Fv(d))
scale = min(1, sqrt(radius / max(predicted_kl, tiny)))
candidate = theta - scale * d

    The damping term stabilizes the solve but is a coordinate-dependent algorithmic choice. The trust-region estimate is local. Both facts belong in experimental reports.

    Worked two-parameter solve¤

    Let

    \[ F=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad g=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}. \]

    The exact natural direction is

    \[ \begin{aligned} d&=F^{-1}g\\ &=\frac13 \begin{bmatrix}1&-1\\-1&4\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}\\ &=\begin{bmatrix}-1/3\\7/3\end{bmatrix}. \end{aligned} \]

    Indeed \(Fd=g\), and \(d^\top Fd=g^\top d=13/3\). The Euclidean descent direction \(-g=(-1,-2)\) and natural descent direction \(-d=(1/3,-7/3)\) even point to different parameter-space quadrants. That is not paradoxical: the metric says that equal coordinate lengths do not represent equal changes of law.

    Structured approximations¤

    K-FAC. For a neural layer, K-FAC approximates a Fisher block by a Kronecker product of an activation covariance and a back-propagated output-gradient covariance. The two smaller factors can be regularized and inverted efficiently. The construction is neither the exact dense Fisher nor merely a diagonal adaptive optimizer. Its factorization, damping, stale statistics, parameter grouping, and distributed update schedule all affect the resulting direction (Martens and Grosse).

    FANG and energy models. Grosse and Salakhutdinov's factorized natural-gradient method approximates inverse-Fisher structure for restricted Boltzmann machines. The paper is especially instructive because the Fisher is a covariance of model sufficient statistics, while estimating those statistics still depends on the quality of persistent model samples. A sophisticated solve does not repair a biased negative phase.

    Diagonal and low-rank methods. A diagonal score-second-moment estimate can be useful, but it discards correlations and may be an empirical rather than model Fisher. Low-rank approximations cannot represent curvature outside their subspace without an additional diagonal or damping term. Names such as “natural,” “second order,” and “adaptive” do not settle these distinctions.

    Numerical and distributed diagnostics¤

    Record at least:

    • the law and sampling distribution used for \(\widehat F\);
    • model Fisher, empirical Fisher, or generalized Gauss--Newton semantics;
    • damping, factorization, rank, update frequency, and excluded parameters;
    • conjugate-gradient residual and iteration count;
    • \(g^\top d\), \(d^\top\widehat Fd\), and the predicted versus measured KL proxy;
    • curvature-estimation batch size separately from gradient batch size;
    • factor eigenvalue ranges and the fraction dominated by damping;
    • precision used for factors, solves, and parameter updates; and
    • wall-clock, memory, communication, and generative evaluation at matched compute.

    Autoregressive models: a conditional Fisher sum¤

    For

    \[ p_\theta(x_{1:T})=\prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t}), \]

    write the joint score as \(S=\sum_t s_t\), where \(s_t=\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x_t\mid x_{<t})\). Under regular conditional support,

    \[ \mathbb E[s_t\mid X_{<t}]=0. \]

    For \(u<t\), \(s_u\) is measurable with respect to the later history, so \(\mathbb E[s_us_t^\top]=0\). Therefore

    \[ F_{\mathrm{AR}}(\theta) =\sum_{t=1}^T \mathbb E_{X_{<t}\sim p_\theta} \left[F_t(\theta;X_{<t})\right]. \]

    The factorization removes cross-time score covariances in the exact expectation; it does not make the shared-parameter Fisher block diagonal. Every token can still update the same embedding and Transformer weights.

    For teacher-forced training, contexts are commonly drawn from the data rather than the model. The resulting conditional curvature can be useful, but its context distribution differs from the exact joint model Fisher. Report which one is estimated.

    Normalizing flows: exact density does not imply a cheap Fisher¤

    An invertible flow makes \(\log p_\theta(x)\) tractable through a base log density and a log-Jacobian term. This permits exact pointwise scores with respect to parameters, but the model Fisher still averages their outer products under \(X\sim p_\theta\). Sampling is direct, so Monte Carlo estimation is conceptually clean; the matrix remains enormous and can be ill-conditioned near redundant transformations.

    Reparameterizing the weights of the same flow and changing the sample coordinates through the flow are different operations. Fisher invariance addresses the first. The change-of-variables theorem addresses the second.

    VAEs: encoder and decoder geometries must be separated¤

    The VAE ELBO

    \[ \mathcal L(\theta,\phi) =\mathbb E_{p_D(x)q_\phi(z\mid x)} [\log p_\theta(x,z)-\log q_\phi(z\mid x)] \]

    couples a generative-model parameter \(\theta\) and an inference parameter \(\phi\). Possible metrics include:

    • the joint-model Fisher of \(p_\theta(x,z)\);
    • the observed-model Fisher of \(p_\theta(x)\), which usually requires posterior expectations;
    • the conditional variational Fisher of \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) averaged over data;
    • a generalized Gauss--Newton approximation for a declared decoder likelihood; and
    • block or Kronecker approximations in the shared networks.

    These metrics answer different questions. In particular, preconditioning the encoder ELBO gradient by \(F_\phi^{(q)}\) is a natural step for the amortized posterior family, not for the marginal data model.

    EBMs: Fisher estimation inherits the negative-phase problem¤

    For an exponential-family EBM

    \[ p_\theta(x)=\exp\{\theta^\top T(x)-A(\theta)\}, \]

    the likelihood gradient and Fisher are

    \[ \nabla_\theta\ell =\mathbb E_{p_D}[T(X)]-\mathbb E_{p_\theta}[T(X)], \qquad F(\theta)=\operatorname{Cov}_{p_\theta}[T(X)]. \]

    Both require model moments. If an MCMC chain has not mixed, it can bias both the gradient and the curvature estimate, and solving the biased system more accurately does not restore maximum-likelihood learning. For nonlinear energies, the parameter score is the centered energy derivative,

    \[ \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x) =-\nabla_\theta E_\theta(x) +\mathbb E_{p_\theta}[\nabla_\theta E_\theta(X)]. \]

    Probabilistic circuits or other tractable structured models may compute some required expectations exactly. Their natural-gradient advantage is therefore a query property of the circuit, not a consequence of neural depth.

    Worked conditional-score cancellation¤

    For a two-step AR model, \(S=s_1+s_2\). The cross term is

    \[ \mathbb E[s_1s_2^\top] =\mathbb E\!\left[ s_1\,\mathbb E[s_2^\top\mid X_1] \right] =0. \]

    Thus \(F=\mathbb E[s_1s_1^\top]+\mathbb E[s_2s_2^\top]\). If both scores depend on the same scalar network weight, however, these are two contributions to one scalar Fisher—not two independent optimizer blocks. Orthogonality of random score increments is not architectural parameter separation.

    A deterministic generator can be statistically singular¤

    Let \(Z\) have a continuous density and let a generator map it into a higher dimensional ambient space. Its pushforward may live on a lower-dimensional manifold and have no ambient Lebesgue density. Classical Fisher formulas based on \(\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(x)\) then need not exist.

    Worked rotating-line generator¤

    Let \(Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\) and

    \[ G_\theta(Z)=(Z,\theta Z)\in\mathbb R^2. \]

    For each \(\theta\), the generated law lies on a line. Distinct nonparallel lines intersect only at the origin, which has probability zero, so their measures are mutually singular. The KL divergence between the endpoint laws is infinite, not locally quadratic in \(\theta\).

    Adding observation noise,

    \[ X=G_\theta(Z)+\varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2I), \]

    produces a full-rank Gaussian law and a well-defined Fisher. But the geometry now depends on \(\sigma\) and belongs to a different, noise-smoothed generative model. “Add noise and compute Fisher” is a modeling choice, not a harmless numerical trick.

    GANs contain a game, not one canonical Fisher¤

    A discriminator can define a normalized Bernoulli conditional \(p_\phi(y\mid x)\) and therefore a conditional Fisher for \(\phi\). That matrix is not the Fisher of the generator pushforward. A generator-side pullback can instead be built from a declared discrepancy on generated measures, but the choice—KL after smoothing, Wasserstein, Sinkhorn, an MMD, or a learned feature metric—changes the geometry.

    Even if both players use positive-definite preconditioners, the coupled update remains a game vector field. Natural preconditioning does not by itself remove the rotational, non-normal, or support-mismatch behavior derived in the GAN optimization chapter.

    Sinkhorn Natural Gradient pulls back a Sinkhorn-divergence geometry through an implicit generator and studies an empirical information matrix. It is a peer-reviewed construction for that declared transport discrepancy. It is not the classical Fisher--Rao natural gradient under another implementation name.

    Game, unrolled, and implicit gradients answer different questions¤

    For a two-player loss \(V(\theta,\phi)\), simultaneous descent--ascent uses the field

    \[ G(\theta,\phi) =\begin{bmatrix} \nabla_\theta V(\theta,\phi)\\ -\nabla_\phi V(\theta,\phi) \end{bmatrix}. \]

    Alternating updates evaluate its blocks at different states. Extragradient evaluates a look-ahead field. Optimistic and negative-momentum variants combine current and previous fields. These are finite game algorithms, not estimators of one scalar gradient that merely differ in variance. Even simple nonconvex-- nonconcave games can converge, cycle, or diverge under different schemes (Grimmer et al.).

    A bilevel formulation instead defines an inner solution

    \[ \phi^*(\theta)\in\arg\min_\phi L_{\mathrm{in}}(\theta,\phi) \]

    and differentiates an outer objective \(J(\theta,\phi^*(\theta))\). If a smooth isolated inner stationary point has nonsingular \(H_{\phi\phi}=\nabla^2_{\phi\phi}L_{\mathrm{in}}\), implicit differentiation gives

    \[ \frac{d\phi^*}{d\theta} =-H_{\phi\phi}^{-1}H_{\phi\theta}, \]

    and the total outer derivative includes the response term

    \[ \frac{dJ}{d\theta} =\partial_\theta J -H_{\phi\theta}^\top H_{\phi\phi}^{-\top} \partial_\phi J. \]

    Differentiating through \(K\) explicit inner updates produces the gradient of the \(K\)-step unrolled system, not automatically the exact best-response objective. Stopping the gradient through the inner state drops the response term entirely. Unrolled GANs demonstrate a specific finite unrolling construction; they do not prove that more unrolling monotonically improves every adversarial game.

    Diffusion and flow models: which time-indexed law?¤

    A diffusion or flow system exposes several possible geometric objects:

    • the endpoint generated law \(p_\theta(x_0)\);
    • a path law over trajectories;
    • corrupted marginals \(p_{\theta,t}(x_t)\);
    • reverse transition conditionals;
    • denoising, velocity, or noise-prediction surrogate conditionals; and
    • a distribution over time and conditioning variables used by the training objective.

    The generalized Gauss--Newton matrix of a weighted denoising regression loss is well defined. Calling it the Fisher of the final generated distribution requires an additional equivalence proof. Changing timestep weights changes that training geometry even if the population-optimal predictor remains convertible.

    Likewise, a flow-matching regression metric measures errors in a vector field under a selected coupling and time distribution. It is not automatically the Fisher--Rao metric of the terminal density or the \(W_2\) metric of the probability path.

    Projection onto a tractable statistical manifold¤

    A different strategy is to project an evolving generative law onto a tractable statistical family and move that projection geometrically. Liu, Wang, and Wang develop such a construction for time-varying generators using an exponential- family manifold and particle approximations (peer-reviewed study). This is peer-reviewed research with declared projections and estimators. The projection retains selected statistics, not necessarily the complete law, so its natural-gradient motion is a population-optimum correspondence rather than an algebraic identity with arbitrary diffusion or flow training.

    Solver output is a parameterized computation¤

    Let an ODE state satisfy

    \[ \begin{aligned} \dot x(t)&=f_\theta(t,x(t)),\\ L(\theta)&=\ell(x(T)). \end{aligned} \]

    The continuous adjoint \(a(t)=\nabla_{x(t)}L\) satisfies

    \[ \begin{aligned} -\dot a(t) &=\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial x} \right)^{\!\top}a(t),\\ a(T)&=\nabla_x\ell(x(T)), \end{aligned} \]

    and, omitting explicit parameter dependence of the endpoints,

    \[ \nabla_\theta L =\int_0^T a(t)^\top \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial\theta}(t,x(t))\,dt. \]

    There are two finite derivative programs:

    • discretize then differentiate: backpropagate through the actual numerical solver operations; and
    • differentiate then discretize: derive continuous sensitivity equations and solve them numerically.

    They approach the same ideal only under convergence and consistency conditions. With finite tolerances, adaptive step decisions, interpolation, event handling, or an inaccurate reconstructed trajectory, their gradients can disagree. CNFs must additionally differentiate the numerical log-density computation, including any stochastic trace estimator. “Adjoint” names a sensitivity construction, not a promise of exact constant-memory gradients for every solver.

    SDE pathwise sensitivities require a noise contract¤

    For

    \[ dX_t=b_\theta(t,X_t)\,dt +\sigma_\theta(t,X_t)\,dW_t, \]

    a pathwise derivative holds a realization of the driving noise fixed while changing \(\theta\). A stochastic adjoint can propagate this sensitivity backward under regularity conditions (Li et al.). In a finite implementation, Brownian increments, adaptive interval queries, random seeds, and any Lévy-area approximation are part of the differentiated program. Reusing an ordinary pseudorandom seed does not by itself guarantee the same Brownian path when the adaptive mesh changes.

    Latent SDE likelihood or ELBO training may combine a pathwise SDE derivative, a Girsanov or KL term, Monte Carlo path sampling, and an adjoint solver. Bias from time discretization and variance from path sampling are different error axes.

    MCMC, SMC, and resampling create target choices¤

    Suppose an approximate posterior sample is produced by \(K\) transitions,

    \[ z_K=T_{\phi,K}(z_0,\xi_{1:K}). \]

    Backpropagating through all transitions differentiates the finite-\(K\) pushforward law. Stopping at \(z_K\), truncating after recent transitions, or replacing the chain by an equilibrium identity produces a different estimator. Even as \(K\to\infty\), interchange of differentiation and the stationary limit requires ergodicity and sensitivity conditions.

    Particle filters add a discrete resampling operation. Common choices include:

    • score-function terms for ancestor indices;
    • stopping gradients through resampling;
    • continuous or straight-through relaxations;
    • entropy-regularized transport resampling; and
    • likelihood-score identities that avoid differentiating the ancestor draw.

    These choices trade estimator variance, bias, and even the finite inference algorithm. Entropy-regularized transport yields a differentiable particle filter with its own convergence statement (Corenflos et al.); it does not make categorical multinomial resampling differentiable retroactively.

    Reward gradients for autoregressive and iterative generators¤

    For a trajectory law

    \[ p_\theta(x_{0:T}\mid c) =p(x_T)\prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,c) \]

    and a possibly non-differentiable terminal reward \(R(x_0,c)\),

    \[ \nabla_\theta\mathbb E[R] =\mathbb E \left[ \sum_{t=1}^T \bigl(R-b_t(x_t,c)\bigr) \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t,c) \right] \]

    for valid state-dependent baselines \(b_t\) that do not depend on the sampled next state \(x_{t-1}\). An autoregressive token model has the same trajectory identity in the forward order. A discrete diffusion has reverse transitions; a continuous diffusion may admit pathwise as well as likelihood-ratio estimators. The estimator must match the sampling law actually used, including temperature, truncation, conditioning, and any off-policy correction.

    Worked two-token reward¤

    Let \(X_1,X_2\) be independent Bernoulli variables with \(q=\sigma(\phi)\) and let \(R=X_1X_2\). Then

    \[ J(\phi)=q^2, \qquad \frac{dJ}{d\phi}=2q^2(1-q). \]

    At \(q=1/2\), the exact derivative is \(1/4\). The trajectory estimator without a baseline is

    \[ \widehat g =R\bigl[(X_1-q)+(X_2-q)\bigr]. \]

    Only \((X_1,X_2)=(1,1)\) contributes; it occurs with probability \(1/4\) and then \(\widehat g=1\). Its expectation is therefore \(1/4\), but its variance is large. Per-prefix critics can reduce variance by assigning expected downstream reward to each token decision. They do not turn an on-policy sample into an off-policy one or repair reward misspecification.

    Policy-gradient fine-tuning has been instantiated for diffusion and discrete iterative generators, including text-to-image diffusion (DPOK), graph diffusion (GDPO), and discrete diffusion (Zekri and Boulle). These are family-specific constructions. A direct preference loss evaluated on fixed pairs uses an ordinary supervised parameter gradient unless its derivation and sampling loop explicitly introduce a policy-gradient estimator.

    Score distillation transfers a state field through a generator¤

    Let a differentiable generator or renderer produce \(x=g_\theta(z)\), and let a teacher diffusion model provide a noisy conditional state score. A broad class of distillation updates has the schematic form

    \[ \begin{aligned} u_\theta =\mathbb E_{z,t,\varepsilon}\bigl[ &J_\theta g_\theta(z)^\top w(t)\\ &\cdot\bigl(s_{q,t}(x_t)-s_{p,t}(x_t\mid c)\bigr) \bigr], \end{aligned} \]

    where the method determines the corruption convention, weight, teacher score, and whether and how the current generator score \(s_{q,t}\) is estimated. Omitting or approximating the source score, scaling guidance, and stopping derivatives through the teacher all change the update.

    In automatic-differentiation code, any supplied vector field \(v(x)\) can be installed through a pseudo-loss

    \[ L_{\mathrm{pseudo}}(x) =\operatorname{stopgrad}(v(x))^\top x, \]

    which yields \(\nabla_\theta L_{\mathrm{pseudo}}=J_\theta x^\top v(x)\). The reported scalar value of this pseudo-loss is not evidence that \(v\) is the gradient of a globally defined divergence. Integrability, omitted score terms, and the distribution over noise levels must be established separately.

    Score Distillation Sampling introduced this kind of teacher-score transfer for differentiable 3D rendering. Distribution- matching and score-implicit methods instead construct or estimate more of the student-distribution contribution (DMD2; SIM). They should be compared by target divergence, score estimator, teacher data, conditioning, guidance, and finite student architecture—not grouped as one generic “diffusion gradient.”

    Nonsmooth and constrained updates¤

    Some generative objectives contain an indicator constraint, an \(L_1\) penalty, a maximum, a clipping operation, top-\(k\) selection, or a discrete projection. A subgradient is a set-valued first-order object for an appropriate nonsmooth function. A proximal step solves a local regularized subproblem. Mirror descent uses a Bregman geometry. A projected or Riemannian step keeps an iterate on a constraint set or manifold. These are not interchangeable synonyms for a backpropagated Euclidean gradient.

    Typical applications include simplex-constrained mixture weights, sparse latent or energy parameters, spectral or Lipschitz constraints in critics, manifold-valued molecular states, and hard feasibility in inverse generation. Straight-through differentiation across a hard projection is a surrogate; implicit differentiation of a unique optimization layer requires its own regularity conditions.

    A functional derivative needs a geometry¤

    Let \(\mathcal F[\rho]\) be a functional of a density \(\rho\). Its first variation \(\delta\mathcal F/\delta\rho\) is defined through

    \[ \left.\frac{d}{d\epsilon} \mathcal F[\rho+\epsilon\chi] \right|_{\epsilon=0} =\int \frac{\delta\mathcal F}{\delta\rho}(x)\chi(x)\,dx \]

    for admissible mass-preserving perturbations \(\chi\). The first variation is analogous to a differential, not yet a unique gradient vector. Converting it to a gradient requires an inner product or metric on density perturbations. \(L^2\), Fisher--Rao, Wasserstein, and kernelized transport geometries therefore produce different flows from the same functional.

    Wasserstein gradient flow¤

    Under the formal \(W_2\) geometry, the gradient flow of \(\mathcal F\) satisfies

    \[ \partial_t\rho_t =\nabla\!\cdot\!\left( \rho_t\nabla \frac{\delta\mathcal F}{\delta\rho}(\rho_t) \right). \]

    The minimizing-movement or JKO discretization replaces this PDE by successive proximal problems,

    \[ \rho_{k+1} \in\arg\min_\rho \left\{ \mathcal F[\rho] +\frac{1}{2\tau}W_2^2(\rho,\rho_k) \right\}. \]

    Each numerical implementation must approximate a density, coupling, transport map, or dual potential. Solving a neural JKO surrogate for finite inner steps is not the same finite algorithm as integrating the exact gradient-flow PDE. Variational and semi-dual neural constructions give useful scalable examples (Fan et al.; Choi et al.).

    Worked free-energy flow becomes Langevin dynamics¤

    Let the target density be \(\pi(x)\propto e^{-V(x)}\) and define

    \[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal F[\rho] &=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\rho\|\pi)\\ &=\int \rho(x)\log\rho(x)\,dx\\ &\quad+\int V(x)\rho(x)\,dx +\text{constant}. \end{aligned} \]

    Its first variation is

    \[ \frac{\delta\mathcal F}{\delta\rho} =\log\rho+1+V. \]

    Substitution into the \(W_2\) flow gives

    \[ \partial_t\rho =\Delta\rho+\nabla\!\cdot(\rho\nabla V). \]

    This is the Fokker--Planck equation for

    \[ \begin{aligned} dX_t &=-\nabla V(X_t)\,dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t\\ &=\nabla\log\pi(X_t)\,dt+\sqrt2\,dW_t. \end{aligned} \]

    Thus Langevin sampling has a Wasserstein gradient-flow interpretation under the stated smoothness and boundary conditions. Euler--Maruyama with finite step size, an approximate score, and minibatch noise has additional discretization and estimation error; the interpretation does not erase them.

    State scores, energy forces, and guidance¤

    The state score

    \[ s_t(x)=\nabla_x\log p_t(x) \]

    is a vector field on sample space. Score matching trains parameters so a neural field approximates it; the optimizer still uses a parameter gradient of the score-matching loss. Hyvärinen's score-matching identity removes the unknown data score under smooth-support and boundary assumptions. Denoising score matching changes the target to a corrupted marginal at each noise level.

    Conditional guidance uses the identity

    \[ \nabla_x\log p_t(x\mid y) =\nabla_x\log p_t(x) +\nabla_x\log p_t(y\mid x), \]

    when the conditional quantities and derivatives are well defined. Classifier guidance estimates the second state gradient with a noise-conditional classifier (Dhariwal and Nichol). Scaling or replacing that term changes the sampled vector field; it need not remain the exact score of the original conditional law. Guidance gradients are sampling-time state derivatives, not training gradients of the base generator.

    Pathwise gradients are transport velocities¤

    For a differentiable scalar-parameter density family \(q_\theta\), or for one declared directional derivative of a vector parameter, a velocity field \(v_\theta(z)\) that satisfies

    \[ \partial_\theta q_\theta(z) +\nabla_z\!\cdot\! \left(q_\theta(z)v_\theta(z)\right)=0 \]

    transports probability mass as \(\theta\) changes. Under boundary and integrability conditions,

    \[ \partial_\theta \mathbb E_{q_\theta}[f_\theta(Z)] =\mathbb E_{q_\theta} \left[ \nabla_z f_\theta(Z)^\top v_\theta(Z) +\partial_\theta f_\theta(Z) \right]. \]

    Multiple velocity fields can satisfy the continuity equation and yield unbiased pathwise estimators with different variance. Explicit reparameterization supplies one field; implicit differentiation can supply fields for Gamma, Beta, Dirichlet, and von Mises families (Figurnov, Mohamed, and Mnih). The transport-equation view also supports alternative low-variance fields (Jankowiak and Obermeyer).

    This is an estimator-level transport correspondence. It does not make a VAE encoder update a Wasserstein gradient flow of the generated data distribution.

    Stein variational gradients move an interacting particle system¤

    SVGD considers infinitesimal maps \(T(x)=x+\epsilon\phi(x)\) and chooses a vector field from a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space to decrease \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(T_\sharp q\|p)\). Under its assumptions, the optimal direction has the form

    \[ \begin{aligned} \phi^*(\cdot) =\mathbb E_{X\sim q}\bigl[ &k(X,\cdot)\nabla_X\log p(X)\\ &+\nabla_X k(X,\cdot) \bigr]. \end{aligned} \]

    The first term follows the target score; the second repels particles through the kernel. The finite algorithm replaces \(q\) by an empirical particle measure and the expectation by a particle average (Liu and Wang). Its particles are interacting and generally are not independent exact samples from \(p\) after a finite number of updates. Kernel choice and particle count define the approximation.

    Sample-manifold gradients¤

    If states lie on a Riemannian manifold \((\mathcal X,g)\), the state gradient is defined by

    \[ g_x(\operatorname{grad}_g f(x),v)=df_x[v] \]

    for all tangent \(v\). In an embedded manifold, projecting an ambient Euclidean gradient can be correct only with the appropriate induced metric and tangent projection. Retractions, exponential maps, chart transitions, volume measures, and numerical integrators then affect the finite sampler.

    This is geometry of generated states. Fisher--Rao geometry is geometry of probability laws. Riemannian score models can use both, but the two metric tensors act on different spaces.

    Natural evolution strategies move a search law¤

    Let \(q_\psi(a)\) be a distribution over candidate model parameters, architectures, prompts, molecules, or other design objects, and let \(R(a)\) be a black-box reward. The search objective is

    \[ J(\psi)=\mathbb E_{A\sim q_\psi}[R(A)]. \]

    Its Fisher and natural gradient are

    \[ F_q(\psi) =\mathbb E_{q_\psi} [\nabla_\psi\log q_\psi(A)\nabla_\psi\log q_\psi(A)^\top], \qquad \widetilde\nabla_\psi J =F_q(\psi)^\dagger \mathbb E_{q_\psi}[R(A)\nabla_\psi\log q_\psi(A)]. \]

    This is the central construction in Natural Evolution Strategies. The Fisher belongs to the search distribution. It says nothing directly about the Fisher geometry of the data distribution produced by a selected candidate.

    Worked categorical search distribution¤

    Suppose three candidate generator designs have search probabilities

    \[ q=(0.5,0.3,0.2) \]

    and exact rewards \(R=(1,4,0)\). The mean reward is \(\bar R=1.7\). The categorical Fisher--Rao natural flow in probability coordinates has replicator form

    \[ \dot q_i=q_i(R_i-\bar R), \]

    giving

    \[ \dot q=(-0.35,0.69,-0.34). \]

    The components sum to zero, preserving total mass infinitesimally. A step of \(0.1\) gives \(q^+=(0.465,0.369,0.166)\), which remains in the simplex. With noisy rewards, rank shaping, baselines, entropy control, and covariance adaptation define different finite algorithms.

    Selection procedure¤

    Before adopting any gradient method, answer in order:

    1. Objective: which scalar functional, game field, conditional score, or outer query is differentiated?
    2. Variable: parameters, latent/state coordinates, a function, a particle map, a density, or a search distribution?
    3. Expectation: which data, model, posterior, path, or search law defines the population derivative?
    4. Exact derivative: what regularity, support, inner-solution, or boundary assumptions make it valid?
    5. Estimator: reverse AD, pathwise, score-function, relaxation, unrolling, implicit solve, particle average, or finite difference?
    6. Geometry: Euclidean, Fisher--Rao, GGN, manifold, Wasserstein, Sinkhorn, RKHS, or another declared metric?
    7. Finite update: what preconditioner, game schedule, damping, clipping, trust radius, timestep weighting, and numerical solver changes the ideal vector?
    8. Validation: is estimator bias/variance measured, does predicted geometry match actual movement, and does held-out generative behavior improve at matched compute?

    If the objective or differentiation variable cannot be answered, the word “gradient” is an analogy rather than an executable method specification.

    Controlled comparison protocol¤

    Compare against strong first-order and structured baselines using matched data, architecture, initialization distribution, global batch, precision, parameter groups, training examples or tokens, and hyperparameter-search budget. Report:

    • objective and held-out likelihood or family-appropriate quality metrics;
    • gradient, preconditioned direction, and actual update norms;
    • predicted and measured KL or declared divergence movement;
    • curvature samples, solve residual, damping fraction, and factor staleness;
    • wall-clock, peak memory, communication, and energy;
    • instability, skipped-step, and restart counts; and
    • uncertainty across seeds.

    A faster fall in training objective does not establish better density estimation, sampling, calibration, diversity, or compute efficiency.

    Evidence map and open questions¤

    Established. Reverse/forward differentiation, score-function and pathwise identities, Fisher--Rao local geometry, exponential-family duality, conjugate SVI natural updates, state scores, Langevin/Fokker--Planck correspondence, and exact infinitesimal invariance have mature foundations under their stated conditions.

    Peer-reviewed research. Implicit/pathwise estimators broaden variational families; SVGD, neural JKO schemes, and scalable Wasserstein flows provide functional or distributional constructions; K-FAC and FANG provide structured parameter-metric approximations; unrolling and extragradient-style methods alter game derivatives and dynamics; Sinkhorn natural gradient supplies a transport-based implicit-generator construction; the ELBO natural-gradient and time-varying-generator studies sharpen specific geometric connections; and the gradient-regularized natural-gradient study evaluates its variants on declared tasks.

    None of these results yields a family-independent ranking over gradient estimators, AdamW, Shampoo, K-FAC, natural-gradient VI, functional flows, transport pullbacks, guidance rules, and game optimizers. Important research questions include:

    • scalable model-Fisher estimation for large sequence generators without silently switching to the empirical Fisher;
    • geometries for singular implicit laws that remain stable in high dimension;
    • joint metrics and convergent finite algorithms for coupled adversarial games;
    • metrics that respect diffusion or flow path semantics and timestep weighting;
    • natural-gradient methods for structured discrete and manifold-valued laws;
    • low-variance unbiased gradients for discrete, branching, and long stochastic generative computations;
    • stable hypergradients through learned samplers and approximate inner inference without hiding truncation bias;
    • interaction among damping, normalization layers, parameter symmetries, and distributed optimizer state; and
    • evaluation that connects predicted local law movement to downstream sample quality and uncertainty.

    Volume synthesis and retrieval practice¤

    There is no single “generative-model gradient.” Parameter derivatives train objectives; Monte Carlo rules estimate them; state scores and forces move samples; game fields coordinate competing players; hypergradients propagate through inner algorithms; functional gradients move maps or measures; and natural gradients transform parameter derivatives under a declared statistical metric. A complete method names the target, variable, law, geometry, estimator, finite algorithm, and validation protocol. Connections among these gradients are useful only when the conversion preserves those typed fields.

    Retrieval questions
    1. In NES, which distribution supplies the Fisher?
    2. What is the first question to ask about a proposed natural-gradient GAN optimizer?
    3. Which measurements distinguish a good linear solve from a good geometric model?
    4. Why is there no single “natural gradient for a VAE” without qualifiers?
    5. Why is a diffusion score not the parameter gradient used to train its score network?

    Solutions. (1) The search distribution over candidates. (2) Which generator-side law or divergence defines the metric. (3) Solve residual checks numerical accuracy; predicted-versus-measured law divergence and held-out generative metrics check geometric and task relevance. (4) A VAE contains distinct generative-joint, observed-marginal, and variational- conditional families with different Fishers. (5) The score differentiates a log density with respect to state; training differentiates a score-matching loss with respect to network parameters.
